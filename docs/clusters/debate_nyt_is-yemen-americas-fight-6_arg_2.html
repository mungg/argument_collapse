"The United States should provide limited logistical, intelligence, and political support to the Saudi-led coalition in Yemen to protect strategic interests and maintain influence over the conflict's outcome."

The United States cannot pretend Yemen is someone else’s problem. It borders a vital shipping lane, has hosted one of the most active Al Qaeda branches targeting Americans, and now risks becoming another arena in which Iran expands influence at the expense of U.S. partners. Washington should not send troops or own Saudi Arabia’s campaign. But providing limited intelligence, logistics and political support to the Saudi-led coalition is a defensible and necessary choice.

The alternative is not neutrality in some tidy diplomatic seminar. It is a rapid Houthi consolidation of power after the collapse of a U.N.-backed transition that, however flawed, represented the only broadly recognized path out of Yemen’s post-Saleh turmoil. President Hadi’s government was weak, but it was internationally recognized. The Houthis did not merely protest; they seized Sana, placed Hadi under pressure, and pushed south. Their alliance with former President Ali Abdullah Saleh’s forces made them a military threat to the rest of Yemen, not just a political faction seeking representation.

Saudi fears are not paranoia. Riyadh sees a hostile armed movement on its southern border, chanting anti-American and anti-Saudi slogans, with at least some ties to Iran. Even if Tehran’s role is exaggerated — and it often is in Gulf rhetoric — Iran has a clear interest in bleeding Saudi Arabia and expanding leverage. If the United States abandoned the Saudis now, it would reinforce the belief among Gulf allies that Washington is prepared to negotiate with Iran over nuclear issues while leaving them alone against Iranian-backed pressure.

That said, support should be bounded. The United States should not give the coalition a blank check, nor should it embrace a purely military solution. Intelligence sharing must be tied to avoiding civilian casualties and distinguishing military targets from urban neighborhoods, arms depots near homes and essential infrastructure. Washington should insist that any campaign have a political endgame: restoring negotiations, not simply humiliating the Houthis. Aid routes and humanitarian pauses should be part of the American message from day one.

Critics correctly note that AQAP may benefit from chaos. That danger is real. But a Houthi takeover backed by Saleh’s loyalists would also generate chaos, perhaps provoking secession in the south, tribal mobilization and sectarian radicalization. There is no risk-free course. Refusing to support allies would not make Yemen stable, and it would not stop the war. It would simply leave Saudi Arabia to act with less American influence over targeting, diplomacy and restraint.

Yemen is not a war the United States should lead. But it is a crisis in which American interests, alliances and counterterrorism concerns are implicated. Limited support, coupled with pressure for a negotiated settlement, is the least bad option.

The United States Should Support Its Regional Partners in Yemen

The situation in Yemen presents a genuine security threat that directly concerns American interests, and the United States would be making a grave strategic error by withdrawing its support for Saudi-led efforts against Houthi forces. This is not a case of America overreaching into someone else's war—it is a case of America working alongside partners to address a shared threat.

The Houthis, though often framed as merely insurgents opposing a corrupt government, have aligned themselves with Iran and are receiving material support from Tehran. This is not a domestic Yemeni affair; it is a proxy conflict in which Iran is extending its influence deeper into the Arabian Peninsula. Allowing Iranian-backed forces to consolidate control over Yemen would hand Tehran a significant strategic victory and establish a hostile presence directly adjacent to Saudi Arabia, America's indispensable ally in the region.

Al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula has long operated from Yemen, and the group represents one of the most dangerous terrorist threats facing the United States. A collapsed or destabilized Yemen provides fertile ground for terrorist organizations to flourish. By supporting the Saudi-led coalition, the United States helps maintain conditions under which counterterrorism operations can continue.

Critics warn of civilian casualties and regional instability, but these concerns, while legitimate, do not justify abandonment of allies. Every military operation carries risk of harm to non-combatants. The answer is not to withdraw but to ensure operations are conducted with precision and with appropriate constraints. American intelligence and logistical support can help the Saudis minimize collateral damage and target legitimate military objectives.

The United States has spent years building partnerships in the Gulf, and those partnerships are worth sustaining. Walking away when our partners face an existential threat from Iranian expansion would undermine American credibility across the region. Future partners would calculate that America cannot be relied upon when the going gets difficult.

Yemen may be complicated, but it is America's fight insofar as American interests and American allies are at stake. The United States should maintain its current level of support and work to ensure the outcome serves both regional stability and American security.

Diversified DeepSeek

"The United States should provide limited, conditional logistical and intelligence support to the Saudi-led coalition in Yemen to maintain regional alliances and leverage influence toward a political settlement."

The United States finds itself in a grimly familiar position: its interests and its values appear to be pulling in opposite directions. The question is not whether Yemen is “America’s fight” in the sense of a vital, direct threat. It isn’t. But a complete withdrawal of support for the Saudi-led operation would carry its own grave risks. The prudent course is to provide targeted, restrained intelligence and logistical backing while using that very assistance as leverage to shape Saudi behavior and push for an urgent political settlement.

The strategic calculus is straightforward. Saudi Arabia, however flawed its leadership, is a key partner in a region where the United States has profound interests: energy market stability, freedom of navigation in the Bab el-Mandeb strait, and containing Iran’s aggressive push for influence. The kingdom, rightly or wrongly, viewed the Houthi takeover of Sanaa and the ouster of President Hadi as an Iranian-backed coup on its border that it could not ignore. If the United States had refused all support, it would have dealt a devastating blow to an already fragile alliance, particularly at a moment when the nuclear framework with Iran has Gulf states questioning America’s commitment to their security. A perceived abandonment could push Riyadh to act even more recklessly unilaterally, with fewer restraints, while also undermining our military access and diplomatic cooperation.

The key is in the framing. “Logistical and intelligence support” should not be a blank check. It must be conditioned, from day one, on stringent commitments from the coalition: strict adherence to no-strike lists for civilian infrastructure, a genuine acceptance of humanitarian pauses, and concrete, public steps toward a political dialogue that includes Houthi representatives. The United States has already begun moving in this direction by pressing for a return to UN-led negotiations; that pressure must be relentless and tied directly to the precision of the intelligence we share. We can help our allies hit military targets while denying them the tools to wage an indiscriminate campaign. This is not a clean division, but it is a more realistic boundary than pretending we can wash our hands of the conflict entirely.

Critics rightly warn of mission creep and blowback. Those dangers are real, and they demand a clear, public red line from the White House: no American boots on the ground and no direct combat role. Our role is to stabilize a spiraling ally while steering it toward a diplomatic off-ramp. A hasty retreat from the partnership will not stop the bombs; it will only ensure they fall without an American voice in the room arguing for restraint. Sometimes loyalty to an old friend means not joining the fight, but also not walking away — and telling them hard truths they do not wish to hear.

Diversified Gemini

"The United States should provide indirect logistical and intelligence support to the Saudi-led coalition in Yemen to maintain strategic alliances and regional stability."

The United States must support the Saudi-led military coalition in Yemen, not because the Houthi rebellion directly threatens the American homeland, but because abandoning our closest Arab allies at this critical juncture would have disastrous strategic consequences for the broader Middle East.

We are currently in the final, delicate stages of negotiating a nuclear framework with Iran. These P5+1 talks have deeply unsettled our traditional Sunni partners in the Gulf, primarily Saudi Arabia, who view an unshackled, nuclear-threshold Iran as an existential threat. In recent years, Tehran has expanded its influence through proxies in Lebanon, Syria, and Iraq. Now, with the Houthis violently ousting the internationally recognized government of Abdu Rabbu Mansour Hadi in Sana, the Saudis see the Iranian noose tightening around their southern border.

If the United States were to refuse logistical and intelligence support to Riyadh’s Operation Decisive Storm, we would send a devastating signal: that Washington is willing to abandon its traditional security guarantees in a bid to appease Tehran. Such a move would likely prompt Saudi Arabia and other Gulf states to act even more aggressively and unilaterally, untethered from American restraint, or worse, to pursue their own nuclear deterrents.

Providing targeting intelligence, aerial refueling, and logistical backing is a low-risk, high-reward strategy. It requires no American boots on the ground, yet it secures vital Saudi cooperation on other pressing American priorities, including the ongoing air campaign against the Islamic State in Syria and Iraq. Furthermore, the Bab el-Mandeb strait, which borders Yemen, is a critical chokepoint for global energy markets. Allowing a militant group aligned with Iran to control this waterway is a risk the global economy cannot afford.

The United States is right to avoid direct combat operations in Yemen’s civil war. But indirect support is the necessary price of alliance maintenance. By standing with Saudi Arabia, we reassure a vital partner, check Iranian expansionism, and maintain the leverage necessary to eventually steer this conflict toward a negotiated political settlement.

"The United States should provide limited, conditional support to the Saudi-led coalition in Yemen rather than remaining neutral, as this approach maintains regional influence and prevents further instability."

The United States should not rush into Yemen with troops or pretend that the Houthis can be bombed into political submission. But it also should not abandon its Saudi and Gulf allies at the very moment they face what they see, with reason, as a hostile armed movement taking over their southern neighbor. The right answer is limited, conditional American support for the coalition — intelligence, logistics, coordination and pressure for restraint — not aloof neutrality.

Yemen matters. It sits beside the Bab el-Mandeb, a choke point for global shipping. It has been the base of Al Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula, the most dangerous Al Qaeda franchise to the United States. Its government, however weak, was internationally recognized and cooperated with Washington against AQAP. When Houthi forces and units loyal to Ali Abdullah Saleh pushed south toward Aden, they were not merely joining a cabinet dispute; they were trying to settle Yemen’s transition by force.

It is true that the Houthis are not simply Iranian puppets. They have local origins and local grievances. But Iran has clearly welcomed their rise, and a Houthi-Saleh military victory would strengthen Tehran’s hand across the Arabian Peninsula. At a time when the Obama administration is negotiating with Iran over its nuclear program, Gulf allies already fear that Washington is accommodating Iran at their expense. Refusing even indirect help in Yemen would confirm their worst suspicions and might drive them toward more reckless unilateral action.

American involvement can reduce, not increase, the danger if handled properly. The Saudis and their partners are going to act regardless of whether Washington participates. If the United States is engaged, it can push for better targeting, fewer civilian casualties, clearer objectives and an exit strategy. If it walks away, it forfeits leverage while still being blamed for the region’s instability.

The conditions matter. The United States should not support a campaign of collective punishment, a blockade that starves civilians, or a march to restore Yemen’s old order by force. It should insist that the coalition’s goal be limited: stopping a Houthi-Saleh military takeover and forcing a return to negotiations. There should be no American ground combat, no open-ended anti-Houthi war, and no suspension of efforts to reach a political settlement through the United Nations.

Avoiding involvement sounds prudent, but in this case it may be the less responsible course. Limited support gives Washington influence over allies, reassures partners, and helps prevent Yemen from becoming an uncontested arena for Iran, militias and jihadists alike.
