"Expanding the definition of corruption to include 'institutional' or 'dependency' corruption is dangerous because it would grant Congress the power to censor political speech based on subjective judgments about an unhealthy political atmosphere."

Lawrence Lessig has offered the most attractive version of the case against Citizens United: measured, historically grounded, and free of the crude claim that corporations have no speech rights because they are not human beings. Yet his argument is finally more dangerous than the decision he criticizes. By expanding corruption from quid pro quo bribery to “institutional” or “dependency” corruption, he would license the government to regulate political speech whenever legislators, judges, or reformers believe that speech creates an unhealthy political atmosphere. That is a power no democracy should entrust to Congress.

The core issue in Citizens United was not whether American campaign finance is pretty. It is not. Nor was it whether money matters in politics. It does. The question was whether the government may prohibit a corporation—here, a nonprofit corporation formed to engage in political advocacy—from distributing a film critical of a presidential candidate near an election. If the First Amendment means anything, it must be at its strongest when citizens, associations, unions, nonprofit corporations, newspapers, and advocacy groups criticize candidates for office. That is not a peripheral case of free speech. It is the central case.

Lessig recognizes this problem and therefore does not rely on the simplistic slogan that “money is not speech.” He concedes that political communication costs money and that government restrictions on spending restrict speech. But having made that concession, he wants the Court to accept a broader anti-corruption rationale. The trouble is that his broader rationale has no limiting principle. If “dependency corruption” justifies suppressing independent expenditures, why would it not justify suppressing editorials by large media corporations? The New York Times Company, Fox News, General Electric through NBC, and countless other incorporated entities influence elections. Their expenditures are also made possible by the corporate form. They too may create gratitude, fear, access, or dependence. If the answer is that institutional press speakers are different, then the First Amendment becomes a licensing scheme: some corporations may speak because we call them press; others may not because we call them economic actors.

The majority in Citizens United was right to resist that path. The First Amendment does not permit Congress to decide which speakers are too powerful, too wealthy, too artificial, or too distorting to participate in electoral debate. Political speech is not less valuable because it is funded through a corporate treasury. A corporation is a legal form used by people to associate and act collectively. That is true of the Sierra Club, the ACLU, the Chamber of Commerce, Citizens United, and many small nonprofit organizations that no one imagines to be sinister. A rule that allows government to silence corporations will not only restrain Exxon or Goldman Sachs; it will restrain incorporated associations of citizens.

Lessig’s appeal to the framers’ desire for dependence “upon the People alone” is rhetorically powerful but constitutionally overextended. The people do not speak only as isolated voters. They speak through churches, unions, businesses, newspapers, trade associations, ideological groups, and nonprofit corporations. Representative government necessarily involves organized influence. Farmers ask for subsidies; environmentalists ask for regulation; businesses ask for tax treatment; unions ask for labor protections; civil-rights groups ask for enforcement. Representatives respond to some of these demands and reject others. That is politics, not necessarily corruption.

Justice Kennedy’s much-criticized phrase—representatives favor “voters and contributors” who support their policies—may be inelegant, but it reflects a mundane reality. Contributors often support candidates because they agree with them. A pro-choice donor gives to a pro-choice candidate; a gun-rights group supports a gun-rights candidate; a business association supports a candidate with deregulatory views. The fact that the candidate later votes consistently with those supporters does not prove corruption. It may prove ideological alignment. To treat such alignment as constitutionally suspect is to invite endless policing of motives.

Lessig responds that contributors and voters do not overlap. Much campaign money comes from outside a candidate’s district or state. That is true, but it does not establish corruption. National elections have national consequences. A senator from Massachusetts votes on wars, taxes, judicial confirmations, environmental law, and financial regulation that affect citizens in every state. Why should citizens outside Massachusetts be forbidden, or even regarded as suspect, for supporting or opposing him? If a California environmentalist spends money to defeat a West Virginia coal-state senator, she is not corrupting democracy. She is participating in a national republic.

The deepest flaw in Lessig’s argument is its trust in Congress as regulator of its own electoral environment. He worries, reasonably, that legislators are self-interested and dependent on funders. But then he asks us to allow those same legislators to restrict the political speech of outside groups in the name of protecting institutional integrity. This is the fox designing rules for the henhouse. Incumbents have every incentive to suppress speech that threatens them. McCain-Feingold’s electioneering-communications rule was not a narrow bribery statute. It was a blackout period before elections, the very time when political speech matters most. That the immediate victim was an anti-Hillary Clinton film should give pause to anyone who thinks such laws will be used only against big business.

The comparison to Lochner is also unpersuasive. The Lochner Court constitutionalized its economic theory and displaced ordinary regulation of working conditions. Citizens United, by contrast, protects a textual constitutional freedom at the heart of democratic self-government. Courts should be deferential when legislatures regulate bakery hours; they should be skeptical when legislatures regulate criticism of candidates. To call that skepticism “First Amendment Lochnerism” risks collapsing the distinction between economic regulation and censorship. The First Amendment exists precisely because elected officials cannot be trusted to decide when speech is too dangerous for voters to hear.

None of this means that the current system is healthy. Lessig is right that fundraising consumes too much time and that access follows money. But the constitutionally appropriate remedies are those that add speech rather than prohibit it. Disclosure, promptly and clearly administered, is legitimate. Shareholders can demand accountability from corporate managers who spend treasury funds on politics. Congress can strengthen anti-coordination rules to ensure that independent expenditures are truly independent. It can provide public financing, small-donor matching, or vouchers, so long as participation is voluntary and private speakers are not punished for opting out. It can lower barriers to ballot access and debates. It can use technology to inform voters better.

But it should not ban independent political advocacy by disfavored speakers. The idea that voters will lose faith if corporations speak assumes that voters cannot evaluate messages once they know the source. That is too pessimistic. Americans are bombarded with political claims from parties, candidates, unions, corporations, celebrities, newspapers, and neighbors. They discount, compare, mock, and reject. A democratic system “presupposes” voter judgment not because voters are omniscient, but because the alternative is worse: empowering government to decide which voices voters may hear.

Lessig fears hidden threats—the iceberg beneath reported expenditures. The concern is real, but it is not unique to corporations or independent expenditures. Newspapers can threaten hostile coverage. Unions can threaten mobilization. Advocacy groups can threaten primary challenges. Wealthy individuals, protected under Buckley, can spend independently without limit. The existence of influence cannot justify suppressing speech. If it did, politics itself would be unconstitutional.

Citizens United may produce more political advertising, much of it ugly and misleading. But ugliness and inequality are not sufficient reasons for censorship. The First Amendment is premised on the belief that the cure for bad speech is more speech, disclosure, organization, and argument. Lessig’s institutional-corruption theory, however well intentioned, would allow those in power to decide that some speech creates too much dependency, too much cynicism, too much distortion. That is a tempting theory in a frustrating age. It is also a theory that incumbents will know how to use.

The proper response to mistrust in Congress is not to give Congress broader power over political speech. It is to make government more transparent, make elections more competitive, enforce bribery laws, expose funding sources, and empower more citizens to participate. Citizens United is not the end of democracy. A government authorized to silence political associations near elections might be.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The First Amendment is at its strongest when protecting the right of associations, including nonprofit corporations, to criticize candidates for office.
  2. 02Lessig’s theory lacks a limiting principle and could be used to justify suppressing the speech of media corporations or any group that influences elections.
  3. 03Political speech is a collective act of association, and a rule allowing the government to silence corporations would unfairly restrain incorporated associations of citizens like the Sierra Club or the ACLU.
  4. 04Ideological alignment between contributors and candidates is a mundane reality of politics rather than evidence of corruption.
  5. 05Contributing to out-of-state or out-of-district candidates is a legitimate form of participation in a national republic with national consequences.
  6. 06Entrusting Congress to regulate its own electoral environment creates a conflict of interest where incumbents can suppress speech that threatens their power.
  7. 07The comparison to Lochnerism is flawed because the Court should be skeptical of regulations targeting political criticism, even if it is deferential toward economic regulation.
  8. 08The appropriate remedies for the flaws in the campaign finance system are those that increase speech, such as disclosure and public financing, rather than those that prohibit it.
  9. 09The First Amendment presupposes that voters are capable of evaluating and discounting political messages from various sources without government interference.
