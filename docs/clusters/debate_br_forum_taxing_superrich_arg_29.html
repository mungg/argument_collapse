Diversified Gemini

"While a wealth tax is a necessary tool to combat inequality, Saez and Zucman’s proposal is destined to fail unless it is paired with aggressive international financial diplomacy to dismantle the global offshore tax haven system."

In their compelling essay advocating for a radical wealth tax, Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman make a persuasive moral and economic case for targeting the accumulated fortunes of the ultra-rich. Their diagnosis of the U.S. tax system as a regressive flat tax at the very top is accurate, and their insistence that we must tax wealth directly to save democracy is a necessary provocation. However, their proposal suffers from a glaring blind spot: it dramatically underestimates the realities of global capital mobility and the formidable machinery of the offshore financial system. By treating the United States as an isolated economic island, Saez and Zucman fail to grapple with the international mechanisms required to make a wealth tax actually function in the twenty-first century.

The authors quickly dismiss the failure of wealth taxes in Europe, attributing those failures to a lack of "political will" and poorly designed valuation methods. This is a profound misreading of recent economic history. In 1990, twelve OECD countries had wealth taxes; today, only a handful remain. Countries like France, Sweden, and Germany did not abandon their wealth taxes because valuing private businesses was too difficult. They abandoned them because of massive capital flight.

When France imposed its *Impôt de Solidarité sur la Fortune* (ISF), thousands of millionaires simply moved across the border to Belgium, Switzerland, or the United Kingdom, taking their capital, their consumption, and their income tax revenues with them. The Swedish wealth tax was repealed in 2007 by a center-right government, but the repeal was supported by many on the left because it had driven iconic Swedish entrepreneurs and billions of kronor in investment capital out of the country.

Saez and Zucman might argue that the United States is different. The U.S. taxes its citizens on their global income regardless of where they live, a system known as citizenship-based taxation. In theory, an American billionaire cannot escape a U.S. wealth tax simply by moving to Monaco; they would have to formally renounce their U.S. citizenship. But the superrich are already prepared for this. The number of wealthy Americans renouncing their citizenship has spiked in the last decade, and a 10 percent annual wealth tax—which is practically confiscatory over a long horizon—would turn this trickle into a flood.

To prevent this, any U.S. wealth tax must be accompanied by a draconian exit tax. Presidential candidates like Elizabeth Warren have proposed an exit tax of 40 percent on all wealth above a certain threshold for those who renounce their citizenship. But executing such a tax on complex, global assets is intensely difficult, and it raises uncomfortable questions about the freedom of movement and the coercive power of the state.

More importantly, the ultra-wealthy do not need to physically leave the country to hide their wealth; they only need to move their assets. The global offshore tax haven industry—stretching from the Cayman Islands to the British Virgin Islands, from Swiss banks to South Dakota trust companies—exists precisely to obscure the true ownership of assets. The wealthy use layers of shell companies, anonymous trusts, and nominee directors to ensure that their names are completely detached from their fortunes.

Saez and Zucman assume that the IRS can easily define and measure the "market value of one's assets net of all debts." But how can the IRS tax wealth if it does not know who owns it? The Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (FATCA), passed in 2010, was a step in the right direction, forcing foreign banks to report the assets of U.S. citizens. But FATCA is riddled with loopholes, poorly enforced, and easily circumvented by moving assets out of financial institutions and into physical assets, real estate, or complex legal structures not covered by the law.

A viable wealth tax cannot be a unilateral American project. It requires an unprecedented level of global financial transparency and international cooperation. Specifically, it requires the creation of a Global Financial Registry—a centralized database that records the ultimate beneficial owners of all financial securities, real estate, and corporate equity worldwide. Without a registry that pierces the veil of offshore secrecy, a domestic wealth tax will essentially function as a tax on honesty, penalizing the billionaires who keep their assets visible in the U.S. while rewarding those who hire the best wealth-concealment lawyers in Geneva and Panama.

Furthermore, the United States must be willing to use its immense geopolitical and economic leverage to crush the tax haven model entirely. This means imposing crippling economic sanctions on jurisdictions that refuse to share beneficial ownership data, barring their financial institutions from accessing the U.S. dollar clearing system. It requires the U.S. to lead a coordinated global effort to harmonize tax rates and share information, ensuring there is no dark corner of the globe left for capital to hide.

Saez and Zucman are right that extreme wealth concentration is an existential threat. But their domestic policy prescriptions are insufficient for a hyper-globalized world. If we implement a 10 percent wealth tax without simultaneously dismantling the offshore financial architecture, we will not de-concentrate wealth; we will simply offshore it. We will enrich the lawyers and accountants of the Caribbean while hollowing out the domestic tax base. A progressive wealth tax is a worthy goal, but it must be viewed as a project of global financial diplomacy, not just an act of congressional legislation.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The authors misinterpret the failure of European wealth taxes, which were abandoned primarily due to massive capital flight rather than valuation difficulties.
  2. 02A high wealth tax would likely trigger a surge in wealthy Americans renouncing their citizenship, necessitating draconian and difficult-to-enforce exit taxes.
  3. 03The ultra-wealthy can easily evade domestic taxes by using complex offshore legal structures, shell companies, and anonymous trusts to obscure asset ownership.
  4. 04The IRS currently lacks the transparency and global reach required to accurately identify and value the hidden international assets of the super-rich.
  5. 05A functional wealth tax requires the creation of a Global Financial Registry to track beneficial ownership across all jurisdictions.
  6. 06The United States must use its geopolitical leverage to sanction tax havens and force global cooperation on financial transparency to prevent capital from simply moving to 'dark corners' of the globe.
