"Atheists should not pray to a supernatural deity they do not believe in, but they can fruitfully engage in prayer-like rituals that foster self-reflection, gratitude, and moral accountability."

Atheists should not pray if prayer means addressing a conscious power they believe does not exist. There is no virtue in make-believe piety, and a ritual that requires self-deception is unlikely to deepen anyone’s integrity. But that does not settle the question, because much of what people value in prayer is not reducible to supernatural petition.

For believers, prayer can be praise, lament, confession, thanksgiving, intercession and the discipline of placing one’s desires before God. Its point is not simply to get things — indeed, serious religious traditions have always warned against treating God as a vending machine — but to be changed: to become more grateful, more honest, more attentive to suffering and less enthralled by the self.

Those are not exclusively theistic goods. An atheist can pause each morning to name what she is grateful for, acknowledge whom she has harmed, rehearse the kind of person she wants to be, and hold friends or strangers in compassionate attention. She can do this silently, communally, with music, candles or repeated words. Whether we call that “prayer” is partly a semantic question and partly a question of cultural inheritance.

The danger for atheist churches is that they may import the consolations of religion while stripping away the demands. A good sermon without God can still be a moral exhortation; a bad one becomes group self-congratulation. Likewise, atheist “prayer” can become mere mood management unless it includes accountability: What will I do for the person I am thinking of? What habit must I change?

So atheists need not pray to anyone. But they may fruitfully practice prayer-like forms of attention: gratitude without illusion, confession without absolution, hope without guarantees, and solidarity without pretending that the universe is listening.

"Atheists can benefit from engaging in secularized forms of prayer and contemplative practices, provided they remain honest about the lack of a supernatural addressee."

Prayer, stripped of its supernatural addressee, is still doing recognizable psychological work: focusing attention, articulating gratitude, naming fears, rehearsing intentions, and binding the speaker to a community when done aloud. An atheist who refuses to do any of these things on the grounds that religious people also do them is impoverishing herself out of mere tribal hygiene. So yes — atheists can profitably engage in something like prayer, provided they are honest about what they are doing and to whom.

The harder question is whether to call it prayer. I think candor matters here. Petitionary prayer — asking an unseen agent to intervene — is precisely what an atheist cannot coherently do, and Sunday Assembly–style services that mimic the form risk a kind of cargo-cult sentimentality. But the contemplative traditions have always contained practices that even believers describe in non-petitionary terms: lectio divina, examen, the Quaker silence, much of Buddhist meditation. These are nearer to what William James called the "religion of healthy-mindedness," and they survive translation into secular vocabulary. Call it meditation, reflection, or, following Comte-Sponville and Alain de Botton's recent arguments, "spirituality for atheists."

What is the point? For believers, prayer is at minimum a relationship; at maximum, theosis. For the rest of us, it can still be a disciplined pause against the tyranny of distraction, a way of taking stock that conversation and therapy do not quite replicate. The danger of the new atheist churches is not that they borrow too much from religion but that they borrow the externals — hymns, sermons, coffee hour — while neglecting the inward practice that gave those externals their point.

The recent spate of atheist “church” services—complete with gathering, singing, and sermons—forces a sharper question: if you can borrow the rituals, can you also borrow the central act of prayer? For atheists, traditional prayer is a category mistake. It presupposes a divine recipient, an agent who hears and might respond. For believers, prayer serves multiple functions—adoration, confession, thanksgiving, and supplication—and its psychological yield is significant. It can calm anxiety, cultivate gratitude, and provide a sense of connection to something larger than oneself. Atheists have access to these same human needs. Secular analogues like meditation, mindful breathing, or reflective journaling can produce similar states of focused awareness and emotional regulation. The difference is that the atheist’s introspection is directed inward or toward the natural world, not outward to a supernatural listener.

The emerging atheist assemblies implicitly recognize this. They offer collective silence or guided reflection without invoking God. Whether to label such moments “prayer” is a matter of linguistic strategy. Using the word risks confusion—suggesting that atheism secretly longs for theism—but it also reclaims a rich tradition of contemplative practice. If participants understand that they are not addressing a deity but are instead deliberately focusing their intentions, expressing hopes, or acknowledging shared vulnerabilities, the activity can be fruitful.

Ultimately, prayer without God is something else: a ritual of attention, an exercise in moral imagination, or an act of communal solidarity. Atheists need not pray in any theistic sense, but they can and do practice the core elements that make prayer meaningful for believers—stillness, gratitude, and aspiration—stripped of supernatural metaphysics. That is not borrowing a falsehood; it is recognizing a universal human search for depth, and meeting it with intellectual honesty.

The emergence of atheist church services raises a genuine philosophical puzzle. If prayer fundamentally presupposes belief in a divine listener, can atheists meaningfully pray at all—and if not, what are they doing when they gather in ways that mimic religious practice?

My answer is that prayer, for believers, serves at least three distinct functions that should not be collapsed: petitioning divine intervention, cultivating gratitude and humility, and marking time through ritual. Only the first requires theism. The second and third do not.

Gratitude practices, moment-taking, and deliberate pauses for reflection are well-documented sources of psychological benefit. Secular meditation and mindfulness exercises borrow from religious traditions precisely because contemplative practices work—whether or not supernatural mechanisms are involved. An atheist who adopts "prayerful" discipline might be doing something analogous: deliberately stepping back from the noise of daily life to cultivate a sense of proportion and connection.

For believers, this misses the point. Prayer is fundamentally relational; it presumes Someone who listens. A prayerful atheist, however sincere, is engaging in self-reflection rather than worship. This is not dishonest so long as everyone understands what is happening.

The more interesting question is whether borrowing religious forms serves atheists well. Community clearly matters, and rituals provide social bonding that bare rational agreement cannot. But adopting the vocabulary and gestures of prayer risks confusion—both for participants who may want more theological content and for outsiders who may see hypocrisy.

If atheist gatherings acknowledge this honestly, they can offer genuine community without false pretenses. Prayer, rethought, might be less about speaking to God and more about quieting the self.

The question “Should atheists pray?” is less important than “What work do we expect prayer to do?” Once we ask that, the answer becomes more complicated and more interesting.

Prayer has always had multiple functions. It can be theology, but also sociology. It gathers people into a shared rhythm. It gives grief a script when ordinary speech fails. It lets the powerless name injustice. It teaches children what a community values. It slows panic. It creates a pause between impulse and action. Whether God hears prayer is the deepest question for believers, but it is not the only reason prayer has endured.

Atheists are right to notice this. The rise of atheist congregations is not proof that unbelievers secretly believe in God; it is proof that human beings are social, symbolic animals. If secular people want durable communities, they cannot rely only on lectures and online comment threads. They need meals, music, holidays, care networks, and rituals for birth, illness and death.

But atheists should be careful about simply rebranding prayer. Words carry histories. To many believers, prayer is sacred address, not a mood-setting exercise. To many atheists, prayer suggests submission to an illusion. A secular group that announces “prayer without God” may please journalists but confuse participants.

A better approach would be functional honesty. If the goal is gratitude, say thanks publicly to the people and conditions that sustain life. If the goal is moral commitment, make vows. If the goal is grief, hold silence and speak the names of the dead. If the goal is awe, contemplate the age of the stars, the fragility of the body and the improbability of being alive. If the goal is compassion, organize service.

Believers, meanwhile, should not pretend that the social and psychological benefits of prayer are irrelevant. Prayer can calm, focus, bind and heal. That does not make it false. A meal nourishes the body whether or not it also expresses love. The fact that prayer has human effects does not disprove divine presence.

The most fruitful path may be pluralism rather than translation. Let believers pray to God. Let atheists develop practices of reflection, gratitude and solidarity. Let shared civic occasions include silence broad enough for both prayer and non-prayer. There is no need to force one vocabulary on everyone.

Atheists should borrow from religion when the borrowing is honest and respectful. They should not be embarrassed to need ritual. But neither should they pretend that prayer is just a generic wellness practice. The point is not to imitate belief. The point is to form people capable of courage, gratitude and love.

What Prayer Actually Does

Most arguments about whether atheists should pray get hung up on metaphysics — is there anyone on the receiving end? — and miss what prayer empirically does for the people who practice it. As someone trained in cognitive science, I find this evasive. We should look at the function before we adjudicate the form.

Decades of research on contemplative practice, much of it summarized in journals like *Psychological Science* and in work by Richard Davidson, Sara Lazar, and others, suggests that structured, repeated, inward-directed verbal practices produce measurable changes: reduced cortisol, altered activity in the default-mode network, improved emotional regulation, and stronger pro-social dispositions. These effects don't seem to require theological assent. Buddhist monks, Catholic contemplatives, and secular MBSR practitioners all show overlapping benefits. Prayer, in other words, is a form of cognitive training, and the trainee gets the benefit whether or not anyone is listening.

Prayer also performs social work. Petitionary prayer for others has been correlated with increased empathy and reduced aggression toward the person prayed for. Gratitude practices — which are essentially prayers of thanksgiving stripped of a recipient — show robust effects on subjective well-being in studies by Emmons and others. Confessional prayer mirrors the disclosure effects Pennebaker documented in expressive writing. These are not small things. They are the everyday tools by which humans regulate themselves.

Given this, the question "should atheists pray?" almost answers itself. If a practice reliably makes you calmer, kinder, and more focused, the rational response is to adopt it, modified as needed to fit your actual beliefs. An atheist who sits quietly each morning and silently articulates what she is grateful for, whom she hopes to help, and what she resolves to do better is praying in every functional sense. She is just declining to add a metaphysical addressee she doesn't believe in.

The Sunday Assemblies and similar gatherings are experimenting in exactly this direction, and they deserve curiosity rather than scorn. The interesting question is not whether atheists should pray but which forms of prayer survive translation into a naturalistic worldview. Petition probably doesn't. Gratitude, reflection, and resolution clearly do. Atheists who refuse them on principle are letting their opposition to bad metaphysics deprive them of perfectly good psychology.

Prayer Without a Listener Is Just Talking to Yourself

The current vogue for "atheist churches" reveals something embarrassing about the New Atheist movement: having spent a decade insisting that religion is intellectually bankrupt, its adherents now find themselves lonely on Sunday mornings. The Sunday Assembly in London, and its imitators springing up in Baton Rouge and New York, are a tacit admission that Dawkins and Hitchens told only half the story. Religion isn't just a set of factual claims about the universe; it's a technology of community, ritual, and consolation. Fine. But prayer is a bridge too far.

Singing together makes obvious secular sense — humans have sung in groups since long before monotheism. Sermons are just lectures with better pacing. Potlucks are potlucks. These can be lifted out of the religious context without contradiction. Prayer cannot. Prayer is, by definition, address: it presupposes a listener. To pray to no one is not a ritual; it's a performance of belief one does not hold. It is the spiritual equivalent of writing letters to Santa as an adult — sweet, perhaps, but fundamentally confused.

Defenders will say prayer can be "reframed" as meditation, gratitude practice, or focused intention. But we already have words for those things, and they don't require the theological vocabulary. Meditation is meditation. Journaling is journaling. Calling these activities "prayer" is either rhetorical sleight of hand or nostalgia for a worldview one has supposedly outgrown. If a former Catholic finds comfort in lighting a candle and reciting words into the void, I won't begrudge them the comfort — but they should be honest that they're soothing a habit, not engaging in a coherent practice.

There's also a strategic point. The intellectual credibility of unbelief depends on its willingness to do without the consolations of belief. Atheists who borrow prayer are vulnerable to the obvious rejoinder: if the rituals are so valuable, perhaps the underlying claims deserve a second look. Better to build genuinely new secular practices — civic rituals, philosophical discussion groups, mutual-aid networks — than to wear religion's hand-me-downs and pretend they fit.

Gather, by all means. Sing. Listen to a good talk. But keep your hands unfolded and your eyes open. Honest unbelief is its own discipline, and it doesn't need to whisper to a god it has already dismissed.

Atheists should not pray. They should gather, sing, mourn, meditate, celebrate, forgive, repent and serve. But they should not call these activities prayer unless they are willing to smuggle into their practice the very premise they deny.

Prayer is not merely a pleasant inward feeling. It is not just taking a deep breath before a difficult conversation or feeling grateful at sunrise. To remove that address and keep the term is like staging a wedding with no spouse, no promise and no union, then saying the important thing was the flowers.

This does not mean atheists lack depth. On the contrary, one of the most admirable features of serious atheism is its refusal to pretend. Atheists often insist that comfort is not a sufficient reason for belief. They should apply that same rigor to ritual. If no one is listening, then petitionary prayer is false. If no one is there to receive thanksgiving, then thanksgiving should be directed toward the people, institutions and natural conditions that actually made one’s life possible.

There are many alternatives. Meditation can cultivate attention without metaphysical confusion. Ethical reflection can examine conscience. Public assemblies can build solidarity. Music can stir courage. Eulogies can honor the dead. Silence can make room for grief. None of these requires the vocabulary of prayer.

Believers, too, should resist reducing prayer to therapy. Prayer may bring psychological benefits, but that is not its essence. For Jews, Christians, Muslims and many others, prayer is an encounter with God: praise, confession, lament, intercession and surrender. It is not primarily a self-improvement technique. It can be dry, difficult, even apparently unanswered. Its meaning depends on its truth.

The fashion for atheist church services reflects a real social problem. Secular modernity can be lonely. People miss singing with others; they miss rites of passage; they miss a moral community larger than consumer preference. Atheists should build institutions worthy of their convictions. Let them have assemblies, songs, lectures and acts of service. Let them cultivate gratitude and awe. But let prayer remain what it is: speech, silence or longing directed toward God. Integrity is better than imitation.
