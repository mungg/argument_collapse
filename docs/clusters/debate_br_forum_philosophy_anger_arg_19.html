The Temporality of Moral Reasons

Agnes Callard’s argument for the rationality of eternal anger rests on a claim about the timelessness of moral facts. “What I did will always diverge from what I ought to have done,” so the reason to be angry is forever. This is a powerful move, but it relies on an unexamined meta-ethical assumption: that moral reasons are atemporal, floating free of the changing circumstances of moral life. If we deny that assumption—if we adopt a contextualist or temporalist view of moral reasons—then Callard’s argument collapses and we can see why apologies and time genuinely change the rational status of anger.

The core issue is whether the “ought” of “I ought not to have stolen” is a simple descriptive fact about the past, akin to “Caesar crossed the Rubicon,” or a fact that is deeply connected to present duties and relationships. Callard treats it as the former: a permanent, unchanging truth that stands as a constant grounds for anger. But suppose we think of moral obligations as essentially tied to our actual circumstances, including temporal ones. When I wrong you, I incur a specific set of obligations: to apologize, to compensate, to reform. Once I have done those things in good faith, the moral landscape is not the same. The past wrong remains part of history, but its moral significance for our ongoing interaction is transformed. The “ought” that was violated is no longer a live demand because the conditions that made it an active obligation—my status as an unrepentant wrongdoer—have been removed.

This is not to say the past wrong becomes acceptable. It is to say that the appropriate moral response to a fully addressed wrong is different from the response to an ongoing, unaddressed wrong. To see this, consider a parallel: suppose I borrow a book from you and fail to return it, thus wronging you. Later, I return it and apologize. The fact that I once kept it past due is true, but does that fact give you a continuing reason to be angry, even after I have returned it? Most of us would say no, because the wrong was precisely that I deprived you of the book and broke trust. Returning it and apologizing fulfills the demands that arose from the wrong. The moral reason for anger was not about abstract historical truth; it was about the state of our interaction. Once that state is set right, the reason dissolves. Callard’s example of theft and compensation is no different; it only seems different because she frames it as an eternal gap.

Callard anticipates this when she says apology and compensation are not “forms of redress” that alter the past fact. But this merely begs the question against the contextualist. The contextualist does not claim the past fact is altered; she claims that the past fact, in itself, is not the whole of the reason for anger. The reason for anger was always the fact that a wrong was done *and has not been addressed*. The phrase “has not been addressed” contains an implicit temporal index. Once it is addressed, the complete reason for anger no longer obtains. The persistence of the bare historical fact is beside the point.

The same analysis applies to revenge. Callard argues that revenge is rational because it is how we hold the wrongdoer accountable by turning their principle against them. But this again assumes that the wrongdoer’s moral stance is frozen in time. A sincere apology and amendment demonstrate a change in that stance. The person who pays restitution and promises not to steal again is no longer operating on the principle that it is acceptable to take your goods. To keep treating her as if she holds that principle is to ignore the updated moral reality. Revenge, then, is rational only against the unrepentant. Against the repentant, it becomes cruelty, not accountability.

Of course, one might doubt the sincerity of apologies, especially for deep historical wrongs. But that is an epistemological, not a metaphysical, problem. If the apology is genuine, the moral situation has altered, and with it the grounds for anger. Callard’s framework leaves no room for this because it is, at bottom, committed to a version of moral realism that locates moral truth in facts unaffected by human responses. But a more plausible meta-ethics sees moral obligations as arising from and being extinguished by social processes. That is why forgiveness is not a denial of the past but a recognition that the moral debt has been paid.

If this is correct, then Callard’s tragic conclusion—that victims are inevitably corrupted by their otherwise rational anger—is a product of a flawed picture of moral reasons. We are not stuck with eternal grudges because the reasons for anger are not eternal. Time and sincere repair do not just soothe emotions; they rationally change the moral score. The grandmother in Callard’s story was not irrational for still feeling pain, but she may have been wrong to treat that pain as a continuing moral warrant for wrath against a child decades removed from the crime. A more temporally sensitive morality would allow her to honor the past without being enslaved to it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Moral obligations and the reasons for anger are not static historical facts but are tied to the current state of a relationship and the presence or absence of redress.
  2. 02A sincere apology and restitution transform the moral landscape, effectively dissolving the active reason for anger by addressing the violation that triggered it.
  3. 03Callard’s defense of revenge fails because it treats the wrongdoer’s moral stance as frozen, ignoring that repentance signifies an abandonment of the harmful principle being punished.
  4. 04Forgiveness and the cessation of anger are rational responses to a 'paid' moral debt rather than a denial of past historical truths.
  5. 05By adopting a temporally sensitive view of morality, victims can honor the past without being rationally committed to eternal grudges or moral corruption.
