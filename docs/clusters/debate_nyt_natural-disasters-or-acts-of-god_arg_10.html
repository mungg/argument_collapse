"Thinking of natural disasters as acts of God is constructive only if it fosters human resilience, solidarity, and moral obligation rather than serving as a tool for victim-blaming or legal evasion."

It depends entirely on what is meant by “act of God.” If the phrase means that God hurled a typhoon at the Philippines as punishment, it is not merely unconstructive; it is cruel. It adds theological accusation to human suffering. The grieving mother in Tacloban does not need a theory explaining why her child deserved the storm. She needs food, shelter, medicine, companionship and time to lament.

But religious language need not function that way. For many believers, saying “leave it to God” is not an explanation of meteorology. It is a way to live with terror that exceeds calculation. “Bahala na” can be fatalism, but it can also be courage: I will do what I can, and entrust what I cannot control to God. Anyone who has watched people face illness, war or disaster knows that this kind of trust can be psychologically and spiritually sustaining.

The best theological traditions are wary of easy answers. The Book of Job rejects the friends who insist that suffering must be deserved. In the Gospel of Luke, Jesus refuses to say that those killed when the tower of Siloam fell were worse sinners than others. These texts do not solve the problem of suffering, but they warn against turning calamity into an indictment of the victims.

A constructive religious response should therefore shift the question. Not “Why did God do this?” but “What does faith require of us now?” In practice, religious communities often answer well. Churches, mosques, temples and faith-based charities are among the first to collect money, shelter neighbors, bury the dead and sustain communities after the television cameras leave. Belief can produce resignation, but it can also produce obligation.

There is still a danger in the legal and political use of the phrase. If “act of God” becomes a loophole for bad engineering, bad planning or official neglect, then it has betrayed both reason and religion. A God worthy of worship should not be invoked to excuse shoddy levees, reckless coastal development or indifference to the poor.

So yes, it can be constructive to think of disasters in relation to God, if doing so produces humility, solidarity and perseverance. It is destructive when it becomes blame, evasion or passivity. The question is not whether God can be mentioned after the storm. The question is whether invoking God helps us love our neighbors more concretely.
