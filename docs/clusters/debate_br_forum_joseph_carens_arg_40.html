"Joseph Carens’s attempt to justify amnesty through an analogy to statutes of limitations is legally and logically flawed because it misapplies principles of criminal law to the ongoing status of unauthorized presence."

In "The Case for Amnesty," Joseph Carens leans heavily on an analogy to statutes of limitations, borrowing from historian Mae Ngai to argue that just as the state’s power to pursue crimes erodes over time, so too should its power to pursue immigration violations. This is a seductive legal parallel, but it fundamentally misconstrues the purpose of statutes of limitations and misapplies the concept to civil immigration law, resulting in a flawed justification for amnesty.

Statutes of limitations in criminal law exist for two primary reasons: the degradation of evidence over time and the right of the accused to a speedy resolution. If the state waits twenty years to prosecute a theft, witnesses die, memories fade, and physical evidence is lost. It becomes impossible to guarantee a fair trial. The statute of limitations forces the state to act diligently and protects individuals from having to defend themselves against ancient claims where exculpatory evidence may no longer exist.

Neither of these rationales applies to the "crime" of being an undocumented immigrant. The violation of unauthorized presence is not a discrete act buried in the past; it is a present, ongoing status. Every single day a migrant remains in the country without authorization, they are renewing the violation. There is no issue of fading memories or lost evidence; the individual either currently has legal status or they do not. To apply a statute of limitations to an ongoing violation is legally incoherent. It would be akin to saying that if you successfully occupy someone else’s home for five years, the police can no longer evict you because the initial break-in happened too long ago.

Furthermore, Carens’s assertion that immigration violations are trivial, akin to "traffic regulations," ignores the systemic nature of the offense. A traffic ticket is a minor infraction that does not alter the foundational social contract. Bypassing a nation's sovereign rules of entry and settlement, however, bypasses the very mechanism by which a democracy decides its composition.

If Carens wishes to argue for amnesty, he must do so on political or humanitarian grounds—which he attempts with his "social membership" theory—but he cannot cloak his argument in the legitimacy of standard legal principles like the statute of limitations. The law does not recognize a right acquired through the continuous, deliberate evasion of the law itself. To codify such a principle would make a mockery of the legal system, replacing the rule of democratically enacted law with a rule of endurance and successful subterfuge.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Statutes of limitations are designed to address the degradation of evidence and ensure fair trials for past discrete acts, neither of which applies to the verifiable and ongoing status of being undocumented.
  2. 02Applying a statute of limitations to unauthorized residence is incoherent because it would essentially reward the continuous and deliberate evasion of the law.
  3. 03Carens wrongly trivializes immigration violations as minor infractions like traffic tickets, ignoring that bypassing sovereign entry rules undermines the democratic social contract.
  4. 04Codifying a right to stay based on the passage of time would replace the rule of law with a system that incentivizes successful subterfuge and endurance.
