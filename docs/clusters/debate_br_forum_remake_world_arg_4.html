"While Walter Johnson correctly critiques the concept of 'dehumanization' and links capitalism to slavery, his dismissal of universal human rights in favor of a vaguely defined justice rooted in the black radical tradition is a dangerous error that abandons essential moral and political tools for progress."

Walter Johnson’s essay is bracing, brilliant, and, in crucial respects, dangerously wrong. He is right to warn against the lazy language of “dehumanization,” right to insist that the violence of slavery was a human violence, perpetrated by humans upon humans for recognizably human purposes: power, profit, and the sick satisfactions of domination. He is right, too, to recover the radical tradition of Du Bois, Williams, and Robinson, and to demand that we see the history of capitalism as inseparable from the history of racial slavery. But his argument takes a wrong turn when it moves from diagnosis to prescription—when it dismisses the language of universal human rights as a neo-imperialist dead end and gestures instead toward a vaguely defined justice rooted in the standpoint of the enslaved. In doing so, Johnson risks abandoning the very moral and political resources that have made possible whatever progress we have achieved against the legacies of slavery.

Let me begin with a point of agreement. Johnson’s critique of “dehumanization” talk is a necessary corrective. The idea that slaveholders had to somehow trick themselves into believing their slaves were not human before they could exploit and abuse them is a comforting fiction for those of us who live after. It implies that atrocity requires a prior ideological erasure of the victim’s humanity—that normal, decent people could never do such things to fellow humans. But history teaches otherwise. Humans have proven themselves abundantly capable of inflicting extraordinary cruelty on people they know perfectly well are human: enemies in war, heretics in religious conflict, political rivals, and, yes, enslaved people whose labor, reproduction, and sentience were the very reasons for their enslavement. The slaveholder who beats a field hand does not need to believe that hand is an animal; he needs to believe he has the right to do it, and that the hand’s humanity—her capacity to feel pain, fear, and humiliation—is precisely what makes the exercise of power satisfying and effective. Johnson is right: the “dehumanization” thesis lets perpetrators off the hook by pathologizing their actions, and it patronizes the enslaved by implying that their humanity is something that can be taken away by an oppressor’s actions rather than an inalienable fact of their existence.

But here the trouble begins. Having diagnosed the problem with one form of universalism—the abstract, bourgeois liberalism that treats enslaved people as proto-citizens striving for rights they could not yet name—Johnson proceeds to indict the entire tradition of human rights as inadequate to the task of justice. He draws on Samuel Moyn’s argument that human-rights thinking is a creature of the late twentieth century, not the age of abolition, and that it has served as “the justifying terms of continuing European and American intervention in the affairs of former colonies.” Against this, he proposes an alternative genealogy of freedom, one rooted in the black radical tradition and culminating in calls for reparations and a thoroughgoing restructuring of the global political economy. He sketches six “virtues” of an account of justice rooted in the history of slavery, including its focus on resource extraction between Global North and South, its attention to past patterns of privilege and abjection, its insistence on restorative rather than merely rights-based remedies, and its linking of racial and environmental domination.

This is stirring stuff, but it raises a question Johnson never adequately answers: Who is going to implement this justice, and by what authority? The universalist language of human rights, for all its flaws and hypocrisies, provides at least a partial answer to that question. It says that every person, by virtue of being human, has certain claims that can be made against any power—against the state, against the corporation, against the international order. Those claims can be articulated in courts of law, in international forums, in the court of public opinion. The language of rights is a language of demand, and it has been taken up by the dispossessed again and again, not because they are dupes of liberal imperialism, but because it gives them a tool with which to fight back.

Consider the movement for reparations that Johnson himself celebrates. The case for reparations does not rest solely on the particular history of African American suffering, important as that history is. It also rests on a universal principle: that when a wrong has been done, the wrongdoer has an obligation to make it right. That principle is not the exclusive property of the black radical tradition; it is a fundamental tenet of justice across cultures and legal systems. When advocates for reparations argue that the United States government owes a debt to the descendants of enslaved people, they are appealing to norms of fairness and accountability that are, in their most general form, universal. They are saying: You did this; you benefited from this; you must repair this. Without that universalist backing, the demand for reparations becomes merely an assertion of group interest—no more binding on white Americans than their own assertion of group interest in keeping what they have.

Johnson might reply that the universalism he rejects is a specifically Western, liberal universalism, one that smuggles in bourgeois assumptions about property, individuality, and the state. Fair enough. But the alternative he gestures toward—a justice rooted in the experiences of the enslaved and the dispossessed—remains frustratingly underspecified. What would such a justice look like in practice? What institutions would embody it? What principles would guide the distribution of resources, the adjudication of disputes, the restraint of power? The black radical tradition is rich in critique, but it has been less forthcoming with blueprints. And for good reason: the task of building a just world is a collective project, one that requires negotiation among people with different histories, different interests, and different visions of the good. The language of universal rights, for all its imperfections, provides a framework for that negotiation. It allows the descendant of enslaved people in Mississippi to make common cause with the indigenous activist in Bolivia, the sweatshop worker in Bangladesh, the dissident in China—all of whom can say, in their own idioms, “I have rights, and they are being violated.”

Johnson is also too quick to dismiss the achievements of the human-rights movement. He quotes Marx approvingly on the limits of political emancipation, but Marx was writing in 1843, before the abolition of slavery in the United States, before the end of European colonialism, before the civil rights movement, before the Convention on the Elimination of All Forms of Discrimination Against Women, before the international campaign against apartheid. Each of these struggles was incomplete; each left new forms of domination in its wake. But to describe them as mere “political emancipation” that leaves the real structures of racial capitalism untouched is to understate their significance. Political emancipation may not be the “final form” of human freedom, but it is not nothing. The right to vote, the right to own property, the right to marry whom one chooses, the right to be free from arbitrary violence at the hands of the state—these are not bourgeois illusions; they are hard-won protections that have made a material difference in the lives of millions of people.

The danger of Johnson’s argument is that, in its zeal to expose the inadequacies of liberal universalism, it risks ceding the moral high ground to those who would dismantle even the limited protections that exist. We live in a moment when the language of human rights is under sustained assault—from ethnonationalists who see it as a cover for globalism, from authoritarians who see it as a threat to sovereignty, from corporate interests that see it as an obstacle to profit. In such a moment, it is not enough to say that liberal rights are insufficient. We must also defend them, even as we push beyond them. The challenge is to hold two thoughts at once: that the rights-bearing individual of liberal theory is an abstraction that obscures the real structures of racial and economic domination, and that the idea of rights-bearing individuals has been a powerful weapon in the hands of the dominated. Frederick Douglass understood this. So did Martin Luther King Jr. So did the women who organized at Seneca Falls, the workers who struck at Flint, the activists who faced down tanks in Tiananmen Square. They did not imagine that winning their rights would bring about the final emancipation of humanity. But they knew that rights were worth fighting for.

Johnson concludes by invoking Du Bois’s warning about the cost of missed opportunities, the blood of subsequent generations who pay for the failures of their forebears. It is a powerful image, and it cuts both ways. If we abandon the language of universal human rights because it fails to live up to its own pretensions—if we dismiss it as a tool of empire and retreat into a particularistic account of justice grounded in the history of a single, albeit foundational, injustice—we may miss an opportunity of our own. We may find ourselves without the moral vocabulary to oppose the next atrocity, or the next, or the one after that. The history of slavery teaches us that human beings are capable of great evil. It also teaches us that human beings are capable of resisting evil, and that one of the most potent resources for resistance has been the simple, stubborn insistence that every person matters. That insistence does not belong to liberalism or to the West or to any particular tradition. It is a human inheritance, and we abandon it at our peril.

Diversified DeepSeek

"While Walter Johnson correctly identifies slavery as a foundational economic system of racial capitalism, his dismissal of universal human rights as a 'bourgeois' or 'imperialist' framework ignores the practical power and moral necessity of rights-based language for the oppressed."

I was halfway through Walter Johnson’s dense, learned, and densely learned essay when I found myself thinking of my grandmother. Not because she was a scholar of racial capitalism—she left school at fourteen to work in a laundry—but because she was, in her quiet way, a philosopher of justice. She grew up in rural Alabama in the 1920s and 1930s, the granddaughter of enslaved people. She knew, without ever having read Du Bois or Marx, that the world was ordered in ways designed to keep people like her at the bottom. She also knew, without ever having read a word of human-rights theory, that she had a right to be treated with dignity, and that when that right was violated, the violation was not just a personal insult but a cosmic wrong. She believed in rights the way she believed in gravity: as a fact about the universe that did not depend on anyone’s acknowledgment.

I think my grandmother would have been puzzled by Johnson’s essay. She would have agreed with his fundamental claim—that slavery was an economic system as much as a moral outrage—because she lived the consequences of that truth every day of her life. The poverty she grew up in, the segregated school she attended, the cousin who disappeared into the prison system, the uncle who was cheated out of his land: all of this she understood as the long tail of an institution that had treated her ancestors as both labor and capital. She did not romanticize the past or imagine that emancipation had settled accounts. She knew, as Johnson knows, that the debt was still outstanding.

But she also believed in rights. She believed in the right to vote; she walked three miles to the polling place every election day until she could no longer walk. She believed in the right to a fair wage; she joined a union and, when the union went on strike, she walked the picket line even though she was scared. She believed in the right to be free from humiliation; she once told a white store clerk who called her “auntie” that her name was Mrs. Williams and she would thank him to use it. These were not, to her, bourgeois abstractions or tools of imperial power. They were the armor she put on every morning to go out into a world that did not want her in it.

I mention my grandmother not to enlist her in an argument by sentiment but to make a point about the audience for Johnson’s essay—and, more broadly, for the intellectual tradition he represents. Johnson is writing for a readership that is, I suspect, largely academic, largely steeped in critical theory, and largely comfortable with the claim that human-rights discourse is a form of liberal neo-imperialism. He cites Samuel Moyn with approval, noting that human-rights thinking has provided “the justifying terms of continuing European and American intervention in the affairs of former colonies.” There is a truth in this. The language of human rights has been used to justify disastrous wars, to impose structural adjustment programs, to dress up regime change as humanitarianism. But there is also a danger in making this the whole story. The same language has been used by indigenous people in Guatemala, by dissidents in the Soviet Union, by women in Iran, by the mothers of the disappeared in Argentina. To dismiss it as an imperialist discourse is to erase those struggles—and to suggest, implicitly, that the victims of imperialism should have known better than to use the master’s tools.

Johnson might reply that he is not dismissing human rights entirely; he is proposing to ground them differently, in the history of slavery rather than in the experience of twentieth-century Europe. But this distinction is less sharp than he imagines. The experience of slavery is, among other things, an experience of radical rights-violation. Enslaved people were denied the right to control their own bodies, to keep their own children, to speak their own languages, to worship their own gods. To say that they were denied these rights is not to impose a liberal framework on their experience; it is to describe, in the plainest possible language, what slavery did. And when enslaved people resisted—when they ran away, when they broke tools, when they poisoned masters, when they sang spirituals that encoded dreams of freedom—they were, among other things, asserting rights. Not rights in the narrow legal sense, but rights in the deeper sense that my grandmother recognized: the right to be a person among persons, to have one’s dignity acknowledged, to not be used as a means to another’s end.

Johnson’s problem with rights language is not just that it is insufficient; it is that it is distorting. By framing enslaved people as rights-bearers manqués, he argues, historians project a liberal subjectivity onto people who lived in a world radically different from our own. This is a legitimate concern. We should not assume that enslaved people wanted the same things we want, or that they would have expressed their aspirations in the vocabulary of the U.S. Constitution. But Johnson overcorrects. The desire for freedom from domination, for bodily integrity, for family intactness, for meaningful control over one’s labor—these are not parochial products of the European Enlightenment. They are, as far as I can tell, human universals, or close to it. They show up in every culture, in every historical period, however differently they are inflected by local circumstances. To deny this, as Johnson comes close to doing, is to risk a different kind of distortion: a kind of radical particularism that leaves us without the moral resources to criticize practices we find abhorrent in cultures other than our own.

Let me put the point more sharply. If the rights tradition is as thoroughly compromised as Johnson suggests—if it is inextricably bound up with imperialism, with capitalism, with the “hitherto existing world order”—then what is the basis for his own critique? From what standpoint does he condemn the “resource-extracting, race-differentiating, world-wasting race to the end of time” that Du Bois described? If the answer is “the standpoint of the enslaved,” I am not sure that gets us very far. The standpoint of the enslaved is not a unified thing; enslaved people had different experiences, different strategies, different aspirations. Some of them collaborated with their masters; some of them became masters themselves when circumstances allowed. The “black radical tradition” is a tradition because later thinkers selected and synthesized elements of that experience, not because it speaks with one voice. And the selection and synthesis were themselves informed by values—equality, solidarity, liberation—that are not the exclusive property of any one tradition.

I do not want to be unfair. Johnson’s essay is a powerful reminder that the history of slavery has not been adequately integrated into our thinking about justice, and that the particular forms of wrong involved in slavery—the turning of human beings into capital, the reproductive exploitation of women, the destruction of family ties, the theft of land and labor—require particular forms of redress. The call for reparations that he endorses is not just a matter of cutting checks; it is a demand for a fundamental reckoning with the structure of the American political economy. On this, we agree completely.

But I worry that Johnson’s rejection of rights language will make that reckoning harder to achieve, not easier. The movement for reparations in the United States has gained traction in recent years not because activists abandoned the language of rights but because they deepened it. They argued that the harms of slavery and Jim Crow were not just private wrongs but violations of legally cognizable rights—rights to property, to equal protection, to due process—that the U.S. government had systematically denied. The groundbreaking work of scholars like Mary Frances Berry and Charles Ogletree has been to show that reparations claims can be framed in the language of American law, drawing on precedents from the restitution of Japanese American internees to the settlements with Native American tribes. This strategy may not satisfy the radical demand for a wholesale overthrow of racial capitalism, but it has the advantage of being actionable in courts and legislatures.

My grandmother died in 2011, before the Movement for Black Lives, before Ta-Nehisi Coates’s essay on reparations, before the current resurgence of white nationalism. I do not know what she would have made of these developments. But I know that she would have been pleased to see so many young people marching, organizing, demanding justice. She would have recognized in their demands something of her own: the simple, stubborn conviction that she was a human being, that her humanity was not up for debate, and that the world owed her more than it had given. Call that conviction what you will. To her, it was a right.
