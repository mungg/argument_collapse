"The federal tax code should be reformed to achieve marriage neutrality, ensuring that joint filers never face a higher tax burden than they would as two single individuals."

The "marriage penalty" is not merely a quirk of tax accounting—it is a structural flaw that penalizes economic partnership and penalizes it unevenly across income levels. When two workers marry and combine their incomes, they frequently discover that their combined tax liability exceeds what they would owe as two single filers. This isn't abstract policy theory; it means couples make real economic decisions—about work hours, career choices, and household arrangements—in response to tax incentives they never asked for. That the tax code simultaneously rewards single-earner couples while discouraging dual-earner married couples reveals a fundamental incoherence in how we treat marriage.

The most straightforward fix would be to equalize the standard deduction for married filers at twice the single amount, so no couple faces a penalty simply from joining returns. This "marriage bonus" approach was partly attempted in the 1980s and 1990s, but the brackets were never fully adjusted for inflation, and alternative minimum tax provisions have eroded the gains. We should complete the work of marriage neutrality: ensure that any two adults filing jointly face tax obligations that are no greater than the sum of what they would owe separately. This doesn't require eliminating marriage from the tax code—it requires making the code indifferent to marital status in terms of overall burden.

Critics will argue this is prohibitively expensive or administratively complex, but the current system already requires detailed calculations for every married couple. The real objection seems to be philosophical: that marriage should be encouraged through the tax code. But if we want to encourage marriage, we should do it directly—not by punishing those who choose otherwise. A neutral tax code respects individual autonomy while achieving the same social goals. It's time to complete the move toward marriage neutrality that policymakers began decades ago.
