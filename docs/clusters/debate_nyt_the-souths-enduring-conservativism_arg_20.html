"The South remains deeply conservative because modern migration patterns from other regions have reinforced, rather than diluted, the area's right-leaning political identity."

A common assumption about the emergence of the "New South" was that an influx of Northerners and Midwesterners would inevitably dilute the region’s conservative politics. Yet, the Sunbelt migration has not liberalized the South; in many ways, it has reinforced its conservative identity.

Who actually moves to the South? For decades, the migration patterns have been dominated by retirees seeking lower taxes and a traditional lifestyle, as well as corporate professionals relocating to business-friendly, right-to-work states. These new arrivals are often already conservative. A middle-class manager fleeing high property taxes in New Jersey or a retiree leaving Illinois for South Carolina is not bringing progressive politics with them; they are actively seeking refuge in a conservative policy environment.

Furthermore, this in-migration creates a self-selecting echo chamber. As the South markets itself as a bastion of low taxes and traditional values, it attracts Americans who prioritize those exact things. The white working class in the South is flanked by an increasingly large, relatively affluent population of conservative transplants. The region’s conservatism isn't just a relic of the past; it is being actively replenished by modern internal migration.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The individuals moving to the South, such as retirees and corporate professionals, are often already conservative and seeking lower taxes and traditional lifestyles.
  2. 02The South's reputation as a bastion of low taxes and traditional values creates a self-selecting migration process that attracts people who prioritize those specific ideals.
  3. 03The region's conservative identity is being actively replenished by an affluent population of transplants rather than simply being a relic of historical tradition.
