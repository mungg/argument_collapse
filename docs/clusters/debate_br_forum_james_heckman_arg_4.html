"While early childhood intervention is vital, it should not be framed as an alternative to income support because financial stability is a necessary precondition for the high-quality parenting and reduced household stress that foster child development."

James Heckman is right that childhood environments matter profoundly. He is right that inequality begins before school. He is right that social and emotional capacities are essential to learning and later success. But his essay draws too sharp a line between “predistribution” and redistribution, and in doing so it risks turning a powerful case for early childhood investment into an unnecessarily narrow argument against income support for poor families.

The sentence that should give readers pause is this: “The scarce resource is love and parenting—not money.” This is a memorable phrase, but it is misleading. Love and parenting are indeed scarce in some homes, and no serious egalitarian should pretend that a cash transfer automatically produces bedtime stories, patience, routines, or emotional security. But money is not incidental to love and parenting. Money buys time, reduces stress, stabilizes housing, keeps utilities on, pays for transportation, makes nutritious food easier to obtain, and gives parents room to breathe. Poverty is not simply a low score on a balance sheet. It is a daily assault on attention.

Anyone who has spent time with low-income parents knows that many are not deficient in love. They are overworked, underemployed, badly housed, medically insecure, and trapped in neighborhoods where ordinary tasks consume extraordinary energy. A mother who works an irregular shift, takes two buses, worries about eviction, and cannot afford reliable child care may speak fewer words to her toddler at night. That fact should not be redescribed too quickly as a deficit in parenting quality, as though the parent’s inner character were the main variable. Exhaustion has consequences. So does fear.

Heckman cites the natural experiment in which casino income improved children’s behavioral outcomes through improved parenting. But that example cuts both ways. He emphasizes that parenting mediated the effect. True. But the change in parenting was produced by money. Additional income did not mechanically raise test scores; it altered the conditions under which parents supervised and engaged their children. That is precisely the point redistribution’s defenders make. Income support can be developmental policy when it reduces stress and makes better parenting possible.

The same is true of welfare reform. Heckman invokes the 1996 reform as though it confirmed the limited value of cash assistance. But the record is more complicated. In the late 1990s, single mothers’ employment rose, poverty fell, and expansions of the Earned Income Tax Credit mattered enormously. Work supports—not simply the withdrawal of welfare—helped families. Since then, especially during the Great Recession, Temporary Assistance for Needy Families has often failed to respond to rising need. Many poor families receive neither stable work nor meaningful cash assistance. It is hard to see how children benefit from that.

The danger in Heckman’s framing is that policymakers may hear: don’t give money to poor parents; send experts to fix them. That would be a mistake both morally and practically. Early childhood programs work best when they respect parents and reduce burdens, not when they treat low-income family life as a pathology to be corrected. Home visiting can be empowering, but it can also become intrusive. Parenting education can be helpful, but it can also become a way of telling poor mothers—especially black and Latina mothers—that their culture is the problem while ignoring labor markets, housing segregation, mass incarceration, and underfunded schools.

A serious mobility agenda should combine Heckman’s insights with a broader anti-poverty strategy. Start before birth: prenatal care, nutrition assistance, smoking cessation, substance-abuse treatment, and nurse home visiting for young mothers. Continue after birth: paid family leave, child allowances or refundable tax credits, high-quality infant and toddler care, and health coverage. Add excellent preschool at ages three and four. Then sustain gains through good elementary schools, after-school programs, summer learning, and access to stable housing. None of this is “simple redistribution.” It is redistribution in service of development.

Indeed, the distinction between redistribution and predistribution can obscure more than it clarifies. If we fund high-quality preschool through progressive taxation, that is redistribution. If we provide a refundable tax credit that allows a parent to reduce hours and spend more time with a newborn, that is also predistribution. If housing vouchers enable a child to leave a dangerous environment, they alter early developmental conditions. If food stamps reduce hunger, they improve the child’s capacity to learn. Money and services are not rival philosophies. They are tools.

Heckman is right that some late remediation programs have weak returns. But the conclusion should not be that social policy must choose the earliest years over the rest of life. Poor children become poor adolescents in institutions that continue to shape them. A child who benefited from preschool can still be derailed by a violent neighborhood, an overwhelmed school, untreated asthma, or a parent’s job loss. Conversely, adolescents are not lost causes. Mentoring, high-quality career and technical education, community college pathways, and youth employment can matter, especially when they connect young people to adults who expect something of them.

We should also be cautious about the language of “non-cognitive skills.” Perseverance and self-control are real and important. But in a deeply unequal society, celebrating grit can easily become a way of asking the disadvantaged to adapt to injustice. Children need persistence, but they also need institutions worthy of their persistence. A young person who waits patiently for opportunity in a neighborhood with few jobs and aggressive policing may not be suffering primarily from a character deficit.

The best response to Heckman, then, is not rejection but enlargement. He has shown that early childhood is a crucial site of inequality, that socio-emotional development matters, and that well-designed interventions can produce lasting gains. Those are indispensable lessons. But early intervention should not be used as a polite technocratic substitute for fighting poverty. The quality of parenting matters; so do wages, housing, health care, work schedules, incarceration policy, and racial inequality. Children are raised in families, but families are embedded in political economy.

If birth is becoming fate, we need to change the conditions of birth. That means supporting parents with services and with income. Love may be the most precious resource in a child’s life, but public policy should not pretend that love floats free of rent, wages, illness, and time. A humane mobility agenda would give families both: the material security that makes attentive parenting possible and the early developmental supports that help children flourish.
