"A country should only boycott the Olympics when the action is specifically requested by the oppressed people it aims to support, is part of a coordinated international strategy, and is likely to reduce rather than increase the danger to those activists."

The first rule of an Olympic boycott should be humility: listen to the people whose freedom is supposedly being defended. Too often, powerful countries turn a boycott into a performance of their own virtue. The victims become scenery. A boycott is justified only when those facing repression broadly ask for that form of solidarity, when the action is coordinated, and when it is likely to reduce rather than increase their danger.

That standard matters in Russia. Pussy Riot has called for a boycott, and its members have earned the right to be heard. Many gay-rights advocates inside and outside Russia have also demanded stronger action against the Sochi Games. Others have argued that athletes should come, speak and make themselves visible, because abandonment would leave Russian activists more isolated. The division is not a reason to do nothing, but it is a reason to avoid pretending that one clean moral answer speaks for all Russians under pressure.

History suggests that boycotts work best when they are part of a larger movement led by those most affected. The isolation of apartheid South Africa had force because it was tied to sustained internal resistance and international demands that were specific and morally unmistakable. By contrast, superpower Olympic boycotts in 1980 and 1984 looked more like statecraft by symbolism. They hurt athletes and hardened blocs without doing much for the people invoked as beneficiaries.

So countries should distinguish between several kinds of “not going.” Heads of state should skip Sochi. Cabinet ministers and royal delegations should stay home. Government hospitality tents and corporate junkets should be treated as endorsements and avoided. But national teams should not automatically be withdrawn unless local movements clearly call for that sacrifice and a significant coalition is prepared to follow. Otherwise, the host gets to stage a smaller but more controllable spectacle, and foreign governments get to feel righteous at relatively low cost to themselves.

The most ethical response to Sochi is solidarity without substitution. Let officials stay away. Let athletes compete and, where they choose, speak. Let embassies open their doors to activists. Let democratic governments press for repeal of discriminatory laws and for the release of political prisoners. Let the IOC know that future hosts must be bound by human-rights rules.

A boycott should not be a reflex. It should be an answer to a request, a strategy with partners, and a burden shared by those who choose it—not imposed from afar in the name of people never consulted.

  4. 04Withdrawing national teams without a broad coalition often allows the host nation to stage a more controllable spectacle while providing foreign governments a low-cost way to feel righteous.
  5. 05The most ethical response involves maintaining a presence that allows athletes to speak out and embassies to provide direct support to local activists.
