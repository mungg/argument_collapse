The Promise of Experimentalist Environmental Governance

The lead essay by Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen offers a compelling vision of environmental regulation that transcends the false choice between rigid centralized command and naive market deregulation. Having followed developments in environmental policy over the past two decades, I find their analysis of the "rolling-rule regime" both intellectually rigorous and practically grounded. The examples they marshal—TRI, TURA, the Chesapeake Bay Program, and Habitat Conservation Plans—demonstrate that a middle path is not only possible but already flourishing in diverse settings.

What strikes me most forcefully is their diagnosis of why both traditional command regulation and market-based approaches have proven inadequate. Centralized command fails because regulators cannot possess the omniscience required to anticipate all consequences of fixed rules. The history of technology-based standards that become obsolete the moment they are promulgated illustrates this point precisely. Meanwhile, market-based approaches founder on the extraordinary information requirements that artificial permit markets demand. Before any trading scheme can function, regulators must determine aggregate emissions, assess environmental harm at various levels, and establish secure property rights—tasks that routinely exceed administrative capacity.

The rolling-rule regime addresses these deficiencies through a mechanism of continuous learning and adjustment. Local actors experiment with diverse approaches to shared problems, while centralized authorities aggregate the resulting information to establish revised minimum standards and aspirational targets. This is not voluntarism; it is structured experimentation with accountability. The Massachusetts Toxics Use Reduction Act exemplifies this architecture. By requiring firms to develop biannual reduction plans and report on progress, TURA creates incentives for self-discovery while building an informational infrastructure that allows the state to raise performance expectations over time. The documented 20 percent reduction in production-adjusted toxic use between 1990 and 1995 demonstrates that this approach delivers measurable environmental improvements.

The Chesapeake Bay Program provides even more compelling evidence. For decades, point-source pollution regulations under the Clean Water Act failed to address the diffuse agricultural and suburban runoff that threatened the Bay's ecological health. The multi-state, inter-agency collaboration that emerged in 1983 and evolved through subsequent agreements has achieved what centralized regulation could not: tributary-specific nutrient reduction strategies developed by teams combining scientific expertise with local knowledge. The monitoring regimes these teams employ generate information that continuously refines understanding of ecosystem dynamics, allowing management approaches to adapt in ways that fixed rules never could.

The authors are right to emphasize the transformative potential of "deep participation." Traditional regulatory participation—commenting on proposed rules, voting in elections, filing administrative complaints—allows citizens to register preferences but not to shape solutions. The new regime calls for something more demanding: joint responsibility for formulating and implementing environmental protection measures. This is precisely what tributary teams in the Chesapeake Bay Program have achieved. By integrating government officials, scientific experts, agricultural representatives, and citizen volunteers into deliberative bodies with genuine authority over implementation, these teams have created forms of civic engagement that transcend familiar categories of public and private.

Critics will object that this vision understates the power inequalities that pervade environmental negotiations. Developers, industrial firms, and well-resourced landowners possess resources and expertise that community activists cannot match. But the authors anticipate this objection. The rolling-rule regime's accountability mechanisms—public reporting requirements, inter-local comparison, oversight by national authorities—are designed precisely to discipline actors who might otherwise dominate local deliberations. TRI's public disclosure requirements have demonstrably disciplined polluters through reputational mechanisms. The Miller Bill's monitoring provisions would institutionalize this accountability at the ecosystem level.

Looking ahead, I see the framework's greatest promise in its adaptability. Environmental challenges will continue to evolve—new chemicals will require regulation, new ecological understandings will demand revised approaches, new technologies will create both problems and solutions. A regulatory architecture designed for continuous learning can respond to these changes in ways that fixed-rule systems cannot. The authors have identified a path that combines environmental effectiveness with democratic participation, and their analysis deserves serious consideration by policymakers at all levels of government.
