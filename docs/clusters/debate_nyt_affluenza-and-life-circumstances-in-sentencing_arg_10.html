"Life circumstances should have minimal impact on sentencing in cases involving severe violence or loss of life because the justice system must prioritize proportional retribution for victims over the offender's background."

When discussing the role of life circumstances in sentencing, we frequently lose sight of the most important people in the courtroom: the victims. The criminal justice system is the mechanism by which society steps in to provide retribution and closure on behalf of those who have been harmed. When the subjective life circumstances of the offender eclipse the objective harm done to the victims, the system fundamentally fails its primary constituency.

In the Texas case, four people are dead. A youth pastor, a mother and daughter, and another woman who had stopped to help a stranded motorist were violently wiped from existence. Two other passengers were severely injured, one left completely paralyzed. Their life circumstances—their futures, their families, their careers—were permanently destroyed.

To tell the grieving families of the deceased that the perpetrator will serve no jail time because his parents gave him too many freedoms is to inflict a profound secondary trauma. It tells the victims that their lost lives are worth less than the perpetrator’s theoretical psychological growth.

Sentencing must be proportional to the damage inflicted. While life circumstances, such as upbringing or financial status, might appropriately sway a sentence for a victimless crime or a property offense, they should hold near-zero weight in cases involving severe violence or loss of life. True justice is restorative, but it also requires a basic acknowledgment of the weight of human life. Allowing "affluenza" to negate a prison sentence tips the scales of justice entirely toward the offender, leaving the victims and their families with nothing but the devastating realization that the law does not value their loss.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The primary purpose of the criminal justice system is to provide retribution and closure for victims rather than focusing on the subjective experiences of the offender.
  2. 02Prioritizing an offender's privileged upbringing over the harm they caused inflicts secondary trauma on the victims' families by devaluing the lives lost.
  3. 03While life circumstances might be relevant for victimless or property crimes, they cannot negate the need for prison time when the objective harm is the death or permanent injury of others.
  4. 04Allowing 'affluenza' to prevent a jail sentence creates an unjust imbalance where the law values the perpetrator's psychological growth more than the victims' lives.
