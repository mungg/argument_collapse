"Daron Acemoglu’s analysis of AI is overly narrow because it focuses on the economic concerns of the Global North while ignoring how AI automation threatens to permanently block the path to economic development for the Global South."

Daron Acemoglu’s essay "AI’s Future Doesn’t Have to Be Dystopian" offers a compelling diagnosis of the threats posed by artificial intelligence to labor markets and democratic institutions. However, his analysis suffers from a severe blind spot: it is almost exclusively focused on the developed economies of the Global North. By framing the AI challenge around the loss of "good jobs" in the American middle class and the need to recapture the post-war *Trente Glorieuses*, Acemoglu ignores the far more devastating impact AI automation is poised to have on the developing world. If AI continues its current trajectory, the true dystopia will not just be American wage stagnation; it will be the permanent foreclosure of economic development for billions of people in the Global South.

For the past half-century, the established blueprint for economic development has been export-led industrialization. Countries like South Korea, Taiwan, and more recently China, lifted hundreds of millions of people out of poverty by moving rural populations into low-skilled manufacturing, leveraging their comparative advantage of cheap labor to produce goods for Western markets. This process accumulated capital, facilitated technology transfer, and built the infrastructure necessary to eventually transition to high-income service and knowledge economies.

Artificial intelligence and advanced robotics are systematically destroying this ladder of development. As machine learning algorithms perfect computer vision and fine motor control, the cost of automated manufacturing and logistics in the West is plummeting. Why would an American multinational offshore its supply chain to a factory in Southeast Asia or Sub-Saharan Africa, dealing with shipping costs, tariffs, and geopolitical risk, when it can operate a highly automated, AI-driven facility in Ohio or Germany for a similar unit cost?

This phenomenon, which economist Dani Rodrik has termed "premature deindustrialization," is already visible. Developing nations are seeing their manufacturing sectors shrink at much lower levels of per capita income than the Western nations did. Acemoglu complains that AI is replacing simple tasks performed by low-wage workers in the US. But on a global scale, those "simple tasks" are the sole entry point into the global economy for the poorest nations on Earth. If AI automates textile manufacturing, electronics assembly, and basic call-center services, it effectively pulls the ladder up behind the rich world.

Acemoglu’s proposed solutions—changing US tax codes to favor labor, funding research that creates "new tasks," and cultivating better norms among AI researchers in Silicon Valley—do absolutely nothing for workers in Bangladesh or Kenya. Even if US government funding succeeds in redirecting AI to create "new adaptive teaching tasks" or "integrated design tasks" for American workers, these are highly skilled, localized services. They cannot be exported to or performed by the massive, undereducated youth populations of the developing world.

Furthermore, the democratic backsliding Acemoglu attributes to AI surveillance is magnified tenfold in developing nations with weak institutional safeguards. While Western democracies debate facial recognition regulations, authoritarian regimes are eagerly importing cheap, AI-powered surveillance architectures from China to crush dissent, free from the "democratic oversight" Acemoglu hopes will save us.

To prevent a global AI dystopia, the conversation must move beyond American tax policy and domestic labor shares. We need a framework for global technology governance. This means rethinking intellectual property regimes to allow developing nations cheaper access to foundational AI technologies. It means international development organizations, like the World Bank, must pivot entirely away from funding traditional industrial infrastructure and instead invest massively in digital literacy and broadband access for the Global South. And it means the developed world must prepare for the massive geopolitical instability and climate-driven migration that will inevitably occur if AI renders the labor of half the globe economically redundant. The future of AI is a global crisis, and it cannot be solved with mid-century American nostalgia.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01AI and advanced robotics are destroying the traditional 'ladder of development' by making automated manufacturing in wealthy nations cheaper than offshoring to low-wage developing countries.
  2. 02The phenomenon of 'premature deindustrialization' means that developing nations are losing their manufacturing sectors at much lower income levels than Western nations did historically.
  3. 03Acemoglu’s proposed solutions, such as US tax reform and the creation of high-skilled 'new tasks,' are localized to the American workforce and offer no benefit to the undereducated populations of the developing world.
  4. 04The threat of AI-powered surveillance is far more acute in developing nations where authoritarian regimes can import cheap technology to crush dissent without the democratic safeguards found in the West.
  5. 05Preventing a global AI dystopia requires international technology governance, including reformed intellectual property regimes and massive investment in digital infrastructure for the Global South.
