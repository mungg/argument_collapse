"Establishing a time-based, automatic transition to legal status for long-settled irregular migrants is a necessary repair to the rule of law that replaces arbitrary enforcement with justice and clarity."

The phrase “the rule of law” appears constantly in immigration debates, usually as a slogan against amnesty. Joseph Carens’s essay invites us to ask what the rule of law actually requires. Does it require the government to deport every unauthorized migrant it can find, no matter how long that person has lived here? Or does it require laws that are general, prospective, humane, and consistent with the social facts they govern? On that question, Carens’s position may be more faithful to the rule of law than the punitive alternative.

A legal system loses legitimacy when it maintains rules that it cannot or will not enforce and then applies them selectively. That is the situation in which the United States now finds itself. Roughly eleven million people live without authorization. They are not hidden in any serious sense. They work, rent apartments, attend churches, send children to school, drive, pay sales taxes, and often pay payroll taxes. The government lacks the capacity, and probably the democratic will, to remove them all. Instead, enforcement is episodic: a workplace raid here, a traffic stop there, a detention tragedy elsewhere. Such a system breeds fear without producing order.

Carens’s proposal for an automatic transition to legal status after a fixed period would replace arbitrary vulnerability with a rule. That is a rule-of-law improvement. People would know the consequences of residence. Officials would have less discretion to decide whose family is sympathetic enough. Courts and agencies could administer clear criteria. Employers would lose some power to use immigration status as a threat. Communities would know who is legally present.

Critics say that amnesty undermines the law because it forgives violations. But law includes mechanisms of forgiveness, repose, waiver, and adjustment. Bankruptcy discharges debts. Prosecutors decline charges. Statutes of limitation bar stale claims. Zoning boards grant variances. Tax authorities settle liabilities. These practices do not abolish law; they recognize that rigid enforcement can become unjust or counterproductive. Immigration law should be no different.

The analogy to statutes of limitation is especially apt. We do not think the state should be able to prosecute every offense forever. Time weakens evidence, changes expectations, and makes punishment less useful. If that is true for many crimes, it is surely true for civil immigration violations. A person who crossed a border nine years ago and has since worked, paid taxes, married, and raised children is not the same policy problem as a person who crossed yesterday. Treating them identically is not principled. It is crude.

Still, a rule-of-law amnesty must be designed carefully. It should not be a vague political pardon announced under pressure. It should be enacted by Congress, administered transparently, and tied to objective criteria. Applicants should prove residence through a broad range of documents, including school records, medical records, leases, church affidavits, employment records, tax filings, and community attestations. Because unauthorized people often live informally, evidentiary rules must not be so strict that only the well-documented can qualify. Fraud prevention is necessary, but suspicion should not swallow justice.

The program should include background checks and exclude those convicted of serious crimes. But lawmakers must avoid defining “criminal” so broadly that minor offenses become traps. Unauthorized migrants often drive without licenses because licenses are unavailable. They may use false numbers to work because work is necessary to live. These acts should not be treated the same as violence, trafficking, or predatory fraud. The law should distinguish between conduct that harms others and conduct that reflects survival under unlawful status.

Carens’s five-to-seven-year suggestion is reasonable. I would favor seven years for ordinary adult entrants, shorter periods for those brought as children, spouses of citizens or lawful permanent residents, and parents of U.S.-citizen children. Seven years is long enough to show settlement and discourage claims that brief unlawful presence creates rights. But the exact number matters less than the principle: after a defined period, the government’s claim to deport should expire.

What about future migration? Here, rule-of-law concerns support reform on both sides. Amnesty alone would be insufficient. The United States needs legal channels that reflect labor demand, especially for lesser-skilled work. It also needs credible workplace enforcement aimed primarily at employers. If Congress legalizes the current population but leaves future workers with no lawful path and employers with continued demand, it will recreate the same unauthorized population. A law that ignores economic reality invites evasion.

At the same time, enforcement must remain bounded by rights. The rule of law is not maximized by raids that deny due process, detention conditions that endanger lives, or local policing that encourages racial profiling. Immigration enforcement must be subject to constitutional norms, judicial review, and humane detention standards. A country cannot defend legality through lawless methods.

Carens’s argument also exposes a contradiction in much anti-amnesty rhetoric. Opponents insist that unauthorized migrants are “illegal” and therefore outside the community, yet they often oppose giving them any way to become legal. If the problem is illegality, regularization is part of the solution. If the real objection is the migrants’ presence regardless of legal status, then “rule of law” is being used as a mask for exclusion.

The rule of law requires that legal categories correspond, at least roughly, to lived reality. When millions of long-settled residents are officially removable but practically integrated, the law becomes a generator of insecurity rather than a guide for conduct. Carens’s amnesty would not reward contempt for law. It would acknowledge that the government, employers, communities, and migrants have together produced a durable social fact. Law should respond to that fact with clarity.

A liberal democracy should enforce its borders, but it should not keep settled people in permanent legal suspense. After enough time has passed, deportation ceases to be ordinary enforcement and becomes banishment. A time-based path to legal status would bring immigration law closer to justice, administrability, and truth. That is not the abandonment of the rule of law. It is one of its necessary repairs.

"While long-settled unauthorized migrants have a moral claim to stay, any amnesty policy must be part of a comprehensive, durable settlement that addresses the systemic causes of illegal immigration to ensure future order and public legitimacy."

The American debate over amnesty is haunted by 1986. Any serious response to Joseph Carens must begin there. The Immigration Reform and Control Act legalized nearly three million unauthorized immigrants while promising employer sanctions and better control in the future. Many beneficiaries became stable residents and citizens; in human terms, that part of the law was a success. Yet the unauthorized population later grew to an estimated eleven million. For opponents of amnesty, this proves that legalization fails. For advocates, it proves that legalization must be paired with better policy. The advocates are closer to the truth, but they should not dismiss the lesson.

Carens argues that long-settled unauthorized migrants acquire a moral claim to stay. I agree. But a political community also has a legitimate interest in not repeating cycles of illegality and emergency legalization. If amnesty is to be enacted again, it must be part of a durable settlement, not merely an act of compassion.

The first component is legalization for those already rooted. Deporting millions is neither feasible nor morally acceptable. Many have been here for years, have U.S.-citizen children, own homes, work steadily, and lack serious criminal records. Keeping them unauthorized does not enforce the law; it perpetuates disorder. A legalization program should require registration, background checks, payment of reasonable fines and taxes, and a probationary period. It should lead eventually to permanent residence and citizenship. A society should not create a permanent caste of workers who may live here but never belong.

The second component is credible workplace enforcement. Unauthorized immigration is driven less by abstract desire to violate law than by jobs. If employers can hire unauthorized workers with little risk, and if workers have no legal way to meet that demand, the system will reproduce itself. Employer sanctions after 1986 were weak and often symbolic. Document fraud flourished. Some employers looked the other way; others used subcontracting to avoid responsibility. A new settlement must include a reliable employment verification system with strong anti-discrimination protections and meaningful penalties for employers who knowingly violate the law.

This is difficult. Verification systems can generate errors that harm lawful workers. They can encourage discrimination against people who look or sound foreign. They can become national ID systems by another name. These risks are real. But refusing to address employment is not an option. The key is to build due process: quick correction of database errors, penalties for discriminatory use, privacy safeguards, and enforcement focused on employers rather than workers.

The third component is legal channels for future workers. This is where many enforcement-only approaches fail. The American economy has long demanded workers in sectors that do not require advanced degrees: agriculture, caregiving, construction, food processing, hospitality, and services. If the law provides almost no realistic route for such workers, illegal channels will fill the gap. Future visas should be flexible, portable between employers, and enforceable. Workers should not be tied to a single boss, because that invites abuse. Those who remain for years should have a path to permanent residence, reflecting Carens’s membership principle.

The fourth component is border enforcement that is firm but humane. The United States has increased border enforcement substantially since the 1990s, pushing crossings into more dangerous terrain and contributing to migrant deaths. A civilized state may control its border, but it may not treat death in the desert as an acceptable deterrent. Legal channels and workplace enforcement would reduce pressure on the border more effectively than walls alone. Smuggling thrives when lawful entry is impossible.

The fifth component is integration. Legalization without English classes, civics education, adult schooling, and support for local communities is short-sighted. States and municipalities need resources to incorporate new legal residents. Integration is not automatic; it is a public investment.

Carens’s proposal for automatic regularization after five to seven years is morally appealing, but as a standing rule it could become politically unstable unless paired with the rest of the system. A permanent statute of limitations on deportation makes sense if the state has realistic tools to prevent and identify unauthorized residence earlier. Without such tools, voters may see the rule as surrender. That perception matters because immigration policy requires public legitimacy. If citizens believe the government is simply legalizing whoever manages to remain long enough, backlash will follow and may produce harsher laws.

A compromise could combine an initial broad legalization with an ongoing but longer limitation rule—perhaps after seven or ten years—for those who fall through the cracks in the future. Children brought here, spouses of citizens, and parents of citizen children should have shorter routes. This would preserve Carens’s insight while giving the state time to enforce newly reformed rules.

The recession makes timing hard. Many Americans are losing jobs and may view legalization as competition. But postponement has costs. Unauthorized workers are already in the labor market. Legalization would make them less exploitable and more taxable. It would not magically create job competition that does not already exist. Still, policymakers should pair immigration reform with labor protections and economic recovery measures, so that citizens do not experience amnesty as indifference to their hardship.

Carens is right that time creates moral claims. But policy must also create future order. The worst outcome would be to reject legalization in the name of law while preserving a lawless labor market. The second-worst outcome would be to legalize without changing the structures that produce illegality. The right path is comprehensive: amnesty, enforcement, legal channels, labor rights, and integration.

The country should stop pretending that eleven million people can be wished away. It should also stop pretending that compassion alone is an immigration system. A durable settlement must honor both membership and law. Carens supplies the first principle. The challenge for Congress is to build the second around it.

"Liberal democracies must grant legal status to long-term irregular migrants because social membership is earned through the reality of lived experience and community integration rather than mere official permission."

Joseph Carens’s most important move is to shift the discussion from border control to membership. That shift is both morally clarifying and politically useful. In the United States, debates over unauthorized immigration usually begin with the first act of unlawful entry and then freeze the migrant forever in that moment. Carens asks us to notice what happens next: work, marriage, children, church, mortgage payments, taxes, Little League, English classes, aging parents, neighbors who rely on one another. A society that refuses to see those facts is not defending the rule of law; it is defending a legal fiction against human reality.

The strongest part of Carens’s argument is his insistence that time changes the moral situation. This is not sentimentalism. Law itself recognizes that time matters. Statutes of limitation, adverse possession, residency requirements for voting and tuition, naturalization rules, and common-law doctrines of reliance all reflect the same insight: human beings organize their lives around settled expectations, and at some point the disruption of those expectations becomes too costly to justify. If a state allows a person to live, work, and raise children in its territory for many years, the state cannot plausibly pretend that the person is a stranger.

This point is especially powerful in the United States because unauthorized migration has not occurred in a vacuum. The country has built a labor market that depends heavily on immigrants who lack formal authorization. Agriculture, meatpacking, construction, restaurants, domestic work, hotels, landscaping, and child care have all absorbed unauthorized workers. Employers have often benefited from the workers’ vulnerability. Consumers have benefited from lower prices. Local communities have benefited from taxes, rents, purchases, and labor. Politicians have denounced “illegal immigrants” while tolerating the economic arrangements that draw them here.

That complicity does not erase the fact that immigration laws were violated. But it does make mass deportation morally grotesque. If the country has, in practice, incorporated millions of people into its economy and neighborhoods, then it cannot suddenly describe them as invaders when convenient. A decent society must regularize the people it has already made use of.

Carens proposes an automatic transition to legal status after a fixed period, perhaps five to seven years. I would support such a policy, though I would design it with safeguards that make it politically durable. Applicants should have to show continuous residence, pay assessed taxes or enter a payment plan, pass criminal background checks, and register openly. They should receive probationary legal status first, followed by permanent residence after several years. Those who entered as children, spouses of citizens and lawful residents, and parents of U.S.-citizen children should receive especially generous treatment. The aim should not be punishment for punishment’s sake, but public recognition that regularization is a serious civic act.

The main objection is that amnesty rewards lawbreaking. But that objection assumes a simple moral world in which lawful migration channels are available and unauthorized migrants choose to cheat. For many low-wage workers, there is no realistic line to stand in. The United States admits many family members, some high-skilled workers, refugees, and seasonal laborers. It does not provide anything close to a lawful pathway matching the labor demand that clearly exists. The result is predictable: people move anyway, employers hire anyway, and the law produces illegality rather than order.

Another objection is deterrence. Won’t legalization invite more unauthorized immigration? Possibly, if legalization stands alone. The lesson of the 1986 Immigration Reform and Control Act is not that legalization was wrong, but that legalization without a workable future system is incomplete. IRCA legalized millions, but employer sanctions were weak, document fraud became common, and legal channels for needed workers remained inadequate. A new legalization program should therefore be paired with real labor standards enforcement, reliable workplace verification that does not become a tool for discrimination, and expanded legal avenues for workers whose labor the economy actually uses.

But deterrence cannot do all the moral work its defenders ask of it. To deport Miguel Sanchez after nine years, a citizen spouse, and a U.S.-citizen child would be to sacrifice an actual family to a speculative message. The message might not even be heard by future migrants deciding whether they can feed their families. Meanwhile, the harm to Miguel’s family would be immediate and severe. Liberal democracies should not treat settled families as billboards for border policy.

Carens is right to resist excessive discretion. A system based on proving “exceptional hardship” or persuading an immigration judge that one is sufficiently sympathetic invites arbitrariness. It also favors those with lawyers, English skills, and tidy biographies. A residency-based rule is imperfect, but imperfection is not an argument against rules. Five years may be somewhat arbitrary; so are voting ages and filing deadlines. The relevant question is whether the line reasonably tracks the underlying moral reality. Five to seven years of peaceful residence does.

The deeper question is what kind of country the United States wants to be. We can continue to sustain an underclass that is present but deportable, employed but exploitable, taxed but voiceless, rooted but insecure. Or we can acknowledge that membership grows from life lived together. Amnesty, properly understood, is not forgetting the law. It is law catching up with society.

Carens’s essay is a reminder that immigration policy is not only about who may enter tomorrow. It is also about what we owe to those who are already here, whose lives are interwoven with ours. If liberal democracy means anything, it must mean that human beings cannot be held indefinitely in civic limbo after they have become part of the community. On that central point, the case for amnesty is not merely compassionate. It is just.

"While amnesty for long-settled irregular migrants is a moral necessity, it must be structured as a national integration compact that addresses the fiscal and social burdens placed on local receiving communities."

Joseph Carens’s essay is persuasive when he writes about children, spouses, and the cruelty of uprooting people after many years. Yet his proposal would be more convincing if it paid greater attention to the communities that receive immigrants, especially poor communities. Too often the national immigration debate is conducted by people who experience immigration primarily as a moral abstraction or as a source of inexpensive services. The pressures of rapid migration are felt most directly in public schools, emergency rooms, rental markets, day-labor corners, county jails, and low-wage workplaces.

This does not refute the case for amnesty. It complicates it. A policy that legalizes settled migrants while ignoring the burdens on local communities will be morally incomplete and politically fragile.

Carens asks us to see Miguel Sanchez as a neighbor. That is exactly right. But neighbors live in actual municipalities with budgets. When a large unauthorized population settles in a city or county, local institutions must respond regardless of federal immigration policy. Children attend school, as they should. Hospitals provide emergency care, as they must. Police departments serve neighborhoods where victims and witnesses may fear contact with authorities. Libraries, clinics, and social-service agencies become de facto integration institutions. Yet local governments cannot print money and often have little say over federal border or visa policy.

In 2009 this problem is sharpened by the fiscal crisis. States and cities are cutting services. Schools are laying off teachers. Hospitals are strained. Legalization would help in some ways: legalized workers would have better jobs, pay more taxes, and participate more openly in civic life. But the transition would also require investment—English classes, legal assistance, document processing, adult education, and public health outreach. If Congress creates a path to status without funding integration, it will leave localities to absorb the costs and then wonder why resentment grows.

Carens is right that long-settled unauthorized migrants are members in a sociological and moral sense. But membership is reciprocal. It involves obligations by newcomers, receiving communities, employers, and governments. Amnesty should therefore be structured as a national integration compact. Applicants should regularize tax records, learn English or enroll in classes where feasible, register with the government, and avoid serious criminal conduct. In return, the federal government should provide legal status, protect workers from exploitation, and support the local institutions that make integration real.

The United States has done this poorly. We oscillate between neglect and panic. For years we allow unauthorized labor to become embedded in local economies. Then raids occur—at workplaces, homes, or traffic stops—creating fear but not a coherent system. The 2008 Postville raid in Iowa showed the human and civic costs of enforcement theatricality. Families were shattered, workers were processed through criminal pleas at remarkable speed, and the town itself was left reeling. Such actions may satisfy a demand for toughness, but they do not build lawfulness.

At the same time, advocates of legalization should not dismiss all local anxiety as xenophobia. Some of it is xenophobia, and it should be named. But some of it arises from real institutional stress. A school district that must educate many English-language learners needs resources. A public hospital that treats uninsured patients needs reimbursement. A neighborhood facing overcrowded housing needs code enforcement that does not become a deportation dragnet. A police chief needs immigrant residents to report crime without fear. These are practical conditions of membership.

Carens’s preference for an automatic time-based rule is attractive because it limits arbitrary bureaucracy. But time alone may not be enough as a public standard. A five-year rule should be paired with evidence of residence, basic civic registration, and a clean serious-criminal record. The process should be simple enough not to exclude the poor, but visible enough to reassure the public that legalization is not a bureaucratic shrug. People need to see that the transition from unauthorized to authorized status involves both mercy and order.

The children of unauthorized migrants deserve special urgency. Young people brought here as children did not choose their status. Many have attended American schools, speak English better than the language of their birth country, and imagine their futures here. The DREAM Act, repeatedly proposed but not enacted, is the clearest example of a policy that recognizes membership formed through education and upbringing. Any broader amnesty should include immediate protection for such young people. To deny them college, work, and legal identity is not enforcement; it is waste.

But adult legalization also serves children. Many U.S.-citizen children live with one or more unauthorized parents. Deporting those parents effectively punishes citizens for their parents’ status. Even when deportation does not occur, fear shapes childhood. Children learn that a traffic stop can destroy a family. They absorb the stress of secrecy. A liberal democracy should not casually create that kind of insecurity for its own citizens.

Still, the receiving community’s children matter too. If classrooms are overcrowded, if teachers lack language support, if poor citizens experience immigration as another strain on already neglected institutions, solidarity will erode. The answer is not to deport Miguel Sanchez. The answer is to make the federal government responsible for the consequences of the immigration system it has allowed to develop.

I support Carens’s case for amnesty, but I would reframe it less as an individual moral entitlement and more as a civic settlement. Legalization should say: these people are here, they are part of us, and we will stop pretending otherwise. But it should also say: integration is a public project requiring resources, responsibilities, and attention to the least powerful citizens as well as to immigrants.

A humane immigration policy cannot be built solely at the border. It must be built in classrooms, workplaces, courthouses, clinics, and neighborhoods. Amnesty is necessary because deporting settled members is unjust. But amnesty will succeed only if it strengthens the communities in which that membership is lived.

The rule of law is not just one consideration to be weighed against the human costs of removal. It is the constitutive structure of liberal democratic governance. A polity that does not enforce its own laws, or that enforces them in such a way as to systematically reward those who evade them, is not just enforcing badly — it is undermining the basis of its own legitimacy. The reason citizens accept the demands of law on themselves is that they expect the law to apply, in some reasonably consistent way, to others. When this expectation breaks down, the entire structure of legal authority weakens.

The eleven million irregular migrants in the United States present a problem for the rule of law of a kind that we have not adequately confronted. The problem is not that the law has been broken; laws are broken all the time. The problem is that the law has been broken on such a scale, and with such effective tolerance by the state, that the state's nominal commitment to the law has become a kind of legal fiction. We have an immigration regime in which the rules are clear, the violations are massive, and the enforcement is selective in ways that nobody can really defend. This is corrosive of legal authority in a much deeper way than ordinary lawbreaking.

Carens proposes to resolve the situation by adjusting the law to fit the facts. Since enforcement of the existing rules against the existing population is impossible, and since the human costs of attempting enforcement are unacceptable, the rules should be changed to legalize the population. There is something to this. But it is also a particular response to a rule-of-law crisis, and it has its own rule-of-law costs.

When a polity responds to mass violation of its laws by legalizing the violators, it sends a particular signal: this kind of law, this kind of rule, is not really binding. The signal may be partial and qualified — "we are legalizing because of the human cost of removal, not because we approve of the violation" — but the practical effect is that violation pays. The next generation of would-be migrants, and the next generation of would-be employers, and the next generation of smuggling networks, all observe what has happened and adjust their expectations. It says: do not enter without authorization; if you do enter without authorization, do not stay; but if you stay long enough, you will receive authorization. Carens proposes something more: that long evasion not only bars removal but actively confers permanent residence and a path to citizenship. This is not a statute of limitations. It is a parallel naturalization scheme.

A one-time legalization, framed explicitly as a response to a particular historical situation, is less corrosive than a prospective rule. He focuses on what we owe the migrants, which is real and substantial, but he says little about what we owe ourselves as a self-governing polity that needs its laws to mean something. A defense of amnesty that does not engage this dimension is not really a defense of amnesty within liberal democracy; it is a defense of amnesty against the priorities that make liberal democracy possible.

The rule of law is not a fetish or a slogan. It is the basis on which we ask citizens to accept policies they did not vote for, to pay taxes they would rather not pay, to accept restrictions they would rather not accept. When we erode it, we erode the basis of collective action itself. Amnesty is sometimes necessary; the rule of law sometimes has to yield to humanitarian urgency. But we should be more reluctant about the yielding than Carens is, and more attentive to what is being lost.

But this ignores the millions of people around the world who are waiting patiently in the lines that do exist. Currently, the family reunification backlog is immense. A legal resident from Mexico or the Philippines seeking to bring a sibling to the United States might wait well over a decade. To reward those who chose to bypass the system, while ignoring the sacrifice of those who respected it, makes a mockery of the law. It signals to prospective migrants that adhering to the legal process is a fool’s errand.

This brings us to the most dangerous aspect of Carens’s proposal: the institutionalization of an automatic transition to legal status after five to seven years. Carens doubts this would encourage further unauthorized migration, suggesting that migrants only consider short-term economic gains. Recent history suggests otherwise. The 1986 Immigration Reform and Control Act granted a one-time amnesty to roughly three million unauthorized immigrants, with the promise that border enforcement would prevent future waves. Because the enforcement mechanisms proved inadequate, the unauthorized population swelled to the estimated eleven million we have today.

If a one-time amnesty helped spur decades of subsequent unauthorized migration, an automatic, permanent statute of limitations would be disastrous. It would officially codify a game of hide-and-seek. A rule stating that anyone who manages to stay undetected for five years receives legal permanent residency is not an immigration policy; it is an open invitation. Migrant networks are highly sophisticated and well-informed. The knowledge that survival in the shadows for sixty months guarantees a green card would vastly increase the number of people willing to risk deadly desert crossings or rely on exploitative human smugglers.

Carens rejects case-by-case discretionary relief because he fears discrimination and inefficiency. Yet, some level of discretion and systemic control is exactly what is needed. The comprehensive immigration reform efforts that recently stalled in Congress—such as the 2007 McCain-Kennedy bill—struggled precisely because of the delicate balance required between securing borders, enforcing workplace laws, and providing a tough, conditional path to legal status (an "earned legalization" requiring fines, back taxes, and a place at the back of the line). Carens’s proposal strips away these necessary balances, replacing the difficult work of comprehensive reform with a blanket, time-based entitlement.

Ultimately, liberal democracies must balance compassion for the individual with the integrity of the system as a whole. It is entirely reasonable to conclude that a blanket policy of mass deportation is both morally unacceptable and practically impossible. Many of the eleven million unauthorized immigrants currently in the United States will, and should, eventually find a way to come out of the shadows. But creating a permanent structural loophole that automatically rewards unauthorized settlement after a few short years would destroy the very foundation of legal immigration. We need a functioning, enforceable immigration system, not a stopwatch for those who manage to circumvent it.

Diversified Gemini

"Joseph Carens’s proposal for a time-based amnesty is a disastrous policy that ignores historical evidence and would incentivize a massive surge in future illegal immigration."

Joseph Carens's essay "The Case for Amnesty" is a textbook example of how noble intentions can lead to disastrous public policy. By focusing entirely on the moral claims of those currently residing illegally in the country, Carens ignores the glaring historical evidence that amnesties, regardless of how they are justified, serve as massive magnets for future illegal immigration. We do not need to rely on abstract theory to understand the consequences of his proposal; we only need to look at our own recent history.

In 1986, the United States passed the Immigration Reform and Control Act (IRCA). Like Carens’s proposal, IRCA was driven by a sense of moral and practical necessity. It provided legal status to nearly three million undocumented immigrants who could prove continuous residence. The promise was that this would be a one-time fix, coupled with strict employer sanctions to prevent future illegal flows. We know how that turned out. The employer sanctions were gutted, document fraud proliferated, and the amnesty sent a clear signal across the globe: if you can get into the United States and hide long enough, eventually, you will be rewarded with legal status.

Today, we have an estimated 11 million illegal immigrants. If we implement Carens’s proposal of an automatic transition to legal status after five to seven years, why should we expect anything other than a massive surge in illegal border crossings and visa overstays? Carens attempts to brush off this "moral hazard" argument by asserting that migrants base their decisions on short-term economic factors, not long-term policy shifts. This is incredibly naive. Migration networks are highly sophisticated. Information about changing policies in destination countries spreads rapidly. If the U.S. codifies a rule that five years of undetected presence guarantees a green card, human smugglers will use that policy as their primary marketing tool.

Carens dismisses the comparison between immigration laws and criminal laws, likening illegal entry to a traffic ticket. But this trivializes the profound impact that millions of undocumented residents have on a nation. The influx of illegal labor depresses wages for the most vulnerable low-skilled American workers. It strains local resources, from public schools mandated to teach children who speak no English, to emergency rooms flooded with uninsured patients. A sovereign nation has an absolute obligation to prioritize the welfare of its own citizens, particularly its poorest, over the desires of foreign nationals who bypass legal systems.

Furthermore, Carens’s "passage of time" argument creates a perverse incentive for migrants to live entirely off the grid, avoiding any interaction with authorities that might lead to deportation before the magical five-year mark is reached. This breeds an underground economy and isolates immigrant communities, precisely the opposite of the social cohesion Carens claims to value.

Before we can even entertain the conversation about what to do with the undocumented population currently here, we must unequivocally secure the border and implement a foolproof, mandatory E-Verify system to eliminate the jobs magnet. Until the spigot is turned off, any form of amnesty—whether called a pathway to citizenship, regularization, or earned legal status—will simply reset the clock for the next crisis. We cannot let sympathy for the individuals currently here blind us to the systemic chaos an amnesty will inevitably invite.
