Diversified Claude

"While large-scale amnesties are morally and practically necessary to address existing irregular populations, states should reject a prospective, automatic rule for legalization based on the passage of time."

I want to write in defense of a position that Carens' essay glides past too quickly: that there is something importantly different between legalization following from a clear, prospectively announced rule, and legalization that takes the form of an "amnesty." Carens uses the term "amnesty" throughout, but his actual proposal is for a rule — automatic legalization after five to seven years of settled residence. These are quite different things, and the difference matters.

An amnesty, properly speaking, is a one-time act of mercy or political compromise. It says: we are not going to enforce against this group of people, this time, because of some combination of practical necessity, political expediency, and human cost. It does not announce a rule for the future. The Reagan amnesty of 1986 was an amnesty in this sense. The Spanish regularizations of 1991, 1996, 2000, 2001, and 2005 were a series of such amnesties — each one a fresh political decision in response to accumulated facts on the ground. The same has been true of the Italian sanatorie.

What Carens is actually proposing is something quite different: a prospective rule, written into the law in advance, that anyone who settles irregularly and remains for five to seven years acquires a legal right to status. This is not an amnesty. It is a parallel admissions system, operating alongside the regular admissions system, with the only condition being successful evasion of enforcement for the specified period.

I want to defend amnesty in the traditional sense and oppose Carens' prospective rule. They are different policies with different implications.

The case for occasional amnesties is strong. As facts on the ground accumulate, the political community confronts a humanitarian situation it cannot enforce its way out of. The choice is between mass deportation, with its tearing of communities and families, and legalization. In most cases the right choice is legalization. The 1986 amnesty was, on balance, the right policy. A similar amnesty today, addressing the eleven million, would also be the right policy. The arguments Carens makes about social membership, family ties, and the disproportion between the violation and the punishment are entirely compelling as arguments for this kind of action.

But the case for a prospective rule is different. A prospective rule changes the calculus of every potential migrant in the future. It is no longer a matter of guessing whether a future amnesty might happen; it is a guarantee that legalization will follow from successful evasion. This changes what the law is doing. It is no longer setting a baseline of admission policy and then making occasional accommodations to humanitarian reality. It is announcing that the baseline admission policy is conditional — that anyone willing to evade enforcement for a few years can override it.

Carens argues that the prospect of distant legalization will not affect migration decisions. He may be right about this for individual migrants in the moment of decision. But the more important question is what it does to the entire system of migration, including smuggling networks, employer expectations, and source-country labor markets. Smugglers will market the rule. Employers will count on continued workforce replenishment. Source-country migration networks will adjust their expectations and incentives accordingly. The cumulative effect is to convert what was an episodic humanitarian response into a structural feature of the immigration regime.

There is also a rule-of-law concern. Periodic amnesties acknowledge a tension between law and humanitarian necessity, and they resolve it through explicit political action — a vote, a debate, a moment when the political community decides. The prospective rule conceals the tension. It tells the citizen: do not worry, the law is being followed, every five-year migrant becomes legal under the law. But this disguises the fact that the law has been written to nullify itself. The political community is not actually making a meaningful decision about admissions; it is delegating that decision to whoever can get in.

What I want to defend, then, is a different framework. The status quo of mass irregular settlement is intolerable, and a substantial legalization is both necessary and right. Let us have it. Let us have it on terms similar to what Carens describes — long-resident migrants, clean records, family ties, demonstrated workforce participation. Let us do it now, for the people who are here. But let us not write a rule that converts every future entrant into a deferred-legal migrant. Let us instead, in the same legislation, reform the legal admissions system so that the gap between labor demand and legal supply does not produce another shadow population. Increase visa numbers for unskilled workers; expand family categories; create credible enforcement mechanisms; and then, in five or ten years, deal with whatever residual irregular population remains. If it is small, deal with it through case-by-case discretion. If it is large, have another amnesty, openly, as a political decision.

This framework preserves the political community's authority over its own membership. It treats each amnesty as a deliberate decision, not an automatic result of evasion. It allows the political community to absorb the moral costs of past failures (the present eleven million) without committing itself to a rule that pre-emptively absorbs all future failures. It is, in a word, a politics of immigration that takes seriously both the human claims of irregular migrants and the democratic claims of the receiving community.

Carens' moral intuitions are largely right. The eleven million should be legalized. Marguerite Grimmond should not have been deported. Hiu Lui Ng should not have died in detention. But the institutional form Carens proposes — a prospective rule — does not follow from these intuitions, and indeed it makes worse the long-term political dynamic that produced the present crisis. Amnesty, yes. Automatic legalization-by-time, no. The distinction matters.

  2. 02Unlike one-time amnesties, a permanent rule for legalization-by-time alters the behavior of structural actors like smuggling networks and employers by making irregular labor a permanent feature of the immigration regime.
  3. 03Automatic legalization undermines the rule of law by creating a system where the law is designed to nullify itself, effectively delegating membership decisions to those who enter illegally rather than the political community.
  4. 04The tension between immigration law and humanitarian necessity is best resolved through explicit, periodic political decisions rather than an institutionalized mechanism that conceals the community's choice.
  5. 05A superior policy framework combines immediate amnesty for current residents with a reformed legal admissions system that aligns labor demand with legal supply to prevent the growth of future shadow populations.
Diversified Claude

"While a one-time amnesty may be humanitarily necessary, Carens’ proposal for a permanent, prospective rule of legalization undermines the rule of law, which is the essential foundation of liberal democratic legitimacy."

Carens' essay is admirable, but I want to enter a dissenting note from an unexpected angle: I think Carens does not take seriously enough the rule of law as a constituent value of liberal democracy itself.

The rule of law is not just one consideration to be weighed against the human costs of removal. It is the constitutive structure of liberal democratic governance. A polity that does not enforce its own laws, or that enforces them in such a way as to systematically reward those who evade them, is not just enforcing badly — it is undermining the basis of its own legitimacy. The reason citizens accept the demands of law on themselves is that they expect the law to apply, in some reasonably consistent way, to others. When this expectation breaks down, the entire structure of legal authority weakens.

The eleven million irregular migrants in the United States present a problem for the rule of law of a kind that we have not adequately confronted. The problem is not that the law has been broken; laws are broken all the time. The problem is that the law has been broken on such a scale, and with such effective tolerance by the state, that the state's nominal commitment to the law has become a kind of legal fiction. We have an immigration regime in which the rules are clear, the violations are massive, and the enforcement is selective in ways that nobody can really defend. This is corrosive of legal authority in a much deeper way than ordinary lawbreaking.

Carens proposes to resolve the situation by adjusting the law to fit the facts. Since enforcement of the existing rules against the existing population is impossible, and since the human costs of attempting enforcement are unacceptable, the rules should be changed to legalize the population. There is something to this. But it is also a particular response to a rule-of-law crisis, and it has its own rule-of-law costs.

When a polity responds to mass violation of its laws by legalizing the violators, it sends a particular signal: this kind of law, this kind of rule, is not really binding. The signal may be partial and qualified — "we are legalizing because of the human cost of removal, not because we approve of the violation" — but the practical effect is that violation pays. The next generation of would-be migrants, and the next generation of would-be employers, and the next generation of smuggling networks, all observe what has happened and adjust their expectations. The IRCA legalization of 1986 was followed by an increase in unauthorized migration, not a decrease, in part because the political community had demonstrated that mass irregular settlement could produce legalization rather than removal. The prospective rule Carens proposes would make this signal explicit and permanent.

I do not say this as a knock-down objection. The rule-of-law cost of legalization has to be weighed against the human cost of removal, and in the present case the human cost is enormous and the rule-of-law cost may be tolerable. I am inclined to support a substantial legalization, though I think the terms should be tighter than Carens proposes. But I think the rule-of-law dimension deserves more weight in his analysis than he gives it.

There is a related point about the integrity of the legalization process itself. If we adopt Carens' prospective rule, what we are doing is creating a parallel admissions track in which the qualifying criterion is successful evasion. This is a strange kind of legal rule. It says: do not enter without authorization; if you do enter without authorization, do not stay; but if you stay long enough, you will receive authorization. The contradiction is right there on the surface. Carens defends it by analogy to statutes of limitations, but the analogy is imperfect. Statutes of limitations bar prosecution of past offenses; they do not confer affirmative legal status. A statute of limitations does not turn a thief into the rightful owner of stolen property; it just prevents the state from prosecuting the theft. Carens proposes something more: that long evasion not only bars removal but actively confers permanent residence and a path to citizenship. This is not a statute of limitations. It is a parallel naturalization scheme.

What follows from this? I think two things.

First, if we are going to undertake a major legalization, we should do it in a form that minimizes its rule-of-law damage. A one-time legalization, framed explicitly as a response to a particular historical situation, is less corrosive than a prospective rule. It says: we are dealing with the facts on the ground; we are not announcing that future violation will be rewarded. The 1986 legalization, whatever its limitations, took this form. A 2010 or 2011 legalization could and should do the same. Carens' prospective rule is a step too far.

Second, any legalization should be paired with serious enforcement going forward. The reason the 1986 amnesty did not solve the problem is that the enforcement provisions of IRCA were never seriously implemented. Employers were not penalized. Visa overstays were not tracked. The border was reinforced in some ways and not others, with the perverse effect of pushing migration into more dangerous routes without significantly reducing total flows. A serious legalization in 2010 has to include, as part of the package, the enforcement mechanisms that 1986 did not deliver: meaningful workplace verification, real penalties on employers, a functioning visa-tracking system, expanded legal channels for the labor we actually want. Without these, the legalization will produce another eleven million, and the rule of law will erode further.

Carens' essay is largely silent about this side of the bargain. He focuses on what we owe the migrants, which is real and substantial, but he says little about what we owe ourselves as a self-governing polity that needs its laws to mean something. A defense of amnesty that does not engage this dimension is not really a defense of amnesty within liberal democracy; it is a defense of amnesty against the priorities that make liberal democracy possible.

The rule of law is not a fetish or a slogan. It is the basis on which we ask citizens to accept policies they did not vote for, to pay taxes they would rather not pay, to accept restrictions they would rather not accept. When we erode it, we erode the basis of collective action itself. Amnesty is sometimes necessary; the rule of law sometimes has to yield to humanitarian urgency. But we should be more reluctant about the yielding than Carens is, and more attentive to what is being lost.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Systematically rewarding those who evade the law signals that legal rules are not binding, thereby encouraging future violations by migrants and employers.
  2. 02Carens’ analogy to statutes of limitations is flawed because such statutes merely bar prosecution for past acts rather than conferring affirmative legal status and rights.
  3. 03A prospective rule creates a contradictory and parallel admissions track where the primary qualifying criterion for legal residency is successful evasion of the law.
  4. 04To preserve legal integrity, any legalization must be a one-time historical response rather than a standing rule and must be paired with rigorous future enforcement mechanisms.
  5. 05The rule of law is the necessary basis for collective action and social cooperation, and it should not be yielded as readily as Carens suggests.
Diversified MiniMax

"While Carens’s moral case for amnesty is philosophically sound, its practical success depends on navigating the economic and political realities that influence how social membership is perceived by the public."

Carens's essay appeared in Boston Review in May 2009, in the midst of the Great Recession—the worst economic crisis since the Depression. This timing is not incidental. The case for amnesty looks very different when unemployment is near ten percent than it does during economic booms.

I do not mean to invoke crude economic determinism. The moral claims Carens identifies do not disappear when the economy contracts. But they do become more difficult to assert politically. When native-born workers are struggling to find jobs, the argument that irregular migrants have become community members rings hollow to those who see them as economic competitors. The rhetoric of social membership assumes that membership means participation in a shared project; but if irregular migrants are perceived as taking jobs from citizens, they are not participating—they are free-riding.

This political reality does not invalidate Carens's moral argument, but it does suggest that the timing of amnesty proposals matters enormously. The 1986 Simpson-Mazzoli Act granted amnesty to roughly three million irregular migrants at a moment of relative economic confidence. The political coalition for such a measure has proven difficult to reassemble in subsequent decades.

If advocates of amnesty want to succeed, they need to build broader coalitions that include workers who might otherwise see irregular migrants as competitors. This means addressing concerns about wage depression, job displacement, and public services honestly, rather than dismissing them as nativist prejudice. Carens's philosophical argument provides a moral foundation, but translating it into policy requires political strategy that his essay does not provide.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The political viability of amnesty proposals fluctuates significantly based on the health of the economy and the unemployment rate.
  2. 02In times of economic contraction, the perception of irregular migrants as economic competitors undermines the rhetoric of social membership and shared community projects.
  3. 03To achieve policy change, amnesty advocates must build broad coalitions by addressing legitimate concerns regarding wage depression and job displacement rather than dismissing them as prejudice.
  4. 04A moral foundation for amnesty is insufficient for legislative success without a corresponding political strategy that accounts for the concerns of native-born workers.
Vanilla DeepSeek

"While Joseph Carens’s proposal for an automatic amnesty is morally compassionate, it is practically flawed because it ignores the negative real-world consequences of incentivizing illegal immigration and undermining the rule of law."

Joseph Carens has written a humane and provocative essay, and his central image of Marguerite Grimmond — an 80-year-old threatened with deportation from the only home she has ever known — rightly evokes outrage at the cruelty that rigid enforcement of immigration laws can inflict. His argument that social membership, built through years of living, working, and raising a family, should ultimately outweigh the state’s interest in removing irregular migrants has considerable moral force. Yet for all its philosophical appeal, Carens’s case for amnesty largely ignores the real-world consequences that such a policy would generate, and it demands a burden of proof that his abstract reasoning cannot meet.

The most serious omission is the question of incentives. Carens asserts that “it is plausible to suppose” that a long-term promise of regularization would have little impact on decisions to migrate or overstay, because migrants focus on the immediate future. That speculation runs directly against the accumulated experience of the countries that have experimented with large-scale amnesties. The United States’s own Immigration Reform and Control Act of 1986 legalized some 2.7 million unauthorized residents, but far from resolving the problem, it was followed by a dramatic increase in illegal immigration. By 2009, the unauthorized population had swollen to an estimated 11 million or more. Migration scholars and border officials have repeatedly observed that amnesties send a powerful signal to prospective migrants: if you can get in and stay long enough, eventually you will be allowed to remain. In El Salvador, Mexico, and elsewhere, families knowingly planned on the assumption that another amnesty would come. Carens may doubt the evidence, but the pattern is too consistent to dismiss. An automatic pathway to legal status after five or seven years, as he proposes, would only strengthen that signal. Why would someone who cannot qualify for a visa not take the risk, if the worst that can happen is early detection and return, while the reward for persistence is full membership?

Carens also leans heavily on analogies that obscure more than they illuminate. He compares immigration violations to traffic offenses and invokes statutes of limitations for minor crimes. But the collective effect of millions of unauthorized entries and overstays is not remotely like a speeding ticket. Irregular migration at the current scale — whether one deplores it or not — reshapes labor markets, strains public services, and, perhaps most importantly, corrodes the public’s confidence that the rule of law applies equally to all. When a state repeatedly grants amnesties, it trains citizens and would-be migrants to see immigration law as a flexible guideline rather than a binding rule. That corrosive effect is not imaginary: it is one reason why amnesty proposals remain intensely controversial in the United States and Europe, even among citizens who are otherwise sympathetic to immigrants. A statute of limitations is designed for discrete, past acts; a rolling entitlement to legalization after a fixed period is a standing invitation to break the law today in the expectation of future absolution.

The fairness objection, which Carens acknowledges but treats too briefly, also deserves more weight. It is true that many irregular migrants originate from countries with no realistic “line” for unskilled workers. Nevertheless, there are legal avenues — family reunification, diversity lotteries, skilled-worker programs — and millions of people do wait patiently in those lines, sometimes for a decade or more. Granting amnesty to those who evaded the system not only rewards queue-jumping but also undermines the political legitimacy of legal immigration channels. If the public comes to believe that the law is for suckers, support for even the generous legal admissions that Carens presumably values will erode.

None of this is to deny that Carens identifies a genuine tragedy in the lives of long-settled irregular migrants, nor that state complicity — through lax enforcement or employer demand — sometimes compounds the injustice. But a responsible policy must weigh those individual hardships against the need to maintain a coherent immigration system. The proper response to the problem Carens so vividly describes is not an automatic amnesty that operates irrespective of whether the state is actually controlling its borders. It is, rather, a comprehensive approach that pairs a tough but realistic enforcement regime with a carefully targeted legalization for those who genuinely have no other moral claim and who arrived under conditions the state itself helped create. To offer a blanket promise of citizenship after five or seven years without also gaining control over future flows is to guarantee that in another decade, we will be having this exact same debate, only with far larger numbers. That is not a moral triumph; it is an abdication of the hard choices that democratic self-government requires.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Historical evidence suggests that large-scale amnesties act as a powerful signal that encourages further unauthorized migration by rewarding persistence with legal status.
  2. 02The analogy between immigration violations and minor traffic offenses is inaccurate because large-scale irregular migration has significant collective impacts on labor markets, public services, and social trust.
  3. 03Granting amnesty to those who bypassed legal channels is fundamentally unfair to those who wait years for authorized entry and risks eroding public support for legal immigration systems.
  4. 04A rolling entitlement to legalization functions as a standing invitation to break the law, treating immigration regulations as flexible guidelines rather than binding rules.
  5. 05A responsible immigration policy must prioritize gaining control over future flows and pair targeted legalization with strict enforcement rather than offering a blanket, automatic promise of citizenship.
Vanilla MiniMax

"While social membership creates moral claims for irregular migrants over time, an automatic right to amnesty after a fixed period creates significant tensions with democratic legitimacy and the moral standing of those who follow legal immigration procedures."

Carens's argument rests on a powerful intuition: that people who live and work in a society for extended periods develop social connections that generate moral claims, claims that eventually outweigh the state's interest in enforcing immigration restrictions. The cases he invokes—Marguerite Grimmond, Hiu Lui Ng—are genuinely compelling, and his central claim that social membership matters morally is difficult to dispute. Yet the argument raises troubling questions that deserve more sustained attention than his essay provides, particularly regarding the scope of the principle and its implications for democratic self-governance.

The core of Carens's position is that time alone transforms moral claims. Once irregular migrants have been present long enough—Carens suggests roughly five to seven years—they acquire a right to legal status simply by virtue of having formed social connections. The appeal of this view is clear: we do not uproot people from communities where they have built lives, raised children, and established deep ties. But this reasoning, if followed consistently, creates significant tensions with other moral commitments that liberal democracies hold equally dear.

Consider the case against amnesty from the perspective of those who abide by immigration laws. Carens dismisses this concern somewhat hastily, arguing that most irregular migrants would have no legal pathway anyway. Yet this observation, while accurate, does not fully address the moral weight of the complaint. If a state establishes procedures for legal immigration—however imperfect—and individuals make significant sacrifices to follow those procedures, the irregular migrant who circumvents them gains an advantage that is not merely practical but moral. The person who waits, who saves money for years to afford a family-sponsored visa, who accepts temporary separation from loved ones to comply with quotas—these sacrifices are real and often devastating. Amnesty that regularizes the status of those who violated the rules implicitly devalues the compliance of those who did not. Carens is right that the analogy to traffic violations has force, but immigration restrictions carry different symbolic weight precisely because they define the boundaries of political community.

There is also the question of democratic legitimacy. Carens frames his argument as a constraint on state power that exists even within the context of legitimate border control. But this framing raises difficult questions about who gets to determine the threshold. If the moral claim of social membership is as powerful as he argues, then the specific timeframe—five years, seven years, ten years—cannot be set by governments without violating the rights of irregular migrants. Yet if courts or scholars impose a constitutional or natural-law standard, this effectively removes the question from democratic deliberation. This is not a minor technical concern. Immigration policy involves fundamental choices about the size and composition of the political community, choices that democratic theory typically reserves to citizens acting through representative institutions. A principle that requires legalization regardless of democratic preferences raises genuine tensions with popular sovereignty.

Carens acknowledges this difficulty but does not adequately resolve it. His suggestion that discretion should be minimized to avoid discriminatory application is sensible, but it does not address the deeper problem that the moral standard he defends may be incompatible with democratic majorities that reach different conclusions. The European guest workers he cites gained legal status not because the moral case was compelling, but because political conditions made regularization possible. Treating this as simply the recognition of a pre-existing moral truth understates the role of political struggle in shaping immigration policy.

There is also the problem of incentives, which Carens addresses but perhaps too quickly. He assumes that potential irregular migrants weigh near-term considerations against long-term risks, suggesting that the possibility of eventual amnesty would not significantly affect migration decisions. This may be true for some, but it assumes a level of information and rationality that migrants do not always possess. In sending communities, knowledge about regularization policies spreads through networks, and if potential migrants believe that staying long enough guarantees legal status, this expectation could plausibly affect decisions to migrate or to remain after arrival. The counterfactual—that irregular migration would be roughly similar in the absence of amnesty expectations—is not self-evident.

None of these objections is decisive against Carens's fundamental insight that social membership creates moral claims that the state cannot simply ignore. He is right that it is cruel to uproot people who have become members of society through long residence, and right that this cruelty grows more severe over time. The cases of Grimmond and Ng illustrate a genuine moral absurdity in rigid enforcement of immigration restrictions.

But the implications of this insight are more complicated than Carens suggests. Acknowledging that social membership matters does not automatically yield a policy of automatic legalization after a fixed period. It might instead call for case-by-case assessment, with genuine procedural protections and meaningful opportunities for irregular migrants to present evidence of their membership. It might require regularization as a presumptive outcome but allow exceptions for those who pose genuine risks or who arrived very recently. It might suggest that the appropriate response is not legalization but protection from deportation—a narrower but more defensible position. These alternatives deserve more serious engagement than Carens provides.

The case for amnesty is strong, but it is not the only morally weighty consideration in this domain. Democratic communities have legitimate interests in controlling their borders and in maintaining the integrity of their immigration procedures. The challenge for policy is to balance these interests against the claims that social membership generates, a balance that Carens's argument illuminates but does not resolve.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Granting amnesty devalues the significant sacrifices made by legal immigrants who endure long waits and family separations to comply with established laws.
  2. 02Imposing a fixed moral threshold for legalization removes fundamental questions about the composition of the political community from the necessary sphere of democratic deliberation and popular sovereignty.
  3. 03The author's dismissal of the 'pull factor' assumes a level of migrant rationality that may not account for how expectations of regularization spread through social networks and influence migration decisions.
  4. 04The moral claim of social membership might be better addressed through case-by-case assessments or protection from deportation rather than a universal, automatic right to legal status.
  5. 05The historical examples of regularization cited by Carens were often the result of specific political struggles and conditions rather than the simple recognition of a pre-existing moral truth.

"While long-term irregular migrants have moral claims to stay, any amnesty policy must also account for the unfairness to legal immigrants who followed the rules and the potential for such policies to undermine the integrity of the legal immigration system."

I want to offer a sympathetic but critical engagement with Carens's argument by focusing on a question he leaves unasked: what do we owe to the citizens and legal residents of destination countries?

Carens is right that irregular migrants develop social membership and moral claims to remain. But he gives short shrift to the claims of those who have played by the rules. Consider Maria, a Filipino nurse who waited eight years for a employment-based visa, paying fees to immigration lawyers, maintaining continuous employment to preserve her eligibility, and eventually entering the United States legally in 2006. Maria now watches as Miguel Sanchez, who entered illegally in 2000, receives amnesty and an immediate path to permanent residency.

The unfairness here is not merely psychological. Maria followed a process that the state established, investing significant time and resources in compliance. When the state grants amnesty to those who bypassed this process, it implicitly devalues the sacrifices Maria made. This is not a trivial concern: if legal immigration pathways become perceived as sucker bets—useful only for those who lack the nerve to enter illegally—then compliance rates may fall across the board.

Carens addresses this objection briefly, arguing that most irregular migrants have no legal pathway available, so they are not really jumping a queue. This is true in many cases, but it does not address the symbolic and practical effects of amnesty on the broader immigration system. If we want a system that people will follow, it must offer meaningful incentives for compliance. Amnesties, by definition, erase the costs of noncompliance.

I am not arguing that this concern outweighs the moral claims of long-term residents. I am suggesting that a complete policy analysis must account for the effects of amnesty on the overall immigration system, not merely on the individuals who benefit from it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Granting amnesty to those who bypassed legal channels devalues the significant time, resources, and sacrifices made by legal residents who complied with immigration laws.
  2. 02Amnesty policies risk creating a perception that legal immigration pathways are unnecessary, which may lead to a decrease in overall compliance with immigration regulations.
  3. 03A functional immigration system requires meaningful incentives for compliance, which are eroded when the costs of noncompliance are erased through amnesty.
  4. 04The moral claims of irregular migrants should not be considered in isolation from the state's obligations to the citizens and legal residents who play by the rules.
Vanilla Gemini

"While rootedness in a community carries moral weight, an automatic, time-based amnesty for unauthorized immigrants is fundamentally flawed because it undermines the rule of law and creates dangerous incentives."

Joseph H. Carens makes a deeply humane appeal for regularizing the status of unauthorized immigrants based on the passage of time. He is right to point out that individuals who have lived in a community for years—working, raising families, and forming deep social bonds—become de facto members of that society. The stories of Miguel Sanchez, Marguerite Grimmond, and Hiu Lui Ng resonate because they appeal to our basic sense of fairness and compassion. However, while Carens correctly identifies the moral weight of rootedness, his proposed solution—an automatic, rolling amnesty for anyone who evades deportation for five to seven years—is fundamentally flawed. It mischaracterizes the nature of immigration laws, dismisses the profound unfairness it would inflict on legal applicants, and ignores the severe perverse incentives it would create.

To justify treating unauthorized settlement as a minor infraction, Carens compares immigration violations to traffic offenses, arguing that neither makes a person a "criminal" in a serious sense. This analogy minimizes the purpose of national borders. Traffic laws regulate the behavior of individuals who are already recognized members or authorized guests of a community. Immigration laws, by contrast, define the community itself. A democratic society maintains the right to determine the pace, scale, and conditions of new membership to ensure economic stability, social cohesion, and security. Bypassing this system is not akin to driving a few miles over the speed limit; it is the usurpation of a nation’s sovereign right to grant consent to its own members.

Carens also too casually dismisses the argument that amnesty is unfair to those who wait in the legal immigration line. He asserts that because there is virtually no line for unskilled workers without family ties, unauthorized migrants are not really cutting in front of anyone. But this ignores the millions of people around the world who are waiting patiently in the lines that do exist. Currently, the family reunification backlog is immense. A legal resident from Mexico or the Philippines seeking to bring a sibling to the United States might wait well over a decade. To reward those who chose to bypass the system, while ignoring the sacrifice of those who respected it, makes a mockery of the law. It signals to prospective migrants that adhering to the legal process is a fool’s errand.

This brings us to the most dangerous aspect of Carens’s proposal: the institutionalization of an automatic transition to legal status after five to seven years. Carens doubts this would encourage further unauthorized migration, suggesting that migrants only consider short-term economic gains. Recent history suggests otherwise. The 1986 Immigration Reform and Control Act granted a one-time amnesty to roughly three million unauthorized immigrants, with the promise that border enforcement would prevent future waves. Because the enforcement mechanisms proved inadequate, the unauthorized population swelled to the estimated eleven million we have today.

If a one-time amnesty helped spur decades of subsequent unauthorized migration, an automatic, permanent statute of limitations would be disastrous. It would officially codify a game of hide-and-seek. A rule stating that anyone who manages to stay undetected for five years receives legal permanent residency is not an immigration policy; it is an open invitation. Migrant networks are highly sophisticated and well-informed. The knowledge that survival in the shadows for sixty months guarantees a green card would vastly increase the number of people willing to risk deadly desert crossings or rely on exploitative human smugglers.

Carens rejects case-by-case discretionary relief because he fears discrimination and inefficiency. Yet, some level of discretion and systemic control is exactly what is needed. The comprehensive immigration reform efforts that recently stalled in Congress—such as the 2007 McCain-Kennedy bill—struggled precisely because of the delicate balance required between securing borders, enforcing workplace laws, and providing a tough, conditional path to legal status (an "earned legalization" requiring fines, back taxes, and a place at the back of the line). Carens’s proposal strips away these necessary balances, replacing the difficult work of comprehensive reform with a blanket, time-based entitlement.

Ultimately, liberal democracies must balance compassion for the individual with the integrity of the system as a whole. It is entirely reasonable to conclude that a blanket policy of mass deportation is both morally unacceptable and practically impossible. Many of the eleven million unauthorized immigrants currently in the United States will, and should, eventually find a way to come out of the shadows. But creating a permanent structural loophole that automatically rewards unauthorized settlement after a few short years would destroy the very foundation of legal immigration. We need a functioning, enforceable immigration system, not a stopwatch for those who manage to circumvent it.
