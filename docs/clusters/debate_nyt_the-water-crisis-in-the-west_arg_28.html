"To prevent the American West from running out of water, states must immediately implement strict, enforceable regulations to manage and preserve groundwater aquifers."

While falling reservoir levels at Lake Mead and Lake Shasta provide the most dramatic visual evidence of the current drought, the most dangerous aspect of the water crisis in the West is happening entirely underground. Across the region, we are engaged in a catastrophic, largely unregulated depletion of our groundwater aquifers. Surface water rationing will completely fail to secure our future if we continue to treat our underground reserves as an endless, free-for-all resource.

In California, currently enduring one of its worst droughts on record, the situation is particularly dire. It remains the only western state that does not have a comprehensive statewide system for managing and regulating groundwater. Because surface water allocations from state and federal projects have been drastically slashed, farmers and municipalities are drilling deeper and deeper wells to make up the difference. They are pumping water out of the ground vastly faster than natural rainfall can replenish it.

The consequences of this underground tragedy of the commons are severe and irreversible. As aquifers are drained, the geological structures collapse, causing the ground above to physically sink. This phenomenon, known as land subsidence, is currently wreaking havoc in the Central Valley, buckling canals, cracking roads, and destroying infrastructure. Once an aquifer collapses, its capacity to hold water is permanently lost; even if record rains return, the storage space is gone forever.

To ensure water lasts for the foreseeable future, western states must immediately implement strict, enforceable groundwater regulations. The days of allowing anyone with a drill and a pump to extract unlimited amounts of water from a shared aquifer must end. Every well must be mandated to have a meter, and pumping data must be transparently reported to state water agencies.

Furthermore, local groundwater sustainability agencies must be empowered to set hard caps on extraction, ensuring that withdrawals do not exceed the safe yield of the aquifer. In areas of severe overdraft, pumping must be sharply curtailed, even if it means some agricultural land must go out of production.

Groundwater is our strategic drought reserve; it is the savings account we rely on when the rains fail. By draining it indiscriminately, we are bankrupting our future to artificially sustain the present. Regulating groundwater is politically difficult and faces fierce opposition from property rights advocates, but it is an absolute necessity. Without aggressive aquifer management, the West will quite simply run out of water.
