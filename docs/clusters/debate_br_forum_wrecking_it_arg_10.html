"The severity of modern cybercrime and the Internet's role as critical infrastructure necessitate a shift from voluntary civic collaboration toward enforceable legal duties and more controlled, secure computing environments."

Jonathan Zittrain’s defense of generativity is eloquent, but it understates the severity of the emergency and overestimates the capacity of civic-minded collaboration to solve it. The Internet is no longer mainly a playground for inventors, scholars, and amateurs. It is critical infrastructure. Banks, hospitals, police departments, airlines, power companies, and militaries depend on networked computers. So do households whose savings, medical records, and livelihoods are increasingly digital. In that context, the claim that we must preserve openness above all else begins to sound less like prudence than sentimentality.

The central fact is that the threat has changed. Zittrain recounts the Morris worm as an episode of youthful recklessness, and contrasts it with today’s professionalized malware economy. That distinction should lead us to a harsher conclusion than the one he reaches. We are not dealing primarily with vandals. We are dealing with criminals. Botnets are not graffiti; they are infrastructure for fraud, extortion, identity theft, and denial-of-service attacks. Their owners have incentives, customers, and supply chains. Against that, the idea that “netizens” armed with dashboards and community norms can meaningfully secure the ecosystem seems inadequate.

The analogy to Wikipedia is especially weak. Wikipedia vandalism is visible. If someone replaces an entry with nonsense, another user can see it and revert it. Malware is deliberately invisible. A compromised machine can behave normally while sending spam, logging keystrokes, scanning for victims, or waiting for instructions. Worse, the owner of the machine may be the least qualified person to determine whether anything is wrong. We can teach users to recognize obvious pop-ups and crashes, but the most dangerous infections will not announce themselves. A neighborhood watch is useful when neighbors can see suspicious activity. It is less useful when the burglars are invisible and the houses themselves have no locks.

Zittrain’s Red/Green virtual machine proposal is more promising, but still too optimistic. Enterprises already attempt versions of this idea through locked-down desktops, restricted privileges, imaging, and network segmentation. These measures are imperfect, expensive, and often resented by users, but they exist because unmanaged freedom is costly. In the home environment, expecting consumers to understand which activities belong in Red and which in Green may be unrealistic. The very users who install spyware because it comes bundled with a game or file-sharing program will also move files across the boundary, approve exceptions, and ignore warnings. Technical containment can help, but only if it is enforced more firmly than Zittrain seems willing to accept.

That brings us to the uncomfortable word that the essay avoids: liability. The software and hardware industries have benefited for decades from a remarkable absence of responsibility for insecure products. If a toaster routinely caught fire, we would not tell consumers to join a toaster-safety community and monitor heat signatures. If an automobile could be hijacked from the next county because the manufacturer shipped it with known defects, we would not put all the burden on drivers. Yet in software, vendors can sell complex systems riddled with vulnerabilities, patch them later if convenient, and disclaim nearly everything.

This does not mean that every bug should produce a lawsuit. Software is complex, and perfect security is impossible. But the present allocation of responsibility is plainly distorted. Vendors are best positioned to design safer defaults, reduce unnecessary privileges, provide automatic updates, and avoid reckless architectures. Internet service providers are best positioned to detect compromised machines sending vast quantities of spam or participating in attacks. Large online services are best positioned to identify suspicious behavior across millions of accounts. Ordinary users are not best positioned to do any of these things.

A serious security policy would create duties for the actors with the capacity to reduce harm. Operating system vendors should be required to provide timely security updates for supported products and to design systems that do not routinely require users to operate with administrative privileges. ISPs should be required to respond when machines on their networks are plainly infected and causing harm, perhaps by quarantining the connection while helping the customer clean it. Commercial software distributors should face penalties for deceptive bundling and undisclosed behavior. Companies that store sensitive personal data should have enforceable security obligations. These are not radical proposals; they are ordinary principles of responsibility translated into the digital environment.

Zittrain worries that too much regulation will chill amateur innovation. That is a real concern, but it should not paralyze us. We can distinguish between a hobbyist posting a small open-source tool and a company shipping software to millions of users. We can create safe harbors for noncommercial developers who act in good faith and respond to notice of problems. We can require higher standards for code that seeks deep system privileges, handles financial information, or auto-installs across networks. Law is capable of gradation.

Nor should we romanticize the PC as the unique source of innovation. Many users do not want a general-purpose computer in every pocket and living room. They want devices that work. The popularity of game consoles, BlackBerrys, iPods, and the iPhone is not merely a symptom of fear. It reflects a legitimate desire for reliability, integration, and support. A hospital should not run life-critical systems on the theory that every endpoint ought to welcome experimentation. A parent may reasonably prefer a child’s game console that cannot run arbitrary code from strangers. A business may reasonably forbid employees from installing unapproved applications on machines that hold customer data.

The question is not whether locked-down systems are bad and open systems are good. The question is where openness is worth the risk. We need a layered ecology. Research labs, universities, hobbyists, and developers should have open machines and open networks. Consumers should have access to programmable platforms if they want them. But many everyday functions should migrate to more controlled environments, not as a betrayal of the Internet but as a maturation of it. We do not allow anyone to modify aircraft avionics in the name of generativity. We should not assume that all computing contexts deserve the same freedom.

Data portability is one place where Zittrain is entirely right. If users choose appliances and hosted services, they must not be trapped. The law should require export of user-created data in usable formats. It should also police deceptive changes in service terms and remote disabling of purchased functionality. But portability is a complement to managed platforms, not a substitute for them.

The Internet’s openness produced extraordinary innovation. It also produced an ecosystem in which millions of poorly secured machines can be conscripted by criminals. The proper response is not to wreck the Internet, but neither is it to preserve every inherited freedom as if architecture designed for a smaller, more trusting world were sacred. We need enforceable duties, safer defaults, ISP intervention, and, yes, more locked-down environments where the stakes justify them. Generativity is valuable. Security is not merely valuable; it is prerequisite to trust. Without trust, the open Internet will not be saved by civic spirit. It will be abandoned.
