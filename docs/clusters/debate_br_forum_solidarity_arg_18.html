The Religious Question

I want to take seriously what almost no other respondent to Mie Inouye's essay will: the religious dimension of her argument. Inouye learned to organize in the Mormon Church. She names this explicitly, and she develops the comparison thoughtfully. But she ultimately treats Mormonism as a source of transferable techniques — "social endurance," repeated co-presence, the cultivation of cooperation across difference — that can be extracted from their theological matrix and applied to secular democratic organizing. I want to question whether this extraction works, and to ask what it would mean for the left to take religion more seriously than the secular framing of "solidarity" has typically allowed.

Let me state the worry directly. The endurance that the Mormon ward produced in Inouye and her family is inseparable from the theological commitments that gave the ward its meaning: the doctrine of the eternal family, the conviction that this life is a probationary period in a vast cosmic drama, the belief that the Saints are quite literally building Zion in preparation for Christ's return. People showed up for three hours on Sunday and an hour before school because they believed that the showing up was tied to the salvation of their souls and the preservation of their families across eternity. Strip away these beliefs and you do not get the same endurance with different content. You get a much weaker form of association that resembles the secular voluntary organizations Inouye contrasts Mormonism with.

This is not a defense of Mormon theology, much of which I do not share. It is a structural observation about how religion produces solidarity. Émile Durkheim understood this; so did William James; so did Charles Taylor more recently in A Secular Age. The thick associational life that Inouye correctly identifies as the source of her organizing formation depends, in religious contexts, on cosmological commitments that secular formations cannot replicate. The question is not whether the left can learn from religious institutions; of course it can and should. The question is whether the lessons are portable across the religious/secular divide without significant loss.

I think the honest answer is: partly, but not as much as we would like. Consider what secular formations have tried to substitute for religious cosmology over the past two centuries. Marxism, at its peak, offered a near-cosmological narrative — historical materialism as a quasi-providential account of human emancipation, the proletariat as a quasi-messianic agent, communism as a quasi-eschatological horizon. This narrative produced enormous solidarity at its strongest, including in conditions of severe repression. As the cosmological force of the Marxist narrative has faded, so has the solidarity it once anchored. This is not coincidence.

Civil rights organizing, which Inouye references, depended heavily on Black church theology — the prophetic tradition, the exodus narrative, the resurrection hope. Cornel West and others have argued that the secularization of the Black freedom struggle, however inevitable, has cost the movement some of the spiritual depth that earlier generations could draw on. Liberation theology in Latin America produced base communities and revolutionary movements precisely because it kept theology and political organizing in tight integration. Catholic Worker houses still produce remarkable endurance because they are rooted in a sacramental vision of the poor.

What does this mean for the contemporary American left, most of whose participants are secular or only loosely religious? I see three honest options.

The first is to acknowledge that secular solidarity will be, almost necessarily, thinner than religious solidarity, and to lower expectations accordingly. This is the implicit position of much liberal political theory: solidarity is a precious but limited resource, and we should design institutions that can do their work with relatively modest demands on it. The welfare state, on this view, substitutes institutional redistribution for the thick mutual aid that religious communities once provided. Whatever else one says about the postwar welfare state, it did real work, and it did so without asking citizens to share a cosmology.

The second option is to attempt to construct quasi-religious commitments within secular movements — to develop rituals, narratives, calendars, ethical practices, and forms of community that approximate what religion does without the theology. Some movements have tried this, with mixed results. The labor movement at its peak had songs, holidays, martyrs, and a near-sacramental sense of solidarity. The early Communist parties had something similar. Contemporary climate movements have tried, with their own iconographies and rituals. The results have been real but limited, and there is always the risk that quasi-religion shades into kitsch or, worse, into cultic dynamics that lack the corrective resources of mature religious traditions.

The third option, which I find most interesting, is to take religion seriously on its own terms and ask whether actually religious people, in actually religious institutions, can become significant partners in left organizing. This has historically been one of the most effective forms of multiracial working-class coalition-building in the United States, and the model still works. PICO (now Faith in Action), the IAF affiliates rooted in congregations, the network of progressive Black churches, the Catholic social teaching tradition, the prophetic Jewish institutions, the small but real progressive evangelical world, the growing Muslim civic infrastructure — these are real solidarity-producing institutions that the secular left often treats with suspicion or condescension, when it should be treating them as partners.

The Reverend William Barber's Poor People's Campaign, which Inouye references in its 1968 incarnation, has been doing significant work for the past several years precisely by reconnecting prophetic religion to economic justice across lines of race and region. The work is uneven; the institutional infrastructure is fragile; the secular media often does not know how to cover it. But it is one of the most genuine attempts at the kind of multiracial working-class coalition Inouye says we need, and it is succeeding to the extent it is because it draws on the theological resources that secular movements cannot easily replicate.

I would push Inouye to be more direct about this. Her essay treats Mormonism, in the end, as a kind of training ground from which she has graduated into a more democratic and pluralistic organizing practice. Fair enough — that is her own trajectory. But the essay's implicit framing is that the lessons of Mormonism can be carried forward into secular organizing without significant loss. I am skeptical. I think we would do better to acknowledge that religious institutions have resources for solidarity that secular ones do not, that the secular left has often been bad at partnering with religious institutions, and that any serious project of building multiracial working-class power in the United States will have to come to terms with religion — both as partner and as potential model — much more thoughtfully than it has.

This also means coming to terms with the specifically religious character of much of the conservative right's solidarity, which is one of the reasons the right has been able to mobilize working-class constituencies the left has lost. Inouye's Uncle Gerald is a real figure: a libertarian-leaning Mormon doctor who served his community fiercely, distrusted institutions, and ultimately died because the religious-political package he had inherited included epistemological commitments that proved fatal. The left's task is not to replicate the right's package, obviously. But it is also not to pretend that there is no package — that solidarity can be built on dispositions alone, without the thick metaphysical commitments that have historically anchored every solidarity worth the name.

I do not have a clean conclusion. Religion is hard. Most secular leftists are not going to become religious, and I am not arguing that they should. But the question of how to build solidarity at the scale and durability the left needs is, at bottom, a question about what holds people together across difference over time, and history is fairly clear that religion has done this better than almost any other human institution. We can refuse this fact, or we can grapple with it. Inouye's essay grapples partially. I am grateful for that, and I want to encourage her, and us, to grapple further.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'social endurance' observed in religious communities is inseparable from metaphysical beliefs about salvation and eternity, which secular voluntary organizations cannot easily replicate.
  2. 02Historical movements that achieved high levels of solidarity, such as early Marxism or the Civil Rights Movement, succeeded largely because they utilized quasi-religious narratives or prophetic theological traditions.
  3. 03Secular solidarity is inherently thinner than religious solidarity, meaning the left must either lower its expectations for secular movements or find ways to create more robust quasi-religious rituals and narratives.
  4. 04Building a powerful multiracial working-class coalition requires the secular left to stop treating religious institutions with suspicion and instead engage them as essential partners that possess unique resources for long-term commitment.
  5. 05The American right's success in mobilizing working-class constituencies is due to its ability to offer a cohesive religious-political 'package' that provides a stronger basis for solidarity than the left's focus on mere dispositions.
