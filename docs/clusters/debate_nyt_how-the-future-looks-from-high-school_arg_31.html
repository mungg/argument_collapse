"High school seniors view the future with skepticism because they recognize that personal effort alone cannot guarantee success in a system that feels increasingly unfair and broken."

If today’s seniors sound skeptical, who can blame them? They have grown up watching institutions fail upward. Banks were rescued. Homeowners were foreclosed on. Politicians blamed one another. Tuition kept rising. Now students are told that if they work hard, play by the rules and borrow responsibly, everything will probably work out.

Probably is doing a lot of work in that sentence.

The Occupy Wall Street protests have resonated with some young people not because every teenager has a detailed theory of finance, but because they sense an unfairness in the air. They are asked to compete ferociously for admission to colleges that may leave them in debt, then compete again for jobs that may not exist. Meanwhile, many of the adults who designed the system seem insulated from its consequences.

A senior looking ahead in 2011 may not reject ambition. He may simply reject the sermon that personal effort alone explains success. Effort matters. So do wages, tuition, family wealth, health insurance and the luck of graduating in a recession.

Students do not need cynicism. But they do need honesty. The future looks more believable when adults admit that the game needs repair.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Students have witnessed a failure of institutional accountability where powerful entities were rescued while ordinary people suffered the consequences of economic instability.
  2. 02The rising cost of tuition and the threat of significant debt make the traditional path to success feel like an unreliable gamble.
  3. 03Young people sense a fundamental unfairness in being forced to compete for dwindling opportunities while the architects of the system remain insulated from its flaws.
  4. 04Seniors are increasingly aware that external factors like family wealth, health insurance, and economic timing are just as influential as individual hard work.
  5. 05A believable future for students requires adults to honestly acknowledge that the current socioeconomic system needs structural repair.
