"The failure of the U.S. mission in Afghanistan stems from a mistaken obsession with building a centralized state, and success requires shifting to a decentralized strategy that works with existing local forms of authority and governance."

Rosen’s essay demolishes one version of American ambition in Afghanistan: the dream that a centralized state in Kabul, supported by a national army and police force, can extend uniform authority into Helmand, Kandahar, Wardak, Kunduz, and the thousands of villages where Afghans actually live. On that point he is convincing. But his conclusion risks throwing out a more plausible political project along with the impossible one. The problem is not that Afghanistan must remain ungoverned. The problem is that the United States has mistaken government for centralization.

Afghanistan has never been simply “without” order. It has had empires, monarchs, communist administrators, mujahideen commanders, Taliban courts, tribal councils, religious authorities, irrigation customs, market networks, and informal systems of arbitration. Some have been brutal; some have been effective; many have been both. The post-2001 international project erred by treating these local forms of authority as obstacles to be bypassed or instruments to be bought. Billions went to ministries, contractors, warlords, and security forces, while the actual political bargains that structure village life remained poorly understood.

Rosen is right that counterinsurgency cannot succeed if it means foreign soldiers occasionally calling a shura and then returning to base. But the failure of that caricature does not mean politics is impossible. It means the political unit must be smaller, more local, and less obsessed with the symbols of sovereignty. The United States and its allies should stop asking how Kabul can control Afghanistan and start asking how Afghans in particular districts can achieve tolerable order.

Consider the road in Helmand. For NATO, Highway 601 is a supply route. For police, it is a dangerous workplace and perhaps an opportunity for bribes. For the Taliban, it is a place to plant IEDs and demonstrate power. For villagers, it is connected to water, markets, fields, and night-time intimidation. A national plan to “secure the road” will fail if it does not address who profits from checkpoints, who adjudicates land and water disputes, which families are aligned with which commanders, and whether people believe informing the police will get them killed. These are local political questions. No number of PowerPoint slides about Afghan National Security Force growth can answer them.

A decentralized strategy would begin with humility about Kabul. The central government should control foreign policy, currency, major infrastructure, and some national security functions. But district governance should rely more heavily on elected or selected local councils, customary authorities, and negotiated power-sharing. This is not a romantic appeal to “tribes.” Rosen rightly warns that Americans overlearned the wrong lesson from Iraq and imagined that Afghan society could be managed by paying tribal sheikhs. Afghanistan’s local structures vary enormously. In some places tribes matter; in others commanders, mullahs, businessmen, or party networks matter more. The point is not to impose tribalism but to discover actual authority.

The most urgent reform concerns justice. Afghans often turn to the Taliban not because they love medieval punishments but because Taliban courts can be faster and less corrupt than government courts. A farmer with a land dispute may prefer harsh arbitration to endless bribery. If the United States wants to weaken the insurgency, it should care less about the number of police trained and more about whether ordinary disputes can be resolved without extortion. That means supporting district-level mediation, mobile courts where feasible, legal aid, and oversight of prosecutors and police. It also means tolerating some customary mechanisms while drawing lines against practices that violate basic rights, especially violence against women and minorities. This is hard, but it is closer to Afghan reality than building courthouses that no one trusts.

Security should likewise be localized but not privatized blindly. The Afghan police described by Rosen are a disaster: underpaid, drug-addicted, illiterate, frightened, and predatory. Expanding such a force multiplies the problem. But a purely national army cannot police every village. Community defense arrangements may be necessary in some areas, provided they are tied to local councils, paid transparently, registered, and subject to removal. The risk of militia abuse is obvious; Afghanistan’s recent history is full of armed men who began as defenders and became predators. Yet the current system already empowers predators in uniforms. The issue is not whether local armed power will exist. It already does. The issue is whether it can be constrained by local accountability and national oversight.

International aid also needs decentralization. Large projects enrich contractors and feed corruption. Small projects can also be captured, but they are easier for communities to monitor. Irrigation repairs, seed distribution, clinic support, teacher salaries, and road maintenance should be negotiated with local councils and publicly accounted for. Aid should not be tied mechanically to counterinsurgency “hot spots.” Rosen notes the perverse incentive: peaceful areas feel punished because violent areas receive more money. A serious development policy would reward communities that maintain access and accountability, not only those where NATO has recently fought.

What about the Taliban? A localist strategy must include negotiation. The movement is not monolithic. Some commanders are ideological hardliners; some are linked to Pakistani sanctuaries; others are local men seeking leverage, revenge, income, or protection. Reintegration programs that ask fighters simply to surrender to a corrupt government will fail. But local ceasefires, prisoner exchanges, guarantees against abusive police, and inclusion in district councils may peel away some factions. The terms must be clear: no hosting of al Qaeda, no attacks on schools and clinics, no assassinations of civilians, and acceptance of negotiated dispute mechanisms. Many will refuse. But the alternative—defeating all Taliban militarily—is fanciful.

Rosen argues that more troops cannot fix Afghanistan. True, if troops are used to chase insurgents and prop up Kabul’s fiction of control. But troops could still play a temporary role in protecting experiments in local order. The measure of success should not be whether a district flies the national flag. It should be whether farmers can irrigate, merchants can travel, teachers can teach, and armed groups can be kept from monopolizing daily life. If foreign troops cannot support those outcomes within a limited period, they should leave those areas rather than continue performing sovereignty.

This approach will offend both centralizers and withdrawal advocates. Centralizers will say it fragments Afghanistan and weakens the state. But the state is already weak; pretending otherwise strengthens only corruption. Withdrawal advocates will say it keeps America entangled in Afghan politics. That danger is real. A decentralized strategy must therefore be paired with a reduced ambition and a timeline for transferring responsibility. The international role should be facilitation and support, not ownership.

Rosen’s “something from nothing” is a fair description of Washington’s fantasy. But Afghanistan is not nothing. The tragedy is that American policy has often ignored the something that already exists: local authority, local grievances, local bargains, local capacities. A better strategy would not build Afghanistan from scratch. It would stop trying to replace Afghan politics with imported institutions and begin, belatedly, to work with the grain of Afghan society. That may still fail. But it is more plausible than either manufacturing a centralized state or walking away while pretending that no Afghan order is possible.
