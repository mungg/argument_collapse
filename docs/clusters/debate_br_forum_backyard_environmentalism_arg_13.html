Whose Backyard? Environmental Justice and the Limits of Deliberation

Reading "Beyond Backyard Environmentalism" from the perspective of the environmental justice movement is a frustrating experience. The essay is sophisticated, theoretically ambitious, and largely indifferent to questions of race, class, and power that have come to define environmental politics outside its academic frame. I want to use this response to make those questions visible, because if they remain invisible the rolling-rule regime the authors champion will likely deepen rather than alleviate the injustices it ignores.

Let us begin with the framing. The authors open with a celebration of Woburn, Love Canal, and "countless other communities" that have organized against pollution in their neighborhoods. They acknowledge the NIMBY tradition and propose to move beyond it. What goes unmentioned is that the locations of the country's most contaminated sites are not random. Robert Bullard's "Dumping in Dixie" (1990) and his subsequent work, the United Church of Christ's 1987 report on toxic waste and race, the Warren County PCB protests of 1982, and the establishment of EPA's Office of Environmental Equity in 1992—a whole body of scholarship and advocacy has documented that hazardous facilities, contaminated water, polluted air, and incomplete enforcement disproportionately affect communities of color and low-income communities. The 1994 environmental justice executive order acknowledged this at the federal level. None of it appears in the authors' essay.

This omission is not incidental. The "deep user" model the authors propose imagines local communities as effective participants in regulatory deliberation. In practice, the capacity to participate effectively is unequally distributed. Communities with educated, well-resourced, organized residents—the kind of communities that produced the Chesapeake Bay Foundation in 1966 or that staffed the early environmental movement—can play the role the authors describe. Communities that lack these resources cannot. The result of devolving regulatory authority to "local units" will, predictably, be that some local units exercise it powerfully and others do not.

Consider the Southern California HCPs the authors celebrate. The negotiations that produced these plans involved developers, public officials, conservation biologists, and representatives of the major national environmental organizations. Notable for their absence were the predominantly Latino and immigrant communities of inland Southern California, whose neighborhoods bore the brunt of the development the HCPs accommodated, and whose access to the remaining open space the agreements limited. The "exemplary" Southern California HCPs are exemplary in part because they served the interests of relatively privileged constituencies on both sides of the deal. They are not models for communities with different demographic profiles and different bargaining capacities.

The TRI experience presents a more complicated picture. On the positive side, TRI data has empowered some environmental justice organizing. Communities that suspected they were being poisoned could finally document the suspicion. Groups like West Harlem Environmental Action, Concerned Citizens of South Central Los Angeles, the Southwest Network for Environmental and Economic Justice, and many others have used TRI data effectively. On the negative side, the technical complexity of TRI data favors organizations with research capacity, and the reductions TRI has produced have not been evenly distributed. Some studies suggest that releases have declined more in white and affluent areas than in communities of color, though this finding is contested. At a minimum, TRI does not automatically correct disparities; it requires concerted advocacy to do so.

The Massachusetts TURA experience, similarly, has produced substantial reductions in toxics use, but the regulated firms have considerable discretion in which toxics to reduce and where. There is no requirement that reductions be concentrated in the communities most exposed. A facility in Lowell can meet its TURA obligations through reductions that primarily benefit workers and surrounding residents who are already comparatively protected, while leaving more exposed sites elsewhere unchanged.

These observations should not be read as a wholesale rejection of the architecture the authors propose. The capacity-building, planning, and disclosure elements of the rolling-rule regime are, in principle, available to environmental justice communities as much as to anyone. The question is whether they will be available in practice, and what conditions would have to obtain for them to be.

I would suggest four such conditions, none of which the authors adequately address.

First, the rolling-rule regime needs robust national substantive floors that protect vulnerable communities against the local bargains they may lack power to influence. The Endangered Species Act's strict take prohibition is a substantive floor of this kind. The Clean Air Act's National Ambient Air Quality Standards are another. The authors' enthusiasm for negotiated flexibility tends to push against such floors. They should be defending floors more vigorously, on environmental justice grounds.

Second, the regime needs serious resource transfers to underrepresented communities. The TURI in Massachusetts is a model of public capacity-building, but it serves regulated firms, not affected communities. We need a counterpart institution—well-funded, accessible, and organizationally independent of regulated industry—that builds the technical capacity of environmental justice communities to engage with rolling-rule processes. Without such an institution, the "deep user" framework will reproduce existing inequalities in participatory capacity.

Third, the regime needs procedural requirements specifically designed to incorporate marginalized voices. "Public participation" as currently practiced—public meetings, written comments, notice-and-comment rulemaking—is a procedural fiction in most communities. Real participation requires outreach, translation, child care, schedule accommodation, compensation for citizen time. These are mundane requirements, but in their absence "deliberation" is a euphemism for elite negotiation.

Fourth, the regime needs disaggregated outcome monitoring. If the rolling-rule regime is to produce environmentally just outcomes, we need to measure outcomes by community demographics, not just in aggregate. The Chesapeake Bay Program tracks nutrient loads and oyster populations; it does not track exposure disparities among Bay-region residents. The TRI tracks releases; it does not adequately track exposure. Until we monitor for justice, we will not produce it.

I want to be clear: I am not arguing that the rolling-rule regime is intrinsically hostile to environmental justice. I am arguing that it is not intrinsically friendly to it either. Like most institutional forms, it produces outcomes that depend on the surrounding distribution of power and resources. The authors' essay is largely silent about this distribution, which is itself a political choice. To speak of "deep users" and "directly deliberative democracy" without specifying whose participation is deep and whose democracy is direct is to risk romanticizing a process that, in actual practice, can be deeply exclusionary.

There is a deeper problem here about democratic theory. The authors invoke John Dewey and the pragmatist tradition, and they are right that this tradition has powerful resources for thinking about participatory democracy. But Dewey was writing in a society he himself recognized as deeply structured by inequalities of wealth, race, and gender. He did not assume that institutional design alone could overcome those inequalities. The participatory ideal, for Dewey, required a much broader transformation in education, economic security, and the structures of public life. To extract the participatory architecture from this broader project and propose it as a self-sufficient regulatory reform is to risk a thin Deweyanism that delivers process without the substantive equality it presupposes.

The "backyard environmentalism" that produced Love Canal-style activism was, in its original form, a movement of relatively privileged communities defending themselves against contamination. The environmental justice movement has insisted, with some success, on broadening that frame to include the communities historically excluded from it. Any regulatory architecture that aspires to move "beyond" backyard environmentalism must reckon with this history. The essay before us does not.

  2. 02The 'deep user' model assumes a level of technical and organizational resource capacity that is unequally distributed across society, favoring privileged constituencies.
  3. 03Existing experiments like the Southern California Habitat Conservation Plans and the Massachusetts Toxics Use Reduction Act often exclude marginalized voices or fail to concentrate benefits in the most exposed areas.
