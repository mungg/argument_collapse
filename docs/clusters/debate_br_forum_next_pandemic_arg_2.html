Vanilla Claude

"While Sheila Jasanoff’s 'technologies of humility' correctly identify the failures of technocratic hubris, the pandemic's true lesson is that effective response requires rebuilding hollowed-out public institutions and integrating political realism into planning rather than just adopting a more modest epistemic stance."

Sheila Jasanoff's call for "technologies of humility" is a welcome corrective to the technocratic hubris that has marked so much pandemic planning. Her central diagnosis—that the United States and United Kingdom topped the Global Health Security Index and then logged some of the world's worst death tolls—is damning evidence that preparedness frameworks were measuring the wrong things. But I want to press on the argument from two directions. First, humility as Jasanoff describes it risks becoming a virtue too capacious to operationalize. Second, the COVID-19 record suggests that the deeper failure was not epistemic but institutional: a hollowing-out of public capacity that no amount of reflective inquiry could substitute for.

Begin with the GHSI puzzle. Jasanoff is right that the index failed to anticipate the dismal U.S. and U.K. performance, and right that it ignored variables like polarization, federalism, racial inequality, and trust. But notice what this concedes: the relevant unknowns were not really unknown. Political scientists have written for decades about American polarization; sociologists have documented Black communities' justified suspicion of medical institutions since at least the public reckoning with Tuskegee; ethnographers have tracked vaccine hesitancy since the Wakefield episode. The data existed. What was missing was not humility but the willingness of public health planners to treat social science as constitutive of their object rather than as a soft afterthought. That is a different problem from the one Jasanoff names. It is less about cultivating a mindset that respects unknown unknowns and more about which expert communities get a seat at the planning table. Calling for humility risks letting the architects of the playbook off the hook for what they could have known had they bothered to ask.

This connects to my second concern. Compare the United States to Germany, which Jasanoff notes outperformed its GHSI ranking. Germany did not succeed because its planners were epistemically humbler. It succeeded because it possessed a denser public health infrastructure at the Land level, more universal health coverage, higher baseline institutional trust, and a less paralyzing partisan divide over basic public health measures. Vietnam, Taiwan, South Korea, and New Zealand also outperformed wealthier nations, and the common thread is less philosophical disposition than institutional thickness: functioning local public health agencies, intact welfare systems that could support people through quarantine, leaders who treated the virus as a non-partisan threat. Jay Kaufman's remark, which Jasanoff quotes, gets closer to the truth than the humility framing does. The United States did not lack reflective practitioners; Anthony Fauci is many things, but unreflective is not among them. What the United States lacked was a state capable of executing a coherent response across fifty jurisdictions whose governors had political incentives to defect.

Jasanoff's most powerful move is her reversal of time's arrow: humility "pulls possible futures back to show us how to act in the present." Yet this is also where the argument is most slippery. To pull possible futures back, one must imagine them, which is itself a predictive exercise—just with attention to distributive consequences and marginal viewpoints. In that sense, the technologies of humility are not really an alternative to prediction; they are prediction done with a broader cast of characters and a longer list of harms. That is valuable, but it does not escape the basic asymmetry Jasanoff identifies between acting and holding back. It only relocates it: someone still has to decide which possible futures to take seriously and which to set aside as paranoia. The ethnographers who flagged vaccine hesitancy were correct; the activists who claimed in March 2020 that lockdowns would devastate the working poor were also correct; and any number of other Cassandras were wrong about catastrophes that never came. Humility as a procedural commitment does not, by itself, tell us how to weight these voices.

Where I think Jasanoff is most clearly right is in her critique of the militaristic framing that pervades Fineberg's editorial and much of U.S. preparedness culture. "Unified command," "crush the curve," "mobilize the public"—the language assumes a polity that consents in advance to be directed. That polity did not exist on February 1, 2020, and pretending it did meant the resulting plans were rendered useless the moment they encountered partisan resistance. Here humility really does have purchase: a planner alert to the fragility of public consent would have invested heavily in building it during peacetime, would have piloted communications strategies for communities with low institutional trust, would have war-gamed scenarios in which the federal government and key states refused to cooperate. That is not soft-edged ethnographic listening; it is hard-headed political realism, and it is precisely what was absent.

So I would refine Jasanoff's prescription. The pandemic did not reveal a deficit of humility in some abstract epistemic sense. It revealed that our preparedness apparatus was built by people who treated politics, distribution, and consent as exogenous variables when they were in fact the dependent ones. The remedy is to bring social and political analysis inside the planning process as co-equal with epidemiology—and, more demandingly, to rebuild the public institutions without which even the most humble plan has nothing to act through.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The failure of preparedness indices was not due to 'unknown unknowns' but to the exclusion of existing social science data regarding polarization, inequality, and trust from the planning process.
  2. 02International success stories like Germany and New Zealand suggest that 'institutional thickness'—such as universal healthcare and local public health infrastructure—was more decisive than the philosophical disposition of planners.
  3. 03Humility functions as a form of prediction with a broader scope of harms, but it does not solve the fundamental problem of how to weight competing voices or decide which possible futures to prioritize.
  4. 04The militaristic 'unified command' model failed because it treated public consent as a given rather than a fragile political variable that must be actively built and maintained during peacetime.
  5. 05The remedy for future crises is to treat social and political analysis as co-equal with epidemiology and to restore the state's capacity to execute coherent public policy.
Vanilla MiniMax

"While Jasanoff correctly identifies that pandemic preparedness failed due to technocratic hubris and a disregard for political realities, her proposed 'technologies of humility' remain an underspecified moral aspiration that lacks the necessary institutional and structural framework to guide actual governance."

Jasanoff's diagnosis of pandemic preparedness is largely persuasive, but her proposed remedy—technologies of humility—risks becoming another technocratic framework that fails the very test she sets for others. Her argument that preparedness failed because it ignored politics, social dynamics, and the limits of knowledge is compelling when applied to the GHSI and Fineberg's wartime mobilization rhetoric. Yet the concept of humility she advances, while rhetorically attractive, remains frustratingly underspecified as a guide to action. If we have learned anything from the pandemic, it is that appeals to moral sensibility, however well-intentioned, require institutional scaffolding to translate into effective governance.

The lead essay rightly indicts the hubris embedded in preparedness frameworks that treated pandemics as logistics problems rather than political phenomena. The GHSI's assumption that all nations would prioritize collective action over domestic politics proved disastrously naive, and Fineberg's vision of unified command bore no resemblance to the fragmented federalism that actually characterized the U.S. response. Jasanoff is correct that these frameworks failed to ask the questions that mattered most: whose perceptions were systematically ignored, and what protections existed for those most vulnerable to policy failure?

Yet the technologies of humility, as presented, offer more aspiration than operational guidance. Jasanoff identifies several institutional mechanisms she believes would help: greater citizen participation, incorporating memory and experience, attending to concerns for justice. These are admirable goals, but the essay provides little insight into how such mechanisms would function under the pressures of an actual emergency. During a pandemic, decision-makers face collapsing information environments, competing claims on scarce resources, and political incentives that reward action over deliberation. Asking officials to adopt "the perspectives of those who are acted upon" is sensible in the abstract, but it does not resolve the fundamental challenge of who decides which perspectives matter when time and capacity are constrained.

This gap matters because the alternative to technocratic overconfidence is not simply more humility but better institutions for democratic deliberation under crisis conditions. The pandemic revealed that meaningful public engagement with science was possible at scales that seemed unimaginable in 2019—the proliferation of community-led testing sites, mutual aid networks, and grassroots contact tracing efforts demonstrated that citizens could participate meaningfully in pandemic response when given the tools and information to do so. Yet Jasanoff's framework offers no analysis of how such participation could be institutionalized in advance of the next crisis, rather than improvised in desperation.

The essay's most penetrating insight concerns the politics of public health sovereignty. Jasanoff observes that securing public health requires people to sacrifice aspects of liberty for the common good, and that this political project cannot be reduced to technical expertise. Her critique of preparedness frameworks for assuming away this reality is sharp. Yet having established that public health is irreducibly political, her argument seems to retreat into a technocratic mode of its own, offering "humility" as a substitute for the harder work of building political coalitions capable of sustaining difficult interventions across polarized societies. The Japanese government's mask distribution, which Jasanoff mentions approvingly, succeeded not because of humility but because Japanese political culture supported cohesive collective action—a precondition that cannot be conjured into existence by invoking Lear on the heath.

This is not to deny the value of Jasanoff's critique. The failure to anticipate vaccine hesitancy, the disregard for racial disparities in infection and mortality, the assumption that the public would defer to unified expert guidance—all represent genuine epistemic failures that better integration of diverse perspectives might have mitigated. But humility is a disposition, not a policy. What the next pandemic will require is not merely a change in mindset among policymakers but structural reforms that embed democratic participation into public health systems before crises arrive. The technologies of humility, to become more than a slogan, must specify who participates, how their input shapes decisions, and what accountability mechanisms ensure that marginalized perspectives actually influence outcomes. Until humility acquires such institutional teeth, it risks remaining a comfortable abstraction that absolves intellectuals of the harder work of political reconstruction.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Jasanoff’s critique of existing preparedness models is persuasive in highlighting how they naively treated pandemics as logistical problems rather than complex political and social phenomena.
  2. 02The concept of 'humility' functions more as a personal disposition than a functional policy, failing to provide operational guidance for decision-makers facing collapsing information environments during an emergency.
  3. 03The essay fails to explain how citizen participation and justice concerns can be institutionalized to withstand the pressures of a crisis rather than being improvised in desperation.
  4. 04By offering humility as a remedy for political fragmentation, Jasanoff risks creating a new technocratic abstraction that avoids the difficult work of building the political coalitions and structural reforms needed for collective action.
  5. 05To be effective, the 'technologies of humility' must move beyond rhetoric to specify concrete accountability mechanisms that ensure marginalized perspectives actually influence policy outcomes.
Vanilla Gemini

"The failure of pandemic policy was not the pursuit of preparedness itself, but a narrow, technocratic definition of it that ignored the social and political realities of a fractured society."

Sheila Jasanoff’s critique of prepandemic public policy exposes a profound and fatal flaw in modern global governance: the assumption that a biological crisis can be managed purely through logistical and technocratic control, devoid of political and social context. She is entirely correct that instruments like the Global Health Security Index (GHSI) and the Obama administration’s playbook operated under the illusion of a compliant, unified polity. They measured laboratory capacity and stockpile volumes, but entirely missed the crucial metrics of social trust, political polarization, and historical marginalization. However, in diagnosing this failure, Jasanoff draws a stark dichotomy between the “heroic” posture of preparedness and the “unheroic but more ethical” stance of humility and precaution. While her call for “technologies of humility” is vital, positioning humility as an alternative to preparedness risks leaving us philosophically enriched but operationally paralyzed. The solution is not to abandon preparedness, but to drastically expand its definition.

Jasanoff argues that preparedness is a “can-do posture, bravely denying the possibility of defeat,” contrasting it with humility, which “admits that defeat is possible.” But this framing mischaracterizes the true failure of our pandemic response. The planners behind the GHSI did not fail because they were preparing; they failed because they were preparing for the wrong disaster. They prepared for a pathogen invading a sterile laboratory, rather than a virus infiltrating a deeply fractured, unequal society. The failure of Harvey Fineberg’s call to “crush the curve” by June 2020 was not a failure of the concept of a unified command, but a failure to recognize that the United States lacked the social cohesion required to execute it. In this sense, the problem was not hubris in the face of nature, but profound ignorance of our own civic fragility.

Furthermore, we must be careful not to discard the very real triumphs of prediction and preparedness in our rush to embrace precaution. The rapid development of mRNA vaccines—undeniably the most significant victory of the last nineteen months—was a masterclass in predictive science and robust, risk-taking preparedness, exemplified by the financial architecture of Operation Warp Speed. If policymakers had applied a rigid precautionary principle—which demands we hold back in the absence of full scientific certainty—the deployment of novel vaccine platforms might have been delayed by years. In this instance, a “heroic” posture saved countless lives. The tragedy of late 2021 is not that we developed these vaccines too aggressively, but that our scientific preparedness vastly outpaced our sociological preparedness. We engineered a medical miracle but failed to anticipate the deep-seated vaccine hesitancy, fueled by historic institutional distrust and partisan division, that currently prevents us from defeating the Delta variant and bracing for the newly identified Omicron threat.

This is precisely where Jasanoff’s “technologies of humility” must be integrated, not as an alternative to preparedness, but as its foundational layer. Humility asks us to look at the margins, to remember past injustices, and to anticipate human behavior. If we had applied this lens in 2019, an honest assessment of American preparedness would have recognized that the nation’s healthcare disparities—which guarantee that communities of color and low-wage workers will suffer disproportionately—are a catastrophic vulnerability. A truly comprehensive GHSI would not just measure hospital beds; it would measure the public’s willingness to wear masks. It would assess the vulnerability of supply chains when citizens inevitably panic-buy, and it would calculate the profound economic and educational impacts on single mothers when schools are shuttered.

To bridge the gap between Jasanoff’s critique and the realities of public administration, we need a paradigm of "socio-political preparedness." This approach acknowledges Jasanoff’s insight that “securing public health and public safety requires human beings to give up aspects of their liberty,” but it actively plans for the resistance that such sacrifices will inevitably provoke. It does not simply wring its hands over the unpredictability of human nature; it maps the contours of our social fractures just as epidemiologists map the genome of a novel virus.

As we look toward the inevitable next crisis, we cannot rely on precaution alone to save us. A society that only practices precaution may find itself too hesitant to act when rapid mobilization is required. Instead, we must forge a more mature, clear-eyed version of preparedness. We must remain ready for "any old thing," as Baden-Powell suggested, but we must finally accept that the most dangerous and unpredictable variable in any crisis is not the pathogen itself, but the deeply flawed, deeply divided human beings it infects.

  2. 02The success of mRNA vaccines demonstrates that aggressive, predictive science and 'heroic' preparedness are essential for saving lives during a crisis.
  3. 03The primary failure of pandemic planning was an ignorance of civic fragility, such as social distrust and political polarization, rather than an overestimation of scientific control.
  4. 04A rigid application of the precautionary principle risks operational paralysis and could prevent the rapid mobilization necessary to address urgent threats.
  5. 05Future governance must adopt a paradigm of 'socio-political preparedness' that maps social fractures and human behavior with the same rigor used for biological pathogens.
