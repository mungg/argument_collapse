"The government has increased wildfire risk by failing to address climate change through energy policy and must now prioritize greenhouse gas reductions to prevent the fire environment from worsening."

The central government failure is not that it has put out too many campfires. It is that it has allowed the atmosphere to become a more dangerous fire environment. A warmer climate does not strike the match in every blaze, but it dries vegetation, lengthens the fire season and increases the odds that a spark becomes a crown fire. The recent attention to heat waves is not a distraction from wildfire policy; it is wildfire policy.

Fuel treatments have a role, especially near communities. But no thinning crew can thin the atmosphere. If summers grow hotter and droughts become more severe, the scale of the problem will outrun chainsaws and fire lines. The West is already seeing earlier snowmelt and longer fire seasons. Firefighters are being asked to solve with courage what elected officials have refused to solve with energy policy.

Government has increased risk by failing to price carbon pollution, by subsidizing fossil fuels, and by allowing land-use decisions that put subdivisions in flammable landscapes. It can reduce risk by doing the reverse: adopt serious greenhouse gas limits, invest in clean energy, and require communities to plan for the climate they are actually entering.

No policy can “prevent” wildfires in a fire-adapted landscape. But policy can prevent a hot future from becoming hotter still. Treating climate change as a separate debate from Colorado’s burning hillsides is a convenient fiction.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A warming climate exacerbates fire conditions by drying vegetation, lengthening fire seasons, and increasing the intensity of blazes.
  2. 02Traditional fuel treatments and thinning efforts are insufficient to combat the large-scale environmental changes driven by rising temperatures and severe droughts.
  3. 03Government failure to price carbon and its continued subsidization of fossil fuels have directly contributed to a more dangerous fire landscape.
  4. 04Current land-use policies have increased risk by allowing residential development in areas that are naturally prone to burning.
  5. 05Effective wildfire prevention requires integrating climate policy with land-use planning to prepare communities for a hotter future.
