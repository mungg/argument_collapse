The Color of "the People": Race and the Limits of Populist Reclamation

Adom Getachew's framing of this forum is honest about something many recent celebrations of populism have not been: that any project to "reclaim" populism in the United States must confront the persistence of white supremacy as a structuring force in American political life. This honesty is welcome. But I want to argue that the persistence of white supremacy is not merely an external problem that a reclaimed populism must overcome; it is woven into the historical fabric of American populism itself in ways that should give us serious pause about whether reclamation is the right metaphor at all. At the same time, I want to argue that the response to this difficulty is not to abandon mass politics in the name of an enlightened pluralism, but to take seriously a different genealogy — one that runs through Black radical, abolitionist, and anticolonial thought — that has offered its own theories of "the people" without falling into the racial traps of producerist populism. The question is not whether to build a mass democratic politics; the question is which traditions we are building from.

Begin with the historical record. The People's Party of the 1890s is the canonical American reference point for populism, and the canonical story about it has long been ambivalent. On one side: the Southern Farmers' Alliance, the early speeches of Tom Watson, the brief moment when Black and white tenant farmers could imagine a common political project against the merchants, railroads, and bankers who were extracting their labor and land. On the other side: the rapid collapse of that interracial moment under the pressure of the Democratic Party's white supremacist counterattack, the subsequent embrace by many former Populists of disenfranchisement and lynching as instruments of "reform," and the trajectory of Watson himself, who ended his life as a senator notorious for anti-Black, anti-Catholic, and antisemitic agitation. C. Vann Woodward's classic biography of Watson presented this as tragedy: a man and a movement betrayed by circumstance. More recent historians — Charles Postel sympathetically, Lawrence Goodwyn movingly, but also critics like Edward Ayers and Steven Hahn — have offered more textured accounts in which the racial limits of the movement were not external impositions but internal features. The Populist appeal to the "producing classes" against "parasites" was always racially structured, even when its formal language was inclusive, because the categories of producer and parasite mapped onto racially coded distinctions in the agrarian South. White yeomen could imagine themselves as producers because Black sharecroppers and tenants were always available as either invisible labor or threatening competition.

This pattern is not specific to the 1890s. Michael Kazin's work on the populist persuasion in American politics traces a recurring rhythm in which mass mobilizations against concentrated economic power tend, in the American context, to articulate themselves through racial idioms of producerism and to fracture along racial lines whenever they confront the choice between cross-racial coalition and white solidarity. The labor movement of the late nineteenth century, the Townsend movement of the 1930s, the followings of Huey Long and Father Coughlin, the Wallace campaigns of 1968 and 1972 — each in different ways illustrates how easily American populism slips its multiracial moorings. This is not because Americans are uniquely racist (they are not), but because the political economy of the United States has, since its founding, been structured by racial extraction in ways that make racially neutral mass politics extraordinarily difficult to sustain. Producerism in a settler colonial slave society is never just producerism.

Getachew is too sophisticated a thinker not to know this, and her essay nods at the problem. But the rhetorical project of "reclaiming populism" tends, in practice, to underweight this difficulty. Reclamation implies that there is a usable kernel of populist tradition that can be extracted from its racial entanglements and reactivated in a new form. I want to suggest that the metaphor of reclamation is misleading, and that a more honest framing would be construction — the construction of a new mass politics that draws selectively on multiple traditions, including some that have historically been excluded from the category of "populist" altogether.

Consider, for instance, the political thought of W. E. B. Du Bois. Black Reconstruction in America (1935) is in many ways the most ambitious theory of multiracial democracy ever produced on American soil, and it offers a vision of mass political agency — the "general strike" of enslaved people that helped bring down the Confederacy, the brief flowering of interracial democratic governance in the Reconstruction South — that does not rely on the populist binary of people-versus-elite. Instead, Du Bois offers a theory of political agency grounded in the practical activity of the formerly enslaved themselves, building schools, churches, militias, and political organizations under conditions of extreme constraint. The "people" in Du Bois is not an empty signifier waiting to be filled by a leader; it is a concrete network of institutions through which Black political agency was exercised and then violently destroyed. The lesson Du Bois draws from this is not that mass politics is dangerous, but that mass politics requires institutional infrastructure and confrontation with the racial state.

Or consider the tradition that runs from Ida B. Wells through Ella Baker to Fannie Lou Hamer — a tradition of Black women's organizing that has explicitly resisted the charismatic, leader-centered model that populism tends to favor. Ella Baker's famous insistence that "strong people don't need strong leaders" was not a piece of rhetorical modesty; it was a substantive theory of how durable mass politics is built. SNCC in its early years, before it was reorganized along more hierarchical lines, was an attempt to enact this theory: decisions made locally, leadership distributed, the work of organizing prioritized over the work of mobilization. This is a different theory of mass democratic politics than the one populism offers, and it has, I would argue, a much better track record of producing durable change. The civil rights movement at its height was the most successful mass political project in American history. It was not a populist movement. Calling it populist would obscure what made it work.

There is a third tradition worth invoking here, which is the tradition of Black internationalism — Du Bois again, but also George Padmore, C. L. R. James, Claudia Jones, Walter Rodney, and many others. This tradition has consistently insisted that the question of who counts as "the people" cannot be answered within national frames alone, because the structures that produce racialized disposability operate at a global scale. Getachew's own scholarly work, especially in Worldmaking After Empire, has reconstructed this tradition with extraordinary care and shown how the anticolonial generation grappled with the question of how to build democratic political subjects across imperial divides. It is striking, then, that the populism literature in the United States rarely engages with this body of thought, which has the most to teach us about the construction of multiracial political solidarity under conditions of extreme inequality. If anything is to be "reclaimed" for a left politics adequate to 2020, it should be this tradition — and "populism" is a misleading name for it.

The COVID-19 moment makes the racial stakes especially clear. Getachew notes the staggering disparities: Black residents of Chicago and Milwaukee dying at rates two to three times their share of the population; the over-representation of people of color in the essential workforce that is now being asked to risk infection without protection; the collapse of immigration detention facilities into petri dishes; the doubling-down on deportations and border closures even as a pandemic respects no border. These are not separate "racial issues" to be managed alongside a populist economic program. They are constitutive of the political economy that COVID has revealed. A politics that treats them as add-ons will not work — not analytically, because it will misdiagnose the problem, and not practically, because it will fail to mobilize the workers of color who make up a growing share of the actually existing American working class.

What does this mean for the project of building a multiracial democratic majority? It means, I think, that the left should be more comfortable using the language of the traditions that have actually built multiracial coalitions and less attached to the language of a tradition that has consistently struggled to do so. The language of solidarity — old-fashioned, perhaps, but specific in its implications — is more useful than the language of populism. The language of abolition, which has been generatively reinvented in the contemporary movement against mass incarceration, is more specific and more honest than the language of people-versus-elite. The language of the commons, which has been developed by both feminist and ecological theorists, is more capacious than the language of "the people." The language of social reproduction, which centers the work — disproportionately done by women of color — that sustains daily life, is more analytically powerful than the producerism that has historically organized populist appeals.

I want to be clear about what I am not arguing. I am not arguing that mass democratic politics is impossible or undesirable in the United States, nor that the project of building a multiracial working-class majority is misguided. On the contrary: I think that project is the only serious political horizon available to the American left. What I am arguing is that the populist tradition is not the best resource for that project, and that reclamation is not the right metaphor for the work required. The work required is constructive, not restorative. It involves drawing on multiple traditions — Black radical, feminist, ecological, internationalist, and yes, some elements of labor and agrarian populism too — to build something genuinely new. The temptation to give that new construction the old name of populism is understandable. The term has a certain insurgent ring; it signals a willingness to break with the technocratic liberalism that has failed; it gestures toward mass mobilization. But the term carries baggage that cannot easily be unpacked, and the baggage is precisely the baggage of racial exclusion that the new construction must overcome.

The Chicago Teachers' Union story that Getachew tells so movingly is, in fact, a story about this kind of construction. The CTU did not "reclaim populism." It built a new kind of unionism — explicitly anti-racist, deeply community-rooted, attentive to the gendered character of teaching as care work, willing to confront the political economy of urban disinvestment as itself a racial project. It allied with Black community organizations, with parent groups, with immigrant rights organizations, with housing activists. When Jeanette Taylor went on hunger strike to save Dyett High School, she was not performing populism; she was performing a specific theory of Black community defense that has its own genealogy in the South Side of Chicago, running back through the welfare rights organizing of the 1960s and 1970s and before that. The political form that emerged in Chicago is something new, and giving it the old name of populism does it a disservice. It deserves its own name. Until we find that name, we should be cautious about the names we attach to it. "Populism" is, I think, not the right one.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01American populist history, from the 1890s People's Party to 20th-century movements, demonstrates that 'producerist' appeals consistently fracture along racial lines or rely on racialized definitions of who belongs to 'the people.'
  2. 02The metaphor of 'reclamation' is misleading because it implies a usable, pure populist kernel exists, whereas the current political task is the 'construction' of something entirely new.
  3. 03Alternative genealogies of mass politics—such as Black radicalism, abolitionism, and internationalism—provide more effective and durable models for multiracial democracy than the populist tradition.
  4. 04The populist focus on charismatic leadership and the people-versus-elite binary ignores the successful, institution-based, and leader-distributed organizing models found in the civil rights movement.
  5. 05Modern progressive successes, like the Chicago Teachers’ Union strikes, are better understood as new forms of anti-racist community unionism rather than a revival of traditional populism.
  6. 06The left should adopt more precise and inclusive frameworks, such as solidarity, abolition, and social reproduction, which better address the racialized political economy revealed by the COVID-19 pandemic.
