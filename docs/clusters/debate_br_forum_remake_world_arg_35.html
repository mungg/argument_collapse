Diversified DeepSeek

"While Walter Johnson correctly identifies reproductive exploitation as the foundation of racial capitalism, a truly transformative politics of justice must bridge the gap between the specific history of enslaved women and the universal moral force of the human rights tradition."

Walter Johnson has done us a service by writing this essay, and the *Boston Review* has done us a service by publishing it. It is a rich, dense, ambitious piece of work that deserves to be read slowly and argued with vigorously. I have learned from it, even where I have disagreed.

I want to focus my response on a single thread that runs through the essay but is never fully pulled: the thread of reproduction. Johnson argues that the history of racial capitalism is, in a crucial sense, a history of reproduction. The enslaved person was both labor and capital, and the reproduction of that dual entity—through the slave trade on one hand, through natural increase on the other—was the hinge on which the entire system swung. “The entire ‘pyramid’ of the Atlantic economy,” he writes, “was founded upon the capacity of enslaved women’s bodies: upon their ability to reproduce capital.” This is a powerful formulation, and it draws on the work of scholars like Deborah Gray White and Angela Davis, who have long insisted that the experience of enslaved women must be at the center of any account of slavery. But Johnson’s treatment of reproduction raises as many questions as it answers, and I want to open some of them up here.

The first question is conceptual. What does Johnson mean by “reproduction”? The term is slippery, and it has a complex history in Marxist and feminist theory. In the Marxist tradition, “reproduction” refers to the processes by which a mode of production maintains itself over time: not just the production of commodities, but the reproduction of the labor force (through food, shelter, child-rearing) and the reproduction of the social relations that make production possible. In the feminist tradition, “reproduction” has been used to highlight the often-invisible work of bearing and raising children, caring for the sick and elderly, and maintaining households—work that is disproportionately done by women and that is systematically devalued under capitalism. These two uses of “reproduction” overlap but are not identical, and Johnson does not always make it clear which sense he has in mind.

When he says that enslaved women’s bodies were the foundation of racial capitalism because of “their ability to reproduce capital,” he seems to mean something like: enslaved women gave birth to children who were themselves enslaved, and those children became the labor force of the next generation of plantations. This is true, and important. But it is also too narrow. Enslaved women did more than give birth. They worked in the fields alongside men; they cooked, cleaned, sewed, and nursed; they raised children not only their own but those of the master’s family; they provided emotional and sexual labor to both white and black communities. All of this work was essential to the functioning of the plantation economy, and all of it is part of what “reproduction” should mean in an adequate account of racial capitalism.

Johnson nods toward this broader sense when he cites J. D. B. DeBow’s argument for reopening the Atlantic slave trade: either import more slaves, DeBow warned, or “await with folded arms the coming of population and of labor which will be the result of natural increase.” The alternative to the slave trade, in other words, was the reproductive labor of enslaved women. This alternative was cheaper than the slave trade—that was the point—but it was not costless. It required the systematic sexual violation of enslaved women, the breaking of family ties, the constant threat of sale that made every mother a potential childless mourner. The violence of slavery was not just about extracting labor from bodies; it was about controlling reproduction, and that control was both a means of economic accumulation and a site of particular, gendered forms of terror.

Johnson is right to insist that this matters for justice. His fourth virtue is that an account of justice rooted in the history of slavery should be “attentive to questions of gender and sexuality, on the ways that reproductive invigilation and natal alienation—the subordination of the social reproduction of one group of people to the purposes of another—were core features of the human wrongs of slavery.” I agree entirely. But I want to press Johnson on what this means in practice. When he calls for reparations, does he mean that reparations should be specifically gendered? Should the descendants of enslaved women receive a different form of compensation than the descendants of enslaved men, to reflect the different forms of violence they suffered? Should there be specific remedies for the ongoing effects of reproductive exploitation, such as the disproportionate rates of maternal mortality among Black women in the United States today? These are not rhetorical questions; they are questions that any serious reparations program will have to address, and they are far from settled within the Movement for Black Lives or the broader left.

There is a deeper issue here, one that connects the question of reproduction to the question of rights that Johnson raises at the beginning of the essay. Johnson is critical of the liberal rights tradition for its abstraction, its universalism, its failure to account for the specific wrongs of slavery. But when it comes to reproduction, the liberal rights tradition has been, historically, one of the most powerful resources for feminist struggle. The right to control one’s own body, to choose whether and when to bear children, to be free from sexual violence and coercion—these are rights claims that have been central to the movement for reproductive justice, a movement that was pioneered by women of color precisely because they understood that reproductive oppression was inseparable from racial and economic oppression. The name of that movement is important: reproductive *justice*, not just reproductive rights. The shift from “rights” to “justice” signals a recognition that formal legal equality is not enough, that the conditions under which women make reproductive decisions—the availability of health care, the absence of coercion, the presence of social support—are as important as the decisions themselves. This is, in many ways, exactly the kind of justice that Johnson is calling for: historically grounded, attentive to the intersections of race and class, focused on the material conditions of life rather than on abstract entitlements.

And yet Johnson seems to want to distance himself from the language of rights entirely, even as his own argument depends on a notion of bodily integrity and self-determination that is, at bottom, a rights claim. When he describes the violation of enslaved women’s bodies as a core wrong of slavery, he is appealing to a standard that goes beyond the particular experience of the enslaved and calls on a principle that is, I would argue, universal. The principle is this: to use a person’s body against their will for the purposes of another is a fundamental violation of that person’s dignity. This principle is not the exclusive property of the liberal tradition; it can be found in many cultures and many philosophies. But it has been articulated most clearly and defended most systematically within the rights tradition, and I do not think we should abandon that tradition just because it has been historically allied with imperialism and capitalism.

The challenge, then, is not to choose between the history of slavery and the language of rights, or between particularism and universalism. The challenge is to hold them together—to use the history of slavery to expose the gaps and silences in rights discourse, and to use rights discourse to give moral force to the demands that emerge from the history of slavery. Reproductive justice is a model for how this can be done. It begins with the specific experiences of women of color—their histories of forced sterilization, of coerced contraception, of inadequate health care, of family separation—and it uses those experiences to articulate demands that are framed in the language of rights while also challenging the limits of that language. The result is a politics that is both radical and legible, both particular and universal, both historical and forward-looking.

Johnson’s essay is a call for such a politics, even if it does not fully achieve it. He is right that the history of slavery is a standpoint from which we can see the inadequacies of liberal justice more clearly. He is right that reproductive exploitation was a core feature of racial capitalism and must be a core concern of any movement for repair. What remains is to do the work of translation: to turn the history into a politics, the critique into a program, the memory of the dead into a promise to the living. That work is not yet done, and Johnson’s essay is an invitation to take it up. I am grateful for the invitation, even as I recognize how much remains to be done.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Johnson’s definition of reproduction is too narrow, as it focuses primarily on the biological creation of capital while overlooking the broader essential labor enslaved women performed to maintain the plantation economy.
  2. 02The gendered nature of reproductive violence under slavery necessitates that any serious program for reparations or restorative justice include specific remedies for the unique exploitation suffered by enslaved women.
  3. 03The liberal rights tradition, despite its flaws, provides the necessary conceptual framework for bodily integrity and self-determination that underpins the moral critique of reproductive violation.
  4. 04The 'reproductive justice' framework serves as a superior model for political action because it successfully synthesizes the historical particularity of racial oppression with the universalizing language of rights.
