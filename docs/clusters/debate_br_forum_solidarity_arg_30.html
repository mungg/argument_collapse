A Note on Henry

I keep returning to Henry. Mie Inouye's essay frames her encounter with him as a moment of clarifying chastening — a first-generation Asian American organizer from a working-class background, asking the fourth-generation Asian American professor why she organizes, and getting an answer that strikes them both, in different ways, as insufficient. Inouye's eventual response, threaded through the essay, is roughly: Henry's material-interest framing is partial because organizing is also about meetings, ideology, and the inscrutable mixture of motives that bring real people into political life. I think this response, while honest, does Henry a kind of subtle disservice that I want to try to name.

Henry, on the essay's account, said he organizes because of "the material changes my family needs to see to our lives in order to survive." This is the answer of someone for whom organizing is not a chosen vocation but a necessity — someone whose presence in the room is conditioned by stakes that he cannot easily set aside. He is not organizing for the meetings; he is organizing because his family's survival, in some real sense, depends on the political outcomes the meetings might produce. Inouye records this exchange honestly, and acknowledges feeling chastened. But the essay then proceeds to make Henry's answer one option among several, and to develop a framework — endurance, humility, the cultivation of dispositions — that has very little to say about people in Henry's situation.

I want to ask: who is the addressee of Inouye's essay? Whom is the prescription of "social endurance" addressed to?

Read carefully, the essay's prescription is addressed primarily to people who have a choice about whether to keep showing up. The whole apparatus of cultivating endurance, of anticipating annoyance and heartbreak, of interpreting discomfort as transformation, assumes a participant whose attachment to the movement is voluntary and whose departure is a real option. Henry, on his own account, does not have this kind of optionality. His family needs the material changes. He shows up because he has to, and the relevant question for him is not whether to endure but how to win.

This is not a small matter. Most of the left writing about solidarity in our moment is, I think, addressed implicitly to people in Inouye's position — to the relatively secure, well-educated, ideologically motivated participant for whom showing up is an act of conviction rather than necessity. The advice we give this participant is sensible: be humble, stay in the room, do not retreat into deference, do not romanticize purity, allow yourself to be transformed. But this advice does not describe what Henry needs from the left, and it does not describe what the left needs from itself in order to be useful to people like Henry.

What does Henry need? At a minimum, he needs the movement to win things. Not necessarily the revolution, not necessarily even big things, but concrete material gains that demonstrate, in his life and in the lives of people like him, that the movement is capable of changing the conditions of life. A movement that cannot win small things will not win big things, and a movement that wins nothing has no claim on the loyalty of people whose participation is conditioned by material stakes.

Henry also needs the movement to be honest about the asymmetry between participants like him and participants like Inouye. The essay registers this asymmetry briefly — the contrast between Henry's reasons and Inouye's own — but does not develop it. What would it mean to develop it? It would mean acknowledging that movements that depend on the participation of people in Henry's situation owe those people something more than dispositions. They owe them strategic seriousness, accountability, and a credible theory of victory.

This is where I want to push Inouye's essay hardest. The essay's affective register — the meditative tone, the willingness to dwell in ambiguity, the move toward "social endurance" as a near-virtue — is the register of someone for whom organizing is, in the end, a chosen and meaningful form of life. This is not wrong; there is real wisdom in this register, and Inouye captures it better than most. But the register can drift, almost imperceptibly, into a kind of consolation — a way of making peace with the limits of the moment that may not serve the people whose stakes are higher than ours.

I worry about this drift in part because I have seen it in myself. I am a left academic, and the temptations of the meditative-consolatory register are well known to me. We write about solidarity in ways that make our own participation feel meaningful even when the movement is losing. We theorize the value of endurance in ways that make our own continued involvement feel virtuous even when the strategic case for our involvement is unclear. We praise epistemic humility in ways that absolve us from the harder work of forming judgments and acting on them. The genre's gravitational pull is toward the affirmation of the participant who is already in the room rather than toward the harder question of how to win things for those who cannot leave.

Henry, I suspect, does not need an essay on endurance. He needs the movement to develop a strategy capable of delivering the material changes his family needs. The essay he might write would probably look different from Inouye's — sharper, more impatient, more focused on outcomes than on dispositions. This is not because Henry is less reflective; it is because the situation he describes himself as being in does not permit the leisure of meditative ambivalence.

I do not say this to dismiss Inouye's essay. The essay is honest about its own position, which is more than most essays in this genre are. The exchange with Henry is reported faithfully, and the chastening it produced is real. What I am saying is that the essay's resolution of the chastening — its move toward endurance and humility as the central virtues — may be a resolution available to Inouye in her position that would be less available, and less useful, to Henry in his.

The deeper question Inouye's essay raises but does not fully confront is: what does the left look like when it takes its accountability to people in Henry's situation as its first commitment? I suspect it looks more strategic, more materialist, more impatient with the affective genres of meditation and consolation, more focused on the concrete tasks of building power and winning fights. It looks more like what Inouye sees in the Bob Lee footage — an organizer with a plan, working a room toward a specific objective — and less like what she gestures at in her own classroom, where the question is what it would take for her students to know why they are taking her class.

I admire Inouye for asking that question of her students. I admire her for asking it of herself in the exchange with Henry. I just wish the essay gave Henry the last word, and let his answer shape the analysis more decisively than it does. Solidarity now, in the sense that matters most, is solidarity with Henry — not as a figure to be understood and integrated into our reflections but as a participant whose stakes are higher than ours and whose needs set the terms of what we should be doing. The essay points in this direction. I hope its readers will go further.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The prescription of 'social endurance' assumes a participant has the optionality to leave, whereas those facing material survival cannot afford to treat organizing as a voluntary exercise in personal transformation.
  2. 02By framing material interest as just one of many opaque motivations for organizing, the essay performs a disservice to those whose presence in the movement is conditioned by high-stakes survival.
  3. 03A movement that prioritizes the meditative and consolatory register of the professional-managerial class risks absolving itself from the harder work of developing a credible theory of victory.
  4. 04True solidarity with the working class requires a shift from focusing on the internal dispositions of participants toward strategic seriousness and the delivery of concrete material gains.
  5. 05The essay’s resolution of conflict through endurance is a luxury of the relatively secure that fails to address the accountability movements owe to those who need immediate social change to survive.
