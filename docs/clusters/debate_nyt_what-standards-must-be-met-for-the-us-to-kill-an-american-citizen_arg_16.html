"The U.S. government should only be permitted to kill its own citizens in immediate emergencies where a threat is currently occurring or plainly about to happen, as established by public legal standards rather than secret executive review."

The standard should be almost impossible to meet. The United States should not assassinate its own citizens except in the same kind of immediate emergency in which a police officer may shoot an armed suspect: to stop a threat that is happening or plainly about to happen. A secret prediction that a citizen is dangerous is not enough.

Anwar al-Awlaki was no civil libertarian’s dream defendant. He preached hatred of the United States and was accused of far more than speech. But constitutional rights are not tested by sympathetic cases. They are tested when the target is despised and the government says, “Trust us.” If the executive can kill a hated citizen abroad today on secret evidence, the principle will not remain limited to the most frightening facts. Powers granted in fear tend to expand.

The phrase “informed, high-level official” is chillingly inadequate. Which official? Informed by whom? Using what evidence? Applying what definition of “imminent”? The leaked white paper suggests that imminence may not require knowledge of a specific pending attack. That drains the word of meaning. Many criminals, gang leaders and extremists are dangerous in a continuing sense. We do not kill them from the sky because arrest is difficult.

The Constitution provides a way to deal with traitors: charge them, try them and, if convicted, punish them. If a citizen is abroad, seek extradition or capture. If he is on a real battlefield, the battlefield rules apply. But Yemen, Pakistan and Somalia cannot become Constitution-free zones simply because drones can reach them.

The killing of a citizen also harms the public’s right to know what its government is doing in its name. Secret evidence may sometimes be necessary; secret legal standards are not. No one is asking that operational details be broadcast to Al Qaeda. But the legal rule governing the executive’s claimed power to kill Americans must be public.

Due process means the government must prove its case before it imposes the ultimate penalty. Internal executive review is not due process. It is an internal memo with lethal consequences. A republic should be more afraid of that precedent than of the inconvenience of following its own Constitution.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The executive branch's use of secret evidence and vague definitions of 'imminence' creates a dangerous precedent that allows government power to expand unchecked.
  2. 02The Constitution requires that citizens accused of treason be afforded due process through public trials and convictions rather than extrajudicial assassination.
  3. 03Internal executive review does not constitute due process because it lacks the independent oversight and transparency required to protect constitutional rights.
  4. 04The government must make the legal rules governing the targeted killing of Americans public to ensure accountability and uphold the public's right to know.
  5. 05Geographic distance or the convenience of drone technology does not justify treating foreign countries as zones where constitutional protections no longer apply.
