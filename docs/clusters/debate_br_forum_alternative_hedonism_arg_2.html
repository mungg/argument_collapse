"While Kate Soper correctly identifies the flaws of consumerism, her 'alternative hedonism' fails by treating technological growth and material abundance as capitalist misdirections rather than essential tools for a democratic and effective climate transition."

Kate Soper is right that the climate debate has too often been trapped between denial and reluctant austerity. She is also right that GDP is a crude proxy for well-being, that advertising manipulates desire, and that much of modern life is uglier and more stressful than it needs to be. But her alternative hedonism goes wrong when it treats growth, technology, speed, and abundance as if they were mostly artifacts of capitalist misdirection rather than deep human achievements and, in many cases, moral necessities.

There is a danger in making consumer society too easy a villain. People do not want warm homes, convenient food, rapid communication, labor-saving appliances, cars, or vacations simply because corporations taught them to. They want them because these things answer real human needs: comfort, mobility, curiosity, status, relief from drudgery, connection to distant loved ones, escape from local constraint. Some desires are manufactured; many are not. A politics that begins by telling people that their pleasures are mostly ersatz will struggle to remain democratic, however gently it speaks.

The strongest climate politics will not be a politics of less. It will be a politics of better abundance: abundant clean energy, abundant housing in walkable places, abundant public transit, abundant childcare, abundant medical care, abundant time made possible by productivity rather than by stagnation. Soper wants to rescue pleasure from consumerism. Good. But the rescue will require more technological capacity, more construction, more innovation, and in many sectors more growth—not an overall retreat from them.

Consider energy. The world still contains hundreds of millions of people without reliable electricity and billions whose energy use is far below what people in rich countries take for granted. Even within affluent countries, the current energy crisis intensified by Russia’s invasion of Ukraine has reminded us that scarcity is not ennobling. When gas prices soar and heating bills become frightening, people do not experience a revelation about the joys of simplicity. They experience insecurity. Clean abundance would mean building enough zero-carbon electricity that heat pumps, trains, factories, hospitals, data centers, and homes can operate without fossil fuels. That requires mining, transmission lines, permitting reform, industrial coordination, and political willingness to build at a scale many degrowth arguments make suspect.

Soper’s essay sometimes acknowledges this, but the mood of the argument leans toward slowing down, shrinking, and doing without. Yet in climate terms, what matters most is not whether an activity feels consumerist but whether it can be decarbonized and made materially sustainable. Electric vehicles are imperfect: they require minerals, take up space, and do not solve congestion. But they are also a major improvement over internal combustion vehicles. For rural residents, disabled people, tradespeople, parents with complex schedules, and many workers, cars are not an optional vice. A humane transition should electrify vehicles while also building alternatives, not treat EVs primarily as a prolongation of car culture.

The same is true of aviation and long-distance travel. Frequent flying by the affluent deserves scrutiny, and some trips should shift to rail. But the desire to see the world is not merely a status competition. It can be educational, relational, and liberating. Migrants visit family across oceans. Scholars, artists, and activists build international solidarities. Young people encounter ways of life not available at home. The problem is carbon, not distance as such. Sustainable aviation fuels may be overhyped; batteries may not soon power long-haul flights; but innovation in fuels, aircraft design, and carbon removal may eventually preserve some of the benefits of flight. We should be cautious about declaring in advance that slower and nearer pleasures are morally superior.

A similar issue arises with work. Soper’s critique of overwork is powerful, and a shorter workweek is an attractive goal. But we should not romanticize artisanal production or assume that slower work is generally better work. Much preindustrial and artisanal labor was exhausting, repetitive, dangerous, and poorly paid. Modern productivity has freed people from many forms of toil. The washing machine, refrigerator, vacuum cleaner, combine harvester, container ship, spreadsheet, and pharmaceutical factory are not merely symbols of alienation; they are instruments that have reduced labor burdens and expanded life chances. The problem with many jobs is not that they are technologically advanced or embedded in large systems. It is that workers lack bargaining power, autonomy, purpose, and fair compensation.

David Graeber’s phrase “bullshit jobs” captures something real, but it can also encourage a careless dismissal of administrative and service labor whose value is not immediately visible. Modern societies are complex. Coordinating hospitals, universities, supply chains, insurance systems, scientific research, environmental regulation, and public benefits requires bureaucracy. Some of it is wasteful, especially where market competition generates duplicative billing, branding, compliance, and legal maneuvering. But the answer is better institutions, not a generalized hope that we can replace complexity with convivial localism.

The most serious weakness in Soper’s framework is political. She calls for a transformation of desire, but desire is plural. Some people want gardens and repair cafés. Others want dense cities, nightlife, advanced medicine, video games, air conditioning, electric bikes, lab-grown materials, and the freedom to move far from where they were born. A postconsumerist politics can easily become an aesthetic politics, privileging the tastes of educated urban environmentalists who find farmers’ markets, cycling, secondhand shops, and slow travel appealing. Many of those things are indeed appealing. But they are not universal substitutes for the diversity of modern aspiration.

There is also an international problem. Soper rightly notes that the richest 10 percent are responsible for a disproportionate share of emissions and that poor countries need room to develop. Yet the language of post-growth can travel badly. Leaders in low- and middle-income countries have reason to suspect that calls to abandon growth arrive just as their citizens seek what rich countries already have: reliable power, transport, refrigeration, hospitals, universities, sanitation, and industry. Even if Soper intends degrowth mainly for affluent societies, global decarbonization will require rich-world innovation that lowers the cost of clean technologies everywhere. Solar power, wind turbines, batteries, green hydrogen, advanced nuclear energy, low-carbon cement and steel, and carbon removal all depend on research, investment, manufacturing scale, and learning-by-doing. These are growth processes.

To be fair, Soper does not reject all technology. She allows a role for green technologies in energy and medicine. But her cultural emphasis risks underplaying the extraordinary success of technological substitution where it has been pursued seriously. Coal has declined sharply in the British power sector. Wind and solar costs have fallen dramatically over the past decade. Electric vehicles are moving from niche to mainstream. Methane leaks can be detected with satellites. Heat pumps can warm homes efficiently. None of this is sufficient, but it shows that material progress is not a fantasy. The faster clean technologies become cheap and abundant, the less climate policy must depend on persuading people to want less mobility, less comfort, or less novelty.

A better answer to consumerism would distinguish between private excess and public abundance, between waste and capability, between status consumption and human development. We should absolutely reduce planned obsolescence, fast fashion waste, manipulative advertising to children, car-dependent zoning, and luxury emissions. We should tax carbon, regulate pollution, build transit, encourage repair, and shorten working hours where productivity allows. But we should also celebrate the possibility of a high-energy, low-carbon civilization in which people enjoy more freedom, not less: freedom from fossil geopolitics, from dirty air, from energy poverty, from involuntary drudgery, from exclusionary cities, from preventable disease.

The language of “hedonism” is useful because pleasure matters. But pleasure should not be narrowed to slowness, locality, and simplicity. There is pleasure in speed, mastery, invention, exploration, and scale. There is pleasure in the city as well as the village, in the laboratory as well as the allotment, in the concert livestream as well as the neighborhood hall. A climate politics that cannot speak to those pleasures will leave many people cold.

The task, then, is not to move beyond abundance but to redefine it. Fossil abundance was dirty, unequal, and geopolitically dangerous. Consumer abundance is often shallow and wasteful. But clean abundance could be emancipatory. It could give us cheap electricity, quieter streets, cleaner air, more comfortable homes, and more productive economies capable of funding care and adaptation. If Soper’s alternative hedonism means asking whether the present consumer bargain really satisfies us, it is a welcome provocation. If it means that ecological sanity requires a broad cultural turn against growth and technological ambition, it is too pessimistic. The future cannot be business as usual. But neither should it be an elegant retreat. It should be a project of building enough clean prosperity that sacrifice is not the central political experience of climate action.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Consumer goods like cars and appliances often fulfill genuine human needs for mobility and relief from drudgery rather than merely being the result of corporate manipulation.
  2. 02The most effective climate strategy is a 'politics of better abundance' that utilizes technological innovation and growth to provide clean energy and public services rather than a retreat into simplicity.
  3. 03Decarbonization should focus on technological substitution, such as electric vehicles and sustainable aviation, rather than morally judging the desire for speed and long-distance travel.
  4. 04Modern productivity and complex institutional bureaucracies are necessary instruments for reducing human toil and managing a sophisticated society, making a return to artisanal production or localism impractical.
  5. 05A post-growth framework risks being undemocratic and exclusionary by privileging the aesthetic tastes of urban environmentalists over the diverse aspirations of the global population.
  6. 06Global decarbonization requires the rich world to drive innovation and manufacturing scale to lower the costs of clean technologies for developing nations, which is inherently a growth-oriented process.
  7. 07True progress lies in distinguishing between wasteful private excess and the emancipatory potential of public, clean abundance that offers more freedom rather than less.
