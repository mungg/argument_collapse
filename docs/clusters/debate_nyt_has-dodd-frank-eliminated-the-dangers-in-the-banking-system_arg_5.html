"Dodd-Frank has not eliminated the dangers in the banking system because it failed to address the inherent instability of modern finance, the complexity of global bank structures, and the risks residing in the shadow banking sector."

The rejection of the living wills should make us more humble about what Dodd-Frank can accomplish. The law addressed many problems exposed by the financial crisis, but it did not eliminate the core vulnerability: modern finance is built around promises that can unravel faster than legal processes can manage. A bankruptcy plan written in peacetime is not the same as a credible failure mechanism in a panic.

The debate between “break up the banks” and “defend Dodd-Frank” is too narrow. Size matters, but resolvability depends just as much on structure, funding and interconnectedness. A large bank with a simple balance sheet and stable deposits may be less dangerous than a smaller institution dependent on overnight funding and derivatives counterparties. Conversely, a huge global bank with thousands of legal entities, operations in dozens of countries and critical roles in clearing and payments is not made safe simply because it files a resolution plan.

Dodd-Frank’s living-will requirement was a smart idea because it forced regulators and firms to confront these details. The fact that several plans failed tells us that the biggest institutions remain too opaque and too hard to unwind. But the remedy should be specific: require simplification of corporate structures; pre-position capital and liquidity where losses are likely to occur; ensure that long-term debt can be converted into loss-absorbing resources; make derivatives contracts less capable of triggering immediate chaos; and coordinate better with foreign regulators. These measures may sound less satisfying than a campaign line, but they are closer to the mechanics of crisis prevention.

At the same time, Dodd-Frank left unresolved dangers outside the traditional banking perimeter. Money market funds, repo markets, securities lending, hedge funds and other forms of shadow banking can create bank-like runs without calling themselves banks. The 2008 crisis traveled through these channels as much as through insured depository institutions. If reform focuses only on JPMorgan and Bank of America, it may miss the next buildup of leverage somewhere less visible.

Nor should anyone believe that regulators, however well intentioned, can perfectly identify risks in advance. Stress tests are useful, but they test scenarios regulators imagine. Capital ratios are essential, but risk weights can be gamed. Living wills are necessary, but crisis politics can overwhelm legal design. A healthy financial system needs redundancy, simplicity and buffers, not just supervision.

So has Dodd-Frank eliminated the dangers? No. It has reduced some, exposed others and created tools that must now be used aggressively. The living-will announcement should not be read as proof that reform is hopeless. It should be read as a warning that financial safety is an ongoing project, not a bill Congress passed in 2010. The next phase should be less ideological and more structural: make failure boring, make complexity costly and watch the shadows as closely as the banks.
