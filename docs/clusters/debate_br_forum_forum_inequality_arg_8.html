"The responding essay argues that while institutional market reform is theoretically sound, progressives should prioritize a tax-and-transfer redistributive agenda because it is politically achievable and provides immediate material relief compared to the 'utopian' and easily co-opted goal of radical institutional overhaul."

David Grusky’s essay is a heartfelt and often brilliant attempt to move the inequality conversation beyond the well-worn path of upper-bracket tax hikes. He rightly points out that pre-tax income inequality has surged, and that the institutional rules of labor, capital, and education markets are complicit. But his conclusion—that we should dedicate ourselves to a “radical overhaul” of education and corporate governance in the name of competitive purity—is a political fantasy that risks disarming the very movement he wishes to support. In the actual arena of American politics in 2012, the choice is not between perfect market repair and imperfect redistribution; it is between a politically achievable tax-and-transfer agenda that can make a difference in millions of lives, and a utopian institutional crusade that will absorb the energy of Occupy and leave us with nothing. The better path is to double down on what’s possible while keeping a clear eye on the real obstacles: not a lack of clever reform proposals, but a ruthlessly mobilized opposition from the wealthy and their political allies.

Start with the education bottleneck. Grusky wants to equalize opportunity by radically expanding high-quality public education from preschool through college, breaking the rationing that currently inflates the wages of the college-educated. I endorse every plank of that platform. But who is going to pay for it, and where is the political coalition to make it happen? In the post-2010 midterm landscape, with austerity fever gripping Washington and statehouses starved of revenue by tax-cut pledges, “child’s play” this is not. Grusky himself admits that we’ve given up on equal opportunity. Why? Because the affluent families who benefit from the current bottleneck—the same ones who influence elite university admissions and the funding formulas of prized suburban schools—have the power to block any serious expansion of slots that would devalue their children’s credentials. Ask any governor who tried to shift state funding from research flagships to community colleges or to raise property taxes for statewide equalization. The political resistance is ferocious. In contrast, a straightforward increase in the top marginal tax rate, or a financial transaction tax, can be sold to a majority as making the wealthy pay their fair share, without directly threatening the positional goods that upper-middle-class parents cling to. Tax policy, for all its limitations, has a clean, conflictual logic that can be mobilized in elections. Institutional reform requires a protracted, multi-front war that progressives have never sustained.

The same dynamic applies to corporate governance. Grusky wants to end the CEO-as-fox-guarding-henhouse scandal by changing pay-setting practices. But the Dodd-Frank Act’s “say on pay” provisions, a very mild shareholder empowerment measure passed in 2010 after immense struggle, have already been neutered by regulatory foot-dragging and non-binding votes. To truly restructure board independence would require a head-on fight with the corporate lobby that controls the Republican Party and significant portions of the Democratic establishment. The Citizens United decision now ensures that any such fight will be met with a flood of dark money. So what happens if we pin all our hopes on institutional repair? We get a decade of CAP-style white papers, a few pilfered pilot programs, and endless foundation-funded convenings, while inequality widens and working-class living standards erode. Meanwhile, simply raising taxes on the top 1 percent—and coupling that with expansions in the Earned Income Tax Credit, food stamps, and housing vouchers—directly redistributes income now, with immediate effect. The evidence from the Clinton-era expansions of the EITC and the 1993 top rate increase is clear: tax and transfer policy can raise incomes at the bottom and lower them at the top, and it’s politically feasible when it’s part of a budget fight tied to visible public goods.

Grusky warns that a tax-only approach leaves the rule-rigging untouched. That is true. But he undersells the extent to which high marginal tax rates themselves shape institutional behavior. As Saez notes, very high rates in the postwar era discouraged executives from negotiating outsized pay packages, because most of the extra dollar would vanish. In effect, steeply progressive taxes changed the incentive to seek rent in the first place—not perfectly, but decisively. The reduction of top rates exactly coincided with the explosion of CEO pay, an empirical correlation that suggests a causal link. So tax policy is not merely a redistributional band-aid; it is a market-shaping tool that operates on a systemic scale, and unlike Grusky’s boardroom reforms, it requires no protracted wrestling with the details of compensation committee charters.

Most worryingly, Grusky’s faith in a “truly competitive economy” as the basis for a cross-class coalition against inequality is deeply naive about American ideology. For decades, the phrase “fair competition” has been a rhetorical cover for deregulation that serves the powerful, not a rallying cry for leveling the playing field. When we say “let’s make the market truly competitive,” the right hears “abolish regulations, cut taxes, break the teachers unions.” Grusky insists that his version of competition is different—it targets the rich’s rigged games. But political messages don’t work that way; they get weaponized by the loudest megaphones. The moment you start a campaign to “equalize opportunity” by, say, reducing the inheritance of advantage, the very same billionaires funding “education reform” will pivot and scream about government overreach and social engineering. And they’ll win, because their definition of “competition” already owns the cultural high ground. A forthright case for redistribution, grounded in the simple moral principle that extreme inequality is harmful and unfair, is at least honest and can build lasting majorities around the tangible benefits of public spending—as Social Security and Medicare have shown. OWS put inequality on the map not by calling for better corporate governance, but by naming a moral outrage: the 1 percent has taken the gains, and the 99 percent are left with the scraps.

Grusky may consider this a retreat to pragmatism. I consider it a realistic assessment of where power lies and how it might be countered. We should absolutely point out the institutional corruption that generates inequality, but as a justification for redistribution, not a substitute for it. Tax the rich, expand the safety net, and let the political struggle over market rules proceed from a position of greater equality, not as an all-or-nothing precondition.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The political resistance from affluent families and powerful lobbies makes radical educational and corporate governance reforms far less feasible than straightforward tax increases.
  2. 02Direct redistribution through tax policy and social safety net expansions has a proven track record of raising living standards at the bottom and lowering them at the top.
  3. 03High marginal tax rates serve as a market-shaping tool that naturally discourages rent-seeking behavior without the need for complex, multi-front institutional wars.
  4. 04The rhetoric of 'market competition' is easily weaponized by the right to justify deregulation, whereas a moral case for redistribution can build lasting majorities around tangible public goods.
  5. 05Institutional corruption should be used as a moral justification for taxing the rich rather than as a substitute for the practical necessity of redistribution.
