"The responding essay argues that the rise in economic inequality is not caused by market failures or institutional rent-seeking as Grusky claims, but is instead the natural result of technological change, global scaling, and anti-competitive government intervention."

David B. Grusky offers a welcome departure from the standard left-wing reflex to simply “tax the rich.” By acknowledging that the bulk of the inequality takeoff over the last thirty years is a pre-tax phenomenon, he forces a more serious conversation about how wealth is generated in the twenty-first century. However, his claim that the modern American economy is characterized by a "lack of competition" and rampant "rent-seeking" rests on a highly selective reading of market dynamics.

Grusky’s argument hinges on two examples of supposed market failure: the college wage premium and executive compensation. In both cases, he misidentifies the root causes of income disparity, mistaking the natural outcomes of a complex, globalized, and technologically advanced economy for corruption and institutional rigging.

Let us first examine the college premium. Grusky claims that elite universities restrict enrollment to create artificial bottlenecks, inflating the wages of the educated class and artificially expanding a "reserve army" of unskilled labor. This betrays a misunderstanding of both higher education and the modern labor market. The rising premium for a college degree over the past few decades is primarily driven by skill-biased technological change. As the American economy shifted from manufacturing to information and services, the marginal productivity of highly skilled workers skyrocketed.

Furthermore, the idea that college is a closed cartel is empirically false. While Ivy League institutions may be highly selective, the United States possesses the most expansive and diverse higher education system in the world, with thousands of state universities, liberal arts colleges, and community colleges that accept the vast majority of applicants. The bottleneck is not a lack of available "slots" in higher education; rather, it is the failure of our heavily unionized, monopolistic K-12 public school system to adequately prepare students for college-level work. If Grusky wants to talk about rent-seeking, he should look at how teachers' unions protect underperforming educators and block school choice initiatives that would actually introduce the fair competition he claims to champion.

Grusky’s second example—CEO compensation—is similarly flawed. He relies on the popular, yet economically dubious, narrative that CEOs are simply padding their own pockets through "sweetheart deals" with captured corporate boards. While cronyism certainly exists in isolated cases, it cannot explain a macro-level trend across the entire economy over three decades.

A far more robust explanation for the rise in executive pay is the massive scaling of modern corporations. As economists like Xavier Gabaix and Augustin Landier have demonstrated, the six-fold increase in CEO pay between 1980 and the early 2000s can be almost entirely explained by the six-fold increase in the market capitalization of large US companies. When a corporation operates globally and manages billions of dollars in assets, the decisions of its chief executive have immensely magnified consequences. Hiring a CEO who is just slightly better than the next candidate can translate into hundreds of millions of dollars in shareholder value. In a highly competitive global market for elite managerial talent, firms must pay a premium to secure the best leadership. This is not "rent"; it is the market efficiently pricing rare human capital in an era of globalization.

Where Grusky and I might find common ground is in the existence of crony capitalism—but he completely misses its true source. The most egregious rents in our economy are not extracted through standard corporate governance or university admissions, but through government intervention. When the federal government bails out failing banks with taxpayer money, when it erects insurmountable regulatory hurdles (like the sprawling Dodd-Frank act) that protect large incumbent firms from upstart competitors, and when it hands out billions in subsidies to favored green-energy companies like Solyndra, it creates true, anti-competitive rents.

The Occupy Wall Street protesters were right to be angry when Wall Street executives privatized their gains and socialized their losses in 2008. But that was a failure of the state, not the free market.

If Grusky truly wants a competitive economy that roots out unearned privilege, he should advocate for stripping away the state-sponsored protections that insulate the politically connected from the harsh winds of market competition. We don't need a "radical overhaul" designed by central planners to engineer an arbitrary level of equality. We need a return to free enterprise, where firms are allowed to fail, regulations are simplified to allow small businesses to compete, and the government stops playing favorites. True market repair means getting the government out of the way.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The college wage premium is driven by skill-biased technological change and a failing K-12 system rather than artificial enrollment bottlenecks at universities.
  2. 02The increase in executive compensation is a market-efficient response to the massive growth in corporate market capitalization and the global demand for elite talent.
  3. 03True rent-seeking and anti-competitive behavior stem from government actions like bailouts, subsidies, and excessive regulation rather than private market dynamics.
  4. 04Achieving a truly competitive economy requires reducing state-sponsored protections and returning to free enterprise rather than implementing radical institutional overhauls.
