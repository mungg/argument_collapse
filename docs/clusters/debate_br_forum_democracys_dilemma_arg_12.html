"The crisis of democratic legitimacy cannot be solved through technical or procedural security measures alone because the erosion of public trust is a rational response to institutions that have prioritized concentrated power and elite interests over the public good."

Farrell and Schneier’s essay is bracing because it refuses two comforting stories. It rejects the old cyber-utopian tale that information naturally liberates, and it rejects the newer fatalism that democracy is doomed by trolls and algorithms. Yet the essay still gives too much ground to a technocratic picture of the problem. It treats democracy as a system whose common knowledge is being disrupted by bad actors exploiting new technologies. That is true, but incomplete. The deeper crisis is that many citizens no longer believe official common knowledge because official common knowledge has too often served power.

This is not an argument for relativism. Climate change is real. Vaccines work. Votes should be counted accurately. The census should count everyone. Fabricated comments and fake grassroots campaigns are corrosive. But the defense of truth in politics cannot begin from the premise that established institutions were basically healthy until social media and foreign operatives poisoned the well. The well was already polluted.

Consider recent American history. The case for the Iraq War was built through a catastrophic failure of political, intelligence, and media institutions. Claims about weapons of mass destruction were amplified by prestigious newspapers and officials from both parties. The financial crisis exposed regulators who had been deferential to the industries they oversaw and experts who mistook complex financial engineering for dispersed wisdom. For decades, communities affected by deindustrialization, mass incarceration, environmental hazard, and predatory lending have been told by respectable authorities that the system is functioning more or less as it should. When such citizens distrust elites, that distrust is not merely a vulnerability. It is often a rational response.

This matters because democracy’s dilemma cannot be solved by restoring confidence in institutions as they currently exist. Confidence may be inappropriate. The question is not how to make people believe the census, the FCC, election administrators, scientists, journalists, or courts. The question is how to make those institutions democratically accountable, independent from domination, and visibly committed to the public good. Trust is not a resource to be manufactured. It is a relationship to be earned.

Farrell and Schneier know this, and they do not propose simple deference to experts. Still, their framework of “common political knowledge” risks treating disagreement about institutions as a problem of destabilization rather than as a struggle over power. The FCC net neutrality example shows why. Millions of fraudulent comments were an attack on the integrity of public consultation. But the larger scandal is that the agency moved against an overwhelmingly popular policy in a manner congenial to powerful telecommunications firms. The fake comments mattered because they provided cover for a decision shaped by concentrated economic power. A perfectly authenticated comment system would not by itself change the fact that regulated industries often enjoy privileged influence over regulators.

The same is true of climate politics. The problem is not simply that one party tells supporters that scientists are lying. It is that fossil fuel interests have spent decades building institutions, funding advocacy, and disciplining politicians to protect their profits. Disinformation is not a free-floating informational virus; it is a strategy in a political economy. If the plausible solutions to climate change threaten powerful asset owners, those owners and their allies will attack the knowledge infrastructure that makes action possible. To respond with better fact-checking alone is to bring a pamphlet to a knife fight.

The political economy of platforms deserves similar attention. The Internet did not simply make speech cheap. It created immensely profitable systems for extracting attention and behavioral data. Facebook and Google sell prediction and influence. They reward content that keeps users engaged, and engagement often means outrage, fear, and identity threat. The distinction between foreign manipulation and ordinary advertising becomes blurry when the whole business model is organized around targeting people at moments of susceptibility. Cambridge Analytica attracted attention because it sounded sinister, but the broader scandal is that surveillance-based persuasion is routine.

A democratic response should therefore be more structural than the essay’s language of resilience sometimes suggests. We should restrict the data collection that makes behavioral targeting possible. Political advertising online should be radically transparent and perhaps far more limited in its targeting. Antitrust enforcement should confront platform concentration. Interoperability and data portability could reduce dependence on a few private governors of speech. Workers inside technology companies should have more power to challenge products that damage democratic life. Users should have rights not merely as consumers but as citizens inhabiting privately owned public spaces.

More ambitiously, we should ask whether essential infrastructures of democratic communication can be left to firms whose fiduciary obligations point elsewhere. Public-interest social media, platform cooperatives, nonprofit search tools, and publicly funded digital spaces may sound utopian, but so once did public broadcasting, public libraries, and universal postal service. Democracies have always required communication infrastructure. The question is whether that infrastructure will be governed for advertising revenue or for public purposes.

Farrell and Schneier’s distinction between making democracy fairer and making it more secure is also too neat. Unfairness is insecurity. Voter suppression, gerrymandering, dark money, felony disenfranchisement, the Electoral College, Senate malapportionment, and the domination of policymaking by donors all provide raw material for delegitimation. When citizens say the system is rigged, the democratic answer cannot be merely to audit machines and secure databases. It must be to reduce the rigging.

This is especially important in discussions of polarization. Much elite commentary treats polarization as a symmetrical excess of passion. But some conflicts are about whether all people will count equally. The civil rights movement polarized America. So did Reconstruction. So did labor struggles. A democracy worth defending must not confuse the anger of excluded groups with the sabotage of dominant groups trying to preserve advantage. “Negative feedback loops” are valuable when they prevent spirals of paranoia and retaliation. They are dangerous when they cool demands for justice in the name of stability.

The census controversy again reveals the point. The citizenship question threatens to undercount immigrant communities and shift representation and resources away from them. Activists who warn immigrants about participation may, in some cases, worsen the undercount. But we should be careful before equating frightened community warnings with strategic manipulation by state officials. When the government adopts policies that make immigrants fear contact with authorities, distrust is not misinformation. It is an anticipated consequence of coercion. The remedy is not only public education or cybersecurity; it is a political settlement that credibly protects census data from enforcement uses and treats immigrants as members of the communities being counted.

I also worry about calls for “vigorous enforcement of the law” against actively deceitful political communication. Fraud, impersonation, hacking, and voter suppression should be punished. But political deception is a capacious category. Governments have a long record of using such categories against radicals, minorities, and dissidents. Antiwar activists, Black radicals, socialists, labor organizers, and whistleblowers have all been portrayed as dupes or agents of foreign power. In 2019, with a president who calls the press the enemy of the people and threatens opponents with investigation, we should be wary of building tools that future administrations can turn against dissent.

A better principle is to regulate power and conduct rather than truth as such. Ban forged identities in official processes. Require disclosure of funding for political ads and astroturf campaigns. Protect voting rights. Penalize false statements about when and where to vote. Secure public databases. Limit surveillance advertising. Enforce antitrust law. Fund independent expertise. Strengthen whistleblower protections. These measures do not require the state to decide whether a political worldview is true. They target domination, deception about identity, and corruption of procedure.

Finally, democracy needs material repair. It is fashionable to discuss disinformation as if citizens were brains floating in digital vats. But people become susceptible to conspiratorial politics when they experience humiliation, precarity, and abandonment. A person with no savings, no union, no local newspaper, no reliable health care, and no reason to think elected officials care about his town is living in a world already stripped of democratic agency. Online propaganda then supplies explanation and enemies. A more equal society would not eliminate lies, but it would reduce their emotional fuel.

This means that Medicare debates, labor law, housing policy, school funding, and climate investment are not separate from democracy’s information crisis. They are part of it. Social democracy is a security policy for democracy because it tells citizens that collective self-government can improve their lives. When democratic government delivers only austerity, gridlock, and deference to donors, authoritarians gain plausibility.

Farrell and Schneier are right that democracy must remain open to disagreement while protecting itself from sabotage. But the central saboteurs are not only trolls in St. Petersburg or consultants with fake Facebook pages. They are also corporations that profit from manipulation, parties that entrench minority rule, agencies captured by industry, and elites who invoke truth while evading accountability. Democracy’s dilemma is not just that openness can be weaponized. It is that unequal power weaponizes openness while demanding trust from those it excludes.

The repair of democracy must therefore be democratic in substance, not merely in procedure. Secure the ballots, yes. Audit the comments, yes. Protect the census, absolutely. But also democratize the economy of information, reduce concentrated wealth’s command over politics, and make public institutions answerable to the people they govern. The best defense against disinformation is not an official truth machine. It is a democracy that gives people more reason to believe in one another than in the forces trying to divide them.
