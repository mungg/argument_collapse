**In Defense of the Concert Hall**

Buried in Peter Singer's essay is a sentence that should provoke more outrage than it has: David Geffen, by donating $100 million to renovate Lincoln Center's concert hall, "could have found better things to do with his money." Singer says this casually, almost as an aside, on the way to a more general claim that "donating to opera houses and museums isn't likely to be doing the most good you can." It deserves a closer look, because the sentence reveals what is genuinely impoverished in the effective altruist worldview.

What, after all, is the implicit argument? It runs: the same $100 million could have saved tens of thousands of lives from malaria; therefore concert halls lose to bed nets; therefore Geffen has misallocated his philanthropy. The logic is so simple it feels invincible. But the strength of the argument depends on a premise that Singer never defends — namely, that the only morally serious goods are the prevention of suffering and the extension of life. Once you let in any other good — the development of human capacities, the transmission of cultural inheritance across generations, the cultivation of beauty, the creation of public spaces in which a city can know itself — the argument loses its grip, because these goods are not commensurable in QALYs and cannot lose a comparison they were never entered in.

A society capable of producing and sustaining a great orchestra is a remarkable human achievement. So is a great university, a great museum, a great library. These institutions are how civilizations remember themselves, how they educate the imaginations of their young, how they continue to produce people capable of, among other things, recognizing that distant suffering matters and that something should be done about it. The very moral sensibility that Singer trades on — the capacity to see one's own comfort against the backdrop of another's distress, to feel the call of impartial concern — is itself a cultural inheritance, transmitted through institutions that include the ones he wants to defund.

Singer offers a fallback: in a world that had eliminated extreme poverty, the arts would be a worthy cause. Until then, triage. But this concession reveals the depth of the problem rather than resolving it. Extreme poverty has been part of the human condition for as long as there has been a human condition, and on no plausible projection does it disappear in any of our lifetimes. The Singerian permission to fund the symphony is therefore deferred forever. We are to live, and our children are to live, and our grandchildren are to live, in a culture that systematically starves its non-instrumental institutions because suffering somewhere else has not yet been eliminated. This is not an ethics. It is a permanent state of emergency.

Compare, for a moment, the actual history of philanthropy that Singer is implicitly criticizing. The great American foundations of the twentieth century — Carnegie, Rockefeller, Ford, Mellon — funded libraries, universities, medical schools, scientific research, museums, music conservatories. They also funded public health: the eradication of hookworm in the American South, the development of yellow fever vaccines, the green revolution. Their philanthropy was pluralistic, betting on multiple goods on the theory that human flourishing is multidimensional and we don't know in advance which investments will pay out in which currency. That pluralism produced the institutional bedrock of modern American civic life. It would not have survived a GiveWell evaluation.

I am not arguing that Geffen made the optimal gift. Maybe he should have given more to public health, or to climate research, or to refugee resettlement. Reasonable people can argue. What I am arguing is that the question "did he do the most good?" is not the right question to settle the matter, because it presupposes a totalizing view of value that no thoughtful person actually holds when they think about their own lives. None of us live by maximizing one good against all others. We try to be decent in many directions at once. Our philanthropy can, and should, reflect this.

The effective altruist will respond that giving to one's preferred art form is just self-indulgence dressed up as ethics. Sometimes it is. But the answer is not to abolish the category of philanthropy that supports culture; it is to give more, give thoughtfully, and recognize that the human good is more textured than a spreadsheet can hold. Geffen Hall will outlast a generation of bed nets, and the people who hear music in it will be better for it. That is not nothing. That is, in fact, quite a lot.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The argument for effective altruism depends on the unproven premise that the only morally significant goods are the extension of life and the reduction of suffering.
  2. 02Cultural institutions like concert halls and museums are not mere luxuries but are essential for transmitting the moral sensibilities and cultural inheritance necessary for a civilized society.
  3. 03Treating philanthropy as a permanent state of triage effectively defers support for the arts and humanities indefinitely, as global suffering is unlikely to ever be fully eliminated.
  4. 04A pluralistic approach to philanthropy, which invests in multiple dimensions of human flourishing simultaneously, has historically been more successful at building the bedrock of civic life than a singular focus on measurable impact.
  5. 05The 'most good' framework fails to account for non-commensurable values—such as beauty, knowledge, and public space—that cannot be quantified in a spreadsheet but are vital to the human experience.
