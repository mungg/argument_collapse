"The authors' 'rolling-rule regime' is not a universal replacement for other regulatory models but rather one specialized tool that should be applied only to site-specific problems where market mechanisms are less effective."

Where Are the Markets in This Picture?

The Fung-Sabel-Karkkainen essay performs an interesting maneuver. It positions the "rolling-rule regime" as an alternative to both command-and-control regulation and market-based instruments. It then proceeds to discuss command-and-control regulation in some detail while dismissing market-based instruments in roughly two paragraphs. This rhetorical asymmetry matters, because the most important environmental policy innovation of the last decade in the United States has been a market mechanism—the acid rain SO2 trading program under Title IV of the 1990 Clean Air Act Amendments—and the authors' framework cannot account for it.

Let me start with the empirical record. The Title IV program created a cap on sulfur dioxide emissions from electric utilities and allocated tradable allowances to existing emitters. Phase I, which began in 1995, covered the dirtiest 110 plants and required substantial reductions. Phase II, beginning in 2000, will extend the program to essentially all utility units above 25 MW with a substantially tighter cap. By any reasonable measure, the program has been a striking success. Compliance has been near-universal. The achieved emissions reductions have exceeded the cap, with substantial early banking of allowances. The cost of compliance has been dramatically lower than pre-program estimates by EPA, industry, and academic analysts—by some estimates an order of magnitude lower. Allowance prices have been roughly half what was projected. The program achieved environmental targets at a fraction of what command-and-control would have cost. It has worked.

The authors' two-paragraph dismissal of tradable permits asserts that such programs require "extraordinary quantities and varieties of information" from a central regulator and that markets "depend on secure ownership rights" which limit post-hoc corrections. Both assertions are misleading in light of the Title IV experience. The information required to run the SO2 program was minimal: existing emissions inventories (which were already required for SIP purposes), an aggregate cap, and continuous emissions monitoring at covered units. The regulator did not need to know abatement costs at each source; the whole point of trading is that the market discovers those costs. Nor have property-rights concerns prevented program adjustment: the cap has built-in tightening from Phase I to Phase II, and Congress retains authority to alter the program prospectively.

What the authors miss, I think, is that well-designed market mechanisms are themselves a form of decentralized learning. The allowance market aggregates information about abatement costs from thousands of separately-managed sources, and produces a price signal that guides further investment. This is exactly the kind of decentralized discovery process the authors say they value—they just don't recognize it when it operates through prices rather than through deliberation.

I want to suggest a different way of organizing the regulatory landscape. The choice in environmental policy is not between command-and-control, markets, and deliberation, but between regulatory instruments well-matched to the problems they address and instruments poorly matched. Different environmental problems have different characteristics, and different instruments are appropriate to different characteristics.

Where a pollutant is well-mixed across a broad airshed, where sources are relatively homogeneous and easily monitored, and where the science of the dose-response relationship is reasonably well established, tradable allowances are the right tool. SO2 is the canonical case. NOx trading programs in the Northeast are working on similar logic. CO2, if and when we get serious about climate, will eventually demand something similar.

Where a pollutant is localized in its effects, where exposure varies sharply by site and demographic group, where toxicity is high at low doses, and where source-specific information is critical—the rolling-rule regime the authors describe may indeed be the right tool. Toxics fit this profile. Habitat conservation, with its irreducibly site-specific character, fits this profile.

Where the science is contested, where the threat is catastrophic and irreversible, and where standard-setting is itself a profound political choice—command-and-control with citizen-suit enforcement remains the right tool. Endangered species protection at the listing-and-take stage fits this profile. So does the protection of ambient air and water quality at health-based standards.

Notice that this taxonomy does not endorse one architecture as the regulatory framework of the future. It treats environmental regulation as an applied problem requiring instrument choice on a case-by-case basis. This is, I would argue, the appropriately pragmatic stance, and it is closer to what the best environmental economists have been arguing for years—people like Robert Stavins, Richard Schmalensee, Paul Portney, the Resources for the Future group.

The authors do gesture at instrument choice in passing, but they cannot bring themselves to take market mechanisms seriously because their underlying intellectual project is to vindicate "directly deliberative" democracy. They want to find an architecture that makes participation efficient, so that democratic engagement is no longer in tension with effective regulation. This is a noble project. But it leads them to misdescribe the policy landscape and to undervalue tools that work for reasons that have nothing to do with deliberation.

Let me also press the authors on the question of cost. Nowhere in the essay is there a serious engagement with the costs of the rolling-rule regime. The "deep participation" they advocate is not free. Citizens who participate seriously in tributary teams or HCP planning give up substantial time, often without compensation. The technical-assistance infrastructure—planners, TURI courses, monitoring programs—requires substantial public funding. The pooling and benchmarking infrastructure requires sophisticated information systems and analysis. By contrast, a well-designed cap-and-trade program is remarkably cheap to administer once set up: a few dozen EPA staff can run a program covering thousands of sources.

I am not arguing that cost is the only consideration. There are values—participation, local knowledge, accountability—that are not reducible to cost. But the authors write as if these values come without economic trade-offs, when in fact the rolling-rule regime is among the most resource-intensive regulatory architectures imaginable. If we are going to spend the resources required to make it work, we ought to spend them on problems where the alternatives are even more expensive. For many environmental problems, the alternatives are not.

A final point. The authors invoke pragmatism repeatedly. Genuine pragmatism, in the William James and Dewey sense, would mean asking: which tools produce the best outcomes for the least cost in the conditions we actually face? It would not mean privileging one architectural form across all problem types. The Title IV SO2 program is a pragmatist success. So, in their own ways, are TRI and the Chesapeake Bay Program. The intellectual error of the essay is to take what is true of some cases and try to make it true of all.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The essay unfairly dismisses market-based instruments like the SO2 trading program, which has proven to be a highly successful and efficient form of decentralized learning.
  2. 02Market mechanisms do not require the 'extraordinary' central information the authors claim, as the market itself discovers abatement costs through price signals.
  3. 03Regulatory instruments should be chosen based on the specific characteristics of the environmental problem, such as using markets for well-mixed pollutants and deliberation for localized, site-specific issues.
  4. 04The authors fail to account for the high economic and time costs associated with the 'deep participation' required by their resource-intensive deliberative framework.
  5. 05The authors' preference for deliberation over market efficiency is driven more by a desire to vindicate a specific theory of democracy than by a pragmatic assessment of what works best.

A Deweyan Cheer, and Some Hard Questions About Scale

This essay does something rare and valuable. It identifies a genuinely novel institutional pattern emerging across diverse policy settings and offers a philosophical framework—broadly pragmatist, recognizably Deweyan—for understanding what binds those settings together. Whatever one ultimately concludes about its prescriptions, Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen have given the rest of us a vocabulary and a set of cases to argue about. That itself is a contribution.

I want to make the case that they are onto something deep, while pressing them on three questions they do not adequately answer: the question of scale, the question of inequality, and the question of which problems this architecture can and cannot address.

First, the deep insight. The twentieth century has been dominated, in democratic theory, by a fundamental tension between two models of the polity. On one side stands the Schumpeterian conception of democracy as electoral competition among elites, with citizens reduced to periodic ratifiers of leadership choices. On the other stands the participatory ideal, of which Dewey's "The Public and Its Problems" is the canonical American expression, holding that democracy is meaningless unless ordinary citizens engage substantively in the inquiry by which collective problems are framed and addressed. Most defenders of participation have struggled to specify institutional forms that could realize the ideal at scales above the New England town meeting. The familiar candidates—worker cooperatives, neighborhood councils, "strong democracy" of the kind Benjamin Barber sketched in the 1980s—either remain marginal or operate at scales too small to engage with serious policy problems.

What the authors are arguing, in effect, is that environmental regulation has stumbled onto an institutional form that resolves this scale problem. The local "deep user" units are small enough to permit genuine face-to-face deliberation and the integration of local knowledge. The pooling infrastructure—TRI databases, INPO benchmarking, Chesapeake monitoring—operates at the larger scale where comparison and discipline become possible. The center authorizes and enforces a framework rather than dictating outcomes. If this works, it is a real institutional discovery, comparable in conceptual significance to the joint-stock corporation or the regulatory agency itself.

The Chesapeake Bay case is, on this reading, the most important case the authors discuss, precisely because it shows the iterative redefinition of citizen-expert relations over thirty years. The initial conception of "participation" as public education gives way to participation as voluntary monitoring under expert protocols, which gives way in turn to the tributary teams as composite expert-citizen entities producing locally adapted knowledge. This is exactly the kind of institutional learning Dewey described as the lifeblood of democratic inquiry.

Now for the hard questions.

The first is the question of scale upward. The authors are persuasive about local-to-regional aggregation, but they are largely silent about the genuinely global environmental problems that have come to dominate the policy agenda: climate change, ozone depletion, biodiversity loss as a global phenomenon, persistent organic pollutants. These problems do not decompose neatly into the local units the rolling-rule regime requires. Carbon dioxide emitted in Pittsburgh affects the climate of Bangladesh. Who is the "deep user" community for global climate regulation? The 1997 Kyoto Protocol negotiations made clear that solutions at this scale will require some combination of treaty-based commitments, international market mechanisms (the Protocol's flexibility provisions), and national implementation policies. The authors' architecture has little to say about any of this. One might respond that climate is sui generis, but I think the truth is that the rolling-rule regime is suited to a particular class of problems—those involving spatially distributed sources whose effects are felt in identifiable localities, where local knowledge has real value—and is less well suited to others.

The second hard question is about inequality. The authors imagine local units composed of citizens with the time, education, and resources to participate as quasi-experts in technically demanding deliberations. The Chesapeake tributary teams, the Southern California HCP negotiations, the workplace toxics reduction planning committees—all of these are populated, in practice, by people of a particular class. They are not representative of the surrounding population. The waters and lands of poor and minority communities in this country have historically been treated as sacrifice zones, and there is little in the authors' architecture that would automatically correct this. The environmental justice movement, drawing on the work of Robert Bullard and others, has documented systematic disparities in the location of hazardous facilities and the enforcement of environmental laws. The 1994 environmental justice executive order acknowledged the problem at the federal level but has changed little on the ground. A regulatory regime that hands decision authority to local deliberative bodies, without first addressing whose voices count in those bodies, risks consolidating rather than correcting these disparities. The authors should engage this critique directly.

The third hard question concerns the relationship between deliberation and substantive values. The rolling-rule regime, as the authors describe it, is essentially proceduralist. It specifies how decisions should be made—through pooled information, local experimentation, iterative revision—but is largely agnostic about what values should govern outcomes. The endangered species case reveals the limits of this agnosticism. The Endangered Species Act expresses a particular substantive commitment: that the avoidance of extinction is a non-negotiable obligation, not subject to local cost-benefit weighing. The HCP process, as it has actually developed, has tended to soften this commitment, treating species protection as one consideration to be traded off against development imperatives. The authors present this as institutional learning. It can equally be read as a process by which a strong substantive norm has been quietly weakened by reframing it as procedural. Whether that is good or bad depends on whether you accept the underlying ethical claim of the ESA—a claim about which the authors' framework has nothing to say.

These three questions—scale, inequality, substance—do not refute the authors' argument. They locate it. The rolling-rule regime is, I think, a genuine institutional innovation suited to a particular range of problems, but it is not the architecture of all future democracy, and it cannot do the work that constitutional commitments and electoral accountability must continue to do.

I want to close with a thought about the political conditions for the architecture's survival. The authors note, rightly, that consolidation of the new regime will require open validation through Congress. But Congress has been moving in directions that make such validation unlikely. The 104th Congress's assault on EPA and the Endangered Species Act, the regulatory takings movement, the persistent attempts to roll back the CWA—these reflect a political coalition that wants devolution as a means of weakening environmental protection, not as a means of deepening democratic engagement with it. The authors' rolling-rule regime risks becoming, in this political context, the friendly face of a much less friendly project. They need to think harder about how to defend their architecture against capture by interests that share none of their democratic commitments.

That said, this essay has done what important essays do: it has named a phenomenon that needed naming and given us terms in which to argue. I would rather argue these questions in the authors' framework than in any of the alternatives currently on offer.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The proposed architecture is less effective for global environmental crises like climate change, which do not decompose into the local 'deep user' units required for the regime to function.
  2. 02A regulatory system that shifts authority to local deliberative bodies risks exacerbating environmental injustices if it does not account for existing disparities in class, education, and political influence.
  3. 03The proceduralist nature of the rolling-rule regime may inadvertently weaken non-negotiable ethical commitments, such as species preservation, by reframing them as flexible trade-offs in local negotiations.
  4. 04The success of this model depends on legislative validation from a Congress that may be more interested in using devolution to weaken environmental protections than to deepen democratic engagement.
  5. 05The rolling-rule regime should be viewed as a complement to, rather than a replacement for, traditional constitutional commitments and electoral accountability.
