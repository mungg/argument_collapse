"Instead of mandating public disclosure of security breaches, the government should require companies to engage in mandatory, real-time technical information sharing with each other and the federal government."

The debate over whether companies should tell "us"—the public—when they get hacked is focusing on the wrong audience. Public disclosure is a sideshow that satisfies media curiosity but does little to stop cybercrime. If we want to genuinely improve our national cybersecurity posture, the focus must be on mandatory, real-time information sharing between companies and with the federal government, rather than public shaming.

When an advanced hacker group, such as the Chinese military unit recently profiled by the security firm Mandiant, breaches a defense contractor or a tech giant, they use specific tools, malicious IP addresses, and unique lines of code. This information is known as "threat intelligence." If the breached company keeps that intelligence to itself, the hackers will simply turn around and use the exact same methods to breach the company's competitors the next day.

Currently, companies are terrified to share this technical data with one another or with the government. They fear antitrust lawsuits, privacy violations, and shareholder lawsuits if they admit vulnerability. Legislation like the proposed Cyber Intelligence Sharing and Protection Act (CISPA) attempts to address this by providing liability protection, but it remains stalled in political debate over civil liberties.

Instead of mandating press releases, we should mandate confidential, anonymized intelligence sharing within industry sectors. If a bank is hit by a new strain of malware, they should be required to instantly push the digital signature of that malware to a secure clearinghouse so every other bank in the country can block it before they are attacked. This is how we build "herd immunity" in cyberspace. Forcing companies to go to the media only guarantees that lawyers and PR executives will slow down the dissemination of the technical details that actually matter. The priority must be actionable, machine-to-machine sharing to thwart attacks in progress, not public announcements after the damage is done.
