"The British 'golden thread' model of integrating local police into national intelligence networks, which Daniel Richman advocates for, actually demonstrates that such systems tend to erode civil liberties and evade democratic accountability."

Daniel Richman invokes the British “golden thread”—the integration of Special Branch officers into every local constabulary—as a promising template for the United States. This comparison deserves more sustained examination than his essay provides, for the British experience offers a sobering counterpoint to his optimism. Over three decades of institutional evolution, the United Kingdom has built precisely the kind of locally embedded domestic intelligence capacity that Richman envisions. It has not produced the happy balance between security and liberty; it has produced a system with many of the pathologies he seeks to avoid, and from which we should draw the opposite lesson.

Britain’s Special Branches were originally established to deal with Irish republican terrorism, but their mandate expanded over the years to encompass domestic extremism, animal rights activism, and now Islamic extremism. They are part of local police forces, yet they operate with a distinct culture, budget, and chain of command that links them directly to MI-5. The theory was much like Richman’s: local officers would have community contacts and sensitivities that the national security service lacked. In practice, the Special Branches became an insular subculture, often hostile to the community policing ethos of the “main” force. A series of reports by Her Majesty’s Inspectorate of Constabulary and the Intelligence and Security Committee have documented how Special Branch officers have collected intelligence on anti-racism campaigners, environmental groups, and even the families of crime victims, with little oversight. The Macpherson Report into the death of Stephen Lawrence revealed that the Metropolitan Police’s Special Branch had failed to act on intelligence about racist attacks and had been institutionally blind to the communities it was supposed to protect. The “golden thread” turned out to be a conduit for prejudice as much as for information.

Moreover, the British system has been persistently criticized for its inadequate accountability mechanisms. While local police authorities exist to oversee regular policing, Special Branch activities were often excluded from their purview on the grounds of national security. The resulting gap was filled only partially by the Intelligence and Security Committee of Parliament, a body that meets in secret and reports to the Prime Minister. For years, the public and even many MPs had no real window into how local intelligence was being collected and used. The Regulation of Investigatory Powers Act 2000, which was supposed to bring statutory clarity, instead created a framework so complex and permissive that it effectively legalized widespread surveillance, including of journalists and lawyers. The lesson is that embedding intelligence in local policing, far from strengthening accountability, can weaken it by fragmenting oversight and leveraging the secrecy of national security to avoid the transparency mechanisms that apply to ordinary policing.

Richman might respond that the American context is different: we have the Bill of Rights, a more robust federal judiciary, and a tradition of state and local independence that Britain lacks as a unitary state. To some degree, that is true. The Supreme Court in Hamdi and Rasul this year reasserted judicial review over executive action, and the Church Committee reforms of the 1970s remain part of the congressional memory. But these differences cut both ways. Our federal structure means that the network Richman proposes would be a patchwork: some cities might develop strong oversight, while others become intelligence free-fire zones. And the U.S. political climate, with its intense partisanship and the aftermath of a divisive election, seems likely to produce more Dearborns but also more places where local governments welcome the chance to police immigrant communities. The British experience suggests that relying on local political culture to restrain intelligence-gathering is a gamble; the forces of centralization and securitization have a way of prevailing.

What should we learn from Britain’s example? Not that domestic intelligence is impossible, but that it requires a national framework of law and oversight that is independent of the operational agencies. The UK is moving—slowly and imperfectly—toward a more integrated oversight model, with proposals for a statutory commissioner to review terrorism laws and an independent complaints mechanism for intelligence. In the United States, we should look to the 9/11 Commission’s recommendation of a strong, independent Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board, and perhaps also to the creation of an Inspector General for domestic intelligence with authority to audit classified programs at all levels of government. We should be legislating standards for information sharing, data retention, and redress, not leaving these to ad hoc intergovernmental negotiation. The “right fight” is not to replicate the golden thread, but to weave a new fabric: one that threads democratic accountability through every level of the intelligence process, from the beat cop to the FBI analyst, by statute and by independent oversight, not by trusting that local parochialism will do the work.
