"The proposal to make American support for Israel conditional on territorial concessions is strategically flawed because it undermines a vital regional partnership that actually serves the goals of offshore balancing."

Stephen Walt’s analysis of the burdens of primacy is characteristically sharp, but his discussion of the Israeli–Palestinian conflict is a prime example of a foreign-policy realism that has lost sight of strategic fundamentals. Walt argues that unconditional American support for Israel does immense harm to the U.S. position in the Arab and Islamic world, fuels anti-Americanism, and incentivizes Israeli intransigence. The real source of anti-American terrorism, he suggests, lies in the Israeli occupation and America’s role as its enabler. The solution, he contends, is to pressure Israel to withdraw to its 1967 borders and, if necessary, to end economic and military aid. This argument gets the geopolitics of the Middle East almost exactly backwards. Israel is not a strategic albatross; it is the most capable and reliable military power in one of the world’s most volatile regions, and a steadfast American alliance with the Jewish state serves both to deter common enemies and to stabilize the broader balance of power. Moving away from that alliance in the name of “offshore balancing” would not reduce threats to the United States—it would multiply them.

One must start with the nature of the security challenge in the Middle East. Walt frames the issue largely in terms of hearts and minds, as if the primary problem were the resentment of Arab publics. But the primary threat to U.S. interests in the region has always been the potential emergence of a hostile regional hegemon—Saddam’s Iraq in 1990, a nuclear-armed Iran today—that could control the Gulf’s oil reserves and wield terrorism as a lever against its neighbors. In that contest, Israel has been an invaluable strategic partner. Its intelligence services have repeatedly provided the United States with information on Soviet, Arab, and Iranian weapons programs that American agencies could not obtain. Its military has blunted the ambitions of radical regimes in conventional wars that might otherwise have drawn in American forces directly. Without the deterrent presence of a powerful Israel, the region’s predatory states would have a freer hand to coerce the conservative Gulf monarchies upon which global energy stability depends. Offshore balancing assumes that the United States can step back and let local powers handle the balance. What Walt fails to see is that Israel is precisely the kind of local power upon which offshore balancing should rely. Weakening that power by cutting aid or making security guarantees conditional on concessions to adversaries that have repeatedly vowed its destruction would not produce a stable regional order; it would invite a war that the United States would eventually have to fight itself.

Walt’s causal chain—that unconditional American support for Israel fuels bin Laden’s recruitment—also rests on a highly selective reading of history and ideology. Osama bin Laden’s fatwas against the United States focused on the presence of American troops in the holy land of Saudi Arabia, on the sanctions against Iraq, and on a broader civilizational grievance against Western influence. While the Palestinian cause has certainly been a rhetorical theme, it was never the primary casus belli for al Qaeda, nor does a just resolution of the conflict promise to defang the jihadist movement. The jihadists’ goal is not a two-state solution; it is the elimination of any non-Muslim sovereignty in the region. Conceding Israeli withdrawal under American pressure would not mollify these extremists; it would be celebrated as a victory for terrorism and as proof that the United States can be pushed around, encouraging more attacks. Walt writes as though anti-Americanism is a rational response to specific grievances that America can address through policy adjustment. But in the crucible of Islamist radicalism, America is hated not for what it does, but for what it is—a liberal, powerful, infidel superpower. Changing the details of settlement policy would do remarkably little to alter that.

There is also a deeper flaw in Walt’s approach to the alliance itself. He wants to treat Israel “the same way it treats any other country,” which implies that the United States should evaluate every foreign relationship through a cold balance-sheet of immediate self-interest. But alliances are not one-off transactions; they are relational commitments that generate their own strategic value over time. America’s willingness to stand by Israel through thick and thin has cultivated a depth of trust that enables intelligence sharing, joint military planning, and diplomatic coordination that is without parallel among non-NATO partners. If Washington signals that its support is contingent and revocable, Israeli leaders will be forced to adopt more unilateral, risk-taking policies—preemptive strikes, an even harsher occupation, possible nuclear escalation—because they cannot rely on the American backstop. Walt imagines that a conditional alliance would give the United States leverage for peace; in practice, it would strip the United States of any constructive influence and produce a more dangerous Israel.

Finally, the notion that American pressure could produce a viable and peaceful Palestinian state misreads the internal dynamics of Palestinian politics. The death of Yasser Arafat and the election of Mahmoud Abbas offer a window for diplomacy, but Abbas’s authority is contested by militant factions—Hamas and Islamic Jihad—that reject the two-state formula entirely. Even if Israel withdrew to the 1967 lines, the result would not be a peaceful Swiss-style canton; it would be a fragmented entity riven by armed factions, some of them allied with Iran and Syria. The United States would then have to guarantee that state’s security and demilitarization, entangling it in precisely the sort of nation-building quagmire Walt decries when it happens in Iraq. A more prudent offshore-balancing approach would accept that the Israeli–Palestinian conflict is not the strategic center of gravity for American interests, that the United States should continue to support the strongest stable actor in the region, and that the peace process, while desirable, should not be purchased at the expense of the alliance that most directly serves American deterrence.

Walt’s broader architecture of offshore balancing has many merits, but its treatment of Israel is a deep structural weakness. A grand strategy that pits the United States against its most capable Middle Eastern ally in the name of public diplomacy is not a strategy for preserving primacy; it is a strategy for handing a strategic prize to America’s adversaries.
