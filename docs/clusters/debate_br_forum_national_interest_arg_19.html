"American grand strategy should shift from a military-centric focus to one that prioritizes economic statecraft, trade, and technological innovation as the primary sources of national power and global influence."

Stephen Walt’s essay rests on a premise that is widely shared but rarely scrutinized: that American primacy rests fundamentally on military power and geopolitical positioning. His entire argument about grand strategy—whether offshore balancing, selective engagement, or global hegemony—is a debate about how to array and employ the instruments of armed force. What is missing almost entirely is a serious reckoning with the economic foundations of American power and the ways in which a grand strategy built around trade, finance, and technological dynamism could secure the nation’s interests more durably than any deployment of troops and ships. Walt mentions economic vitality in passing, but he never develops the insight that America’s true strategic advantage lies not in its capacity to project force, but in its capacity to project prosperity. A national-interest grand strategy for the twenty-first century should make economic statecraft the centerpiece, using military power only as a narrow shield for the global system of commerce and innovation that is the real source of American strength.

Consider the core ends that Walt identifies for a grand strategy: preventing the rise of a hostile hegemon, discouraging WMD proliferation, combating terrorism, and preserving American influence. Each of these is better served by a strategy of deep economic integration than by the permanent management of regional military balances. The rise of China, for instance, is often framed as a looming military challenge, but China’s ascendance is overwhelmingly an economic phenomenon driven by its integration into the global trading system that the United States designed and still leads. A China whose prosperity depends on access to American markets, technology, and capital is a China with massive incentives to avoid conflict and to play a responsible role in international institutions. The most effective “containment” of aggressive Chinese ambitions is not a ring of naval bases but a dense web of trade agreements, foreign direct investment, and educational exchange that makes war economically cataclysmic for Beijing. Conversely, a military-centric strategy that seeks to balance China from offshore bases risks creating exactly the kind of hostile encirclement that would drive Chinese nationalism toward aggressive revisionism. Offensive realism is not destiny; it is a choice that economic interdependence can foreclose.

The same logic applies to the problem of terrorism and Middle Eastern instability. Walt astutely notes that the United States must “persuade the rest of the world that American primacy is preferable to the likely alternatives.” But persuasion does not come from public-diplomacy campaigns or from the demonstration of military forbearance alone. It comes when American primacy delivers tangible economic benefits to ordinary people. The post–World War II order was legitimated not by the Seventh Fleet but by the Marshall Plan, the open American market, and the stability of a dollar-based financial system that enabled other nations to grow. Today, the single most powerful tool for draining the swamps of jihadism is not military occupation or offshore balancing, but a massive, sustained investment in the economic development of the greater Middle East—trade access, microfinance, educational infrastructure, and partnership with indigenous reformers. Walt criticizes the Bush administration for investing $145 billion in Iraq instead of in a settlement of the Israeli–Palestinian conflict, but even a settlement will not produce stability if the Palestinian economy remains a ward of foreign aid and the Arab world’s youth face 25-percent unemployment. A strategy of economic inclusion—a grand bargain that opens Western markets to Middle Eastern goods, cancels the odious debts of reforming states, and creates a regional development bank modeled on the European Recovery Program—would do more to undercut bin Laden’s appeal than any number of carrier groups.

Walt’s almost exclusively military framing also misses the most dangerous long-term threat to American primacy: the erosion of the nation’s human-capital and technological edge. The United States can sustain a military that is second to none only if it continues to lead the world in scientific research, higher education, and entrepreneurial innovation. Yet the country’s infrastructure is creaking, its K-12 education lags behind international peers, and its visa policies after 9/11 are driving talented foreign students and scientists to Europe, China, and India. A grand strategy focused on national interest would treat these domestic deficits as first-order strategic problems. It would massively increase public investment in basic research, reform immigration to attract the world’s best minds, and make the United States the global hub of the emerging green-energy revolution that will define the next industrial era. By contrast, the defense-heavy postures that Walt and his realist colleagues debate—whether forward or offshore—lock the nation into a spending path that starves the very sources of long-term economic advantage. The current U.S. defense budget exceeds the combined spending of the next fourteen nations. Even a modest redirection of a fraction of that sum into energy independence and education would do far more to secure American primacy for another generation than any rearrangement of bases.

Even the challenge of nuclear proliferation, which Walt rightly elevates, is more amenable to economic than military solutions. The surest way to persuade North Korea to abandon its nuclear program is not to threaten regime change or to sign a nonaggression pact, but to present a credible path to economic modernization, with large-scale energy assistance, trade, and investment conditioned on verifiable denuclearization. The model is not the grand bargain between nuclear arsenals that Walt proposes, but a bargain between a starving command economy and a dynamic global market. Iran, too, will only permanently forgo the nuclear option if its ruling elites can see a future of prosperity linked to reintegration into the world economy, rather than a future of pariah status punctuated by airstrikes.

Walt’s essay is a necessary, bracing critique of military overreach, but it remains trapped in the assumption that grand strategy is primarily about the disposition of armed forces. The most effective grand strategy for an era of American primacy would flip that assumption on its head. The main purpose of military power should be to maintain the security of the global commons—the sea lanes, the air routes, the digital networks—through which commerce and ideas flow. Beyond that narrow mission, American influence should be projected through the magnetic pull of a prosperous, innovative society at home and a foreign economic policy that enlarges the circle of shared wealth. That is not isolationism; it is the most ambitious internationalism of all, because it bets that the greatest power of the twenty-first century is not the power to destroy but the power to build.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Deep economic integration and interdependence are more effective than military balancing at containing the rise of hostile hegemons like China.
  2. 02The most sustainable way to combat terrorism and regional instability is through massive investment in economic development and market access rather than military presence or diplomatic persuasion alone.
  3. 03The greatest threat to American primacy is the domestic erosion of human capital and technological leadership, which is exacerbated by excessive defense spending that starves investment in education and research.
  4. 04Nuclear proliferation is best addressed by offering rogue states a credible path to economic modernization and global market reintegration in exchange for denuclearization.
  5. 05The role of the military should be narrowed to the specific task of securing the global commons—such as sea lanes and digital networks—to facilitate the flow of international commerce.
