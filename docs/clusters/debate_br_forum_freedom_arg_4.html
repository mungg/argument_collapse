Diversified DeepSeek

"Aziz Rana’s proposal to replace individual liberty with a model of collective self-rule is a flawed project that mischaracterizes the classical liberal tradition and risks creating a system of majority tyranny."

Aziz Rana’s essay is an erudite but deeply flawed piece that, despite its radical ambitions, reveals more about the intellectual cul-de-sacs of the academic left than about the genuine meaning of freedom. Its central error lies in a persistent conflation of the authentic, time-tested concept of liberty—rooted in the protection of the individual from coercive power—with the historical atrocities of colonialism, slavery, and imperialism. By tying the very idea of individual freedom to these sins, Rana attempts to discredit a political tradition that has in fact been the greatest force for human flourishing and for the liberation of the oppressed. In its place, he offers a vision of “collective self-rule” that is nothing less than a recipe for majority tyranny, dressed up in the language of solidarity.

Rana’s historical narrative begins with a telling move: he defines freedom not as the absence of arbitrary interference but as “actually experiencing independence and control over the basic conditions of one’s life.” This formulation immediately shifts the goalposts from the classical liberal concern with limits on state power to a participatory ideal that demands extensive collective control over economic and social arrangements. The settlers’ yearning for land and independence, he argues, was corrupted into a zero-sum logic because of the way they acquired it—through conquest and expropriation. The implication is that any conception of freedom that does not fully embrace “interdependence” and shared decision-making is suspect. But this is a non sequitur. The fact that some people pursued their freedom through unjust means does not indict the ideal of personal liberty; it indicts their violations of others’ liberty. The classical liberal tradition has always held that one person’s freedom ends where another’s begins, and that the core purpose of government is to protect those equal spheres of liberty—not to manage the expropriation of land for insiders.

From this shaky foundation, Rana constructs a genealogy in which the American right’s advocacy of limited government, market economics, and personal responsibility is merely the latest iteration of settler freedom. He lumps together the absurd “freedom fries” episode with the House Freedom Caucus’s push for fiscal restraint, as if the desire to limit the reach of a sprawling administrative state is on a continuum with white nationalism. This is an intellectually dishonest caricature. The millions of Americans who believe that high taxes, regulatory burdens, and government mandates encroach on their liberty are not all crypto-racists grieving the loss of settler privileges. Many are small-business owners, working-class people who resent being told what kind of healthcare they must buy, and parents who want to direct their children’s education. Their concern is not with domination over racial others but with the constant, creeping encroachment of a paternalistic state that treats them as subjects rather than as free agents. Rana’s essay does not seriously engage with this perspective except to pathologize it.

The positive alternative Rana offers—a freedom grounded in shared governance and solidarity—sounds benevolent in the abstract, but it collapses under scrutiny. He calls for “expanding and equalizing voting rights” and making workplaces “far more amenable to collective self-rule” through unions and sectoral bargaining. But the moment one asks how such a vision is to be implemented, the coercion inherent in it becomes apparent. If “the people” or “the workers” are to exercise control over the basic structures of economic and political life, who decides the boundaries of those collectives? What happens to the dissenting individual—the worker who prefers to negotiate her own contract, the community that wants land-use policies different from the regional norm, the person who believes that their most fundamental freedoms are not up for democratic grabs? Rana’s framework has no room for such dissidents except to treat them as obstacles to be overcome through the very institutional power he wants to build. The “continuous exercise in shared decision-making” quickly becomes a machine for compelling compliance.

The libertarian tradition, by contrast, begins with the recognition that every individual is a distinct moral agent, possessed of her own body and faculties, and that freedom consists in being able to exercise those capacities without subjecting them to the will of others, whether a monarch or a majority. That does not mean a world without cooperation; far from it. Voluntary association, mutual aid, and markets are all expressions of freedom precisely because they rest on consent. The worker who accepts a job with a large corporation does so because she judges it her best option, not because she is a serf. Real freedom is about the right to say no—to a boss, a union, a state program—and to forge one’s own path. Rana might call this atomistic, but it is merely respectful of human plurality.

He is correct that the right’s rhetoric can sometimes be hijacked by nativists who wrap their ethnonationalism in “freedom” talk. But that is a perversion, not the essence. The same can be said of the left’s long history of justifying tyranny in the name of collective liberation. The cure for the right’s excesses is not to jettison individual liberty for a mushy, all-encompassing vision of “solidaristic freedom,” but to recommit to the principle that government’s sole legitimate job is to protect the equal rights of all. That means dismantling the military-industrial complex Rana rightly critiques, ending corporate cronyism, and strictly limiting state power so that no group can use it to dominate another. A truly free society is one in which no one, inside or outside the border, is subject to coercive rule. That is a radical vision, too—but it is one anchored in the recognition that the greatest threat to freedom has always been the concentration of power, whether in the hands of settler majorities, corporate titans, or the would-be architects of collective self-rule.

"The responding essay argues that the classical liberal conception of freedom, centered on individual rights and limited government, remains the most coherent and morally defensible framework for liberty, whereas the lead essay’s collectivist vision of 'self-rule' inevitably leads to coercion and the dissolution of personal freedom."

Aziz Rana’s essay mistakes the object of his critique. He sets out to reclaim “freedom” for the left, but what he ends up describing as the right’s notion of freedom—extreme individualism, market fundamentalism, resistance to government programs—is in fact the classical liberal understanding of liberty that has been the default in American public philosophy since the founding. And, despite his best efforts to tar it with the brush of settler colonialism and white nationalism, that understanding remains the most coherent and morally defensible conception of freedom available. Rana wants to replace it with a collectivist, democratic vision of “self-rule” that elevates the group over the individual and mistakes majority participation for liberation. But the record of the twentieth century shows that such visions, when implemented, lead not to a broader freedom but to its dissolution.

Rana’s fundamental move is to collapse the distinction between a principled commitment to limited government and the historical crimes committed by those who invoked liberty while oppressing others. That some settlers used libertarian rhetoric to justify land theft no more discredits the principle of individual rights than King Nyerere’s use of solidarity rhetoric to justify one-party rule discredits the ideal of community. Ideas must be judged on their own merits, not on the worst behavior of those who claim them. The liberal tradition, from Locke to Hayek, has consistently maintained that freedom is fundamentally a condition of non-domination by other persons—the security to live one’s life according to one’s own plans without being subject to the arbitrary will of a master, whether that master is a slaveholder, a bureaucrat, or a democratic majority. This is not “zero-sum” in the way Rana imagines. It is universalizable: my right to be free from coercion is identical to yours. A society ordered around this principle demands that all are equally protected.

Rana might reply that this purely negative freedom is hollow without the “experience of self-rule.” But here he runs headlong into a contradiction. He wants people to enjoy genuine control over their lives, but he simultaneously argues that this can only happen through collective decision-making in workplaces and political institutions. The problem is that collective self-rule is not, for any particular individual, self-rule at all; it is subjection to the group. If I am outvoted by my coworkers on a major question of production, or by my fellow citizens on the allocation of tax dollars, I am exactly in the position of having my life directed by others. Rana may call this “solidarity,” but it is merely a softer name for coercion. The libertarian insight is that true self-mastery is precisely the protected ability to say no to the collective, to opt out, to chart a separate course. That is why the core institutions of a free society are private property, voluntary contract, and a minimal state restricted to protecting those rights. Without them, the individual is absorbed into the mass.

Rana’s attempt to link the right’s freedom talk to “resurgent white nationalism” is a rhetorical sleight-of-hand that confuses the fringe with the core. The typical conservative or libertarian voter who wants lower taxes, less regulation, and the right to bear arms is not fantasizing about racial domination. She is concerned that an overweening state will misuse its power, as it always has, to favor the well-connected and to trample the dissident. Indeed, the greatest victims of state power have been precisely those Rana wants to shelter: racial minorities, political radicals, the poor. The War on Drugs, zoning laws that enforce segregation, the surveillance state—all are products of the very government activism that the right’s freedom discourse opposes. To indict that discourse as inherently racist is to ignore the ways in which limited government has been a shield for the vulnerable, not just a sword for the privileged.

Rana’s alternative relies heavily on a reform of the workplace to make it a site of democratic participation. But this, too, strips individuals of the freedom to choose their own forms of cooperation. In a genuinely free labor market, no one is forced to join a union or to participate in workplace governance; she can negotiate her own terms and move if she dislikes the arrangement. Rana’s sectoral bargaining and mandated co-determination would, in practice, restrict the options available to workers and entrench the power of union bureaucracies that have their own interests. The 2018 *Janus* decision by the Supreme Court affirmed what should be obvious: that compelled association for the sake of a “public good” violates the First Amendment rights of dissenters. Rana’s vision runs straight into that constitutional reality, and he would need to override it—a project that would itself require a massive expansion of state power.

Even Rana’s historical claim that empire underwrote domestic freedom for white workers is open to serious empirical challenge. The post-World War II middle-class prosperity he references was driven less by imperial rents than by a combination of technological innovation, a devastated global competition, and a domestic energy boom. The flight of manufacturing overseas in recent decades is often blamed on free trade deals, but it is also the result of regulatory costs and a sclerotic labor market that made it difficult to adapt. The libertarian cure for that is not a protectionist retreat but the removal of special privileges and subsidies that prop up corporate behemoths at the expense of smaller entrepreneurs and workers.

The essay ends with an appeal to “revitalizing the institutions and social bases” for an emancipatory narrative. But the institutions most in need of revival are not those of collective governance, but those of civil society that protect individual agency against the state: the family, the church, the voluntary charity, the small business. These are the seedbeds of genuine freedom, where people learn responsibility, mutual aid without compulsion, and the dignity of earned success. Rana dismisses this as “atomized” individualism, but it is anything but atomistic. It is the fertile ground of a pluralist society where people can pursue their own visions of the good without imposing them on one another.

That is the freedom worth reclaiming—not a warmed-over socialism that asks us to merge our identities into a collective will, but a confident, pluralist liberty that honors the individual and sees in her the unalienable right to direct her own life. The left’s mistake is to think that freedom is something the state or the majority can bestow. History shows it is something they usually take away.
