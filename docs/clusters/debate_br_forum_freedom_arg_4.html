Diversified MiniMax

"While Aziz Rana correctly identifies the historical link between American empire and exclusionary freedom, his optimism regarding the current decline of right-wing ideology underestimates the ability of dominant political narratives to persist even after their material foundations have collapsed."

Let me end with a note of cautious optimism.

Aziz Rana has written an essay that is analytically rigorous, historically grounded, and politically engaged. It takes seriously the relationship between ideas and institutions, between domestic policy and foreign intervention, between the past and the present. This is exactly the kind of contribution that American political discourse needs more of.

The historical section is particularly strong. The argument that settler expansion was constitutive of white freedom, rather than merely correlated with it, recasts our understanding of American history in important ways. So does the analysis of how imperial projection in the twentieth century reproduced the logic of settler colonialism in new forms. These connections are not always made explicit in the scholarly literature; Rana has done a service by weaving them together.

Where I have reservations, they concern the political optimism of the final sections. Rana argues that the right's version of freedom is losing resonance, and that this creates space for a more inclusive alternative. This may be true. But we should not underestimate the resilience of politically dominant ideologies, even when the material conditions that generated them have changed. The persistence of homeownership rhetoric long after home ownership became unaffordable for many; the endurance of "opportunity" as a political slogan despite declining mobility; the continued appeal of "small government" even as people depend on and want public services: all suggest that political language can detach from material reality and continue to shape cognition and preference.

This doesn't mean the left should give up. But it suggests that the work ahead is long, the victories uncertain, and the need for strategic patience acute. Rana's essay is a valuable contribution to that long struggle. Let us learn from it and build on it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Rana’s historical analysis successfully demonstrates that settler expansion and global imperialism were constitutive elements of white American freedom rather than mere coincidences.
  2. 02Political ideologies and language often detach from material reality, allowing concepts like 'small government' or 'opportunity' to retain psychological and cultural power despite declining economic mobility.
  3. 03The resilience of entrenched rhetoric suggests that the left's project of reclaiming freedom will be a much longer and more uncertain struggle than Rana’s assessment of the right's 'fragility' implies.
  4. 04Strategic patience is required because the disconnect between political slogans and the lived experience of the citizenry does not automatically result in the abandonment of those slogans.
Diversified MiniMax

"While Aziz Rana correctly identifies the historical and material roots of the American right's exclusionary version of freedom, his analysis overlooks the persistent political psychology and identity-based functions that sustain this ideology even as its material benefits decline."

I want to engage with Aziz Rana's essay at the level of political psychology, which is undertheorized in his account.

The essay's historical analysis is compelling: American freedom discourse has been shaped by settler colonialism, imperial projection, and racial exclusion in ways that make individualistic, zero-sum versions of liberty dominant. But how do these historical structures persist? Why do working-class white Americans continue to vote against their material interests in ways that entrench the very power structures that harm them?

Rana's answer emphasizes material benefits: empire subsidized a certain standard of living, which made individualistic freedom seem viable. But this explanation faces a puzzle. The material benefits have declined significantly; Rana acknowledges this. Yet the political psychology persists. Working-class whites still embrace "freedom" in the individualistic sense even as their economic situation deteriorates. This suggests that the explanation is not purely material.

There is something deeper going on: identity. The racialized concept of freedom that Rana describes has become a way of distinguishing "real Americans" from outsiders. This distinction persists even when material interests would seem to recommend solidarity with marginalized groups. It persists because it serves a psychological function: it provides a sense of superiority and belonging that cannot be easily given up.

Understanding this dynamic suggests that the left's institutional work must be supplemented by cultural struggle of a very specific kind. We need to understand what it would take to give working-class whites an alternative source of identity and dignity—not one based on racial exclusion but one based on democratic participation and collective achievement. This is a harder task than organizing unions or passing the PRO Act, though those tasks are essential.

Rana's essay helps us understand what we're up against. It is less helpful on the question of how to overcome it. I don't say this as a criticism; that question may not be answerable in a single essay.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The continued adherence of working-class white Americans to individualistic freedom despite deteriorating economic conditions suggests that the explanation for this worldview is not purely material.
  2. 02Racialized freedom serves a psychological function by providing a sense of superiority and belonging that distinguishes 'real Americans' from outsiders.
  3. 03To successfully reclaim freedom, the left must supplement institutional building with a cultural struggle that offers an alternative source of identity and dignity based on democratic participation rather than racial exclusion.
  4. 04Rana’s proposed solutions, such as union organizing and legislative reform, are necessary but insufficient to overcome the deep-seated psychological attachments to reactionary narratives of liberty.
