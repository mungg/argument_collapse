"The Democratic Party’s shift toward muscular industrial policy and state intervention is primarily driven by the geopolitical and technological challenge posed by China rather than domestic electoral dynamics alone."

Jacob S. Hacker and Paul Pierson’s account of the Democratic Party’s surprising economic pivot is an insightful look at domestic coalition dynamics, but it suffers from a glaring omission: it entirely ignores the rest of the world. In reading "The New Blue Divide," one would think the Biden administration’s embrace of industrial policy, massive infrastructure spending, and the revitalization of domestic manufacturing were solely the products of progressive pressure, Covid-19 disruptions, and a desire to win back white working-class voters. The authors manage to dissect the shift in American political economy without ever mentioning the primary catalyst driving the bipartisan consensus for state intervention: the geopolitical and technological challenge of the People's Republic of China.

The shift from the neoliberal "Rubinomics" of the Clinton and Obama eras to the muscular industrial policy of the Biden years cannot be understood merely as a victory of the left over the center. It is, more accurately, a profound reassessment of national security in an era of renewed great power competition.

For three decades, the core assumption of both Democratic and Republican economic establishments was that global economic integration—facilitated by free trade, off-shoring, and frictionless supply chains—would inherently serve American interests, ultimately liberalizing authoritarian regimes like China. By the time Biden took office, that assumption had collapsed. The COVID-19 pandemic laid bare the perilous fragility of America's supply chains, revealing that the U.S. was critically dependent on a strategic rival for everything from basic personal protective equipment to the active pharmaceutical ingredients in crucial medications.

Simultaneously, China’s aggressive state-backed investments in strategic sectors—artificial intelligence, electric vehicles, quantum computing, and advanced manufacturing—demonstrated that the laissez-faire approach of the West was losing ground to state capitalism. When Hacker and Pierson point to the "unprecedented investments in green and high tech manufacturing" and the "requirements or incentives for U.S.-made materials" in Biden's agenda, they frame these purely as redistributive measures or sops to organized labor.

But look at the CHIPS and Science Act, a cornerstone of this new paradigm that the authors scarcely analyze. The push to subsidize domestic semiconductor manufacturing was not born out of a desire to appease the Congressional Progressive Caucus or to manage the "U-shaped" coalition. It was driven by the terrifying realization that over 90% of the world's most advanced semiconductors are manufactured in Taiwan, sitting squarely in the crosshairs of the People's Liberation Army. If that supply chain were severed, the U.S. economy would face an immediate, catastrophic depression, and the U.S. military would lose access to the components necessary for modern defense systems.

The Biden administration’s economic agenda is an attempt to achieve economic security and strategic autonomy. The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), while undeniably a historic climate bill, is also a profound piece of geoeconomic legislation. Its stringent domestic content requirements for electric vehicle tax credits are explicitly designed to freeze China out of the American clean energy supply chain and force companies to on-shore or "friend-shore" the processing of critical minerals.

Hacker and Pierson wonder how the Democratic establishment broke so sharply with the fiscal restraint and free-trade dogmas of the past. The answer is that the national security establishment and the economic establishment merged. The "Bigger, Bolder, Broader" agenda was politically viable because it appealed not just to climate activists and labor unions, but to defense hawks and foreign policy realists. This is precisely why pieces of this agenda, like the CHIPS Act and the infrastructure bill, garnered bipartisan support in a way that the purely social-welfare components of Build Back Better did not.

The failure of the authors to acknowledge the China factor leads to a misinterpretation of the durability of this economic shift. They worry that the moment has passed and that the "Brahmin Left" might revert to the mean. However, because the structural driver of this new industrial policy is the inescapable reality of Sino-American competition, the shift is likely far more durable than they assume. Even if Republicans take total control of government, the drive to subsidize domestic manufacturing in strategic sectors and restrict technology transfers to China will continue.

By viewing the Democratic Party's transformation purely through the lens of domestic electoral geography and factional disputes, Hacker and Pierson miss the defining global dynamic of our time. The "New Blue Divide" is certainly a fascinating domestic political phenomenon, but the new American political economy is being forged in the crucible of a new Cold War. The return of the active state is not just a choice made by Democratic elites to placate their progressive base; it is an absolute necessity for American survival in a multipolar world.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The transition from neoliberalism to industrial policy reflects a merger of national security and economic interests aimed at achieving strategic autonomy.
  2. 02The fragility of global supply chains exposed by the COVID-19 pandemic necessitated a move away from free-trade dogmas toward on-shoring and 'friend-shoring.'
  3. 03Key legislative achievements like the CHIPS and Science Act were motivated by the urgent need to secure advanced semiconductor production against potential Chinese aggression.
  4. 04The Inflation Reduction Act functions as geoeconomic legislation designed to exclude China from the American clean energy supply chain.
  5. 05The bipartisan support for certain economic initiatives suggests that national security concerns, rather than progressive pressure, made the new agenda politically viable.
  6. 06The durability of this economic shift is guaranteed by the permanent reality of Sino-American competition, making a return to the 'Brahmin Left' model unlikely.
