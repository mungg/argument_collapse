"While Lea Ypi’s class-based diagnosis of the migration crisis is correct, her cosmopolitan prescription to move beyond national citizenship is strategically dangerous and ignores the practical necessity of the nation-state as the primary site for democratic struggle."

**The Class Analysis Is Right. The Strategy Is Harder Than Ypi Admits.**

Lea Ypi's essay is the most lucid statement I have read in some time of what should be the left's basic position on migration. Her central insight — that the moral weight of the citizen/migrant distinction is "parasitic" on the prior identification of working-class interests with the borders of the state — cuts to the heart of why social-democratic parties cannot win their way out of the current crisis by mimicking the right. She is right that the migration debate is really a debate about whether capital or labor sets the terms of social cooperation. She is right that selling residency to oligarchs while shackling Honduran farmworkers exposes the lie that border enforcement is about "the rule of law." And she is right that Sahra Wagenknecht's welfare-trade-off argument cannot be honestly entertained without first asking who actually captured the gains of globalization in Germany — and the answer is not the Syrian refugee in Hamburg.

Where I want to push back is not on the diagnosis but on the prescription, or rather on the breezy confidence with which Ypi waves away the strategic problem. "In a contest on ethnocentrism and immigrant scapegoating, the left will never win against the right," she writes. True. But the conclusion she draws — that the left should therefore campaign to "shape alternative discourses" even at the price of "a short-term loss" — papers over what those short-term losses actually look like in 2025. A short-term loss is Donald Trump's second administration. A short-term loss is Reform UK polling at 30 percent. A short-term loss is the AfD now the largest party in three German states, with practical influence over deportation policy, citizenship law, and the contours of permitted speech. A "short-term loss" in a system this brittle is not a tactical retreat from which one regroups; it is the consolidation of an authoritarian-nationalist machinery that will, among other things, smash the very transnational labor organizing Ypi (rightly) places at the center of her alternative.

This is not a counsel of despair. It is a request that Ypi take seriously a constraint she acknowledges only glancingly: the question of *sequencing*. Cross-border worker solidarity of the kind embodied in the Make Amazon Pay coalition is real and inspiring, but it is also, in the global division of labor, marginal. The conditions that made the great twentieth-century gains of organized labor possible — high wartime employment, capital-intensive Fordist production, communist competition, citizenship-based welfare bargains — are gone. We are trying to rebuild solidarity in conditions of fragmented logistics work, gig employment, hyper-mobile capital, and a state that has been actively hollowed of redistributive capacity. To rebuild that under conditions of *open* contestation over borders — where every wage negotiation can be reframed by employers as "we'll just hire the undocumented worker for half" — is harder, not easier. Ypi knows this; she just doesn't dwell on it.

The Calhoun line she borrows — cosmopolitanism as "the class consciousness of frequent travelers" — should be applied to herself as well as to Davos. There is a recognizable register in this essay, familiar from the cosmopolitan-left intellectual scene, in which the structural denunciation of national membership comes easily because the speaker is, in practical terms, a citizen of the academic Schengen. The factory worker in Sunderland or Mulhouse who is asked to disidentify with her nation in favor of a transnational solidarity that does not yet exist is being asked to make a leap of faith Ypi does not have to make. The right understands this; the left often does not.

What would it look like to take Ypi's diagnosis seriously without the cosmopolitan-romantic prescription? I think it would look more like the politics of, say, Bernie Sanders in his 2015 incarnation, or the recent CGT-led pension strikes in France, than like the deconstruction of citizenship per se. That is: defend and extend the welfare state aggressively, including for migrants already present and contributing; sharply distinguish between the right of asylum (non-negotiable) and the management of labor migration (a legitimate object of democratic deliberation); insist that whatever borders exist must be transparent, rule-bound, and not for sale; and refuse, point blank, the equivalence between an undocumented dishwasher and a Russian oligarch with a Maltese passport. That last refusal is precisely Ypi's, and it is the rhetorically powerful core of her piece. But it is also compatible with a politics that retains the idea of bounded democratic citizenship as the *site* of struggle, rather than the obstacle to it.

Here I think Ypi runs together two distinct claims. The first is that liberal-democratic citizenship has been transformed into a commodity at the top and a tightening cage at the bottom — that golden visas and integration tests are two ends of the same enclosure. This is correct and damning. The second is that citizenship as such, the bounded political community as such, is the carrier of these pathologies. This does not follow. The labor movements, the suffragists, the anticolonial parties she cites all fought *to enter* citizenship, not to dissolve it. They expanded the demos. They did not, on the whole, propose to abolish it. Indeed, anticolonial movements were almost without exception nationalist; the postcolonial state, however compromised, was the instrument through which the wretched of the earth proposed to remake themselves into political subjects rather than imperial objects. Ypi's framework slides between "citizenship has been corrupted by capital" (true, fixable) and "citizenship as such is the wrong frame" (much stronger, much harder to operationalize).

The Nathan the Wise framing is lovely but does some unacknowledged work here. Lessing's cosmopolitanism is a moral disposition; it tells you how to treat the stranger you meet, not how to allocate housing, health care, schooling, and political voice among 67 million people. The Enlightenment Ypi invokes also produced Kant's federation of republics, not a world without polities. *Sapere aude* is compatible with thinking critically about the genuine goods that bounded democratic communities provide — predictability, accountability, the possibility of holding governments to promises — even while denouncing the racialized hierarchies that have historically structured access to them.

The most powerful section of the essay, to my mind, is the comparison between the chained deportees and the Platinum Card. This is the real obscenity of the current order: not that borders exist, but that they exist asymmetrically, structured entirely by capital. A left politics on migration could be built around this single contrast. It does not require abolishing borders; it requires abolishing their commodification. Tax citizenship sales out of existence. Treat them as the corruption they are. Make the rules the same for the cleaner and the hedge fund manager. Make pathways to status legible, affordable, and rights-bearing for everyone already inside the territory. Defend asylum law uncompromisingly against the Rwanda schemes and their imitators. Couple all of this with serious labor enforcement so that the existence of undocumented workers cannot be used to suppress wages, and with a foreign policy that stops manufacturing the conflicts that produce refugees.

None of this is "open borders." None of it is the dissolution of the demos. All of it follows from taking seriously Ypi's class analysis without her cosmopolitan-philosophical leap.

The deeper issue her essay raises, and does not quite resolve, is what to do when the working class of the rich countries has — through forty years of defeats, deindustrialization, and the disorganization Ypi describes so well — come to identify its interests with national rather than class membership. You cannot wish this away by lecturing it about Lessing. You have to give it material reasons to revise the identification: secure jobs, housing it can afford, public services that work, a state that punishes wage theft more aggressively than visa overstays. That is a project that runs through, not around, the democratic nation-state. It is also, I suspect, the only project that could ever vindicate the universalist horizon Ypi rightly insists we should not abandon.
