Diversified MiniMax

"While a class-based analysis of migration is useful, it is ultimately insufficient because it treats borders as economic symptoms rather than addressing the fundamental moral injustice of restricting human movement."

While I share Lea Ypi's critique of how migration has been weaponized by right-wing movements and co-opted by parts of the left, I find her framework insufficiently attentive to something that cannot be reduced to economics: the moral weight of borders themselves. Her class-based analysis is persuasive as far as it goes, but it tends to treat migration restrictions as problems of distribution rather than questions of justice in their own right.

Consider what it means to deny someone entry to a territory. Ypi is right that the conditions producing migration are often manufactured by global capital and Western foreign policy. But even if we reformed those structural conditions tomorrow, people would still move—seeking better opportunities, joining family members, exploring different ways of life. The question of whether to allow this movement would remain. And the answer that states currently give—that movement requires permission, that crossing a line requires justification—represents a profound denial of human freedom.

Ypi's analysis treats borders as symptoms rather than problems. But is it only symptomatic that we allow wealthy people to purchase citizenship while criminalizing the desperate? Is the class analysis sufficient to capture the moral horror of a child dying in the Mediterranean while European states debate rescue obligations? The structural argument may explain why these things happen, but it does not exonerate them.

I want to propose a more radical position: that the right to movement is a basic human right, not derivable from economic arguments but grounded in individual autonomy. This is not a new position—it has roots in early liberal thought and finds expression in contemporary human rights discourse. But Ypi's essay, despite invoking Enlightenment values, largely brackets this dimension in favor of class analysis.

Her invocation of Nathan the Wise is instructive here. Nathan's response to the question of which civilization is best is to look forward rather than backward, to ask what we want to become. But Nathan does not say we should restrict movement based on economic considerations or wait until structural conditions improve. On the contrary, the entire dramatic arc of Lessing's play suggests that the recognition of our common humanity must come now, regardless of whether the conditions that produced conflict have been fully addressed.

The Enlightenment project Ypi celebrates was never purely about redistribution. It was about recognizing the inherent dignity of every person—that each human being has claims upon us that do not depend on their productivity, their legal status, or their class position. When Ypi argues that we should link migration to broader struggles for economic justice, she is right. But she should not imply that justice for migrants is only legitimate insofar as it serves broader class interests.

Let me be concrete. If employers benefit from vulnerable migrant labor, that is an argument against employer exploitation, not against migration. If migration creates competitive pressures on domestic workers, that is an argument for stronger unions and social protections, not for restricting migration. The moral claim of the migrant to move and work does not depend on whether doing so serves someone else's interests.

This distinction matters because the framing determines the politics. A class-based approach to migration can never fully escape the logic of competition—workers versus migrants, us versus them. Only a rights-based approach centered on human dignity can fully dissolve the distinction that enables exclusion.

Ypi concludes that the universal values liberal societies claim to subscribe are most critically tested in their commitment to a world where no one is forced to leave home. I would go further: those values are also tested in whether we welcome those who come, regardless of why they came and whether their arrival serves our interests. The cosmopolitanism of Nathan the Wise was not conditional on economic utility.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The right to movement is a basic human right grounded in individual autonomy and dignity that exists independently of economic or structural conditions.
  2. 02Focusing primarily on class interests risks maintaining the logic of competition between domestic and migrant workers rather than dissolving the distinctions that enable exclusion.
  3. 03The Enlightenment values of freedom and equality require recognizing the inherent claims of every person regardless of their economic utility or legal status.
  4. 04Justice for migrants should be pursued as a matter of individual rights rather than being made conditional on whether it serves broader socioeconomic or class-based goals.
  5. 05A truly cosmopolitan approach must welcome individuals based on their common humanity rather than waiting for the resolution of global economic injustices.
