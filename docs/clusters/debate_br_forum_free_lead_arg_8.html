Beyond the Checkbook: What Roemer Gets Right—and Wrong—About Reform

Governor Roemer’s speech is a poignant reminder that something fundamental has changed in the marrow of American governance. When he contrasts his own days on the House Banking Committee, where Jim Leach could oppose derivatives deregulation because he was “free to think,” with today’s permanent fundraising culture, he illuminates a truth that defies partisan caricature. The problem is not just that politicians sell access; it is that the very rhythm of their working lives is dictated by the need to fill campaign coffers. And Roemer is right that this obsession stands as a gatekeeper to solving every other crisis, from the national debt to the loss of manufacturing jobs. Yet for all his moral clarity, his proposed remedy—a voluntary $100 limit and no PACs—directs our gaze too narrowly at the campaign contribution while ignoring the broader institutional corruption that makes Congress so fragile.

Roemer’s own narrative proves the point. He laments that the Research and Experimentation tax credit, an unmitigated success, has never been made permanent. Why? Because, he says, “any time there’s a permanent and wealthy interest on the other side, you can expect our tax system to make their benefits temporary so that each time there’s an election, there’s an endless list of people calling to donate.” But is the problem really the campaign calls, or is it the endless list of lobbyists who spend their careers cycling through the tax-writing committees and the IRS? The temporary nature of the credit creates a recurrent lobbying bonanza, and even if members of Congress took only $100 checks, those lobbyists would still be there to provide the technical arguments, the fundraisers, and the future jobs that keep the policy in perpetual limbo. Contribution limits alone cannot break the captivity of the committee process, where a handful of members, over months of closed-door markup, can redistribute billions in tax benefits while the public dozes.

Roemer’s star witness, Jim Leach, was free not just because he refused PAC money but because he had the institutional space to study an issue and assert his judgment. That space has shrunk dramatically. Today, members fly in for three-day workweeks, committee hearings are performative and brief, and the real drafting work is outsourced to leadership offices and lobbyist-supplied language. Restoring independence requires easing the fundraising treadmill, to be sure, but it also demands serious changes in how Congress legislates: open rules that allow amendments on the House floor, a ban on the revolving door that sends staffers into K Street for a minimum of six years, an end to anonymous holds in the Senate, and a return to a schedule that forces members to actually stay in Washington and negotiate with one another. These process reforms are not as emotionally satisfying as a clean pledge, but they might do more to restore the kind of deliberation that Roemer remembers.

Moreover, the small-donor revolution Roemer envisions is not a neutral force for moderation. The Tea Party wave of 2010 demonstrated that grassroots fundraising, organized through the Internet and amplified by talk radio and cable news, can produce a Congress just as rigidly ideological and just as contemptuous of compromise as one bought by Wall Street. The $100 donor may be driven as much by anger and cultural grievance as the $2,400 PAC check is driven by economic self-interest. The source of the money matters less than the political culture it feeds. Roemer’s plan does nothing to address the extreme partisan sorting that makes every election a battle for the base and leaves countless Americans without a representative they trust.

Roemer’s diagnosis is urgent, and his personal example is admirable. But the reform agenda must move beyond the contribution check to confront the entire ecosystem of influence: lobbying disclosure, the revolving door, the broken committee system, and the media environment that rewards outrage over reason. A Congress free to lead would not only be free of the donor call; it would have the time, the structure, and the institutional culture to hear one another and to govern. That is a project far larger than any single campaign pledge.
