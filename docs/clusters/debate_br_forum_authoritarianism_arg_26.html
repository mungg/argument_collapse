"While the lead essay correctly identifies that ideological moderation is an ineffective strategy in a nationalized political environment, it overlooks the fact that defeating authoritarianism requires rebuilding trust through local presence and cultural rootedness rather than just national mobilization."

As someone thinking from the vantage point of rural and small-town politics, I find Bonica and Grumbach’s essay both bracing and incomplete. They are right that “moderation” has become a lazy incantation. Many rural Democrats have tried to prove they are not like national Democrats and have still been buried under the national brand. A yard sign saying “independent voice” cannot compete with a media ecosystem telling voters every Democrat is an agent of urban contempt. But it does not follow that local identity and cultural fit no longer matter. In places where Democrats have collapsed, the problem is not that they failed to triangulate enough. It is that they ceased to be present.

The essay’s argument about nationalization is undeniable. Voters in rural counties often know more about national controversies than about their state legislator’s record. Fox News, Facebook, talk radio, and nationalized online politics have made every race feel like a referendum on the same symbolic enemies: liberals, cities, immigrants, professors, bureaucrats, “woke” corporations, and Washington. A Democrat running for county office may be asked about a slogan uttered by an activist in a city a thousand miles away. That is the reality.

Yet nationalization is not total. If it were, no Democrat would ever overperform in hostile territory, no Republican would ever hold on in a blue state, and candidate recruitment would be irrelevant. The exceptions matter not because they disprove polarization, but because they show what kind of politics can still travel against the wind. Voters are more willing to listen to people who seem rooted in their world: a teacher everyone knows, a veteran, a farmer, a sheriff, a nurse, a union local president, a mayor who fixed the water system. This is not simply “moderation.” It is trust.

Bonica and Grumbach gesture toward this when they call for authentic working-class candidates. That point should be central. Rural voters often do not experience the Democratic Party as too left-wing in a policy sense. They experience it as alien. Its messengers sound like people who left, people who judge, people who speak in institutional jargon, people who know how to write grants but not how to repair a road or keep a hospital open. The party’s problem is less a point on the ideological spectrum than a broken relationship.

Consider economic issues. Rural communities need hospitals, broadband, schools, clean water, fair crop prices, addiction treatment, ambulance service, and protection from monopoly power in agriculture, meatpacking, rail, banking, and health care. Many of these needs call for more public investment and more aggressive regulation than centrist Democrats have typically offered. A genuinely populist Democrat could speak to them. But if that Democrat sounds culturally contemptuous, or appears only during election season, the message will not land.

Anti-corruption could be powerful in rural America because people already believe the system is rigged. They see family farms squeezed by agribusiness, local newspapers bought or closed, hospitals consolidated and shuttered, main streets hollowed out, and young people leaving because opportunity has vanished. They know that wealthy outsiders can extract resources and leave communities with pollution, debt, or empty promises. They also see politicians enriching themselves while preaching sacrifice. A campaign against corruption, monopoly, and insider privilege could connect deeply.

But Democrats must be careful about who delivers the message. Rural voters have heard national Democrats promise to fight for them while treating their communities as photo opportunities. They have heard speeches about “rural broadband” from politicians who seem uncomfortable in rural rooms. They have watched policies designed elsewhere arrive with strings, acronyms, and moral instruction. Credibility cannot be manufactured in a media buy. It is built by showing up for years, losing races, helping with local problems, and letting local people shape the agenda.

The moderation debate often becomes a debate over whether Democrats should compromise on guns, religion, immigration, policing, or climate. In rural places, these issues are not abstractions. Guns are tied to family, land, safety, and identity. Churches are often the remaining civic infrastructure. Immigration may be experienced through labor needs, demographic change, or nationalized fear. Policing may look different in a small town than in a big city, though abuses exist everywhere. Climate policy may be understood through droughts and floods, but also through suspicion that urban environmentalists will sacrifice local jobs. A party serious about democracy must be able to speak respectfully in these contexts without surrendering its principles.

That is not pandering. It is democratic politics. If Democrats want rural votes, they should stop treating rural culture as a problem to be solved and start treating rural citizens as co-authors. On climate, that means talking about soil, water, crop insurance, conservation, energy jobs, and utility bills, not just carbon targets. On guns, it means distinguishing between responsible ownership and the gun industry’s absolutism. On immigration, it means acknowledging both the dignity of migrants and the need for orderly systems. On policing, it means public safety and accountability together. On schools, it means respecting parents without letting extremists censor history or target vulnerable students.

Bonica and Grumbach are right that strategic repositioning can look fake. That is why Democrats should not send candidates into rural districts with a list of positions chosen by Washington. They should cultivate leaders whose views arise from the communities themselves. Some will be more culturally conservative than national activists prefer. Some will be economically more populist than donors prefer. Some will speak in ways that make professional Democrats nervous. Good. A party that wants to compete beyond metropolitan America must tolerate genuine local variation.

At the same time, rural Democrats should not be asked to perform endless distancing from the national party while receiving little support. The national brand will not improve unless the party visibly fights for rural institutions. Keeping hospitals open should be democracy work. Breaking agricultural monopolies should be democracy work. Funding local journalism should be democracy work. Protecting mail service, public schools, water systems, and union jobs should be democracy work. When government disappears from rural life except as police, prisons, and paperwork, anti-government politics becomes common sense.

The essay’s turnout argument also looks different from rural America. In many counties, there are not enough Democratic base voters to mobilize. The party must persuade, but persuasion may mean long-term trust-building rather than centrist messaging. It means convincing people that voting Democratic is not a betrayal of place. That cannot be done by pretending cultural disagreements do not exist. It can be done by demonstrating loyalty to the community and by drawing a sharper line between local dignity and the national politicians who exploit rural resentment while serving corporate power.

Authoritarianism has found fertile ground in rural areas partly because it offers recognition. It tells people they are the real nation, that their decline is someone else’s fault, that a strong leader will avenge them. Democrats cannot answer with technocracy. But neither should they answer with contempt for the people drawn to that promise. They need a politics of repair: repair of institutions, repair of economic life, repair of civic trust, repair of the relationship between the party and places it has written off.

So yes, moderation as a magic formula should be abandoned. But presence, respect, and local rootedness should not be dismissed as nostalgia for Tip O’Neill. All politics is not local anymore, but no politics is healthy if it is only national. To defeat authoritarianism, Democrats must rebuild democratic life in the places where authoritarianism feels most at home. That requires not a leftward or centerward lurch, but a return: to county fairs, union halls, school boards, church basements, farm meetings, local newspapers, and the slow work of being known.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Democratic Party's failure in rural areas is less about its position on the ideological spectrum and more about a broken relationship characterized by perceived urban contempt and institutional jargon.
  2. 02Authentic working-class candidates are effective not because they are 'moderates,' but because their local roots and shared identities with voters foster a sense of trust that nationalized media cannot easily erode.
  3. 03An anti-corruption and anti-monopoly platform can resonate deeply in rural communities, provided it is delivered by messengers who have demonstrated long-term loyalty to those places.
  4. 04To compete effectively, the national party must tolerate genuine local variation in candidate platforms rather than imposing a standardized set of positions curated in Washington.
  5. 05Defeating authoritarianism requires a 'politics of repair' that focuses on restoring local institutions like hospitals and schools to prove that democratic government can practically serve rural life.
