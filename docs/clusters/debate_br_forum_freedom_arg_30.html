Messaging Is Not a Sideshow

Aziz Rana wants to convince us that the right's monopoly over "freedom talk" cannot be undone by communications strategy, that what is needed instead is a long march through institutions — unions, workplaces, voting systems, immigration regimes — that would make solidaristic freedom an everyday felt experience rather than a rhetorical claim. He is half right. But the half he gets wrong matters, and dismissing the work of language and persuasion as superficial threatens the very project he wants to build.

Begin with the historical record Rana invokes. The New Deal coalition did not assemble itself out of pre-existing institutional facts. Franklin Roosevelt's "Four Freedoms" speech in 1941 — freedom of speech, freedom of worship, freedom from want, freedom from fear — was not a description of conditions Americans already inhabited. It was a rhetorical act that named aspirations and gave a generation of Americans a vocabulary to demand them. The Wagner Act, Social Security, and the GI Bill came after, not before, the discursive reframing. The same is true of the civil rights movement Rana rightly celebrates. The March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom did not emerge from already-thriving institutions of interracial solidarity; it summoned those institutions into being through extraordinary acts of public language. King's "I Have a Dream" was speech, not infrastructure. The Freedom Rides were performances, not policies.

Rana's framing — institution-building versus messaging — is a false dichotomy. Institutions do not build themselves, and they do not sustain themselves without stories that explain why they matter. The Protecting the Right to Organize Act he mentions has been stalled in the Senate for years not because workers fail to experience workplace domination, but because the political coalition needed to overcome a filibuster has not been assembled, and assembling it requires persuading marginal voters in marginal states that union freedom is their freedom too. That is a communications problem as much as an organizing one.

There is also a strategic question about audience. Rana writes as if the relevant task is to reach the disaffected working class through transformed daily experience. But American politics is decided at the margins, and at those margins are voters who have been told for forty years that "freedom" means low taxes, gun rights, and getting government off their backs. These voters do not work at sites of imminent unionization. Many of them are retired, or self-employed, or work in small businesses where collective bargaining is structurally impossible. For them, the only available route to a different account of freedom is rhetorical — through churches, talk radio, social media, family conversation, and yes, the speeches of politicians they may or may not vote for. Conceding the language to the right while waiting for institutional transformation to do the persuasive work amounts to unilateral disarmament.

Consider what Democratic politicians have actually done when they take freedom language seriously. Elizabeth Warren's stump speeches in 2019–2020 routinely framed antitrust, financial regulation, and child care as freedom issues. Bernie Sanders has done the same with Medicare for All. More recently, Vice President Harris has framed reproductive rights explicitly in the vocabulary of freedom, and Democratic candidates in 2022 successfully ran ads accusing Republicans of stripping women of their liberty. These were communications choices, made by political professionals, and they moved votes. The 2022 midterms — historically anomalous for a first-term president's party — suggest that the rhetorical battlefield is not lost; it is being fought on, with some success.

Rana might respond that such gains are fragile precisely because they are not anchored in transformed daily experience. Fair enough. But the inverse is also true: transformed daily experience without a vocabulary to interpret it produces, historically, not solidarity but resentment. The white steelworker quoted in Jefferson Cowie's Stayin' Alive whom Rana cites — "if what we lived through in the 1950s was not liberation, then liberation never happens" — inhabited a deeply unionized institutional environment. That experience did not automatically produce solidaristic freedom; it produced, by the late 1970s, Reagan Democrats. What made the difference was the story workers told themselves about what their unions and their prosperity meant, and the right won that story.

There is also a deeper philosophical point about how political identities form. Rana's model assumes a kind of materialist sequencing: change the institutions, and consciousness will follow. But the past half-century of political science suggests something more recursive. People interpret their material conditions through pre-existing narrative frames, and they often interpret identical conditions in opposite ways depending on which frame is culturally available. The same factory closure becomes, for one worker, evidence that capitalism has betrayed them; for another, evidence that immigrants and welfare queens are stealing what is rightfully theirs. The institutions did not determine the interpretation. The language did, working in conjunction with media ecosystems, religious affiliations, and partisan signals.

None of this is to dismiss Rana's institutional program. He is correct that a freedom politics without union density, voting rights, and demilitarized foreign policy is a freedom politics of pure exhortation. The PRO Act matters. So does Puerto Rican self-determination, so does decriminalizing border crossing, so does cutting the Pentagon budget. I would happily sign onto every concrete reform he names. The disagreement is about sequencing and emphasis. Rana treats persuasion as downstream; I think it is constitutive. The institutions he wants to build will not be built by people who lack a shared vocabulary for why they matter, and that vocabulary has to be spoken aloud, repeatedly, by candidates and journalists and organizers, before institutional change becomes politically achievable.

There is one more thing to say about the right's appropriation of freedom. Rana attributes it to structural conditions — settler expansion, then global primacy — that emboldened a zero-sum, racialized account. This is partly true but incomplete. The right's freedom talk also reflects deliberate, generational investment by conservative intellectuals, foundations, think tanks, and media operations. From the Mont Pelerin Society to the Federalist Society to talk radio to Fox News to the manufactured grievance machinery of the Tea Party and Trump, the right built an apparatus designed precisely to seize the language of liberty. That apparatus is institutional, yes, but its primary product is rhetoric. The left does not lack institutions in any absolute sense; it lacks an equivalently coordinated rhetorical apparatus committed to a different vision of freedom. Building that apparatus is messaging work, and it is urgent.

Reclaiming freedom, then, requires both the long institutional project Rana describes and a more aggressive, more immediate rhetorical struggle. To say "this is a matter of institution building, not communication strategy" is to set up a contrast that the historical record does not support. The civil rights movement, the New Deal, the suffragists, the abolitionists — all of them built institutions and spoke a language of freedom simultaneously, each reinforcing the other. We should do the same, and we should start now, in the speeches of the next campaign, not after the institutional revolution has already happened.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Historical movements like the New Deal and the civil rights struggle demonstrate that transformative rhetoric and public language summon new institutions into existence rather than merely following them.
  2. 02The choice between institution-building and messaging is a false dichotomy, as institutions require compelling stories to explain their value and to assemble the political coalitions necessary for their survival.
  3. 03Relying solely on transformed daily experience ignores the fact that many voters at the political margins are structurally isolated from the specific institutions, like unions, that the author proposes.
  4. 04Material conditions do not dictate political identity on their own; rather, people interpret their experiences through pre-existing narrative frames that must be contested at the level of language.
  5. 05The right's dominance over 'freedom talk' is the result of a deliberate, generational investment in a coordinated rhetorical apparatus that the left must match to be successful.
  6. 06Waiting for institutional transformation to perform the work of persuasion amounts to unilateral disarmament in an immediate and urgent political struggle.
