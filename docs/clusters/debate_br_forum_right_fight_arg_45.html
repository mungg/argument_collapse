Diversified Gemini

"The essay argues that relying on institutional friction between federal and local agencies is an insufficient substitute for legal and constitutional oversight in protecting civil liberties during counterterrorism efforts."

In “The Right Fight,” Daniel Richman arrives at a deeply pessimistic conclusion disguised as an optimistic one. After outlining the immense complexities of integrating local police into a federal domestic intelligence network, he concludes that traditional mechanisms of accountability—namely, the courts and the legislature—are essentially useless in the realm of counterterrorism. Decisions about who is monitored, who is interviewed, and how communities are policed, he argues, are "simply not amenable to effective judicial or legislative constraint or oversight." Therefore, we must rely on "institutional structure"—the inevitable friction between local mayors and federal bureaucrats—to provide "appropriately tempered zeal."

This redefinition of accountability is a staggering capitulation to the permanent emergency of the post-9/11 era. By arguing that we must rely on the ad-hoc political interests of local police departments to protect civil liberties because the law can no longer do so, Richman abandons the very concept of constitutional rights in favor of a free-market equilibrium of bureaucratic power. We must fiercely reject this premise. Structure is not a substitute for law, and political friction is not a substitute for justice.

Richman’s argument rests on a profound conflation of electoral accountability with legal accountability. He assumes that because mayors are elected, they will restrain police from violating the rights of their constituents. But the history of American civil rights is the history of the majority enthusiastically voting to violate the rights of the minority. Electoral accountability is precisely what produced Jim Crow laws, the internment of Japanese Americans, and the racial profiling of the 1990s. If a local police chief institutes a sweeping surveillance program targeting mosques, and the frightened majority of the city approves of this tactic, electoral accountability will encourage the abuse, not restrain it.

Legal accountability, by contrast, does not depend on the popularity of the victim. It depends on the enforcement of constitutional boundaries by an independent judiciary and the clear articulation of limits by a democratically elected legislature. When the USA PATRIOT Act expands the use of "sneak-and-peek" warrants or allows the FBI to seize library records with administrative subpoenas, the primary defense against the abuse of these powers is not the grumbling of a local police chief. It is the ability of citizens to challenge these laws in court, and the obligation of Congress to write laws that strictly define the scope of executive power.

Richman dismisses these tools too quickly. He argues that even if controversial provisions of the PATRIOT Act were repealed, prosecutors could still use grand-jury subpoenas to get the same information. This is a counsel of despair. The fact that the executive branch possesses immense discretionary power is exactly why legislative oversight and judicial review must be strengthened, not abandoned. Congress has the power to strictly limit how grand jury subpoenas are used in intelligence investigations; courts have the power to review the use of material witness warrants; the legislature can ban the domestic use of data-mining programs. To say these decisions are "not amenable" to oversight is simply to say that Congress and the courts currently lack the political will to do their jobs.

Relying on "institutional structure" to save us provides a false sense of security. It leaves the protection of our most fundamental rights to the vagaries of inter-agency negotiation. Will the NYPD push back against the FBI today? Will the mayor of Detroit stand up to the Department of Homeland Security tomorrow? Rights that depend on the daily fluctuating leverage of local politicians are not rights at all; they are temporary privileges.

As we navigate this new era of domestic intelligence, we must demand more than just bureaucratic friction. We must demand that Congress rewrite the laws governing surveillance to clearly protect the privacy of all Americans. We must demand that the courts stop deferring blindly to claims of "national security" and actively scrutinize the actions of the executive branch. The fight for civil liberties cannot be outsourced to the local beat cop. It must be waged in the halls of Congress and the courtrooms of the federal judiciary. If we accept Richman’s premise that the law can no longer constrain the state, we have already lost the right fight.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Electoral accountability at the local level often encourages the violation of minority rights by responding to the fears of the majority, making it an unreliable safeguard for civil liberties.
  2. 02Legal accountability through an independent judiciary and legislative limits is essential because it protects individuals regardless of the popularity of their cause or identity.
  3. 03The existence of executive discretionary power is a reason to strengthen, rather than abandon, legislative oversight and judicial review.
  4. 04Rights that depend on the shifting political leverage of local officials are merely temporary privileges rather than fundamental constitutional protections.
  5. 05The protection of civil liberties must be actively enforced through the law and the courts rather than being outsourced to bureaucratic negotiations.
