"While Walter Johnson’s critique of racial capitalism and the concept of dehumanization is historically profound, his framework for justice is overly backward-looking and fails to provide the positive, universal principles necessary for constructing a future just society."

Let us begin with a concession: Walter Johnson is a historian of formidable talent and moral seriousness. His essay prosecutes a case against the concept of “dehumanization” with a prosecutor’s zeal and a philosopher’s precision. He is right that the term does more harm than good—that it obscures the true nature of slaveholding violence, patronizes the enslaved, and offers a cheap grace to those of us who come after. And he is right that the history of capitalism cannot be told without the history of slavery, and that the history of slavery cannot be told without the history of race. These are important arguments, and I am grateful to have them made so forcefully.

But I want to suggest that Johnson’s essay, for all its radical ambitions, is curiously conservative in its understanding of what justice requires. By “conservative,” I do not mean politically right-wing—Johnson’s politics are clearly left—but rather something like “rooted in the past.” Johnson’s account of justice is almost entirely backward-looking. It insists that present distributions of privilege and abjection are related to past patterns; that reparations are the only adequate form of redress; that the standpoint from which we should critique modern injustice is the standpoint of the enslaved and the dispossessed. What it does not do is offer a vision of the future. It does not describe, even in outline, what a just society would look like once the wrongs of the past had been acknowledged and repaired. It does not specify the principles that should govern our common life going forward. It leaves us, in the end, with a diagnosis but not a cure.

This backward-looking orientation is not unique to Johnson; it is characteristic of a certain strand of left thought that has gained prominence in recent years, one that is more comfortable with critique than with construction. The reasons for this are understandable. The left has been burned many times by utopian projects that turned into nightmares; by blueprints for a just society that, when implemented, produced new forms of injustice. Better, perhaps, to focus on exposing the injustices of the present than to risk repeating the mistakes of the past. But this posture of permanent critique has costs. It cedes the terrain of the future to those who are perfectly willing to offer their own visions: the libertarian who promises freedom through markets, the nationalist who promises security through walls, the techno-optimist who promises abundance through innovation. If the left cannot say what kind of world it wants, it should not be surprised when other people fill the silence.

Johnson’s essay is, in this sense, a missed opportunity. He has put his finger on something profound: the entanglement of capitalism, racism, and empire in the making of the modern world. He has cited a tradition of thinkers—Du Bois, Williams, Robinson, Davis—who, between them, have provided the intellectual resources for a radical critique of the existing order. And he has gestured toward a notion of justice that goes “beyond liberal notions of human rights.” But beyond is a preposition, not a destination. What lies beyond? What replaces the “bourgeois freedoms of classical liberalism” once we have recognized their inadequacy? Johnson does not say.

Let me try to say something on his behalf, and then explain why I think it is still not enough. One answer to the question “what lies beyond?” is suggested by Johnson’s invocation of the “black radical tradition.” In Robinson’s account, as Johnson summarizes it, the black radical tradition is “the democratic practices and revolutionary thought of black people living under conditions of racial capitalism.” This tradition, Robinson argues, offers a path toward justice that avoids both the “authoritarian” pronouncements of orthodox Marxism and the “simplications” of black nationalism. It is, presumably, an ongoing tradition, one that is still being made and remade in the struggles of the present. But what does it actually prescribe? If we look at the thinkers Johnson names—Du Bois, Williams, Robinson, Davis—we find a range of political commitments that cannot easily be reconciled. Du Bois was, at various points, an integrationist, a pan-Africanist, and a communist. Williams was the prime minister of Trinidad and Tobago who presided over a project of state-led development that disappointed many of his leftist supporters. Robinson was a theorist of the “black radical tradition” who ended his career at the University of California. Angela Davis is a prison abolitionist and a feminist. To invoke these figures as sources of a “tradition” is to paper over real disagreements about strategy, tactics, and ends.

The deeper problem, I think, is that Johnson is trying to derive a normative theory of justice from a historical analysis of injustice. This is a category mistake. Knowing how the world became unjust does not, by itself, tell us what a just world would look like. The history of slavery and racial capitalism can teach us what not to do: do not treat human beings as property; do not steal land from indigenous peoples; do not structure the global economy so that the wealth of some is built on the immiseration of others. These are important lessons, and they have implications for policy—for reparations, for land reform, for labor rights. But they are negative lessons. They tell us what to avoid, not what to pursue. They are like the instructions on a bottle of poison: they can prevent us from drinking it, but they cannot tell us what to drink instead.

A positive theory of justice requires something more: a conception of the good, or at least of the right. It requires an account of what human beings need to flourish, and of what we owe each other as a matter of principle. The liberal rights tradition that Johnson dismisses has, for all its flaws, attempted to provide such an account. It has said, in various formulations, that every person has a claim to certain basic liberties, certain material minimums, and a certain degree of social recognition. These claims can be debated—and they have been, endlessly—but they provide a starting point for argument and a language in which demands can be made. Johnson’s alternative, rooted as it is in a particular history of oppression, struggles to articulate principles that could be accepted by people who do not share that history. If I am the descendant of enslaved people, I can understand why reparations are owed to me. But what is the argument that should persuade the descendant of, say, a Russian Jewish immigrant who arrived in the United States in 1900 with nothing? What do I say to the Hmong refugee whose family was decimated by the CIA’s secret war in Laos? What do I say to the white Appalachian whose ancestors were exploited by coal companies but never owned slaves? Johnson’s framework does not offer clear answers to these questions, because it is built around a specific, albeit foundational, historical wrong rather than around a general account of what human beings owe one another.

This is not to say that a universalist account of justice is easy to construct, or that the liberal tradition has gotten it right. Far from it. The liberal tradition has been blind, again and again, to the ways that its universalism has been undercut by racism, sexism, and imperialism. But the solution to that blindness is not to abandon universalism; it is to make it more genuinely universal—to demand that the circle of concern be expanded to include those who have been left out, and that the principles of justice be tested against the experience of the most vulnerable. That work has been done, brilliantly, by thinkers like Amartya Sen, Martha Nussbaum, and Charles Mills, who have taken the liberal tradition seriously while exposing its failures. Johnson, by contrast, seems to want to throw the whole tradition overboard and start fresh from the history of slavery. I am not convinced that this is possible, or desirable.

Let me conclude with a suggestion. Johnson’s six “virtues” of an account of justice rooted in the history of slavery are, in fact, excellent criteria for evaluating any theory of justice. Any adequate theory should be attentive to global inequalities, to historical legacies, to gender, to the entanglement of race and class, to the relationship between human and environmental exploitation. These are not alternatives to universalist principles; they are tests that any universalist principle must pass. The task before us is not to choose between the history of slavery and the language of rights, but to bring them into productive tension—to use the history of slavery to expose the gaps and hypocrisies in rights discourse, and to use rights discourse to generalize the insights gained from the history of slavery. This is a dialectical project, not a dismissive one. It requires patience, nuance, and a willingness to live with contradiction. But it is, I believe, the only way to honor the legacy of those whom Johnson rightly remembers, while also building a future in which their suffering can finally be redeemed.
