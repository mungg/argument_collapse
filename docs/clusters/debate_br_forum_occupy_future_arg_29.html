"The Occupy movement must reject the myth that the United States is a principled democracy in need of reform and instead recognize that the current system is fundamentally designed for exploitation and must be replaced by entirely new, radical institutions."

The opening premise of this forum—that the United States is "deeply committed to principles" of equality and opportunity, and that our history is merely a "tension" between these pure ideals and our flawed practices—is a comforting liberal myth. It is also a myth that the Occupy movement must unequivocally reject if it is to achieve any meaningful liberation.

The history of the United States is not a story of noble ideals slowly being realized over time. It is a story of an empire founded on the genocide of indigenous peoples and the enslavement of Africans, designed from its inception to protect the property rights of wealthy, white land-owning men. When the editors cite the end of slavery, the extension of the franchise, and the securing of civil rights, they present these as evidence of the system righting itself. In reality, these concessions were bled out of a deeply hostile, violent state apparatus through massive, often bloody, radical resistance. The state did not self-correct; it was forced to yield ground to prevent total revolution.

Occupy Wall Street is powerful precisely because it has begun to shed this historical amnesia. The young people freezing in encampments across the country are not asking to be brought into the fold of the current system; they are recognizing that the system is functioning exactly as it was designed to. The crisis of extreme inequality we see today is not a "disjuncture" or an aberration. It is the logical, inevitable endgame of global capitalism.

Capitalism requires infinite growth on a finite planet. It requires the perpetual exploitation of labor and the commodification of every aspect of human life. The 2008 financial crash was not a mistake caused by a few greedy bankers who strayed from American principles; it was the system operating in its purest form, accumulating wealth at the top while socializing the devastation. Trying to "bring our institutions into better alignment" with democratic ideals is a fool's errand when those institutions—Wall Street, the corporate media, the two-party duopoly, the military-industrial complex—are fundamentally anti-democratic by design.

Therefore, the question is not whether we are entering a moment where the disjuncture is cast into sharp relief. The question is whether we have the courage to stop begging for a seat at a fundamentally corrupt table. Occupy must not be co-opted into a reformist project that seeks merely to regulate the banks a little more or tax the rich a few percentage points higher. Such minor adjustments will quickly be undone by capital, just as the New Deal regulations were methodically dismantled over the past forty years.

We must build an open, democratic movement, yes, but its goal cannot be the preservation of the American mythology. The goal must be the creation of entirely new, horizontal institutions of mutual aid, alternative economies, and radical community self-defense. We must look beyond the borders of the United States and recognize our solidarity with the global working class, the victims of American imperialism abroad, and the indigenous communities still fighting colonization. The future cannot be occupied until the illusions of the past are abandoned.
