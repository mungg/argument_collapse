"The legal system must distinguish between protected, albeit repulsive, fantasy and criminal conduct by requiring proof of intent, agreement, and concrete steps toward a crime rather than punishing speech based on its offensiveness."

The law should not punish people for having ugly fantasies, even fantasies that make the rest of us recoil. But neither should it wait until a woman is in a trunk before treating words as evidence of a crime. The hard question in the “cannibal cop” case is not whether the online talk is disgusting; it plainly is. The question is whether the government can prove that the talk crossed from fantasy into a real plan directed at real people.

American law has long drawn that distinction, though imperfectly. The First Amendment protects advocacy, imagination, role-playing and even violent rhetoric unless it falls into a narrow unprotected category: incitement, solicitation, conspiracy, harassment or a “true threat.” In Watts v. United States, the Supreme Court protected crude anti-presidential hyperbole. In Brandenburg v. Ohio, it protected extremist speech unless it was intended and likely to produce imminent lawless action. In Virginia v. Black, the Court recognized that “true threats” may be punished because they place people in fear and disrupt their lives, but the point is intimidation, not mere offensiveness.

That framework matters online, where people often write in voices and communities that are performative, exaggerated or fictional. The Internet has made it easier for people with bizarre sexual interests or violent imaginations to find one another. Some of that speech is repellant. Some of it may be evidence of mental illness. But a free society cannot criminalize fantasy as such. If private chat-room role-play becomes punishable simply because it is vile, the same logic could reach violent fiction, rap lyrics, horror writing, political rage and sexual subcultures that most people do not understand.

At the same time, the word “fantasy” cannot be a magic shield. A person who says “I want to kidnap Mary,” identifies Mary by name and address, researches her schedule, discusses tools and dates, and coordinates with someone else has moved into a very different zone. The law of conspiracy exists precisely because people who agree to commit a crime and take concrete steps toward it present a danger before the final act. In that setting, words are not being punished as ideas; they are evidence of agreement, intent and preparation.

So the line should be drawn by context and conduct. Courts should ask: Was the speech directed at identifiable people? Did the speaker have apparent access to them? Were there specific plans, dates, prices, routes or methods? Did the speaker take steps outside the fantasy space — surveillance, purchases, database searches, travel, contact with accomplices, attempts to lure the victim? Did the alleged victim reasonably experience the communication as a threat, or was it hidden role-play never meant to reach her? Was there an actual agreement, or only two people elaborating a grotesque story?

In the New York case, those questions are crucial. The fact that the women were real and that a police database may have been used to gather information is far more legally significant than the cannibalism talk itself. A police officer’s access to personal information can turn an otherwise private fantasy into something reasonably alarming. But prosecutors still should have to prove more than depravity. They should have to prove intent, agreement and an overt act toward a crime.

That burden is not a technicality; it is the safeguard that separates criminal justice from moral panic. The more revolting the speech, the more tempting it is to lower the standard. We should resist that temptation. The proper rule is neither “it was only words” nor “it was horrible, therefore illegal.” Words become criminal when they function as threats, solicitations or steps in a concrete criminal plan. Everything short of that may deserve condemnation, therapy, employment consequences or investigation — but not imprisonment.

The distinction between protected speech and criminal threats is among the most consequential and contested questions in First Amendment law. The case of the New York police officer accused of plotting cannibalism illustrates why this line matters and why courts have struggled to draw it with precision.

At the heart of this issue lies the "true threat" doctrine, established in landmark cases from Watts v. United States onward. A true threat is speech that a reasonable listener would understand as a serious expression of intent to harm. The doctrine recognizes that some communications, though falling short of incitement to imminent lawless action, still pose sufficient danger to warrant legal intervention. The key insight is that not all frightening speech is illegal, but some speech crosses from the realm of the offensive or disturbing into the domain of the criminal.

What separates protected online fantasies from actionable threats? The answer involves both objective and subjective elements. Courts look at the context: Who was the audience? Was the statement made in a setting of heightened emotion or political debate? Was it conditional or ambiguous? They also consider the speaker's intent—not merely what the speaker subjectively intended, but what a reasonable recipient would have understood. In the context of online forums and anonymous communication, this analysis becomes more complex. The isolation of online communication can amplify threatening language, but it can also foster hyperbole and role-playing that would be understood as such by reasonable observers.

The facts alleged in the New York case are troubling precisely because they suggest more than idle fantasy. Plots involving detailed planning, research, and actual steps toward commission suggest an intent that goes beyond protected speech. The distinction between fantasy and planning is not always clear, but it matters enormously for legal purposes. First Amendment protection is broad, but it has never extended to agreements to commit crimes or to communications made with the specific intent to frighten victims into changing their behavior.

What concerns me most about cases like this is the temptation to collapse the distinction between speech we find distasteful and speech we can legitimately prohibit. Online communities often develop their own norms and internal enforcement mechanisms. When someone posts violent fantasies, other users may respond with disgust or ostracism. This self-regulation is itself a form of speech-based accountability. The state's power to criminalize should be reserved for communications that cross identifiable legal lines—not merely for statements that disturb or offend reasonable observers.

The rule of law requires that we articulate and apply consistent standards. The true threat doctrine, properly understood, provides such a standard: speech that a reasonable person would understand as a serious expression of intent to commit unlawful violence against a specific person or group falls outside First Amendment protection. What remains protected is speech that, however offensive or disturbing, does not meet this threshold. Maintaining this distinction is essential to preserving both public safety and the robust exchange of ideas that the First Amendment safeguards.

In a free society, the state may punish what people do and what they truly set out to do. It may not punish them simply for the nightmares they describe.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Transcripts of role-playing or online subcultures can easily make performative speech look like criminal intention when stripped of its context.
  2. 02The revolting nature of certain fantasies can bias jurors into assuming guilt, making it dangerous to treat detailed imaginings as evidence of a conspiracy.
  3. 03Words should only be treated as evidence of a crime when accompanied by real-world steps such as surveillance, travel, or the purchase of weapons.
  4. 04Incidental crimes, such as the misuse of police databases, should be punished separately and not used to automatically transform surrounding speech into a kidnapping plot.
  5. 05Treating morally repellent private fantasies as criminal acts creates a chilling effect that threatens the privacy and freedom of writers, artists, and political radicals.
