"Because the contemporary university has been fundamentally captured by corporate and neoliberal interests, intellectuals must move beyond trying to reform academic institutions and instead build independent intellectual structures embedded directly within social movements."

The university has never been the neutral space it claimed to be. From its origins in medieval ecclesiastical institutions to its contemporary incarnation as a site of corporate accumulation, the academy has always been embedded in structures of power that transcend the pursuit of knowledge. Robin D. G. Kelley's essay performs a valuable service in reminding us that the "organic intellectual" must be understood not as an exception within the university but as the model toward which all genuine intellectual work should aspire. Yet I want to extend this argument by examining how the corporate capture of higher education has fundamentally altered the terrain on which intellectuals must now operate—and why the solution lies not in returning to some lost golden age of academic freedom but in building new institutions outside the dying structure of the contemporary university.

The lead essay correctly identifies the neoliberal transformation of higher education as a critical threat to intellectual freedom. The embrace of market fundamentalism, the casualization of labor, the financialization of university governance—these are not mere administrative changes but represent a qualitative shift in the university's social function. Where once the university could plausibly claim to serve the public good, even if that claim was always compromised by racial and gendered exclusions, today it functions explicitly as a training ground for capital accumulation. The chancellor who calls police to disperse student protesters is not betraying some prior commitment to academic values; she is faithfully executing the logic of an institution that has been systematically hollowed out from within.

Yet we must be careful not to mourn a past that never actually existed for most intellectuals. Chomsky's privilege as a tenured MIT professor in 1967 was extraordinary by any measure—then as now, most intellectuals did not enjoy such protection. The difference is that today even this diminished form of academic security is being stripped away. The adjunct who depends on semester-to-semester contracts cannot afford to speak truth to power; the graduate student facing a brutal job market cannot risk the enmity of donors and trustees. The attack on tenure is therefore not incidental but central to the project of silencing dissent. Without job security, there is no academic freedom.

This is why I want to push back against a certain strain of optimism in the lead essay—the suggestion that we might "create a new university" within the shell of the old one. The examples Kelley offers of insurgent intellectual work—the Institute on Inequality and Democracy at UCLA, various activist-in-residence programs—are valuable, but they operate under conditions of institutional survival. They exist because they have not yet attracted sufficient attention to be eliminated. The moment they become genuinely threatening to the existing order, they will be shut down, as the history of academic repression makes abundantly clear.

The more promising model is the one suggested by the very intellectuals Kelley celebrates: C.L.R. James, Claudia Jones, W.E.B. Du Bois. These figures were not primarily defined by their institutional positions—though they occupied important ones—but by their embedding in movements. Their intellectual work was inseparable from organizing, from agitation, from the construction of alternative institutions. The Civil Rights Congress that produced We Charge Genocide! was not a university; it was a political organization that used intellectual work as one tool among many for building power. The Communist Party cells and socialist study groups that shaped a generation of Black radicals were not academic departments; they were sites of political education organized around the needs of struggle.

The lesson here is that intellectual work must be organized outside the university precisely in order to be done properly within it. The campus should be understood as one terrain among many for political engagement, not the primary or privileged site. This means building independent intellectual institutions—publishing houses, research organizations, educational projects—that can survive the vicissitudes of academic politics. It means connecting with labor movements, tenant organizations, prison abolitionists, and other forces that operate outside the academy. It means, most fundamentally, accepting that the university as currently constituted cannot be reformed but must be bypassed.

This is not an argument for abandoning campus struggles. The repression of student protesters at UC Irvine and elsewhere shows that the university remains a site of genuine conflict, and intellectuals who stand with students are performing vital work. But we should be clear about what we are fighting for: not the restoration of academic freedom as traditionally conceived, but the construction of new forms of intellectual practice that can exist independently of institutional capture. The goal is not tenure but liberation; not academic freedom but the freedom that comes from building collective power.

There is also the question of what intellectuals owe to the movements that make their work possible. The lead essay notes that Black antifascist intellectuals "fought for an oppressed class—a large proportion of which would not fight for them." This asymmetry is not accidental; it reflects the structural position of intellectuals within class society. We are often the children of the working class who managed to acquire formal education, or members of the middle class who have chosen to identify with the oppressed. In neither case do we share the material conditions of those we claim to serve. This creates an ethical obligation to be accountable to movements, to take direction from those most affected by the systems we analyze, to accept that our intellectual contributions are valuable only insofar as they contribute to collective struggle.

The organic intellectual, in Gramsci's formulation, is not simply an intellectual who chooses the right side. She is an intellectual who is embedded in a class or social group, who reflects as well as articulates its interests. For most academics, this embedding must be deliberately constructed through sustained engagement with movements, through the subordination of individual career goals to collective needs, through the acceptance of material sacrifice in service of larger purposes. This is harder than it sounds, precisely because the academy trains us to prioritize individual achievement, publication records, and institutional advancement. Unlearning these habits requires ongoing political work, not just occasional acts of solidarity.

The fascism we face is not merely a matter of bad policies or unpleasant politicians, though we have plenty of both. It is a systemic crisis of a social order that has failed to deliver on its promises and cannot do so within its existing structure. Intellectuals have a role to play in understanding this crisis and in contributing to the movements that might transform it. But that role is not the traditional one of detached analysis or neutral truth-telling. It is the role of the organic intellectual: embedded in struggle, accountable to the oppressed, prepared to sacrifice privilege for the sake of collective liberation. This is the responsibility that the age of fascism demands.

  3. 03Insurgent intellectual projects within the university are inherently fragile because they are subject to elimination the moment they pose a genuine threat to the institutional order.
