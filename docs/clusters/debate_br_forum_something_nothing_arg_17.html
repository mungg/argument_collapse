Diversified Claude

"The United States should adopt a 'counterterrorism-plus' strategy in Afghanistan that focuses narrowly on preventing transnational terrorist attacks rather than pursuing an unrealistic and costly state-building counterinsurgency."

Rosen's essay is sharp on the failures of counterinsurgency and weak on what the actual alternative looks like. I want to fill in that alternative, because the strongest argument against the McChrystal plan is not that it is impossible — Rosen makes that case capably — but that something narrower and cheaper would serve American interests at least as well, and probably better.

Call it counterterrorism-plus. The basic idea was advanced inside the Obama administration during the autumn review by Vice President Biden, and was substantially rejected, but it deserves more sustained attention than it has received in either the official discussion or in Rosen's critique.

The proposition is this. The American national interest in Afghanistan is narrowly bounded. It consists of preventing the country from again becoming an operational base for transnational terrorist attacks against the United States and its allies. It does not consist of building a centralized Afghan state, or of defeating the Taliban as a political movement, or of guaranteeing the rights of Afghan women, or of any of the other expanded purposes the war has acquired. Each of these expanded purposes is morally serious. None of them is a vital American interest.

If our actual interest is narrow, our actual means can be narrow too. A counterterrorism-plus posture would consist of: a residual force of perhaps 15,000 to 20,000 American troops, concentrated near major Afghan urban centers and key bases, focused on training the Afghan special operations forces and on direct action against high-value targets; sustained intelligence and surveillance presence, including the drone campaign in the Pakistani tribal areas; continued bilateral support for the Afghan national army at a modest level; and a diplomatic effort to engage regional actors — Pakistan, Iran, India, Russia, China, and the Central Asian states — in a settlement framework that recognizes their interests and constrains their proxies.

What we would not do, under this posture, is attempt to clear, hold, and build in every district of Helmand and Kandahar. We would not flood the country with infantry brigades. We would not pretend that the Karzai government has either the legitimacy or the capacity to extend its writ across Pashtun territory. We would not condition our withdrawal on the success of a state-building project that has, as Rosen correctly notes, no historical precedent in Afghan history.

Rosen's essay implicitly rejects this option, because his closing argument is that al Qaeda itself is not a serious enough threat to justify a sustained American military commitment of any kind. I think he is wrong about this, and I want to explain why.

The threat from al Qaeda is not zero, and it is not unbounded. It sits somewhere in between, and the policy challenge is to calibrate our response to its actual scale. Rosen is right that 9/11 was an attack by al Qaeda's "A-team" against a "slumbering nation," and that a more vigilant United States should be much harder to attack than it was on September 10, 2001. He is right that the response to the attacks did far more economic damage to the United States than the attacks themselves. He is right that al Qaeda's appeal flows from political grievances — American support for Israel, for friendly Arab dictators, for the post-2003 occupation of Iraq — that long predate it and that will outlast it.

What he underplays is the persistence of the operational threat. Since 2001, al Qaeda or affiliated actors have conducted significant attacks in Madrid, London, Bali, Istanbul, Mumbai, and elsewhere, plus a long list of attempted attacks in the United States itself, several of which came close to succeeding. The Christmas Day attempt on Northwest 253, just a week before Rosen's essay went to press, is a fresh reminder that the threat is real and current. The fact that catastrophic attacks have not been more frequent is in significant part because of sustained intelligence, financial, and military pressure on the network — pressure that the counterterrorism-plus posture would maintain.

This is where I think the alternative formulation has real force. It accepts most of Rosen's structural critique of COIN-as-state-building. It accepts that the McChrystal plan is unrealistic on its own terms. It accepts that the cost-benefit calculation of full-scale counterinsurgency has tipped against us. But it does not require us to embrace the harder claim that al Qaeda was always essentially a manageable problem we could have ignored.

There are objections to this option, and they should be aired. The most serious is that the residual force model has a poor track record. The American experience in Lebanon in 1983, the Israeli experience in southern Lebanon, the British experience in many places — these are cautionary tales about small footprints that turn out to be just large enough to absorb casualties and just small enough to lack leverage. There is also the question of what happens to Kabul if the Americans visibly draw down. The Taliban may well overrun southern districts. They may besiege provincial capitals. The Karzai government may collapse. None of these outcomes is good, and the counterterrorism-plus advocates need to be honest about them rather than pretending that a smaller posture produces clean results.

But the McChrystal plan does not produce clean results either, as Rosen's reporting documents in detail. It produces years of escalating commitment in pursuit of a transition that cannot be executed. The choice is not between a costly success and a costly failure; it is between two costly failures with different cost structures and different residual capacities. A counterterrorism-plus posture preserves the ability to act against direct threats while accepting that we cannot determine Afghan political outcomes. A full-scale counterinsurgency commitment ties American capability to Afghan political outcomes we cannot in fact deliver.

Rosen's essay does the field a service by puncturing the Petraeus-McChrystal mystique and the Beltway pundit class that sustains it. But his conclusion — that we should essentially walk away because the underlying threat has been exaggerated — is harder to defend than he allows. The right conclusion is a middle position that almost no one in the current debate is willing to occupy because it has neither the moral satisfaction of withdrawal nor the strategic ambition of escalation. That is precisely why it deserves more attention than it has received.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The American national interest in Afghanistan is limited to preventing the country from becoming an operational base for terrorists, not transforming its political or social structure.
  2. 02A small residual force of 15,000 to 20,000 troops focused on special operations and intelligence is sufficient to maintain necessary pressure on al Qaeda.
  3. 03The McChrystal plan's goal of 'clear, hold, and build' is based on the false premise that the Karzai government has the legitimacy or capacity to govern Pashtun territory.
  4. 04Contrary to Rosen’s view, al Qaeda remains a persistent operational threat that requires a sustained, albeit calibrated, military and intelligence presence.
  5. 05A narrower strategy is preferable because it decouples American security from Afghan political outcomes that the United States cannot realistically deliver.
  6. 06A diplomatic framework involving regional actors like Pakistan and Iran is a more effective way to manage Afghan stability than a massive infantry presence.
