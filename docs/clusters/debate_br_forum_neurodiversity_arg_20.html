"While Robert Chapman’s critique of 'Liberal Neurodiversity' is valid, the movement should not abandon rights-based frameworks and liberal reforms, as they provide essential protections and tools for survival that can coexist with more radical, systemic goals."

Robert Chapman’s call to transcend “Liberal Neurodiversity” and build a politics of Neurodivergent Power is bracing and, in many ways, inspiring. The essay rightly spotlights the structural limits of a rights-based framework that often bends to the demands of capital, leaving behind the most marginalized among us. I share Chapman’s fury at a world that incarcerates neurodivergent people, denies them life-saving care, and valorizes our “superpowers” only when they can be exploited for profit. The diagnosis of elite capture is accurate: we’ve seen our radical vocabularies repackaged as corporate inclusivity theater. And yet, I worry that in our eagerness to move beyond liberal reformism, we risk discarding tools that remain essential for the survival and flourishing of millions—tools that, for many, are the only bulwark against utter abandonment.

Let me be clear: I am not a starry-eyed defender of the Americans with Disabilities Act or its global counterparts. I know that “reasonable accommodations” are often interpreted in ways that serve employers, not employees. I’ve watched autistic friends lose jobs because their request for a predictable schedule was deemed an undue burden, while the company spent more on a single executive lunch. The school-to-incarceration pipeline Chapman describes is real, and it tears apart families and communities. But we should not mistake the inadequacy of existing laws for their irrelevance. The ADA, the Equality Act in the UK, and similar legislation were not handed down by benevolent state actors; they were won through decades of relentless, often deeply unglamorous organizing—sit-ins, lobbying, test cases, and personal sacrifice. They are imperfect instruments, but they have also stopped Burger King from rescinding a job offer because someone needed a coach, kept some disabled children out of electric shock torture, and made it possible for my own dyspraxic child to receive extra time on exams and a quiet room in which to take them. These victories are fragile; in many places, they are being rolled back under austerity and reactionary politics. Jettisoning rights-based advocacy now would be akin to discarding a leaky lifeboat while still in the middle of the ocean.

Chapman’s analogy to the Black Power movement is evocative but deserves closer scrutiny. Stokely Carmichael’s call for Black Power did not mean abandoning the fight against discriminatory laws; it added a demand for collective self-determination and economic justice. The NAACP and the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee did not dissolve—they coexisted, sometimes in tension, with the Panthers. Similarly, a neurodivergent movement can and must contain multitudes. We can grieve the co-optation of “superpowers” discourse and still celebrate the fact that some autistic people now feel proud enough of their identity to demand workplace adjustments. We can denounce the prison-industrial complex and still use disability rights law to challenge the deadly conditions inside prisons. The binary that Chapman posits—liberal reformism versus revolutionary anticapitalism—is too sharp. What about the vast middle ground of mutual aid networks, cooperative workplaces, community-based crisis alternatives, and universal design advocacy? These are not simply liberal concessions; they prefigure a different world while meeting immediate needs and can be the ground on which a more radical consciousness grows.

Moreover, I worry about what gets lost when we center the demand for a revolutionary party. Chapman, referencing Jodi Dean, suggests that without such a party, progressive energies dissipate. I do not share that Leninist certainty. Many neurodivergent people—particularly those with intellectual disabilities, chronic fatigue, or executive function challenges—will never be able to participate in the kind of disciplined, cadre-based organizing that party building demands. That is not a failure of will; it is a fact of our bodyminds. If our liberatory politics require sustained attendance at meetings, quick decision-making, and the navigation of complex factional dynamics, we will reproduce the neuronormative exclusions we claim to oppose. Instead, we need forms of collective power that honor our diverse capacities: pods, affinity groups, network forms of solidarity that allow people to move in and out as their energy permits. The very structure of our organizing must embody the anti-ableist future we desire.

Finally, Chapman’s internationalist vision, while moving, tends to flatten the very different conditions facing neurodivergent people across the globe. In many countries, disability rights legislation barely exists, or exists only on paper. For a parent in a rural part of the Global South whose child is diagnosed with epilepsy and learning disabilities, the immediate need is access to basic medication, education, and freedom from chaining and institutional abuse. Liberal rights discourse, for all its faults, has provided a shared language that transnational disabled people’s organizations have used to pressure governments and international bodies. The UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities is a product of this flawed, reformist tradition, but it has given activists in Nigeria, Bangladesh, and Peru a tool to demand community-based services and legal capacity. Abolishing that tradition in favor of a singular anticapitalist struggle risks leaving those comrades without usable leverage.

None of this means we should stop dreaming of a world beyond capitalism, neuronormativity, and empire. But we can walk and chew gum at the same time. We can file the lawsuit and build the alternative. We can accept the imperfect protections of the state while cultivating the power to eventually transcend it. The Black Panthers ran free breakfast programs and health clinics—reforms within the system that simultaneously built revolutionary consciousness. Liberal Neurodiversity is not our final destination, but it remains a vital, life-sustaining vehicle for many of us on the road. Let’s keep its engine running even as we make the map for what comes next.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Existing disability rights legislation, though imperfect and often co-opted by capital, was won through hard-fought activism and provides a necessary defense against total abandonment and abuse.
  2. 02The choice between liberal reformism and revolutionary anticapitalism is a false binary; a robust movement can utilize legal rights while simultaneously building mutual aid and alternative community structures.
  3. 03Centering a revolutionary party as the primary vehicle for change risks reproducing neuronormative exclusions by demanding levels of executive function and social navigation that many neurodivergent people cannot sustain.
  4. 04Discarding liberal rights discourse ignores its practical utility for activists in the Global South who use international rights frameworks to demand basic services and an end to institutional abuse.
  5. 05The neurodiversity movement should emulate the Black Panthers by providing immediate, life-sustaining reforms within the current system as a means of building the consciousness necessary for long-term transformation.
