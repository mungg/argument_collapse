"To effectively combat sexual abuse, the military justice system requires comprehensive reforms that prioritize the victim's experience and institutional trust over narrow changes to prosecutorial authority."

The debate over military sexual assault often becomes a debate about who gets to prosecute: commanders or independent lawyers. That question matters, but it is too narrow. The crisis is not only that too few offenders are convicted. It is that too many service members believe the institution will punish them for telling the truth.

A rape prosecution is only one point in a long chain of decisions. A victim must decide whether to report. A supervisor must decide whether to believe her or him. Investigators must decide how to question witnesses. Doctors must decide whether to preserve evidence. A commander must decide whether the victim can be moved, whether the accused should be separated, and whether the rest of the unit will be allowed to retaliate through gossip, ostracism or career sabotage. Failure at any stage can make a later trial impossible.

For that reason, reform should begin with the victim’s actual experience. Every service member who reports sexual assault should have an independent lawyer whose sole client is the victim, not the command. The services have begun experimenting with special victims’ counsel, and that idea should be expanded. Victims need clear advice about restricted and unrestricted reporting, privacy, military protective orders, transfers and the risks of retaliation.

Second, investigations should be handled by trained professionals, not improvised by a local command that may know both parties. Sex assault cases are difficult even in civilian jurisdictions; they require expertise in trauma, forensic evidence and the dynamics of coercion. The military should create specialized teams with enough independence to be trusted.

Third, retaliation must be punished. If a young sailor or soldier sees that reporting assault leads to being labeled a liar, a troublemaker or “not a team player,” no reform in the courtroom will matter. Commanders should be evaluated on whether victims in their units are protected after reporting, not simply on whether their units avoid scandal.

Finally, Congress should limit commanders’ power to overturn convictions and require review when serious cases are not charged. Whether prosecution authority is fully removed from the chain of command or not, secretive discretion is no longer acceptable.

The military asks extraordinary loyalty from its members. It owes them more than slogans about zero tolerance. It owes them a system that a frightened 19-year-old private can trust before, during and after a report.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Every service member who reports an assault should be provided with an independent lawyer dedicated solely to the victim's interests and legal guidance.
  2. 02Sexual assault investigations should be conducted by specialized, independent teams of trained professionals rather than local commands.
  3. 03The military must implement strict accountability and punishment for retaliation against victims to ensure that reporting does not lead to career sabotage or social ostracism.
  4. 04Congress should restrict the power of commanders to unilaterally overturn convictions and mandate transparency when serious cases are not prosecuted.

"To effectively combat sexual assault, the military justice system must be reformed by prioritizing the needs and protections of the victim rather than focusing solely on the rights of the accused or the architecture of command."

Start with the Victim

Most of the current argument is structured around the accused — who decides to charge him, who can overturn his conviction, who sits on his panel. That is the wrong starting point. If you want a military justice system that actually addresses sexual assault, design it around the person who has to walk into an office and report being raped by a colleague she will see at formation tomorrow morning.

From that vantage point, several reforms are non-negotiable.

A complainant must have her own lawyer, independent of the prosecution, from the moment she reports. The Air Force's Special Victims Counsel program has demonstrated, in just a few months, that victims with counsel are dramatically more likely to stay with the process. This should be law across all services.

A complainant must be able to transfer, immediately and without career penalty, out of the unit of the accused. Right now the burden of separation falls on her — informally, through ostracism and reassignment — while the accused often remains in place. Reverse the default.

A complainant's prior sexual history, mental health records, and personal communications must be protected from fishing expeditions by defense counsel beyond what civilian rape-shield laws allow. The military's looser discovery norms are weaponized against victims.

A commander's power to grant post-trial clemency — to wave away a jury's guilty verdict, as happened in the Wilkerson case — must end. Whatever its origin, it now functions as a betrayal of every panel that does the hard work of convicting.

And retaliation against reporters must be prosecuted as a distinct offense, aggressively. Surveys show a majority of victims who report experience some form of professional retaliation. Until that changes, every other reform is theater.

The broader question of whether prosecutorial authority should be moved outside the chain of command can wait — or rather, it should be informed by whether these victim-centered reforms work. Begin where the system fails most concretely. The architecture of command can be debated; the indignity inflicted on victims cannot.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Every complainant should be provided with an independent lawyer immediately upon reporting to ensure they remain engaged with the legal process.
  2. 02Victims must be granted the right to transfer out of the accused's unit immediately and without career penalty to prevent ostracism and professional harm.
  3. 03Military discovery norms must be tightened to protect a victim's sexual history, mental health records, and personal communications from defense fishing expeditions.
  4. 04The authority of commanders to grant post-trial clemency and overturn jury convictions must be abolished to maintain the integrity of the judicial process.
  5. 05Retaliation against those who report sexual assault must be prosecuted as a distinct and aggressive offense to eliminate the professional risks of seeking justice.
