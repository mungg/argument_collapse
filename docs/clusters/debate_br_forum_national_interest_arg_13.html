"The United States should pursue a strategy of selective engagement rather than offshore balancing in Europe, maintaining NATO as a vital institutional anchor to preserve continental stability and facilitate global cooperation."

Stephen Walt argues that Europe no longer needs a large American security role. On the surface, this seems obvious. The Red Army is gone, Germany is unified and democratic, the European Union has expanded, and war among the great powers of Western Europe is almost unimaginable. Why should American troops remain in Europe in large numbers? Why should NATO continue to occupy the center of transatlantic relations? The answer is that Europe’s stability is more recent, more incomplete, and more useful to American interests than Walt’s offshore-balancing framework suggests.

Walt is correct that Cold War deployments should not be preserved by inertia. The United States does not need to defend the Fulda Gap. Europe should spend more intelligently on defense and assume greater responsibility for crises on its own continent. American officials have complained for years, with reason, about European free-riding. There is no strategic logic in maintaining every base, headquarters, and heavy unit left over from another era.

But the American presence in Europe is not simply a tripwire against invasion. It is the institutional anchor of a democratic security community that has transformed the continent. NATO helped solve the German problem by embedding German power in an alliance. It reassured smaller states. It tied the United States permanently to European security, preventing the renationalization of defense policies. After 1989, NATO enlargement helped consolidate democracy in Central and Eastern Europe by giving new democracies a framework for civilian control, military reform, and Western integration. Poland, Hungary, the Czech Republic, and the Baltic states did not seek NATO membership because they expected an imminent Russian invasion. They sought it because institutions shape political identity and strategic confidence.

Walt’s claim that Europe faces no significant external military threats is mostly true in the narrow sense. Russia is weak compared with the Soviet Union. Yet Russia remains nuclear-armed, politically uncertain, and increasingly resentful of Western influence in its former empire. The wars in Chechnya, pressure on Georgia, and disputes over Ukraine’s political direction show that Europe’s eastern frontier is not geopolitically dead. The recent drama in Ukraine’s presidential election, with mass protests against fraud and intense Russian interest, should remind us that the post-Soviet space remains contested. A visible American commitment to Europe helps deter coercion and gives vulnerable democracies confidence.

The Balkans also caution against complacency. In the early 1990s, Europeans announced that the hour of Europe had arrived. It had not. Bosnia burned until the United States became decisively engaged. Kosovo required NATO action. Peace in both places remains dependent on international troops and diplomacy. These missions are not glorious, but they have prevented renewed mass violence. A Europe left entirely to its own devices might manage such crises better today than it did a decade ago, but that improvement owes much to NATO habits and American participation.

Moreover, NATO is not only about Europe. Since September 11, the alliance invoked Article 5 for the first time in its history in defense of the United States. European allies have served in Afghanistan. They provide intelligence, police cooperation, financial tracking, and legitimacy in counterterrorism. Even where European publics opposed the Iraq war, European governments and institutions remain indispensable partners in dealing with Iran, securing nuclear materials, stabilizing Afghanistan, and managing relations with Russia. If Washington downgrades NATO too sharply, it may save money while losing political machinery that cannot be quickly rebuilt.

Walt favors multilateral institutions as sources of legitimacy, yet offshore balancing could weaken one of the most successful multilateral security institutions ever created. NATO’s value lies not only in military capabilities but in consultation. It forces Americans and Europeans to argue before crises harden. The Bush administration’s gravest error was not that it maintained alliances; it was that it treated them as optional ornaments. A wiser United States would use NATO more, not less, as a venue for reconciling strategy and legitimacy.

Of course, Europeans must adapt too. They cannot demand American consultation while refusing meaningful capabilities. The gap between European rhetoric and military readiness is real. Many European armies remain configured for territorial defense or social employment rather than deployable missions. Defense spending is often inefficient. If Europe wants influence in Washington, it must be able to act beyond communiqués. The European Union’s emerging security and defense policy can complement NATO if it produces usable forces rather than bureaucratic rivalry. A more capable Europe would strengthen, not weaken, the Atlantic partnership.

The proper policy is therefore not American withdrawal from Europe, but transformation. The United States should reduce heavy ground forces designed for a Soviet threat and retain lighter, more deployable units, airlift, command structures, intelligence links, and training facilities. It should encourage European leadership in nearby crises while remaining engaged enough to shape outcomes. It should support EU integration where it increases European capacity, while resisting efforts to define European identity in opposition to America. Above all, it should restore the habit of consultation that Iraq badly damaged.

Walt’s “hard-to-get” recommendation is particularly ill-suited to Europe. Allies are not children who need to be made anxious so they behave. They are democratic partners whose publics must be persuaded that cooperation with America serves shared purposes. If Washington deliberately makes its support seem conditional and scarce, European leaders will face stronger incentives to build an identity independent of, or opposed to, the United States. Some degree of European autonomy is healthy. An Atlantic divorce would not be.

Nor should Americans assume that European criticism is simply balancing behavior to be discouraged. Much of it has been a response to U.S. policy errors: the Iraq war, disdain for treaties, rhetoric about “old Europe,” and Guantanamo. When friends warn that America is squandering legitimacy, they may be doing Washington a favor. A mature great power should distinguish between obstruction and counsel.

Walt is right that America’s role in Europe can no longer be justified by habit. It must be justified by present interests. Those interests are substantial: keeping Europe whole and free, integrating new democracies, managing Russia’s evolution, preventing Balkan relapse, cooperating against terrorism and proliferation, and sustaining the institutional core of the liberal order. None requires massive permanent garrisons. All require more than offshore distance.

The United States should not be Europe’s pacifier forever. But neither should it forget that the peace of Europe is one of the great achievements of American statecraft. To dismantle too much of that achievement in the name of strategic tidiness would be a mistake. The task is to update the Atlantic bargain: fewer Cold War relics, more deployable capabilities, more equal consultation, and a renewed commitment to law and legitimacy. That is not global hegemony. It is selective engagement at its best.
