"While the United States should adopt a more restrained foreign policy and reject preventive war, it must not abandon the promotion of democracy and human rights through non-military means."

Stephen Walt’s plea for restraint is welcome after the excesses of Iraq, but it risks reviving an older American habit: sacrificing democratic aspirations abroad in the name of stability. He argues that the United States should preserve primacy through offshore balancing, reduce its military footprint, avoid crusades, and lead mainly by example. Much of this is sensible. Yet for those living under authoritarian rule, a foreign policy that treats regime character as secondary can sound less like wisdom than abandonment.

The Bush administration has damaged the cause of democracy by associating it with invasion. That damage is real. When American officials speak of liberty while images from Abu Ghraib circulate around the world, dissidents suffer. Autocrats point to Iraq and say: this is what American democracy means—chaos, humiliation, foreign occupation. The Iraq war has made it easier for regimes in the Middle East, Central Asia, Russia, and elsewhere to crack down in the name of sovereignty and anti-terrorism.

But the conclusion should not be that democracy promotion is inherently hubristic. The conclusion should be that means matter. There is a vast difference between supporting free institutions and imposing them by force. The former helped transform parts of Europe, Latin America, and Asia over the last several decades. The latter is a gamble that can discredit the very values it claims to advance.

Walt is right that America should rely more on example. The United States attracts others when its own institutions are seen as fair, open, and self-correcting. Foreign students, independent courts, a free press, religious pluralism, and peaceful transfers of power are strategic assets. Visa barriers that keep students out, detention policies that evade law, and rhetoric that divides the world into good and evil all weaken those assets. Public diplomacy cannot sell a product that policy contradicts.

Yet example alone is not enough. Dissidents in closed societies often need outside attention, funding, training, broadcasting, and protection. Independent newspapers, election monitors, women’s groups, labor unions, legal-aid centers, and human-rights organizations do not grow automatically under dictatorships. They are harassed, infiltrated, taxed, censored, and sometimes murdered. International support can help them survive. To call this imperialism is to accept the dictator’s definition of sovereignty.

The recent events in Ukraine show what is at stake. The popular mobilization against electoral fraud was not an American coup, as Moscow and local authoritarians would like to claim. It was a civic uprising by Ukrainians who wanted their votes counted. But Western support for election monitoring, civil society, independent media, and diplomatic pressure mattered. So did the prospect of integration with European institutions. Democracy assistance did not impose an outcome; it helped citizens defend one. A grand strategy that focuses mainly on offshore balancing has trouble explaining why such efforts matter.

The same is true in the Arab world. Walt correctly argues that American support for autocratic allies has undermined U.S. credibility. For decades Washington has spoken of freedom while embracing rulers who jail opponents, censor speech, and treat women as legal minors. This hypocrisy has fed radicalism. But Walt’s proposed return to balancing could perpetuate it if “access,” “stability,” and “oil” again become the dominant terms. The United States should not invade countries to make them democratic, but it should stop protecting autocrats from peaceful pressure.

This means attaching political conditions to aid, especially to Egypt and other large recipients. It means speaking publicly about prisoners of conscience, not only privately about counterterrorism cooperation. It means supporting independent education, uncensored media, judicial reform, and women’s rights. It means working with Islamists who accept peaceful democratic rules rather than treating all religious politics as terrorism. It means accepting that real elections may produce governments critical of American policy. Democracy is not genuine if Washington supports it only when its preferred candidates win.

Walt’s Israel–Palestine argument intersects with this issue. The United States cannot credibly advocate Arab freedom while Palestinians live under occupation and settlement expansion. A viable Palestinian state is not only a diplomatic objective; it is a test of whether American talk of rights applies universally. At the same time, Palestinian democracy requires more than statehood. It requires accountable institutions, an end to corruption, security forces under civilian authority, and rejection of attacks on civilians. Supporting Palestinian rights should not mean romanticizing armed factions. Supporting Israel’s security should not mean excusing permanent occupation. A democracy-centered policy would insist on both peoples’ political rights.

Walt’s skepticism about preventive war is essential. The Iraq invasion should warn democracy advocates against shortcuts. Tyranny creates habits of fear, distrust, and dependency that do not disappear when a statue falls. Institutions must be built, and they require legitimacy. Foreign soldiers are poor midwives for national self-government, especially when they do not speak the language and are seen through the history of colonialism. The courage of Iraqis voting this week deserves admiration, but their bravery does not settle the question of whether the war was wise. Democracy born under occupation faces burdens no purple finger can erase.

Still, the Iraqi election also cautions against cynicism. Millions of Iraqis have demonstrated that they want a voice in their future. Those who opposed the war should not respond by minimizing that fact. The United States now has a responsibility to help Iraqis build institutions and to internationalize assistance as much as possible, while making clear that it seeks no permanent domination. Withdrawal should be tied to Iraqi capacity and political agreement, not to American impatience alone. Having broken the state, America cannot simply sail offshore.

Walt’s nonproliferation proposals also need a democratic dimension. Security assurances to regimes such as North Korea and Iran may be necessary to prevent nuclear weapons, but they should not become moral absolution. Negotiating with dictators is sometimes unavoidable. But agreements should be paired with human-rights pressure, information flows, and support for civil society. The Helsinki process during the Cold War showed that security diplomacy and human rights need not be separate. Authoritarian regimes fear not only invasion but their own citizens. A wise policy reduces incentives for nuclearization without consigning those citizens to silence.

The danger in Walt’s realism is that it treats legitimacy primarily as something America needs in order to maintain primacy. But legitimacy also belongs to people struggling against their rulers. A government that tortures, censors, and rules without consent lacks legitimacy even if it helps balance a rival. American policy should recognize that fact. Not every illegitimate regime can or should be overthrown by outsiders; most should not. But neither should they be treated as morally equivalent partners in a chessboard of power.

The right synthesis is democratic restraint. Reject preventive war. Reduce unnecessary bases. Restore law. Work through allies. But keep democracy and human rights near the center of policy, pursued through nonmilitary tools and consistent standards. Stop giving blank checks to friendly dictators. Support peaceful reformers before crises explode. Accept that freedom may produce governments that disagree with us. That is the price of taking democracy seriously.

America’s example matters, but so does its solidarity. People who risk prison to publish, organize, vote, and worship freely listen carefully to what Washington says and does. They do not ask the United States to rule them. They ask that it not strengthen the hands that hold them down. A grand strategy worthy of a democratic nation should answer that plea.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The failure of the Iraq war demonstrates that democracy cannot be imposed by force, but it should not discredit the support of free institutions through diplomatic and civic assistance.
  2. 02Leading by example is necessary but insufficient, as dissidents in closed societies require active international support, funding, and protection to survive authoritarian repression.
  3. 03The United States must end its hypocrisy of supporting autocratic allies for the sake of stability and instead attach political conditions to foreign aid.
  4. 04A credible commitment to universal rights requires the U.S. to actively support both Palestinian statehood and the development of accountable, democratic Palestinian institutions.
  5. 05Grand strategy should prioritize 'democratic restraint,' which combines military forbearance with a consistent, non-military commitment to global human rights and peaceful reformers.
  6. 06Nonproliferation efforts and security negotiations with dictators should be paired with human rights pressure rather than offering regimes moral absolution in exchange for stability.
