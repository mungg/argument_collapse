"The left should reclaim the language of freedom through a pragmatic, issue-based strategy that addresses immediate threats to individual liberty rather than through the lead essay's proposed radical structural overhaul and academic reframing of American history."

A Democratic Pragmatist's Dissent

Aziz Rana's essay is a thoughtful work of intellectual history grafted onto a political program. The history is interesting. The political program, I want to suggest, is mostly wishful thinking dressed up in academic prose, and Democrats who take it seriously will lose more elections than they win.

Begin with the diagnosis. Rana says the right's hold on freedom-language reflects deep structural features of American history — settler conquest, imperial primacy, the entanglement of racial domination with individualist liberty. As a historical thesis this is partly true. As a guide to current politics it is nearly useless. The voters Democrats need to win — suburban moderates, exurban independents, working-class voters across racial lines — are not making decisions based on Lockean settler ideology. They are making decisions based on inflation, immigration, crime, schools, and whether they trust the person on the ballot to be a reasonable steward of public institutions. To address them with a vocabulary borrowed from postcolonial theory is to lose them before the argument starts.

Consider the recent electoral evidence. In 2022, Democrats overperformed expectations, holding the Senate and minimizing House losses, primarily by running on three issues: defending democracy against election denial, defending reproductive freedom against Dobbs, and casting MAGA Republicans as too extreme for mainstream voters. None of these messages required a settler-imperial reframing of American freedom. They worked because they spoke directly to threats voters could see and feared. Democratic governors elected in red and purple states — Whitmer in Michigan, Shapiro in Pennsylvania, Kelly in Kansas — won by speaking ordinary English, addressing pocketbook concerns, and presenting themselves as reasonable adults. They did not win by reading Adom Getachew.

Rana's program, by contrast, is a wishlist of long-term structural reforms — sectoral bargaining, decriminalized immigration, defense cuts of historic magnitude, voting reforms that would require constitutional amendments or Supreme Court overhauls, reparative policies, presumptive admission for migrants. Each of these is, on its merits, debatable. Few of them command majority support in current polling. Several of them are political poison in the swing districts that decide American elections. Bundling them together as the program of a "freedom" politics does not make them more popular; it makes them more vulnerable to caricature, because it invites opponents to point at the most controversial item in the bundle and ask voters whether they really want that.

Decriminalize border crossings? That is currently the position of approximately ten percent of the American electorate. Defense cuts to fund redistribution? Try running on that in Norfolk or San Diego. Reparations? Polling consistently shows majority opposition, including among substantial minorities of Black voters who find existing proposals inadequate or symbolic. None of these positions are unreasonable as policy debates. As electoral platforms, in the country we actually live in, they are catastrophic.

The pragmatist response to the right's appropriation of freedom-language is simpler and more effective than Rana's structural project. It is to take the language back, item by item, on issues where ordinary Americans already feel their freedom under threat from conservative governance, and to do so without the freight of academic theorizing.

Reproductive freedom: working, demonstrably, in state after state. Keep using it.

Freedom to read, freedom from book bans, freedom from religious imposition in public schools: working in suburban districts where parents have soured on conservative culture-war overreach.

Economic freedom from corporate domination: works in messaging when paired with specific antitrust, labor, and consumer protection enforcement. Lina Khan's FTC has done more for left freedom-talk than a dozen think tank essays.

Freedom from gun violence: working with younger voters and suburban women.

Freedom of the press, freedom of association, freedom from politically motivated prosecution: works when the threat is concretely named (Trump's promises of retribution, state-level censorship laws).

Notice what these have in common. They are individual freedoms threatened by specific, identifiable conservative policies. They speak the negative-liberty vocabulary that ordinary Americans recognize. They do not require a settler-imperial reframing of the entire history of American thought. They just require Democrats to say "freedom" clearly and loudly and on issues where they have the high ground.

Rana objects that this is mere messaging, and that without institutional transformation the rhetoric will not stick. But Democrats winning elections is itself a precondition for institutional transformation. There is no left freedom politics if Republicans control the federal government and pack the Supreme Court for another generation. The institutional reforms Rana wants — voting rights, labor law, public investment — require Democratic governing majorities. Those majorities will not materialize from a freedom politics that begins from the premise that ordinary Americans' intuitions about liberty are settler-imperial residues to be dismantled.

There is a related problem of self-marginalization. Rana writes from the academic left, addresses the academic left, and proposes a politics that the academic left would find compelling. This is not the same as a politics that wins elections in Wisconsin. The academic left has been getting a great deal of intellectual attention in the past decade — through outlets like Boston Review, n+1, Dissent, Jacobin — without translating that attention into electoral power. Indeed, in some cases, the rhetorical style of academic left freedom-talk has actively repelled the working-class voters of all races whom that left ostensibly seeks to represent.

I am not arguing that the left should abandon its analysis or its ambitions. I am arguing that the analysis Rana offers is not actually useful for the political work he says it serves. Better analyses are available, and they are more pragmatic and more electorally productive. They begin from the observation that American voters across demographic lines value freedom, that they have specific and recognizable views about what threats to their freedom are, and that Democrats can win by addressing those threats in language voters already understand.

The right's grip on freedom-language is real but not unshakeable. It is being shaken right now by the post-Dobbs reproductive rights movement, which is winning ballot initiatives in red states by speaking simple, individualistic, negative-liberty freedom-talk. It is being shaken by the growing recognition that conservatives, not progressives, are the ones banning books, restricting medical care, jailing librarians, and threatening political opponents with prosecution. The opening is real. The path through it does not require dismantling the entire American liberal tradition. It requires Democratic politicians to speak plainly, identify their opponents accurately, and run on issues where ordinary Americans already agree with them.

That is a different freedom politics from Rana's. It is also one that might actually win.
