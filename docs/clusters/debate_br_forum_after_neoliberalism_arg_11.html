"To move beyond neoliberalism, the economics profession must fundamentally reform how the discipline is taught—particularly in introductory courses—to ensure students understand that markets are socially constructed institutions rather than natural, self-regulating systems."

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman make a compelling case that economics is not inherently neoliberal. Yet if the public believes that economics teaches “markets always work,” that misconception begins in the classroom. The future of economics after neoliberalism depends not only on policy briefs but on how economists train students, especially in introductory courses.

The lead essay notes that the competitive equilibrium model is dominant mainly in introductory economics, while advanced courses quickly move to market failures, imperfect information, strategic behavior, and institutional complexity. This is meant to reassure critics. It should instead alarm the profession. For most students, introductory economics is the only economics they will ever take. It shapes journalists, lawyers, voters, policymakers, business leaders, and citizens. If Econ 101 leaves them with a simplified pro-market worldview, later sophistication inside the discipline cannot undo the public damage.

Introductory economics often begins with scarcity, choice, opportunity cost, supply and demand, gains from trade, and efficiency. These are important ideas. Students should learn them. The problem is sequencing and framing. Markets are introduced as the baseline; institutions, power, inequality, and instability arrive later, if at all. Government appears as an intervention rather than as a constitutive part of markets. Distribution is separated from efficiency. Labor is treated like other commodities. Firms are often black boxes. Money and finance are simplified. History is absent. The result is not neutral simplification but ideological pedagogy, even when instructors do not intend it.

A post-neoliberal economics curriculum should begin from a different premise: economies are institutional systems for organizing production, distribution, care, and ecological exchange. Markets are one powerful coordination mechanism within those systems. They require law, property rights, money, information, trust, infrastructure, and political authority. They can generate innovation and efficiency; they can also generate exploitation, exclusion, instability, and environmental harm. The point is not to indoctrinate students against markets. It is to teach them that markets are made.

This would change how supply and demand are taught. A supply-and-demand diagram is useful, but students should learn what lies behind the curves: bargaining power, technology, legal rules, social norms, credit, public investment, and expectations. In a labor market, the wage is not merely the intersection of supply and demand. It is shaped by minimum wage laws, unions, discrimination, monopsony, immigration status, credentials, scheduling practices, and household responsibilities. In a housing market, prices reflect zoning, segregation, mortgage finance, tax subsidies, local schools, and speculation. In health care, demand is shaped by insurance, fear, expert authority, and urgency. Context is not an advanced complication; it is the subject.

Market failure should also be taught earlier and more broadly. Externalities, public goods, asymmetric information, market power, coordination failures, and incomplete contracts are not rare exceptions. They are pervasive. Climate change, financial crises, antibiotic resistance, platform monopolies, and underprovided care are not footnotes. They are central cases for understanding capitalism in the twenty-first century.

The efficiency-equity distinction needs reworking. Students are often taught that economics identifies efficient outcomes while society chooses distribution. But distribution affects efficiency. Inequality shapes education, health, bargaining power, aggregate demand, political capture, and financial instability. A child in poverty is not merely a distributional concern; she represents wasted human capability. A worker without voice may be less productive and less free. A democracy dominated by wealth may choose inefficient policies. Equity is not external to economics.

Economic history should be required. Students should learn about industrialization, the Great Depression, the New Deal, Bretton Woods, decolonization, stagflation, the Volcker shock, structural adjustment, the rise of global supply chains, financialization, and the 2008 crisis. Without history, students mistake current institutions for natural facts. They cannot understand why unions declined, why central banks became independent, why trade agreements changed, why housing segregation persists, or why welfare states differ. History teaches contingency.

History of economic thought also matters. Smith was not a cartoon advocate of greed. Marx analyzed capitalism as a system of power and accumulation. Keynes challenged the idea that economies automatically return to full employment. Hayek warned about dispersed knowledge and state overreach. Polanyi emphasized embedded markets. Friedman argued for rules and market freedom. Sen broadened welfare to capabilities. Students need not accept all these thinkers, but they should see economics as an ongoing argument, not a settled doctrine.

Methods training should include humility. The rise of empirical economics has improved the discipline, but students should understand what causal identification can and cannot do. A well-identified estimate in one context may not generalize. Some questions are too large or long-term for clean experiments. Measurement choices embed values. Data reflect social categories and administrative priorities. Quantitative evidence should be respected, not fetishized.

Interdisciplinary engagement should be built in. Economists often rediscover concepts long studied by other fields—social norms, power, culture, institutions—and translate them into models. Translation can be useful, but it can also breed arrogance. Students should read sociology on stratification, political science on institutions, law on rights, anthropology on exchange, and philosophy on justice. This will make them better economists, not less rigorous ones.

Policy pedagogy should emphasize implementation and political economy. Too many economics exercises ask students to identify the optimal tax or regulation under stylized assumptions. Real policy must consider administrative capacity, compliance, capture, federalism, legal constraints, and feedback effects. A carbon tax, for example, is not just a price; it is a political object whose design affects legitimacy. A minimum wage depends on enforcement. A housing voucher depends on landlords and supply. A financial regulation depends on regulators, lobbying, and shadow markets.

The profession should also change who studies economics. Economics majors in the United States have often been disproportionately male and, in many institutions, less diverse than the student body. The culture can be competitive, abstract, and dismissive of moral concerns. If economics is to serve inclusive prosperity, it must include people whose experiences reveal different economic realities: first-generation students, students of color, women, immigrants, working adults, and students from regions harmed by deindustrialization or extraction. Diversity is not charity; it improves inquiry.

Textbooks need reform. A few newer efforts have begun to present economics through institutions, history, and inequality, but the mainstream textbook market still often reproduces the old frame. Accreditation, graduate training, and hiring also matter. If graduate programs reward only technical virtuosity, future instructors will teach accordingly. Communication should be valued. Public engagement should not be treated as a distraction from scholarship.

The lead essay asks economists to convince outsiders that economics can be part of the solution. The classroom is where that case must be made first. Students should leave economics not with the belief that markets always work, nor with the opposite belief that markets are always oppressive, but with a disciplined understanding of when, how, for whom, and under what institutions different arrangements work.

Economics after neoliberalism requires new policies, but it also requires new common sense. Introductory economics has helped create the old common sense. It can help create a better one.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Introductory courses currently cause public harm by framing markets as the default baseline and treating government, power, and history as secondary complications.
  2. 02The curriculum should be re-sequenced to teach that markets are 'made' and depend entirely on legal, political, and social infrastructures to function.
  3. 03Market failures, such as climate change and financial instability, should be taught as pervasive features of modern capitalism rather than as rare exceptions to the rule.
  4. 04The traditional distinction between efficiency and equity should be abandoned because distribution and inequality directly impact economic performance and human capability.
  5. 05Economic history and the history of economic thought should be required to help students understand that current institutions are contingent and subject to change.
  6. 06Methodological training should emphasize humility regarding the limits of quantitative data and encourage interdisciplinary engagement with fields like sociology and political science.
  7. 07The profession must diversify its student body and faculty to incorporate a broader range of lived economic experiences into the discipline's inquiry.
  8. 08Textbooks and graduate training must be overhauled to reward institutional analysis and public engagement over narrow technical virtuosity.
