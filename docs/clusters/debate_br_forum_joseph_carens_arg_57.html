Diversified Claude

"While Joseph Carens’ moral argument for amnesty is philosophically compelling, its practical application in the 2009 American political climate is unfeasible and potentially counterproductive to achieving more modest, necessary reforms."

Joseph Carens writes for "liberal democracies" in general, but his proposal would be implemented, if at all, in specific countries with specific histories and specific political configurations. I want to focus on what his argument looks like applied to the United States in 2009, and in particular on a political question he does not address: how would such a policy be enacted?

The American political system has been trying and failing to pass comprehensive immigration reform for at least a decade. The McCain-Kennedy bill of 2007, which would have created a path to legalization for the existing irregular population, collapsed under a combination of conservative opposition (focused on the legalization provisions) and labor opposition (focused on the guest-worker provisions). The 2005 Sensenbrenner bill, which would have criminalized irregular presence and aiding migrants, sparked the largest immigrant-rights demonstrations in American history but also showed the depth of restrictionist sentiment in much of the country. The Bush administration spent political capital on reform and got nothing. The Obama administration has now signaled that immigration reform is on its second-term agenda — meaning, in effect, that it is not on the agenda for the present Congress.

In this political environment, what does Carens' proposal look like as a practical matter? It is, I would argue, considerably more ambitious than what has previously been attempted. Carens proposes not just a one-time legalization of the existing population but an ongoing, prospective rule that would automatically legalize future irregular migrants after five to seven years. No serious legislator in the 2007 debate proposed anything like this. The McCain-Kennedy bill included tough conditions: payment of fines, back taxes, English proficiency, "touchback" requirements. It was a bargain, not an entitlement. Carens proposes something closer to an entitlement.

I do not say this to dismiss Carens' argument. Philosophy is not policy, and an argument can be morally correct without being politically achievable. But the gap between Carens' principle and any plausible American legislation matters in several ways.

First, it raises the question of what we are arguing about when we argue about immigration. If the argument is over what is morally right, then Carens' essay is a serious contribution. If the argument is over what we should advocate as policy, then we have to ask whether advocating Carens' more ambitious position helps or hurts the cause of more modest reform. There is a long history in American politics of moral perfectionism undermining political progress. The labor movement learned this in the 1930s; the civil rights movement learned this in the 1960s. The immigration reform movement may be in danger of learning it now.

Second, it raises the question of how the argument lands politically. Carens' essay presents an argument for amnesty that addresses the standard objections — fairness, lawbreaking, magnetism — and rebuts them. But the rebuttals, however philosophically sound, do not necessarily move the politics. Most Americans who oppose amnesty are not philosophical restrictionists with carefully worked-out theories of state sovereignty. They are anxious about wages, about jobs, about cultural change, about a sense that the country is being transformed without their consent. Carens' philosophical arguments do not engage these anxieties. He may be right that the anxieties do not generate a moral case against amnesty. But the anxieties exist, and they have political force, and any serious advocacy of amnesty has to find ways of speaking to them.

Third, the political moment in 2009 is not promising for amnesty. The unemployment rate is near nine percent and climbing. The financial crisis has produced a generalized economic anxiety that is unfavorable to any policy perceived as expanding labor competition. The Republican Party has lurched rightward on immigration after the 2008 election; figures like Tom Tancredo, marginal a few years ago, are now within the conservative mainstream. State-level enforcement (287(g) agreements, Joe Arpaio's antics in Arizona, the rise of harsh state legislation in Oklahoma and elsewhere) has accelerated, not slowed. The Obama administration has continued worksite enforcement at a high pace, in what is widely understood as an attempt to demonstrate enforcement credibility before later pursuing reform. The political space for Carens' proposal in this moment is essentially nil.

Does this matter for the moral argument? In one sense, no. Moral truths do not depend on political feasibility. In another sense, yes: the form an argument takes, and the audience it addresses, are partly a function of what is politically possible. Carens' essay is addressed to readers of Boston Review — that is, to a left-liberal intellectual audience that is already sympathetic to amnesty in principle. It is not addressed to swing voters in Ohio or to wavering Democratic senators from red states. Its effect, if any, will be on the philosophical articulation of the immigration debate among people already on one side of it.

I want to suggest that the most useful contribution Carens' essay can make is not to push the policy debate further toward his preferred outcome but to clarify what is at stake when we discuss more modest reforms. The McCain-Kennedy bill, for example, included a legalization path that was conditional and contingent. Carens' argument suggests that the conditionality and contingency are themselves morally problematic — that the people we are legalizing are not supplicants seeking mercy but people whose long settlement has generated genuine claims against the political community. This reframing matters, even if the policy outcome is still a conditional path rather than an entitlement. It changes the moral register of the debate.

What we need, in the American political context, is a coalition that can pass legalization. That coalition probably includes business interests that depend on the labor, Latino organizations that have been mobilizing for years, religious organizations (Catholic, evangelical, mainline Protestant) that have come around to a humanitarian position, and labor unions that have come to understand that legalization is a precondition for organizing. Carens' philosophical argument can support the moral confidence of this coalition. It cannot substitute for the political work the coalition has to do. We are in for several more years of grinding, incremental, unsatisfying political effort. The reward, with luck, will be a partial version of what Carens proposes — and it will be worth fighting for.

  2. 02The philosophical rebuttals to anti-amnesty arguments fail to address the underlying economic and cultural anxieties that drive restrictionist political sentiment.
  3. 03Advocating for a morally perfect but politically impossible policy may undermine the progress of more pragmatic, incremental immigration reforms.
  4. 04The current economic crisis and rising unemployment in 2009 create a political environment that is uniquely hostile to policies perceived as expanding labor competition.
  5. 05The primary value of Carens’ argument lies in reframing migrants as people with genuine moral claims rather than supplicants, which can bolster the moral confidence of the existing reform coalition.
