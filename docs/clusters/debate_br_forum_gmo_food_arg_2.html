"The GMO controversy is primarily a conflict over political economy and corporate institutional power rather than a misunderstanding of molecular biology or food safety."

**What Ronald Leaves Out: The Political Economy of Seeds**

Pamela Ronald's essay is a useful corrective to the wilder claims of the anti-GMO movement, and I find little to dispute in her summary of the toxicological and ecological evidence. But to read her piece is to be told that the entire controversy reduces to a misunderstanding of molecular biology, with a footnote acknowledging that, yes, corporate consolidation is a concern. This is exactly backwards. The molecular biology is the footnote. The political economy is the controversy.

Consider the history Ronald glosses. Public agricultural research in the United States, once the engine of the land-grant system, has been progressively defunded since the 1980s. Into that vacuum stepped private firms whose business model depends on intellectual property — patents on traits, patents on genes, patents on the seed itself. The Plant Variety Protection Act and the Supreme Court's *Diamond v. Chakrabarty* decision laid the legal groundwork; the Bayh-Dole Act, by allowing universities to patent federally funded research, completed the enclosure. By the time GE crops arrived in the mid-1990s, the institutional infrastructure for treating seeds as proprietary commodities was already in place. The technology did not create that infrastructure, but it accelerated and entrenched it.

This is why farmers who want to save seed — a practice ten thousand years old — now face license agreements and infringement suits. Ronald cites *Bowman v. Monsanto* as evidence that farmers want the seed; she might equally have cited it as evidence of how thoroughly seed has been transformed from a common resource into a licensed product. Hybrid seed, she notes, has functioned similarly since the 1920s. True — and the consolidation of the seed industry under hybrid corn is itself a cautionary tale, one well documented by Jack Kloppenburg in *First the Seed*. The point is not that GE caused the problem. The point is that GE intensifies a longer enclosure of the agricultural commons that has serious consequences for farmer autonomy, agrobiodiversity, and the resilience of food systems.

Ronald counters that farmer adoption is itself evidence of benefit: nobody forces a Punjabi cotton grower to buy Bt seed. This is true in the narrow sense and misleading in the broader one. Once a seed system is dominated by a handful of firms, once extension services promote those varieties, once credit is tied to input packages, "choice" becomes a thin reed on which to rest the legitimacy of the system. We don't usually consider the fact that everyone uses Microsoft Windows as proof that Microsoft offers the best operating system; we consider it evidence of network effects and market power. The same applies to seed.

A second silence in Ronald's essay concerns the herbicide treadmill. She praises glyphosate-tolerant crops because glyphosate is less acutely toxic than the herbicides it replaces, and because reduced tillage saves carbon. Both true. But the introduction of HT crops has driven a roughly tenfold increase in glyphosate use in the United States, and as Ronald herself notes, twenty-four glyphosate-resistant weed species have emerged. The industry response has been predictable: stacked-trait crops resistant to multiple herbicides, including 2,4-D and dicamba, drift-prone chemicals with worse toxicity profiles. The "less toxic herbicide" story is becoming the "more toxic herbicide" story, and the structural cause is the same monoculture/single-tactic logic that the technology was supposed to transcend.

I want to be careful here. None of this argues against the responsible deployment of genetic engineering. Virus-resistant papaya saved an industry. BXW-resistant bananas, if they work, will save livelihoods. Golden rice, despite the regulatory and IP obstacles that have delayed it for over a decade, addresses a real nutritional deficit. These are public goods, and a sensible left-of-center position should support them while rejecting their use as ideological cover for the proprietary input-intensive model that dominates commercial agriculture.

What would a serious response to Ronald's challenge look like? It would invest heavily in public-sector breeding and open-source seed initiatives — the kind of work the Gates Foundation supports for African staples, but at a scale matching the magnitude of the agricultural challenge. It would reform plant patent law to preserve farmers' right to save seed for replanting. It would tie regulatory approval of new GE traits not only to food safety but to integrated pest management protocols designed to slow resistance. And it would stop using subsistence farmers in Uganda as rhetorical shields for commercial soybean monocultures in Iowa.

Ronald's essay is a defense of a technology. The argument we actually need is about institutions: who develops seeds, who owns them, who benefits, and who decides. Until that argument is engaged seriously, the suspicion that fuels the anti-GMO movement will not subside, and it should not.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The transition of seeds from a common resource to proprietary intellectual property has eroded farmer autonomy and entrenched corporate control over the agricultural commons.
  2. 02High farmer adoption rates of GE seeds are more a reflection of market power and the lack of viable alternatives than a simple proof of the technology's superiority.
  3. 03The reliance on herbicide-tolerant crops has created a 'treadmill' effect, where the emergence of resistant weeds necessitates the use of increasingly toxic chemical combinations.
  4. 04The humanitarian benefits of specific GE crops, like golden rice or resistant bananas, should not be used as rhetorical cover for the industrial monoculture model.
  5. 05A meaningful resolution to the debate requires institutional reforms, such as investing in public-sector breeding and protecting the traditional right of farmers to save seeds.

"While the science of genetic engineering is sound, the opposition to GMOs is primarily a political and economic critique of industrial agriculture that cannot be dismissed as mere scientific illiteracy."

**The Science Is Settled. The Politics Isn't.**

Pamela Ronald has written the clearest popular defense of agricultural biotechnology I have read in some time, and on the science she is essentially unimpeachable. The major national academies, the WHO, the European Commission's own Joint Research Centre, and the AAAS have all converged on the same conclusion: the genetically engineered crops currently on the market are as safe to eat as their conventional counterparts, and several of them confer real environmental benefits. To pretend otherwise is to engage in the same epistemic vandalism that climate denialists practice on the right.

And yet I want to push back on one move Ronald makes repeatedly, because I think it both misdiagnoses the opposition and ultimately weakens her case. She frames anti-GMO sentiment as a problem of scientific illiteracy, comparable to the anti-vaccine movement. This is partly true and partly a category error. The anti-vaccine movement is built on a falsified empirical claim (vaccines cause autism). Much of the anti-GMO movement, by contrast, is built on a *political* claim that happens to wear scientific clothing: that the consolidation of seed, chemical, and intellectual-property power in a handful of multinational firms is bad for farmers, bad for rural economies, and bad for democratic control over the food system.

These are not equivalent worries. You can think Bt cotton is perfectly safe to eat and perfectly safe for non-target insects, and still believe that Monsanto's licensing regime is coercive, that patent enforcement against farmers is heavy-handed, or that public agricultural research has been hollowed out by private capture. Ronald acknowledges some of this — she calls for stronger public-sector breeding and antitrust regulation of seed firms — but these acknowledgments arrive late and quietly, after a long stretch of treating opposition as essentially irrational.

Consider the labeling fight. Ronald argues that labels imply a danger that isn't there, and that "GMO" isn't a coherent category for testing. Both points are technically correct. But she elides why people actually want labels: not because they think transgenic DNA is dangerous, but because they want to express a political preference at the cash register against a production system they distrust. That is not scientific illiteracy. That is consumer politics. One can argue labels are a clumsy instrument for that purpose — I think they are — but treating the demand itself as evidence of irrationality concedes the rhetorical high ground without engaging the actual grievance.

A second concern: Ronald moves fluidly between two very different kinds of GE crops, and the conflation does real argumentative work. There is golden rice, virus-resistant papaya, BXW-resistant bananas, drought-tolerant cassava — public-good crops, often developed in public or philanthropic labs, addressing the needs of subsistence farmers. Then there is Roundup Ready soy, which exists primarily to sell more Roundup and to be planted in vast monocultures feeding the industrial livestock complex. The case for the first set is compelling and humanitarian. The case for the second is, at best, "it's no worse than what it replaced." When opponents bundle these together as "GMOs," Ronald rightly objects. But she herself bundles them when she lists Bt cotton and herbicide-tolerant soy in the same breath as golden rice as triumphs of the technology. If we want consumers to disaggregate, scientists must lead by example.

Finally, the "we've always genetically modified crops" argument has become a tic in pro-GE writing, and I think it should be retired. It's true that mutagenesis breeding involves more genetic disruption than a typical transgene insertion. It's true that grafted citrus and triploid bananas are odd creatures by any natural standard. But the rhetorical move — *therefore Bt corn is just more of the same* — proves too much. The interesting questions about modern GE are not whether moving genes is unprecedented (it isn't) but whether the regulatory, economic, and ecological context surrounding it is well-managed (often it isn't). Telling a worried citizen that "you already eat altered food" sounds, to that citizen, like a dodge.

Ronald ends with Mama Moses and her dying bananas, and the image deserves to land. If a transgenic resistance gene can save Ugandan banana farms from BXW, opposing that on principle is indefensible. The science community has a real obligation to develop and deliver such technologies, and the activist community has a real obligation not to obstruct them out of reflexive suspicion. But the activist community will be more easily persuaded if scientists separate the genuinely humanitarian applications of GE from its more banal commercial uses, and if they stop treating every political objection as a failure of high-school biology. The truth about GMOs is more complicated, and more interesting, than either side's slogans suggest.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Anti-GMO sentiment is often a political claim regarding the consolidation of corporate power and intellectual property rather than a misunderstanding of empirical safety.
  2. 02The demand for GMO labeling is a form of consumer politics used to express distrust in a production system rather than an irrational fear of transgenic DNA.
  3. 03Proponents of GE crops should distinguish between humanitarian public-good crops and commercial monoculture crops rather than bundling them together to justify the technology.
  4. 04The argument that humans have always genetically modified crops is a rhetorical dodge that fails to address valid concerns about the modern regulatory and ecological context of the industry.
  5. 05Scientists must engage with the actual political grievances of activists instead of treating every objection as a failure of biological education.

"Opposition to genetically engineered crops is not a result of scientific illiteracy or an information deficit, but rather a rational response to institutional distrust and the systemic socio-economic structure of industrial food production."

**The Problem Isn't Illiteracy**

Pamela Ronald, like many scientists who have entered the public debate over agricultural biotechnology, treats opposition to GE crops as fundamentally an information deficit. If only people understood the science, they would not oppose the technology. Whole Foods shoppers, in her account, suffer from a misapprehension that could be corrected by better communication. The model is the so-called "deficit model" of public understanding of science, and decades of scholarship in science and technology studies have demonstrated that it does not describe how public attitudes toward technologies are actually formed.

Public attitudes toward technologies are formed through a combination of trust in institutions, lived experience of risk and benefit, distributional considerations, cultural meanings attached to particular domains (food, sex, death, the body, the land), and the perceived legitimacy of decision-making procedures. Information matters, but it matters as one input among many, and rarely as the decisive one. This is not a deficit. This is how human reasoning about technology works, and it is the same way that scientists themselves reason when they are outside their domain of expertise.

Consider the nuclear power example. The scientific consensus is that modern nuclear plants are statistically safer than the coal plants they would replace, both in routine operation and in terms of overall mortality, and that nuclear power is essential to any plausible decarbonization pathway. Yet large majorities in many countries oppose new nuclear construction. Are these majorities scientifically illiterate? Many of them are, but the explanation cannot be illiteracy, because educated and scientifically literate populations show similar patterns. The explanation has to do with the lived memory of Chernobyl and Fukushima, distrust of the nuclear industry, the cultural resonance of invisible contamination, and the perception that the benefits accrue to utilities while the risks accrue to neighbors. These are not irrational concerns. They are concerns that the technical risk assessment does not, on its own, address.

The same structure applies to GE crops. Public concern about GE is not primarily concern about whether transgenes per se are toxic. It is concern about a system: a system in which food production has become industrial, in which a handful of firms control the major inputs, in which farmers have been progressively dispossessed of traditional autonomy, in which regulatory agencies are perceived (rightly or wrongly) as captured by the industries they oversee, and in which scientific reassurance about safety has been weaponized in commercial disputes. To address the underlying concern requires addressing the system. Telling people that the science is settled does not address the system. It addresses one component of one part of the system, and it does so in a register that many citizens experience as condescending.

Ronald draws an analogy between anti-GE views and anti-vaccine views. The analogy is instructive but not in the way she intends. Both movements share a structural feature: they are responses to perceived loss of control over decisions that affect the body, the family, and the future, made in institutional contexts where trust has eroded. The anti-vaccine movement is wrong about the empirical question of whether vaccines cause autism. It is not wrong that pharmaceutical companies have, at various points, behaved badly, that regulatory capture is real, and that ordinary parents have legitimate interests in the medical procedures performed on their children. To respond effectively to the anti-vaccine movement requires both correcting the empirical error and addressing the underlying institutional distrust. Correcting the error alone, as a substantial sociological literature now shows, often deepens distrust rather than dispelling it.

The same will be true of GE crops. Ronald's essay is, on its scientific merits, correct. It will not persuade those it most needs to persuade, because those it most needs to persuade are not opposing GE crops on scientific grounds. They are opposing a food system, and to them GE is the most visible symbol of that system. To engage them productively requires meeting them on the terrain they are actually on, not the terrain scientists wish they were on.

What would that look like? It would acknowledge corporate concentration as a real and serious problem, and propose institutional remedies. It would invest in public-sector breeding and open-source seed at a scale commensurate with the role of private breeding. It would tie the introduction of GE crops to integrated pest management protocols and refuge requirements with teeth. It would build participatory varietal selection programs that give farmers meaningful input into what gets developed. And it would distinguish, in its public messaging, between the humanitarian applications of the technology — virus-resistant cassava, BXW-resistant bananas, golden rice — and its commercial applications, rather than treating skepticism of the latter as if it were ignorance of biology.

Ronald is a serious scientist with serious things to say. She would do more good if she stopped treating her opponents as a problem of education and started treating them as participants in a political debate about institutions. They are participating whether she recognizes it or not.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Public attitudes toward technology are shaped by complex factors like institutional trust, cultural meanings, and perceived legitimacy rather than just technical data.
  2. 02The 'deficit model' of science communication incorrectly assumes that providing more information will resolve public skepticism.
  3. 03Skepticism toward GE crops is often a proxy for broader concerns regarding corporate concentration, the loss of farmer autonomy, and regulatory capture.
  4. 04Analogies to the anti-vaccine movement demonstrate that correcting empirical errors is ineffective if the underlying erosion of institutional trust is not addressed.
  5. 05To productively engage critics, scientists must move beyond biological arguments and address political and economic issues such as public-sector breeding and corporate accountability.
