"While Zittrain correctly identifies the decline of the generative Internet, his proposed solutions fail to address the root cause: the deliberate concentration of private corporate power and the legal frameworks that enable closed digital empires."

Jonathan Zittrain has written the definitive anatomy of the generative Internet’s crisis, but the most incisive parts of his essay point to a threat he names without fully confronting: not just bad code, but the private concentration of power over the platforms that mediate our digital lives. He is right that the PC and the open Net are losing ground to sealed appliances; he is right that this threatens the generativity that produced the Web, Wikipedia, and the free software movement. But his remedies—virtual machines, community code review, data portability—treat the symptoms while leaving the underlying malady largely untouched. The generative Internet is being killed not primarily by malware, but by firms that have learned to exploit network effects and intellectual property law to erect closed empires. To save the open Net, we must be willing to deploy the legal tools of structural antitrust and platform regulation with a vigor Zittrain only hints at.

The historical arc Zittrain traces—from the Apple II, a machine whose uses its creator could not foresee, to the iPhone, whose every application is pre-filtered by Apple—is a parable about corporate power, not just security. Apple did not lock down the iPhone because a botnet forced its hand. It locked down the iPhone because control is fantastically profitable: it allows the extraction of a 30 percent cut of all third-party software sales, the ability to ban applications that compete with Apple’s own services, and the capacity to enforce exclusive carrier deals like the one with AT&T. Security is a convenient veneer. The same goes for the Xbox 360, the TiVo, and countless other sealed platforms. These firms are not reluctantly solving a security crisis; they are actively building the walled gardens that the open Internet was supposed to displace.

Zittrain’s most powerful insight is that the generative PC serves as a “safety valve,” a place users can retreat to when appliances become too restrictive. But he underplays how that safety valve is itself under assault not from viruses, but from the legal and technical strategies of dominant platform owners. Microsoft, having been found to abuse its operating system monopoly to crush browser competition, is now extending its control into the living room with a locked-down Xbox. If the PC ecosystem is so weakened by the security crisis that it can no longer serve as an effective counterweight, the fault lies as much with the legal order’s inability to prevent Microsoft, Apple, and others from leveraging their market power across adjacent markets as it does with the Morris worm’s descendants.

Zittrain’s call for “appliance neutrality” is a step in the right direction, but it is far too timid. He suggests that if a platform vendor welcomes third-party contributions and then later revokes access, it commits a legally recognizable bait and switch. That is a solid common-law instinct, but it leaves untouched the more fundamental problem: the platform vendor that never opens its gates at all. A world where Apple’s iPhone and Microsoft’s Xbox never allow user-installable software is exactly the future Zittrain fears, and his neutrality principle would sanction it so long as the closure is consistent from day one. A real solution demands a structural separation between the platform and the applications that run on it—an antitrust principle that any device positioned as a general-purpose platform must provide non-discriminatory access to third-party developers. If a video game console doubles as a “general purpose computer,” as Bill Gates boasted of the Xbox, then the public interest demands that its owner, not the manufacturer, decides what code runs. This is not wild regulation; it is the logic of the Microsoft consent decree applied to the next generation of computing.

Zittrain’s legal proposals for generative software also need sharpening. Changing the statute of limitations to prevent patent sandbagging is wise, but it does not address the core injustice of software patents themselves. At a time when the U.S. Patent Office is granting exclusive rights over fundamental techniques like “one-click shopping” or online backups, the unaccredited tinkerer whom Zittrain lauds cannot possibly avoid infringement even if he acts in good faith. The solution is not just to shorten the period during which patent holders can sue; it is to abolish software patents entirely, or at minimum to dramatically raise the bar for non-obviousness and to create a broad, compulsory licensing scheme for software. Zittrain’s piece is punctuated by a careful reluctance to offend the proprietary software industry, but that reluctance leads him to soft-pedal the structural reforms that are necessary.

Finally, Zittrain’s celebration of Wikipedia as a model must grapple with the fact that the platform on which Wikipedia runs—the Web—is itself becoming a battleground for control. He notes that Web 2.0 platforms like Facebook are “tethered to their makers,” their generativity contingent on the pleasure of a single corporation. This is where the next great fight for network neutrality will occur, not at the ISP level. If Facebook can unilaterally disable a popular third-party application because it competes with its own features, or Google can tweak its search algorithm to bury a rival service, then the migration of generativity to the browser layer—which many appliance advocates tout—is a fool’s paradise. Here, Zittrain’s suggestion that the Federal Trade Commission should police “unfair or deceptive” practices might be extended, but it needs the teeth of an active competition authority, not just a disclosure regime.

Zittrain’s essay is an invaluable framework, and his instinct to look to a community ethos is admirable. But ethos alone is no match for corporate treasuries and armies of lobbyists. The generative Internet was built on public investment, university governance, and a legal framework that initially tolerated, even encouraged, radical openness. It is now threatened by a counter-revolution of private enclosure that uses security fears as a fig leaf. To reclaim it, we need not only better tools and a renewed spirit of netizenship, but a willingness to break up the trusts, enforce interoperability mandates, and dismantle the legal thicket of intellectual property that makes it impossible for the next Dan Bricklin to write a VisiCalc without hiring a patent lawyer. Zittrain closes by invoking the generosity of spirit as society’s first line of moderation. I agree. But the second line, when generosity fails, has always been law—and the law has been asleep for too long.

  2. 02Zittrain’s 'appliance neutrality' is too timid because it only targets 'bait and switch' tactics while ignoring the fundamental problem of platforms that are closed to third-party innovation from the start.
  3. 03To preserve generativity, the legal system must move beyond community ethos and deploy aggressive structural antitrust measures, such as mandating non-discriminatory access for third-party developers on general-purpose platforms.
  4. 04Protecting open-source innovation requires more than just adjusting statutes of limitations; it necessitates the abolition or radical reform of software patents that currently stifle independent tinkerers.
  5. 05The migration of activity to Web 2.0 platforms like Facebook creates a new 'tethered' environment that requires active competition authority to prevent platform owners from unilaterally crushing rival applications.
  6. 06Data portability and disclosure regimes are insufficient 'symptomatic' treatments that must be replaced by interoperability mandates and the dismantling of restrictive intellectual property laws.
