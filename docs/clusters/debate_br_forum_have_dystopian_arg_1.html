"Redirecting AI toward socially beneficial outcomes requires dismantling or strictly regulating the market concentration of dominant tech firms whose business models inherently favor harmful practices like data extraction and automation."

Daron Acemoglu asks us to redirect AI. I agree, but the redirection cannot be achieved without confronting the market structure of the digital economy. The central fact about AI in 2021 is not simply that researchers favor automation or that governments subsidize capital. It is that the most important AI capabilities are concentrated inside a handful of dominant firms whose business models depend on data extraction, platform control, and scale. If we do not break or discipline that concentration, appeals to social purpose will be overwhelmed.

The list is familiar: Google, Facebook, Amazon, Microsoft, Apple, and, globally, Alibaba, Tencent, and Baidu. These firms differ in products and culture, but they share structural advantages. They control vast datasets, cloud infrastructure, user networks, advertising markets, app ecosystems, e-commerce platforms, and talent pipelines. They can buy promising startups before they become threats. They can subsidize expansion in one market with profits from another. They can shape academic research through grants, fellowships, consulting, and hiring. They can influence regulation through lobbying and public relations. They do not merely participate in AI markets; they organize them.

This concentration helps explain why AI develops in socially troubling directions. Advertising platforms want prediction and influence. E-commerce platforms want logistics optimization, pricing power, and labor discipline. Cloud companies want enterprise automation. Social media platforms want engagement. These incentives do not naturally produce AI that empowers teachers, nurses, local governments, small firms, or workers. They produce AI that reinforces the platform’s central position.

Consider Facebook. Its AI systems are not built primarily to improve democratic discourse. They are built to rank content, target ads, detect some forms of abuse, and keep users engaged. Even when the company invests heavily in safety, the underlying model depends on attention and behavioral data. The same machinery that allows small businesses to find customers allows political operatives to microtarget voters and misinformation to spread rapidly. The problem is not a lack of ethical awareness among engineers. It is the business model.

Consider Amazon. AI improves warehousing, recommendation, cloud services, voice assistants, and delivery. Some of this is genuinely useful. But Amazon’s scale allows it to set the terms for sellers, workers, and even cities competing for investment. Algorithmic management in warehouses can raise productivity while increasing injury risk and stress. Marketplace data can be used to identify successful third-party products. Cloud dominance gives Amazon a central role in the infrastructure of countless firms. The issue is not whether a particular algorithm is fair; it is whether one company should have so much power over commerce, labor, and infrastructure.

Antitrust therefore belongs at the center of AI policy. Acemoglu mentions concentration, but his remedies emphasize funding, norms, and democratic oversight. Those are necessary. Yet a concentrated industry can absorb or deflect them. Large firms can comply with complex regulations more easily than startups, turning rules into barriers to entry. They can fund “AI for good” projects while preserving harmful core businesses. They can hire ethicists and publish principles while lobbying against enforceable limits. They can shape standards in ways that protect incumbents.

A serious response requires structural separation and competition policy. Platforms that operate marketplaces should not compete on those marketplaces using privileged data. Firms that control essential digital infrastructure should face nondiscrimination obligations. Dominant platforms should be restricted from acquiring nascent competitors. Interoperability and data portability should allow users and businesses to leave without losing their networks. App stores should not be able to impose arbitrary rents on dependent developers. Digital advertising markets should be investigated for conflicts of interest and opacity.

Breaking up companies is not a magic solution. Smaller firms can also misuse AI, exploit workers, or invade privacy. But concentration magnifies harm and reduces democratic leverage. When a few platforms mediate speech, commerce, and information, individual choice becomes weak. “Use another service” is not meaningful when all one’s friends, customers, suppliers, or documents are locked into the incumbent. Competition can create space for alternative business models: subscription services, cooperatives, public-interest platforms, privacy-preserving tools, and sector-specific AI built around user needs rather than advertising scale.

Data concentration is especially important. AI systems improve with data, and dominant platforms collect data because they are dominant. This feedback loop can entrench power. Some propose treating data as labor and paying users for it. That may have limited value, but it risks legitimizing extraction. A better approach is to limit collection, require interoperability, and create data trusts or public data sets governed for specific purposes. Health, transportation, climate, and education data can support innovation, but they should not simply become proprietary assets of whoever collected them first.

Cloud computing is another underappreciated bottleneck. Modern AI requires enormous computational resources. If a small number of cloud providers control access to computing infrastructure, they shape who can innovate. Public investment in academic and nonprofit computing resources would reduce dependence on corporate platforms. Universities should not have to partner with tech giants simply to train advanced models. Public-interest AI requires public infrastructure.

Acemoglu’s proposal to shift research norms is valuable but must be insulated from corporate capture. Many AI researchers move between universities and companies because that is where the data, compute, salaries, and prestige are. Government funding can counterbalance this, but only if it is large, sustained, and pluralistic. Public grants should support projects that dominant firms would not pursue: labor-enhancing tools for small businesses, privacy-preserving systems, public health applications, interpretable models for government benefits, assistive technologies, and AI for underrepresented languages. Funding should also support social scientists, legal scholars, historians, and community organizations—not only computer scientists.

The labor question also intersects with competition. Monopsony power allows firms to suppress wages and impose bad conditions. In local labor markets dominated by a few employers, workers have limited exit options. Platform companies can use algorithmic systems to manage large workforces while avoiding traditional employment obligations. Antitrust law has too often focused narrowly on consumer prices, missing harms to workers, suppliers, privacy, and innovation. A modern competition policy should consider whether AI-enabled business models reduce labor bargaining power.

The democracy question likewise runs through concentration. Facebook and Google dominate digital advertising. YouTube shapes video politics. Twitter, though smaller, has outsized influence among journalists and politicians. Amazon Web Services hosts critical infrastructure. Apple and Google control mobile app distribution. When private firms become gatekeepers of public discourse, democratic accountability becomes indirect and improvised. Conservatives accuse platforms of censorship; liberals accuse them of enabling extremism; civil libertarians worry about both state pressure and corporate power. The common problem is that too few institutions have too much control.

Regulating content decisions is hard and dangerous. But reducing platform dominance would help. A more interoperable social media ecosystem could allow communities to choose different moderation rules without losing access to broader networks. Users could select ranking algorithms. Researchers could study harms without depending on company permission. Political advertising could be made more transparent. The goal should not be a government ministry of truth; it should be a less centralized information environment.

International competition complicates the picture. Some argue that breaking up American tech giants would weaken the United States against China. This argument should be treated skeptically. Monopolies often wrap themselves in the flag. But democratic resilience is itself a strategic asset. A society whose information systems are dominated by unaccountable firms is vulnerable. Moreover, competition has historically been a source of American innovation. The breakup of AT&T did not end telecommunications innovation; it helped open space for new technologies. Strong antitrust can coexist with public investment in strategic capabilities.

Acemoglu is right that the market will not automatically choose the best AI path. But “the market” is not a neutral collection of firms. It is structured by law. For forty years, American law allowed digital platforms to consolidate power with little interference. That choice shaped AI. A different legal regime can shape it differently.

AI’s future will not be decided only in research labs. It will be decided in antitrust agencies, procurement offices, labor boards, courts, standards bodies, and legislatures. If we want AI that serves human beings rather than dominant platforms, we must reduce the power of the platforms. Otherwise, we will keep asking the same companies that profit from surveillance, automation, and dependency to lead us out of the problems they helped create.

  3. 03Structural separation is necessary to prevent platforms from using their dominance in infrastructure and data to stifle competitors and exploit dependent users or workers.
  4. 04Public investment in independent cloud computing and data infrastructure is essential to reduce the academic and commercial reliance on corporate giants for AI development.
  5. 05The feedback loop of data concentration must be broken through interoperability requirements and the creation of public data trusts to prevent entrenched power.
  6. 06A less centralized digital ecosystem is the most effective way to protect democratic discourse from the opaque and unaccountable content moderation of a few gatekeepers.
  7. 07National security arguments against breaking up tech giants are often self-serving justifications for monopolies that ignore the fact that competition and democratic resilience are strategic assets.

"Redirecting AI toward a socially beneficial future requires moving beyond top-down policy changes to empower workers and citizens with the institutional authority to contest and shape how these technologies are deployed."

Daron Acemoglu is right to insist that AI’s future is a political choice rather than a technological fate. The most important contribution of his essay is to move the debate away from two sterile fantasies: that artificial intelligence will either magically deliver abundance if markets are left alone, or inevitably usher in mass unemployment and authoritarian control. The historical record does not support either view. Technologies arrive embedded in institutions, incentives, and social conflicts. They can complement workers or replace them; they can broaden access to knowledge or concentrate power; they can help citizens govern themselves or help states and corporations govern citizens more efficiently.

But if we accept that the direction of AI can be changed, we should be even more concrete about what kind of political economy could change it. Acemoglu emphasizes government funding, professional norms, and democratic oversight. These are indispensable. Yet on their own they may not be enough, because the problem is not only that AI research is pointed in the wrong direction. It is also that the organizations deploying AI often have too little accountability to the people most affected by it. The crucial missing actor in much of today’s AI governance is not merely the state or the enlightened researcher, but the worker, the patient, the student, the tenant, the defendant, and the citizen whose life is reorganized by algorithmic systems.

Consider the workplace. Acemoglu’s distinction between automation and technologies that create new tasks is powerful, but in practice the boundary between the two is often determined after invention, at the point of deployment. A scheduling algorithm in a hospital can be used to deskill nurses, intensify shifts, and monitor “productivity” minute by minute. The same broad family of tools could also reduce administrative burdens, flag dangerous understaffing, and give nurses more time with patients. A warehouse robot can be introduced as a partner that reduces injury and expands the range of work humans can safely perform, or as part of a surveillance regime that pushes workers to meet machine-set rates until their bodies give out. The technology matters, but so do bargaining power, job design, and the right to contest managerial decisions.

This is why labor institutions should be treated not merely as compensation for technological disruption, but as part of innovation policy itself. Strong unions, works councils, sectoral bargaining, and worker representation on corporate boards can affect which technologies firms demand and how they are implemented. Germany’s system of codetermination is not perfect, but it illustrates a broader point: when workers have a formal voice, firms are more likely to pursue productivity-enhancing adjustment rather than simple labor shedding. In the United States, where private-sector union density has collapsed, employers face fewer internal constraints when they adopt software that fragments jobs, classifies workers as contractors, or uses algorithmic management to shift risk downward. Redirecting AI therefore requires rebuilding institutions of worker voice, not only changing tax incentives for capital.

The same applies outside the workplace. In education, Acemoglu imagines AI that helps teachers adapt instruction to students. That is a worthy goal. But many educational technologies already promise personalization while producing data extraction, standardized nudging, and new forms of vendor dependence. A school district under budget pressure may adopt AI not to empower teachers but to reduce staff, outsource curriculum, or monitor students in ways that disproportionately affect poor children and children of color. The question is not simply whether “AI in education” complements teachers in the abstract. It is who procures it, who evaluates it, who owns the data, whether teachers can refuse harmful tools, and whether parents and students have meaningful rights.

Health care presents a similar dilemma. AI systems may improve diagnosis, triage, and care coordination. They may also encode existing inequalities if trained on biased data, deny care through opaque risk scores, or become another layer of administrative control imposed by insurers and hospital systems. The history of American health care should make us cautious about assuming that productivity gains will automatically serve patients or clinicians. Without institutional safeguards, AI may optimize billing, throughput, and liability avoidance rather than healing.

For this reason, democratic oversight must be more than periodic public outrage after scandals. It must be built into procurement, auditing, liability, and rights. Governments should require algorithmic impact assessments before public agencies deploy high-stakes AI systems in policing, welfare, immigration, education, and health. Affected communities should be able to examine, challenge, and halt systems that fail basic standards of fairness, accuracy, and necessity. There should be clear lines of responsibility: agencies and firms should not be allowed to hide behind trade secrecy when an algorithm denies benefits, misidentifies a suspect, or determines workplace discipline. Transparency alone is insufficient if no one has the power to act on what transparency reveals.

Acemoglu is also right that surveillance is not an accidental side effect of AI but one of its most profitable and politically attractive uses. Facial recognition, predictive policing, targeted advertising, and behavioral analytics all grow out of a business model and a governing model that treat human activity as raw material. The response cannot be limited to better technical privacy tools, valuable though differential privacy and cryptography may be. Some uses should simply be prohibited. Democratic societies should be willing to ban real-time facial recognition in public spaces, restrict biometric databases, prevent employers from continuous worker surveillance, and sharply limit behavioral microtargeting in politics. If certain technologies are structurally hostile to freedom of association, protest, and equality before the law, then “responsible use” may be the wrong frame.

At the same time, I would qualify Acemoglu’s skepticism toward redistribution. He is correct that redistribution cannot substitute for meaningful work, social status, and political voice. A society of billionaire-owned robots plus universal basic income would not be a healthy democracy if most people had no role in production or collective decision-making. But redistribution and predistribution are not rivals. Public health insurance, child allowances, wage subsidies, paid leave, and stronger unemployment insurance can increase workers’ bargaining power and reduce desperation in the face of technological change. A person who can leave a bad job is better positioned to resist degrading technology. A community with fiscal capacity is better able to invest in complementary tools. The safety net is not only a mop for market outcomes; it is part of the structure that shapes them.

We should also avoid nostalgia for the postwar compact. Its achievements were real: rising wages, strong productivity growth, and a broad sense of economic inclusion for many workers. But that compact excluded many people, especially Black Americans, women, migrant workers, domestic workers, and agricultural laborers. Some of the “good jobs” of the era rested on racial hierarchy, gendered unpaid labor, and environmental destruction. The goal should not be to restore the mid-twentieth century, but to build a more inclusive version of shared prosperity suited to a service-heavy, care-intensive, decarbonizing economy. AI could help with that project, but only if care work, teaching, maintenance, and public administration are treated as sites of innovation rather than as cost centers to be squeezed.

The international challenge is real, but it should not become an excuse for paralysis. The United States and Europe have market power, research institutions, standards-setting capacity, and public procurement budgets. They can condition government contracts on labor-complementing design, privacy protection, and auditability. They can restrict exports of surveillance tools to authoritarian governments. They can fund open, public-interest AI infrastructure so that universities, nonprofits, small firms, and public agencies are not wholly dependent on the priorities of a few dominant platforms. They can use antitrust law to reduce the concentration of data, talent, and cloud infrastructure that gives today’s technology giants such influence over the research agenda.

Ultimately, the question is not whether AI will be “good” or “bad.” It is whether democratic societies can still make collective choices about powerful technologies before those technologies narrow the space for choice. Acemoglu’s essay rightly calls for a redirection of AI. That redirection should be understood as a broader democratization of technological development: public funding with public obligations, worker voice in deployment, enforceable rights for affected people, limits on surveillance, and institutions that reward complementing human capabilities rather than bypassing them. AI’s future does not have to be dystopian. But preventing that future will require not only better incentives for innovators, but more power for everyone else.

  3. 03Certain AI applications, such as real-time facial recognition and continuous worker surveillance, are structurally hostile to liberty and should be prohibited rather than merely regulated for 'responsible use.'
  4. 04Redistribution and a strong social safety net are necessary complements to innovation policy because they provide individuals with the economic security needed to resist exploitative technological implementations.

  6. 06The goal of technological redirection should be to build a more inclusive version of shared prosperity than the postwar era by valuing innovation in care work, teaching, and public administration.

"While the redirection of AI is necessary to prevent inequality and surveillance, this effort must be globalized to ensure that AI development does not entrench a new form of dependency or close off development paths for the global South."

Daron Acemoglu’s essay speaks primarily to the United States and other rich democracies. Its core warning—that AI may intensify inequality and surveillance unless redirected—is powerful. But from the perspective of developing countries, the question looks somewhat different. The danger is not only that AI will destroy good jobs in wealthy societies. It is also that the rules created by wealthy societies may entrench a global division between countries that own AI, countries that supply data and raw materials, and countries whose workers are rendered less valuable before they have had a chance to industrialize.

For much of the twentieth century, development was built on labor absorption. Countries became richer by moving workers from low-productivity agriculture into manufacturing and services. Export-oriented manufacturing, in particular, allowed countries in East Asia to create jobs, acquire capabilities, and climb the technological ladder. This path was not easy or automatic, but it offered a way for poorer countries to participate in global growth.

AI and robotics may narrow that path. If apparel, electronics assembly, logistics, call centers, business processing, and even some coding or accounting work become more automated, the comparative advantage of low-wage labor weakens. Rich-country firms may reshore production or keep more value in highly automated facilities. Multinational companies may need fewer workers in supplier countries. The result could be premature deindustrialization: countries losing manufacturing opportunities before they reach middle-income prosperity.

Acemoglu is right to worry about automation’s impact on workers in the United States. But for a worker in Bangladesh, Kenya, Vietnam, Mexico, or the Philippines, the relevant question is not simply whether AI replaces existing employment. It is whether AI prevents future employment from emerging. A technology that allows a U.S. company to produce cheaply at home may look like productivity growth in America while closing off a ladder abroad. The social cost will not be visible if we measure only domestic labor shares.

This complicates the call to redirect AI toward “human-complementary” uses. Complementing which humans? A medical AI system that empowers nurses in Boston is desirable. But if the global AI agenda is shaped entirely by the needs of rich-country professionals, it may neglect the needs of community health workers, small farmers, informal traders, and local manufacturers in poorer countries. AI for education should not only personalize lessons in well-equipped classrooms; it should help under-resourced schools with multilingual materials, teacher training, and low-connectivity environments. AI for agriculture should not only optimize industrial farms; it should support smallholders facing pests, climate variability, and limited credit. AI for finance should not only improve fraud detection for major banks; it should expand fair access to credit while avoiding predatory scoring.

The current structure of AI development is badly suited to these goals. The largest firms are headquartered in the United States and China. They control cloud infrastructure, data pipelines, computing resources, platforms, and talent. Their business models depend on advertising, e-commerce, enterprise software, surveillance, and consumer services for large markets. Poorer countries often become users rather than producers. Their languages are underrepresented in datasets. Their institutions have limited capacity to regulate. Their citizens’ data may be extracted without building local capability. Their startups may be bought or crushed by platform giants.

This is a new form of dependency. It is not colonialism in the old sense, but it can reproduce unequal power. If African, Latin American, and South Asian governments rely on foreign vendors for digital identity, policing, welfare targeting, education platforms, and health systems, they may surrender public capacity to companies that are not accountable to their citizens. If those vendors provide opaque algorithmic systems, errors and biases become difficult to challenge. If data are stored abroad or controlled by foreign cloud providers, sovereignty is weakened.

The surveillance concern is especially acute outside established democracies. Many governments want tools to monitor opponents, journalists, minorities, and social movements. AI-powered surveillance is attractive because it promises control without the visible brutality of older repression. Facial recognition, phone hacking, social media monitoring, and predictive analytics can help regimes identify dissent early. Even governments that hold elections may use these tools to tilt the playing field. The export of surveillance technology from China, Israel, Europe, and the United States to fragile democracies should be treated as a human rights issue.

But developing countries also have legitimate security and administrative needs. They face crime, tax evasion, corruption, disaster risk, and limited state capacity. Digital systems can help deliver benefits, track disease, manage infrastructure, and reduce leakage. A blanket suspicion of state technology would be unrealistic. The challenge is to build accountable capacity rather than import digital authoritarianism. That requires local democratic institutions, independent courts, civil society, data protection laws, and technical expertise inside the public sector.

Acemoglu’s analogy to climate policy is useful but incomplete. In climate negotiations, developing countries have long argued that rich countries used fossil fuels to become wealthy and now ask poorer countries to constrain emissions. Something similar could happen with AI. Rich countries benefited from earlier waves of automation and industrialization. If they now set AI standards that protect their own workers while limiting technology transfer or imposing costly compliance burdens, poorer countries may see AI governance as protectionism disguised as ethics.

This does not mean that labor and privacy standards should be abandoned. On the contrary, workers everywhere need protection. But global AI governance must include development concerns. It should support open research, affordable computing access, multilingual datasets governed by communities, public-interest AI tools, and technology transfer. International institutions should help poorer countries build regulatory capacity rather than merely pressure them to adopt standards written in Brussels, Washington, or Beijing.

There is also a need to rethink intellectual property. If AI becomes a general-purpose technology essential to health, education, agriculture, and climate adaptation, excessive concentration of patents, models, and infrastructure will harm global welfare. Publicly funded AI research should be open where possible. Models developed for public health, disaster response, and education should be treated as global public goods. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the world has seen how unequal access to technology—vaccines most dramatically—can deepen global injustice. We should not reproduce that pattern in AI.

Labor policy in developing countries must also adapt. Many workers are informal, outside standard employment relationships. Calls for collective bargaining and wage floors remain important, but they must be designed for realities of informality, migration, and small enterprises. AI could help extend social protection by improving identification and payment systems, but it could also exclude people through rigid databases and automated eligibility decisions. Welfare algorithms in countries with weak appeal systems can become instruments of bureaucratic cruelty. Human discretion, local knowledge, and grievance mechanisms are essential.

A more hopeful path is possible. AI could help community health workers diagnose conditions, assist teachers in local languages, provide farmers with weather and market information, improve disaster warning systems, map pollution, support small manufacturers with design tools, and make government services more accessible. These applications would not simply copy Silicon Valley models. They would be rooted in local problems and local institutions. They would require public investment, universities, regional cooperation, and participation from the communities affected.

This is why the question “who controls AI?” must be globalized. It is not enough to reduce the influence of American tech giants if control merely shifts to Chinese giants or to a small circle of rich-country regulators. Countries in the global South need seats at the table. Their researchers need funding. Their languages and knowledge systems need representation. Their citizens need rights over data. Their workers need a development strategy that does not assume the old manufacturing ladder will remain intact.

Acemoglu’s insistence that AI’s future is not inevitable is the right starting point. But the “we” who must choose that future cannot be limited to affluent democracies worried about their postwar compact. There was no postwar compact for much of the world; there was colonial aftermath, Cold War intervention, debt crisis, structural adjustment, and uneven development. A just AI future must not only restore shared prosperity where it has eroded. It must expand the possibility of prosperity where it has long been denied.

  2. 02The definition of 'human-complementary' AI must be expanded beyond the needs of rich-country professionals to address the specific challenges of smallholders, community health workers, and under-resourced schools in poorer regions.
  3. 03The concentration of AI infrastructure and data in a few powerful nations creates a new form of digital dependency that undermines the sovereignty and public capacity of developing states.
  4. 04The export of AI surveillance tools to fragile democracies and authoritarian regimes in the global South poses a distinct and acute threat to international human rights.
  5. 05Global AI governance must move beyond rich-country standards to include technology transfer, open-source public goods, and the protection of informal workers.
  6. 06A just AI future requires that developing nations have the funding, representation, and regulatory capacity to shape technology according to their own local institutions and needs.

"Instead of attempting to redirect AI research away from automation, society should embrace technological progress while ensuring shared prosperity through universal basic income and robust redistribution."

Daron Acemoglu wants to redirect artificial intelligence away from automation and toward human-complementing tasks. His argument is intelligently crafted, but it rests on a mistaken assumption: that preserving “good jobs” through technological design is the only, or even the best, route to shared prosperity in an age of intelligent machines. The real solution is to accept that automation will accelerate and to build an economic floor beneath everyone through universal basic income and broad-based redistribution. Redirecting AI is a Quixotic attempt to fight the market’s natural trajectory; strengthening the safety net is a pragmatic and proven strategy.

Acemoglu’s historical narrative contends that shared prosperity in the postwar period was built on “human-friendly” technological change that created new tasks, not just on redistribution. But this downplays the role of fiscal transfers. The three decades after World War II were marked by steeply progressive taxation, massive public investment in infrastructure and education, and the expansion of Social Security, Medicare, and other welfare programs. The Nordic countries, which he cites approvingly, combine high levels of unionization and productivity with extremely generous social benefits and active labor market policies. The Nordic compact is not just about good jobs; it is about a state that ensures no one falls into destitution regardless of their employment status. In other words, redistribution is not merely a supporting pillar; it is the foundation that makes labor-market bargains politically sustainable.

If we decouple basic economic security from employment, the anxiety about AI-driven automation largely dissolves. When people know they will have a decent income regardless of how the labor market evolves, technological displacement becomes a matter of transition rather than catastrophe. Acemoglu worries that without good jobs, democracy will wither because people will lack “viable social purpose.” This undervalues the myriad ways people find purpose outside formal employment — through family, community, creative endeavors, and civic participation. The postwar male-breadwinner model of dignity-through-labor was already crumbling before AI arrived, as women entered the workforce, career durations shortened, and the gig economy expanded. A UBI could liberate people to pursue education, caregiving, and entrepreneurship in ways that the current job-locked system inhibits.

Acemoglu’s case for redirecting AI also assumes a degree of state capacity that simply does not exist in the current political climate. He calls for a three-pronged approach of government funding, norm shifting, and democratic oversight. But as he himself acknowledges, democratic governance is impaired, money dominates politics, and the tech giants wield enormous influence. In such an environment, a government-led effort to decide which AI is “socially beneficial” and which is not is an open invitation to regulatory capture. The big players will use their lobbying muscle to ensure that the government-funded “good” AI is defined in ways that complement their business models while small, disruptive innovators are squeezed out by compliance costs. The clean-energy analogy is again instructive: large incumbents have often supported subsidies that benefit their own technologies while blocking more radical challenges. A similar dynamic in AI would entrench the very concentration of power Acemoglu laments.

Instead of the impossible task of micro-managing technological direction, we should focus on what we actually can do: tax the winners and redistribute the gains. A robustly progressive tax system, including a digital services tax, a financial transactions tax, and higher top marginal rates on both personal income and corporate profits, could fund a universal basic income sufficient to guarantee a dignified existence. This is not a utopian fantasy; it is the logical extension of the welfare state that already exists in many countries. Alaskans have a form of basic income from oil revenues. The pandemic relief payments in the United States demonstrated that direct cash transfers are administratively feasible and politically popular. The challenge is institutionalizing them permanently.

Acemoglu might object that redistribution alone cannot sustain democracy because it breeds resentment and social division. But the evidence points in the opposite direction. Countries with strong universal welfare states tend to have higher levels of social trust and political participation. Resentment flourishes not from redistribution itself but from means-tested programs that stigmatize recipients and drive a wedge between the “deserving” and “undeserving.” A universal program, by contrast, reinforces solidarity: everyone gets it, everyone pays in. That solidarity is a much sturdier basis for democratic resilience than the fragile hope that we can design technology to create good jobs for all.

Finally, the international dimension that Acemoglu raises actually strengthens the case for UBI. If global competition makes it difficult for any one country to redirect AI without others undercutting it, that same competition also makes UBI attractive as a domestic stability measure. Nations that successfully cushion their populations against technological disruption will be more resilient and less prone to nationalist backlash. They can then lead by example, demonstrating that automation plus redistribution produces a freer and more prosperous society than the surveillance-driven model of authoritarian competitors.

Acemoglu’s heart is in the right place. He wants technology to serve humanity, and he understands the dangers of ceding control to unaccountable market forces. But his proposed remedy — steering the ship — overestimates our navigational skills and ignores the simpler solution: equip everyone with a life jacket and let them swim. In an age of intelligent machines, unconditional income is not a consolation prize for a jobless future; it is the ticket to a genuinely free society.

"While the trajectory of AI is a political choice rather than a technological destiny, implementing a program to redirect it toward human augmentation faces significant practical, geopolitical, and technical hurdles that the lead essay underestimates."

Daron Acemoglu has written a bracing and largely persuasive essay, and the argument's structure — that the trajectory of AI is a political choice, not a technological destiny — is the right place to start any serious public discussion of this technology. But I want to push on three points where I think the analysis, while correct in its broad strokes, papers over difficulties that will determine whether his program is workable.

The first concerns the labor-share measurement framework Acemoglu proposes as the policy lever for distinguishing "good" AI from "bad" AI. He suggests that since automation tends to reduce labor's share of value added while new-task technologies tend to raise it, governments can use this distinction to tilt incentives toward the latter. This is elegant in theory, but as a basis for actual policy it strikes me as fragile. Labor share is determined by an enormous number of forces — bargaining power, market concentration, trade, the price of capital goods, the composition of output across sectors — and disentangling the contribution of a specific class of AI applications would be extraordinarily difficult, especially in real time, before tax credits or subsidies are issued. Worse, the metric is gameable. A firm that fires call-center workers and rehires them at lower wages as "AI-assisted customer experience associates" could plausibly raise its measured labor share while doing exactly the kind of low-road automation Acemoglu worries about. We saw analogous problems with R&D tax credits in the 1980s, where firms reclassified ordinary engineering as research. Without sharper categories — perhaps tied to occupational task content, not aggregate labor share — the policy lever will bend.

Second, I think Acemoglu underplays the degree to which the postwar "shared prosperity plus democracy" compact was historically peculiar rather than a template easily reconstructed. He acknowledges that the Trente Glorieuses depended on a particular configuration of antitrust, union strength, federal R&D funding, and competitive product markets — but he tends to treat that configuration as a choice we could simply make again. The international comparison he draws to clean energy is instructive but also somewhat misleading. The carbon-abatement story worked in part because emissions are physically measurable, because the EU and a handful of large states could create a price signal that propagated globally through trade, and because the relevant innovations (solar panels, wind turbines, batteries) were embodied in discrete capital goods that could be subsidized at the point of deployment. AI is software. It diffuses through APIs and open-source repositories at near-zero marginal cost, the relevant talent is globally mobile, and there is no physical analogue to a tonne of CO2 against which to denominate "harm." This makes the coordination problem he flags at the end — what if China keeps pushing surveillance and automation? — not a peripheral worry but the central one. A national policy that taxes automation in the U.S. while Chinese firms continue down the current path could simply shift rents abroad without changing the underlying trajectory.

Third, and most importantly, I think the essay underestimates how much of the "automate everything" bias of current AI is downstream not just of who funds research but of the underlying technical paradigm. Acemoglu rightly notes that the modern AI revival came from reframing services as prediction problems amenable to statistical learning over big datasets. But this framing has a built-in directional bias: the cheapest and most legible applications of supervised learning are precisely those in which a human is already performing a repetitive labeled task whose outputs can be used as training data. In other words, the technology learns to do what humans already do, because that's what the training signal contains. Building AI that creates *new* tasks — that augments a teacher's adaptive instruction, or extends a nurse's diagnostic range — requires a different research program: one focused on interfaces, on tacit knowledge elicitation, on human-AI collaboration, and on domains where labeled data does not yet exist. This is not just a matter of redirecting funding from automation projects to augmentation projects; it requires investing in fundamentally less mature subfields (HCI, cognitive science, mixed-initiative systems) that have languished while deep learning has absorbed nearly all the oxygen. Acemoglu mentions these areas but doesn't quite grapple with the fact that they are scientifically harder, not just politically neglected.

Where I think Acemoglu is unambiguously right, and where his essay should be required reading, is on the inadequacy of the "let the market choose, then redistribute" position. The argument that good jobs are not merely instruments for income but constitutive of democratic citizenship is the strongest move in the essay, and it cuts cleanly through the universal-basic-income discourse that has filled this space for the past few years. A polity in which most people receive transfers from a small productive elite is not one in which democratic accountability survives long; the political economy of redistribution requires a citizenry that is also a producing citizenry.

So I read Acemoglu's essay less as a finished blueprint than as a successful reframing: AI policy is industrial policy and democratic policy, not consumer-protection policy. The measurement problems, the international coordination problems, and the technical-paradigm problems are real and may be binding. But they are problems to be worked on, not reasons to accept the current path as fate.

That institutional ecosystem has been dismantled over decades, and AI itself now accelerates its erosion by enabling platform monopolies, gig-work arrangements, and algorithmic management that further undermine worker power. Redirecting research funding without simultaneously rebuilding those countervailing institutions risks producing a kinder, gentler automation that still leaves labor disempowered.

Then there is the democratic oversight prong. Social media platforms powered by AI have already proven adept at inflaming polarization, spreading disinformation, and enabling micro-targeted manipulation. The 2020 U.S. election and the January 6 Capitol riot made plain that a significant portion of the electorate now inhabits an epistemological bubble actively maintained by recommender systems. If we are to redirect AI, we must not only measure the labor share but also rebuild the institutional muscle that once forced technology to serve broad-based prosperity. That is a taller order, and one that AI itself makes harder with each passing year.

The real choice is not between laissez-faire and technocratic steering. It is between a future where AI serves a tiny elite and one where it becomes a genuine commons, governed by and for the many. That democratic project, hard as it is, must be the centerpiece of any response to the challenge Acemoglu so brilliantly outlines.

The authoritarian model of AI—characterized by pervasive surveillance and state-directed automation—is advancing rapidly.

This means aggressively exploring universal basic income, expanding the social safety net, decoupling healthcare from employment, and fostering new models of collective bargaining that allow citizens to share in the wealth generated by digital capital. The post-war era of shared prosperity that Acemoglu highlights was not just a product of lucky, pro-labor technological developments; it was forced into existence by muscular labor unions, high marginal tax rates, and robust public investments.

We cannot force AI to be inherently pro-labor, but we can build an economy where the automation of human labor is a collective triumph rather than a personal tragedy. Our democratic oversight should not be focused on regulating the algorithms in the lab, but on fiercely redistributing the unprecedented wealth those algorithms will generate in the market.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Unlike carbon emissions in green energy, the labor impact of general-purpose AI is impossible to measure or predict before a technology is fully developed and deployed.
  2. 02Market incentives to reduce variable costs and achieve scale will always favor automation over human labor, regardless of shifting social norms or tax adjustments.
  3. 03Unilaterally restricting or slowing AI automation in democratic nations would risk ceding global technological and geopolitical leadership to authoritarian regimes like China.
  4. 04The 'automation versus augmentation' distinction is often a false binary because increasing human productivity through augmentation frequently leads to the redundancy of workers.
  5. 05Rather than trying to micromanage the direction of research, society should focus on robust redistributive mechanisms and new social institutions to decouple economic security from traditional labor.
