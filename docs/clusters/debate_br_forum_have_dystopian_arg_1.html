Vanilla Claude

"While the trajectory of AI is a political choice rather than a technological destiny, implementing a program to redirect it toward human augmentation faces significant practical, geopolitical, and technical hurdles that the lead essay underestimates."

Daron Acemoglu has written a bracing and largely persuasive essay, and the argument's structure — that the trajectory of AI is a political choice, not a technological destiny — is the right place to start any serious public discussion of this technology. But I want to push on three points where I think the analysis, while correct in its broad strokes, papers over difficulties that will determine whether his program is workable.

The first concerns the labor-share measurement framework Acemoglu proposes as the policy lever for distinguishing "good" AI from "bad" AI. He suggests that since automation tends to reduce labor's share of value added while new-task technologies tend to raise it, governments can use this distinction to tilt incentives toward the latter. This is elegant in theory, but as a basis for actual policy it strikes me as fragile. Labor share is determined by an enormous number of forces — bargaining power, market concentration, trade, the price of capital goods, the composition of output across sectors — and disentangling the contribution of a specific class of AI applications would be extraordinarily difficult, especially in real time, before tax credits or subsidies are issued. Worse, the metric is gameable. A firm that fires call-center workers and rehires them at lower wages as "AI-assisted customer experience associates" could plausibly raise its measured labor share while doing exactly the kind of low-road automation Acemoglu worries about. We saw analogous problems with R&D tax credits in the 1980s, where firms reclassified ordinary engineering as research. Without sharper categories — perhaps tied to occupational task content, not aggregate labor share — the policy lever will bend.

Second, I think Acemoglu underplays the degree to which the postwar "shared prosperity plus democracy" compact was historically peculiar rather than a template easily reconstructed. He acknowledges that the Trente Glorieuses depended on a particular configuration of antitrust, union strength, federal R&D funding, and competitive product markets — but he tends to treat that configuration as a choice we could simply make again. The international comparison he draws to clean energy is instructive but also somewhat misleading. The carbon-abatement story worked in part because emissions are physically measurable, because the EU and a handful of large states could create a price signal that propagated globally through trade, and because the relevant innovations (solar panels, wind turbines, batteries) were embodied in discrete capital goods that could be subsidized at the point of deployment. AI is software. It diffuses through APIs and open-source repositories at near-zero marginal cost, the relevant talent is globally mobile, and there is no physical analogue to a tonne of CO2 against which to denominate "harm." This makes the coordination problem he flags at the end — what if China keeps pushing surveillance and automation? — not a peripheral worry but the central one. A national policy that taxes automation in the U.S. while Chinese firms continue down the current path could simply shift rents abroad without changing the underlying trajectory.

Third, and most importantly, I think the essay underestimates how much of the "automate everything" bias of current AI is downstream not just of who funds research but of the underlying technical paradigm. Acemoglu rightly notes that the modern AI revival came from reframing services as prediction problems amenable to statistical learning over big datasets. But this framing has a built-in directional bias: the cheapest and most legible applications of supervised learning are precisely those in which a human is already performing a repetitive labeled task whose outputs can be used as training data. In other words, the technology learns to do what humans already do, because that's what the training signal contains. Building AI that creates *new* tasks — that augments a teacher's adaptive instruction, or extends a nurse's diagnostic range — requires a different research program: one focused on interfaces, on tacit knowledge elicitation, on human-AI collaboration, and on domains where labeled data does not yet exist. This is not just a matter of redirecting funding from automation projects to augmentation projects; it requires investing in fundamentally less mature subfields (HCI, cognitive science, mixed-initiative systems) that have languished while deep learning has absorbed nearly all the oxygen. Acemoglu mentions these areas but doesn't quite grapple with the fact that they are scientifically harder, not just politically neglected.

Where I think Acemoglu is unambiguously right, and where his essay should be required reading, is on the inadequacy of the "let the market choose, then redistribute" position. The argument that good jobs are not merely instruments for income but constitutive of democratic citizenship is the strongest move in the essay, and it cuts cleanly through the universal-basic-income discourse that has filled this space for the past few years. A polity in which most people receive transfers from a small productive elite is not one in which democratic accountability survives long; the political economy of redistribution requires a citizenry that is also a producing citizenry.

So I read Acemoglu's essay less as a finished blueprint than as a successful reframing: AI policy is industrial policy and democratic policy, not consumer-protection policy. The measurement problems, the international coordination problems, and the technical-paradigm problems are real and may be binding. But they are problems to be worked on, not reasons to accept the current path as fate.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The proposed labor-share measurement framework is too fragile and gameable to serve as a reliable basis for tax policy or subsidies.
  2. 02The historical postwar compact of shared prosperity is difficult to reconstruct because AI is a globally mobile software technology that lacks a physical, measurable metric for harm like carbon emissions.
  3. 03The current 'automate everything' bias is deeply embedded in the dominant statistical learning paradigm, making the creation of human-augmenting tasks a fundamentally harder scientific challenge than simply redirecting funds.
  4. 04The lead essay correctly identifies that meaningful work is essential for democratic citizenship and that redistribution alone cannot sustain a healthy political economy.
Vanilla DeepSeek

"While Acemoglu’s goal of redirecting AI toward human-centered innovation is vital, his proposed solutions underestimate the deep structural, geopolitical, and institutional obstacles that make a return to the postwar 'shared prosperity' model far more difficult today."

Daron Acemoglu’s diagnosis of AI’s present trajectory is both trenchant and urgent, and his refusal to accept the inevitability of job-destroying automation and surveillance is a vital corrective to a discourse that too often oscillates between techno-utopianism and fatalism. Yet for all its moral clarity, his proposed redirection of AI relies on an overly sanguine reading of postwar history and underestimates the structural obstacles that make a repeat of that era’s human-centered innovation far more difficult today. The vision of a three-pronged approach—government leadership, shifting norms, and democratic oversight—is admirable, but it elides the degree to which AI itself is already transforming the very conditions for democratic agency, and it places too much faith in the state as a neutral steward of technological direction.

Acemoglu’s master analogy is the postwar compact, where technological change was channeled into productivity-enhancing tasks rather than pure automation, aided by vigorous government funding, strong labor institutions, and competitive markets. The picture is compelling, but it obscures crucial differences. Postwar America invested heavily in science and technology at a moment of extraordinary geopolitical dominance and domestic political consensus. The Cold War created a bipartisan appetite for research, and the state’s role as a lead purchaser—particularly through the Department of Defense—was not simply a benign coordinator but an actor with its own security imperatives that often drove innovation toward surveillance and control from the very start. The same military-industrial complex that underwrote the internet and semiconductors also pioneered early AI, including the very pattern-recognition techniques that now power facial recognition. It is not an accident that today’s surveillance giants draw on the same government funding pipelines that Acemoglu wants to redirect; the state has always been deeply ambivalent, not a pure force for the democratic good.

The challenge is deeper still when we examine the market-reshaping tools he proposes. Acemoglu suggests a measurement framework based on the labor share of value added to distinguish “good” AI that creates new tasks from “bad” AI that automates them away. The intuition is elegant, but it assumes a stable, measurable link between a given AI application and aggregate labor share. In reality, most technologies do both: a medical diagnostic AI might displace some tasks for radiologists while simultaneously creating new roles for technicians who interpret and communicate results, and the net effect on labor share depends on bargaining power, organizational choices, and regulatory context—all of which are already skewed by the very political dynamics Acemoglu laments. A tax code that penalizes labor and subsidizes capital is one such distortion; but fixing that tax asymmetry, while sensible, does not guarantee that the new equilibrium will produce good jobs. In an economy where firms face little competitive pressure and workers have limited bargaining power, productivity gains from AI can simply be captured as rents by a handful of dominant companies, even if the AI itself is task-creating. The lesson from the postwar period is not merely that technology was human-friendly, but that a dense web of institutions—strong unions, sectoral bargaining, progressive taxation, vigorous antitrust—forced firms to share the gains. That institutional ecosystem has been dismantled over decades, and AI itself now accelerates its erosion by enabling platform monopolies, gig-work arrangements, and algorithmic management that further undermine worker power. Redirecting research funding without simultaneously rebuilding those countervailing institutions risks producing a kinder, gentler automation that still leaves labor disempowered.

Then there is the democratic oversight prong. Acemoglu acknowledges that democracy is weakening, but he treats this as a reversible trend—something that can be revitalized by the very process of redirecting AI. This risks circularity. Social media platforms powered by AI have already proven adept at inflaming polarization, spreading disinformation, and enabling micro-targeted manipulation. The 2020 U.S. election and the January 6 Capitol riot made plain that a significant portion of the electorate now inhabits an epistemological bubble actively maintained by recommender systems. In such an environment, the kind of broad public consensus on what AI applications are socially harmful is extraordinarily hard to achieve. The same technologies that need to be governed are corroding the shared factual basis required for democratic governance. Moreover, even if a democratic awakening miraculously occurred in the United States, Acemoglu’s international dimension becomes an almost insurmountable hurdle. China’s AI trajectory, with its deep integration of surveillance and social credit systems, is not merely a competing paradigm—it is an alternative political-economic model that exerts competitive pressure on Western democracies. If U.S. firms voluntarily forswear lucrative facial recognition contracts, Chinese firms will eagerly fill the gap, and authoritarian regimes around the world will have a ready supplier. International coordination on AI is not just difficult; it is structurally opposed by the very geopolitical rivalries that Acemoglu invokes as a spur to innovation. One cannot wish this away with a call for “a modicum of international coordination.”

Finally, the norms-shifting component places a heavy burden on individual researchers and public pressure. The examples Acemoglu cites—NSO Group’s reputational crisis, IBM and Microsoft temporarily halting facial recognition sales—are notable but hardly transformative. They occurred in a context of heightened racial justice protests and exceptional media scrutiny, not sustained structural change. Tech companies have already resumed lobbying against facial recognition bans and quietly continue developing the underlying capabilities. Academic researchers remain dependent on corporate funding and data access; the revolving door spins ever faster. Changing norms is possible, but without altering the material incentives that channel the brightest minds toward automation and surveillance, moral exhortation will remain a niche pursuit.

None of this means Acemoglu’s project is hopeless. His insistence that AI’s direction is a choice, not a destiny, is a necessary first step. But realizing that choice requires confronting the deep entanglement of AI with the very structures—political, economic, geopolitical—that need to be reformed. The postwar analogy offers inspiration, but the conditions that made Les Trente Glorieuses possible were historically exceptional, and the digital era has produced a form of capitalism far more resistant to the shared-prosperity compact. If we are to redirect AI, we must not only measure the labor share but also rebuild the institutional muscle that once forced technology to serve broad-based prosperity. That is a taller order, and one that AI itself makes harder with each passing year.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The postwar era was driven by a military-industrial complex that prioritized security and surveillance, suggesting the state is not a neutral or purely democratic steward of technological direction.
  2. 02A measurement framework for 'good' AI is insufficient because productivity gains will still be captured as rents by dominant firms unless the dismantled ecosystem of labor unions and antitrust enforcement is rebuilt.
  3. 03AI-driven polarization and disinformation have already corroded the shared factual basis and democratic agency required to achieve a public consensus on regulating the technology.
  4. 04The competitive pressure from China’s surveillance-integrated AI model makes the necessary international coordination on ethical AI standards structurally unlikely.
  5. 05Shifting the norms of AI researchers is unlikely to succeed as long as the material incentives of corporate funding and data access continue to favor automation and surveillance.
Vanilla MiniMax

"While the future of AI is not predetermined, Daron Acemoglu’s proposed three-pronged strategy for redirecting it underestimates the depth of institutional entrenchment and the practical difficulty of implementing such reforms in a polarized, competitive global landscape."

Acemoglu's essay makes a compelling case that the direction of AI development is not inevitable, but his optimistic framing obscures how deeply the forces he describes are already entrenched. While I share his conviction that alternative paths exist, the practical obstacles to redirection demand more rigorous attention than his essay provides.

The historical parallel with postwar prosperity is instructive but incomplete. The technological dynamism of 1945-1975 occurred within a specific geopolitical context—the Cold War—that created powerful incentives for democratic nations to demonstrate the superiority of their system. Federal funding for research was motivated not primarily by concern for workers but by competition with the Soviet Union. This raises a troubling question: if the external pressure that helped generate broadly shared growth was strategic rather than altruistic, what comparable pressure exists today? The answer, uncomfortably, may be that nothing analogous is in force.

Acemoglu rightly identifies market failures in AI development, but his proposed corrections face challenges he understates. His measurement framework—using the labor share of value added to distinguish beneficial AI from harmful automation—is theoretically elegant but practically difficult. Firms routinely resist detailed measurement requirements, and the political will to impose such frameworks has proven elusive even for less complex interventions. The experience of regulatory efforts in financial technology suggests that by the time adequate measurement systems are developed, the technologies they target have often become so embedded that reform becomes politically near-impossible.

The norm-shifting dimension is perhaps the most speculative part of Acemoglu's argument. He points to the backlash against NSO Group and facial recognition vendors as evidence that social pressure can work. But these cases also reveal the limits of such pressure. The companies he mentions faced consequences only after scandals became impossible to ignore—not because society had developed consistent ethical frameworks for evaluating AI. The average technology worker's understanding of how their products might affect democracy or labor markets remains shallow, and professional norms in the field continue to reward technical achievement over social consideration.

More troubling is Acemoglu's treatment of international competition. He acknowledges this challenge but treats it as a complicating factor rather than a potentially fatal one. The logic of his three-pronged approach depends on democratic governance capable of sustained action, but democratic systems are precisely what authoritarian competitors hope to undermine or circumvent. China's AI development proceeds with state coordination that Western democracies cannot replicate and under political conditions that preclude the kind of transparency and accountability Acemoglu advocates. If redirection requires international coordination, and international coordination requires functioning multilateral institutions, and those institutions are weakened by the very technologies we seek to govern, the circularity becomes vicious rather than virtuous.

There is also a dimension Acemoglu's framework largely ignores: the temporal mismatch between technological change and institutional response. Democratic governance operates on electoral cycles; technology development operates on venture capital cycles. The gap between these rhythms helps explain why the "market" has achieved such influence in the first place. Parliaments and regulatory agencies lack the capacity to evaluate AI systems in real time, let alone redirect development. Building that capacity would require not merely new policies but fundamental institutional reform—an undertaking of enormous difficulty in polarized political environments.

None of this means Acemoglu is wrong to insist that the future is not predetermined. The technological possibilities he describes are genuine, and there is no law of nature requiring AI to serve narrow commercial interests at the expense of human flourishing. But recognizing possibility is not the same as charting a credible path to realization. The essay's confidence in the three-pronged approach underestimates how thoroughly the current trajectory has been institutionalized—in corporate strategies, academic incentive structures, government procurement practices, and professional identities. Changing course will require not just policy reform but a transformation of the political and economic conditions that make current trajectories profitable and convenient.

Acemoglu concludes by urging action while acknowledging enormous challenges. This is responsible counsel, but responsibility also demands clarity about what would actually be required. Redirecting AI is not merely a matter of adopting better policies; it is a project of political reconstruction that would need to succeed precisely where democracy is weakest and competitors most aggressive. The alternative paths he describes are real, but accessing them requires facing the full depth of the obstacles, not just their existence.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The historical postwar prosperity Acemoglu cites was driven by unique Cold War geopolitical pressures that are absent in the current era.
  2. 02Measuring the 'labor share of value added' to regulate AI is theoretically elegant but practically difficult to enforce against corporate resistance and rapid technological embedding.
  3. 03Norm-shifting among AI researchers is speculative and currently lacks a consistent ethical framework beyond reacting to major public scandals.
  4. 04The necessity of international coordination is a potentially fatal obstacle given that authoritarian competitors like China operate under political conditions that preclude democratic accountability.
  5. 05There is a fundamental temporal mismatch between slow-moving democratic governance and the rapid cycles of venture-capital-driven technological development.
  6. 06Redirecting AI requires a total reconstruction of political and economic conditions rather than just the adoption of better policies.
Vanilla Gemini

"While Acemoglu’s diagnosis of AI’s risks is accurate, his proposal to redirect AI research toward human-complementary applications is impractical due to economic, epistemological, and geopolitical realities."

Daron Acemoglu accurately captures the defining anxiety of our current technological era: artificial intelligence, left to its current trajectory, threatens to fracture the foundations of shared prosperity and democratic governance. His diagnosis of the post-1980s shift—where technology ceased to reliably create new, wage-boosting tasks for human workers and instead became an engine for pure automation and capital concentration—is both historically grounded and deeply unsettling. We are indeed drifting toward a political economy dominated by algorithmic surveillance and routine job displacement. However, while Acemoglu’s call to deliberately redirect AI research toward "human-complementary" applications is morally compelling, the practical mechanisms he proposes to achieve this face insurmountable economic, epistemological, and geopolitical hurdles.

The core of Acemoglu’s solution relies on government policy, shifting norms, and democratic oversight to actively steer AI research away from automation and toward augmentation. To do this, he suggests developing a measurement framework based on how technologies affect the labor share of value added, allowing policymakers to subsidize the good AI and tax the bad. The fatal flaw in this approach is the assumption that we can accurately categorize the economic impact of general-purpose technologies before they are fully developed or deployed.

Unlike the transition to green energy, which Acemoglu uses as a primary comparison, the "pro-labor" AI transition lacks an objective, easily measurable metric like carbon emissions. A ton of carbon avoided is a universal good. A new machine learning architecture, however, is fundamentally neutral and highly malleable. Consider the recent breakthroughs in large language models, such as OpenAI’s GPT-3. Is natural language processing an "automating" technology or an "augmenting" one? The answer depends entirely on the business model of the firm deploying it. It could be used to entirely replace frontline customer service representatives, driving down the labor share. Conversely, it could be used as an adaptive tutoring tool—exactly the kind of application Acemoglu praises—empowering educators and increasing their productivity. Because the same algorithmic breakthrough enables both the dystopian and the utopian application, attempting to steer the basic research via government grants or tax incentives based on unpredictable downstream labor impacts is an exercise in futility.

Furthermore, relying on shifting cultural norms among researchers and tech companies to prioritize human-centric AI underestimates the sheer force of market incentives. Acemoglu is absolutely correct that our current tax code artificially subsidizes capital investments at the expense of human labor, and correcting this imbalance should be an immediate legislative priority. But even with a neutralized tax code, the fundamental drive of the modern firm is to reduce variable costs and achieve scale. Human labor is expensive, complex, and unscalable in the way software is. As long as algorithms can perform cognitive tasks at a fraction of the cost of human wages, the gravitational pull of automation will persist. We cannot simply shame corporations into abandoning massive cost-saving efficiencies, nor can we expect researchers to artificially kneecap the capabilities of the systems they are building just to keep humans in the loop. The "automation" vs "augmentation" binary is often a false one; true augmentation makes humans so productive that fewer of them are needed to achieve the same output, which inevitably looks like automation to the workers who are rendered redundant.

Most critically, Acemoglu introduces the international dimension as an afterthought, treating it as a complicating factor rather than the central, defining constraint on domestic AI policy. In 2021, we are in the midst of a fierce geopolitical competition over artificial intelligence, primarily with China. Just months ago, the National Security Commission on Artificial Intelligence explicitly warned that the United States must aggressively accelerate AI development to avoid losing its technological edge. The authoritarian model of AI—characterized by pervasive surveillance and state-directed automation—is advancing rapidly.

If the United States and its democratic allies impose heavy bureaucratic friction on AI development in the name of preserving the labor share, we risk ceding global technological leadership. An approach that deliberately slows down automation or restricts commercial surveillance technologies without a binding international framework—which, as Acemoglu admits, is highly unlikely given the current geopolitical climate—could hollow out the very tech industries that underpin Western geopolitical strength. We cannot afford to unilaterally disarm in the AI arms race, even if the domestic consequences of rapid automation are painful. Authoritarian regimes will not hesitate to maximize the efficiencies of AI, and if they dominate the global market, they will export their technological paradigms, surveillance and all.

If we cannot reliably predict which research will help or hurt workers, and if geopolitical realities prevent us from deliberately stifling automation, how do we avoid the dystopia Acemoglu fears? The answer is that we must lean far more heavily on the redistributive and institutional mechanisms that he dismisses as insufficient. Acemoglu argues that shared prosperity based predominantly on redistribution is a "fantasy," insisting that "good jobs" must remain the engine of society. But if AI truly lives up to its promise as a transformative general-purpose technology, the link between human labor and economic output may be permanently weakened.

Instead of trying to micromanage the direction of AI research to preserve the 20th-century model of labor, we must build 21st-century institutions capable of managing a highly automated economy. This means aggressively exploring universal basic income, expanding the social safety net, decoupling healthcare from employment, and fostering new models of collective bargaining that allow citizens to share in the wealth generated by digital capital. The post-war era of shared prosperity that Acemoglu highlights was not just a product of lucky, pro-labor technological developments; it was forced into existence by muscular labor unions, high marginal tax rates, and robust public investments.

We cannot force AI to be inherently pro-labor, but we can build an economy where the automation of human labor is a collective triumph rather than a personal tragedy. Our democratic oversight should not be focused on regulating the algorithms in the lab, but on fiercely redistributing the unprecedented wealth those algorithms will generate in the market.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Unlike carbon emissions in green energy, the labor impact of general-purpose AI is impossible to measure or predict before a technology is fully developed and deployed.
  2. 02Market incentives to reduce variable costs and achieve scale will always favor automation over human labor, regardless of shifting social norms or tax adjustments.
  3. 03Unilaterally restricting or slowing AI automation in democratic nations would risk ceding global technological and geopolitical leadership to authoritarian regimes like China.
  4. 04The 'automation versus augmentation' distinction is often a false binary because increasing human productivity through augmentation frequently leads to the redundancy of workers.
  5. 05Rather than trying to micromanage the direction of research, society should focus on robust redistributive mechanisms and new social institutions to decouple economic security from traditional labor.
