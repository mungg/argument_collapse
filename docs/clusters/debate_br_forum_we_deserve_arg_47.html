Diversified Gemini

"Evgeny Morozov’s critique of AI is undermined by a rigid, structuralist dichotomy between instrumental and ecological reason that ignores the creative agency of current users and offers politically impractical, state-centric solutions."

In “The AI We Deserve,” Evgeny Morozov sets out to rescue artificial intelligence from both the fatalism of its critics and the corporatism of its creators. By tracing AI’s teleological obsession back to Cold War funding and contrasting it with the “eolithic” craftsmanship championed by Hans Otto Storm, Morozov seeks a technological paradigm grounded in open-ended “ecological reason.” He argues persuasively that current generative AI models like ChatGPT offer glimpses of this eolithic freedom, but are ultimately shackled by the profit motives of the “Efficiency Lobby.” To achieve true democratization, he proposes aligning AI with radical political projects, drawing inspiration from socialist experiments in 1970s Latin America. While Morozov’s critique of instrumental reason is a tour de force of intellectual history, his reliance on a structuralist, almost determinist, framework ultimately traps him in the very binaries he seeks to escape, rendering his proposed solutions both politically naive and practically vague.

Morozov’s argument hinges on a sharp dichotomy between “instrumental reason” (bureaucratic, goal-oriented, market-driven) and “ecological reason” (playful, contextual, socially embedded). This binary maps neatly onto his historical villains (the Cold War military-industrial complex, neoliberal tech giants) and his heroes (the prehistoric flâneur, progressive Latin American planners). But this structuralist categorization flattens the complex, messy reality of technological development and human agency.

First, the characterization of all early AI as purely “instrumental” ignores the profound weirdness and speculative nature of much mid-century computing research. While DARPA funding certainly demanded deliverables, the pioneers of AI and cybernetics were often engaged in deeply philosophical, open-ended inquiries about the nature of mind, language, and society. The lines between the “Efficiency Lobby” and the “Humanity Lobby” were never as cleanly drawn as Morozov suggests; figures like Terry Winograd and Seymour Papert, whom he praises, emerged from the very heart of the establishment he condemns.

Second, and more importantly, Morozov’s framing of generative AI completely misinterprets how the technology actually functions in the wild. He concedes that ChatGPT allows for some eolithic bricolage—like his language-learning app—but insists that because it is operated by a corporate entity charging an API fee, the “whole setup constrains the full potential and play of ecological reason.” This is a textbook example of genetic fallacy: assuming that because a technology is produced by a capitalist enterprise, every use of it must inherently reproduce capitalist alienation.

In reality, the user base of generative AI is engaging in massive, decentralized acts of ecological reason every day, regardless of OpenAI’s corporate structure. People are using LLMs to navigate neurodivergence, to role-play complex emotional scenarios, to generate collaborative interactive fiction, and to rapidly prototype community mutual-aid tools. The “stone” is being picked up and repurposed in millions of ways that provide no direct arbitrage opportunity and serve no bureaucratic mandate. The fact that OpenAI charges fractions of a cent per token does not negate the fundamentally eolithic nature of the interaction. Morozov’s insistence that our “emancipation through such powerful technologies feels so truncated” may reflect his own ideological rigidities more than the actual experience of millions of users who find genuine creative liberation in these tools.

When it comes to solutions, Morozov’s retreat to 1970s Latin America is politically baffling. Project Cybersyn and the work of Oscar Varsavsky are fascinating historical footnotes, but they are entirely inadequate as blueprints for governing 21st-century planetary-scale computation. Cybersyn was, fundamentally, a cybernetic control system designed to manage a closed national economy. It was heavily reliant on top-down instrumental reason—using telex machines to track production quotas and manage logistics. It was not a tool for “messing about” or playful bricolage; it was a desperate attempt to optimize production under siege. To present it as a triumph of “ecological reason” is to misread the history of Allende’s Chile.

Furthermore, Morozov’s call to “untether these tools from their market-driven development model” and redirect them toward “public, solidarity-based, and socialized infrastructural alternatives” is glaringly light on mechanics. Generative AI requires staggering concentrations of capital, highly specialized silicon, and vast energy resources. How exactly does a “radical political project” socialize this infrastructure without resorting to massive state centralization—which would inevitably reintroduce the very bureaucratic instrumentalism Morozov despises?

If the goal is to foster ecological reason, the answer is not to replace corporate monopolies with state monopolies. The most viable path forward is to champion the open-source AI ecosystem. The proliferation of open-weights models allows researchers, hobbyists, and activists to run powerful AI locally, bypassing corporate APIs entirely. This movement is actively dismantling the concentration of power in Big Tech, enabling exactly the heterogeneous, vernacular solutions Morozov desires. Yet, because the open-source movement is largely decentralized and often overlaps with commercial enterprise, it doesn’t fit neatly into Morozov’s grand, state-led socialist narrative.

Morozov is correct that we need a deeper vision for technology than mere efficiency. But by clinging to a rigid dichotomy between pure ecological play and tainted instrumental reason, he dismisses the messy, compromised, yet profoundly creative ways humans are already bending AI to their will. The “AI we deserve” will not be handed down by a normative planning committee; it is currently being hacked together, one prompt at a time, by users refusing to play by the rules of either the market or the state.
