"To effectively respond to the challenges posed by the Trump administration, the American press must first address the underlying structural and financial crises that have weakened its institutional capacity."

Behind every story about Trump and the press lies an uncomfortable truth: American journalism was already in crisis before January 2017. Declining revenues, staff reductions, the collapse of local news in many markets—these trends have weakened the institution now facing its greatest test. We cannot discuss how the press should respond to Trump without addressing whether the press can respond at all.

The numbers are sobering. Between 2008 and 2016, U.S. newspaper employment fell by more than a third. Newsroom budgets have been cut repeatedly at most major outlets. The Washington Post, which once maintained a foreign bureau comparable to small countries' intelligence services, has been rebuilt only recently after years of contraction. The president-elect's warning that he would move journalists out of the White House comes at a moment when fewer journalists are available to be moved.

This matters because adversarial coverage requires resources. A reporter who knows a subject deeply is harder to intimidate than one parachuting in for a single story. Institutional knowledge—understanding how agencies work, who the key officials are, what questions to ask—cannot be replaced bygoogling. Yet that knowledge is being lost as experienced journalists are laid off and not replaced.

The business model problem compounds the political problem. If the press cannot fund itself, it cannot cover power effectively. Subscription models show promise, but they favor outlets with established audiences, potentially widening the gap between elite national media and local coverage. Philanthropic support for journalism has grown, but it remains unevenly distributed. ProPublica and similar organizations do vital work, but they cannot replace the infrastructure of daily news.

Perhaps most troubling is the disappearance of local news. National outlets will cover the White House, though with varying vigor. But who is covering the state legislature? Who is watching the local school board? Who reports when a small city's government descends into corruption or mismanagement? These absences matter for democracy, and they create vacuums that propaganda and misinformation fill.

The press cannot respond effectively to Trump without first addressing these structural weaknesses. This means readers and viewers must support journalism financially. It means tech platforms that profit from distributing news must contribute to its production. It means foundations and philanthropists must recognize that a healthy press is public infrastructure as necessary as roads and bridges.

Journalists should acknowledge their vulnerability honestly. Pretending institutional strength that no longer exists invites overreach. The press should do what it can with what it has: prioritize stories that matter, maintain standards, and advocate publicly for their own survival. This is not defeatism. It is realism that might, paradoxically, strengthen journalism by forcing it to be selective and purposeful. Better a leaner press that maintains credibility than a bloated one that squanders it.

Trump's hostility is a crisis. But it is layered atop an existing emergency. Both require attention.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Significant reductions in newsroom staffing and budgets have eroded the institutional knowledge and resources necessary for rigorous adversarial reporting.
  2. 02The collapse of the traditional business model for news makes it difficult for journalists to fund the deep, investigative work required to hold power accountable.
  3. 03The decline of local news creates information vacuums that allow propaganda and misinformation to flourish at the community level.
  4. 04A sustainable response requires a collective effort from readers, tech platforms, and philanthropists to treat journalism as essential public infrastructure.
  5. 05Journalists must adopt a realistic strategy of prioritizing high-impact stories and maintaining strict standards to preserve their remaining credibility.
