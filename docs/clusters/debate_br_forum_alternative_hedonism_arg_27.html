Diversified Gemini

"Kate Soper’s "alternative hedonism" is a misguided political program that romanticizes material precarity and ignores the proven potential for technological innovation to decouple economic growth from environmental harm."

In her defense of “alternative hedonism,” Kate Soper offers a seductive vision of the future. It is a world where the frantic pace of consumer capitalism has been replaced by the quiet satisfactions of artisanal labor, leisurely train rides, and community gardens. For those of us exhausted by the relentless demands of modern life, the appeal of this pastoral retreat is undeniable. But as a political program for confronting the climate crisis and securing human flourishing, Soper’s vision is fundamentally misguided. It misdiagnoses the relationship between economic growth and environmental degradation, and it romanticizes a past of “slow working” and “self-provisioning” that was, for the vast majority of human history, characterized by backbreaking toil and material precarity.

Soper is right to point out that our current economic model has severe environmental costs. The reality of climate change is undeniable, and the need to transition away from fossil fuels is absolute. However, Soper falls into the trap of assuming that the only way to reduce carbon emissions and environmental impact is to arrest economic growth and drastically scale back consumption. This assumption ignores the massive empirical evidence of decoupling—the phenomenon where economies grow while their environmental impact shrinks.

Over the past two decades, dozens of affluent nations, including the United States, the United Kingdom, and Germany, have successfully decoupled their GDP growth from their carbon emissions. They are getting richer while emitting less. This is not the result of a mass spiritual awakening or a widespread adoption of alternative hedonism; it is the result of technological innovation, the shift from coal to natural gas and renewables, and improvements in energy efficiency. The solution to climate change is not to convince people to want less, but to build the technologies that allow them to have what they want without destroying the planet.

Soper’s critique of “green growth” and “tech utopians” relies on a static view of technology. She dismisses electric vehicles because they still use plastic and require electricity, ignoring the reality that energy grids are rapidly decarbonizing and that battery recycling technologies are advancing. She argues against the expansion of technology, preferring instead a return to “traditional artisanal modes of production.” But artisanal production is inherently inefficient. It requires more human labor to produce fewer goods. Soper argues this will create jobs and give people meaning, but she ignores the fact that when labor productivity falls, the cost of goods rises. A society that abandons mass production for artisanal craftsmanship is a society where only the wealthy can afford basic necessities.

Furthermore, Soper’s vision of “slow working” and “making do” relies on a profound historical amnesia. The convenience technologies that Soper derides—ready-meals, affordable appliances, fast transport—were revolutionary precisely because they freed humanity, and women in particular, from the drudgery of household labor. The washing machine, the refrigerator, and the supermarket did more to liberate women and the working class than any philosophical tract. To suggest that we should return to self-provisioning and mending is to suggest that we should voluntarily return to a life of domestic servitude. Soper attempts to wave this away by suggesting that a reduction in work hours would allow for “novel forms of cooperative consumption,” but the reality is that the burden of this “slow” domestic labor would inevitably fall on the most vulnerable.

The political failure of the degrowth movement, which Soper attempts to rebrand as alternative hedonism, is not due to a failure of marketing or the insidious influence of advertising. It is due to the fact that people actually like the benefits of modernity. They like being able to travel across the globe to see different cultures. They like having access to a vast array of affordable goods and services. They like the medical advancements, the cultural dynamism, and the material security that only a high-energy, growth-driven society can provide. To tell them that their desires are merely the result of being "groomed" by advertisers is not only deeply patronizing; it is a guaranteed recipe for political marginalization.

The transition to a sustainable future requires mass political buy-in. If the environmental movement tells the global public that saving the planet requires giving up air travel, giving up cheap goods, and spending their weekends darning socks and growing cabbages in community allotments, they will be fiercely rejected. The working and middle classes of the developed world are already facing a cost-of-living crisis. Telling them that they need to embrace austerity—even an aestheticized, “hedonistic” austerity—is political suicide.

Instead of a politics of scarcity, we need a politics of abundance. We need to deploy next-generation nuclear power, expand renewable energy infrastructure, invest heavily in carbon capture technologies, and pioneer new methods of synthetic agriculture. We must ensure that the Global South has the abundant, cheap, clean energy it needs to build hospitals, universities, and modern infrastructure. We do not need to shrink our footprint by retreating into artisanal enclaves; we need to use technology to master our footprint, creating a world where humanity can flourish and expand without despoiling the natural world.

Soper’s alternative hedonism is a luxury belief, a philosophy perfectly tailored for affluent intellectuals who already possess all the material comforts of modernity and can afford to view them with detached disdain. For the rest of the world, the promise of progress remains intrinsically linked to technological advancement and material growth. If we want to save the planet, we must build a world of clean abundance, not romanticize a return to the miseries of the past.
