"Christians should be willing to abandon the label 'evangelical' if it has become a partisan obstacle that obscures the gospel and misrepresents the faith's core mission."

Perhaps the most evangelical thing to do now is to stop defending the word “evangelical.”

Labels are tools. They help people communicate. When they no longer do that, they become obstacles. In much of American public life, “evangelical” now communicates a set of assumptions before any believer can speak: anti-gay, anti-science, Republican, white, angry, nostalgic, judgmental. Some of those assumptions are unfair. Some have been earned. Either way, if the purpose of Christian witness is to point people toward Jesus, a label that immediately points them toward a partisan stereotype may not be worth saving.

This does not mean abandoning evangelical convictions. A Christian can believe in the authority of Scripture, the need for conversion, the centrality of Christ’s death and resurrection and the duty to share the faith without insisting on the institutional brand. The early Christians were not called evangelicals. The Protestant Reformers were not evangelicals in the modern American sense. Many believers around the world who live out evangelical faith would not recognize the American political baggage attached to the word.

The Trump vote makes the problem visible because it highlights the gap between profession and formation. If evangelical Christianity has spent decades teaching personal morality, family values and biblical truth, yet many of its adherents are drawn to a candidate celebrated for swagger, insult and domination, then something has gone wrong in discipleship. The issue is not simply one election. It is the thinning of Christian identity into cultural resentment.

Younger Christians already sense this. Many remain orthodox in belief but are weary of being treated as foot soldiers in the culture war. They care about abortion and religious liberty, but also about racial justice, refugees, poverty, mass incarceration and the tone of Christian public life. They do not want a faith that baptizes whatever the conservative movement happens to need this cycle.

So what does it mean to be evangelical today? For some, it means reclaiming the old word. For others, it may mean letting it die in order to preserve the thing it was supposed to name. Russell Moore’s “gospel Christian” may sound awkward, but the instinct is right: identity should begin with good news, not voting behavior.

The church’s task is not brand management. It is faithfulness. If “evangelical” helps that task, use it. If it obscures Christ behind a red-white-and-blue fog, lay it down.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02Core theological convictions can be maintained and practiced without reliance on the specific institutional brand of evangelicalism.
  3. 03The widespread support for Donald Trump among evangelicals reveals a failure in spiritual discipleship where cultural resentment has replaced biblical values.
  4. 04Younger Christians are increasingly alienated by a version of the faith that functions primarily as a tool for conservative political movements.
  5. 05The primary responsibility of the church is faithfulness to the gospel rather than the preservation of a specific sociopolitical identity or brand.

"The term 'evangelical' has been so thoroughly corrupted by partisan politics and the pursuit of temporal power that faithful Christians should abandon the label in favor of a gospel-centered identity."

When Russell Moore, a leader within the nation’s largest Protestant denomination, says the evangelical label “is at the moment subverting the gospel of Jesus Christ,” he is not engaging in hyperbole. He is issuing a long-overdue admission: the term has been hollowed out by decades of political capture and now serves primarily as an identity marker for a particular kind of white, conservative, partisan voter. To be an evangelical today, in the public imagination and, tragically, in the voting booth, means to be a member of a bloc that can be mobilized by appeals to nostalgia, fear, and power, not by the scandal of the cross.

This is not merely about Donald Trump. The rot set in earlier, when “evangelical” became synonymous with the moral majority’s partisan checklist, reducing the gospel’s wide-angle vision for human flourishing to a narrow set of culture-war litmus tests. The result is a movement so absorbed by temporal power that it can wave away the boastful, thrice-married casino magnate as “baby Christian” or useful strongman, while younger believers and outsiders see only hypocrisy. Moore’s preferred alternative, “gospel Christian,” is not an evasion; it is a theological declaration. It signals that the center of faith is not an electoral map but the announcement that Jesus is Lord—an announcement that judges all earthly kingdoms, including America’s. To be a gospel Christian today means to reclaim the weirdness of mercy, enemy-love, and a crucified Messiah, even when that puts one at odds with the political tribe.

Will abandoning the name solve everything? Probably not. Generations of cultural machinery won’t vanish overnight. But words matter. When a label has been so thoroughly co-opted that it obscures rather than clarifies the message, faithful stewardship may call for laying it down. The early disciples were first called Christians at Antioch; they didn’t cling to “follower of the Way” when a better term served the mission. If the American “evangelical” brand now distorts the good news, the courageous act is to leave it behind, and let a new, unsettling, gospel-centered witness begin.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The evangelical label has shifted from a theological descriptor to a cultural and political identity marker for white, conservative voters.
  2. 02The movement’s focus on culture-war litmus tests has led to a hypocrisy that prioritizes political utility over moral consistency.
  3. 03Adopting the term 'gospel Christian' re-centers the faith on the lordship of Jesus rather than on electoral maps and nationalistic nostalgia.
  4. 04Relinquishing a co-opted brand is a necessary act of stewardship to ensure that the core message of Christianity is not distorted by political tribalism.

"The term "evangelical" has become so politically corrupted and disconnected from its spiritual roots that believers should consider abandoning the label to recover the true essence of the Christian faith."

As someone who identifies with the evangelical tradition, I find Russell Moore's request to be called a "gospel Christian" almost painfully understandable. The word "evangelical" has been seized, bent, and in some cases deliberately weaponized by a political movement that cares far more about political power than about the Christ we claim to follow. When Moore says the label is "subverting the gospel," he is diagnosing a real spiritual emergency.

Think about what the term has come to signify in public discourse. For millions of Americans — especially younger Americans, minorities, and outsiders — "evangelical" now conjures images of political rallies, opposition to same-sex marriage, climate denial, and a cozy alliance with wealth and power. That is not a complete picture of the tradition I love, which has produced remarkable missionaries, theologians, abolitionists, and civil rights advocates. But it is the picture that dominates, and it is one that the church itself has allowed to calcify.

Moore is not running from the faith; he is running toward it. By asking to be called a "gospel Christian," he is doing what the earliest followers of Jesus did when they refused to be defined by the political and religious categories of their day. They called themselves "followers of the Way." The name pointed not to an ideology but to a person.

If shedding a corrupted label helps us recover the heart of the faith — the scandal of grace, the dignity of every human being, the call to repentance and justice — then perhaps it is time to let the word go and find language that actually speaks.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02Public perception of evangelicalism is now dominated by partisan ideologies and exclusionary social stances rather than the tradition's history of justice and theology.
  3. 03Adopting new terminology like "gospel Christian" mirrors the early church's practice of defining themselves by their devotion to Christ rather than existing political categories.
  4. 04Shedding a compromised identity allows the church to refocus on core values such as grace, human dignity, and social justice.

"The term 'evangelical' has become so inextricably linked to white conservative Republican politics that it no longer functions as a theological descriptor and should be retired by believers."

“Evangelical” now means, in ordinary American public speech, a white conservative Protestant who votes Republican. Theologians may object that this is a vulgarized definition. They are right historically, but language is not governed by footnotes. If reporters, pollsters, candidates and millions of citizens use the word primarily as a political identifier, then that is what the word has become in the public square.

Donald Trump did not create this problem. He revealed it. For decades, evangelical identity in national politics has been tied to abortion, marriage, school prayer, judges, Israel and opposition to secular liberalism. Some of those concerns are serious and defensible. But the political packaging has trained Americans to hear “evangelical” less as a confession of faith than as a cultural signal: white, Southern or suburban, Republican, suspicious of elites, angry about cultural displacement.

That is why Trump’s evangelical support is so jarring to many pastors. His public persona does not resemble the piety evangelicals have traditionally praised: humility, fidelity, biblical literacy, self-control, care in speech. But if evangelicalism has been functioning as an identity of cultural resistance rather than a community of theological discipline, his appeal is not mysterious. He promises to fight. For many voters, that is enough.

The honest response is to admit that the word may be beyond repair, at least for now. Christians who want to center their lives on Scripture, conversion, the cross and mission can still do so. But they should not expect the public to hear all of that when they say “evangelical.” They may need to describe themselves more plainly: Christian, Protestant, Baptist, Pentecostal, Anglican, “gospel Christian,” or simply members of a local church.

There is no shame in retiring a word when it obscures more than it clarifies. The danger is not that Christians will lose a brand. The danger is that they will cling to the brand because it still delivers political influence. If “evangelical” has become a demographic category before it is a spiritual one, then the most evangelical act may be to let it go.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Public language is defined by common usage rather than theological footnotes, and the public now views 'evangelical' primarily as a political and demographic identifier.
  2. 02For decades, the movement has prioritized cultural and political grievances over spiritual discipline, making Donald Trump’s combative persona a natural fit for the brand.
  3. 03The disconnect between traditional Christian piety and current evangelical political behavior suggests the movement functions more as an identity of cultural resistance than a faith community.
  4. 04Retiring the label allows Christians to focus on their core spiritual mission without the baggage of a term that obscures their actual beliefs.
  5. 05Clinging to the 'evangelical' brand is dangerous because it suggests a preference for political influence over spiritual integrity.
