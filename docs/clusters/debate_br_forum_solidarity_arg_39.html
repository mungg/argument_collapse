Diversified Gemini

"Mie Inouye’s vision of solidarity is overly conservative and disciplinary because it prioritizes formal organizational endurance and bureaucratic meetings over the transformative power of direct action and mutual aid."

Mie Inouye’s "Solidarity Now" presents a vision of left organizing that is deeply conservative in its methods, if not its goals. By valorizing the act of "showing up to meetings" and learning to endure the presence of people we dislike, Inouye inadvertently reproduces the logic of the institutions she claims to oppose. From an anarchist perspective, this focus on formal organization, meetings, and the painful endurance of ideological friction misses the true locus of radical solidarity: mutual aid and direct action.

Inouye’s reliance on the Mormon Church as a template for left endurance is telling. The church is a rigid, hierarchical institution designed to produce conformity and assimilation. Inouye admits that the church facilitated her family's assimilation into "Caucasian democracy" and American capitalism. Why, then, should the left emulate its methods? Demanding that people subject themselves to the misery of bad meetings and toxic interpersonal conflict in the name of "transformation" sounds less like liberation and more like discipline.

The assumption underlying Inouye's argument is that solidarity must be mediated by formal organizations—that to be politically active, one must join a group, sit in a circle, and argue over strategy. But solidarity is not something that happens *in* a meeting; it is something that happens *in the world*.

When people come together to block an eviction, distribute food, or defend a forest from logging, they do not need to spend months cultivating "epistemic humility" or learning to endure each other's bad takes. The action itself organizes the collective. In prefigurative politics, we build the world we want to see right now, through direct relationships of care and resistance.

Inouye writes about the summer of 2020, noting that people were in the streets because they needed to be together. She laments that this did not translate into a sustained movement because "forging those connections explicitly would have involved conflict, risk, and time." But the failure of 2020 was not a lack of "social endurance" in meetings. The failure occurred when the organic, disruptive energy of the streets was channeled back into the stagnant, NGO-dominated meeting rooms of the institutional left.

We do not need to train people to endure the boredom and alienation of bureaucratic organizing. We need to create avenues for direct action where solidarity is experienced viscerally, through shared risk and shared joy. If a meeting is making you miserable, you shouldn't endure it in the hopes of personal transformation; you should leave and go do something useful. Liberation should not feel like going to church.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Mormon Church is a poor model for left organizing because its methods are rooted in hierarchy, conformity, and assimilation rather than liberation.
  2. 02Solidarity is effectively forged through the shared experience of direct action in the world rather than through mediated ideological friction within formal organizations.
  3. 03The failure of the 2020 uprisings resulted from channeling organic street energy into stagnant institutional spaces rather than a lack of social endurance among participants.
  4. 04Demanding that individuals endure alienating or miserable meetings in the name of personal transformation reproduces institutional discipline rather than fostering radical change.
  5. 05Prefigurative politics and direct relationships of care provide a more immediate and joyful basis for solidarity than the 'epistemic humility' required by bureaucratic organizing.
