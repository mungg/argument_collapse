"To effectively regulate high-frequency trading and protect investors, regulators must reform the fragmented market structure and structural advantages created by Regulation NMS."

To understand the rise of high-frequency trading and the ensuing chaos of events like Knight Capital, we have to look in the mirror. HFT is not an alien force that invaded the stock market; it is the direct, predictable result of the SEC’s own rulemaking, specifically Regulation NMS (National Market System), implemented in 2007.

Reg NMS was designed to modernize the market and ensure that investors always received the best available price across any exchange. It mandated that trade orders be routed to whichever exchange displayed the best national best bid and offer (NBBO). While the intention was noble, the execution fragmented the stock market into dozens of competing electronic exchanges and private "dark pools."

Because prices are now spread across so many venues, the market became a race. High-frequency trading firms realized that by building faster networks—shaving milliseconds off the time it takes to travel between the data centers in New Jersey and Chicago—they could detect an order on one exchange and race ahead to buy and raise the price on another exchange before the rest of the original order arrived. This is latency arbitrage, and it is a tax on institutional investors, pension funds, and mutual funds.

The average investor is absolutely being harmed, not because HFT steals from their E-Trade account directly, but because the mutual funds holding their 401(k)s are getting nickel-and-dimed on large block trades.

Regulators cannot fix this by simply telling firms to slow down. They must address the fragmented market structure that Reg NMS created. One step is to eliminate the "maker-taker" pricing model, where exchanges pay rebates to HFT firms for providing quotes. This system creates a massive conflict of interest, where brokers route orders not to the exchange with the best execution quality, but to the exchange that pays the highest rebate. Furthermore, we need to reform the way market data is distributed. Currently, exchanges sell proprietary, ultra-fast data feeds to HFT firms, while the public consolidated tape lags behind.

Leveling the playing field requires eliminating the structural advantages that regulators themselves built into the system.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Regulation NMS fragmented the market into numerous electronic exchanges, enabling high-frequency traders to profit from latency arbitrage at the expense of institutional investors.
  2. 02The current market structure harms average investors by increasing the costs for the mutual funds and pension funds that manage their retirement savings.
  3. 03The 'maker-taker' pricing model should be eliminated because it creates conflicts of interest by incentivizing brokers to prioritize exchange rebates over execution quality.
  4. 04Regulators must reform the distribution of market data to prevent high-frequency trading firms from gaining an unfair advantage through proprietary, ultra-fast data feeds that outpace the public consolidated tape.
