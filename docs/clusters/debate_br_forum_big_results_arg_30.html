"While behavioral economics and randomized controlled trials effectively address the demand side of development, they fail to solve the more critical and intractable crisis of inadequate service delivery on the supply side."

In "Small Changes, Big Results," Rachel Glennerster and Michael Kremer offer a compelling defense of the randomized controlled trial (RCT) and behavioral economics as tools for international development. Their essay paints a picture of a discipline finally coming to grips with the messy reality of human behavior, moving away from grand theories and rigid assumptions of perfect rationality. While their argument is persuasive regarding the consumer behavior of the poor, it suffers from a fatal blind spot. By focusing almost exclusively on the demand side of development—how to get parents to send kids to school or mothers to use clinics—the behavioral approach neglects the far more intractable crisis on the supply side. Generating demand for broken services is not a recipe for development; it is a recipe for frustration.

The examples marshaled by Glennerster and Kremer are, on their face, impressive. They show that conditional cash transfers in Mexico increased girls' enrollment in secondary school by nearly 15 percentage points. They show that providing a free school uniform in Kenya increased attendance significantly. They highlight how offering a kilogram of lentils increased full immunization rates in rural Rajasthan from 5 percent to 38 percent. In every one of these cases, the intervention is designed to alter the decision-making calculus of the beneficiary. The implicit assumption is that the primary bottleneck to human development is the reluctance or inability of the poor to utilize available services.

But what if the services themselves are fundamentally inadequate? In much of the developing world, the supply side of public goods is in a state of profound crisis. Consider the education sector in India or Kenya. Numerous studies—many conducted by the very same network of researchers championing RCTs—have documented staggering rates of teacher absenteeism. Even when teachers are present, they are often disengaged, under-trained, or teaching curricula that are vastly disconnected from the students' actual learning levels. If a researcher successfully uses a behavioral nudge or a free uniform to convince a poor family to send their child to a school where the teacher does not show up, or where no actual learning takes place, can we truly call this a "big result"? We have successfully manipulated the demand side, but the supply side has failed to deliver the expected human capital returns.

The same dynamic plagues the health sector. The authors celebrate the lentil incentive for immunization. But this intervention presupposes the existence of a functioning clinic with a reliable cold chain for vaccines and a nurse present to administer them. In many rural areas across sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia, clinics routinely face stock-outs of essential medicines. Health workers, who are often underpaid or paid late by dysfunctional government ministries, frequently absent themselves or charge informal, illicit fees to supplement their income. A behavioral nudge that drives a mother to walk three miles to a clinic, only to find the clinic locked or the vaccines spoiled, does not just fail to improve health; it destroys trust in the public health system, making future interventions even more difficult.

Glennerster and Kremer are not entirely oblivious to this problem. Toward the end of their essay, they briefly acknowledge that we "don't know the impact of fees on the supply side" and note that testing the impact of policies on health and education workers is a "key challenge." But this passing acknowledgment deeply understates the magnitude of the problem. The supply side is not merely an additional variable to be tested; it is the foundational infrastructure upon which all demand-side interventions rest.

Why, then, has the new wave of development economics focused so heavily on consumer behavior rather than state capacity and service delivery? The answer lies in the methodology itself. Randomized controlled trials are a spectacular tool for measuring discrete, individual-level interventions. It is very easy, relatively speaking, to randomize who gets a bag of lentils or a free uniform, and then measure the immediate behavioral response. It is extraordinarily difficult, if not impossible, to randomize civil service reform, political accountability, or the restructuring of a national health ministry. Because the RCT methodology demands a specific type of rigorous, isolatable intervention, it naturally biases the entire field of development economics toward small, demand-side tweaks. We end up studying what is measurable, rather than what is most important.

Improving the supply side of development is fundamentally a political and institutional challenge. It requires tackling entrenched corruption, reforming public sector unions, building administrative capacity, and fostering democratic accountability so that citizens can demand better services from their leaders. These are massive, messy, long-term endeavors. They do not yield clean data sets in eighteen months. They cannot be easily packaged into a TED talk or a neat academic paper demonstrating a precise percentage-point increase in an outcome variable.

If development economics continues to follow the path laid out by the behavioral revolution, we risk creating a perverse situation. We will possess an incredibly sophisticated, scientifically rigorous understanding of how to motivate the poor to seek out services, while remaining entirely powerless to ensure those services are worth seeking out. We will have mastered the art of getting the rural poor to line up for empty promises. True development requires both a willing populace and a capable state. Until the experimental approach can figure out how to rigorously reform the supply side, its "big results" will remain dangerously incomplete.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Nudging the poor to utilize services like education and healthcare is futile if those services are characterized by high absenteeism, poor training, and lack of essential resources.
  2. 02Incentivizing demand for broken systems can be counterproductive by destroying public trust when beneficiaries encounter locked clinics or empty classrooms.
  3. 03The methodology of randomized controlled trials inherently biases development economics toward small, measurable consumer tweaks because institutional reforms are too complex to isolate and randomize.
  4. 04True development is a political and institutional challenge that requires building state capacity and accountability, which cannot be achieved through behavioral nudges alone.
  5. 05Focusing on demand-side interventions risks creating a sophisticated system for motivating the poor to seek out services that the state is ultimately powerless to provide.
