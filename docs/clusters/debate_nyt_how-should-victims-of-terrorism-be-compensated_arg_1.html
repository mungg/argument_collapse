Diversified GPT

"The U.S. government should not provide special federal compensation for victims of terrorism, but should instead rely on private charity, civil litigation, and a strengthened general social safety net."

The impulse to compensate victims of terrorism with federal money is humane, but it is also dangerous. It creates a hierarchy of suffering that government cannot justify and cannot administer fairly.

Consider the moral comparison. A runner who loses a leg in Boston is plainly deserving of help. So is a child paralyzed by a stray bullet in Chicago, a woman permanently disabled by a drunk driver, or a family wiped out by a factory explosion. If Washington writes checks to one category because the violence is labeled “terrorism,” it implicitly tells the others that their losses are private misfortunes. That distinction may matter to prosecutors and intelligence agencies; it should matter far less to a compensation system built on human need.

The Sept. 11 fund was created in extraordinary circumstances, partly to stabilize the airline industry by channeling claims away from litigation. It should not become the model for every national trauma. The more we federalize compensation, the more every tragedy becomes a political contest over recognition. Was the shooter ideologically motivated? Were enough people killed? Did the news coverage last long enough? Was the event symbolically national? These are terrible questions to ask grieving families to answer.

Private charity is imperfect, but it has virtues government lacks. It is voluntary, flexible and capable of responding quickly to local circumstances. The One Fund Boston, for example, collected donations from people who felt a direct desire to help. Nonprofits, hospitals, employers and community organizations can tailor support in ways a federal formula may not. Some victims need prosthetics and years of rehabilitation; others need counseling, childcare or help navigating insurance.

Lawsuits should also remain available where negligence contributed to harm. If a security contractor failed, a gun seller broke the law, or a property owner ignored known risks, civil liability can uncover facts and deter future misconduct. Government compensation often comes with pressure to waive claims, as after Sept. 11. That trades accountability for administrative efficiency.

The better federal role is not to create special payouts for headline crimes. It is to strengthen the ordinary safety net: health coverage, disability benefits, mental health treatment, victim assistance grants and emergency response. A nation that truly cares about victims should care about all of them, not only those whose injuries fit the story line of an attack on America.

Compassion should not depend on whether a president gives a speech at the memorial service.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Federal payouts for terrorism create an unjust hierarchy of suffering that prioritizes victims of high-profile attacks over those who suffer similar injuries from everyday violence or accidents.
  2. 02Federalizing compensation turns national tragedies into political contests over which events are sufficiently symbolic or ideologically motivated to deserve funding.
  3. 03Private charity is a superior alternative because it is voluntary, flexible, and better equipped to tailor support to the specific local needs of victims.
  4. 04Maintaining the right to file lawsuits ensures that negligent parties are held accountable and deterred from future misconduct, which federal compensation funds often discourage.
  5. 05The federal government's role should be to improve universal social services like healthcare and disability benefits rather than creating special payouts for headline-grabbing crimes.

"The U.S. government should not provide taxpayer-funded compensation to victims of mass violence, instead relying on the civil court system, private philanthropy, and insurance to provide relief and accountability."

**Let Lawsuits Do Their Job**

Before we reach for the taxpayer's wallet, we should remember that the United States already has a sophisticated system for compensating people who are wrongfully harmed: the civil courts. The tort system, for all its flaws, has real virtues that government compensation funds lack. It investigates fault. It deters negligence. It forces institutions — landlords, security firms, gun sellers, event organizers — to internalize the costs of the risks they create. And it pays for itself.

The 9/11 fund deserves more skepticism than it usually receives precisely because it short-circuited this process. In exchange for compensation, families had to waive their right to sue the airlines, whose lax cockpit-door policies and screening lapses had been criticized for years. We will never know what discovery in those lawsuits might have revealed, or what safety reforms litigation might have driven. Congress decided the airlines were too important to fail and used taxpayer money to make that decision palatable to grieving families. That is a precedent, not a model.

Boston is instructive in the opposite direction. The One Fund Boston distributed roughly $61 million in private donations without preempting anyone's legal claims. Victims can still pursue the bombers' estates (admittedly thin), negligent parties if any are identified, and their own insurers. The charity money fills a gap; it doesn't foreclose accountability.

This is the right division of labor. Private philanthropy provides immediate relief in the days and weeks after a tragedy, when people need money for travel, funerals, and medical co-pays. Insurance — health, life, disability — handles long-term costs, which is what insurance is for. And litigation pursues whoever bears legal responsibility, whether that's a gun manufacturer that marketed irresponsibly or a building owner that ignored security warnings.

Government compensation should be reserved for cases where the state itself bears clear responsibility — soldiers, first responders, victims of failures by federal agencies that had specific intelligence and failed to act. Otherwise, taxpayer-funded compensation is a tempting shortcut that absolves the actually-culpable and inflates the public's sense that Washington can solve any problem by writing a check. It can't, and shouldn't try.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The tort system is superior to government funds because it investigates fault, deters negligence, and forces responsible parties to internalize the costs of risks.
  2. 02Government compensation funds like the 9/11 fund improperly shield negligent entities from legal accountability and prevent the discovery of systemic failures.
  3. 03Private philanthropy and insurance are better suited to handle immediate relief and long-term costs without foreclosing a victim's right to seek justice through litigation.
  4. 04Taxpayer-funded compensation should be strictly limited to cases where the state itself bears direct responsibility for the harm caused.
  5. 05Relying on government checks to solve tragedies creates a dangerous precedent that absolves culpable private actors and inflates the expected role of the federal government.

"The U.S. government should not provide special, taxpayer-funded compensation for victims of terrorism, but should instead rely on existing state crime-victim programs, insurance, and the tort system to ensure all victims of violence are treated equally."

**No Special Treatment for Terror Victims**

There is something deeply uncomfortable about the premise that victims of terrorism deserve a different — and usually far more generous — response than victims of ordinary violence. The 9/11 Victim Compensation Fund paid an average of roughly $2 million per death. The family of a child killed by a stray bullet in Chicago receives nothing remotely comparable. The mother of a soldier killed in Iraq receives a $100,000 death gratuity. Why is the manner of one's death the measure of one's worth?

The honest answer is that it isn't, and we should stop pretending otherwise. The 9/11 fund was not a principled exercise in victim compensation; it was a political bargain to shield the airlines from ruinous litigation. Congress essentially bought a tort waiver from the families. That deal made some sense in the unique aftermath of an event that threatened the solvency of an entire industry, but it set a precedent we cannot afford and should not want.

What we need instead is a level baseline. Every American who is killed or seriously injured by a violent crime — terror or otherwise — should be eligible for the same package of benefits through existing state crime-victim compensation programs, which should be modestly expanded and better publicized. Health insurance and disability insurance should cover the rest, as they would for any other catastrophic injury. Where private actors have been negligent — a marathon organizer that ignored security warnings, a school district that left doors unlocked — let the tort system do its work, with no special carve-outs.

Beyond that, private philanthropy can and does respond when the public is moved. Americans gave tens of millions to Newtown and Boston families. That generosity is admirable, but it should not be confused with a coherent public policy. Letting it remain voluntary acknowledges what it actually is: an expression of solidarity, not a statutory entitlement.

The deeper problem with bespoke terror funds is that they encourage us to rank victims by the political salience of their deaths. That is a moral hierarchy we should be ashamed to maintain. Equal protection of the laws ought to mean equal treatment after the worst happens, too.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Providing more generous compensation to terror victims than to victims of ordinary violence or fallen soldiers creates an unjust moral hierarchy based on the manner of death.
  2. 02The 9/11 Victim Compensation Fund was a unique political bargain designed to protect the airline industry from litigation rather than a principled model for public policy.
  3. 03Existing state crime-victim compensation programs and private insurance should be the primary mechanisms for providing a level baseline of support for all victims of violent crime.
  4. 04The tort system should be allowed to function without special government carve-outs when private actors are negligent in mass violence events.
  5. 05Private philanthropy should remain a voluntary expression of solidarity rather than being codified into a statutory entitlement for specific types of victims.
Diversified Claude

"The United States should rely on private fundraising and civil society rather than federal programs to compensate victims of mass violence, except in cases of extreme catastrophe."

**The Real Lesson of Boston: Trust Civil Society**

Watching how Boston handled the marathon bombing should make us cautious about expanding federal compensation programs. Within hours, neighbors were offering their homes to stranded runners. Within days, a fund had been organized. Within weeks, more than $60 million had been raised and Ken Feinberg had agreed to administer it without taking a fee. By the one-year mark, every dollar had been distributed under a transparent protocol that the public broadly accepted as fair.

This is what a functioning civil society looks like, and it is something American communities are unusually good at. We should think hard before we replace it with a federal program, because government compensation tends to crowd out private generosity. When Washington is on the hook, donors give less. When professional bureaucrats run the show, communities lose the rituals of collective response — the bake sales, the benefit concerts, the GoFundMe pages — that are themselves part of how a wounded community heals.

This doesn't mean the federal role should be zero. There is a legitimate case for a backstop in truly catastrophic cases like 9/11, where the scale exceeds any plausible private response. There is a case for ensuring that no victim of a federally preventable attack goes bankrupt from medical bills, which is mostly an argument about health insurance generally. And there is a strong case for compensating first responders and federal employees harmed in the line of duty.

But for the run of mass-violence events — the shootings and bombings that, terribly, recur every few months — the right model is the one Boston demonstrated. Private fundraising, transparently administered, supplemented by existing state crime-victim programs and the tort system where culpable parties can be found. The government's role is to coordinate, not to write checks.

There is also something to be said for the moral meaning of voluntary giving. A check from the Treasury says: the state owes you this. A check from millions of strangers says: we, your fellow citizens, choose to stand with you. Both are valuable. But in a country where civic ties feel increasingly thin, we should be careful not to nationalize one of the few remaining occasions when Americans reliably show up for each other.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Boston Marathon bombing response demonstrated that private funds can be raised, administered, and distributed quickly and fairly without federal intervention.
  2. 02Federal compensation programs tend to crowd out private generosity and diminish the community rituals that facilitate collective healing.
  3. 03Voluntary giving carries a unique moral significance by signaling the solidarity of fellow citizens rather than a bureaucratic obligation from the state.
  4. 04The government's role should be limited to coordinating efforts, providing a backstop for catastrophic events, and ensuring medical coverage rather than acting as the primary source of funds.
  5. 05Nationalizing victim compensation threatens to weaken the civic ties that are strengthened when Americans choose to support one each other voluntarily.

"The U.S. government should not compensate victims of mass violence, as this responsibility is better handled through the tort system and private charity."

The impulse to have the government write checks after every mass shooting or bombing is understandable but misguided. It nationalizes private grief, turns taxpayers into insurers of last resort, and erodes the very mechanisms meant to deter negligence and distribute responsibility: the tort system and voluntary charity. The government’s proper role is to prevent crime and punish perpetrators, not to compensate victims from the public purse every time horrors occur.

The tort system, for all its flaws, serves a vital function. When a property owner fails to provide adequate security, when a gun manufacturer knowingly markets weapons to dangerous people, or when an airline lets a terrorist slip through screening, lawsuits force accountability. They generate discovery, expose negligence, and drive safer practices. The 9/11 fund, by shielding the airlines from litigation, arguably short‑circuited that accountability. A blanket government fund for mass violence would similarly let potential defendants off the hook—why fix security if Uncle Sam will pick up the tab?

Private charity, meanwhile, is flexible, decentralized, and genuinely voluntary. After the Boston Marathon bombing, One Fund Boston raised over $60 million and distributed it with relative speed and transparency. Yes, there were debates about how to allocate funds, but those debates reflected genuine community deliberation, not a bureaucratic formula imposed from Washington. Charity allows donors to target their support to causes and communities they care about, and it preserves the personal, human dimension of aid—neighbors helping neighbors.

There is also a moral hazard in making the state the perpetual compensator. If every tragedy comes with a guaranteed payout, we risk normalizing mass violence as just another insurable event, and we blunt the urgent question of why these attacks happen in the first place. Compensation should not become a sedative that quiets demands for real prevention. Instead, we should strengthen the ability of victims to sue those responsible and encourage a robust culture of private giving, so that assistance flows from authentic solidarity rather than a government mandate. Let the state focus on policing and security, and let citizens—freely and directly—care for the wounded.

"The U.S. government should not be the primary provider of compensation for victims of mass violence, instead leaving that role to private charities and community-driven funds."

When disaster strikes, the instinctive response of the American people is to open their wallets and their hearts. This civic generosity, unburdened by bureaucratic red tape, is the most effective and compassionate way to support victims of mass violence. The government should resist the urge to step in as the primary compensator, and instead allow private charities and community-driven funds to lead the way.

The success of the One Fund Boston following last year’s marathon bombings serves as a perfect model. Within months, the One Fund raised over $60 million from corporations, foundations, and everyday citizens. Because it was a private, community-based initiative, it operated with remarkable agility. It held town halls, listened directly to the survivors, and distributed payouts swiftly. The administrators did not have to wait for a deeply divided Congress to pass an appropriation bill, nor did they have to navigate the sluggish, inflexible machinery of federal rulemaking.

If we were to make the federal government the default provider of victim compensation, we would inevitably politicize the grieving process. Imagine the legislative battles over which events qualify for federal funds. Does a shooting with four victims count, or only one with ten? Must the perpetrator be motivated by foreign ideology, or does domestic mental illness qualify? Every tragedy would become a subject of partisan debate, with victims forced to lobby Washington for their rightful share.

Furthermore, relying on taxpayer dollars creates a chilling effect on private giving. If Americans believe the federal government is automatically cutting checks to victims, the immense outpouring of grassroots charity we see after tragedies will dry up. This would rob communities of the healing that comes from collective, voluntary action. The profound emotional power of the One Fund Boston was that it represented the voluntary solidarity of millions. Government mandates cannot replicate that empathy. We should trust our communities, our nonprofits, and the enduring generosity of the American public to care for those affected by mass violence.

"Victims of mass violence should be compensated through civil litigation and private donations rather than taxpayer-funded government programs."

Let victims sue. That's the most honest answer, even if it's an uncomfortable one. American law has long trusted that civil litigation—when properly functioning—can both compensate the injured and deter future wrongdoing. If airlines cut corners on security, if corporations facilitate terrorist financing, if security contractors provide inadequate services, then those entities should face accountability in court.

The 9/11 VCF was a mistake dressed as compassion. By creating a taxpayer-funded compensation scheme, Congress accomplished two things: it shielded the airline industry from meaningful liability, and it established a dangerous precedent that government will simply write checks whenever tragedy strikes. Where's the incentive for private actors to improve security if losses get socialized? The VCF transferred wealth from ordinary citizens to both victims and corporate defendants who avoided proper accountability.

Newtown and Boston showed the private sector responding with genuine generosity. Private donors—many of them ordinary families moved by tragedy—provided substantial support without requiring victims to prove negligence or navigate complex legal standards. This charitable impulse deserves encouragement, not replacement by bureaucratic formulas.

What we truly need is tort law reform that allows victims to pursue legitimate claims efficiently while filtering frivolous lawsuits. caps on noneconomic damages, reasonable statutes of limitations, and specialized courts could balance access to justice with avoiding strike-suit abuse. But the fundamental principle should remain: those who cause harm should pay for it, not taxpayers who had no role in the wrongdoing.

The federal government should stay out of the compensation business entirely. I recognize this puts me outside the bipartisan consensus that emerged after 9/11, but I believe that consensus was wrong—and we've been living with its consequences ever since.

When government promises to compensate victims of terrorism, it makes political promises it cannot keep. The 9/11 VCF ran out of money within a decade; beneficiaries who expected lifetime security found themselves facing benefit cuts. Post-Newtown, Connecticut legislators made emotional promises that took years to fulfill. Each tragedy generates momentum for promises that erode once public attention moves elsewhere.

Private charity, while imperfect, responds to genuine human solidarity rather than political calculation. People give because they're moved to help, not because they're compelled to fund government obligations. The Boston One Fund demonstrated both the strengths and limitations of this approach—swift organization, substantial resources, but inconsistent distribution.

Suing perpetrators and their enablers remains valuable not just for compensation but for accountability. Every successful lawsuit against a negligent security company or complicit financial institution creates precedent that incentivizes future vigilance. Government compensation removes this deterrent effect.

I'm not arguing that victims should suffer without help. I'm arguing that help should come from organized compassion—churches, community groups, mutual aid societies—rather than government programs that inevitably disappoint. Civil society is stronger when it bears responsibility for its members; we weaken communal bonds every time we delegate caring to the state.

Diversified DeepSeek

"The U.S. government should not compensate victims of mass violence because doing so distorts the role of the state and erodes personal and communal responsibility."

No. The government should not be in the business of compensating victims of mass violence, terrorism included. Doing so distorts the very purpose of government, which is to protect rights, not to redress every misfortune. Once we accept that the state must write a check when citizens are harmed, we concede that the state is the ultimate guarantor of safety—a promise it can never keep, and one that erodes personal and communal responsibility.

Private charity and mutual aid have always been the proper response to tragedy. They are voluntary, reflecting genuine compassion, not state coercion. After the Boston bombing, individuals and businesses gave millions because they wanted to, not because a bureaucrat demanded it. That generosity builds social bonds far more effectively than a line item in the federal budget. For those who fall through the cracks, family, churches, and local nonprofits step in. That patchwork may seem messy, but it is human. A government fund, by contrast, turns victims into claimants and charity into an entitlement, breeding dependency and resentment.

Lawsuits are the proper channel for redress when negligence occurs. If a venue fails to secure its perimeter or a gun store sells to a known felon, victims can and should seek damages in court. That process is adversarial because it must be: it determines fault and forces the guilty to pay. Removing that mechanism with a no‑fault government payout eliminates accountability. Airlines after 9/11 famously avoided litigation; that precedent should worry anyone who believes that institutions must answer for their failures.

The deeper problem is philosophical. A government that compensates victims of terrorism will soon be asked to compensate victims of school shootings, then victims of hurricanes, then victims of industrial accidents. Once the principle is established that suffering equals a claim on the public treasury, there is no logical stopping point. Better to hold the line: government prevents and punishes violence; private citizens and communities heal its wounds. That keeps liberty intact and compassion voluntary, which is where both belong.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Government compensation turns victims into claimants and charity into an entitlement, which breeds dependency and resentment.
  2. 02Private charity and mutual aid are superior because they are voluntary expressions of compassion that build stronger social bonds than state-mandated payouts.

  4. 04Establishing a precedent for government compensation creates a slippery slope where any form of suffering could be seen as a valid claim on the public treasury.
  5. 05The proper role of government is to protect rights and punish violence, while the healing of wounds should be left to private citizens and communities.
