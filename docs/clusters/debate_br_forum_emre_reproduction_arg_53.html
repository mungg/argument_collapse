"Merve Emre’s focus on the emancipatory potential of assisted reproductive technology is a dangerous political distraction from the immediate, existential crisis of basic bodily autonomy and reproductive rights in America."

Reading Merve Emre’s “All Reproduction Is Assisted” in August 2018 is a dizzying experience. As we live through an era marked by the rollback of reproductive rights—with a hostile administration actively working to restrict access to abortion and contraception, and Brett Kavanaugh’s looming confirmation to the Supreme Court threatening *Roe v. Wade*—Emre asks us to look in the opposite direction. She asks us to focus on the cutting edge of assisted reproductive technology (ART) and the intense, medicalized efforts people undertake to *create* life. While her essay is beautifully written and deeply empathetic to the individuals navigating the fertility industry, its fundamental premise rests on a dangerous political miscalculation. By centering the expensive, boutique struggles of IVF and egg freezing, Emre inadvertently distracts from the immediate, existential crisis facing bodily autonomy in America today.

Emre explicitly positions her argument within the framework of reproductive justice, citing Dorothy Roberts’s crucial mandate that women have the right *to* have children, not just the right *not* to. This is undeniably true in theory. Historically, women of color, disabled women, and poor women have been subjected to forced sterilization and state violence to prevent them from reproducing. However, translating this historical mandate into a defense of the modern, for-profit fertility industry is a staggering leap.

The characters in Emre’s essay—S, with her $20,000 corporate egg-freezing perk; B, a university lecturer crowdfunding her IVF; N and K, navigating a sperm bank; Spataro and cárdenas—are dealing with profound emotional and physical challenges. But they are engaging with a highly privileged sector of the healthcare system. IVF costs upwards of $15,000 to $20,000 per cycle. It is a luxury good. Meanwhile, across the country, maternal mortality rates are rising, rural maternity wards are shutting down, and millions of women cannot access basic prenatal care, let alone birth control or abortion services.

Emre chides feminists for not doing a "good enough job articulating what alternate strategies of reproduction may be," and suggests that making assisted reproduction the "preferred strategy" could build solidarity. This is a techno-utopian fantasy completely disconnected from the material reality of 2018. How can we rally around ART as a unifying feminist strategy when the basic right to govern one’s own body is actively being dismantled?

When Emre mocks the "natural" childbirth movement as a Goop-ified lifestyle choice for white women in the Global North, she is making an easy, localized critique. Yes, organic prenatal vitamins and $500 hypnosis tutorials are absurd. But the core feminist fight for "natural" or demedicalized birth was never about consumerism; it was about preventing unnecessary surgical interventions and securing informed consent for laboring mothers. In a country where Black women are routinely ignored by doctors and face catastrophic complication rates, the fight for bodily autonomy during childbirth is a matter of life and death, not a mere aesthetic preference.

Furthermore, Emre’s invocation of Shulamith Firestone’s artificial womb feels incredibly out of touch with the current political threat. In 2018, the right wing doesn't need artificial wombs to control women; they are simply forcing women to remain pregnant against their will. The dystopian reality we face is not the Huxleyan nightmare of ectogenesis, but the Atwoodian reality of forced birth. To pivot the feminist conversation toward the "emancipatory promise" of high-tech egg freezing while the fundamental right to abortion is on the chopping block is a profound strategic error.

If we want to build the cross-class, cross-race solidarity Emre desires, we cannot do it by centering a multi-billion-dollar fertility industry that inherently caters to those with capital. We must build it around the absolute, non-negotiable right to bodily autonomy. This means fighting for abortion access, fighting for maternal healthcare that doesn't kill Black women, fighting against the separation of immigrant families at the border (a true reproductive justice crisis happening right now), and fighting for universal healthcare.

"All reproduction is assisted," Emre concludes. Indeed it is. But the assistance most urgently needed by the vast majority of women in this country is not a hormone injection or a transvaginal ultrasound in a San Francisco fertility clinic. It is the assistance of a society that guarantees them the right to survive their pregnancies, the right to end them if they choose, and the right to raise their children free from poverty and state violence. Until those basic rights are secured, focusing on the utopian possibilities of ART is a distraction we cannot afford.
