"The United States should pursue a disciplined, businesslike engagement with Russia that recognizes its enduring status as a great power while maintaining clear boundaries on human rights and sovereignty."

Americans should resist the temptation to interpret Putin’s return only as a moral drama about democracy versus authoritarianism. The election’s flaws matter, and the protesters deserve attention. But U.S.-Russian relations will not be stabilized by pretending Russia is merely a misbehaving medium-sized country whose interests can be ignored until it becomes more liberal. Russia remains a great power in the realist sense: it has territory, nuclear arms, resources, scientific capacity, a permanent Security Council seat and influence across a vast neighborhood.

Much of what irritates Washington about Moscow is precisely evidence of power. Russia can affect the Iranian nuclear issue. It can shape outcomes in Syria. It can complicate NATO plans for missile defense. It can make life easier or harder for American logistics into Afghanistan. It has relationships with India, China, the Arab world and former Soviet republics that do not depend on American approval. That is not the profile of a superpower, but it is not negligible.

The United States should also recognize that some Russian suspicions are not invented from thin air. NATO enlargement, the Kosovo war, the Iraq war, missile defense plans, and the Western interpretation of the Libya resolution have all contributed to a Russian belief that Washington uses legal language when convenient and power when possible. One need not accept the Kremlin’s propaganda to see why Russian elites worry about encirclement and regime change. Dismissing every objection as paranoia only strengthens hard-liners.

That said, respect for Russian interests cannot mean granting Moscow a veto over its neighbors’ sovereignty. Georgia, Ukraine and other states are not buffer-zone furniture. Nor should Washington be silent about ballot fraud, harassment of nongovernmental groups or the killing and intimidation of journalists. A stable relationship requires candor as well as restraint.

The best course is a durable, businesslike reset, not a sentimental one. Continue implementing New START. Keep channels open on missile defense, while making clear that consultation is not surrender. Bring Russia further into the rules-based economic system as it enters the World Trade Organization, and graduate beyond outdated Cold War trade restrictions. Cooperate on counterterrorism and nonproliferation. Disagree openly on Syria and human rights, but do not turn every disagreement into a test of national prestige.

Should the United States worry more about rising powers? Yes, especially China. But this is not a zero-sum allocation of attention. Russia’s power is different: less dynamic, more defensive, often obstructive, but still capable of shaping the environment in which American policy operates. Putin’s return means Washington will face a proud, prickly and insecure Moscow. The answer is neither alarmism nor neglect, but disciplined engagement grounded in interests and limits.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Russia remains a significant global power due to its nuclear arsenal, natural resources, and strategic influence across Eurasia and the Middle East.
  2. 02Russia possesses the capacity to significantly impact or obstruct key American foreign policy objectives in Iran, Syria, and Afghanistan.
  3. 03Legitimate Russian security concerns regarding NATO expansion and Western interventionism must be acknowledged to avoid empowering Kremlin hard-liners.
  4. 04Respecting Russian interests should not equate to granting Moscow a sphere of influence over the sovereignty of neighboring states like Georgia and Ukraine.
  5. 05A stable bilateral relationship requires a pragmatic approach that prioritizes arms control and economic integration while remaining candid about human rights abuses.
  6. 06While Russia may be less dynamic than rising powers like China, its ability to shape the international environment makes it too important for the United States to neglect.
