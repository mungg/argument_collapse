"While Walter Johnson correctly links the history of racial capitalism to environmental destruction, his framework for justice remains incomplete because it is too human-centered and fails to recognize the natural world as a subject of justice in its own right."

I come to Walter Johnson’s essay as a student of environmental history, and what strikes me most forcefully is not what he says about slavery but what he says about the Earth. In the closing paragraphs, as he enumerates the six virtues of a justice rooted in the history of racial capitalism, Johnson makes a startling claim: “Over and against many recent efforts which assert that a forthright treatment of global environmental history requires the elevation of the categories of the ‘human’ and the ‘Anthropocene’ over and against other historical categories—principally those of race, class, gender, and colonialism—it insists upon the intimate and dialectical relationship between domination and dominion.”

This is a sentence dense enough to require unpacking. Johnson is entering a debate that has been roiling the environmental humanities: the debate over the Anthropocene, the proposed name for a new geological epoch defined by human impacts on the Earth system. The Anthropocene concept has been criticized from the left for leveling a profound moral and political problem. If “humanity” as a whole is responsible for climate change, species extinction, and ocean acidification, then the specific responsibilities of specific human groups—the industrialists of the global North, the colonizers who remade ecosystems around the world, the corporations that have profited from fossil fuels—disappear into a cloud of undifferentiated “human activity.” The CEO of ExxonMobil and a subsistence farmer in Bangladesh are, on this account, equally members of the species that is transforming the planet. This is, to put it mildly, an unhelpful way of thinking about justice.

Johnson is right to resist this flattening. He is right to insist that the history of environmental destruction is inseparable from the history of racial capitalism. The plantation was an environmental event as much as an economic one: it cleared forests, drained wetlands, exhausted soils, and replaced biodiverse ecosystems with monocultures of cotton, sugar, and tobacco. The slave trade was an environmental catastrophe for the regions it depopulated, disrupting agricultural systems and ecological knowledge that had been developed over centuries. The expansion of the cotton kingdom across the American South was, in a very literal sense, a process of terraforming—reshaping the land to suit the needs of a global commodity market. And all of this was done through the labor of enslaved people, whose own relationship to the land was violently disrupted.

But Johnson’s objection to the Anthropocene concept goes deeper than a call for more precise attribution of responsibility. He is arguing for a different way of thinking about the relationship between human beings and the natural world—one that does not rely on a generic category of “the human” at all. This is the force of his claim about the “intimate and dialectical relationship between domination and dominion.” The word “dominion” here carries a theological echo, recalling the biblical injunction that humans should “have dominion” over the Earth. Johnson is suggesting that the impulse to dominate nature is of a piece with the impulse to dominate other humans—and, more specifically, that the history of racial capitalism has been the primary vehicle through which that double domination has been enacted. The plantation, the mine, the factory farm, the oil field: these are sites where the instrumentalization of human beings and the instrumentalization of nature have marched together, each reinforcing the other.

This is a powerful and provocative claim, and I am largely in agreement with it. But I want to push Johnson to take it further than he does, and to grapple with its unsettling implications. If the domination of nature and the domination of humans are dialectically related, then a movement for justice cannot be content with redistributing resources among human beings. It must also transform the relationship between human beings and the rest of the living world. And that means, I think, that Johnson’s account of justice—which is, for all its radicalism, still a human-centered account—is incomplete.

Let me put the point more concretely. Johnson’s sixth virtue, as he describes it, “insists upon the intimate and dialectical relationship between domination and dominion.” But the other five virtues are almost entirely about relations among human beings: the Global South versus the Global North, the extraction and distribution of resources among classes, past patterns of privilege and abjection, gender and reproductive justice, the connection of race and class. The natural world enters the picture only in the sixth virtue, and even there it enters as the object of “instrumentalization”—something that is done to nature, not something that nature does or is in itself. The enslaved person, in Johnson’s account, is a subject of history, a bearer of a tradition, a source of critique. The forest, the river, the soil, the cotton plant are not. They are the backdrop against which the human drama unfolds.

This is not a minor omission. It is a structural feature of the intellectual tradition Johnson is drawing on. The black radical tradition has been, from Du Bois onward, deeply concerned with the material conditions of life under racial capitalism. But it has been less attentive to the nonhuman dimensions of those conditions—to the plants, animals, and ecosystems that were also transformed, often violently, by the processes it describes. When Du Bois writes, in the passage Johnson quotes from *The World and Africa*, about the slaughter of elephants for ivory—“fifty to seventy-five thousand each year were slaughtered in cold, cruel, lingering horror of living death; sending their teeth to adorn civilization on the bowed heads and chained feet of thirty thousand black slaves”—he is doing something remarkable. He is drawing a parallel between the suffering of enslaved Africans and the suffering of nonhuman beings. But Johnson does not comment on this parallel. He reads the passage as an “account of the spatial aspect of racial capitalism,” emphasizing “the intimate, violent proximities and the material and cognitive distances of region, race, and scale.” The elephants, in his reading, are part of the “material culture,” part of the “sensory pleasures” of imperial parlors. But they are not, apparently, subjects of justice in their own right.

I want to suggest that they should be. Not because elephants are the same as humans, or because their suffering matters in exactly the same way. But because a truly adequate account of justice must reckon with the fact that the systems of domination Johnson describes—racial capitalism, imperialism, plantation slavery—have always operated through the medium of the natural world. They have extracted, transformed, and destroyed ecosystems on a planetary scale. They have treated rivers and forests and mineral deposits and animal populations as “resources,” as inputs into a production process whose only purpose is human profit. This is not incidental to the history of racial capitalism; it is constitutive of it. And it is not incidental to the wrongs of slavery; it is part of what made those wrongs possible.

What would it mean to take this seriously? It would mean, at a minimum, expanding the circle of moral concern beyond the human. It would mean recognizing that the justice claims we make on behalf of enslaved people and their descendants are only a subset of the justice claims we should be making on behalf of the countless other beings—animal, vegetable, mineral, ecosystemic—that were also violated by the processes of racial capitalism. It would mean, perhaps, thinking about reparations not just in terms of wealth transfers among human groups but in terms of ecological restoration: the healing of damaged landscapes, the return of stolen lands to indigenous stewardship, the rehabilitation of soils depleted by monoculture, the reclamation of rivers poisoned by industrial runoff. It would mean, in short, taking Johnson’s sixth virtue and elevating it to the position it deserves: not as an afterthought, but as the foundation of a genuinely ecological account of justice.

I am aware that this suggestion will strike many readers as fanciful, even offensive. How dare I compare the suffering of elephants to the suffering of enslaved human beings? But that is not what I am doing. I am not comparing; I am connecting. I am suggesting that the same logic that treated enslaved people as things also treated elephants as things, and that a movement for justice that does not challenge that logic at its root will remain incomplete. The Movement for Black Lives platform that Johnson cites includes a demand for environmental justice, noting that “the extraction and exploitation of natural resources has historically created and continues to create devastating social and health impacts on Black communities.” This is an important start. But it does not go far enough, because it frames the environment as something that affects Black communities rather than as something that has its own standing in a court of justice.

Johnson ends his essay with a call to heed Du Bois’s warning about the cost of missed opportunities. The opportunity we are missing now, I fear, is the opportunity to build a politics that is adequate to the scale of the crisis we face. That crisis is not just racial or economic; it is ecological. It is not just about the distribution of wealth and power among humans; it is about the survival of the web of life on which all humans, along with countless other species, depend. A justice rooted in the history of slavery and racial capitalism can contribute to that politics. But it cannot, on its own, complete it. For that, we need a justice rooted in the Earth.
