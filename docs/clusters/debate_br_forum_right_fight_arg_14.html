"While local police participation in domestic intelligence is necessary for information sharing, relying on federalism as a structural safeguard is overly optimistic because it risks expanding police powers and eroding democratic accountability through secrecy."

Daniel Richman offers a subtle and appealing argument: if domestic intelligence must be built, then local police participation may make it more accountable and more effective. He is right to reject the crude tradeoff between liberty and security. But his confidence in local police as a moderating force is too optimistic. The danger is not only that federal agencies will dragoon unwilling local departments into abusive intelligence work. It is also that local departments, once invited into the national-security world, will acquire new powers, new secrecy, new prestige, and new excuses for practices that communities have struggled for decades to restrain.

The essay rests heavily on a contrast between the local police of the COINTELPRO era and the local police of today. In the late 1960s and early 1970s, local intelligence units gathered files on lawful dissent, civil rights organizations, antiwar activists, and “subversives.” Today, Richman argues, community policing and local electoral accountability have changed the incentives. Police departments now understand that alienating communities undermines crime control. Therefore, they will resist federal tactics that damage local trust.

There is truth in this account, but not enough to bear the weight placed on it. Community policing has indeed changed police rhetoric and, in some places, police practice. Many departments have learned that crime prevention requires relationships. But the record is mixed. The same decades that brought community policing also brought aggressive order-maintenance policing, gang databases, drug-war sweeps, and racial profiling controversies. Many minority communities have experienced “partnership” and saturation policing at the same time. A department can hold meetings with community leaders in the afternoon and conduct humiliating street stops in the evening. The existence of liaison officers and advisory boards does not ensure meaningful accountability.

Nor is local electoral control always protective of vulnerable minorities. Richman notes that mayors, county executives, and prosecutors face voters. But that fact cuts both ways. Local majorities may demand harsh action against unpopular minorities, especially in moments of fear. Arab and Muslim residents, recent immigrants, and noncitizens may lack political power even in cities where they are visible and organized. Some cannot vote. Others fear publicity. Still others are divided by nationality, sect, class, or generation. A mayor who calculates local political costs may conclude that cooperation with federal surveillance carries little downside if the burden falls on a marginal group. Localism does not automatically mean pluralism.

Indeed, American history gives us many examples of local police as engines rather than checks of repression. The federal government has often been the institution—however imperfectly—called upon to curb local abuses. Civil rights enforcement, police misconduct investigations, and constitutional criminal procedure all emerged because local systems could not be trusted to police themselves. To be sure, the federal government has its own ugly record: Palmer Raids, Japanese-American internment, COINTELPRO, surveillance of Martin Luther King Jr., and more recent post-September 11 detentions. But the conclusion should not be that one level of government will save us from the other. The conclusion should be that power at every level requires enforceable limits.

Richman is strongest when he criticizes the use of immigration enforcement as a counterterrorism tool. He rightly observes that making local officers responsible for civil immigration arrests may destroy trust and produce an intelligence loss. But his proposed remedy—negotiation among federal, state, and local officials—does not go far enough. Some lines should be drawn by law. Local police should not be conscripted into routine civil immigration enforcement. Intelligence files should not be opened on people based on religion, ethnicity, protected speech, or association absent a factual predicate tied to criminal activity or a specific threat. Information collected for ordinary policing should not be fed into national databases without standards governing relevance, retention, access, and correction. Residents should have some way to know whether rules exist and whether violations have consequences.

The problem is that domestic intelligence operates in the shadows. Richman acknowledges that many decisions—whom to interview, which meetings to monitor, what communities to question—are not easily controlled by courts or legislatures. That is precisely why structural optimism is dangerous. If the law cannot see the conduct, and the public cannot evaluate it, we should be especially wary of expanding the number of agencies involved. A “network” can diffuse responsibility. When something goes wrong, federal officials can say the locals collected the information; local officials can say the federal task force directed the operation; prosecutors can say intelligence rules are classified; police chiefs can say they cannot comment. Accountability may disappear into the seams.

Joint Terrorism Task Forces illustrate the concern. They may improve operational coordination, but they also blur lines of control. When a local detective is assigned to a federal task force, whose rules govern? The city’s? The FBI’s? If local civilian review boards cannot access relevant information because of federal restrictions, then local participation has weakened rather than strengthened democratic oversight. Residents may believe their police department is accountable to city policy, while in practice officers are operating under federal secrecy. That is not federalism as friction; it is federalism as laundering.

Richman argues that necessity requires participation. A national domestic intelligence network is needed, and local police have knowledge that federal agencies lack. I do not deny the need for information sharing. The federal government must be able to alert local authorities to threats; local police must be able to pass along credible information about potential violence. But the difference between targeted information sharing and generalized intelligence collection is crucial. The former can be justified by specific threats or crimes. The latter risks turning ordinary civic and religious life into an object of state scrutiny.

One can accept the reality of terrorist danger without accepting the premise that every felony arrest is an “intelligence opportunity.” That phrase should give us pause. It suggests a world in which the ordinary criminal justice system becomes a feeder system for national-security databases. Every arrest, every interview, every street encounter may generate information of uncertain reliability, stripped of context, and made available to agencies with missions far removed from the original event. We have already seen, in the drug war, how incentives to gather information can encourage informant abuse, selective enforcement, and unreliable tips. Adding national-security urgency will not make these problems smaller.

What, then, should be done? First, domestic counterterrorism should remain tied to criminal predicates wherever possible. The goal should be preventing violence, not mapping ideology. Investigations should require articulable facts, not demographic hunches. Second, any federal-local intelligence arrangements should be public in their basic structure. Operational details may be secret, but memoranda of understanding, applicable rules, oversight mechanisms, and complaint processes should not be hidden. Third, local officers assigned to federal task forces should remain subject to local rules that are more protective of civil liberties, unless a democratically accountable local body expressly approves a different arrangement. Fourth, Congress should strengthen reporting requirements and inspector-general review for domestic intelligence activities involving First Amendment-protected conduct. Fifth, communities should not merely be “consulted” after policies are designed; they should have representation in the formation of guidelines that affect them.

The federal government’s current approach has already damaged confidence: secret detentions, special registration, aggressive immigration tactics, material-witness detentions, and a Justice Department too often unwilling to admit error. Abu Ghraib, though overseas and military rather than domestic and police-based, has further eroded trust in official assurances that extraordinary powers will be used carefully. In this climate, asking communities to trust an expanding intelligence network because local police will moderate it is not enough.

Richman is right that empty resolutions condemning federal policy do little by themselves. Local governments should do more than posture. But one thing they can do is set enforceable boundaries. They can refuse participation in civil immigration sweeps. They can prohibit intelligence gathering based solely on political or religious activity. They can require public reporting on task-force participation. They can fund community liaison work independent of intelligence collection. They can insist that security be pursued as a public safety mission, not as a license for surveillance.

The “right fight” is not only to integrate local police into federal counterterrorism. It is to prevent the normalization of domestic intelligence as a routine function of local governance. If local police become the eyes and ears of a national-security apparatus, communities may lose the very trust Richman hopes to protect. Effective counterterrorism requires information, but democracy requires zones of life not organized around informing. We should build channels for specific warnings and genuine threats. We should not build a permanent architecture of suspicion and then hope that federalism will civilize it.
