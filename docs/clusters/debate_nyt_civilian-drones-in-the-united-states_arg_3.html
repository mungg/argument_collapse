"The government should establish a middle layer of rights that distinguishes between public navigable airspace and the intimate low-altitude space above private property to protect privacy while allowing for innovation."

The most sensible drone policy begins with a distinction that American law has blurred for too long: public airspace is not the same thing as the intimate space immediately above a home.

If an aircraft is flying at a lawful altitude over a city street, taking images of traffic or storm damage, it should not need every homeowner’s permission. But a small drone hovering twenty feet over a fenced backyard is not meaningfully equivalent to an airplane passing overhead. It is more like a mechanical trespasser. The homeowner cannot ignore it, cannot easily exclude it, and may be exposed in a place long treated as private in ordinary life.

Congress and the FAA should therefore separate navigable airspace from low-altitude intrusion. The FAA’s main concern is safety: keeping aircraft from hitting one another or falling on people. Privacy, however, is largely a state and local concern. States can create a civil action for low-altitude drone intrusion onto private property when the flight substantially interferes with use and enjoyment of land, especially when cameras or recording devices are involved. Cities can regulate takeoff and landing sites, noise, and repeated hovering, so long as they do not create an impossible patchwork for legitimate aviation.

This approach is better than saying either “drones may go anywhere” or “drones may film nowhere.” A utility company inspecting lines after a storm should be able to work quickly. A farmer should be able to fly over his fields. A filmmaker should be able to use a drone over a permitted set. But a gossip site should not be able to park an unmanned camera outside an actress’s bedroom window and claim that she had no privacy because photons escaped the glass.

The law should also require transparency for commercial operators. Registration numbers, operator identification and basic retention policies would make abuse easier to punish without banning beneficial use. If a drone injures someone or invades privacy, the victim should be able to find out who operated it.

The old maxim that one’s property extends indefinitely upward is obsolete; modern aviation could not function under it. But the opposite maxim—that anything visible from the sky is fair game—is equally obsolete in an age of cheap hovering robots. We need a middle layer of rights: enough public airspace for innovation, enough private control to keep the backyard from becoming a stage.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Low-altitude drone flights over private property should be treated as a form of mechanical trespass rather than equivalent to high-altitude aviation.
  2. 02States and cities should have the authority to regulate low-altitude intrusions, noise, and hovering to protect the use and enjoyment of private land.
  3. 03Drone regulations should differentiate between beneficial commercial or utility uses and invasive activities like unauthorized surveillance of private residences.
  4. 04Commercial drone operators should be subject to transparency requirements, such as registration and identification, to ensure accountability for privacy violations or injuries.

"The government should address drone privacy concerns by establishing a clear boundary between federal navigable airspace and state-regulated low-altitude property rights based on traditional concepts of trespass and nuisance."

The privacy debate over drones is often framed as a clash between technology and personal dignity. It may be better framed as a property problem. Americans do not need to pretend that the sky belongs entirely to drone operators, nor that every landowner owns the heavens. We need a workable boundary.

The Supreme Court’s old Causby case, involving low-flying military aircraft over a chicken farm, recognized that land ownership includes at least some control over the immediate reaches of the airspace. That principle is useful here. A passenger jet at 30,000 feet is not trespassing over your house. A quadcopter hovering 20 feet above your patio probably is, whether or not it carries a camera.

This suggests a practical compromise. Federal aviation authorities should regulate navigable airspace for safety: altitude, traffic rules, airports, emergency restrictions and operator competence. States should be allowed to define low-altitude trespass, nuisance and invasion of privacy. The rule should focus less on “filming” in the abstract and more on where the aircraft is and what it is doing. A drone traveling at a lawful height over a subdivision should not be illegal simply because it photographs roofs. A drone lingering over a fenced backyard without permission should expose the operator to damages, injunctions and perhaps criminal penalties if the conduct is voyeuristic.

Such a rule would protect privacy without requiring the impossible. We cannot forbid every aerial image that includes a person, and we should not give neighbors veto power over weather research, journalism or real estate photography. But neither should we accept the claim that a window or a backyard is fair game merely because a machine can be flown near it.

The answer lies in reviving ordinary concepts: trespass, nuisance, consent, and reasonable use. Drones are new; the problem of one person invading another’s quiet enjoyment of property is not. Let them fly where flight is safe and lawful. Let people sue when drones become mechanical peeping toms.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Land ownership should include control over the immediate reaches of airspace above a property, consistent with legal precedents like the Causby case.
  2. 02The federal government should focus on regulating the safety and technical standards of navigable airspace, while states should define and enforce low-altitude trespass and privacy violations.
  3. 03Legal restrictions should focus on the drone's location and behavior, such as lingering over private backyards, rather than banning aerial photography in general.
  4. 04Applying existing legal concepts like trespass and nuisance allows for the protection of personal privacy without stifling beneficial uses of drone technology like journalism or research.
