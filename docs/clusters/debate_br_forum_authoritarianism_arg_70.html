"The proposed shift toward a colorblind anti-corruption strategy is a strategic failure because it ignores the fact that modern American authoritarianism is fundamentally rooted in white nationalism and racialized voter suppression."

There is a glaring omission in Bonica and Grumbach’s analysis of the 2024 Democratic collapse, one that undermines their entire prescription for an "anti-corruption" pivot. In their discussion of turnout gaps, shifting demographics, and the failure of moderation, they completely ignore the central, organizing principle of the modern authoritarian threat: the racialized nature of democratic backsliding.

The authors casually mention the widening turnout gap between Black and white Americans, framing it merely as a strategic problem to be solved with better messaging. But this drop in Black participation was not a passive occurrence resulting from uninspiring "kitchen-table" popularism. It was the direct result of a decade-long, targeted campaign of voter suppression, gerrymandering, and election subversion engineered by the Republican right.

By pivoting the proposed solution to a colorblind "anti-corruption" narrative, Bonica and Grumbach repeat the oldest mistake of the American left: attempting to subsume specific racial oppression under a generalized banner of populist reform. Framing the crisis merely as "elites vs. the people" fundamentally misreads the right-wing appeal. The authoritarianism we face is not simply corrupt; it is explicitly white nationalist. It promises its base not a clean government, but a return to a racial hierarchy.

You cannot defeat white nationalism by merely talking about Clarence Thomas’s vacations or congressional stock portfolios. When Black voters disengage, it is often because they recognize that the Democratic Party is unwilling to directly confront the racial animus of the opposition, preferring instead to seek "broadly resonant grievances." A true anti-authoritarian movement in the United States must be explicitly anti-racist. It must prioritize the protection of voting rights, the dismantling of structural white supremacy, and the defense of marginalized communities over the comfort of cross-partisan, class-based anti-corruption rhetoric. Failing to name the racial core of the authoritarian threat guarantees its eventual triumph.

  1. 01The decline in Black voter turnout was not caused by uninspiring messaging but by a deliberate, decade-long Republican campaign of voter suppression and election subversion.
  2. 02Framing the political crisis as a general struggle of 'elites vs. the people' repeats the historical mistake of subsuming specific racial oppression under broad populist reform.
  3. 03Right-wing authoritarianism appeals to its base by promising a restoration of racial hierarchy rather than just a corrupt alternative to clean government.
  4. 04A successful anti-authoritarian movement must be explicitly anti-racist and prioritize the defense of marginalized communities and voting rights over class-based rhetoric.
  5. 05Black voters disengage when the Democratic Party avoids confronting the racial animus of the opposition in favor of seeking broadly resonant, non-racial grievances.
