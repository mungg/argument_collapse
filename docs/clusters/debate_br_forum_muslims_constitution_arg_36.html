"The responding essay argues that Countering Violent Extremism (CVE) programs, despite their flaws, are essential public health-style interventions that provide necessary alternatives to the harsh law enforcement measures criticized by Akbar and Theoharis."

Amna A. Akbar and Jeanne Theoharis offer a deeply cynical view of the last fifteen years of American counterterrorism policy in “Islam on Trial.” By painting the entire post-9/11 national security apparatus with the broad brush of “Islamophobia,” they fail to grapple with the genuine, evolving security threats the United States faces. Nowhere is this blind spot more apparent than in their dismissal of Countering Violent Extremism (CVE) programs. By characterizing CVE as merely a tool for “shaping the very ideas that Muslim communities discuss” and criminalizing religious practice, the authors throw the baby out with the bathwater, rejecting the very programs designed to offer alternatives to the harsh law enforcement measures they claim to despise.

To understand the necessity of CVE, one must acknowledge the reality of the threat landscape in the winter of 2017. We are not fighting the Al Qaeda of 2001, which relied on complex, multi-year plots hatched in physical training camps. We are confronting the ideological contagion of the Islamic State (ISIS), an organization that has weaponized social media to reach into American bedrooms and radicalize vulnerable youth. ISIS’s propaganda is sophisticated, emotionally manipulative, and specifically tailored to prey on alienated individuals, offering them a twisted sense of purpose, belonging, and religious duty.

When a teenager in Minnesota or Virginia begins consuming this digital poison, withdrawing from their family, and expressing sympathy for jihadist violence, what is the appropriate response? Under a strict law enforcement paradigm, the authorities must wait until that teenager takes a tangible step toward violence—such as attempting to purchase a weapon or buy a plane ticket to Syria—and then arrest them, charge them with material support for terrorism, and lock them in federal prison for decades. This is precisely the “preemptive prosecution” model that Akbar and Theoharis decry.

Countering Violent Extremism was conceived as a public health-style intervention to disrupt this trajectory *before* a crime is committed. The goal of genuine CVE is not to spy on mosques or suppress political dissent regarding U.S. foreign policy. It is to empower parents, teachers, mental health professionals, and local religious leaders to recognize the signs of radicalization and provide targeted interventions. It is about creating non-punitive “off-ramps” for young people who are being groomed by terrorist recruiters.

Are there valid criticisms of how CVE has been implemented? Absolutely. In some jurisdictions, CVE initiatives have been overly driven by law enforcement rather than public health officials, leading to understandable community distrust. The Obama administration struggled to consistently decouple community engagement from intelligence gathering, a blurring of lines that rightly frustrated civil liberties advocates. Furthermore, the Trump administration’s reported desire to rebrand CVE to focus *exclusively* on Islamic extremism, while ignoring the rising and lethal threat of white supremacist and neo-Nazi violence, is a dangerous and bigoted mistake. A successful prevention strategy must be ideologically agnostic, targeting the process of radicalization to violence regardless of the underlying ideology.

However, recognizing the flaws in CVE implementation should lead us to reform and improve the programs, not abolish them. Akbar and Theoharis’s blanket condemnation leaves Muslim communities dangerously isolated. Many Muslim community leaders, parents, and imams desperately want resources to help protect their youth from the predatory recruitment tactics of ISIS. They do not want their children to become martyrs for a death cult in Syria, nor do they want them to end up in federal prison. By stigmatizing CVE as inherently Islamophobic, the authors deny these communities the supportive infrastructure they need, forcing them to navigate a terrifying digital threat landscape entirely on their own.

Furthermore, the authors’ assertion that CVE marks "certain forms of religious practice or critique of U.S. policies as criminally dissident" is a gross exaggeration of how modern prevention programs function. Debating U.S. foreign policy, opposing drone strikes, or practicing conservative forms of Islam are not, and should not be, indicators of violent extremism. Genuine CVE practitioners understand that robust political debate and strong religious identity are actually *protective* factors against radicalization. It is precisely when individuals feel that they have no legitimate outlet for their political grievances that they become vulnerable to those who offer violence as the only solution.

The authors seem to long for a world where national security policy is entirely divorced from the cultural and ideological realities of terrorism. But we cannot combat a movement that explicitly uses religious justification for mass murder without engaging with the ideological battleground. This is not about the government deciding what constitutes "acceptable" Islam. It is about the government partnering with the overwhelming majority of American Muslims who reject violence to protect their communities from a fringe, predatory ideology.

As we face the chaotic and often hostile rhetoric of the Trump administration, it is more important than ever to defend the nuanced, community-oriented approaches developed in recent years. If we abandon CVE, we are left with only the blunt instruments of surveillance, border bans, and long-term incarceration.

To protect both civil liberties and public safety, we must fight for a better CVE—one that is fully decoupled from intelligence gathering, rooted in public health methodologies, transparent in its operations, and applied equally to all forms of violent extremism. But we must not succumb to the false binary presented by Akbar and Theoharis, which suggests that any attempt to understand and prevent radicalization is inherently rooted in prejudice. Ignoring the very real phenomenon of online radicalization does not protect Muslim communities; it abandons them.
