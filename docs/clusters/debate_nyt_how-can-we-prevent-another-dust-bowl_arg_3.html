"Preventing a modern Dust Bowl requires a dual approach of adapting agricultural practices to a warmer climate and mitigating the underlying causes of climate change."

The question should not be whether we can avoid “another Dust Bowl” in the literal 1930s sense. The better question is whether we are willing to confront the new conditions under which droughts now occur. The 2012 drought is unfolding in a warmer climate than the one American agriculture knew a century ago. Heat intensifies evaporation, dries soils faster and makes crops more vulnerable even when rainfall deficits are not historically unprecedented. A warmer drought is a harsher drought.

That does not mean every dry summer is “caused” by climate change. Weather varies. The Plains have always had droughts. But it is also true that adding heat-trapping gases to the atmosphere loads the dice toward more heat extremes. The current disaster should be understood as part of the risk profile scientists have been warning about: more stress on water supplies, more volatile growing conditions, and greater pressure on food systems.

Preventing another Dust Bowl therefore requires two kinds of action. First, adaptation. We need soil practices that hold moisture: cover crops, organic matter, reduced tillage, diverse rotations and perennials where annual row crops are too risky. We need to stop mining aquifers as if groundwater were an annual crop. We need drought planning that reaches beyond emergency declarations and subsidized losses.

Second, mitigation. It is not enough to help farmers endure an increasingly hostile climate while continuing to worsen the problem. Agriculture itself can reduce emissions through better fertilizer management, methane controls, energy efficiency and carbon-storing soils. But the larger task is national: reduce dependence on coal and oil, put a price on carbon, and accelerate the shift to cleaner energy.

Some will say that invoking climate change politicizes a drought. In truth, ignoring climate change politicizes it more. It asks farmers, taxpayers and rural communities to absorb growing risks while pretending those risks have no cause and no remedy.

The original Dust Bowl led to a new ethic of soil stewardship because the nation finally recognized that private land decisions could produce public calamity. The 2012 drought should lead to a broader ethic: climate stewardship. Soil blowing across highways is one visible failure. A warming atmosphere is less visible, but it may prove even harder to escape.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Modern droughts are intensified by a warmer climate, which increases evaporation and puts unprecedented stress on water supplies and food systems.
  2. 02Farmers must adopt adaptation strategies such as improved soil management, reduced tillage, and sustainable aquifer usage to hold moisture in the ground.
  3. 03The agricultural sector and the nation at large must mitigate climate change by reducing greenhouse gas emissions and transitioning to cleaner energy sources.
  4. 04Ignoring the link between climate change and drought unfairly forces rural communities and taxpayers to absorb risks that have identifiable causes and remedies.
  5. 05Just as the original Dust Bowl created an ethic of soil stewardship, the current climate crisis demands a new national ethic of climate stewardship.
Diversified Claude

"Preventing another Dust Bowl requires a dual strategy of adapting agricultural practices to a drier climate and implementing long-term climate mitigation policies to address the underlying driver of drought."

The honest answer to whether we can prevent another Dust Bowl is: not entirely, because the fundamental driver — drought — is beyond our control, and it is getting worse. The climate science has been clear for some time that the interior of the continent is likely to experience more frequent and more intense dry periods as global temperatures rise. The summer of 2012 is not an aberration; it is a preview.

This reframes the question. The Dust Bowl was a compound disaster: a natural drought amplified by terrible land use. We have largely fixed the land-use half of that equation, at least compared to 1930. What we are now doing is reintroducing the drought half at greater intensity, while also undermining some of the gains we made on soil conservation.

Consider where we stand. Conservation Reserve Program acreage has fallen from a peak of about 37 million acres to under 30 million, with more contracts expiring. The ethanol mandate has pushed corn into rotations and onto land that previously stayed in grass or small grains. Groundwater levels in the Ogallala have dropped more than 150 feet in some areas. Average summer temperatures across the Plains are noticeably higher than they were a generation ago, increasing evapotranspiration and the moisture stress on crops even when rainfall is normal.

Prevention, then, requires both adaptation and mitigation. On adaptation: defend and expand CRP, invest in perennial grain research (the work coming out of the Land Institute, for instance), tighten groundwater regulation in states that still treat aquifers as private property to be drained at will, and shift crop insurance subsidies toward producers who maintain cover crops and reduced tillage. On mitigation: take seriously that agriculture is both a victim and a contributor to climate change, and that long-term carbon policy is part of long-term drought policy.

There will be no single black Sunday this time. The next Dust Bowl, if it comes, will look more like a slow grinding-down — declining yields, abandoned irrigation wells, rural depopulation, and dust events that are bad but not biblical. Preventing that requires acknowledging the climate dimension honestly, which our politics has so far refused to do.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Rising global temperatures are making frequent and intense droughts the new normal for the interior of the continent.
  2. 02Current economic and policy pressures, such as the ethanol mandate, are undermining soil conservation gains by pushing marginal land back into intensive cultivation.
  3. 03The depletion of the Ogallala aquifer and increased evapotranspiration from higher temperatures are creating unsustainable moisture stress on crops.
  4. 04Agricultural policy must be reformed to prioritize the Conservation Reserve Program, perennial grain research, and stricter groundwater regulations.
  5. 05Crop insurance subsidies should be restructured to incentivize sustainable practices like cover cropping and reduced tillage.
  6. 06Addressing the climate dimension of drought through carbon policy is essential to preventing the slow economic and environmental decline of rural regions.
Diversified Gemini

"To prevent a 21st-century agricultural collapse worse than the Dust Bowl, we must address the root cause of the current drought crisis by aggressively mitigating climate change and adapting our agricultural practices to a hotter, drier reality."

The devastating drought of 2012 is not a historical anomaly; it is a preview of our climate future. While it is tempting to look backward to the 1930s to understand what is happening to the American heartland, we must instead look forward. The fundamental driver of the current crisis is a rapidly warming planet, and until we acknowledge and act on that reality, no amount of agricultural tinkering will save us.

Climate models have long predicted that as global temperatures rise, the interior of the North American continent will experience more frequent and more intense droughts. The record-shattering heat waves we have seen this July are exacerbating the lack of rainfall, increasing the rate of evaporation, and literally baking the moisture out of the soil.

Preventing a 21st-century agricultural collapse requires a two-pronged approach: mitigation and adaptation. On the mitigation front, we must aggressively transition away from fossil fuels to reduce the greenhouse gas emissions that are supercharging these weather extremes. The longer we delay comprehensive climate legislation, the more severe the baseline conditions for American farmers will become.

On the adaptation front, we have to prepare for a hotter, drier norm. This means shifting our agricultural footprint. We may need to accept that certain regions of the country are no longer suitable for water-intensive crops like corn. We must invest in research to develop farming techniques that can thrive in arid conditions, and we must rethink our massive water infrastructure to prioritize conservation over consumption. If we treat this summer's drought as a freak occurrence rather than a systemic climate warning, we will be walking blindly into an ecological disaster far worse than the Dust Bowl.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current drought is a direct result of a rapidly warming planet that causes more frequent and intense heat waves and soil moisture loss.
  2. 02We must transition away from fossil fuels and pass comprehensive climate legislation to reduce the greenhouse gas emissions that are supercharging weather extremes.
  3. 03Agricultural footprints must be shifted away from water-intensive crops in regions that are no longer climatically suitable for them.
  4. 04Investment in research for arid-condition farming techniques and a restructuring of water infrastructure to prioritize conservation are essential for long-term survival.
  5. 05Treating the current drought as a temporary anomaly rather than a systemic climate warning will lead to an inevitable ecological disaster.
