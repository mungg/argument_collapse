"While mass unemployment is a moral and social disaster, a government-engineered full-employment program based on large-scale public investment and industrial planning is an unsustainable and economically flawed solution."

Robert Pollin’s essay is admirable in its moral seriousness and troubling in its confidence that the federal government can engineer full employment by redirecting large flows of spending. He is surely right that unemployment at today’s levels is a human disaster. He is also right that the recession was made worse by financial excess and that public policy should not be indifferent to whether Americans can find work. But the leap from these premises to a permanent full-employment program centered on public investment, industrial planning, and a large reallocation away from military and fossil-fuel spending is less persuasive.

The first problem is definitional. Pollin says full employment must mean “an abundance of decent jobs,” not merely people scratching out a living. Who could disagree? But once full employment is defined as both enough jobs and jobs of a certain quality, we have moved from a measurable macroeconomic goal to a broad social aspiration. Decent pay, benefits, safe workplaces, fair treatment, and meaningful work are all desirable. Yet policy cannot maximize all of them simultaneously without tradeoffs. Higher mandated wages may reduce demand for some workers. Generous benefits may encourage firms to hire fewer permanent employees. Strict workplace rules may be justified, but they can also raise costs. The hard question is not whether good jobs are better than bad jobs; it is which policies produce more of the former without reducing total opportunity.

Pollin treats the late 1960s and late 1990s as evidence that very low unemployment raises wages and reduces poverty. That is true, but those episodes do not establish that government can sustainably hold unemployment near 4 percent. The late 1960s were inseparable from Vietnam spending and were followed by inflationary pressures that policymakers failed to contain. The late 1990s benefited from a remarkable combination: rapid productivity growth, falling import prices, restrained health-care inflation for a time, welfare reform pushing workers into the labor force, and a technology boom that ended badly. It is possible to learn from these periods without assuming they can be replicated by shifting expenditures.

Pollin’s sectoral comparison also deserves caution. He argues that education and clean energy create more jobs per million dollars than the military or fossil fuels. This is plausible because teaching and retrofitting buildings are labor-intensive. But job counts per dollar are not the same as economic value. By that metric, one could favor any activity that uses many workers and little equipment, regardless of output. Productivity matters. A capital-intensive industry may create fewer jobs directly but produce cheaper energy, better tools, or higher incomes elsewhere. If fossil fuels create only five jobs per million dollars, that is partly because they deliver enormous quantities of energy with relatively little labor. That is not a defect from the standpoint of living standards.

Nor is military spending simply a jobs program. One may favor reducing defense expenditures—especially as the wars in Iraq and Afghanistan wind down—but the correct size of the defense budget should be determined by national security needs, not employment multipliers. If a weapons system is unnecessary, cancel it. If a base is strategically obsolete, close it. But if a military expenditure is necessary, its lower labor intensity is not an argument against it. Likewise, clean energy should be judged by whether it can reduce emissions and provide reliable energy at reasonable cost, not chiefly by how many jobs it creates. A clean-energy project that requires excessive labor to produce expensive power may be a poor climate policy even if it is a good short-run employment policy.

The deficit issue is also too lightly handled. Pollin notes that deficits have reached about 10 percent of GDP and argues, in effect, that more job-generating measures are crucial. In a deep recession, deficits are appropriate; premature austerity can be self-defeating. But the United States entered this crisis with large unfunded entitlement obligations, a tax system insufficient to finance promised benefits, and a political culture that finds it easier to borrow than to prioritize. A credible recovery policy must distinguish between temporary countercyclical deficits and permanent expansion of federal commitments. Pollin proposes a reallocation rather than simply additional spending, which is welcome, but the transition itself would be politically difficult and economically messy. Workers displaced from defense contracting and fossil-fuel industries cannot be instantly transformed into teachers, insulation installers, or wind technicians.

This points to the deeper weakness in the essay: insufficient attention to entrepreneurship, business formation, and the conditions under which private firms hire. Pollin presents business largely as a political obstacle to full employment. Sometimes it is. Employers may prefer slack labor markets because they weaken wage demands. But businesses also create most jobs, and they hire when they see customers, predictable rules, access to credit, and the prospect of profit. A full-employment agenda that treats profit as something to be tolerated rather than encouraged will fail in an economy still organized largely around private enterprise.

Financial regulation is necessary, but regulation must be designed to restore prudent lending, not to turn banks into instruments of political allocation. We need higher capital standards, better resolution authority, transparent derivatives markets, and an end to the expectation that large institutions will be rescued. But if “channeling” credit becomes a euphemism for directing capital to politically favored industries, we risk repeating old mistakes: subsidized failures, lobbying contests, and mispriced risk.

What would a more balanced employment agenda look like? First, continue temporary support for demand while unemployment remains extremely high, especially through payroll-tax relief and aid that prevents state and local layoffs. Second, reform the tax code to reduce penalties on work and investment while broadening the base. Third, expand wage subsidies or an earned-income tax credit for low-wage workers, which encourages employment without pricing marginal workers out of the market. Fourth, invest in infrastructure where benefits clearly exceed costs—ports, bridges, congestion relief, basic research, and energy efficiency—but subject projects to rigorous review rather than treating all public spending as equally virtuous. Fifth, improve education through accountability and competition as well as funding; simply hiring more people will not ensure better learning. Sixth, reduce regulatory uncertainty, especially for small and medium-size firms that lack armies of lawyers.

Finally, monetary policy should remain attentive to unemployment, but not abandon price stability. Inflation is not merely a concern of bond traders. It hurts workers whose wages lag prices, retirees on fixed incomes, and savers. The lesson of the 1970s is not that full employment is impossible, but that ignoring inflation can discredit progressive economic policy for a generation.

Pollin is right to challenge complacency. Mass unemployment is corrosive, and a country with America’s wealth should not accept it passively. But full employment cannot be achieved by moral urgency alone. It requires respect for tradeoffs, fiscal credibility, private investment, and productivity. The goal should be not a permanent mobilization of government to guarantee “decent jobs” as defined from above, but an economy in which private initiative, sound public investment, stable money, and a reliable safety net together make work widely available. That is less sweeping than Pollin’s vision, but it may be more durable.

"Robert Pollin’s proposal for a government-managed full-employment economy is flawed because it relies on centralized planning and increased spending rather than the decentralized private-sector innovation that truly drives prosperity."

Robert Pollin’s essay is a lucid statement of the modern Keynesian case for full employment. It is also an example of why that case remains politically and economically suspect to many Americans. Pollin describes unemployment as a failure of demand, capitalism as dependent on a reserve army of labor, and government as the institution capable of redirecting resources toward socially useful employment. But this framework underestimates the knowledge problem, the incentive problem, and the debt problem that attend large-scale economic management.

No humane person can be indifferent to today’s unemployment. The financial crisis and recession have caused severe hardship. But a crisis should not become an excuse to expand permanently the reach of government. The United States did not become prosperous because public officials selected the correct mix of industries and guaranteed an abundance of “decent jobs.” It became prosperous because entrepreneurs, workers, investors, and consumers made decentralized decisions in a system that rewarded innovation and adaptation. That system is imperfect, sometimes brutally so, but it has generated wealth on a scale no planned employment policy can match.

Pollin’s argument rests partly on the claim that the stimulus prevented a depression but was too small to restore full employment. This is now the standard defense of the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act: if conditions remain bad, the spending was insufficient; if conditions had worsened further, the spending would have been credited with preventing disaster. Such counterfactuals are difficult to disprove. But they should not blind us to the stimulus’s weaknesses. Much of the money was slow to move. Some went to temporary tax rebates with little effect on permanent hiring. Some preserved public-sector jobs without reforming the state and local governments that employ them. “Shovel-ready” projects turned out to be less ready than advertised. Meanwhile, uncertainty over taxes, health-care mandates, financial regulation, and energy policy made many employers cautious.

Pollin wants to redirect $330 billion per year from military and fossil-fuel sectors to education and clean energy. This sounds precise, but it assumes government can identify superior uses of capital better than markets can. Education spending is not automatically investment. The United States already spends heavily on education, with uneven results. Some school systems fail not because they lack employees but because they are governed by rigid bureaucracies, weak accountability, and union rules that protect adults more reliably than children. Hiring more teachers may reduce class sizes, but unless schools can reward excellence, remove incompetence, and give families choices, more spending may produce more employment without better education.

Clean energy is equally complicated. Government has a role in basic research and perhaps in pricing pollution, but directing large sums into favored technologies risks waste. Politicians are not good venture capitalists. Projects attract support because they promise jobs in particular districts, not because they are efficient. If wind, solar, or retrofitting programs produce energy savings at reasonable cost, private firms and households will adopt them, especially if regulatory barriers are removed and energy prices reflect true costs. But subsidizing labor-intensive energy in the name of job creation can make energy more expensive, reducing employment elsewhere. A job created by a subsidy is visible; the jobs lost because consumers and businesses pay higher taxes or higher energy bills are less visible.

The same problem applies to Pollin’s job-per-million-dollar calculations. More jobs per dollar is not always better. We could create many jobs by banning power tools and requiring construction workers to use spoons, but we would be poorer. The purpose of economic activity is not to maximize labor input; it is to maximize value. Productivity allows higher wages and living standards. A sector that creates few direct jobs may be highly productive and may support many jobs indirectly by lowering costs throughout the economy. Fossil fuels employ relatively few workers per dollar because they are productive. That productivity has powered factories, transportation, heating, and agriculture. We should certainly reduce pollution and encourage innovation, but we should not confuse labor intensity with social benefit.

Pollin’s treatment of globalization is also too sanguine. He says the issue is not globalization per se but neoliberal globalization. Yet global competition is not merely a policy choice imposed by Wall Street. Hundreds of millions of workers in China, India, Eastern Europe, and elsewhere have entered the world economy. They want the same chance at rising living standards that Americans once had. American workers cannot be insulated entirely from that fact. Protectionism would invite retaliation, raise consumer prices, and harm exporters. The better response is to make the United States the best place to start and expand businesses: lower and simpler taxes, predictable regulation, flexible labor markets, strong property rights, better basic education, and openness to skilled immigrants.

The moral language of a “right to a decent job” is appealing but dangerous. Rights normally impose duties. If every person has a right to a decent job, who has the duty to provide it? The federal government? Private employers? Taxpayers? And what counts as decent? If the state guarantees work at specified wages and benefits, it risks becoming employer of last resort on a vast scale, competing with private firms and politicizing employment. If it mandates that private firms provide jobs on terms set by law, it will discourage hiring. Good intentions do not repeal constraints.

A better moral goal is opportunity: a society in which people are free to work, save, start businesses, acquire skills, move to where jobs exist, and keep enough of what they earn to improve their lives. Government should provide a safety net for those in distress, enforce the rule of law, prevent fraud, maintain sound money, and fund genuine public goods. It should not attempt to manage the economy to a politically determined full-employment target.

What about the Federal Reserve? Pollin worries that inflation policy has been left to Wall Street. But inflation control is not a Wall Street conspiracy. It protects ordinary people from the erosion of wages and savings. The 1970s damaged confidence in government precisely because policymakers tried to exploit a supposed tradeoff between unemployment and inflation. The result was stagflation. Paul Volcker’s disinflation was painful, but it restored the monetary stability on which later growth depended. We should not relearn that lesson the hard way.

To reduce unemployment now, policy should focus on removing barriers to private hiring. Temporarily reduce payroll taxes. Allow immediate expensing of business investment. Simplify permitting for energy production, manufacturing, and infrastructure. Reform unemployment insurance so it supports job search and retraining without discouraging work. Encourage apprenticeships and community-college partnerships with employers. Address housing-market dysfunction so workers can move. Restrain future spending so businesses and households do not expect large tax increases. Reform entitlements before they crowd out everything else. These measures lack the drama of an epochal break with neoliberalism, but they are more consistent with how jobs are actually created.

Pollin is right about one thing: work matters. It is tied to dignity, family stability, and citizenship. But precisely because work matters, we should be wary of turning employment into a government project. The best employment program is a growing private economy disciplined by competition and supported by limited, competent government. The alternative—however humane in aspiration—risks substituting political allocation for enterprise, debt for investment, and dependency for opportunity.

Let Markets Work: Why Government Cannot Engineer Full Employment

Robert Pollin’s manifesto for full employment is an ambitious throwback to a bygone era of Keynesian demand management, but it is founded on a fundamental misunderstanding of how labor markets actually function. The tragic unemployment in the wake of the Great Recession is not a problem of insufficient spending or a lack of federal jobs programs; it is largely a consequence of labor market rigidities, perverse incentives, and a prolonged policy-induced uncertainty that has hamstrung private investment. If we truly want a high-employment economy, the path is not to double down on government intervention but to unleash the dynamism of the private sector by removing the barriers that prevent wages from adjusting and workers from moving into productive jobs.

Pollin’s history of economic thought is revealing. He sets up a clash between the Keynesian view, which he endorses, and the Friedmanite natural-rate framework, which he caricatures. But the Friedmanite insight—that unemployment largely reflects voluntary choices and structural impediments, not a chronic shortage of aggregate demand—is more relevant than ever. Milton Friedman’s concept of the natural rate of unemployment is not a claim that unemployment is always zero; it is the recognition that, in a dynamic economy, there are frictions: workers between jobs, skill mismatches, geographic immobility, and the inevitable dislocation that comes from technological change and global competition. The natural rate of unemployment in the United States today, given the depth of the housing bust and the mismatch between the skills of the long-term unemployed and the jobs available, is probably somewhere between 6 and 8 percent. Trying to force unemployment below that level through massive government spending and sectoral reallocation would lead not to permanent prosperity but to accelerating inflation and a misallocation of resources that would ultimately destroy jobs.

Pollin dismisses the 1970s inflation as a result of oil shocks, but the evidence shows that the wage-price spiral was already in motion before the 1973 embargo, driven by overly accommodative monetary and fiscal policy aimed at pushing unemployment below its natural rate. The same pattern would repeat today if we adopted his plan. He proposes shifting $330 billion in spending away from the military and fossil fuels toward education and clean energy, arguing that this would create 4.8 million jobs. Even if the input-output math is correct in a static sense, it ignores the general-equilibrium effects: the jobs created in education and green energy would be offset by the destruction of jobs in other sectors as resources are forcibly reallocated by political fiat rather than market signals. Moreover, the spending would be financed by deficits that, at $1.4 trillion annually, are already frightening investors and undermining business confidence. The prospect of even larger deficits would push up long-term interest rates, crowd out private investment, and further delay the recovery.

The more pressing obstacle to employment today is not a shortage of government spending but a regulatory and tax environment that makes businesses reluctant to hire. The Obama administration’s stimulus may have blunted the recession, but its aftereffects—an explosion of new financial, health-care, and environmental regulations, combined with the scheduled expiration of the Bush tax cuts and the looming costs of the new health-care law—have created a cloud of uncertainty. Small businesses, which generate the majority of new jobs, are sitting on record amounts of cash because they are terrified of what comes next. Reducing the corporate tax rate, simplifying the tax code, reining in the growth of entitlements, and signaling a credible commitment to fiscal discipline would do far more to restore the animal spirits Keynes talked about than any government “jobs program.”

Pollin also overlooks the crucial role of the housing market in driving today’s unemployment. Millions of homeowners are underwater and unable to move to where jobs are. Construction workers, who made up a disproportionate share of the job losses, cannot simply retrain overnight to build wind turbines or teach elementary school. The fastest way to reduce unemployment would be to accelerate the foreclosure process, allow the market to clear, and free up labor mobility. Government programs to modify mortgages have only prolonged the pain.

Finally, the idea that full employment with decent wages can be achieved without confronting globalization is a fantasy. Pollin brushes off the trade deficit and immigration as minor issues, but the fact is that the integration of China, India, and the former Soviet bloc into the global economy has added hundreds of millions of workers to the effective labor supply, holding down wages for low-skilled workers in advanced economies. The correct policy response is not to wall off trade but to accept that the wage structure must adjust, and to remove the floor of minimum wages, occupational licensing, and union protections that prevent it. In a flexible market, everyone who is willing to work at the market-clearing wage can find a job. The path back to full employment lies through market flexibility, not government central planning.

  4. 04Large federal deficits and a burdensome regulatory environment create policy-induced uncertainty that discourages private investment and hiring.
