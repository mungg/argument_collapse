"While the standards-based reform movement has been implemented in ways that are often corrosive and politically motivated, a moderate version of public standards and accountability is necessary to provide professional scaffolding for teachers and to ensure educational equity for vulnerable students."

A Friendly Quarrel with Deborah Meier

I have spent twenty-two years teaching in public schools, the last eight as a middle-school English teacher in a working-class district outside Hartford. I admire Deborah Meier enormously. Her books sit on my desk; her schools have inspired thousands of teachers like me; and most of what she says in "Educating a Democracy" rings true to my experience. So I want to disagree with her only where I must, and to do it in the spirit of a friendly quarrel.

Where Meier is right — and the standards reformers are wrong — is on the texture of teaching and learning. A classroom is a relationship before it is anything else. Children learn from teachers they trust, in schools where they feel known. Big anonymous high schools, where teachers see 150 students a day and no one knows whose kid is whose, are factories of failure for exactly the reasons Meier names. The push toward smaller learning communities, advisory systems, looping, teaching teams — all the things the Coalition of Essential Schools has been advocating — is plainly good for children. And Meier is right that the reduction of teaching to test prep is corrosive. When my district adopted its new curriculum map last fall, half the joy went out of my seventh-grade reading workshop. I now spend February reviewing multiple-choice test-taking strategies instead of finishing The Giver. That is not what I went into teaching to do, and the children know it.

Meier is also right about the politics. The Chicago story she opens with is real, and it is ugly. "Standards" has become a slogan that can be used to justify almost anything: expelling difficult children, ending desegregation programs, firing veteran teachers, handing schools over to for-profit operators. The word has been laundered. Whenever I hear a state legislator who has never set foot in a classroom hold forth about "raising standards," I instinctively check my wallet.

And yet. And yet.

I cannot follow Meier all the way to her conclusion that the standards movement is "fundamentally misguided." Three reservations hold me back.

First, I have come, late in my career, to believe that some common expectations across schools are genuinely useful — not because experts should dictate to teachers, but because teachers need them too. When I sit down to plan my seventh-grade year, I want to know what a seventh-grader should be able to do. I want to know what my colleagues at the high school expect kids to bring with them. I want a shared professional vocabulary about what "writing a persuasive essay" or "understanding fractions" actually means. Standards documents, at their best, give us that vocabulary. Meier herself describes how Mission Hill faculty developed graduation standards through years of community dialogue. Wonderful — but what about a new teacher who arrives mid-year and needs to know what her fifth-graders are supposed to be working toward? Some shared scaffolding helps. It does not have to be a 400-page state framework with 87 grade-four social-studies objectives. But it can be more than nothing.

Second, I worry about Meier's reliance on small schools of choice as the model for reform. I have visited Central Park East twice, and I taught for a summer at a small alternative school in New Haven. These places work. But they work in part because the families who arrive there want to be there, the faculty have self-selected for a particular vision, and the principal has the autonomy to fire people who don't fit. My current school cannot do any of these things. The kids come because they live on these streets. The teachers come because the district hired them. The principal cannot fire anyone, because the union contract — which I helped negotiate — makes it very hard. None of this is Meier's fault, and her response would presumably be to change the system so that more schools can operate like Mission Hill. But that is a very long lever to pull. In the meantime, the thirty kids in my second-period class need someone to know what they are supposed to be learning this year. Standards, imperfect as they are, do some of that work.

Third, I want to say something gentle but firm about equity. Meier acknowledges that the heaviest burdens of bad schooling fall on poor children, and she is right. But the data of the last twenty years suggest that "local control" has often been the cover under which low expectations for poor and minority children are maintained. The black child in a Mississippi Delta school whose teacher has been told that "these kids" can't really do algebra is not helped by an appeal to local judgment. She is helped, sometimes, by an outside force — a state framework, a court order, a federal mandate — that says: this child must have access to algebra, and we will check. Meier knows all this. Her own progressive tradition, after all, has often relied on outside intervention to protect the most vulnerable. The standards movement, for all its excesses, is partly an attempt to extend that protection into the academic core. We should not throw it away.

What I would urge instead is a kind of compromise that Meier's own essay gestures toward but doesn't quite embrace. Yes, let there be public standards — written by educators, not just politicians, and pruned to a manageable size. Yes, let there be assessments — but multiple ones, including portfolios and performance tasks, not just bubble tests. Yes, let there be public reporting — but with information about resources alongside outcomes, as Meier proposes. Yes, let there be consequences — but consequences directed at the adults and the institutions, not primarily at individual children. And yes, let there be wide latitude for schools, especially small schools of choice, to demonstrate that they meet the standards in their own ways. A 1999 reform package along these lines would, I think, be far better than either the crude high-stakes regime Meier rightly fears or the loose localism that I am afraid she sometimes idealizes.

The deepest thing in Meier's essay, and the thing I keep returning to, is her insistence that children need to see adults exercising judgment. She is absolutely right. In my own classroom, the most important thing I do every day is to model what it looks like to think seriously, to disagree honestly, to admit error, to keep my temper, to read carefully. No state framework will accomplish this; only the people in the building can. To the extent that the standards movement reduces teachers to script-readers and test administrators, it injures the most important thing schools do.

But the answer is not less accountability. It is better accountability — accountability for the things that actually matter, which are precisely the harder-to-measure habits of mind Mission Hill has tried to articulate. Meier's quarrel, in the end, is not with standards but with bad standards, badly used. So is mine. I just think she should say so, instead of giving aid and comfort to the people who would prefer no public expectations at all.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Common educational expectations provide a shared professional vocabulary and necessary scaffolding for teachers to coordinate learning across grade levels.
  2. 02The model of small, autonomous schools of choice is difficult to scale to traditional public schools that lack the same flexibility in staffing and student enrollment.
  3. 03External standards and mandates serve as a vital safeguard against the low expectations often found in local districts serving poor and minority children.
  4. 04Accountability should be reformed rather than rejected, moving toward a system that uses multiple assessment measures and directs consequences at institutions rather than individual children.
  5. 05The goal of reform should be 'better accountability' that focuses on meaningful habits of mind rather than a total return to localism.
