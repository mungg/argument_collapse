"Joseph Carens’s proposal for a time-based amnesty is a disastrous policy that ignores historical evidence and would incentivize a massive surge in future illegal immigration."

Joseph Carens's essay "The Case for Amnesty" is a textbook example of how noble intentions can lead to disastrous public policy. By focusing entirely on the moral claims of those currently residing illegally in the country, Carens ignores the glaring historical evidence that amnesties, regardless of how they are justified, serve as massive magnets for future illegal immigration. We do not need to rely on abstract theory to understand the consequences of his proposal; we only need to look at our own recent history.

In 1986, the United States passed the Immigration Reform and Control Act (IRCA). Like Carens’s proposal, IRCA was driven by a sense of moral and practical necessity. It provided legal status to nearly three million undocumented immigrants who could prove continuous residence. The promise was that this would be a one-time fix, coupled with strict employer sanctions to prevent future illegal flows. We know how that turned out. The employer sanctions were gutted, document fraud proliferated, and the amnesty sent a clear signal across the globe: if you can get into the United States and hide long enough, eventually, you will be rewarded with legal status.

Today, we have an estimated 11 million illegal immigrants. If we implement Carens’s proposal of an automatic transition to legal status after five to seven years, why should we expect anything other than a massive surge in illegal border crossings and visa overstays? Carens attempts to brush off this "moral hazard" argument by asserting that migrants base their decisions on short-term economic factors, not long-term policy shifts. This is incredibly naive. Migration networks are highly sophisticated. Information about changing policies in destination countries spreads rapidly. If the U.S. codifies a rule that five years of undetected presence guarantees a green card, human smugglers will use that policy as their primary marketing tool.

Carens dismisses the comparison between immigration laws and criminal laws, likening illegal entry to a traffic ticket. But this trivializes the profound impact that millions of undocumented residents have on a nation. The influx of illegal labor depresses wages for the most vulnerable low-skilled American workers. It strains local resources, from public schools mandated to teach children who speak no English, to emergency rooms flooded with uninsured patients. A sovereign nation has an absolute obligation to prioritize the welfare of its own citizens, particularly its poorest, over the desires of foreign nationals who bypass legal systems.

Furthermore, Carens’s "passage of time" argument creates a perverse incentive for migrants to live entirely off the grid, avoiding any interaction with authorities that might lead to deportation before the magical five-year mark is reached. This breeds an underground economy and isolates immigrant communities, precisely the opposite of the social cohesion Carens claims to value.

Before we can even entertain the conversation about what to do with the undocumented population currently here, we must unequivocally secure the border and implement a foolproof, mandatory E-Verify system to eliminate the jobs magnet. Until the spigot is turned off, any form of amnesty—whether called a pathway to citizenship, regularization, or earned legal status—will simply reset the clock for the next crisis. We cannot let sympathy for the individuals currently here blind us to the systemic chaos an amnesty will inevitably invite.
