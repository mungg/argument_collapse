The Enemy Was Uncertainty, Not Science

Five years on, the pandemic feels like a dream that the world has collectively agreed to forget. Macedo and Lee are right to jolt us out of that lethargy, but the argument they advance—that U.S. institutions systematically failed to tell the truth, to weigh tradeoffs, and to look inward—is at once too sweeping and, in a peculiar way, not sweeping enough. It is too sweeping because it conflates the genuine epistemological predicament of the early pandemic with institutional bad faith. It is not sweeping enough because it never quite confronts the profound challenge that radical uncertainty poses to democratic governance. The real failure of the COVID-19 response was not that “science” was misrepresented but that our public institutions proved structurally incapable of navigating the fog of a truly novel threat. Both the lockdown enthusiasts and their libertarian critics shared the same fallacy: the conviction that, if only the right data had been applied, the road not taken would have been clearly superior.

Begin with the question of scientific communication. The lead essay documents, with meticulous sourcing, that the pre-pandemic consensus warned against broad society-wide NPIs. The WHO’s 2019 report, the Johns Hopkins preparedness blueprint, the UK’s 2011 influenza strategy—all stressed that the evidence for lockdowns, school closures, and border restrictions was weak, that unintended consequences were severe, and that such measures should not be undertaken lightly. The essay then argues that when COVID-19 struck, these warnings were ignored, and the public was told a triumphalist story of “following the science” that papered over deep uncertainties. That much is undeniable. But is it evidence of a cover-up, or of a genuine shift in expert judgment? The documents in question were not holy writ; they were context-dependent heuristics written for an unspecified future pandemic. When the future arrived with a pathogen that had a high reproduction number, a significant infection fatality rate, and an apparently frightening capacity to overrun intensive care units, decision-makers—not just in the United States but across every continent—reassessed. They looked to on-the-ground reports from Wuhan and Bergamo, not just to models, and concluded that the old playbook was insufficient. This was not, as the essay sometimes implies, a paternalistic conspiracy; it was adaptive, albeit hasty, reasoning under fire.

The real sin, if there is one, was not the decision to intervene but the failure to build learning into the intervention from the start. Macedo and Lee note that policy “only weakly adjusted to changing conditions” and that partisan identity soon locked in divergent approaches regardless of epidemiological indicators. That is a critique of political polarization and bureaucratic inertia, not of the scientific advisory process per se. Yet the essay repeatedly attributes this rigidity to a moralized public health establishment that crushed dissent. There is truth there—the demonization of the Great Barrington Declaration was a shameful episode, and the suppression of legitimate debate about lab origins and cloth mask efficacy should alarm anyone who values open inquiry. But the causal arrow points both ways. The right-wing media ecosystem’s embrace of hydroxychloroquine, ivermectin, and anti-vaccine conspiracies created an environment where any admission of uncertainty by officials was instantly weaponized. Public health communicators who might have preferred to level with the public faced a classic “noble lie” dilemma: speak with nuance and risk emboldening the forces of denial, or project confidence and risk losing credibility later. No amount of institutional virtue can resolve a Catch-22 of its own making, but that Catch-22 was itself a product of political tribalism, not the other way around.

The essay’s empirical anchor is the state-level comparison of stringency and mortality. The authors show that from March 2020 through mid-2021, Republican and Democratic states implemented sharply different policies, yet their cumulative COVID-19 mortality was nearly identical. This is an important and under-discussed finding, and it deserves serious engagement. But as any epidemiologist will quickly note, the absence of a correlation in observational state-level data does not prove the absence of a causal effect. States are interconnected. Florida’s relative openness may have contributed to New York’s later outbreaks, and vice versa, through travel. Voluntary behavior—mask-wearing, social distancing—was often more widespread in places where mandates were absent but citizens were fearful. And the most vulnerable populations, like nursing home residents, were subject to federal regulations and common supply-chain problems that did not vary by state policy. To interpret the null finding as proof that “NPIs did not work” is to ignore the complex ecology of a pandemic. The same data could equally be read to suggest that early, aggressive action by blue states in March and April saved lives that were then lost to later, less restrictive policies in red states, creating a wash in totals. The truth is we simply do not know. The lead essay, while accusing others of false certainty, veers dangerously close to asserting a certainty of its own.

This points to a deeper philosophical issue: what does it mean for a policy to “work” in a pandemic? If the goal was to permanently suppress the virus until a vaccine arrived, then almost all countries failed, because the virus eventually infected most of the planet. If the goal was to “flatten the curve” and prevent hospital collapse, then the United States arguably succeeded, albeit at staggering collateral cost. The lead essay is entirely correct that many prominent figures—in the Safra Center roadmap, for instance—explicitly invoked a wartime mobilization metaphor that implied total victory over the virus, and that this framing was a catastrophic mistake. But the error was one of ambition and rhetoric, not necessarily of policy substance. School closures, for example, were indeed imposed with far too little attention to the evidence that children were low risk and poor transmitters. But part of that oversight stemmed from a genuinely uncertain science: in the spring of 2020, we did not know with confidence whether children were major drivers of spread, and the cautious course (closing schools) seemed, to many, the safer bet. That it proved to be a disastrously wrong bet for millions of children is a truth we must face, but facing it requires us to accept that in environments of deep uncertainty, plausible bets can still lose catastrophically. That is not a pathology; it is the human condition.

A better postmortem would thus focus not on rooting out the villains who misled the public but on redesigning the machinery by which evidence is translated into policy. The lead essay laments that we have not had a comprehensive reckoning, and it is right. But the kind of reckoning it envisions—a truth commission that establishes the factual record of what was known when—is only half the project. We also need an institutional architecture that can make policy more nimbly, with built-in mechanisms for randomized evaluation, transparent updating, and political accountability for tradeoffs. When the next novel pathogen emerges, we will once again be in the dark. The question is not whether we will make mistakes but whether we will have created systems that correct mistakes quickly, rather than entrenching them for months or years.

Macedo and Lee have performed an invaluable service by insisting that we stop looking away. But their essay, for all its careful scholarship, occasionally slides from indictment to overcorrection. To say that the public health establishment failed is true; to imply that a better establishment would have had clear, ex ante answers to the question “lock down or not?” is to succumb to the very illusion of certainty that they rightly criticize. The real lesson of COVID-19 is that liberal democracy has not yet learned how to govern under catastrophic uncertainty without either falling into technocratic hubris or retreating to partisan bunkers. That is a problem bigger than any one agency or profession. It is a challenge for the entire political order.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The shift away from pre-pandemic planning toward lockdowns was an adaptive, albeit hasty, response to new data from early outbreaks rather than a paternalistic conspiracy.
  2. 02The primary institutional failure was not the initial intervention, but the inability to build learning mechanisms into policy to allow for adjustments as new evidence emerged.
  3. 03Public health officials were trapped in a 'noble lie' dilemma where admitting uncertainty was discouraged by a polarized political environment ready to weaponize any nuance.
  4. 04State-level data showing similar mortality rates across different policy regimes is too complex to prove that non-pharmaceutical interventions were ineffective, as voluntary behavior and interstate connectivity confound the results.
  5. 05The catastrophic costs of policies like school closures were the result of losing a high-stakes bet under conditions of deep uncertainty rather than a deliberate disregard for tradeoffs.
  6. 06A proper postmortem should focus on redesigning institutional architecture to enable nimble, evidence-based policy updates rather than simply identifying who misled the public.
