"A marriage is only truly over when one partner repeatedly refuses to participate in the mutual work of repair and reciprocity."

A marriage is not over when two people are unhappy. It is not over when sex has become awkward, when parenting has made them strangers, or when old grievances rise again. Those are painful conditions, but they are also ordinary human conditions. The more reliable test is whether both people are still willing to participate in repair.

By repair I mean something concrete: telling the truth, listening without immediate self-defense, accepting responsibility, changing behavior, seeking help, and returning to the conversation after anger cools. A couple can survive almost any single season if both partners remain capable of that work. They may need a therapist, a religious counselor, a trial separation or a new division of labor. But if there is still good faith, there is still a marriage to work with.

The breaking point comes when one partner repeatedly refuses repair. This may look like contempt, not just anger: eye-rolling, mockery, name-calling, or treating the other spouse’s pain as a nuisance. It may look like stonewalling: “This is your problem,” “I’m not talking about it,” “Nothing needs to change.” It may look like promises made only to end the argument, followed by no action. At that point the injured spouse is no longer deciding whether to “try harder.” He or she is deciding whether to accept a life in which only one person is married.

This standard avoids two mistakes. It does not make divorce the answer to every disappointment. But it also does not demand endless sacrifice from the spouse who has already tried, explained, waited and hoped. Marriage requires more than love; it requires reciprocity. When the possibility of mutual repair has been exhausted, leaving is not a failure of commitment. It is recognition that commitment cannot be performed alone.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Ordinary marital struggles like unhappiness, lack of intimacy, or old grievances are not definitive reasons to end a relationship if both partners are willing to work on them.
  2. 02A marriage can survive almost any crisis as long as both individuals maintain the good faith necessary to engage in concrete actions like listening, taking responsibility, and changing behavior.
  3. 03The definitive breaking point occurs when a partner exhibits contempt or stonewalling, effectively treating the other spouse's pain as a nuisance rather than a shared concern.
  4. 04Leaving a marriage is a recognition of reality rather than a failure of commitment when one person is forced to carry the relationship alone without a reciprocal partner.
