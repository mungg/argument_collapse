Diversified Gemini

"While Dan Breznitz correctly critiques the Silicon Valley model, his proposed alternatives in Taiwan and China ignore the systemic labor exploitation and power imbalances that underpin their economic success."

In his essay "What Silicon Valley Gets Wrong about Innovation," Dan Breznitz offers a compelling critique of the techno-fetishism that leads policymakers to endlessly chase the venture-capital-backed "unicorn" model. His diagnosis of the inequality generated by places like Silicon Valley and Israel’s tech sector is spot on. However, when Breznitz points to alternative models of innovation—specifically Stage 3 component innovation in Taiwan and Stage 4 assembly in China’s Pearl River Delta—he falls into a different kind of trap. He equates regional economic dominance with "inclusive local prosperity," entirely ignoring the deeply exploitative labor conditions that actually make these global manufacturing hubs competitive.

Breznitz describes the Pearl River Delta as a place that "produced sustainable economic growth and jobs to millions of people." He frames the success of companies like Foxconn, Huawei, and BYD as the triumph of "stage four innovation," where expertise in systematizing production margins allows these firms to dominate the global market. What is missing from this sanitized economic picture is the human cost of this specific type of innovation. The "efficiency" of Shenzhen's assembly lines is not merely a product of clever engineering or sophisticated supply chain management; it is fundamentally predicated on the exploitation of a highly vulnerable labor force.

The millions of jobs Breznitz mentions are largely filled by rural migrant workers operating under China's *hukou* system. This internal passport system ties a citizen's social benefits—healthcare, education for their children, pension—to their rural hometown. When these workers migrate to Shenzhen or Dongguan to work in the factories driving Stage 4 innovation, they arrive as second-class citizens, easily exploitable because they lack local social safety nets. They are subjected to grueling hours, mandatory overtime, and harsh disciplinary environments. The tragic wave of worker suicides at Foxconn facilities a decade ago, which prompted the installation of "suicide nets" outside factory dormitories, is the dark, necessary corollary to the razor-thin profit margins and hyper-flexible production ramps that Breznitz celebrates.

Similarly, while Taiwan’s Stage 3 innovation in semiconductors and electronics is indeed impressive, the model is built on an incredibly intense work culture that systematically burns out engineers and technicians. And increasingly, Taiwan’s foundational manufacturing relies on migrant labor from Southeast Asia, who face exorbitant broker fees, restricted mobility, and systemic abuses.

If this is what Breznitz considers "inclusive local prosperity," we need to urgently redefine our terms. True inclusive prosperity cannot be measured solely by a region's GDP, its export volume, or even the sheer number of jobs it creates. It must be measured by the quality of those jobs, the dignity afforded to the workers, and the equitable distribution of the value they create.

Breznitz attempts to rescue the idea of inclusive prosperity by focusing on how innovation can be distributed across different stages of production. But shifting the locus of innovation from software design in California to component assembly in Guangdong does not change the fundamental power dynamics of global capitalism. The lead firms—whether they are based in Cupertino or Taipei—relentlessly squeeze their suppliers, who in turn squeeze the workers on the shop floor.

To genuinely achieve inclusive local prosperity, we cannot merely change the *stage* of innovation a region focuses on; we must change the *ownership and power structures* underlying that innovation. We need to look toward models of economic development that prioritize worker power and democratic control of resources.

What would this look like? It would mean supporting worker-owned cooperatives, where the fruits of continuous, incremental innovation (Breznitz's Stage 3) are captured by the workers themselves rather than foreign shareholders. It would mean robust unionization across all sectors, ensuring that even if a community focuses on Stage 4 assembly, the workers have the collective bargaining power to demand safe conditions, reasonable hours, and a living wage. It would involve community land trusts that prevent the gentrification Breznitz rightly identifies as a curse of the tech-hub model.

Innovation policy cannot just be industrial policy; it must also be labor policy. If the United States, as Breznitz notes, is to reclaim some of its lost capabilities in Stage 3 and Stage 4 manufacturing—perhaps fueled by the recent push for supply chain resilience—it must do so with strong labor protections attached. Operation Warp Speed or the upcoming infrastructure investments will only yield inclusive prosperity if the federal government mandates union representation, prevailing wages, and strict worker safety standards for any company receiving public funds.

Breznitz is right that we have more choices than the Silicon Valley evangelists want us to believe. But we must be careful not to trade the elitist inequality of the venture capital model for the brutal, systemic exploitation of the global assembly line. Innovation is indeed a collective endeavor, but until the workers who actually execute these innovations are given a seat at the table and a fair share of the wealth, "inclusive prosperity" will remain a hollow slogan.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'inclusive prosperity' Breznitz attributes to Stage 4 innovation in China's Pearl River Delta is actually built on the exploitation of vulnerable migrant workers under the restrictive hukou system.
  2. 02The manufacturing success of Taiwan's Stage 3 innovation relies on a culture of worker burnout and the abuse of Southeast Asian migrant labor.
  3. 03True inclusive prosperity must be measured by job quality and worker dignity rather than just GDP, export volume, or job quantity.
  4. 04Shifting the stage of innovation does not fix the power dynamics of global capitalism where lead firms relentlessly squeeze suppliers and workers.
  5. 05To achieve genuine inclusion, innovation policy must be paired with labor policies that prioritize worker ownership, unionization, and democratic control of resources.
  6. 06Any government effort to reclaim manufacturing capabilities must include strict mandates for union representation and prevailing wages to avoid replicating the exploitative global assembly line.
