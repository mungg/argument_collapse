"While Dan Breznitz correctly identifies the flaws of the Silicon Valley model, his focus on global manufacturing supply chains overlooks the potential for inclusive prosperity within the local, non-tradable service economy."

Dan Breznitz’s thoughtful essay provides a necessary corrective to the obsession with creating the "next Silicon Valley." By breaking down the innovation process into four distinct stages, he demonstrates how local economies can find sustainable niches within the global supply chain. However, while Breznitz broadens our understanding of *how* innovation happens, his focus remains entirely trapped within the realm of tradable goods and high-tech manufacturing. Whether he is discussing bicycles, smartphones, vaccines, or luxury shoes, Breznitz’s paradigm of inclusive prosperity is fundamentally rooted in exporting physical products to global markets. In October 2021, as we attempt to build a more resilient and equitable post-pandemic economy, this framework misses where the vast majority of people actually work: the foundational, non-tradable service economy.

If the goal is truly "local inclusive prosperity," we cannot rely solely on the hope that our city might become the next global hub for bicycle frames or semiconductor packaging. The reality of modern advanced economies is that manufacturing, even highly innovative manufacturing, employs a steadily shrinking percentage of the workforce. In the United States, over 80 percent of workers are employed in the service sector. They are nurses, teachers, retail workers, care aides, logistics drivers, and hospitality staff.

Breznitz argues that "continuous innovation... is the unsung hero of economic growth and improved welfare," but he applies this insight almost exclusively to the industrial supply chain. We need to apply this exact same logic to the foundational economy. How do we innovate in the delivery of elder care? How do we use new processes to make local food networks more resilient and affordable? How do we reorganize early childhood education to better support working parents while paying educators a dignified wage?

These are not questions that can be solved by a Taiwanese semiconductor foundry or an Italian shoe design cluster. They require a completely different model of local innovation, one anchored not by export-oriented private firms, but by civic institutions.

To achieve local inclusive prosperity, communities should turn their focus toward "anchor institutions"—hospitals, universities, municipal governments, and regional utilities. These entities are rooted in their communities; they cannot up and leave when the macroeconomic winds change, unlike the factories of Schwinn or the corporate headquarters of a venture-backed startup. Anchor institutions have massive procurement budgets, large employment bases, and significant real estate holdings. By intentionally directing their economic power inward, they can drive Stage 2 and Stage 3 innovation in the local service and foundational sectors.

For example, the Cleveland model (the Evergreen Cooperatives) is an innovation in local economic development that Breznitz overlooks. By leveraging the procurement needs of local hospitals and universities, Cleveland created a network of worker-owned cooperatives specializing in green laundry services, renewable energy installation, and hydroponic agriculture. This is process innovation (Breznitz's Stage 3) applied directly to the needs of the local, non-tradable economy. It creates sustainable, living-wage jobs for people with varied skill levels, directly attacking the inequality that Breznitz rightly decries in the Silicon Valley model.

Furthermore, focusing on the foundational economy provides a buffer against the very globalization that Breznitz assumes as a constant. The supply chain crises we are currently experiencing in late 2021 demonstrate the fragility of relying on a globally fragmented production system. While Breznitz sees the collapse of the vertically integrated system as an opportunity for cities to find specific niches, the current port backlogs and semiconductor shortages reveal the massive risks of hyper-specialization. If a city’s entire economic model is based on being a node in a global supply chain—even a highly innovative node—it remains deeply vulnerable to geopolitical shocks, pandemics, and climate disruptions.

Innovating within the non-tradable sector builds local resilience. When a community innovates in retrofitting its own housing stock for energy efficiency, or develops new municipal broadband networks, or creates localized mutual aid networks for health and social care, the wealth generated circulates locally. It is not siphoned off to foreign investors, nor is it dependent on the smooth functioning of container ships across the Pacific.

Breznitz is entirely correct that we need to escape the techno-fetishism of the venture capital model. Tech teens and mid-sized manufacturing firms certainly have a role to play. But if we want prosperity to be genuinely inclusive, we must look beyond the factory floor and the export market. The most vital innovations of the next decade will not be in the components of a smartphone; they will be in the social technologies and community processes that allow us to care for one another, educate our children, and sustain our local environments. That is the true frontier of inclusive local prosperity.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The vast majority of the modern workforce is employed in service sectors like healthcare and education, which require their own forms of process and social innovation.
  2. 02Local 'anchor institutions' like hospitals and universities can drive inclusive growth by directing their procurement and employment power toward the local community rather than global markets.
  3. 03The Cleveland model of worker-owned cooperatives demonstrates how innovation in the foundational economy can create living-wage jobs and reduce inequality more effectively than export-oriented manufacturing.
  4. 04Focusing on the non-tradable sector builds economic resilience against the geopolitical and logistical shocks inherent in globally fragmented production systems.
  5. 05True inclusive prosperity requires prioritizing social technologies and community processes that allow wealth to circulate locally rather than being siphoned off by global investors.
