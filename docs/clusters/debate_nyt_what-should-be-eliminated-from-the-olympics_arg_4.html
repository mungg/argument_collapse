Diversified Claude

"The Olympics should eliminate all judged sports to ensure the competition remains a pure, objective contest of measurable human achievement."

Cut the judged sports. All of them.

The Olympics should be a contest of measurable human achievement: who ran fastest, jumped highest, lifted most, scored more goals, crossed the line first. The moment a panel of judges hovers over a scorecard deciding whether one gymnast's toe-point was prettier than another's, we have left sport and entered the realm of dance recital. That includes rhythmic gymnastics (yes, the scary-puppet ribbon-twirlers), synchronized swimming, synchronized diving, dressage, and the artistic components of figure skating in the Winter Games.

I am not saying these aren't difficult. Rhythmic gymnasts are extraordinary athletes. Synchronized swimmers can hold their breath longer than most of us can hold a thought. But difficulty is not the criterion. The criterion is objectivity. Every judged sport at every Olympics produces a fresh scandal — the 2002 Salt Lake City pairs skating fiasco, persistent rumors of bloc voting in gymnastics, boxing decisions in London this week that have left commentators sputtering. (Boxing, by the way, belongs on this list too: it's nominally objective, but the scoring system is so opaque it might as well be interpretive dance with gloves.)

Compare that to the 100-meter final. Usain Bolt crosses the line; the clock stops; nobody argues. That is what the Olympics should be.

The counterargument is that the Games would lose color and television appeal without the sequins and swan dives. Maybe. But the Olympics already has plenty of color: the opening ceremonies, the human-interest packages, the flags. The competition itself should be a pure meritocracy of stopwatch and tape measure. If we keep adding subjective sports — and the IOC keeps trying, with breakdancing apparently on deck — the Games will eventually feel less like a sporting championship and more like "America's Got Talent" with national anthems.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Judged sports are more akin to artistic performances or dance recitals than objective athletic competitions.
  2. 02Subjective scoring systems frequently lead to judging scandals, corruption, and opaque results that undermine the integrity of the Games.
  3. 03The inclusion of subjective sports risks turning the Olympics into a talent show rather than a meritocracy based on stopwatches and tape measures.
  4. 04Unlike judged events, objective sports like the 100-meter dash provide clear, indisputable winners that do not require human interpretation.
Diversified Gemini

"The International Olympic Committee should eliminate events where the outcome is determined by subjective judging rather than objective measurement."

If the International Olympic Committee is truly looking to trim the fat from the Summer Games, the guiding principle should be simple: eliminate events where the outcome is decided by a panel of subjective judges rather than a clock, a measuring tape, or a scoreboard.

The prompt rightly pokes fun at synchronized swimming, an event that consistently blurs the line between athletic competition and aquatic vaudeville. When a sport requires waterproof makeup, elaborate sequined costumes, and theatrical facial expressions, it has crossed over into performance art. The same can be said for rhythmic gymnastics, where competitors twirl ribbons and toss hoops in what amounts to a Cirque du Soleil audition.

The core of the Olympic ideal is the pursuit of human physical limits: *Citius, Altius, Fortius*—faster, higher, stronger. It is not "more expressive, more synchronized, more aesthetically pleasing." We do not need to diminish the tremendous physical conditioning required to perform these routines to recognize that they belong in a different category of human endeavor. When athletes cross a finish line, or lift a barbell, or score a goal, the result is undeniable. The winner is a matter of objective fact.

In judged events, however, controversies are inevitable. We are relying on the aesthetic preferences and potential biases of officials to award medals. Boxing and traditional gymnastics at least have heavily structured point systems based on landed punches or specific physical maneuvers. But synchronized swimming and rhythmic gymnastics lean entirely on subjective interpretation of artistry. Let’s return the Olympics to measurable athletic feats and leave the choreographed pageantry to the theater.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Events like synchronized swimming and rhythmic gymnastics function more as performance art than as traditional athletic competitions.
  2. 02The Olympic motto emphasizes physical limits like speed and strength rather than aesthetic qualities like expressiveness or synchronization.
  3. 03Subjective judging relies on the aesthetic preferences and potential biases of officials, leading to inevitable controversy.
  4. 04The Olympics should prioritize measurable athletic feats where the winner is determined by undeniable, objective facts.
