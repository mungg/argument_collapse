"To ensure a sustainable water future in the American West, water policy must move beyond urban conservation and focus on reforming agricultural practices and groundwater management."

If the West is serious about making water last, it has to talk plainly about agriculture. Urban residents should conserve, and many have. But the largest share of developed water in western states goes to farms and ranches. Any water policy that focuses mainly on shorter showers and ignores irrigation is theater.

This does not mean blaming farmers. Many western farmers have become remarkably efficient, using drip systems, laser-leveled fields, soil-moisture sensors and other techniques. Many also grow crops under contracts, debt burdens and federal water arrangements that reward production more than conservation. The question is not whether agriculture should survive in the West. It must. The question is what kind of agriculture makes sense in a hotter, drier region.

We should begin by paying farmers to save water, not just to use it. Rotational fallowing programs, in which farmers are compensated for leaving land idle in dry years, can free water for cities or ecosystems without permanently destroying farming communities. Irrigation districts should be allowed and encouraged to share conserved water, so long as the savings are real and not simply water that would have returned to the river or aquifer.

Crop choice also matters. It is hard to justify using scarce western water to produce low-value, water-intensive crops in places where supplies are collapsing. Alfalfa, cotton and some pasture uses deserve scrutiny, particularly when the product is exported overseas in effect as “virtual water.” Permanent crops such as almonds and pistachios pose a different problem: once planted, they must be watered even in drought or the investment dies. That makes the decision to expand orchards in dry basins a public concern, not merely a private bet.

Groundwater must be brought under control. In much of California, surface water is allocated through elaborate rules while groundwater pumping remains dangerously underregulated. The result is predictable: when canals deliver less, wells go deeper. Aquifers become the drought reserve no one admits is being depleted. Every basin should have a water budget, mandatory measurement of significant pumping, and enforceable limits tied to recharge.

Federal and state policy should also be aligned with water reality. Reclamation contracts, crop insurance and disaster aid should not encourage planting that assumes every year will be wet. Conservation grants should prioritize real reductions in consumptive use, not merely more efficient equipment that allows farmers to irrigate more acres.

Finally, we should protect farmworkers and rural towns. When fields are fallowed, the people who lose wages are often not landowners receiving compensation. Water deals must include transition funds, job programs and drinking-water protections for communities already suffering from dry wells and contaminated supplies.

The West cannot conserve its way out of drought by lecturing homeowners while leaving the largest uses untouched. A durable settlement must keep farming viable, but it must also ask agriculture to adapt to the hydrology we have, not the hydrology we wish we had.
