Diversified Claude

"To increase interest and diversity in STEM, education reform should prioritize building social infrastructure and mentoring networks that foster a sense of identity and belonging rather than focusing solely on curriculum changes."

Reading these education debates, I'm always struck by how they oscillate between two poles: technocratic reform from above (new standards, new tests, new curricula) and inspirational rhetoric (we need passionate teachers, engaged students, real-world relevance). Both miss something basic about how young people actually decide what to do with their lives.

Adolescents choose paths primarily through identification with people they admire and contexts where they feel they belong. The students who go into STEM fields are overwhelmingly those who, by middle school, have come to see themselves as the kind of person who does science. This identity is built less by classroom experiences than by relationships — with parents who are engineers, with an uncle who's a programmer, with a teacher who singled them out, with friends who shared the interest, with a summer camp where they met other kids like them.

This is why the diversity problem is so persistent. Kids from families and communities without STEM professionals lack the everyday exposure that builds identification. A girl who has never met a female engineer is much less likely to imagine herself becoming one. A black kid in a high school where the AP Physics class is all white and Asian sees that class as not for him, regardless of his actual aptitude. This is not a deficit in the kids; it is a deficit in the environment around them.

The implication for policy is that the highest-leverage interventions might not be curricular at all. They might be programs that put kids in sustained contact with STEM adults and STEM peers like themselves. Summer programs like MITES at MIT and SHPE Jr. Chapters for Latino students have impressive track records, much better than any classroom intervention I know of. Mentoring relationships that persist over years, not afterschool sessions, change trajectories.

This is harder to do at scale than rewriting a curriculum. It requires money, time, and committed adults. But the evidence suggests that what changes a kid's path is rarely a great lesson plan. It's a relationship and a sense of belonging.

If we want more young people, and a more diverse group, choosing STEM, we should think less about curriculum reform and more about building the social infrastructure — clubs, camps, mentoring networks, near-peer programs — that lets kids try on STEM identities before they decide who they are.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Adolescents choose career paths based on personal identification with role models and environments where they feel they belong.
  2. 02The lack of diversity in STEM persists because students from underrepresented backgrounds often lack everyday exposure to professionals who look like them.
  3. 03Sustained relationships with STEM mentors and peers are more effective at changing a student's trajectory than classroom-based curricular interventions.
  4. 04Extracurricular programs like summer camps and clubs provide the necessary social infrastructure for students to 'try on' STEM identities before making career decisions.
