"Robert Pollin’s proposal for a massive federal reallocation of funds to achieve full employment is an unrealistic central-planning strategy that ignores the economic dangers of debt and regulatory uncertainty."

Robert Pollin’s essay diagnosing the persistent agony of American unemployment is a timely reminder that the economic crisis is far from over. Nearly two years into the Obama administration, with official unemployment stubbornly hovering around 9.5 percent, it is clear that the 2009 stimulus package was not the panacea it was sold to be. However, Pollin’s prescription—a massive federal reallocation of $330 billion annually from the military and fossil-fuel sectors to education and clean energy—is a triumph of spreadsheet mathematics over economic reality.

Pollin waves away the looming threat of the federal deficit, noting only that the stimulus generated short-term deficits of $1.4 trillion in 2009 and 2010. He characterizes the rising chorus in Washington in favor of fiscal restraint as a mere "mantra," an ideological hangover of neoliberalism. But this dismisses the very real, structural threat that unprecedented national debt poses to long-term economic growth and, by extension, employment. The recent 2010 midterm elections demonstrated a profound voter anxiety about the scale of federal borrowing, and this anxiety is not misplaced. When the government runs deficits equivalent to 10 percent of GDP, it inevitably risks crowding out private investment. Capital that could be used by small businesses to expand and hire is instead absorbed by sovereign debt issuance.

Furthermore, Pollin’s confidence in the job-creating superiority of government spending in education and green energy relies on a static view of the economy. He cites a multiplier effect, claiming that moving funds from fossil fuels to clean energy will yield a net increase of 4.8 million jobs. But this relies on the assumption that government directed investment is as efficient as private sector investment. We already have ample evidence from the American Recovery and Reinvestment Act that "green jobs" are far easier to create on a chalkboard than in reality. Subsidizing clean energy often means subsidizing politically connected firms, leading to malinvestment. If wind and solar were ready to sustain millions of high-paying, productive jobs without massive, permanent government life-support, private capital would be rushing into those sectors on its own.

Pollin essentially wants to double down on the very central-planning impulses that have paralyzed the current recovery. The reason businesses are not hiring right now is not a lack of government spending—it is a lack of certainty. The looming costs of the recently passed Affordable Care Act, combined with the opaque regulatory mandates of the Dodd-Frank financial reforms, have created a climate where employers are terrified to expand their payrolls. If the government truly wants to achieve full employment, it must foster an environment of certainty, lower corporate tax rates to make the U.S. competitive globally, and rein in the explosive growth of the regulatory state. Milton Friedman, whom Pollin eagerly uses as a foil, understood that sustainable employment comes from productive private enterprise, not from endlessly reshuffling tax dollars between favored and unfavored sectors.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The unprecedented national debt and high federal deficits risk crowding out private investment and stifling long-term economic growth.
  2. 02Government-directed investments in sectors like green energy are often inefficient, prone to malinvestment, and less productive than private sector capital allocation.
  3. 03The primary barrier to hiring is not a lack of government spending, but rather the economic uncertainty created by burdensome regulatory mandates and the growth of the regulatory state.
  4. 04Sustainable employment is best achieved by lowering corporate taxes and fostering a stable environment for private enterprise rather than reshuffling tax dollars between politically favored sectors.
