"Climate change is the primary existential threat to Africa's development, potentially negating all recent progress in democratization and economic growth unless the global community takes immediate, large-scale action."

Of all the headwinds Edward Miguel identifies, the most ominous, and the least within Africa’s control, is climate change. He devotes a section of his essay to the Sahel’s vulnerability and mentions the cruel irony that those least responsible for global warming will suffer the most. But his treatment, while suggestive, still places climate change as one risk among many. I contend that climate change is not merely an additional threat; it is the master threat that could undo every fragile gain made in the past decade, rendering moot the debates about trade policy, aid effectiveness, and democratization. If the world does not take dramatic action soon, there may be no African “turn” — only a descent into environmental and humanitarian catastrophe.

The scientific picture, even as of 2008, is grim. The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change’s Fourth Assessment Report, released in 2007, confirms that Africa is disproportionately exposed. By 2020, between 75 and 250 million people are projected to face increased water stress. Yields from rain-fed agriculture, which supports the livelihoods of the majority of Africans, could fall by 50 percent in some countries as early as 2020. The Sahel, as Miguel notes, faces a particularly alarming combination of rising temperatures and declining rainfall. But the risks extend far beyond the Sahel: East Africa’s highlands could lose much of their tea and coffee suitability, while coastal cities from Lagos to Dar es Salaam are threatened by sea-level rise. These are not distant, speculative hazards; they are already manifesting in more frequent droughts, floods, and shifting growing seasons.

The link between climate and conflict that Miguel draws on is real, but he understates its potential scale. It is not just that extreme weather reduces incomes and makes rebellion more attractive. Climate-induced migration, competition over dwindling water and pasture, and the collapse of state capacity under fiscal strain can create a cascade of instability that overwhelms even relatively peaceful societies. Darfur itself is often cited as a conflict with ecological roots: desertification and population pressure exacerbated tensions between farmers and herders long before the political violence erupted. As large regions become uninhabitable, the pressure on cities and across borders will be immense. The international community has no framework for managing such large-scale displacement.

Against this backdrop, Miguel’s call for rapid conflict prevention support is a sensible palliative, but it is akin to buying fire insurance while the house is drenched in gasoline. The necessary response is twofold and far more ambitious. First, the major emitters — the United States, Europe, and increasingly China and India — must commit to steep, binding emissions reductions in the near term. As of mid-2008, the international negotiations are floundering, with the U.S. still refusing to ratify the Kyoto Protocol and the post-2012 framework deeply uncertain. Africa has no voice in these negotiations commensurate with its vulnerability; its future is being decided in Washington and Beijing, and it is being sacrificed.

Second, rich countries must finance a massive program of adaptation for Africa. This is not charity; it is a debt. The accumulated emissions from the industrial world are what have locked in the warming already underway. Yet adaptation funding remains pathetically low. The UN’s Adaptation Fund is expected to raise only a few hundred million dollars over the next several years — a rounding error compared to the trillions spent on stimulus packages and bank bailouts in the current financial crisis. African farmers need drought-resistant seeds, improved water management, and strengthened early-warning systems. Coastal cities need protective infrastructure. Pastoralists need support to diversify livelihoods. All of this costs money, and it must be new and additional, not diverted from existing aid budgets.

The Africa story, as Miguel frames it, is one of cautious hope. But that hope depends, more than any other single factor, on a stable climate. Without immediate and sustained cuts in emissions and a massive increase in adaptation finance, that stability will vanish, taking with it the schools, the roads, the democratic experiments, and the economic growth. The biggest question of “Is it Africa’s turn?” may not be answered by Africans at all, but by the citizens and leaders of the industrialized world, whose decisions in the next few years will determine whether the planet they bequeath allows any kind of turn at all.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Scientific projections indicate that Africa is disproportionately vulnerable to climate change, with water stress and agricultural yield collapses expected to manifest much sooner than other regions.
  2. 02Climate change acts as a catalyst for large-scale conflict and instability by driving mass migration and intensifying competition over dwindling natural resources.
  3. 03Proposed conflict prevention measures are insufficient because they address the symptoms rather than the root cause of environmental degradation.
  4. 04Major global emitters have a moral and financial obligation to implement steep, binding emissions reductions to protect the continent's future.
  5. 05Wealthy nations must provide massive, dedicated adaptation funding to Africa as a debt for the historical emissions that have caused the current environmental crisis.
