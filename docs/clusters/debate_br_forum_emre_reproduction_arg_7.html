Diversified Gemini

"Merve Emre’s claim that 'all reproduction is assisted' relies on a false equivalence that flattens the profound ethical and biological distinctions between physiological processes and invasive medical technologies."

Merve Emre’s "All Reproduction Is Assisted" offers a fascinating, if sometimes contradictory, exploration of how technology shapes the modern reproductive experience. Through a series of compelling case studies, Emre illustrates how assisted reproductive technology (ART) compresses and expands our experience of time, subjects the body to rigorous optimization, and forces us to confront the structural inequalities embedded in family building. Yet, for all its incisive observation, the essay ultimately stumbles into a philosophical trap of its own making. In attempting to dismantle the concept of "natural" reproduction by declaring that *all* reproduction is assisted, Emre stretches the definition of "assistance" so thin that it loses all analytical utility, flattening profound ethical and biological distinctions into a homogenous techno-optimism.

Emre’s rhetorical maneuver is clear: by aligning the social and economic conditions required for standard biological reproduction with the highly invasive medical procedures of IVF and egg freezing, she seeks to normalize the latter. "If you do not have to pay money to conceive, it may not occur to you that conception can be prohibitively costly," she writes. "If you do not have to transform your body to gestate, it may not occur to you that gestation is hard and risky work." Her goal is to generate solidarity, suggesting that the fertile, heterosexual couple and the single woman undergoing IVF are essentially engaged in the same mediated project.

But this is a false equivalence. There is a categorical, ontological difference between a biological process that occurs spontaneously within the human body (even if that body exists within a supportive or oppressive social matrix) and a process that requires the surgical extraction of gametes, fertilization in a laboratory petri dish, genetic screening, and mechanical transfer back into a chemically prepared uterus.

To point this out is not to engage in reactionary "hippie-Rousseauean" romanticism, nor is it an attack on the individuals who rely on ART. It is simply to acknowledge reality. When Emre writes that "all reproduction... is assisted," she conflates the *environment* of reproduction with the *mechanism* of reproduction.

This conflation allows Emre to bypass the immense ethical questions that arise specifically because of the *mechanism* of ART. When reproduction is moved outside the body, human life is instantly commodified in ways that spontaneous reproduction is not. Gametes are bought and sold. Embryos are graded on their morphology, genetically tested for viability and sex, and either frozen indefinitely, donated, or destroyed. The fertility industry operates on a model of quality control and planned obsolescence that is entirely alien to physiological reproduction.

Emre’s own reporting highlights this reality. S retrieves fifteen eggs as an "insurance policy." B pares down her embryos to the three that are "free of chromosomal abnormalities." K and N test their embryos and know the sex of the one remaining in the freezer. These are acts of curation and mastery. They represent a fundamental shift in the human relationship to the future, transforming the child from a subject of unconditional hospitality into an object of design and selection.

Emre acknowledges the physical and emotional toll this takes on her subjects, but she frames it largely as a problem of structural inequality and internalized stigma. "The 'unnaturalness' of your endeavor becomes a proxy for your subjecthood," she laments. But perhaps the alienation her subjects feel is not merely the result of societal prejudice. Perhaps it is a rational response to a deeply alienating process. When B looks at her body and feels that the hormones have pushed her into "alien territory," she is registering the profound dissonance of a technology that treats the female body as an extraction site.

By insisting that there is no meaningful distinction between the "natural" and the "technological," Emre forecloses our ability to critique the march of biomedical capitalism. If all reproduction is assisted, on what grounds do we object to the commercialization of surrogacy, where women in the Global South are paid to gestate embryos for wealthy Westerners? On what grounds do we object to the future of ectogenesis (artificial wombs), where human gestation is entirely outsourced to machines? If we accept Emre’s premise, these are merely different points on the same spectrum of "assistance."

We can and must fight for a society that supports all families, regardless of how they are formed. We must dismantle the stigma faced by queer, single, and disabled parents. But we do not achieve this by erasing the boundary between human biology and industrial technology. Recognizing the unique vulnerability, mystery, and un-engineered reality of physiological reproduction is not an act of exclusion; it is a necessary bulwark against a future where human life is entirely subsumed by the logic of the marketplace and the laboratory. We need a reproductive politics that is brave enough to say: technology can be a life-saving tool, but it is not our nature, and it should not be our master.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Emre conflates the social environment of reproduction with the physical mechanism of reproduction, rendering the term 'assistance' analytically useless.
  2. 02The essay ignores the unique commodification of human life inherent in ART, such as the grading, genetic screening, and quality control of embryos.
  3. 03The alienation experienced by women undergoing ART is a rational response to a process that treats the body as an extraction site, rather than merely a result of social stigma.
  4. 04Erasing the boundary between biology and technology forecloses the ability to critique the expansion of biomedical capitalism and the potential exploitation of surrogacy or ectogenesis.
  5. 05A truly emancipatory reproductive politics must recognize the un-engineered reality of physiological reproduction as a bulwark against the total marketization of human life.
Diversified Gemini

"Merve Emre’s attempt to redefine all reproduction as 'assisted' is a reductive category error that flattens the sacred, biological reality of life into a transactional, technological process."

In “All Reproduction Is Assisted,” Merve Emre offers a deeply reported, sympathetic look at the physical and emotional labyrinths of assisted reproductive technology (ART). Emre’s core argument—that we must jettison the concept of "natural" reproduction because it excludes marginalized groups and obscures the "assistance" all families need—is emblematic of a modern progressive discourse that seeks to level all human experience through the lens of power and privilege. While Emre’s compassion for her subjects is evident, her ultimate conclusion represents a profound reduction of the human experience. In attempting to stretch the word "assisted" to cover everything from a supportive community to a laboratory petri dish, Emre does not elevate ART; rather, she flattens the sacred, mysterious, and uniquely human reality of biological life into a mere transactional process.

Emre’s essay is driven by the premise that the desire to reproduce is a "fantasy" and that the "natural" is simply an ideology constructed to oppress those who cannot easily conceive. She dismisses the natural childbirth movement as a consumerist farce, mocking women who seek out physiological birth as engaging in an aesthetic lifestyle choice. In doing so, she betrays a fundamental misunderstanding of what the "natural" actually signifies in the context of human reproduction.

The "natural" is not merely about organic prenatal vitamins or water births. It is an acknowledgment that human beings are mammals, deeply embedded in an ecological and biological order that precedes technology, politics, and capitalism. Physiological reproduction—conception through the union of two bodies, the silent, un-engineered growth of a child within the womb, and the physiological cascade of birth—is a profound encounter with the givenness of life. It requires a surrender to forces beyond our conscious control.

When Emre insists that "all reproduction... is assisted," she commits a category error. To equate the social and economic conditions that support a mother (paid leave, a stable home, medical care during complications) with the biomedical engineering required for IVF (extracting gametes, genetically screening embryos, mechanically transferring them) is to equate the soil that helps a seed grow with the genetic modification of a laboratory crop. Both involve human intervention, but the nature of the relationship to the life being formed is fundamentally different.

The danger of Emre’s techno-utopian vision, where ART becomes the "preferred strategy" for feminist solidarity, is most apparent in her descriptions of the procedures themselves. Her subjects are trapped in a paradigm of optimization and control. S speaks of her frozen eggs as an "insurance policy." B genetically screens her embryos, keeping the "normal" ones and discarding the rest, proud of her "two girls and a boy" waiting in a freezer. K and N navigate the sterile, alienated environment of the clinic, trying to force their biology to conform to their desires.

Emre views the suffering in these stories primarily as the result of a society that has not yet normalized these procedures. But what if the suffering, the profound sense of "alien territory" that B experiences, is actually the soul’s reaction to the deep unnaturalness of the process? When we turn the creation of human life into a manufacturing process—subject to genetic screening, quality control, and financial transaction—we fundamentally alter the relationship between parent and child. The child is no longer a gift to be received, but a project to be managed.

Furthermore, Emre’s vision relies on a profound severing of the child from its biological origins. The use of anonymous donor sperm, donor eggs, and surrogates (which Emre mentions only in passing) intentionally fractures the biological identity of the child to fulfill the desires of the adults. We are currently seeing the first generations of donor-conceived children come of age, and many are speaking out about the profound trauma of genealogical bewilderment, of being intentionally denied access to half of their biological identity because they were purchased to complete a family. Emre’s framework of reproductive justice entirely centers the desires of the adults, treating the resulting child simply as the end product of a successful technological intervention.

We must, of course, treat everyone struggling with infertility, as well as queer and single people, with the utmost compassion. The desire for a child is one of the deepest human longings. But true compassion does not require us to pretend that a laboratory is a womb, or that a genetic screen is no different than a mother's embrace.

Emre wishes to close the gap between women by making technology the great equalizer. But true solidarity cannot be found in the sterile, commodified realm of the fertility clinic. It must be found in a renewed respect for the limits of our biology, an acceptance of our vulnerabilities, and a recognition that some aspects of the human experience should remain beyond the reach of our engineering. To defend the "natural" is not an act of exclusion; it is a defense of our shared humanity against the encroachment of a machine age that knows the price of every embryo, but the inherent value of none.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Equating social support systems with biomedical engineering ignores the fundamental difference between supporting a natural process and manufacturing life in a laboratory.
  2. 02The 'natural' in reproduction represents a vital acknowledgment of human beings as biological creatures embedded in an ecological order that precedes political or technological control.
  3. 03The suffering and alienation experienced by those undergoing ART may be a legitimate reaction to the 'unnaturalness' of the process rather than merely a result of social stigma.
  4. 04Turning the creation of life into a managed project of optimization and genetic screening fundamentally alters the parent-child relationship from one of receiving a gift to one of quality control.
  5. 05Emre’s framework focuses exclusively on adult desires while ignoring the potential trauma and 'genealogical bewilderment' experienced by children whose biological identities are fractured by these technologies.
  6. 06True solidarity and compassion should be found in respecting biological limits and shared humanity rather than seeking equality through the commodified and sterile realm of the fertility clinic.
