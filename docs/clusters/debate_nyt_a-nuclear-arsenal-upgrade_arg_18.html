"The United States should reject the expansion of its nuclear capabilities and instead modernize its policy by reducing the role of nuclear weapons in its national security strategy."

Calling a trillion-dollar nuclear rebuilding program “modernization” makes it sound sanitary, almost bureaucratic. But the object being modernized is the capacity to incinerate cities, poison land and water, and end organized human life on a scale no other weapon can approach. The United States should not modernize its nuclear arsenal in ways that entrench nuclear weapons for another half-century. It should modernize its policy by moving away from them.

The case for new, smaller nuclear weapons is especially dangerous. Their advocates say they are more credible because they are more precise and less destructive. That is precisely the problem. The world has avoided nuclear war in part because leaders understand that nuclear use is not a normal military option. If Washington builds weapons that appear more “thinkable,” other states will draw the obvious conclusion: nuclear weapons are not just for deterrence; they are for fighting wars.

An appropriate modernization should include safety improvements, dismantlement capacity, secure storage of fissile material and cleanup of the nuclear weapons complex. It should exclude new delivery systems, new warhead capabilities and new rationales for first use. The United States should remove weapons from hair-trigger alert, adopt a no-first-use policy, ratify the Comprehensive Nuclear-Test-Ban Treaty and pursue verifiable reductions beyond New START. These steps would not abolish nuclear danger overnight, but they would reduce the chances of accident, miscalculation and arms racing.

The usual reply is that Russia, China and North Korea will not be moved by American restraint. Perhaps not immediately. But U.S. behavior shapes the nonproliferation system. When Washington insists that nuclear weapons are indispensable forever, it weakens its own argument that others must not acquire them. The nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty rests on a bargain: non-nuclear states forgo the bomb, and nuclear states pursue disarmament. A vast modernization program looks like bad faith.

Deterrence is not made safer by multiplying options for nuclear use. It is made safer by reducing the role of nuclear weapons, strengthening diplomacy, preventing proliferation and making accidental launch less likely. The United States can maintain security during a transition to lower numbers. What it should not do is spend a trillion dollars teaching the next generation that nuclear weapons are permanent instruments of statecraft.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Developing smaller, more precise nuclear weapons is dangerous because it makes nuclear war appear more 'thinkable' and usable rather than a tool of last resort.
  2. 02Modernization efforts should prioritize safety, dismantlement, and secure storage rather than the creation of new delivery systems or warhead capabilities.
  3. 03Adopting a no-first-use policy and removing weapons from hair-trigger alert would significantly reduce the risks of accident, miscalculation, and arms racing.
  4. 04A massive nuclear rebuilding program undermines U.S. credibility in nonproliferation efforts and violates the spirit of the nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty.
  5. 05True deterrence is achieved through diplomacy and the reduction of nuclear options rather than by treating nuclear weapons as permanent instruments of statecraft.
