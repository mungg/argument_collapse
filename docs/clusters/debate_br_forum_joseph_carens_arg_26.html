"While Joseph Carens correctly identifies that social membership creates a moral right to amnesty, his proposed five-to-seven-year residency threshold is too long and should be significantly shortened to reflect the rapid pace of social integration and the state's complicity in irregular migration."

Joseph Carens’s argument for amnesty is a rare achievement: it sets out a moral case with such clarity that it forces even the most hardened restrictionist to confront the human reality behind immigration statistics. I find myself in full agreement with its central thesis—that the passage of time transforms an irregular migrant into a member of society, and that this membership eventually generates a moral right to legal residence. Indeed, I believe Carens’s own conclusions do not go far enough. If we take social membership seriously, the threshold for legalization should be even lower than the five to seven years he proposes, and the state’s complicity in creating the conditions for irregular migration demands an even more robust response.

Carens is right that time matters profoundly. The story of Marguerite Grimmond, who lived in Scotland for 74 years without legal status, illuminates the absurdity of treating all irregular presence as equally deportable. But Carens’s examples of 15 or 20 years make the case too easy. His own principle—that social membership is built through living, working, and forming relationships—applies to people who have been in a country for a much shorter period. A family that has settled in a community for three years, whose children attend local schools, whose parents hold steady jobs, is already embedded in the fabric of daily life. To deport them after three years is to rip out those roots; the harm is real even if the roots are less deep. If we accept, as I think we should, that the state’s interest in enforcing immigration law diminishes over time, then the moment at which that interest becomes outweighed by the migrant’s claim to stay arrives sooner than Carens suggests.

The statute-of-limitations analogy Carens invokes offers a useful benchmark. Most jurisdictions allow three to five years for noncapital crimes. Immigration violations are, as he notes, more like administrative infractions than serious crimes. So why not recognize that after, say, three years of continuous residence without criminal conduct, an irregular migrant should be entitled to legal status? This is not to deny the state’s right to control borders; it is simply to acknowledge that the enforcement of those borders must have temporal limits if it is not to become inhumane. A three-year rule would still give the state ample opportunity to identify and deport those who have recently arrived. And it would provide the kind of predictability that irregular migrants and their families desperately need.

The complicity argument, too, supports a more generous timeline. Carens rightly warns against overusing complicity, but I think he understates the degree to which the U.S. economy relies on a vulnerable, undocumented workforce. It is naive to imagine that the presence of 11 million irregular migrants is an accident of inadequate enforcement. Sectors such as agriculture, construction, hospitality, and domestic work have depended for decades on workers who are easily exploited because they fear deportation. The state has not only failed to enforce employer sanctions in any serious way; it has actively designed immigration quotas that leave no legal pathway for low-skilled workers who are demonstrably in demand. This is complicity, not mere failure of enforcement. If the state bears responsibility for creating a shadow economy of labor, it cannot then claim the moral high ground in deporting the very people who have been invited, however informally, to fill that labor demand. Acknowledging this responsibility pushes us toward a more generous recognition of irregular migrants’ claims—perhaps a universal amnesty for all those present in the country as of a certain date, regardless of length of residence, coupled with a fundamental overhaul of immigration law to prevent future exploitation.

Carens is also correct that the fairness-to-those-in-line argument is largely a myth. For a poor agricultural worker from Mexico, there is no line to wait in. The U.S. immigration system offers virtually no visas for unskilled workers without close family ties. This structural exclusion makes it disingenuous to claim that irregular migrants have jumped the queue. A real queue would include a meaningful temporary worker program and expanded family reunification channels. In the absence of such reforms, amnesty is not a reward for line-jumping; it is a necessary corrective to a system that is deliberately designed to provide cheap labor without legal protection.

Finally, I want to stress the human costs of delayed amnesty. Miguel Sanchez’s story is one of millions, but it is not only the fear of deportation that marks these lives. It is also the inability to report crimes, to seek medical care without anxiety, to travel to one’s own child’s school play without risking disaster. The longer we delay granting legal status, the more we compound these harms. Carens’s argument, with its five-to-seven-year horizon, suggests a kind of probation. But why should we impose probation on people who are already working, paying taxes, and contributing to their communities simply because they entered without authorization? The moral momentum of Carens’s own premises points toward legalization as soon as possible, not after an extended waiting period. A society that values human dignity and recognizes de facto membership should do no less.

I part with Carens, then, not in rejecting his reasoning but in finding it too timid. The logic of social membership, if followed honestly, demands a lower threshold for amnesty and a more forthright acknowledgment of the state’s own role in creating the irregular migrant population. Only then can we begin to talk seriously about rebuilding an immigration system that is both lawful and just.
