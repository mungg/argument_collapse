"While experimentalist governance offers a sophisticated framework for climate action in advanced economies, it is fundamentally flawed as a global solution because it ignores the lack of state capacity and the urgent development needs of the Global South."

The essay by David Victor and Charles Sabel arrives as a provocation at a moment when the conversation about climate change is poised for a radical turn. For decades, the center of gravity in climate policy has been the dreary, diplomatically intricate world of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC). That world has produced a string of agreements that, as the authors rightly note, have coincided with a two-thirds rise in global emissions since 1990. Their analysis, however, is not another autopsy of failure; it is a field guide to a hidden landscape of success. The Montreal Protocol, the California Air Resources Board, the EU Water Framework Directive—these are the ruins from which they propose to build a new temple. Their experimentalist governance framework, with its interlocking cycles of local innovation and central review under penalty defaults, is a serious intellectual achievement. Yet for an argument so rich in practical detail, it carries an eerie silence at its heart: it has no answer for the vast, unequal world beyond the boundaries of the advanced industrial economies that produced its exemplary cases. The model of experimentalism, as presented, is a tool for the capable, built on the assumption of a strong state and a thick infrastructure of regulatory capacity. It risks becoming a blueprint for a Green Aristocracy unless it is fundamentally reimagined for the Global South, where emissions are rising fastest and state capacity is often thinnest.

The foundational case for experimentalist governance, the Montreal Protocol, worked because the negotiating table was set by rich countries for rich countries. The key actors were a handful of chemical companies, mostly American and European, and the regulatory agencies that oversaw them. When developing countries were later brought into the fold, the terms of their participation were underwritten by the Multilateral Fund, a mechanism that paid for the incremental costs of compliance. This was a simple transaction: North paid South to use different chemicals, and a small, dedicated fund oversaw the transfer. But the climate problem cannot be reduced to a transaction. Decarbonising the economy of India, Nigeria, or Indonesia is not a matter of paying the incremental cost of swapping out one industrial input for another. It involves a wholesale transformation of energy systems, land use, transportation networks, and urban design at a moment when these countries are still building the bulk of their infrastructure and lifting hundreds of millions out of poverty. The state capacity required to coordinate the “local problem-solving” Victor and Sabel extol, let alone to monitor peers in a system of intrusive review, is precisely what is lacking in many parts of the world. Experimentalism demands regulators who can sit at the table with industry and civil society, assess technological alternatives, and credibly threaten laggards with market exclusion. But what happens when the regulator is understaffed, politically captured, or simply ignored by informal economies that dwarf the formal sector?

The authors gesture at hybrid cases like forestry and palm oil, where international standards attempt to govern local production. Their honesty about this sector is commendable but devastating to their thesis. “Progress here is slow,” they write, “but it continues.” Slow progress is not a bug to be ironed out with better peer review. It is a feature of a global economic system in which the demand for palm oil and cheap beef creates an overwhelming incentive for deforestation, and in which the state agencies charged with arresting that deforestation are often complicit in it. The global supply chain that links a Brazilian cattle rancher or an Indonesian palm oil smallholder to a European supermarket is not a partnership in experimental learning. It is a power relation, deeply asymmetrical and frequently extractive, in which Northern buyers demand sustainability certification while paying prices that make the required investments economically impossible for small producers. The “penalty default” in this relationship—exclusion from the European market—is a nuclear option that can destroy rural livelihoods without building anything in their place. Unless the experimentalist framework can specify how the risks and costs of the transition are shared globally, not just managed procedurally, it will replicate the North-South dynamic that has poisoned climate diplomacy from the start.

What the Global South needs is not a procedural innovation but a material one. Victor and Sabel nod to the Green New Deal as “strong on mandates and vision and often silent on governance.” Their critique is useful, but it overlooks the central insight of the Green New Deal concept: the decarbonisation and the development agenda must be the same thing. A global experimentalism worth its name would start not with the question of how to set emission standards for globalized industries like steel and shipping, but with how to massively channel public and private capital into building zero-carbon energy systems in the places that will add the equivalent of a new city the size of Lagos every month for the next several decades. The Montreal Protocol’s Multilateral Fund was an afterthought, a lubricant for diplomatic consent. In the climate context, the fund must be the centerpiece, a global public investment vehicle on the scale of the Marshall Plan, designed to build the very regulatory and technical capacity that experimentalism presupposes.

The authors are, I suspect, too sanguine about the politics of their own proposal. The kind of sectoral bargaining they celebrate in the International Maritime Organization or the EU Water Framework Directive depends on a dense fabric of trust and institutional memory. It assumes that regulators and firms can meet repeatedly, share proprietary information, and hold each other accountable without the process collapsing into common lobbying or outright corruption. This is hard enough to achieve in Brussels or Sacramento. It is a fantasy in settings where large corporations effectively own the state, or where the state itself is a major owner of the fossil fuel assets that must be dismantled. In petrostates like Saudi Arabia or Russia, experimentalist governance as a framework for decarbonization faces an ontological obstacle: the government is not a neutral convener of sectoral committees but the principal beneficiary of the carbon economy. In such polities, the penalty default of market exclusion is an act of economic warfare, not a nudge toward cooperation.

It would be a mistake, then, to read the Victor and Sabel essay as a global prescription. It is more accurately an insightful description of what is possible within the club of highly institutionalized, capable states that already share a degree of political trust. The mistake lies in universalizing this model to a world in which most people live under political conditions that look nothing like Denmark’s or California’s. If the experimentalist agenda is to be more than a technocratic retreat from the messiness of global justice, it must be turned on its head. The first experiment must be in building capacity itself, in creating the institutions of planning, regulation, and social insurance that make transition politically feasible in the Global South. That requires a transfer of resources so large, and a sharing of technological knowledge so open, that it makes the current climate finance commitments look like the faint stirrings of a conscience not yet awake. The authors have shown us a way to walk when the path is visible and the company trustworthy. They have not yet told us how to walk through a swamp in the dark with partners we have historically betrayed. That is the true geography of the climate crisis, and any map that does not chart it is a map of a more comfortable but less relevant world.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The model assumes a high level of regulatory infrastructure and state capacity that is absent in many developing nations where emissions are rising fastest.
  2. 02Unlike the simple chemical swaps of the Montreal Protocol, decarbonizing the Global South requires a total economic transformation that cannot be managed through mere procedural experimentation.
  3. 03In many developing regions, 'penalty defaults' like market exclusion act as extractive power plays that destroy livelihoods rather than fostering collaborative learning.
  4. 04The essay's reliance on sectoral bargaining fails to account for petrostates and regions where political capture and corruption make neutral regulatory oversight impossible.
  5. 05To be viable globally, experimentalism must be replaced by a massive, Marshall Plan-scale transfer of material resources and technology to build the capacity it currently takes for granted.
