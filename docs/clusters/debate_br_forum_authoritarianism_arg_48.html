"The Democratic Party cannot defeat authoritarianism through empirical reframing or anti-corruption messaging alone because it fails to address the deep-seated cultural and moral alienation of ordinary Americans."

Bonica and Grumbach mount an extensive empirical case against moderation and popularism, but they are curiously silent on the elephant in the room: the pervasive cultural and moral alienation that drives significant swaths of the country toward authoritarianism. Their anti-corruption frame, while clever, is a classic liberal move—it diagnoses the malady as a failure of process and elite rent-seeking, when the larger wound is the collapse of meaning, belonging, and moral order in the lives of ordinary Americans. Until Democrats can speak to that wound without contempt, no reframe will stop the authoritarian advance.

The essay correctly notes that many voters who appear moderate hold extreme, contradictory positions. That insight should lead to a deeper inquiry: why do people who want to ban abortion also want to confiscate billionaire wealth? Because they are not ideological in the sense that political scientists assume. They are operating with a different moral grammar, one in which the world has become disordered: families are breaking down, communities are hollowing out, elites are indifferent, and the old verities have been replaced by a cultural elite that preaches tolerance but practices disdain. Trumpism, for all its grotesquerie, is to millions of people a revolt against that disorder—a promise to restore a world in which there were clear rules, respected authorities, and stable identities.

The authors dismiss this as a reactionary impulse to be out-organized or reframed. But you cannot out-organize a crisis of meaning. The modern Democratic Party is inextricably associated with the forces that many Americans see as destroying their way of life: relentless cultural revolution, a globalized economy that treats communities as disposable, and a meritocratic ideology that tells them they are losers who deserve their fate. The party’s standard response—more economic redistribution, better messaging—fails to engage with the moral and spiritual dimensions of the crisis. As the social psychologist Jonathan Haidt and others have argued, people are driven by sacred values, not just material interests. When you insult those sacred values, no amount of free college will win their vote.

The anti-corruption frame might seem to sidestep the culture war by focusing on a procedural evil everyone hates. But it doesn’t. For many culturally conservative voters, the “corrupt” elite isn’t just a stock-trading congressman; it’s the entire cultural apparatus—universities, Hollywood, the mainstream media, the tech giants—that they believe is hostile to their faith, their families, and their patriotism. To them, the Democratic Party is the political arm of that apparatus. An anti-corruption message that doesn’t grapple with the party’s deep cultural bonds to those institutions will sound like more of the same: a party of affluent, secular cosmopolitans telling the heartland to trust them to clean up the mess they created.

What would it take for Democrats to authentically address this cultural chasm? It would require a genuine reckoning with the party’s own class and cultural biases. The authors’ call for “authentic, working-class candidates” is a start, but authenticity isn’t just about occupation; it’s about values. A nurse who repeats the party’s standard line on gender identity, defunding the police, and unrestricted abortion is not going to be heard as authentic by many working-class voters, regardless of her résumé. The party would need to allow candidates in conservative areas to dissent from national orthodoxy on cultural issues without being ostracized. It would need to embrace a pluralism that includes cultural conservatives as well as progressives, and to articulate a vision of a common good that values stable families, local community, and the dignity of work, not just individual autonomy.

This is not a call for moderation in the old sense. It’s a call for a different kind of politics altogether—one that takes the moral concerns of ordinary people seriously, even when those concerns are messy, inconsistent, or shaped by institutions the left dislikes, such as religion. The anti-corruption frame could be part of this, but only if it is tethered to a broader story about rebuilding a moral economy: one in which work is honored, families can flourish, communities are safe, and the powerful are held accountable, but also one in which the cultural and moral foundations of a decent society are protected. This would mean, for example, championing family policy that is universal (e.g., a child allowance) while also respecting those who believe marriage is between a man and a woman, without embracing bigotry. It would mean defending law enforcement reform while also supporting police and public safety unequivocally. It would mean welcoming immigrants while also enforcing borders. These are not contradictions; they are the balancing acts of a pluralistic democracy.

The essay’s failure to wrestle with the cultural dimension leads it, ironically, into the very trap it warns against: an obsession with polling and issues at the expense of leadership. The authors denounce popularism, but then propose a new issue (corruption) that happens to poll very well. They treat voters as consumers, just with a different shopping list. They don’t ask what it would mean to *lead* people out of the moral and spiritual cul-de-sac that makes authoritarianism attractive. Leadership in such a moment requires acknowledging the legitimate grievances that drive people to embrace strongmen: not just economic anxiety but the sense of cultural displacement, the loneliness, the feeling that the country they grew up in is vanishing and nobody in power seems to care. A leader who can do that without fueling xenophobia or cruelty is a rare thing, but that is the kind of leader the country needs.

In the end, the Democratic Party faces a choice not between message frames but between two visions of its role: as a coalition of interest groups seeking to outvote the other side, or as a national movement that seeks to heal a broken country. The latter requires a humility and a breadth that are currently in short supply. The authors are right that the old rules are dead. The new rule might be that you cannot defeat authoritarianism without first renewing the cultural and moral bonds that authoritarianism feeds on. That is a tall order, but nothing less will do.
