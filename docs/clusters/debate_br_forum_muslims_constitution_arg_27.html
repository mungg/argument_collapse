# The Global Dimension Cannot Be An Afterthought

Akbar and Theoharis acknowledge in their introduction that they are "focused largely on the domestic War on Terror" and only "briefly touching on the interconnected global dimension." This is a significant limitation. The domestic and foreign dimensions of the War on Terror are not separate phenomena; they are two facets of a single imperial project. Any serious analysis of Islamophobia in America must begin with the global role of the United States, or it will miss the root cause of the problem.

The authors note that the United States has bombed six of the seven countries listed in Trump's ban, dropping more than 23,000 bombs on Muslim-majority countries in 2015 alone. This is a striking statistic, but it is presented almost as an aside. It deserves far more attention. The United States has been at war continuously for nearly seventeen years, primarily in Muslim-majority countries. It has killed hundreds of thousands of people, displaced millions, destroyed infrastructure, and created the very conditions of instability and desperation that feed terrorist recruitment.

The domestic surveillance and policing of American Muslims is not separate from this global war. It is an extension of it. The government does not simply fear Muslim terrorism; it fears the political consequences of its own actions. American Muslims are treated as suspect populations partly because of genuine bigoted prejudice, but also because they are potential critics of U.S. foreign policy. The FBI's focus on "anti-American" sentiment is not incidental; it is central. The government wants Muslim communities to be compliant, to support American wars, and to suppress any dissent.

This analysis has implications for resistance. The authors call for scrutinizing domestic counterterrorism policy and pushing courts to protect civil liberties. But this is insufficient. We also need to confront the global dimension of American imperialism. The airport protests were inspiring, but they did not challenge the underlying policies that make American Muslims suspect. They did not demand an end to the wars, the drone strikes, or the assassination programs. They did not demand that the United States stop bombing Muslim-majority countries and start taking responsibility for the refugee crisis it has helped create.

This is not to dismiss the domestic struggle. Domestic policies matter, and they can be changed even without a fundamental shift in foreign policy. But the two struggles must be linked. Any serious coalition against Islamophobia must also be a coalition against American imperialism. It must demand that the United States stop intervening in Muslim-majority countries, close its foreign bases, end the drone wars, and accept the political costs of a more modest foreign policy.

The authors write that "the domestic and global wars on terror are intimately connected." I agree. But they do not draw out the full implications of this connection. If we take it seriously, we must recognize that opposing Islamophobia means opposing the entire architecture of American empire, not just its domestic manifestations. This is a tall order, but it is the only analysis that can get us to the root of the problem.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Domestic surveillance and policing of Muslims function as an extension of global warfare designed to suppress dissent against U.S. foreign policy.
  2. 02The root cause of Islamophobia and the suspicion of Muslim populations is the instability and desperation created by decades of U.S. military intervention in Muslim-majority countries.
  3. 03Focusing primarily on domestic civil liberties and court challenges fails to address the underlying imperial project that makes Muslims suspect in the first place.
  4. 04A meaningful resistance must demand an end to drone strikes, assassinations, and foreign military presence rather than just protesting domestic policy manifestations like travel bans.
