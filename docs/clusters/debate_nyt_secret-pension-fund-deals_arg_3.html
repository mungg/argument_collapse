Diversified Claude

"While transparency in pension fund investments is a necessary hygiene measure, it fails to address the root cause of the crisis: the structural insolvency created by politicians making unfunded promises and using unrealistic accounting."

The Problem Isn't Secrecy. It's the Promises.

The transparency debate around public pension investments, while not unimportant, is a sideshow that lets the real culprits off the hook. State and local pension funds did not start chasing hedge funds and private equity because trustees suddenly developed exotic tastes. They did it because legislators and governors, over decades, promised retirement benefits they had no intention of funding honestly, and assumed rates of return high enough to paper over the gap on paper.

Look at the arithmetic. Across the fifty states, the unfunded liability is estimated at somewhere between $1 trillion (using the funds' own assumptions) and $3-plus trillion (using risk-free discount rates favored by financial economists). New Jersey has skipped or shorted its pension contributions almost every year since the Whitman administration. Illinois has effectively run its pension system as a credit card for general operating expenses. Kentucky, Connecticut, and Rhode Island are not far behind. When you are this far underwater and you cannot raise taxes or cut benefits politically, the only remaining lever is to bet the portfolio. Hence the rush into "alternatives."

Once you understand that, the transparency conversation looks different. Yes, the secrecy around alternative investment terms hides excessive fees, mediocre returns, and sometimes outright corruption. All of those should be exposed. But even if every fee were posted on a public website tomorrow, the underlying problem — that pensions are structurally insolvent and management has been pushed into Hail Mary investing — would remain. You can disclose your way to a fairer pricing of hedge fund services. You cannot disclose your way out of a $1 trillion funding hole.

There is also a perverse dynamic at work. Greater transparency, by exposing how lousy a deal alternatives often are, will likely accelerate calls to abandon those strategies. Fine. But then trustees still have to hit 7.5 percent somehow. If they retreat to bonds, the funding gap widens immediately on an actuarial basis, forcing larger contributions from strapped state budgets or, more likely, benefit cuts for retirees. The political class would much rather have an opaque hedge fund to blame than admit the math.

So by all means, end the gag orders, ban placement agent payola, publish the fees. But understand that transparency is a hygiene measure, not a solution. The real reform agenda is honest discount rates, ironclad funding requirements that politicians cannot skip, and an adult conversation with public workers about what their plans can actually deliver. Until that happens, we will keep arguing about the wrapping paper while the package rots.

Diversified Gemini

"While transparency in pension funds is important, the industry cannot be truly reformed until politicians abandon unrealistic rate-of-return assumptions that force funds into risky, opaque investments."

Focusing solely on the secrecy and fees of hedge funds in the state pension industry is like complaining about the cost of bandages while ignoring a severed artery. Yes, the lack of transparency in alternative investments is deeply troubling. But transparency alone will not fix the public pension crisis, because secrecy is merely a symptom of a much deeper, systemic disease: the mathematically impossible rate-of-return assumptions that politicians refuse to abandon.

Across the United States, public pension funds operate on the assumption that they will earn an annualized return of 7.5 to 8 percent on their portfolios. In the late 1990s, when interest rates were higher and equity markets were roaring, this was plausible. Today, in 2014, with bond yields hovering near historic lows, an 8 percent guaranteed return is a fairy tale.

Yet, acknowledging that a realistic return is closer to 4 or 5 percent is political suicide. Lowering the discount rate would immediately expose massive unfunded liabilities, requiring state and local governments to drastically increase their pension contributions. This would mean cutting current public services or raising taxes—actions politicians desperately want to avoid.

So, rather than face reality, pension boards have chosen to "reach for yield." They have systematically shifted billions of dollars out of transparent, low-risk public markets and into highly opaque, high-risk alternative investments like hedge funds, private equity, and real estate derivatives. They tolerate the strict confidentiality agreements, the high fees, and the political cronyism because these fund managers are the only ones willing to project the 8 percent returns the politicians need to balance their actuarial spreadsheets.

If we force complete transparency tomorrow, what changes? The public will see the exorbitant fees, and perhaps some of the most egregious pay-to-play deals will be halted. But the underlying math remains broken. Unless the required rate of return is lowered, pension managers will simply find new, slightly more transparent ways to take on excessive risk.

True reform of the state pension industry requires more than just opening the books on alternative investments. It requires an honest accounting of our liabilities. We must mandate that pension funds use realistic, conservative discount rates tied to municipal bond yields. Once the desperate need to chase an impossible 8 percent return is removed, the reliance on high-fee, secret hedge funds will naturally evaporate. Transparency is a worthy goal, but honest math is the only salvation.

  4. 04Transparency alone will not solve the crisis because pension managers will continue to take excessive risks as long as the underlying return requirements remain broken.
