Diversified Gemini

"Student protests against commencement speakers and honorary degrees are not acts of censorship, but rather a form of democratic accountability and active participation in the exchange of ideas."

Critics of this spring’s campus protests are quick to cry censorship, but they fundamentally misunderstand both the nature of the protests and the definition of free speech. When students at Rutgers objected to Condoleezza Rice, or when students at Smith College protested Christine Lagarde, they were not shutting down the exchange of ideas. They were actively participating in it.

There is a vast difference between inviting a scholar to debate a contentious issue on campus and awarding an honorary degree to a powerful political figure. A commencement address is not a rigorous academic seminar; it is a celebratory monologue, accompanied by an institution’s highest honor. Protesting the decision to bestow such honors on the architects of the Iraq War or the leaders of the International Monetary Fund is an act of democratic accountability. It is student speech answering institutional speech.

Furthermore, framing these students as "intolerant" ignores the power dynamics at play. The figures being protested already possess massive global platforms, wealth, and influence. A group of undergraduates successfully pressuring their administration to rescind an honorary degree does not deprive these elites of their ability to speak, publish, or shape policy.

To suggest that students should sit quietly and applaud individuals whose policies they find morally repugnant is the true demand for orthodoxy. The expectation of polite deference to authority figures is a conservative standard that universities should not enforce. By organizing, debating, and making their voices heard, these students are demonstrating exactly the kind of critical thinking and civic engagement that a university education is supposed to instill. They are not stifling thought; they are proving that they are paying attention.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Commencement addresses are celebratory honors rather than academic seminars, making protests against them a legitimate response to institutional speech.
  2. 02Protesting powerful global figures does not deprive them of their influence or ability to speak, as they already possess massive public platforms.
  3. 03Demanding that students remain silent and deferential to authority figures is a form of enforced orthodoxy that contradicts the goals of a university education.
  4. 04Organizing and debating against university decisions demonstrates the critical thinking and civic engagement that higher education is intended to foster.
