"While economics is an essential tool for solving modern crises, the discipline must move beyond technical refinements of neoliberalism to become a more humble, democratic, and institutionally literate craft that explicitly addresses power and social values."

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman are right about two crucial things. First, neoliberalism should not be confused with economics as such. The discipline has always contained more than one politics, and its best recent work has complicated, not vindicated, market fundamentalism. Second, the country’s most urgent problems—stagnant wages, racial wealth gaps, insecure work, climate change, financial fragility, declining life expectancy—cannot be solved without careful attention to incentives, institutions, empirical evidence, and tradeoffs. A politics of inclusive prosperity that refuses economics altogether will be weaker for it.

But the authors’ defense of economics is also a little too easy. It is not enough to say that neoliberalism was a “primitive, simplistic perversion” of modern economics. Perversions do not become governing common sense by accident. For decades, highly credentialed economists helped provide neoliberalism with its authority, vocabulary, and prestige. They did not merely fail to correct politicians who misused their ideas. They often supplied the models, metaphors, and policy rankings that made deregulation, tax cutting, privatization, trade liberalization, and austerity appear not just contestable, but scientific.

This matters because the public distrust of economics is not based only on misunderstanding. It is based on experience. Many people were told that trade agreements would raise overall welfare and that the losers could be compensated later. The first part was emphasized; the second was deferred or ignored. Workers were told that unions were labor-market distortions, that minimum wages priced the least skilled out of jobs, and that financial innovation allocated risk efficiently. Then came deindustrialization in many regions, the weakening of organized labor, a financial crisis caused in part by opaque and overleveraged institutions, and a recovery in which many gains accrued to asset holders. It is understandable that citizens suspect the discipline’s caveats arrive after the damage is done.

The lead essay recognizes some of this, especially the tendency of economists to reason in partial-equilibrium terms and then leave institutional repair to someone else. But that habit is not a minor professional tic. It is one of the main ways economics becomes political while pretending not to be. To say “free trade increases aggregate income” while treating compensation as a separate administrative detail is already to make a political judgment about whose losses count, when they count, and how credible future redistribution is likely to be. To say “carbon pricing is the efficient climate policy” while paying insufficient attention to regional dependence on fossil-fuel employment, distrust of government, and the political power of carbon-intensive industries is to risk designing a beautiful policy with no constituency.

The authors’ emphasis on second-best reasoning is therefore welcome. Yet the second-best world is not only a world of market failures. It is a world of state weakness, racial hierarchy, organized business power, constitutional veto points, and deep social distrust. Economists have become much better at estimating causal effects under specific conditions. They are less consistently good at explaining whether a policy can survive the institutions that must enact it. Evidence that a wage board could reduce inequality is important. So is the question of how wage boards would be staffed, how they would avoid capture, how they would interact with immigration status and fissured workplaces, and whether courts would tolerate them. These are not objections to the policy. They are part of the policy.

For that reason, “economics after neoliberalism” must be more than better economics. It must be more democratic economics. Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman rightly stress that economics can clarify tradeoffs but cannot provide distributional weights. That concession should be pushed further. If the weights come from democratic politics, then economists should be careful not to smuggle their own weights back in under the language of efficiency. Cost-benefit analysis, for example, can illuminate choices, but it can also obscure moral judgments by translating everything into willingness to pay, a measure already shaped by unequal income. A poor person’s need and a rich person’s preference are not morally equivalent simply because the latter commands more dollars.

The same point applies to “inclusive prosperity.” The phrase is attractive, but it risks treating inclusion as a distributive add-on to an otherwise familiar growth project. Many citizens want higher wages and better public services, but they also want dignity, voice, stability, and control over the conditions of their lives. A laid-off worker does not experience job loss only as a decline in lifetime consumption. A community that loses an industry loses institutions, identity, tax capacity, and intergenerational expectation. These losses are hard to capture in the standard language of welfare, but they are politically explosive. If economics is to serve democracy, it must learn to hear such claims before translating them into its own categories.

This is especially important for race. The lead essay mentions racial disparities at the outset, but the policy architecture it summarizes does not place racial domination at the center. In the United States, inequality has never been merely a matter of impersonal market outcomes. Slavery, land theft, Jim Crow, exclusion from New Deal protections, discriminatory housing finance, unequal schools, mass incarceration, and segmented labor markets have shaped wealth and opportunity. Economists have produced valuable work on discrimination, neighborhood effects, criminal justice, and intergenerational mobility. But an inclusive prosperity agenda that does not explicitly confront racialized state action and racialized markets will reproduce the very abstraction that makes many people skeptical of the discipline.

The same is true of gender and care. A great deal of socially necessary labor is unpaid, underpaid, or treated as peripheral because it does not fit comfortably within market production. Child care, elder care, domestic labor, and emotional labor sustain the economy, but they are often invisible in policy debates organized around productivity and wages. A post-neoliberal economics should put care infrastructure near the center: paid leave, child allowances or credits, universal pre-K, professionalized care work, and schedules compatible with family life. These are not merely “social” policies. They shape labor supply, human development, bargaining power, and equality.

The authors are strongest when they insist that institutions are not given by nature. Property rights, corporate governance, patent rules, bankruptcy procedures, labor law, and trade agreements are all designed. Once that is acknowledged, the old opposition between “state” and “market” collapses. The question is not whether government intervenes, but on whose behalf, through which rules, and with what accountability. A corporation is not a natural market actor; it is a legal construction with privileges granted by the state. Intellectual property is not simply property; it is a policy choice about how to reward innovation and allocate access. Labor markets are not spot markets for widgets; they are structured by dependence, authority, law, and social norms.

This institutional perspective opens the door to bolder reforms than economists have often endorsed: sectoral bargaining, codetermination, public options, postal banking, stronger automatic stabilizers, public investment in green infrastructure, antitrust revival, and taxation of wealth and inheritances. Some of these ideas can be justified in efficiency terms; others rest on democratic and egalitarian commitments. That is fine. The effort to redescribe every egalitarian reform as a free lunch is tempting but limiting. There are free lunches in an economy full of monopoly, monopsony, externalities, and informational failures. But sometimes justice will cost something, at least for some people, and a mature politics should be able to say so.

Climate change makes this unavoidable. Carbon pricing is a powerful tool, but climate policy cannot be reduced to finding the right price. The transformation required is infrastructural, technological, regional, and political. It involves public investment, regulation, planning, industrial policy, and compensation for workers and communities tied to fossil fuels. Economists can help design these policies, but they must abandon the reflex that treats direct public coordination as suspect and price mechanisms as presumptively elegant. The atmosphere does not care whether emissions reductions are achieved through the most textbook-efficient instrument. It cares whether they happen quickly enough and durably enough.

So yes: economics can serve inclusive prosperity. But it will do so only if economists change not just their policy conclusions, but their public posture. They should speak with more humility about uncertainty, more candor about values, and more respect for other forms of knowledge. They should treat history, sociology, political science, law, and moral philosophy not as decorative supplements but as necessary partners. They should teach introductory students that markets are instituted, power is real, and efficiency is not a synonym for justice. And they should be judged not only by the sophistication of their models, but by whether their advice helps build durable democratic capacity.

The lead essay is therefore an important invitation. It shows that mainstream tools can support egalitarian, institution-building reforms. But the deeper task is to make economics answerable to the society it seeks to improve. After neoliberalism, economics should not aspire to be the queen of the social sciences, dispensing rigor to the less formal disciplines. It should aspire to be a useful democratic craft: empirically serious, institutionally literate, morally explicit, and willing to share authority with the people whose lives its policies will shape.

  2. 02The common practice of treating economic efficiency as separate from political and administrative implementation is itself a biased political judgment.
  3. 03Economic analysis must account for non-market factors such as state weakness, racial hierarchy, and organized business power to be truly effective in the real world.
  4. 04A truly inclusive prosperity must prioritize human dignity, voice, and community stability rather than treating 'inclusion' as a mere distributive add-on to growth.
  5. 05The discipline must explicitly confront the history of racialized state action and the invisibility of unpaid care work to avoid reproducing harmful abstractions.
  6. 06Economists should acknowledge that markets are legal and social constructions rather than natural phenomena, opening the door to bolder structural reforms.
  7. 07The profession must move away from seeking 'free lunches' and be honest about the moral and political trade-offs where justice may come at an economic cost.
