"While the Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) project correctly identifies that economics can address inequality, it fails to sufficiently challenge the traditional prioritization of economic growth and the inherent trade-offs it creates with social and environmental well-being."

The essay by Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman represents a significant contribution to the debate over economics and economic policy. Its central argument—that economics has been wrongly identified with neoliberalism and that mainstream economists have substantial resources for addressing inequality—is correct and important. The EfIP project provides concrete evidence of this potential, and the specific policy proposals are generally sound.

But I want to push back on one aspect of the essay: its treatment of growth. The authors write that "the recipes thus call for comparative institutional analysis of economic performance—not glib 'markets work' slogans." This is a fair critique of the efficiency-first approach, but it does not adequately question the priority given to growth itself. The authors assume that policies should aim at "prosperity" and that efficiency and equality can both be enhanced through appropriate interventions. But this assumption deserves scrutiny.

The history of economic development suggests that growth does not automatically translate into inclusive prosperity. The United States experienced rapid growth for decades while median wages stagnated and inequality increased. China has achieved extraordinary growth rates while generating massive environmental damage and severe inequalities between urban and rural populations. Growth has often come at the expense of workers, communities, and ecosystems, and it has not necessarily improved the distribution of its benefits.

The authors recognize that "inclusive prosperity" requires attention to distribution and to nonpecuniary sources of well-being, including health, climate, and political rights. But they do not adequately address the possibility that there are inherent conflicts between growth and these other values. A truly inclusive approach to prosperity might require accepting lower rates of output growth in exchange for greater security, better health outcomes, more leisure, and a healthier environment. The current economic framework does not provide good tools for thinking about such trade-offs.

This is not an argument against all growth, but it is an argument for questioning the assumption that growth is always good or that faster is better. A more nuanced approach would recognize that the benefits of growth depend on how those benefits are distributed, what costs are imposed on workers and communities, and what environmental damage is generated. It would also recognize that there are limits to growth—not merely physical limits, but also social and psychological ones—and that a good society might choose to prioritize other values over the relentless expansion of output.

The authors write that "there are numerous policy 'free lunches' that could push us towards an economy that is morally better without sacrificing (and, indeed, possibly enhancing) prosperity." This claim is too optimistic. Many of the proposed policies—including wage boards, financial regulation, and social insurance—would likely reduce growth, at least in the short term. This does not mean they are bad policies; it means that the authors should be more candid about the trade-offs involved.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Historical evidence from the United States and China demonstrates that rapid economic growth does not automatically result in inclusive prosperity or equitable distribution.
  2. 02The authors overlook the potential for fundamental conflicts between the pursuit of output growth and nonpecuniary values like health, leisure, and environmental sustainability.
  3. 03A truly inclusive economic framework must be willing to accept lower growth rates in exchange for greater social security and ecological health.
  4. 04The claim that policy 'free lunches' can improve morality without sacrificing prosperity is overly optimistic and obscures the genuine economic trade-offs involved in structural reform.

"While the Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) project successfully decouples economics from neoliberalism, its policy framework is insufficient because it fails to address the deep-seated structural power of global corporations and the inherent conflict between economic growth and environmental sustainability."

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman have produced an important essay that challenges the conflation of economics with neoliberalism and points toward a more progressive economic agenda. Their specific proposals are well-grounded in theory and evidence, and the EfIP project represents a serious effort to redirect economic research toward questions of distribution and power.

However, the essay is weak on the question of international economic policy. The authors discuss "hyper-globalization" and argue that trade agreements should include social safeguards, but they do not adequately address the structural factors that have driven globalization or the interests that have benefited from it. The removal of trade barriers, the expansion of financial flows, and the emergence of global supply chains have been driven not merely by economic theory but by the political power of multinational corporations and the financial industry. Changing this dynamic will require more than clever proposals for trade agreements; it will require a fundamental renegotiation of the terms on which the global economy is organized.

The authors' proposal to tax multinationals based on sales rather than profits is a good example. This approach would indeed make it harder for companies to manipulate transfer pricing and shift profits to low-tax jurisdictions. But it would not address the broader issue of corporate power in global economic governance. Companies would still be able to lobby governments for favorable treatment, to threaten to relocate production if tax rates were raised, and to use their economic power to shape the rules under which they operate.

A more ambitious approach would consider fundamental reforms to the international economic order, including restrictions on capital flows, reforms to the governance of international economic institutions, and the development of alternative models of economic organization that are less dependent on global supply chains and more oriented toward local production and consumption. The authors mention "putting sand in the wheels of financial globalization" as a step in this direction, but they do not fully develop the implications of this proposal.

The essay would also benefit from more attention to the environmental dimensions of economic policy. The authors mention climate change as one of the challenges facing the contemporary economy, but they do not adequately address the relationship between economic growth and environmental sustainability. The policy proposals focus on redistribution and market failures, but they do not consider the possibility that the current scale of economic activity is itself unsustainable.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The authors' trade proposals overlook the fact that globalization is driven by the political power of multinational corporations and the financial industry rather than just economic theory.
  2. 02Technical fixes like sales-based taxation are inadequate because they do not dismantle the broader influence corporations exert over global economic governance and rule-making.
  3. 03A truly progressive agenda requires fundamental reforms to the international order, such as restricting capital flows and shifting away from global supply chains toward local production.
  4. 04The essay fails to reconcile the pursuit of economic growth with the reality that the current scale of global economic activity may be ecologically unsustainable.
Diversified MiniMax

"While the authors successfully demonstrate that economics can support progressive policies, their framework remains limited because it treats inclusion as a secondary modification to traditional prosperity rather than fundamentally reconceptualizing economic goals around human flourishing and democratic control."

The authors make a compelling case that economics is not the handmaiden of neoliberalism and that mainstream economists have valuable contributions to make to inclusive prosperity. Their specific proposals are thoughtful and well-grounded, and the EfIP project represents an important effort to redirect economic research toward questions of distribution and power.

But I want to focus on a more fundamental question: what does it mean for economics to serve inclusive prosperity? The authors write that prosperity "is the traditional concern of economists" and that "inclusive" adds the requirement to consider distribution and nonpecuniary sources of well-being. This framing treats prosperity as the primary goal and inclusion as an add-on—a modification of the traditional economic agenda rather than a fundamental reconceptualization of it.

A more radical approach would start from different premises. Instead of asking how to make markets work better or how to redistribute the gains from growth, it would ask what kind of economic organization is consistent with human flourishing and ecological sustainability. This approach would not abandon economics, which provides valuable tools for analyzing incentives, allocation, and trade-offs. But it would situate economics within a broader framework of values and goals that would guide the choice of policies and institutions.

The authors write that "economics does not provide the appropriate distributional weights (how to weigh the returns to workers, employers, and the government, and what procedural and deontological constraints should be respected)." This is true, but it is also an abdication of responsibility. Economics cannot determine distributional weights by itself, but it can contribute to a broader democratic deliberation about what values should guide economic policy. The authors do not adequately address how such deliberation is to be organized or how its conclusions are to be implemented.

The essay is weakest on the question of political economy. The authors recognize that "power asymmetries shape our contemporary economy" and that "democratic political economy—where people's influence on policy is roughly equal—must be considered." But they do not explain how to move from the current situation to one of genuine democratic control over economic policy. The proposals they make—including restrictions on campaign contributions, measures to increase voter turnout, and regulations to reduce the influence of money in politics—are all reasonable, but they do not add up to a coherent strategy for democratizing the economy.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The authors' framing of 'inclusive prosperity' incorrectly prioritizes market growth as the primary objective with distribution as a mere add-on.
  2. 02A more effective approach would situate economic tools within a broader, value-driven framework focused on ecological sustainability and human flourishing from the outset.
  3. 03The authors abdicate responsibility by claiming economics cannot determine distributional weights, failing to explain how the discipline should contribute to the democratic deliberation of those values.
  4. 04The essay lacks a coherent strategy for democratizing the economy, as its specific political economy proposals do not sufficiently address how to achieve genuine democratic control over economic policy.
