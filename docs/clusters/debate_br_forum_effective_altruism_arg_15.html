"Effective altruism is a morally superior and psychologically viable framework because it uses reason to channel human compassion toward the most impactful global outcomes."

Peter Singer’s case for effective altruism is, in its essentials, unassailable. He reminds us that we live in a world of staggering inequality, where a modest amount of money can save a child’s life, and that reason demands we take that fact seriously. The movement he describes—young people like Matt Wage choosing high-earning careers to give more, or others donating significant portions of their income to rigorously vetted charities—represents a genuine moral advance. Yet effective altruism is often caricatured as cold, calculating, and inhuman. This criticism misses the point. The real power of effective altruism lies not in suppressing our emotions but in channeling them toward outcomes that actually improve the world.

Consider Singer’s Batkid example. He acknowledges the emotional pull of granting a sick child’s wish, and he admits that even he feels that pull. Effective altruists are not Spock-like creatures devoid of empathy; they are people who, having felt that warmth, ask a further question: what else could that $7,500 do? The answer—three or more lives saved from malaria—does not erase the desire to help the child in front of us, but it does contextualize it within a broader moral landscape. Effective altruists don’t deny the value of making a child smile; they simply recognize that preventing a child’s death is a weightier good. To refuse that comparison, to insist that we should just follow our heart, is not compassionate. It is willful ignorance in service of our own comfort.

The real objection to effective altruism is not that it is too demanding but that it asks us to think systematically about our benevolence. For most of history, charity has been a fundamentally local and emotional enterprise. We give to the church down the street, the alma mater that shaped us, the disease that claimed a loved one. There is nothing wrong with these impulses, but they are unreliable guides to doing good. A donation to a rich university like Princeton, with its $21 billion endowment, may produce less welfare than the same sum directed toward bed nets in malaria-endemic regions. Singer’s point is not that education is worthless, but that at the margin, the additional dollar does vastly more for the global poor. That’s not cold; it’s a recognition of the radical inequality that defines our world.

Singer is careful to address the worry that effective altruism erodes special obligations to family. He grants that parents may justifiably prioritize their own children, though not without limit. Some critics will find even this concession insufficient, arguing that any impartial calculus undermines the partiality essential to loving relationships. But Singer’s view is more nuanced. He notes that close families require loving partiality, and that effective altruists do not seek to maximize the good in every waking moment. They leave room for rest, relationships, and personal projects. The demand is not to become a saint but to live what he calls “a minimally acceptable ethical life” by using a substantial portion of spare resources for the good of others. That standard is demanding, but it is also flexible, and it respects the psychological realism that any viable ethic must incorporate.

Another common objection is that effective altruism’s focus on measurable outcomes such as lives saved or years of suffering averted ignores the intrinsic value of justice, equality, or art. Singer’s response—that these goods are valuable because they contribute to human well-being, and that in a world of extreme poverty, art funding is a lower priority—may strike some as reductive. But again, the question is one of trade-offs in a non-ideal world. If a billionaire can fund a concert hall or prevent thousands of deaths from preventable disease, the choice should not be difficult. The fact that we are uncomfortable making such comparisons does not mean they are false. Effective altruism forces us to confront the uncomfortable truth that our choices have opportunity costs, and that the glow of supporting the arts can sometimes blind us to the greater needs we could meet instead.

Perhaps the most exciting aspect of effective altruism is its melding of reason and compassion. It shows that moral concern is not limited to kin or tribe, but can be extended through careful thought and evidence. The millennial generation, often stereotyped as self-absorbed, is producing thousands of people who are living out this ethos. They are proof that a rational, impartial altruism is not only possible but can be a source of deep fulfillment. That alone is a rejoinder to the cynics who insist that human nature is essentially selfish. Effective altruism, as Singer presents it, is not a joyless calculus. It is a pathway to a morally serious life, one that takes both the heart and the head seriously. It deserves not only our admiration but our emulation.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The movement does not suppress empathy but rather contextualizes emotional impulses within a broader moral landscape to prioritize saving lives over fulfilling wishes.
  2. 02Systematic thinking about benevolence is necessary because traditional, local charitable impulses are unreliable guides that often ignore the radical inequality of the world.
  3. 03Effective altruism is a psychologically realistic ethic that allows for personal relationships and self-care while still demanding a significant commitment to the global good.
  4. 04The focus on measurable outcomes is a necessary response to the reality of opportunity costs, where choosing to fund less urgent causes like the arts results in preventable deaths.
  5. 05The success of the movement among millennials proves that rational, impartial altruism is a possible and fulfilling alternative to innate selfishness.
