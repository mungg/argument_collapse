"To address campus sexual assault fairly, institutions must adopt a system of provisional belief and comprehensive support for survivors while maintaining a preponderance of evidence standard in investigations."

Every time a high-profile sexual assault case falls apart, the same dreary script plays out: we hear dire warnings about the danger of false accusations, while the far larger tragedy of endemic campus rape is pushed aside. The unspooling of Rolling Stone’s UVA story is being used to stoke panic over false claims, but the data tell a very different story. The best research consistently finds that false reporting rates for sexual assault are between 2 and 10 percent—no higher than for any other crime. Yet the default response to a woman who reports being raped remains one of suspicion: What was she wearing? Had she been drinking? Why didn’t she report sooner? This institutional skepticism is the real scandal, and it drives underreporting, leaving serial predators free to assault again.

The answer is not to dismantle campus adjudication systems out of fear of the rare false claim. It is to build a system rooted in the understanding that sexual violence is a widespread, traumatic crime that requires a different kind of response. When a student says she has been raped, the first and overriding imperative must be to ensure her safety and well-being. That means providing immediate, comprehensive support—crisis counseling, medical care, academic and housing adjustments—without demanding she retraumatize herself in a cross-examination. It also means treating her account with provisional belief. Provisional belief is not a conviction; it is a working stance that allows investigators to gather evidence without the corrosive assumption that the victim is lying.

The investigative process itself can be redesigned to minimize adversarial trauma while still testing the facts. Trained sexual assault investigators can collect forensic, digital, and witness evidence. The complainant’s narrative can be probed for consistency without accusatory hostility. Crucially, the standard of proof should remain preponderance of the evidence, which is appropriate for a civil, educational context. That standard does not strip away the accused’s rights; it simply acknowledges that campus tribunals are not criminal courts and that a survivor should not have to meet the beyond-reasonable-doubt threshold to gain protection from her assailant.

What about the falsely accused? The Rolling Stone debacle actually demonstrates that our collective skepticism is alive and well. The story was immediately met with journalistic scrutiny and, when cracks appeared, the public recoiled. A true victim of false accusation—like any student—deserves a fair process, but that process must not be built on the myth that women routinely fabricate gang rapes to explain away a bad night. The far greater danger on American campuses today is a culture of impunity that shields assailants while crushing survivors. We need a system that finally errs on the side of believing those who come forward, not one that treats every report as a potential trap for innocent men.
