"The Affordable Care Act is only marginally sustainable because it relies on profit-driven private insurers to fulfill a public obligation, a conflict that can only be resolved by moving toward a public-centered system like Medicare-for-all."

The Aetna and Oscar withdrawals reveal a deeper issue than whether this or that exchange has enough carriers. The Affordable Care Act asks private insurance companies to perform a public obligation: guarantee access to health coverage for people who may be sick, low-income or expensive to treat. But private insurers are designed to manage risk for profit. When a market stops being profitable, they leave. Patients do not have that option.

In that sense, Obamacare is sustainable only in a limited and uneasy way. It has done real good. It ended some of the cruelest practices of the individual market, expanded Medicaid in many states, and made coverage possible for millions. But it preserved a fragmented system: employer plans for some, Medicaid for others, Medicare for the elderly, and subsidized private plans for those left over. The exchanges are the narrowest and most volatile slice of that arrangement.

The underlying cost problem is not that Americans have too much insurance competition. It is that medical prices are high, administrative costs are high, and insurers often compete by designing narrow networks, avoiding bad risks and negotiating separately with providers. Even when exchange competition works, it may produce plans that are cheaper because they exclude major hospitals or impose large deductibles. That is not the same as an efficient health system.

Long-term viability requires broad risk pools and public discipline over prices. One route would be a robust public option in every exchange, paying rates closer to Medicare’s and open to anyone who lacks affordable employer coverage. Another would be all-payer rate setting, so hospitals and insurers cannot shift costs through opaque negotiations. The most coherent solution is eventually a Medicare-for-all-style system, or at least a Medicare buy-in that grows over time. Such systems do not depend on whether Aetna finds a county profitable next year.

Critics will say single payer is politically unrealistic. Perhaps. But the current system is politically unrealistic in a different way: it promises universal access while preserving every private stakeholder’s veto. Insurers want profits, hospitals want high reimbursements, drug companies want pricing freedom, employers want tax subsidies, and consumers want affordable care. The ACA tried to balance all of that, and its exchange troubles show the limits of compromise.

The immediate fixes are still worth making: better subsidies, reinsurance, tougher enrollment rules and a fallback public plan. But we should not confuse those repairs with the final destination. If health care is a right, it should not rest on the business judgment of insurers who can withdraw whenever the numbers disappoint.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Private insurers prioritize profit over universal access and will inevitably withdraw from markets when they are no longer financially lucrative.
  2. 02The current fragmented system creates volatility because it isolates the most vulnerable populations into narrow, unstable exchange markets.
  3. 03High medical prices and administrative costs are the true drivers of instability, rather than a lack of competition between private insurers.
  4. 04Long-term viability requires broad risk pools and public control over pricing, such as a public option or all-payer rate setting, to prevent cost-shifting.
  5. 05The ACA’s attempt to balance the competing interests of private stakeholders like hospitals and insurers limits its ability to provide truly affordable universal care.
