"To align with its mission as a health care provider, CVS should stop selling products that are inherently dangerous when used as intended or that lack medical evidence while masquerading as medicine."

CVS should not turn itself into a monastery. Pharmacies sell aspirin, ice cream, cough syrup, sunscreen, candy and cosmetics because customers live ordinary lives, not because every item is a medical endorsement. But if CVS wants to be treated as a health care company, it should draw a bright line around products that are dangerous when used exactly as intended, or that trade on medical credibility without evidence.

That puts all tobacco products first: cigarettes, cigars, smokeless tobacco and the new nicotine devices that are not approved as smoking-cessation aids. E-cigarettes may prove less harmful than cigarettes, but in 2014 their contents, marketing and long-term effects remain too uncertain for a pharmacy counter. If a nicotine product is tested and approved as a quitting aid, sell it. If it is marketed as a recreational nicotine product, do not.

CVS should also reconsider alcohol, at least in stores attached to clinics and pharmacies. Alcohol is not tobacco; many adults use it without addiction, and outright removal would be controversial. But a company counseling patients on liver disease, pregnancy, depression, diabetes and drug interactions should not be promoting cheap beer and sweet malt drinks beside prescription pickup. A reasonable compromise would be to stop discounting and advertising alcohol, and phase it out where local economics allow.

Finally, CVS should stop selling remedies that masquerade as medicine while lacking good evidence: homeopathic pills, dubious “detox” products and supplements making implied disease claims. The harm is not only chemical; it is substitution. A customer who buys magic instead of treatment may get sicker.

Candy and soda are different. They contribute to obesity and diabetes, but they are not inevitably harmful in ordinary use. CVS should shrink, not sanctify, that aisle. Health care requires judgment, not puritanism.

"To remain consistent with its mission as a health care provider, CVS should stop selling sugar-sweetened beverages."

The decision to pull tobacco from CVS shelves is a watershed moment, a clear admission that a pharmacy cannot genuinely promote health while profiting from the nation’s leading cause of preventable death. But if the company is serious about its mission, it must look past this single, glaring contradiction. The next logical category to eliminate is sugar-sweetened beverages.

This is not about banning pleasure or acting as a nanny state. It is about consistency. The evidence linking sugary drinks to the epidemics of obesity, type 2 diabetes, and metabolic syndrome is now as robust as the case against tobacco was a generation ago. A 20-ounce bottle of soda delivers around 16 teaspoons of sugar and empty calories that directly fuel chronic illness. These products sit prominently in CVS coolers, often at the checkout, normalizing a daily habit that the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention identifies as a key driver of rising health care costs. For a company that is rapidly expanding its in-store MinuteClinics to manage diabetes and heart disease, selling the very products that cause these conditions is a profound hypocrisy.

The tobacco analogy holds. Decades of public health campaigns taught us that certain products are so uniquely harmful and so aggressively marketed that their availability in a trusted health setting becomes a stamp of approval. A pharmacy should be a sanctuary for wellness, not a convenience store for disease. By removing sugary drinks, CVS would not only align its shelves with its white coats, it would send a powerful, science-backed message: we refuse to profit from the slow, sweetened path to illness. The tobacco ban was brave; following it with a ban on liquid sugar is the necessary next step toward becoming a true health care provider.

"While CVS's decision to stop selling tobacco is a principled move that aligns with its health mission, the company is not obligated to remove other legal products and should avoid crossing into corporate paternalism."

CVS's decision to drop tobacco was a principled move, but extending that logic too far risks crossing from corporate conscience into corporate paternalism. Tobacco stands apart: it's the only legal consumer product that, when used as intended, kills half its long-term users. No similar argument applies to alcohol, sugary snacks, or lottery tickets, all of which CVS and virtually every other retailer sell.

That said, CVS could rationally go further without hypocrisy. Alcohol shares tobacco's addictive properties and contributes meaningfully to disease, car accidents, and social harm. If CVS genuinely wants to align its product mix with its health brand, phasing out alcohol sales—particularly in locations near MinuteClinics—would be defensible.

But there's a harder question lurking beneath this debate: whether retailers have any obligation to curate our choices at all. CVS is a private company free to stock whatever it likes. Its tobacco decision was admirable precisely because it sacrificed short-term revenue for long-term credibility. Demanding it go further implies an obligation that doesn't exist. Consumers who want healthier retailers can shop elsewhere; those who don't can stay.

The real legacy of CVS's announcement may not be what else it stops selling, but whether it sparks a broader conversation about how retail and health intersect—and whether that conversation leads to better consumer information rather than narrower product availability.

If CVS wants to be a genuine health care company, it must stop selling illusion disguised as medicine. The most urgent category for removal isn’t soda or beer—it’s the vast array of unproven homeopathic remedies, dubious dietary supplements, and “natural” treatments that line its aisles under the deceptive halo of wellness. A pharmacy that has pledged to put health first has a fundamental obligation not to profit from products that lack scientific evidence and, in some cases, actively harm patients who forgo effective treatment.

Walk through any CVS and you’ll find shelves of homeopathic pellets that contain no active ingredient, marketed for serious conditions alongside legitimate over-the-counter drugs. You’ll find weight-loss supplements with little oversight, herbal concoctions that can interact dangerously with prescription medications, and cold remedies whose claims rest on nothing more than tradition. A health care company cannot have it both ways: employing licensed pharmacists trained in evidence-based medicine while simultaneously selling placebos at a markup. This isn’t mere hypocrisy; it erodes trust in the entire enterprise. A patient who trusts CVS as a health destination may reasonably assume that products on its shelves have been vetted for safety and efficacy, yet the opposite is often true.

The 1994 Dietary Supplement Health and Education Act largely shields supplement manufacturers from pre-market safety testing, leaving retailers as a critical gatekeeper. CVS can seize that role. Instead of waiting for FDA enforcement actions, the company could adopt a simple standard: sell only those non-prescription health products that are supported by credible clinical evidence or meet recognized compendia. This would not mean eliminating every vitamin—many have proven benefits for specific deficiencies—but it would mean dropping the outright quackery. If CVS truly wants to be seen as a trusted community health provider, it must stop treating the placebo effect as a business model and start ensuring that every health claim on its shelves can survive a pharmacist’s scrutiny.
