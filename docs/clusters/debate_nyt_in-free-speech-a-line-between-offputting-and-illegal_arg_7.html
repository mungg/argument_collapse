Diversified Claude

"Speech should be considered a punishable threat rather than protected expression when it involves specific, identifiable targets and real-world surveillance that causes reasonable fear."

Whenever a case like this arises, the conversation gravitates toward abstractions — the First Amendment, true threats doctrine, the dignity of fantasy. What gets lost is the perspective of the people whose names, photographs, addresses, and daily schedules were being traded in those chat rooms. Ask them where the line is.

For years, women who report online harassment have been told that what happens on the internet is not real, that the men typing about raping or killing them are venting harmlessly, that prosecutors and police cannot do anything until there is "actual" violence. Then a case arrives in which a police officer — the very person women are told to call when they feel unsafe — has compiled detailed surveillance on specific women he was discussing torturing and eating, and the civil liberties commentariat rushes to explain that this, too, is protected expression. One begins to understand why so few women trust the system to take threats seriously.

I am not suggesting that every dark fantasy belongs in court. I am suggesting that the question "what's the difference between protected speech and a threat?" cannot be answered solely by looking at the speaker's intent or the speech's literary form. It must also take account of the listener and the target. When the subject of the speech is a real, named person, and when the speaker has taken real-world steps to learn about and locate that person, the speech has crossed into conduct that menaces an identifiable human being. The First Amendment has never required us to treat such speech as if it floated in a vacuum.

This is consistent with how the law already treats stalking. Stalking statutes punish patterns of conduct — much of which is "speech" in a literal sense — that put a specific person in reasonable fear. They have generally survived First Amendment challenge because the harm tracks identifiable victims, not unpopular viewpoints. Valle's conduct, as alleged, looks far more like aggravated stalking than like writing transgressive fiction. He was not posting general fantasies about anonymous women; he was researching particular women he knew, in collaboration with strangers, over months.

The "offputting versus illegal" framing in the headline subtly stacks the deck. It implies that the only thing wrong with this kind of speech is bad taste, and that the question is whether prudishness should be allowed to send a man to prison. That is not the question. The question is whether the targets of such speech — women, almost always — have any legal interest the rest of us are bound to respect before someone is physically harmed.

If the answer is no, we should be honest that this is the trade-off we have chosen. I would choose differently.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The legal distinction between protected speech and illegal threats must prioritize the perspective and safety of the targets rather than focusing solely on the speaker's intent.
  2. 02Speech that involves researching and locating specific individuals crosses the line from abstract fantasy into menacing conduct.
  3. 03Existing stalking statutes provide a legal precedent for punishing speech-based conduct that puts specific victims in reasonable fear.
  4. 04Framing the debate as a choice between 'offputting' and 'illegal' ignores the legitimate legal interest women have in being protected from targeted harassment before physical harm occurs.
