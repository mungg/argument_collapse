"The perceived decline of free speech on college campuses is a distraction from the far more significant structural threats to expression, such as government surveillance, corporate control, and the criminalization of dissent."

Focusing on trigger warnings and campus newspapers is like monitoring a paper cut while the patient hemorrhages from a severed artery. If we truly care about the future of free expression, we should be looking not at students but at the National Security Agency’s mass surveillance, the aggressive prosecution of whistleblowers, the corporate consolidation of media, and the economic precarity that makes people terrified to speak out at work. A generation of college students debating how to discuss race and trauma is a sideshow to the structural forces that actually chill speech in America.

The average student who requests a trigger warning will graduate into an economy where employers routinely monitor social media and where contract workers can be fired for a single tweet. The very same politicians and pundits who decry campus “snowflakes” are often the ones cheering laws that restrict protests, expand government secrecy, and treat journalists as criminals. Where is the moral panic about the erosion of speech in those realms? The silence is telling.

The Wesleyan controversy ended with the newspaper still standing, a student body that had aired a painful argument, and a campus that continued to function. That messy resolution — full of shouting, writing, and voting — was free speech in action, not its collapse. In contrast, the true threats to discourse are quiet and legal, not loud and adolescent. If we want the next generation to lead a free country, we should model that freedom by fighting the surveillance state, corporate censorship, and the criminalization of dissent. The kids on campus are doing fine. It’s the adults in power who need a lesson in what speech is worth.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The real chilling effect on speech comes from economic precarity and employer monitoring of social media rather than student requests for trigger warnings.
