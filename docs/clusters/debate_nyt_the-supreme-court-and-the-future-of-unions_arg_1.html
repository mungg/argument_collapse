"Public employees should be required to pay fair-share fees to unions because these fees prevent free-rider problems and ensure the financial stability necessary for effective collective bargaining."

The recent Supreme Court decision in *Harris v. Quinn* is crafted narrowly, yet it cracks open a much larger debate about the future of public-sector unions. The ruling concerned a specific category of home-health workers, but the underlying question—whether non-members can be compelled to pay any fees to a union—is now squarely in the crosshairs. For decades, the answer under *Abood v. Detroit Board of Education* has been a careful yes: employees who decline full union membership may still be required to pay an “agency” or “fair share” fee covering the costs of collective bargaining, contract administration, and grievance processing. This arrangement is not just a convenience; it is the linchpin of stable labor relations.

The justification is straightforward. Federal law obligates a union that gains majority support to represent *all* employees in that bargaining unit, regardless of whether every individual joins. Without some form of mandatory fee, the workplace breeds an obvious free-rider problem. Workers can enjoy the wage increases, benefits, and job protections the union negotiates while contributing nothing, draining the union’s resources and sowing resentment among dues-paying members. Agency fees simply require everyone who benefits to contribute to the cost of securing those benefits, no different in principle from a mandatory bar association fee for attorneys or student activity fees at public universities.

If the Court were to extend its reasoning and strike down fair-share fees across the public sector, the effects would be seismic. In states that have already adopted “right-to-work” laws for government employees, union density and revenues have fallen sharply, not because workers reject collective representation but because it becomes economically rational to ride for free. A nationwide shift would starve unions of the stable funding needed to hire skilled negotiators, conduct research, and process grievances. The predictable result is not a harmonious workplace of freely choosing individuals, but a downward spiral in public-sector wages and working conditions, making it harder to attract and retain qualified teachers, police officers, and sanitation workers. The core mission of public services would suffer, as would the middle-class stability that public employment has historically provided.

The question should not be framed as “union dues or nothing.” Public employees who do not join a union should not be charged full dues, and they certainly should not be compelled to support partisan activity. But an outright ban on all fees is a blunt instrument that would destabilize labor relations without solving the real problem: how to reconcile individual conscience with a workable system of collective bargaining.

The current agency-fee model is defensible in theory. If a union has a legal duty to represent every worker, including workers who oppose it, it needs resources to do that work. Grievance handling, contract negotiation and enforcement cost money. The danger is that the line between chargeable workplace costs and nonchargeable political costs is often opaque. Public employees may reasonably suspect that their fees indirectly strengthen an institution whose broader agenda they oppose.

A better approach would be to narrow and clarify the fee rather than abolish it. Nonmembers should pay only for demonstrable, auditable representation expenses: bargaining, contract administration, arbitration and legally required representation. They should receive clear annual notices, meaningful opportunities to object, and independent review of disputed charges. Unions should not be able to pad the bill with public relations, lobbying or organizing expenses. At the same time, objectors should not be able to receive individualized representation entirely for free.

States could also experiment with alternatives. If nonmembers object to paying the union, perhaps unions should not be required to represent them in certain individual grievances beyond the negotiated contract’s general terms. Or the state could reimburse unions for legally mandated representation, making the cost transparent in the public budget rather than hidden in payroll deductions. Each model has complications, but each is more honest than pretending there is no cost.

Harris v. Quinn exposes the fragility of the existing compromise, particularly for workers who do not fit neatly into conventional public employment. But the Court should be cautious about constitutionalizing a nationwide right-to-work rule for the public sector. Labor law is full of imperfect bargains, and states have legitimate reasons to prefer stable, exclusive bargaining relationships.

Organized labor should take this moment seriously. It needs cleaner accounting, more internal democracy and a stronger case to nonmembers. But public employers also need stable representatives with whom to negotiate. Fair-share fees, tightly limited and transparently administered, remain the best balance.

It is worth resisting the urge to treat *Harris v. Quinn* as either Armageddon or liberation. The decision is genuinely narrow, and the most interesting question it raises is not whether unions will survive but whether the Court is preparing to redraw the line between public employment and citizenship itself.

Justice Alito's majority opinion did not overrule *Abood v. Detroit Board of Education*. It carved out a category — "partial public employees" — to describe the Illinois home-care workers, who are paid through Medicaid but employed in private homes by the disabled and elderly clients they serve. Because they are not full state employees, the Court reasoned, the state's interest in "labor peace" is weaker, and the First Amendment burden of compelled fees is therefore unjustified. Full-fledged public employees — teachers, firefighters, transit workers — are not directly affected. Yet.

The "yet" is the interesting part. Alito spent pages questioning *Abood*'s reasoning, accusing it of resting on a misreading of precedent and a naive faith in the public/private distinction. He all but invited a follow-up case asking the Court to overrule *Abood* outright. The four dissenters understood this perfectly well; Justice Kagan's opinion reads like a brief for a future defense of fair-share fees.

So the right answer to the question — should public employees be required to pay fees? — depends on whether you think the public sector is meaningfully different from the political process. *Abood* said yes: when government acts as employer, it has managerial interests in stable bargaining that justify limited compelled support. The *Harris* majority is openly skeptical of that distinction, treating every interaction between a public union and the state as inherently political speech.

If *Abood* falls, the entire public sector becomes right-to-work overnight. Unions in states like New York, California, and Illinois — where density is high precisely because of fair-share rules — would face significant revenue losses and likely membership declines. Whether that is a feature or a bug depends on one's priors. But everyone watching this docket should understand: Monday was not the destination. It was a road sign.
