"The Democratic Party’s recent economic shift is a temporary, crisis-driven alignment rather than a durable transformation, as the party remains fundamentally constrained by the material interests of its affluent, professional-class base."

Jacob S. Hacker and Paul Pierson offer a comforting narrative for progressives: the Democratic Party has managed a near-miraculous synthesis, uniting affluent suburbanites and a multi-racial working class behind the most ambitious economic agenda in generations. In their telling, the U-shaped coalition is not a trap of contradictory class interests, but an engine for a new era of big government, momentarily stalled by the idiosyncrasies of the Senate filibuster and Joe Manchin. It is an elegant thesis, heavily reliant on the legislative text of the original Build Back Better (BBB) framework. But it fundamentally misunderstands the limits of a political coalition that attempts to graft a working-class economic agenda onto a donor base of the professional-managerial class and corporate elite.

The problem with Hacker and Pierson’s analysis is that they mistake a temporary, pandemic-induced suspension of fiscal constraints for a durable ideological realignment within the Democratic establishment. The authors celebrate the ambitious initial proposals of the American Jobs Plan and the American Families Plan, noting their staggering price tags. But we must look closely at how these plans were designed and, crucially, how they were constrained by the very affluent suburbanites the authors claim are compatible with a redistributive agenda.

As the authors concede, Biden’s pledge not to raise taxes on anyone making under $400,000 severely limits the horizon of social democracy in America. Hacker and Pierson brush this off, arguing that the concentration of wealth at the very top means the rich and corporations can foot the bill. But this is a profound misreading of comparative political economy. As Thomas Piketty—whom the authors cite—has exhaustively documented, robust welfare states are built on broad-based taxation. The Nordic models are sustained not just by soaking the rich, but by a social contract where the middle class pays significant taxes in exchange for universal, high-quality public goods. By cementing the $400,000 threshold, Democrats have structurally handicapped their ability to build durable universal programs. They have signaled to their affluent suburban base that the costs of redistribution will always be borne by someone else. When that proves mathematically or politically impossible, the redistribution simply won't happen.

Furthermore, the actual substance of what passed in the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) and the CHIPS and Science Act reveals the true nature of the Brahmin Left's economic pivot. This is not the redistribution of the New Deal or the Great Society; it is "de-risking" corporate investments through massive public subsidies. The industrial policy championed by the Biden administration relies primarily on tax credits and grants to private capital—to semiconductor manufacturers, green energy conglomerates, and electric vehicle companies. It is a trickle-down economics for the green era. While these bills include welcome provisions for prevailing wages, they do not fundamentally alter the balance of power between labor and capital. They enrich the progressive-aligned sectors of the corporate world while hoping the benefits reach the working class.

Hacker and Pierson also vastly underestimate the fragility of a coalition held together by "negative polarization." It is true that many affluent suburbanites loathe Donald Trump and the MAGA right. But holding your nose to vote against Christian nationalism is not the same as endorsing a transformation of the American political economy. When the specter of Trump fades—if it ever does—the fundamental material interests of the affluent suburbs will reassert themselves. These voters are the primary beneficiaries of the current economic order. They benefit from soaring asset prices, exclusionary zoning, and a tax code that privileges capital gains and homeownership.

The authors point to the expansion of the child tax credit (CTC) as a crowning achievement, noting its dramatic impact on child poverty. Yet they gloss over its expiration. Why did the expanded CTC die? It wasn't just Joe Manchin. It was the fact that a policy transferring wealth to the lowest-income Americans lacked a powerful, organized constituency within the Democratic party's ascendant professional-class base. If the party were truly grounded in the working class, the expiration of a policy that cut child poverty by 40 percent would have sparked a political firestorm. Instead, it faded quietly into the background, while the party expended enormous political capital on the IRA's corporate subsidies.

Hacker and Pierson’s suggestion that the Democratic leadership deliberately chose a "robust but carefully curated economic program" to avoid the schisms of race and class is too generous. The reality is that the party has been captured by a class of voters who are socially liberal but economically conservative when their own material interests are at stake. The refusal to tackle "opportunity hoarding" at the local level—zoning, school funding, regressive local taxation—is not a clever strategic evasion; it is a surrender. A political party cannot claim to be the vehicle for multi-racial working-class liberation while fiercely protecting the localized mechanisms of segregation and inequality that oppress that very class.

The "Brahmin Left" critique remains potent because it describes the underlying material reality of the party's donor and activist base. The leftward shift on economics during the pandemic was a response to an extraordinary crisis, fueled by temporary social movements and the brief dominance of progressive think tanks in a vacuum of ideas. But as inflation surged and the crisis waned, the U-shaped coalition reverted to form. The party of the affluent suburbs is perfectly willing to subsidize green capital and tweak the regulatory state. But it will not fundamentally challenge the distribution of wealth and power, because to do so would require confronting its own most reliable voters.

If the Democratic Party wants to build a lasting, majoritarian coalition capable of defending democracy, it cannot rely on a tenuous alliance between the victims of the knowledge economy and its greatest beneficiaries. It must root itself unambiguously in the working class, which means making hard choices about whose material interests it ultimately serves. Hacker and Pierson's optimism is a snapshot of a fleeting moment in 2021, not a roadmap for the future.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The party's commitment to not raising taxes on those earning under $400,000 structurally prevents the creation of a robust, universal welfare state similar to successful European models.
  2. 02The Biden administration's industrial policy focuses on 'de-risking' private capital through corporate subsidies rather than fundamentally redistributing wealth or power to labor.
  3. 03The quiet expiration of the expanded Child Tax Credit demonstrates that the party's ascendant professional-class base lacks a genuine commitment to policies that primarily benefit the poor.
  4. 04The refusal to challenge 'opportunity hoarding' like exclusionary zoning proves the party is unwilling to confront the localized mechanisms of inequality that protect its affluent voters.
  5. 05The coalition is held together by a shared dislike of the far-right rather than a shared economic vision, making it fragile once the immediate threat of the opposition fades.
  6. 06A truly majoritarian coalition requires the party to root itself unambiguously in the working class and make hard choices that may alienate its wealthy donor base.
