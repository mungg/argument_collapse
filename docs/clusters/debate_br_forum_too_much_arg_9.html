"A feminist politics of care must move beyond the narrow focus on mothering healthy children to include disability and chronic dependency, shifting the justification for public support from the production of future citizens to a justice-based recognition of universal human need."

Nancy Hirschmann’s essay raises necessary questions about care, power, and gender. Yet one feature of the discussion remains troubling: care is treated mostly through the example of mothering healthy children who will become citizens, workers, or failures. That frame leaves out disability and chronic dependency, and the omission matters. Once we attend to disability, the limits of both sentimental care feminism and work-centered feminism become clearer.

Much feminist writing about care begins with children because all human beings pass through childhood. But not all dependency is temporary. Some children will not become independent in the conventional sense. Some adults require daily assistance because of physical, intellectual, or psychiatric disabilities. Many elderly people need care for years. Illness can make any of us dependent suddenly. A politics of care built around launching children into citizenship is too narrow. Care is not merely the preparation of future autonomous persons; it is part of the ongoing fabric of human life.

Hirschmann worries that if care is defended as a public good because mothers produce good citizens, then public support may be withdrawn or supervised when the product disappoints. This is an important critique. Disability exposes the danger even more sharply. If caregiving is justified by the future productivity or civic contribution of the cared-for person, where does that leave people who will never be economically productive, who communicate atypically, who need lifelong support, or whose contributions cannot be measured by ordinary civic standards? A just society supports care not because every recipient will repay the investment, but because human beings have needs and dignity.

For that reason, the public-good argument for care should be supplemented, perhaps replaced, by a justice argument grounded in dependency. Eva Kittay and other dependency theorists have insisted that dependency work creates claims not only because it benefits society, but because dependency is a universal condition and because caregivers themselves become vulnerable through caring. The mother of a severely disabled child may leave paid work not because she has romanticized domesticity but because no adequate services exist. Telling her to retain economic clout by staying employed may be unrealistic, even cruel, unless the social supports are actually there.

This does not mean Hirschmann is wrong about women’s vulnerability. Caregivers of disabled family members are often economically devastated. They lose wages, retirement savings, promotions, sleep, health, and social participation. Marriages may strain or break. Single parents may be overwhelmed. Siblings may become secondary caregivers. Mothers are especially likely to be held responsible. The gendered division of labor is not softened by disability; it is often intensified.

But the remedy cannot simply be that women should care less or insist that men do half. Men should do half, but “half” of extraordinary care can still be more than any family can bear alone. Some care requires trained aides, respite services, accessible transportation, medical equipment, inclusive schools, and income supports. Families need public infrastructure. Without it, appeals to personal bargaining become hollow. A husband may do more, a mother may remain employed, and still the family may be crushed.

Disability also complicates Hirschmann’s concern about state oversight. Families who rely on Medicaid, special education services under the Individuals with Disabilities Education Act, Supplemental Security Income, or state disability programs already experience bureaucracy. They fill out forms, prove impairment, attend meetings, fight school districts, document need, and submit to eligibility determinations. Public support always carries some administrative judgment. The question is whether that judgment is respectful, rights-based, and adequately funded, or suspicious, stingy, and punitive.

The fear of “housework police” should not lead us to reject public responsibility. In disability contexts, the absence of oversight can mean neglect, abuse, or abandonment; the wrong kind of oversight can mean humiliation and control. We need democratic accountability, not maternal surveillance. We need services designed with disabled people and caregivers, not imposed on them. We need to ask what forms of support increase freedom for both the person receiving care and the caregiver.

Hirschmann’s critique of privileged mothers remains relevant. Some parents of disabled children must fight relentlessly for services, and affluent parents are better equipped to do so. They can hire advocates, move to better school districts, pay for therapies, or sue. Poor families may be labeled noncompliant when they cannot attend meetings or navigate paperwork. Here again, “good mothering” is classed. The mother who becomes an expert advocate is praised if she has resources; the mother who is exhausted and distrustful may be judged deficient.

Care feminism at its best should illuminate these inequities. It should not be reduced to praise for maternal devotion. Indeed, disability communities have long warned against portraying caregivers as saints, because sainthood can erase disabled people’s agency and caregivers’ needs alike. A mother who sacrifices everything may be admired while receiving no respite. A disabled child may be treated as an object of maternal heroism rather than a person with preferences, relationships, and rights. Care must be reciprocal where possible, attentive to voice, and compatible with autonomy—not autonomy as self-sufficiency, but as supported self-direction.

The emphasis on women’s paid employment also needs revision. Employment can provide identity, income, and escape from total caregiving. It can also be impossible without flexible schedules and reliable services. Rather than treating continuous full-time employment as the feminist norm, we should fight for income security across life courses that include caregiving interruptions. Social Security caregiver credits, paid family leave, subsidized home care, respite, and health insurance independent of employment would protect caregivers without forcing them into poverty or dependence on spouses. These supports should apply to fathers, grandparents, siblings, and other caregivers as well as mothers.

Men’s participation is essential. A father of a disabled child who becomes an expert in medications, therapies, school law, feeding routines, and emotional care challenges gender norms more profoundly than one who merely “helps.” Policies should presume his involvement. Medical offices and schools should contact fathers, not only mothers. Leave policies should be available and expected for men. Support groups should welcome them. But we should also resist congratulating fathers excessively for doing what mothers are assumed to owe.

Ultimately, disability teaches that care is not a sentimental add-on to justice. It is one of justice’s foundations. The liberal fantasy of equal independent citizens meeting in public life has always depended on hidden care. Hirschmann is right that hidden care has trapped women. But if feminism responds by seeking escape from care rather than a just distribution of it, disabled people and caregivers will remain marginal.

The question is not whether mothers care too much. Some do care possessively, indulgently, or competitively. Some care beyond endurance because no one else will. Some are blamed for caring too little when they lack resources. The better question is: what would make care non-dominating? For disabled people, that means support without paternalism. For caregivers, it means recognition without imprisonment. For women, it means economic security without denying dependency. For men, it means obligation without applause. For the state, it means provision without degradation.

A feminist politics adequate to disability would value care, distribute it, fund it, and democratize it. It would not measure success by whether every woman remains continuously employed or whether every child becomes a productive citizen. It would measure success by whether all people, in their varying dependencies and capacities, can live with dignity—and whether those who support them can do so without losing themselves.
