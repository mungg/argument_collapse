"While the United States must adopt a more balanced and critical approach toward Israel to achieve a two-state solution, it should maintain an unshakeable commitment to Israeli security rather than threatening a rupture in the relationship."

Stephen Walt’s essay contains much wisdom about American overreach, but his treatment of Israel and the Palestinians is unbalanced and strategically dubious. He is right that the United States should oppose settlement expansion and work seriously for a two-state solution. He is right that American and Israeli interests are not identical. But he goes much further, making the Israeli–Palestinian conflict bear more explanatory weight than it can carry and proposing a rupture in U.S.–Israeli relations that would likely weaken moderates, embolden rejectionists, and reduce America’s influence rather than increase it.

Let us begin with common ground. Israel’s settlement enterprise has been a grave error. It has burdened Israeli democracy, humiliated Palestinians, complicated any future border, and allowed extremists on both sides to argue that compromise is impossible. The route of the security barrier, where it cuts deep into the West Bank rather than following the Green Line, creates legitimate fears of de facto annexation. A viable Palestinian state cannot be a collection of disconnected cantons. Washington should say these things plainly.

It is also true that the United States has too often acted as if support for Israel required public indulgence of every Israeli government. That has not helped Israel. Friends do not encourage friends to avoid hard choices. If the United States had been firmer earlier about settlements, Israelis and Palestinians alike might now face a less poisonous landscape. A serious American president should put forward parameters close to those discussed at Camp David, in the Clinton proposals, and at Taba: two states, borders based on 1967 lines with agreed swaps, security arrangements, a shared Jerusalem formula, and a refugee solution that acknowledges Palestinian suffering without ending Israel as a Jewish state.

But Walt’s argument falters when he describes America’s support for Israel as a central reason for terrorism against the United States and anti-Americanism in the Muslim world. It is one reason, but not the master key. Al Qaeda’s ideology is not a human-rights campaign for Palestinians. Its declared grievances include U.S. troops in Saudi Arabia, sanctions on Iraq, support for Arab regimes, Western cultural influence, and the existence of secular and pluralist politics. Its ambitions extend far beyond ending occupation in the West Bank and Gaza. Osama bin Laden invokes Palestine because it resonates emotionally, but his movement’s program is theocratic revolution. If Israel disappeared tomorrow, jihadists would not become liberals.

Moreover, Arab regimes have long used Palestine to deflect attention from their own failures. State-controlled media in the region have promoted grotesque conspiracy theories, anti-Semitism, and fantasies about American power. The anger is real, but it is shaped by governments that deny their own citizens meaningful political participation. Walt acknowledges U.S. support for autocrats, but he understates how deeply authoritarian stagnation drives resentment. A just Israeli–Palestinian peace would improve America’s standing. It would not cure the political sickness of the region.

Walt also gives insufficient attention to Palestinian agency. The familiar claim that Arafat did not simply “reject” the Clinton parameters is technically defensible; both sides had reservations. But it obscures a larger truth: Palestinian leadership failed catastrophically in 2000 and 2001. The second intifada, with suicide bombings in buses, cafes, and hotels, convinced many Israelis that territorial concessions brought terror rather than peace. Israeli politics did not move rightward in a vacuum. It moved rightward because ordinary Israelis concluded that the other side would not accept any Jewish state within any borders. That conclusion may be too sweeping, but it is understandable.

Any American strategy that pressures Israel while failing to build accountable Palestinian institutions will fail. The death of Yasser Arafat and the election of Mahmoud Abbas offer an opening. Abbas has spoken against the militarization of the intifada and may be a genuine partner. But he must establish one authority, one law, and one gun. Hamas, Islamic Jihad, and the al-Aqsa Martyrs Brigades cannot retain independent military capacities while diplomacy proceeds. Israel should freeze settlement activity and ease conditions where security permits; Palestinians must stop attacks on civilians and end incitement. The United States should help train security forces accountable to the Palestinian Authority, support economic reconstruction, and enlist Arab states to provide money and political cover for compromise.

Walt’s threat to end U.S. economic and military support if Israel refuses a viable settlement may sound like tough love, but it is more likely to be heard in Israel as abandonment. Israelis live in a small country with memories of extermination and wars of survival. They face Hezbollah rockets from Lebanon, Iranian hostility, Syrian rejectionism, and Palestinian terrorism. To announce that America may cut support unless Israel accepts Washington’s view of justice would strengthen those Israelis who argue that the world cannot be trusted and that only unilateral force matters. It would also encourage Palestinian maximalists to believe that time and violence can split Israel from its only indispensable ally.

American leverage works best when paired with reassurance. The first President Bush showed this during the loan-guarantee dispute over settlements, but he also convened Madrid after the Gulf War from a position of regional strength. Pressure without trust produces defiance; trust without pressure produces drift. The United States should be Israel’s closest friend and its most candid critic. Those roles are not contradictory.

Walt’s broader offshore-balancing framework compounds the problem. In the Middle East, alliances and perceptions matter. If the United States appears to downgrade Israel dramatically while seeking bargains with Iran or Syria, regional actors may not become more moderate. They may conclude that American commitments are fluid and that pressure works. Conversely, if Washington remains firmly committed to Israel’s security while insisting on Palestinian statehood, it can make compromise less frightening. Israel withdrew from Sinai because peace with Egypt came with American guarantees. Future withdrawals may require similar guarantees, perhaps including international monitors, early-warning arrangements, and long-term aid.

There is also a moral dimension Walt’s strategic language misses. Israel is not simply a client. It is a democratic society with a free press, independent courts, competitive elections, and real internal debate about occupation and peace. Its flaws are serious, but it shares with the United States a political vocabulary that no neighboring regime yet fully shares. That does not entitle Israel to a blank check. It does mean the relationship has a foundation deeper than expedience. Americans do not support Israel only because of lobbyists; many do so because they see a small democracy under siege and because they recognize the Jewish people’s historical claim to self-determination after centuries of persecution.

None of this negates Palestinian rights. Palestinians deserve freedom, dignity, and a viable state. Occupation is corrosive and must end. The United States should say so, and it should act accordingly. But a durable peace will be built by reconciling two national movements, not by treating one as a strategic liability to be disciplined and the other as a passive victim.

Walt is right to urge American self-restraint and wiser diplomacy. He is right that Iraq has damaged U.S. credibility and that the war on terrorism cannot be won by force alone. But on Israel, he risks replacing one simplification with another. The United States should not give Israel unconditional support. It should give conditional support within an unshakeable commitment to Israel’s security and to Palestinian statehood. That is a harder policy than either blank-check friendship or threatened abandonment. It is also the only policy likely to work.
