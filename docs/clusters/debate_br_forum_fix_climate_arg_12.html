"While experimentalist governance is a useful framework for managing climate uncertainty, it will only be effective and legitimate if it centers global equity and ensures that wealthy nations provide the necessary finance and technology transfer to the Global South."

The most important sentence in David Victor and Charles Sabel’s essay may be easy to miss. Discussing the Montreal Protocol, they note that developing countries joined because they were given more time and because “essentially all costs of compliance” were paid by a Multilateral Fund financed by rich countries. That is not an administrative footnote. It is the political core of the ozone success story. It is also the part of the analogy that climate policy has so far failed to honor.

Experimentalist governance is an appealing idea. It recognizes that climate action cannot be reduced to a single treaty, price, or plan. It values practical knowledge. It treats uncertainty as something to be managed through learning rather than invoked as a reason for paralysis. These are real virtues. But when applied to climate change, experimentalism risks becoming an elegant theory that avoids the hardest question: who pays?

From the perspective of much of the Global South, the history of climate diplomacy does not look like a shared failure of overly global ambition. It looks like a long sequence in which rich countries, having built their wealth through fossil fuels, have declined to make emissions cuts at the pace justice requires and have failed to provide promised finance. The United States, Europe, Japan, Canada, Australia, and other industrialized economies have contributed a disproportionate share of cumulative emissions. Many still have far higher per capita emissions than poorer countries. Yet the impacts of warming—drought, flooding, crop loss, storms, heat stress, disease burdens, displacement—fall heavily on countries and communities with the least historical responsibility and the fewest resources for adaptation.

This is not a secondary issue to be addressed after the governance model is chosen. It determines whether any governance model can be legitimate.

Victor and Sabel criticize the old United Nations climate process for requiring global consensus among sovereign states, thereby allowing the least ambitious to dilute the outcome. There is truth in that. But consensus rules have also been one of the few tools available to weaker countries in a world where economic and geopolitical power is profoundly unequal. Small island states, least developed countries, and vulnerable African, Asian, and Latin American nations do not possess the market power of the United States, European Union, or China. They cannot force attention through trade sanctions or technological dominance. Global climate negotiations, frustrating as they are, give them a forum to insist that mitigation, adaptation, finance, and loss and damage be considered together.

A shift toward sectoral clubs and experimental regimes could accelerate innovation. It could also marginalize those countries. If standards for green steel, shipping, aviation, forestry, or agriculture are set mainly by powerful governments and large firms, the resulting rules may reflect the interests of incumbents in rich economies. They may reward firms already close to the technological frontier while punishing producers in countries that lack cheap capital, research infrastructure, or administrative capacity. What Victor and Sabel call “penalty defaults” may look, from outside the club, like coercion by the powerful.

This is not an argument against standards. The world needs high standards. It is an argument that standards must be paired with finance, technology access, and voice. Otherwise climate policy will reproduce the inequalities of the global economy while claiming to solve a universal problem.

Consider electricity. It is easy for analysts in wealthy countries to say that developing countries should leapfrog coal and build renewable grids. In many cases this is increasingly plausible: the cost of solar and wind has fallen dramatically, and many countries have extraordinary renewable resources. But integrating variable renewables requires transmission, storage, grid management, regulatory capacity, and often backup systems. It requires capital at low cost. Poorer countries often pay much higher interest rates than rich countries, which can make a solar project expensive even when the technology itself is cheap. If climate cooperation does not address the cost of capital, exhortations to decarbonize will ring hollow.

Or consider industrialization. Many developing countries want to move beyond exporting raw materials and low-value commodities. They want steel, cement, chemicals, manufacturing, infrastructure, housing, and jobs. Rich countries already built these things with high emissions. If new global standards make industrial goods more expensive, poorer countries need support to meet them. A border adjustment imposed by Europe or the United States on carbon-intensive imports may be defensible in climate terms, but if it is not linked to technology transfer and development finance, it can become another barrier to development.

Forests pose an even sharper dilemma. Victor and Sabel describe hybrid sectors such as palm oil and forestry, where international markets can impose standards on local production. That is true, and deforestation must be stopped. But forests are not empty carbon sinks. They are inhabited by Indigenous peoples, small farmers, forest communities, and workers whose livelihoods are shaped by land tenure, commodity prices, corruption, violence, and state neglect. International certification schemes have a mixed record. Some improve practices; others shift burdens onto small producers while large actors adapt or evade. A climate regime that treats forests primarily as carbon assets may invite land grabs and exclusion unless local rights are central.

The Montreal Protocol succeeded partly because the financial mechanism was concrete, targeted, and connected to implementation. It paid not only for technologies but for regulatory capacity. That lesson should be expanded dramatically for climate change. The Green Climate Fund and other climate finance channels remain far from sufficient. The long-standing pledge by developed countries to mobilize $100 billion per year by 2020 has been politically important precisely because trust is so low. Even if met, it would be modest compared with the scale of need. Adaptation alone will require vast resources, and mitigation in developing countries cannot be financed mainly through loans that deepen debt burdens.

A just experimentalism would begin with the premise that climate cooperation must build capacity where capacity has been denied or underdeveloped. It would fund public institutions, not just private projects. It would support national development strategies compatible with decarbonization. It would make clean technology affordable, including through licensing arrangements, joint ventures, open standards, and public research partnerships. It would recognize that intellectual property rules can obstruct diffusion if treated as sacrosanct. It would measure success not only by tons of carbon avoided but by energy access, public health, employment, food security, and democratic control.

The Covid-19 pandemic has made this more urgent. Many developing countries face collapsing revenues, rising debt, capital flight, and severe public health burdens. Calls for a green recovery mean very different things in countries that can borrow cheaply in their own currencies and countries that cannot. If the global response to the pandemic leaves poorer countries with austerity while rich countries subsidize their own green industries, the climate transition will become another chapter in unequal globalization. Debt relief, concessional finance, and support from multilateral development banks are climate policies, whether or not they are labeled as such.

Victor and Sabel are correct that Paris cannot directly manage decarbonization sector by sector. But Paris and the broader UN climate system remain essential because they keep equity visible. The principle of “common but differentiated responsibilities and respective capabilities” is not an outdated obstacle. It is a recognition of historical reality. Paris modified the sharp Kyoto divide between developed and developing countries, but it did not erase responsibility. A regime that ignores differentiation will not be politically sustainable.

This matters for domestic politics in rich countries as well. Climate policy that presents decarbonization as a race for technological advantage may gain support from industrial strategists, but it can easily slide into green nationalism. The United States and Europe may seek to dominate clean energy supply chains, set standards others must follow, and protect domestic firms. Some of that may be politically unavoidable. But if green industrial policy becomes a tool for hoarding the benefits of transition, poorer countries will resist, and rightly so. Climate cooperation must create shared prosperity, not merely cleaner competition.

There is also a moral hazard in focusing too much on innovation by leading firms. Many of the communities most vulnerable to climate change do not need breakthrough technologies first. They need flood defenses, drought-resistant agriculture, early warning systems, decentralized renewable energy, public health infrastructure, and secure land rights. They need adaptation. The lead essay is primarily about mitigation, but “fixing the climate” cannot mean reducing emissions while neglecting the harms already locked in. For small island states and climate-vulnerable communities, adaptation and loss and damage are not bargaining chips. They are matters of survival.

Experimentalist governance could help here too. Adaptation is highly local, uncertain, and dependent on community knowledge. River basin management, coastal planning, water systems, and agricultural extension all fit the experimentalist model. But adaptation finance has been far weaker than mitigation finance, in part because it generates fewer profits for private investors. If experimentalism is tied too closely to market incentives and firm innovation, it will miss the areas where public and community capacity matter most.

The proper response is not to reject Victor and Sabel’s framework but to radicalize it. The experimentalist question should not be only: how do we induce sectors to innovate? It should also be: how do we build fair institutions for shared learning across unequal power? Who sits at the table? Who controls data? Who receives funds? Who owns the resulting technology? Who bears the risk of failed experiments? Who can veto projects that threaten their land or livelihood? Who decides when a laggard is culpable and when it is simply under-resourced?

A climate regime worthy of the name must combine experimentation with reparative justice. It must acknowledge that rich countries owe not charity but support rooted in responsibility. It must give developing countries room to pursue dignified development while helping them avoid the fossil path rich countries took. It must protect vulnerable communities from both climate impacts and unjust climate policies.

Victor and Sabel are right that the world should learn from Montreal. But the lesson is not just sectoral problem solving. It is that cooperation became possible when the costs of transition were recognized and shared. For climate change, those costs are larger, the inequalities deeper, and the stakes higher. The world does not merely need better experiments. It needs a settlement that makes experimentation fair.
