"Global warming can be stopped by replacing economic fatalism and rigid regulation with market-based incentives and human ingenuity."

In the often overheated debate over climate change, Kerry Emanuel’s essay is a welcome injection of empirical sobriety. He tells us what the science can assert with high confidence, what it merely suspects, and where the blanks on the map still lie. But his closing metaphor of an insurance policy, while evocative, does not quite answer the question his editors have posed. Insurance is a contract we enter into to protect against losses we hope never to incur. When it comes to climate, the far more constructive question is whether we can stop—or at least stabilise—the ongoing warming by designing a response that is not merely prudent but actively beneficial, a source of new wealth and technological vitality rather than a burden of self-denial. The answer, I believe, is a qualified but emphatic yes: we can stop global warming, but only if we abandon the twin dogmas of economic fatalism and command-and-control regulation, and instead trust in the power of market incentives and human ingenuity.

There is a rich and instructive history of successful environmental policy that points the way. In the 1980s, acid rain was widely touted as an ecological catastrophe in the making, brought on by the sulfur dioxide emissions from coal-fired power stations. The United States responded not by shutting down the power sector or imposing rigid technological mandates, but by creating a cap-and-trade system that put a price on sulfur dioxide and allowed emitters to find the cheapest way to reduce their pollution. The result was a steep drop in acid deposition, achieved at a fraction of the cost that even the most optimistic ex ante estimates had projected. Not a single generating station was forced to close; the system’s flexibility unleashed a wave of innovation in scrubber technology and fuel switching, and the sky above the northeastern forests grew cleaner by the year. The same pattern held for the Montreal Protocol’s phase-out of ozone-depleting substances: a clear regulatory signal that a harm needed to be stopped, combined with a reliance on industry to find the how.

Climate change is a vastly larger and more complex problem than acid rain or the ozone hole, but the basic structure of a successful solution is the same. We need a transparent, economy-wide price on carbon. Whether that price is implemented through a carbon tax or a cap-and-trade system with tradable permits matters less than that it is stable, predictable, and gradually rising over time. Such a price would do what prices do everywhere in a market economy: it would make clean energy competitive, penalise waste, and send an unmistakeable signal to engineers, venture capitalists, and corporate research labs that the next great wave of innovation lies in low- and zero-carbon energy systems. Once that signal is in place, the state need not play the role of central planner picking winners. The market will do the sorting.

The technological toolkit is already far richer than the doom-sayers appreciate. Consider nuclear power. The popular imagination, shaped by the accidents at Three Mile Island and Chernobyl, paints it as too dangerous to expand. But the next generation of reactor designs—the so-called Generation IV reactors—incorporates passive safety features that make a meltdown virtually impossible, and several designs burn existing nuclear waste as fuel, radically reducing the long-lived radioactivity problem. France generates nearly 80 percent of its electricity from nuclear plants and has done so for decades with an exemplary safety record. If the United States and other industrialised nations are serious about decarbonising their electricity grids, a pragmatic reappraisal of nuclear energy is in order.

Simultaneously, the cost of renewable energy is falling along learning curves that are reminiscent of the early days of the semiconductor industry. Wind power is already competitive with natural gas in the windiest corridors of the Great Plains and the North Sea. Solar photovoltaics remain expensive, but thin-film technologies and economies of scale in manufacturing have the potential to bring costs below grid parity within a decade or two. The challenge of intermittency—the fact that the sun does not always shine and the wind does not always blow—is being addressed through advances in battery storage, compressed-air energy storage, and the creation of high-voltage direct-current transmission lines that can move electricity from where it is generated to where it is needed across continental distances. And for the transition period, a new generation of natural-gas combined-cycle plants can serve as flexible backup, emitting roughly half the carbon dioxide per kilowatt-hour of an old-fashioned coal plant.

There is also the seductive promise of carbon capture and sequestration. Coal is the most abundant and cheapest fossil fuel, and it will be burned for decades regardless of what treaties are signed. If we can learn to capture the carbon dioxide from coal-fired power stations and inject it into deep saline aquifers or depleted oil and gas fields, we can have our cake and eat it too. The technology is in its infancy, and there are legitimate concerns about the permanence of storage, but several pilot projects are already underway, and the scale of the geological resource appears vast. With the right price on carbon, there is every reason to expect that these technical hurdles will be overcome.

What, then, of the developing world? The objection that a carbon price would impose an intolerable burden on poor nations is not a permanent barrier; it is a design problem. The solution is to combine a domestic carbon tax in the developed world with a border tax adjustment—a tariff on imports from countries that do not have an equivalent carbon price—and to use a portion of the revenue to fund clean-technology transfers to developing nations. Such a system would not only protect domestic industry from unfair competition but also create a powerful incentive for emerging economies to adopt their own climate policies and join an international carbon-pricing club. This is not protectionism in disguise; it is the only way to prevent the leakage of emissions to countries with lax regulation and to bring the whole planet into a cooperative framework.

The political obstacles to any such rational scheme are, of course, immense. The mere mention of a carbon tax sends legislators into paroxysms, particularly in a United States where anti-tax sentiment has been a staple of electoral politics for a generation. But the politics of climate are changing, and more quickly than many realise. A coalition is forming that includes not only environmentalists but also evangelicals, farmers who see opportunity in biofuels and carbon credits, venture capitalists looking for the next big thing, and national-security hawks who want to reduce dependence on Middle Eastern oil. This coalition does not need to be persuaded that the planet is hurtling toward catastrophe; it needs to be shown that the cure is not worse than the disease, and that in fact the cure is a source of comparative advantage and long-term prosperity.

That is the message the defenders of the market should be spreading. We can stop global warming, not by retreating into a pre-industrial past of hair shirts and self-abnegation, but by unleashing the same forces of competitive innovation that gave the world the personal computer, the mobile phone, and the internet in the space of a single generation. Kerry Emanuel’s science tells us we have a problem. The task now is to design a solution that works with human nature rather than against it. And we already have the blueprint.

"While Emanuel provides a precise scientific overview of climate change, his measured tone risks understating the existential urgency of the crisis and the necessity of immediate, large-scale political action to avoid catastrophic, irreversible tipping points."

Kerry Emanuel has performed a vital public service by laying out the state of climate science with an almost surgical precision. His essay is clear about what we know—the indisputable rise in greenhouse gases, the warming that has already occurred, the peculiar and ominous amplification of hydrological extremes—and equally clear about where the gaps in our understanding lie. Yet for all its level-headedness, the essay’s greatest strength is also its greatest weakness: the measured cadence of the scientist can easily lull the casual reader into a false sense that the risks we face are, in the end, manageable uncertainties.

They are not. Or rather, they are manageable only if we take them with the existential seriousness they demand, and only if we act with a speed and determination that our political systems have so far conspicuously failed to show. The question “Can we stop global warming?” must be reframed. The proper question is “Can we afford not to try?”—and the answer, when one looks squarely at the tail-end risks Emanuel only hints at, is an emphatic no.

Emanuel mentions, almost in passing, one of the most frightening features of the paleoclimate record: the ice cores from Greenland and Antarctica show that the climate system has never responded to gradual changes in radiative forcing in a smooth, linear way. Instead, it has repeatedly stayed stable for long periods and then jumped abruptly to a wholly different state, sometimes within a single human lifetime. We do not understand the triggers for these jumps. We do not understand why the overturning circulation of the oceans, the extent of sea ice, or the distribution of vegetation might suddenly reorganize. But we know that by pushing atmospheric carbon dioxide to levels not seen in at least 650,000 years—and doing so at a rate that far exceeds any natural variation in the paleo-record—we are pulling blind levers on a machine we only crudely understand.

This is not to dismiss the constructive work of the IPCC, but rather to underscore the poverty of the precautionary principle as it is actually applied in public debate. The precautionary principle holds that when an action carries a risk of severe or irreversible harm, the burden of proof falls on those who wish to proceed. By that standard, the continued unrestrained loading of the atmosphere with greenhouse gases is almost recklessly irresponsible. The worst-case outcomes being discussed by climate scientists—a collapse of the West Antarctic ice sheet leading to a twenty-foot rise in sea level, a shutdown of the North Atlantic thermohaline circulation creating regional cooling even as the globe warms, a massive dieback of the Amazon rainforest turning a carbon sink into a carbon source—are not merely remote possibilities; they are specific, physically plausible pathways that lie within the envelope of uncertainty our own models project. And they are, on any human timescale, irreversible.

Emanuel is right to point out that there would be some winners in a warmer world. High-latitude agriculture might expand, heating bills would fall, and fewer people would die from extreme cold. But he understates—perhaps because of his own professional restraint—the overwhelming character of the downside. The same climate models that project marginal agricultural gains in Canada and Siberia project agricultural collapse in the tropics and subtropics, where hundreds of millions of subsistence farmers already live on the edge. The fact that the tropics are expected to warm less than the polar regions is cold comfort when one considers that tropical ecosystems and human societies are already adapted to a narrow temperature range and are far less able to absorb shocks. Moreover, the projected increase in the intensity of both floods and droughts—a consequence Emanuel elegantly derives from basic thermodynamics—will not evenly distribute itself. It will fall disproportionately on the very regions least able to cope: the monsoon belts of Asia, the drylands of Africa, the hurricane-prone coasts of the Caribbean and Central America.

And then there are the hurricanes, which Emanuel knows better than almost anyone. His own work shows that the integrated power of North Atlantic tropical cyclones has jumped by some 60 percent since the 1970s, a change that is strongly correlated with rising sea surface temperatures. The 2005 season, with Katrina, was not an aberration but a preview. Even if the frequency of such storms declines, as some models suggest, a world in which every tropical cyclone that does form has a higher probability of being a Category 4 or 5 monster is a world in which coastal megacities are gambling with their futures. The $200 billion price tag from Katrina will look quaint if a comparable storm scores a direct hit on Miami or Houston, or if a rapidly intensifying storm catches the already vulnerable millions in the Ganges-Brahmaputra delta.

All of this brings us to the crux of the matter. Can we stop global warming? In the absolute sense of returning the climate system to its pre-industrial state, no. The thermal inertia of the oceans, the long atmospheric lifetime of carbon dioxide, and the doses we have already administered guarantee a further warming of at least a degree Fahrenheit, even if we were to cease all emissions today. But can we stabilise the climate at a level that avoids the catastrophic tail risks? That is still within our power, though only just. Models suggest that keeping the total warming below about 3.6°F (2°C) above pre-industrial levels would give us a good chance of avoiding the most calamitous ice-sheet and ecosystem responses. This would require global greenhouse-gas emissions to peak within the next ten to fifteen years and then decline sharply—by something on the order of 60 to 80 percent by mid-century. That is a monumental challenge, but it is not technically impossible. The technologies already exist: efficiency gains far beyond business as usual, a massive expansion of renewable energy, a renewed consideration of nuclear fission with enhanced safety and waste solutions, and, in the longer run, the development of carbon capture and sequestration for the fossil fuels we will inevitably burn in the transition.

What is missing is not technological capacity but political will. The United States, with its unique historical responsibility as the largest cumulative emitter and its disproportionate per-capita energy consumption, has been the principal obstacle to international action. The refusal to ratify the Kyoto Protocol, the stoking of false controversies about the science, and the insistence on purely voluntary measures have squandered a decade in which serious progress could have been made. There are signs that this is changing—the recent midterm elections, the growing engagement of evangelical Christians, and the pressure from corporate leaders who see regulatory certainty as preferable to chaos—but the clock is ticking.

In the end, Emanuel’s insurance analogy is exactly right, but it must be taken to its logical conclusion. Any prudent person insures their house against fire not because they believe a fire is likely, but because the consequences of being uninsured are catastrophic and unaffordable. The same logic applies to the global commons. We cannot say with certainty that a sudden climate jump will happen, any more than a homeowner can predict a lightning strike. But the house is made of wood, the landscape is dry, and we have already been piling kindling under the eaves for decades. The time to act as if our lives depend on it is now—because increasingly, they do.

"The appropriate response to climate change is a disciplined, cost-effective portfolio of policies that balances mitigation with economic development and adaptation rather than a singular focus on stopping warming at any cost."

Kerry Emanuel’s essay is a welcome corrective to the lazier forms of climate skepticism. Greenhouse gases are increasing; carbon dioxide has risen dramatically since the industrial revolution; the earth has warmed over the past century; sea level has risen; and arctic sea ice has retreated. These facts should be conceded. It is not serious to claim that human beings cannot affect the atmosphere. We plainly can, and we plainly have.

But conceding these points does not settle the policy question. Indeed, Emanuel’s own essay shows why the policy debate must be more disciplined than much of the public rhetoric surrounding climate change. He lists knowns, likely findings, uncertainties, possible surprises, benefits, costs, and complex regional effects. That complexity matters. Climate change is not a meteor headed toward earth, for which the only rational response is to divert every available resource. It is a long-term, uneven, uncertain, partly harmful, partly beneficial, and deeply entangled global externality. The right response is not denial. But neither is panic.

The central question is not “Can we stop global warming?” If “stop” means halt increases in average temperature over the next century, the answer is almost certainly no. The carbon already emitted, the inertia of the oceans, the longevity of energy infrastructure, and the growth aspirations of developing countries make some continued warming unavoidable. If “stop” means eliminate the use of fossil fuels in the near future, the answer is also no, unless we are prepared to impose enormous human costs, especially on the poor. The real question is: what combination of mitigation, adaptation, research, and institutional reform will reduce climate risk at reasonable cost?

That phrase “reasonable cost” is often treated as morally suspect, as though counting costs were a way of evading responsibility. It is not. Cost-benefit analysis can be crude, but refusing to compare costs and benefits does not make policy more humane. Resources spent on one problem are resources not spent on another: clean water, vaccination, malaria control, primary education, sanitation, nutrition, or protection against existing natural hazards. A dollar spent poorly on climate policy is not an abstract accounting loss. It may be a dollar not spent saving a life today.

This is especially important because many climate damages are mediated by wealth and institutions. Hurricanes are dangerous meteorological events, but their human toll depends enormously on preparedness, warning systems, building standards, evacuation capacity, and governance. Emanuel cites the catastrophic 1970 Bangladesh cyclone, which killed hundreds of thousands, and Hurricane Mitch in Central America. These tragedies should move us. But they also demonstrate that vulnerability is not simply a function of storm intensity. It is a function of poverty. A storm that kills many thousands in a poor country may kill far fewer in a wealthy one with radar, roads, communications, emergency shelters, and functioning public administration. One of the most reliable forms of climate adaptation is economic development.

This does not mean we should ignore emissions. It means we should resist policies that slow development in poor countries in the name of preventing future harm. The world’s poor need energy: for refrigeration, irrigation, transport, fertilizer production, hospitals, schools, and industry. At present, the cheapest available energy in many places is fossil energy. Telling India or China to stop using coal while Europe and the United States grew rich on it is not only politically futile; it is ethically precarious. If we want cleaner development, rich countries will have to make it cheap, not merely demand it.

Emanuel emphasizes uncertainty, and properly so. But uncertainty cuts both ways. Climate sensitivity may be higher than expected; ice sheets may respond abruptly; hydrological changes may be severe. These possibilities argue for a degree of precaution. Yet uncertainties also surround the economic damages of warming, the costs of abatement, the pace of technological change, and the capacity of societies to adapt. Some regions will likely benefit from moderate warming. Agriculture may shift; high-latitude growing seasons may lengthen; cold-related mortality may decline. These benefits do not cancel the risks, but they complicate claims that warming is unambiguously catastrophic at all margins.

One danger in the current debate is the tendency to treat every extreme weather event as a political advertisement. Emanuel’s own work on tropical cyclone power has been central to the discussion, and his findings deserve attention. But hurricane science remains unsettled. The North Atlantic has seen an upswing in activity, and sea-surface temperatures are relevant to storm intensity. At the same time, hurricane records are uneven, natural multidecadal variability is real, and the relationship between frequency, intensity, wind shear, ocean heat content, and regional circulation is complicated. It is reasonable to expect stronger storms in a warmer world; it is less reasonable to present any single season, even the extraordinary 2005 season, as decisive proof of a policy conclusion. Good science is not helped by overclaiming.

What, then, should be done? First, governments should put a modest, predictable price on carbon. I prefer a carbon tax to cap-and-trade. A tax is transparent, administratively simpler, and less vulnerable to the creation of politically allocated permits. It gives businesses and households a reason to economize on carbon without pretending that regulators know the best technologies in advance. The tax should start low and rise gradually, allowing adjustment and investment planning. Its revenues should be returned through reductions in other taxes or through rebates that protect low-income households. The purpose should be to correct an externality, not to create a new fiscal trough.

Second, we should dramatically increase public funding for energy research. The most plausible path to deep emissions reductions is not permanent sacrifice but technological substitution. People will choose low-carbon energy when it is reliable, affordable, and convenient. Solar, wind, nuclear, carbon capture, advanced geothermal, batteries, efficient lighting, biofuels, and grid improvements all deserve research support, though not all will succeed. Government is poor at picking commercial winners, but it has a legitimate role in funding basic research and early demonstration projects whose benefits are too diffuse for private firms to capture.

Third, nuclear power should be brought back into the mainstream of environmental discussion. No large-scale energy source is risk-free. Coal kills through mining accidents, air pollution, and carbon emissions. Oil brings geopolitical entanglements and pollution. Gas is cleaner but still emits carbon. Wind and solar are promising but diffuse and intermittent. Nuclear power raises problems of waste, safety, and proliferation, but modern reactors can produce large amounts of electricity with very low carbon emissions. If climate change is as serious as many environmentalists say, then reflexive opposition to nuclear energy is hard to justify.

Fourth, adaptation must be taken seriously rather than treated as a concession to denial. Sea walls, zoning rules, wetland restoration, drought planning, crop research, water markets, heat-wave warning systems, disease surveillance, and stronger building codes are not distractions. They are practical measures that reduce vulnerability to climate variability whether or not particular events are caused by greenhouse gases. Even if emissions were stabilized tomorrow, societies would still face floods, droughts, hurricanes, and heat waves. Adaptation often yields benefits in the present, unlike some mitigation policies whose benefits arrive mainly in the distant future.

Fifth, we should be careful with symbolic treaties. The Kyoto Protocol was a diplomatic achievement, but its architecture is inadequate. It imposes targets on industrialized countries while leaving developing giants without binding limits; it encourages accounting games; and it lacks a credible enforcement mechanism. The United States Senate signaled its opposition to such an arrangement before the treaty was even completed. A future agreement must include all major emitters, but in differentiated ways. It should focus less on grand promises and more on measurable actions: technology standards, sectoral agreements, research cooperation, reduction of deforestation, methane capture, and removal of subsidies for the most carbon-intensive fuels.

There is also a political danger in framing the issue as an emergency requiring wholesale transformation of society. Emergency politics may rally the already persuaded, but it often breeds backlash among those who suspect that climate policy is a vehicle for unrelated agendas: anti-capitalism, hostility to suburbs, hostility to trade, or a desire for greater state control over private life. If climate policy is to endure, it must be compatible with economic growth, personal freedom, and democratic consent. Policies that require voters to accept permanent austerity are unlikely to survive, and policies that do not survive do not protect the climate.

This is where Emanuel’s “insurance” metaphor is useful, but only if used carefully. Insurance is not the same as spending any amount to avoid any risk. Rational people buy insurance at a price proportionate to the risk and the loss. They also reduce risk through sensible precautions: smoke detectors, building codes, diversification of savings, vaccination. Climate insurance should be similar. We should pay a premium, but we should argue seriously about its size and form. A modest carbon tax, strong research program, no-regrets efficiency measures, and adaptation investments are sensible premiums. Abruptly forcing energy prices to levels that damage industry, burden households, and slow development would be an excessive premium, particularly if it merely shifts production to countries with fewer constraints.

The climate problem also requires humility about governance. The atmosphere is global, but politics remains national. Democracies respond to voters with short time horizons; authoritarian states may move quickly but often hide failures; international agreements are difficult to verify; and energy systems are embedded in every sector of life. Anyone who proposes a simple solution has not understood the problem. We are not going to regulate our way out of climate change by decree, nor will voluntary virtue suffice. The best approach is a portfolio: price signals, research, infrastructure, adaptation, and international bargaining.

A further reason for moderation is that technological and economic forecasts over a century are notoriously unreliable. Imagine someone in 1907 predicting the energy, agriculture, medicine, aviation, computing, and population patterns of 2007. Some physical principles are stable; human systems are not. This does not mean we should ignore model projections, but it does mean we should avoid locking ourselves into rigid plans based on assumed futures. Flexible policies that can tighten or loosen as evidence accumulates are preferable to sweeping commitments that become either obsolete or politically unbearable.

None of this should comfort those who say we should do nothing. Doing nothing is itself a policy: a decision to use the atmosphere without charge and pass the risks forward. That is neither economically efficient nor morally defensible. But doing something is not the same as doing everything. Climate change is a serious problem among other serious problems. It deserves action proportionate to the evidence and the risk.

So can we stop global warming? Not in the literal sense, and probably not in the rhetorical sense implied by some campaigners. We can slow it. We can reduce the chance of extreme outcomes. We can make societies more resilient. We can develop technologies that may eventually allow deep cuts in emissions without condemning billions to energy poverty. We can avoid both complacency and hysteria.

Emanuel has done a service by presenting the science in measured terms and by acknowledging uncertainty. The policy world should follow his example. The right response to climate change is not to dismiss it, nor to declare the end of the world, but to govern prudently: price the externality, invest in knowledge, protect the vulnerable, and preserve the economic dynamism that will make future adaptation and innovation possible. If we do that, we may not “stop” global warming, but we will handle it better than either the deniers or the alarmists would lead us to believe.

"To effectively address global warming, we must shift from a politics of restraint and moralizing to a strategy of technological realism that makes carbon-free energy cheaper and more reliable than fossil fuels."

Kerry Emanuel asks whether we can stop global warming. My answer is: not with the tools most commonly offered, not on the timetable implied by much environmental rhetoric, and not by imagining that modern societies will voluntarily retreat from energy abundance. But we may be able to do something more realistic and ultimately more important: build an energy system that makes carbon obsolete.

The distinction matters. Much of the climate debate is conducted as though the principal obstacle were moral failure. People drive too much, consume too much, live in houses that are too large, fly too often, and refuse to accept limits. There is truth in this criticism. Waste is real. American energy habits are especially extravagant. But a politics built mainly on restraint will not carry the world. Billions of people want more energy, not less, and they have compelling reasons. Energy means clean water, light after sunset, refrigeration, transport, communications, mechanized agriculture, and industrial employment. A climate strategy that sounds like a demand for permanent scarcity will fail morally in poor countries and politically in rich ones.

Emanuel’s essay makes the case that the climate risk is serious. The accumulation of greenhouse gases is not speculation. Carbon dioxide has risen from about 280 parts per million before industrialization to about 380 today. The earth has warmed; sea level has risen; arctic sea ice has declined; precipitation patterns are expected to change; ice sheets may behave in ways we do not fully understand. Emanuel’s own work on hurricanes adds a vivid example of how warming may affect damaging extremes. One need not accept every worst-case scenario to conclude that the present course is unwise.

But the structure of the problem should push us toward technological realism. Carbon dioxide is emitted not because people enjoy emitting it, but because fossil fuels are extraordinarily useful. Coal is energy-dense, abundant, storable, and easy to burn. Oil is unmatched for transport. Natural gas is flexible and relatively clean at the point of use. The modern world is built around these advantages. To change that world, alternatives must compete not only in moral appeal but in cost, reliability, scale, and convenience.

This is why the question “Can we stop global warming?” may be less useful than “Can we invent and deploy non-carbon energy fast enough?” The atmosphere responds to cumulative emissions, and the energy system turns over slowly. Power plants last decades. Automobiles, refineries, pipelines, furnaces, ships, aircraft, and urban forms are not replaced overnight. Even if the United States and Europe cut emissions aggressively, rising demand in China, India, and other developing economies could overwhelm those reductions unless clean energy becomes the obvious choice for growth.

The first implication is that research and development should move to the center of climate policy. We need a scale of public energy innovation comparable to other national missions. The United States spends vast sums on defense and health research; energy research receives far less attention than the problem warrants. This is shortsighted. Better solar cells, cheaper energy storage, advanced nuclear reactors, carbon capture and storage, enhanced geothermal systems, cellulosic biofuels, efficient lighting, superconducting or otherwise improved transmission, smart grids, and new materials could change the economics of decarbonization. Not all will work. That is precisely why a broad portfolio is needed.

Some environmentalists distrust this emphasis on innovation because they fear it will become an excuse for delay. That fear is understandable. Politicians love research programs that postpone hard choices. But the opposite error is also dangerous: imposing near-term targets without creating the technologies that make long-term reductions feasible. If policy raises energy prices while alternatives remain inadequate, voters will rebel or industries will relocate. Durable climate policy must make clean energy cheap, not merely make dirty energy expensive.

A carbon price is therefore necessary but insufficient. It should be predictable and broad, preferably through a tax whose revenue is returned to citizens or used to reduce other taxes. A price on carbon tells millions of decision makers that emissions have a cost. It encourages efficiency and guides investment. But if the price is too high too fast, it will be politically unstable; if too low, it will not transform technology. The best role for carbon pricing is to pull cleaner technologies into the market while public research pushes them toward commercial readiness.

Second, we must reconsider nuclear power. It is difficult to take seriously a climate strategy that excludes the largest existing source of carbon-free baseload electricity. Nuclear energy has serious problems: high capital costs, waste disposal, accident risk, security, and proliferation. But the comparison should be with real alternatives, not imaginary perfection. Coal plants release pollutants and carbon continuously. Natural gas emits less carbon but still emits. Wind and solar are valuable but intermittent and geographically uneven. Hydropower is limited and ecologically disruptive. If climate change threatens severe harm, then expanding nuclear power under rigorous safety and nonproliferation rules is not a betrayal of environmentalism; it is a test of seriousness.

Waste is a political problem as much as a technical one. The volume of nuclear waste is small compared with the waste streams from fossil fuels, but it is long-lived and frightening. A responsible nuclear program must include transparent regulation, secure storage, and designs that reduce proliferation risk. It must not be a blank check for industry. Still, refusing nuclear power while warning of catastrophic warming is a contradiction that cannot be sustained indefinitely.

Third, carbon capture and storage deserves urgent demonstration. Coal will be burned in enormous quantities unless a practical alternative is available. China is building coal plants at a pace that should sober anyone who thinks climate policy can be made by treaty language alone. The United States also has abundant coal reserves and powerful coal interests. If carbon from coal can be captured and stored safely underground, the climate equation changes. If it cannot, we need to know that soon. Pilot projects, liability rules, monitoring systems, and geological surveys should be accelerated. Carbon capture may prove expensive or limited, but ignorance is not a strategy.

Fourth, efficiency remains the nearest-term opportunity. Efficiency is sometimes presented as a matter of personal virtue—turn down the thermostat, drive less, use compact fluorescent bulbs. Those actions help, but the larger gains come from standards and design: buildings that require less heating and cooling, appliances that use less electricity, industrial systems that recover waste heat, vehicles that travel farther on a gallon, and cities that reduce the need for long car trips. Efficiency has the rare advantage of often saving money. But it faces barriers: split incentives between landlords and tenants, lack of information, higher upfront costs, and habits embedded in infrastructure. Government can help through standards, labeling, financing, and procurement.

Fifth, adaptation should not be treated as defeatism. Emanuel rightly worries about sea level, hurricanes, floods, and droughts. Even a successful mitigation policy will not eliminate these risks. Coastal communities should revise zoning and insurance systems that encourage building in dangerous places. Wetlands should be restored where they provide storm protection. Water systems should be redesigned for variability rather than historical averages. Agricultural research should focus on heat and drought tolerance. Public-health agencies should prepare for heat waves and changing disease patterns. These steps make sense under a wide range of climate futures.

Yet adaptation has limits. One can elevate buildings, but not indefinitely. One can improve irrigation, but not create water without cost. One can build levees, but every levee creates a residual risk of catastrophic failure. Therefore adaptation is not an alternative to energy transformation. It buys time and reduces suffering while the larger work proceeds.

The international dimension is where a technology-centered strategy is most important. The Kyoto Protocol’s central weakness is not merely that the United States rejected it. It is that the treaty’s architecture does not match the energy reality of the twenty-first century. Binding caps on some industrialized countries cannot solve a problem increasingly driven by global development. Conversely, demanding immediate binding caps from poor countries is a nonstarter. A better approach would combine carbon pricing in rich countries, massive clean-energy research, technology partnerships with developing countries, sectoral agreements for major industries, and financing mechanisms that make low-carbon infrastructure cheaper than conventional alternatives.

For example, if advanced coal plants with carbon capture are more expensive than ordinary coal plants, who pays the difference in India or China? If efficient appliances cost more upfront, how are they financed? If tropical forests store carbon valuable to the whole world, how are local communities compensated for preserving them? These are not secondary details. They are the substance of climate diplomacy. A treaty that sets targets without answering investment questions will disappoint.

Emanuel’s emphasis on uncertainty also has implications for technological policy. Climate surprises may be worse than expected, but they may also take forms we have not imagined. A robust response should preserve flexibility. That means avoiding dependence on a single solution. Corn ethanol, for instance, is already politically popular in the United States, but it is not a climate panacea. It may compete with food, require significant energy inputs, and encourage questionable land use. Wind power is valuable, but transmission and intermittency matter. Solar power has enormous potential, but cost remains a hurdle. Nuclear can provide scale but raises governance problems. Carbon capture may help coal but could fail economically. A portfolio reduces the risk of betting wrong.

There is one more subject that should be discussed, though cautiously: deliberate climate engineering. Ideas such as injecting reflective particles into the stratosphere or brightening clouds are often dismissed as science fiction or moral hazard. Perhaps they should never be used. They could have dangerous side effects, would not address ocean acidification from carbon dioxide, and raise terrifying governance questions. Who decides the thermostat of the planet? Still, if Emanuel is right that abrupt climate surprises are possible, then research into emergency options should not be taboo. Research is not deployment. Understanding possible last-resort interventions may be prudent, provided it does not distract from emissions reduction. A fire extinguisher is not a substitute for not playing with matches, but one may still want to know whether the extinguisher works.

The politics of all this will be hard. Energy incumbents will resist change. Environmental groups will divide over nuclear power, coal capture, transmission lines, and large renewable projects. Conservatives will resist taxes and regulation. Liberals will be tempted to load climate bills with unrelated social programs. Developing countries will insist, with reason, that rich countries move first. The public will support climate action in the abstract but punish policies that feel like sudden increases in the cost of living. Any durable program must therefore be honest, gradual where necessary, firm over the long term, and visibly connected to practical benefits: cleaner air, technological leadership, reduced oil dependence, modern infrastructure, and resilience against disasters.

We should also abandon the fantasy that individual consumer choice alone can solve the problem. Buying efficient bulbs and hybrid cars helps, and symbols can shift norms. But the carbon intensity of daily life is largely built into systems: the electricity mix, transportation networks, building codes, industrial supply chains, and settlement patterns. People choose within structures. Climate policy must change the structures.

At the same time, we should abandon the fantasy that government command alone can solve it. Central planners cannot know the best energy future in advance. Markets are powerful discovery mechanisms. Entrepreneurs, engineers, investors, and consumers will find solutions regulators cannot predict. The role of government is to set the rules: price carbon, fund research, build infrastructure, regulate safety, protect the vulnerable, and ensure competition. Within those rules, innovation should be allowed to surprise us.

Can we stop global warming? Not by asking the world to become less modern. Not by pretending that a treaty can repeal the energy demands of billions. Not by relying on voluntary virtue or apocalyptic rhetoric. But we can change the technological basis of modernity. We can make prosperity less dependent on carbon. We can build machines, grids, fuels, buildings, and cities that emit far less while providing the energy people rightly desire.

Emanuel’s essay warns us that the climate system may be less forgiving than we hope. The proper response is not despair. It is a new seriousness about energy. If the industrial revolution unintentionally began a planetary experiment, the next revolution must be intentional: an energy revolution driven by science, engineering, markets, and public purpose. We may not stop warming altogether. But we can stop making carbon the foundation of civilization. That is the task.

## The Case for Measured Optimism

Kerry Emanuel's essay provides a valuable service by laying out both the evidence for anthropogenic climate change and the considerable uncertainties that surround it. Yet I fear that even this measured treatment may lead readers to conclude that the appropriate response is one of alarm rather than thoughtful action. Let me offer a counterpoint: the history of human civilization is largely a story of adaptation to environmental change, and there are good reasons to believe that our ingenuity, combined with proper policy incentives, can address the challenges that warming will bring.

First, let us acknowledge what Emanuel's essay largely dismisses: there are genuine benefits to a warmer world. The lead essay mentions them almost in passing, as if to say "sure, there will be winners and losers, but will the world really suffer?" This framing understates the magnitude of potential benefits. A world a few degrees warmer would see dramatically expanded agricultural zones in Canada, Scandinavia, and Russia. TheGreen Revolution of the 1960s and 70s, which saved perhaps a billion lives through increased crop yields, occurred partly because of favorable climate conditions. The Norse colonized Greenland during a warm period and then abandoned it when conditions changed—that was adaptation. The Dutch have spent centuries engineering systems to manage water at the edge of the sea. The American Midwest was transformed from prairie to breadbasket by agricultural technology. Today, we possess tools of analysis, prediction, and intervention that our ancestors could not have imagined. If sea level rises by sixteen inches over the next century as the IPCC projects, we will have decades to adjust infrastructure, breeding programs, and settlement patterns accordingly.

I am not suggesting we do nothing. The externality problem is real: carbon emissions impose costs on future generations that are not reflected in current market prices. This is a legitimate case for policy intervention. But the appropriate response is a carbon tax or emissions trading system—not the sweeping transformations of energy infrastructure that some advocate. A properly calibrated carbon tax would internalize the externality, drive innovation toward lower-carbon technologies, and allow the market to find cost-effective solutions. We do not need to guess which technologies will work; we need only put the right price signal in place and let entrepreneurs and engineers do their work.

Consider what has happened in the energy sector over the past generation. Natural gas, once considered a minor fuel, now provides over 20 percent of American electricity, largely because deregulation and market forces made it cost-competitive with coal. Solar photovoltaic costs have fallen by a factor of four since 1990. Ethanol and biodiesel, whatever their limitations, represent significant private investment in alternatives to petroleum. Wind power is now competitive with conventional sources in many regions. These developments occurred not because of mandates or subsidies for particular technologies, but because market signals encouraged innovation.

The lead essay correctly identifies hurricane intensification as a serious concern, and I do not wish to minimize the suffering of those affected by storms like Katrina. But we should keep this in perspective. The United States has, over the past generation, invested hundreds of billions of dollars in weather forecasting, emergency management, and disaster response infrastructure. We have building codes, insurance markets, and evacuation systems that did not exist fifty years ago. The death toll from hurricanes has fallen dramatically even as the population and property value of coastal areas has increased. We are not helpless in the face of severe weather; we are learning to live with it more safely.

Emanuel's concerns about tipping points and climate surprises are legitimate, and I share his uncertainty about what lies ahead. But uncertainty cuts both ways. The same logic that warns us against assuming benign outcomes also warns us against assuming catastrophic ones. The medieval warm period, the little ice age, the sudden jumps documented in ice cores—these are reasons for humility, not panic. We do not understand the climate system well enough to know whether our emissions will trigger a cascade of positive feedbacks or whether negative feedbacks we have not yet identified will moderate the warming.

This is precisely why the insurance argument for carbon regulation makes sense, but at a measured level. We should price carbon to reflect reasonable estimates of damage. We should invest in basic climate science to reduce uncertainties. We should develop next-generation energy technologies. But we should resist the urge to restructure the global economy on the basis of speculative scenarios.

The question posed in the title—whether we can stop global warming—is perhaps the wrong one. We are already committed to some warming given the lag between emissions and atmospheric response. The more useful questions are: How much warming can we tolerate? How can we adapt to the changes that are coming? And how can we internalize the costs of carbon emissions in a way that harnesses rather than suppresses human ingenuity? These are the questions that policy should address, and they point toward carbon pricing and technological investment rather than the panicked overreaction that seems to grip so many commentators on this issue.

The history of environmental concern is littered with predictions of catastrophe that did not materialize. Paul Ehrlich's population bomb, the coming ice age of the 1970s, the ozone hole as an existential threat—each generated headlines and policy responses that now seem overblown. I am not suggesting climate change is a hoax or that we should ignore the evidence. But I am suggesting that a measured response based on cost-benefit analysis serves us better than alarm. We have time to adapt, and we have the tools to do so. Let us use them wisely.

## Beyond the Science: What Policy Makers Should Learn

Kerry Emanuel has provided a clear and accurate summary of climate science as it stood in early 2007. The facts he presents are not in dispute: CO2 concentrations are rising, temperatures are increasing, ice is melting, sea level is rising, and extreme weather events are likely to become more common. What is less clear from his essay is what follows from these facts—what actions policy makers should take and how they should think about the choices before them. That is the gap I want to address.

The first thing to recognize is that the climate change problem is fundamentally an economics problem. The atmosphere is a common resource, and using it to absorb carbon is an externality—a cost imposed on others that is not reflected in the market price of carbon-intensive goods. Left to their own devices, individuals and firms will emit too much carbon because they bear only a fraction of the costs. The solution is to put a price on carbon that reflects the full social cost of emissions. This can be done through a carbon tax or through a cap-and-trade system. Both approaches have advocates and critics, but both internalize the externality in a way that market forces can then respond to.

A carbon price is not sufficient by itself, but it is necessary. Without a price signal, there is no incentive for businesses to invest in lower-carbon technologies, for consumers to choose efficiency, or for entrepreneurs to develop new solutions. We have seen this played out in the electricity sector: when natural gas prices fell in the 1990s, utilities switched from coal because it was economically rational to do so. Market forces responded to price signals. The same would happen with carbon if the price were right.

The challenge, of course, is political. A carbon tax is regressive—it hits the poor harder than the rich because lower-income households spend a higher proportion of their income on energy and transportation. This political economy problem has blocked action for decades. The solution is to return the revenue from a carbon tax as a per-capita dividend, ensuring that low-income households come out ahead. This approach—popularized by economists like James Hansen and Greg Mankiw—would reduce emissions while protecting vulnerable households. It would also build political support for carbon pricing by making it feel like a dividend rather than a tax.

Beyond carbon pricing, government has a role to play in funding basic research and development. The private sector underinvests in energy research because the benefits of successful innovation are widely shared and cannot be fully captured by the innovator. We would not expect the market to develop adequate levels of research on nuclear fusion, advanced solar cells, or carbon capture technologies without public support. The Department of Energy's research programs, the national laboratories, and university research all play essential roles in developing the technologies that will power our future economy.

I am sympathetic to those who argue that international agreements are essential—that climate change is a global problem requiring global solutions. But international negotiations have been slow and will remain so as long as major emitters like the United States and China have incentives to free-ride on others' efforts. The better approach is to lead by example: adopt carbon pricing at home, demonstrate that it is compatible with economic growth, and negotiate agreements from a position of strength rather than hypocrisy. The United States cannot credibly demand sacrifices from developing nations while refusing to take action itself.

Emanuel's essay mentions that media coverage of climate change has been obsessed with sensational debate. I would extend this critique to much of the policy debate as well. We have spent too much time arguing about whether climate change is real (it is) and whether it is caused by humans (it is) and not enough time developing the mechanisms that will actually reduce emissions. The science is largely settled; the policy is not. This is where scholars and commentators should focus their efforts.

One area that deserves more attention is the potential for technological breakthroughs. The same forces that produced the exponential improvement in computing power—the combination of venture capital, public markets, and government research—could work in energy technology. The venture capital industry has already begun to invest in clean energy startups. Regulatory frameworks that allow these investments to flourish will be crucial. We need to reform permitting for transmission lines and nuclear plants, streamline approval processes for new technologies, and create markets for clean energy that do not distort the price signals.

Finally, we should not forget adaptation. Even if we stopped emitting carbon today—which we will not—the atmosphere will continue to warm for decades due to the long lifetime of CO2 already emitted. We need to invest in resilience: seawalls and flood control infrastructure, drought-resistant crops and irrigation systems, early warning systems for extreme weather. Adaptation does not excuse inaction on mitigation, but it is a necessary complement to it.

The science tells us what is happening. Now we need the policies to address it. A carbon price, public investment in research, regulatory reform, and adaptation funding—this is an ambitious but achievable agenda. What we lack is not knowledge but political will.

Kerry Emanuel’s sober assessment of the state of climate science offers a much-needed antidote to the polarized, sensationalist debate that too often dominates our public discourse. We buy fire insurance for our homes not because we are certain they will burn down, but because the cost of losing the home is unacceptably high. Economically speaking, it is one of the most favorable cost-benefit analyses ever presented to humanity. Our modern, heavily populated, and deeply interconnected global economy, with its highly optimized supply chains and fragile agricultural belts, may be even more vulnerable to sudden shifts in the hydrological cycle. Paying a 1 percent premium to prevent profound global destabilization is not environmental radicalism; it is fiscal prudence.

To halt the exponential rise in atmospheric water vapor and the attendant extreme weather Emanuel describes, we must radically reduce greenhouse gas emissions. The current international framework, the Kyoto Protocol, is fundamentally inadequate for this task. By exempting rapidly developing nations, Kyoto ignores the demographic and economic realities of the 21st century. China is currently on the verge of surpassing the United States as the world's largest emitter of carbon dioxide, fueled by a massive expansion of coal-fired power plants. India's energy demands are similarly surging. Any insurance policy that does not cover the entire globe will ultimately fail to prevent the worst consequences of warming.

Therefore, the United States must abandon its posture of defensive isolationism regarding climate policy. We must lead the forging of a post-Kyoto consensus that includes binding commitments from both the industrialized and developing worlds. To achieve this without stifling the economic growth necessary to lift billions out of poverty, we cannot rely solely on command-and-control regulations. We must harness the power of the market.

Implementing a global price on carbon—whether through a revenue-neutral carbon tax or a carefully structured cap-and-trade system—is the most efficient way to pay our insurance premium. A predictable price on carbon emissions would internalize the costs of the floods, droughts, and intensified hurricanes that Emanuel outlines. More importantly, it would send a massive price signal through the global economy, incentivizing the private sector to aggressively develop and deploy clean technologies. We need a renaissance in wind and solar power, next-generation nuclear energy, and carbon capture and sequestration. Furthermore, increasing energy efficiency will not only reduce carbon emissions but will also alleviate our dangerous geopolitical reliance on unstable fossil-fuel-producing regions.

Kerry Emanuel has done us a great service by laying out the physical realities of global warming with unflinching clarity. He has made it clear that while there may be some localized, short-term benefits to a warming world, the net threat to global stability, coastal geography, and human life is profound. The era of debating the fundamental physics of the greenhouse effect is over. As we look ahead to the rest of 2007 and beyond, the debate must shift entirely to the mechanics of our response. We possess the technological potential and the economic rationale to stabilize the climate. All that remains to be seen is whether we have the political maturity to sign the insurance policy before the disaster strikes.
