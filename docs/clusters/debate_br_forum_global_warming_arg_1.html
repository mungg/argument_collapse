"Global warming can be stopped by replacing economic fatalism and rigid regulation with market-based incentives and human ingenuity."

In the often overheated debate over climate change, Kerry Emanuel’s essay is a welcome injection of empirical sobriety. He tells us what the science can assert with high confidence, what it merely suspects, and where the blanks on the map still lie. But his closing metaphor of an insurance policy, while evocative, does not quite answer the question his editors have posed. Insurance is a contract we enter into to protect against losses we hope never to incur. When it comes to climate, the far more constructive question is whether we can stop—or at least stabilise—the ongoing warming by designing a response that is not merely prudent but actively beneficial, a source of new wealth and technological vitality rather than a burden of self-denial. The answer, I believe, is a qualified but emphatic yes: we can stop global warming, but only if we abandon the twin dogmas of economic fatalism and command-and-control regulation, and instead trust in the power of market incentives and human ingenuity.

There is a rich and instructive history of successful environmental policy that points the way. In the 1980s, acid rain was widely touted as an ecological catastrophe in the making, brought on by the sulfur dioxide emissions from coal-fired power stations. The United States responded not by shutting down the power sector or imposing rigid technological mandates, but by creating a cap-and-trade system that put a price on sulfur dioxide and allowed emitters to find the cheapest way to reduce their pollution. The result was a steep drop in acid deposition, achieved at a fraction of the cost that even the most optimistic ex ante estimates had projected. Not a single generating station was forced to close; the system’s flexibility unleashed a wave of innovation in scrubber technology and fuel switching, and the sky above the northeastern forests grew cleaner by the year. The same pattern held for the Montreal Protocol’s phase-out of ozone-depleting substances: a clear regulatory signal that a harm needed to be stopped, combined with a reliance on industry to find the how.

Climate change is a vastly larger and more complex problem than acid rain or the ozone hole, but the basic structure of a successful solution is the same. We need a transparent, economy-wide price on carbon. Whether that price is implemented through a carbon tax or a cap-and-trade system with tradable permits matters less than that it is stable, predictable, and gradually rising over time. Such a price would do what prices do everywhere in a market economy: it would make clean energy competitive, penalise waste, and send an unmistakeable signal to engineers, venture capitalists, and corporate research labs that the next great wave of innovation lies in low- and zero-carbon energy systems. Once that signal is in place, the state need not play the role of central planner picking winners. The market will do the sorting.

The technological toolkit is already far richer than the doom-sayers appreciate. Consider nuclear power. The popular imagination, shaped by the accidents at Three Mile Island and Chernobyl, paints it as too dangerous to expand. But the next generation of reactor designs—the so-called Generation IV reactors—incorporates passive safety features that make a meltdown virtually impossible, and several designs burn existing nuclear waste as fuel, radically reducing the long-lived radioactivity problem. France generates nearly 80 percent of its electricity from nuclear plants and has done so for decades with an exemplary safety record. If the United States and other industrialised nations are serious about decarbonising their electricity grids, a pragmatic reappraisal of nuclear energy is in order.

Simultaneously, the cost of renewable energy is falling along learning curves that are reminiscent of the early days of the semiconductor industry. Wind power is already competitive with natural gas in the windiest corridors of the Great Plains and the North Sea. Solar photovoltaics remain expensive, but thin-film technologies and economies of scale in manufacturing have the potential to bring costs below grid parity within a decade or two. The challenge of intermittency—the fact that the sun does not always shine and the wind does not always blow—is being addressed through advances in battery storage, compressed-air energy storage, and the creation of high-voltage direct-current transmission lines that can move electricity from where it is generated to where it is needed across continental distances. And for the transition period, a new generation of natural-gas combined-cycle plants can serve as flexible backup, emitting roughly half the carbon dioxide per kilowatt-hour of an old-fashioned coal plant.

There is also the seductive promise of carbon capture and sequestration. Coal is the most abundant and cheapest fossil fuel, and it will be burned for decades regardless of what treaties are signed. If we can learn to capture the carbon dioxide from coal-fired power stations and inject it into deep saline aquifers or depleted oil and gas fields, we can have our cake and eat it too. The technology is in its infancy, and there are legitimate concerns about the permanence of storage, but several pilot projects are already underway, and the scale of the geological resource appears vast. With the right price on carbon, there is every reason to expect that these technical hurdles will be overcome.

What, then, of the developing world? The objection that a carbon price would impose an intolerable burden on poor nations is not a permanent barrier; it is a design problem. The solution is to combine a domestic carbon tax in the developed world with a border tax adjustment—a tariff on imports from countries that do not have an equivalent carbon price—and to use a portion of the revenue to fund clean-technology transfers to developing nations. Such a system would not only protect domestic industry from unfair competition but also create a powerful incentive for emerging economies to adopt their own climate policies and join an international carbon-pricing club. This is not protectionism in disguise; it is the only way to prevent the leakage of emissions to countries with lax regulation and to bring the whole planet into a cooperative framework.

The political obstacles to any such rational scheme are, of course, immense. The mere mention of a carbon tax sends legislators into paroxysms, particularly in a United States where anti-tax sentiment has been a staple of electoral politics for a generation. But the politics of climate are changing, and more quickly than many realise. A coalition is forming that includes not only environmentalists but also evangelicals, farmers who see opportunity in biofuels and carbon credits, venture capitalists looking for the next big thing, and national-security hawks who want to reduce dependence on Middle Eastern oil. This coalition does not need to be persuaded that the planet is hurtling toward catastrophe; it needs to be shown that the cure is not worse than the disease, and that in fact the cure is a source of comparative advantage and long-term prosperity.

That is the message the defenders of the market should be spreading. We can stop global warming, not by retreating into a pre-industrial past of hair shirts and self-abnegation, but by unleashing the same forces of competitive innovation that gave the world the personal computer, the mobile phone, and the internet in the space of a single generation. Kerry Emanuel’s science tells us we have a problem. The task now is to design a solution that works with human nature rather than against it. And we already have the blueprint.

Vanilla GPT

"Climate change should be addressed as a problem of risk management under deep uncertainty, requiring a proactive combination of carbon pricing, technological investment, and ethical international cooperation."

Kerry Emanuel’s most important point is not that every place will be harmed equally by global warming, or that every scientific uncertainty has been eliminated. It is that climate change is a problem of risk management under deep uncertainty. That framing should move the public discussion beyond the sterile question of whether climate change is “proved” in some courtroom sense. We routinely spend money to insure houses against fires that are unlikely, maintain armies against threats that may not materialize, and regulate medicines and bridges before catastrophe supplies perfect evidence. The atmosphere deserves the same prudence.

The common rejoinder—that warming of a few degrees sounds mild—confuses averages with consequences. A small shift in average temperature can mean large shifts in extremes, water availability, crop reliability, disease ranges, and coastal exposure. Emanuel is right to emphasize that the danger lies not only in gradual warming but in the distribution of effects: more heat waves, stronger downpours, longer droughts, rising seas, stressed ecosystems, and possibly abrupt changes we do not yet understand. The question is not whether humanity can survive. Humanity survived ice ages, plagues, and wars. The question is how much avoidable suffering, displacement, ecological loss, and geopolitical stress we are willing to impose, especially on people least responsible for the emissions.

That ethical point deserves more emphasis. The “winners and losers” language can sound balanced, but the ledger is not morally neutral. If Canada or Russia gains a longer growing season while Bangladesh loses land to the sea, or if wealthy Americans buy air conditioners while poor farmers endure failed monsoons, we cannot simply add gains and losses as if they fell on the same households. Climate change is regressive across both geography and time. The industrial countries have placed most of the long-lived carbon dioxide in the air; the greatest vulnerability lies in poorer countries with less capital for irrigation, sea walls, public health, and disaster relief. Future generations, who have no vote in current energy policy, inherit the consequences of decisions made for our convenience.

Still, recognizing the danger is not the same as proving that “we can stop global warming,” if by “stop” we mean halt it quickly. We cannot. The climate system has inertia, oceans store heat, and carbon dioxide remains in the atmosphere for a long time. Some additional warming is already built into the system. The relevant goal is to prevent the most dangerous warming, slow the rate of change, and leave ourselves options. That requires reducing the flow of greenhouse gases toward the atmosphere and eventually moving the world economy toward very low net emissions. It also requires adaptation, because mitigation alone will not protect those already exposed to drought, flood, storm surge, and heat.

The encouraging news is that the first decades of reductions do not require waiting for miraculous inventions. Work by Robert Socolow and Stephen Pacala has popularized the idea of “stabilization wedges”: large but comprehensible blocks of action that, combined, could hold emissions on a safer path. Greater efficiency in buildings, vehicles, appliances, and industry is often the cheapest wedge. Better insulation, efficient lighting, combined heat and power, improved motors, and tighter fuel-economy standards can reduce energy demand without reducing welfare. There are also supply-side wedges: wind power, solar power, nuclear power, carbon capture and storage for coal, reduced deforestation, improved agricultural practices, and substitution of natural gas for coal where appropriate. None is sufficient by itself; all have costs and complications. But the claim that we must choose between modern civilization and climate protection is false.

The harder problem is political design. Energy systems are built around long-lived infrastructure, powerful incumbents, and prices that do not reflect environmental damage. If emitting carbon is free, we should not be surprised that firms and consumers emit too much. A carbon tax or a cap-and-trade system would put a price on the risk we are creating and allow millions of decentralized decisions to find the cheapest reductions. Economists may disagree about the exact price, but the direction is clear: carbon should no longer be treated as an unpriced sewer. Revenues from a carbon tax could be returned through lower payroll taxes, dividends, or assistance to low-income households, reducing the burden on families while preserving the incentive to conserve and innovate.

The United States has a special responsibility. It is among the largest emitters, has contributed heavily to the accumulated stock of greenhouse gases, and possesses extraordinary technological and financial capacity. Its refusal to ratify the Kyoto Protocol has damaged international trust, even though Kyoto itself is only a modest first step and excludes binding commitments for major developing countries. A workable global agreement must eventually include China, India, Brazil, and other growing economies, but it is unrealistic and unjust to demand that poor countries accept permanent energy poverty while rich countries continue wasteful habits. The bargain should be: industrial countries cut first and fastest, help finance clean development, and support technology transfer; developing countries accept increasingly serious commitments as their incomes and emissions rise.

One reason for urgency is that delay raises costs. Power plants, highways, suburbs, and factories built today shape emissions for decades. If the world builds a new generation of conventional coal plants without capturing carbon, the emissions path becomes far harder to bend. Conversely, clear policy signals now can influence investment before infrastructure is locked in. Business leaders often say they need certainty. A predictable, gradually rising carbon price, accompanied by public support for basic energy research and demonstration projects, would provide that certainty better than sporadic subsidies and speeches.

Adaptation must not be treated as surrender. Even an aggressive mitigation program will not eliminate climate-related risks. We need better floodplain management, stronger building codes in hurricane-prone regions, improved water storage and pricing, heat emergency plans, protection of wetlands, and more resilient agriculture. Katrina showed that disaster is never purely natural: vulnerability is shaped by poverty, planning, insurance, infrastructure, and government competence. Climate policy therefore overlaps with ordinary good governance. The same levee, hospital, evacuation system, or public-health network that protects against today’s extremes will be needed even more in a warmer climate.

Emanuel’s essay is strongest when it resists false certainty. Climate science contains uncertainties, but they cut both ways. Some feedbacks may be benign; others may amplify warming or produce abrupt changes. The possibility that the climate system is less sensitive than expected is not a reason for complacency when the possibility that it is more sensitive carries such grave consequences. In ordinary life, uncertainty about whether a loaded gun will fire is a reason to handle it more carefully, not less.

Can we stop global warming? Not entirely, and not immediately. But we can stop treating the atmosphere as free waste space. We can stop building policy around the hope that all uncertainties will favor us. We can stop pretending that cheap fossil energy is cheap when its costs are shifted to coasts, farms, forests, and future generations. The task is immense, but not mysterious: price carbon, invest in clean technology, improve efficiency, protect the vulnerable, and build an international bargain worthy of the scale of the problem. The real uncertainty is not whether action is possible. It is whether we will act before necessity removes the easier choices.
