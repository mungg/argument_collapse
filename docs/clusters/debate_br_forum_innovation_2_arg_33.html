"Dan Breznitz’s framework of localized, incremental innovation niches is insufficient for the current historical moment because the climate crisis requires a centralized, mission-oriented approach that collapses and accelerates all stages of production simultaneously."

In his essay, Dan Breznitz offers a measured, pragmatic view of innovation, arguing that local communities should abandon the dream of becoming the next Silicon Valley and instead find their niche within the four stages of global production. His examples of Taiwanese bicycle frames and Italian luxury shoes present a comforting vision of an economy where regions specialize, trade, and slowly build inclusive prosperity. But as we sit in the fall of 2021, with the window to avert catastrophic climate change rapidly closing, Breznitz’s framework feels radically insufficient. We do not have the luxury of time for incremental, localized, market-driven specialization. The climate crisis demands a paradigm shift in how we conceive of and deploy innovation.

Breznitz briefly mentions decarbonization under his "Stage 1: Novelty" category, framing it as a "fundamental system innovation" that requires government intervention. But characterizing the energy transition merely as a "Stage 1" problem vastly understates the challenge. Decarbonization is not a new product that can be invented in one place, designed in another, improved in a third, and assembled in a fourth over the course of decades. It is an existential imperative that requires the simultaneous, rapid mobilization of all four stages of innovation on a planetary scale.

The fragmented global supply chain that Breznitz accepts as the baseline reality is, in many ways, the enemy of the rapid transition we need. The model of hyper-globalization—where components are shipped back and forth across oceans multiple times to shave pennies off the margin—is itself incredibly carbon-intensive. More importantly, leaving innovation to the decentralized decisions of private firms and local policymakers seeking competitive advantage has utterly failed to reduce global emissions at the necessary pace.

To tackle the climate crisis, we need to move beyond Breznitz’s descriptive taxonomy of market niches and embrace a "mission-oriented" approach to innovation, as championed by economist Mariana Mazzucato. This means that the state must not just act as an enabler of local tech teens or a funder of basic research; it must act as the primary director of technological development, setting bold, clear goals—like a fully decarbonized grid by 2035—and coordinating all stages of innovation to achieve it.

Consider the challenge of deploying next-generation battery technology or zero-carbon green steel. We cannot afford the traditional timeline where a U.S. national lab makes a Stage 1 breakthrough, which is eventually picked up by an Italian Stage 2 design firm, then optimized by a Taiwanese Stage 3 manufacturer, and finally scaled in Shenzhen. The urgency of the crisis requires that we collapse these stages.

The U.S. government, mirroring the most aggressive aspects of Operation Warp Speed that Breznitz rightly praises, must concurrently fund the basic research, build the prototyping facilities, guarantee the market for the early, expensive iterations of the product, and construct the massive assembly plants. This is not about chasing "Silicon Valley" or seeking a narrow local advantage; it is about planetary survival.

Furthermore, the green transition offers the exact kind of "inclusive local prosperity" Breznitz desires, but on a much broader scale. Retrofitting millions of buildings for energy efficiency, building out smart grids, and deploying solar and wind infrastructure are inherently localized activities. You cannot offshore the installation of a heat pump or the weatherization of a home. This represents a massive opportunity for Stage 3 (process) and Stage 4 (deployment/assembly) innovation that is distributed across every single community in the country, not just isolated in specialized global hubs.

Breznitz warns against "techno fetishism," and he is right that we cannot just rely on an app to save us. But we must also avoid a kind of "incremental fetishism." Making bicycle gears slightly more efficient or lowering the cost of a smartphone chipset are impressive feats of engineering, but they are inadequate to the historical moment.

Innovation policy today must be entirely subsumed by climate policy. We must direct all our public and private capital toward the singular goal of decarbonization. If we succeed in this mission-oriented approach, the inclusive local prosperity that Breznitz seeks will be a natural byproduct of the massive, nationwide mobilization required to rebuild our infrastructure. If we fail, because we were too busy trying to optimize luxury shoe designs or chase software margins, no amount of local economic strategizing will save us from the ensuing ecological collapse.
