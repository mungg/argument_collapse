"Merve Emre’s essay effectively deconstructs the harmful myth of 'natural' reproduction by demonstrating that all procreation is technologically mediated and that the 'natural' ideal is a political construct used to marginalize non-normative families."

Merve Emre's essay performs a valuable service by debunking the myth of natural reproduction. In the current political climate, where "natural" has become a rallying cry for those who would restrict reproductive rights, this debunking is both intellectually important and politically urgent.

The essay's genealogical method is effective. By tracing the history of artificial wombs from 1894 to the present, Emre shows that reproductive technology is not new—it's been part of medical practice for over a century. The "natural" pregnancy and birth that many people imagine as their birthright is already thoroughly mediated by technological intervention: ultrasound imaging, prenatal testing, obstetric monitoring, hospital-based delivery with physician attendance. Home birth and midwifery are themselves interventions, choices made within a technological system.

This historical perspective undermines the nostalgic appeal of "natural" reproduction. There was never a golden age of unmediated birth, a time when technology didn't shape human reproduction. The distinction between natural and artificial is always already retrospective, constructed after the fact to serve particular political purposes.

Emre is particularly effective in her critique of "natural" as exclusionary. Her examples—lesbians, trans people, single women, women who can't carry pregnancies, adoptive parents—show how the discourse of natural reproduction marginalizes those who don't fit its mold. The ideology of natural reproduction isn't just mistaken; it's actively harmful to people whose reproductive lives don't conform to its templates.

The essay's case studies illustrate this marginalization with painful clarity. N and K's doctors dismiss them from reproduction on grounds of disability and mental illness—judgment calls that reveal how medical authority police who is "fit" to reproduce. B's fertility clinic assumes she's accompanied by her husband, then her friend, never her partner. These aren't individual failures of sensitivity; they're systemic expressions of heteronormativity embedded in medical practice.

Emre's treatment of cárdenas's bioart project is the essay's most politically engaged section. cárdenas's poem captures the urgency that many trans women feel about reproduction—the knowledge that they may not live long enough to have children, the determination to make life anyway. Her DIY approach to sperm banking is creative and defiant, a refusal to accept medical authority's pronouncements about trans fertility.

But Emre doesn't fully explore the implications of cárdenas's practice. DIY reproductive technology points toward a democratization of reproduction, a vision of individuals taking control of their own reproduction outside institutional settings. This vision is exciting but also concerning—what are the risks of self-managed reproduction? How do we ensure safety and quality? These questions require engagement that the essay doesn't provide.

The essay's political recommendations—universal healthcare, paid parental leave, LGBT protections—are welcome but underdeveloped. Emre states these demands almost in passing, without arguing for their connection to her philosophical analysis. A more integrated approach would show how the critique of natural reproduction leads directly to demands for structural change.
