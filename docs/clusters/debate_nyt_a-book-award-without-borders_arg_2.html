Diversified Claude

"The decision to expand the Man Booker Prize to all English-language writers is a wise move that reflects the global reality of modern literature and moves past outdated colonial boundaries."

The howls of protest greeting the Booker's expansion betray a curious anxiety: the fear that British and Commonwealth writers cannot compete with Americans on a level playing field. This is both insulting to those writers and almost certainly wrong. Hilary Mantel, Eleanor Catton, NoViolet Bulawayo, Jhumpa Lahiri — the Commonwealth-and-Ireland field is bursting with talent that would hold its own anywhere. If British literary culture really believes its writers can only win when Americans are barred from entering, it has a confidence problem far deeper than any prize rule can address.

The expansion is the correct decision for a simple reason: the category "English-language literature" no longer maps onto the borders of the old British Empire, if it ever did. Readers in Edinburgh and Auckland and Cape Town read Jonathan Franzen and Marilynne Robinson alongside Ian McEwan and J.M. Coetzee. To pretend otherwise — to construct a prize as if American fiction inhabited a separate planet — is to indulge a fiction that no actual reader believes.

There is also a slightly embarrassing colonial residue in the old eligibility rules. The Commonwealth is a historical artifact of empire; using it as the unit of literary citizenship implies that English somehow still belongs more to those who inherited it through British rule than to those who developed it elsewhere. Opening the prize gently dissolves that implication. English is a global language now, and its highest-profile literary prize is rightly catching up.

Will Americans dominate? Possibly, for a season. But prize cultures self-correct: judges, conscious of perceived bias, often lean the other way, and the Booker's distinctive sensibility — its taste for structural ambition, its impatience with mere domestic realism — is unlikely to vanish overnight. More likely, we'll see a more interesting longlist, with Anne Enright and George Saunders and a Nigerian debut novelist genuinely competing, and the discussion that follows will be richer for it.

The Booker is making a bet that excellence travels and that its own judgment is robust enough to identify it wherever it appears. That is exactly the bet a serious literary prize should make.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The fear that British and Commonwealth writers cannot compete with Americans is an unfounded insult to the high level of talent currently existing within the Commonwealth.
  2. 02The category of English-language literature no longer aligns with the borders of the former British Empire, making the exclusion of American authors an artificial distinction that readers do not recognize.
  3. 03Maintaining eligibility based on Commonwealth citizenship is a colonial residue that wrongly implies the English language belongs more to former British territories than to the rest of the world.
  4. 04The expansion will likely result in a more diverse and interesting longlist that fosters a richer global literary discussion.
  5. 05A serious literary prize should prioritize identifying excellence regardless of a writer's nationality rather than maintaining protective geographic barriers.
Diversified Claude

"The decision to open the Man Booker Prize to all English-language writers is a necessary and overdue evolution that aligns the award with the reality of a transnational literary culture."

Good. About time.

The notion that the world's most prestigious English-language literary prize should exclude writers from the country that produces the largest share of English-language literature was always strange. It was defensible mainly as historical accident: the Booker grew out of a particular post-imperial moment, and its Commonwealth-plus-Ireland frame made a kind of sense in 1969. It makes far less sense in 2013, when literary culture is unmistakably transnational and readers cheerfully ignore the national-origin labels publishers stick on book jackets.

The strongest objection to expansion is that American writers will swamp everyone else. I find this unpersuasive on two grounds. First, it underestimates the Booker juries, which have repeatedly shown an independent streak — favoring formal experiment, historical fiction, postcolonial voices — that no amount of American submission volume will simply override. Second, it overestimates American dominance. The most exciting English-language fiction of the past fifteen years has come at least as often from outside the United States as inside it: Mantel, Coetzee, Adichie, Mitchell, McCarthy yes, but also Catton, Atwood, Ondaatje. A genuinely open Booker will produce shortlists that reflect this diversity, not bury it.

The deeper point is that literary nationalism, however well-intentioned its protective forms, has costs. Prizes that fence off a domain to "protect" writers from competition tend, over time, to feel slightly minor-league — and to be seen that way by the very writers they meant to elevate. The Booker has avoided this fate largely because the Commonwealth contains so much extraordinary writing. But the fence was always going to look more anachronistic with each passing year.

Will something be lost? Perhaps a certain coziness, a certain sense that the Booker was "ours" in a way that Anglophone prizes administered from New York are not. That feeling has its charms. But a prize that aspires to identify the best novel in English should actually be open to the best novel in English. The administrators have caught up with reality. The reality was overdue.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Excluding the United States, the world's largest producer of English-language literature, is an anachronistic practice based on a post-imperial historical accident.
  2. 02Concerns about American dominance are unfounded because Booker juries have a history of independent taste and high-quality fiction is being produced globally.
  3. 03Literary nationalism and protective barriers ultimately diminish the prestige of an award and make it feel minor-league.
  4. 04A prize that claims to identify the best novel in English must be open to all writers in that language to maintain its relevance and integrity.
