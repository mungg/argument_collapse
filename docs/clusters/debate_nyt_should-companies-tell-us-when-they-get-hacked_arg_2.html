Diversified GPT

"Mandating broad public disclosure of all security breaches is a counterproductive policy that can compromise investigations and distract from meaningful security improvements."

Mandating broad public disclosure of hacks sounds attractive, but it may make us less secure. The problem is that “being hacked” is not a simple event. It can mean anything from a stolen employee password to a sophisticated, ongoing espionage campaign. Forcing companies to speak publicly before they understand the scope of an intrusion may reward attackers, mislead customers and discourage cooperation with investigators.

The recent attacks on newspapers illustrate the point. If a foreign intelligence service has entered a newsroom’s systems, the first priority should be to identify the access, monitor the intruder, preserve evidence and close the hole. Public disclosure at the wrong moment can tell the attacker exactly what the victim knows. It can also push the intruder to destroy evidence or move to other targets. In cases involving national security, trade secrets or organized crime, a premature announcement may do real damage.

There is also a danger of creating security theater. Companies will comply with whatever rule is written, and lawyers will shape the disclosure. The result may be a stream of vague notices that say little more than “an unauthorized party may have accessed certain systems.” Consumers will receive more letters, change a password or two, and then become numb. Meanwhile, the important work—better authentication, network segmentation, encryption, patching, employee training and incident response—will remain underfunded.

This is not an argument for letting companies bury breaches that expose credit card numbers or Social Security numbers. When customers face a concrete risk, they should be told. Existing state breach notification laws already move in that direction. But expanding that principle into a general requirement to announce “hacks” is too crude. What matters is harm, not intrusion. A company that loses unencrypted personal data should notify affected people. A company that detects a probe, a failed phishing campaign or a contained compromise of a non-sensitive system should not have to issue a public confession.

A better approach is confidential mandatory reporting to a trusted government or industry clearinghouse, paired with public notification only when individuals or markets face material risk. That would allow patterns to be detected without broadcasting vulnerabilities. It would also reduce the stigma of reporting, which is important: if every disclosure becomes a lawsuit, a news cycle and a stock-price event, executives will be tempted to define incidents narrowly and say as little as possible.

Security improves when information flows to the people who can act on it. Sometimes that is the public. Often it is investigators, vendors and peer companies. The law should recognize the difference.

"Mandatory public disclosure of security breaches should not be required because it often prioritizes symbolic transparency over practical security and can actively hinder effective incident response."

Be Careful What You Mandate

Everyone loves transparency in the abstract. The trouble with mandatory breach disclosure is that, in practice, it can produce more theater than security.

Consider what "disclosure" actually means in most existing state laws. A company discovers an intrusion, lawyers spend weeks deciding whether the legal trigger has been met, a carefully worded letter goes out months later offering a year of free credit monitoring, and customers either ignore it or sigh and move on. The information is too generic to act on, too late to matter, and too frequent—we are now numb to breach notices. If the goal is to empower consumers, the current system mostly fails them while imposing real costs on firms.

There is a more serious worry. The Times disclosed its intrusion only after a months-long investigation, working quietly with Mandiant to understand the attackers' tools, identify backdoors, and watch how they moved. Premature public disclosure would have tipped off the intruders, who would have burned their access and re-entered through a different door. Good incident response sometimes requires silence. A rigid timetable for going public can actively harm the defender.

Mandatory disclosure also creates perverse incentives. If reporting triggers lawsuits, regulatory inquiries, and stock-price hits, firms have every reason to define "breach" narrowly, to avoid looking too hard at suspicious activity, or to push detection responsibilities onto vendors. The same dynamic explains why so many hospitals were slow to embrace infection reporting: when you punish discovery, you get less discovery.

None of this means companies should be free to hide everything. Where personal data is genuinely exposed in a way that creates risk of fraud, customers should be told, and told quickly. But that is a narrow category. For the broader universe of intrusions—espionage, intellectual-property theft, probing attacks—what we need is confidential reporting to a trusted government body that can aggregate intelligence and warn peers, not press releases that mainly serve plaintiffs' lawyers.

The right question is not "should companies tell us?" but "what kind of telling actually makes us safer?" A blanket public-disclosure mandate confuses the symbolic value of openness with the practical work of defense. We can do better than turning every hack into a press cycle.

Diversified DeepSeek

"A blanket federal requirement for companies to disclose all security breaches would be counterproductive, as it would overwhelm consumers with trivial alerts and undermine effective security responses."

The demand for mandatory disclosure of every hack sounds like common sense. Who could be against transparency? But a blanket federal requirement to reveal security breaches would create more problems than it solves, punishing responsible companies and overwhelming consumers without meaningfully improving security.

First, we need to understand what actually happens when a company gets “hacked.” The term spans everything from a sophisticated nation-state intrusion into proprietary code to a script kiddie temporarily defacing a public-facing web page. A law that requires reporting all such events would generate an avalanche of meaningless alerts. Every bored teenager probing a firewall would trigger a press release. Consumers, already drowning in spam and privacy policies, would swiftly learn to ignore them — precisely the “cry wolf” effect that would allow serious breaches to go unnoticed.

Second, mandatory disclosure plays directly into the hands of attackers. Sophisticated adversaries often don’t immediately monetize stolen data; they lurk inside networks for months, mapping infrastructure and escalating privileges. If a company detects an intrusion and is forced to announce it within 48 hours, the hackers know they’ve been spotted and will cover their tracks, destroy evidence, or trigger a destructive payload before extraction is complete. Law enforcement and forensic investigators need time to conduct a covert response, trace the intruders, and lock down the damage. Mandatory notification blows that cover.

Small and mid-sized businesses would bear disproportionate costs. Unlike large banks or tech giants, a local retailer with a ten-person IT staff doesn’t have a crisis communications team or an army of lawyers to assess liability. Forcing them to issue a public statement before they even understand the scope of a breach invites lawsuits from every customer who ever set foot in the store, regardless of whether their data was actually taken. The result would be a wave of prophylactic bankruptcies, not better security.

The current system, though messy, has its own checks. State laws already require notification in cases where identity theft is likely. Customers can vote with their wallets, abandoning companies that mishandle their data. And security researchers share information informally through trusted circles, alerting peers to new threats without broadcasting them to the world. A smarter approach would invest in industry-wide sharing hubs — like the Financial Services Information Sharing and Analysis Center — where anonymized threat data can flow quickly without sacrificing ongoing investigations.

Mandatory disclosure is a blunt instrument that pretends all hacks are equal. They aren’t. Companies need the discretion to assess the real risk and respond appropriately, not a one-size-fits-all mandate written by politicians who still think a firewall is something out of a medieval castle.

Diversified GPT

"Companies should not be subject to a broad mandate to disclose every security breach because universal disclosure would create public confusion and undermine actual cybersecurity efforts."

No, not if the requirement is framed broadly as “companies must tell us when they get hacked.” That sounds attractive, especially after highly visible attacks on newspapers and technology companies, but it would produce more confusion than security.

“Hacked” is not a legal category. It can mean anything from a defaced Web page to an employee clicking on a malicious attachment to a sophisticated espionage campaign inside a network for months. Some events expose customer data. Some expose trade secrets. Some expose nothing but the fact that a company’s spam filter missed a message. Treating all of these as public disclosure events would create a blizzard of notices that consumers will stop reading.

Broad disclosure can also make defense harder. In the early stages of an investigation, a company may be trying to determine which machines are compromised, what accounts were used and whether the attackers are still inside. A premature announcement may alert intruders that they have been detected. If the company describes the method of attack, copycats may use it against others. If it says too little, the public learns nothing and assumes the worst. Either way, lawyers and public relations departments will dominate the response.

There is another perverse incentive: if every detected intrusion creates a public relations crisis, some firms will be tempted to know less. Better logging and monitoring will increase the number of reportable incidents. A badly drafted disclosure mandate could punish the companies that look hardest for intrusions and reward those that remain willfully blind.

This does not mean companies should be free to hide losses of personal information. Existing state breach notification laws already require notice in many cases involving unencrypted personal data. Regulators can and should punish deceptive promises about security. Banks, hospitals, defense contractors and utilities may need stricter sector-specific rules. Customers should absolutely be told when they face a concrete risk of identity theft, account takeover or exposure of private records.

But the broader cybersecurity problem is better addressed through confidential information sharing, stronger security standards, cyber insurance, enforcement against reckless practices and clear contractual duties between business partners. Companies need a safe way to share attack signatures without inviting lawsuits or headlines every time they detect an intruder.

Public disclosure is a tool, not a cure. Used when personal data or material business interests are at stake, it is essential. Used as a universal rule for every hack, it becomes security theater.
