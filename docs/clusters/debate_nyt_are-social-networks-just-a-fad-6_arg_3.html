"Social networks in their current form are a fad because the model of compulsive personal disclosure and public performance is socially and economically unstable."

Yes, social networks may well be a fad — not in the sense that people will stop using the Internet to communicate, but in the sense that today’s model of compulsive personal disclosure is unstable.

The current enthusiasm depends on a strange bargain: millions of people provide intimate information, photographs, preferences, friendships and emotional labor to companies whose business model is still only partly proven. The companies then sell access to attention. That arrangement may persist, but it is not obvious that it will remain attractive once users understand its costs.

The examples in the question point to the problem. A young woman in India who must worry that a Facebook photograph could affect her marriage prospects is not experiencing liberation. A family member who learns painful news about an estranged relative through a screen is not necessarily enjoying richer connection. Social networks often promise intimacy and deliver exposure. They collapse audiences that used to be separate: relatives, employers, classmates, political acquaintances and strangers. Human beings have always managed different selves in different settings. Facebook asks them to merge those settings and then calls the result authenticity.

The recent I.P.O. anxiety matters because it reveals how inflated the expectations have become. Investors are not merely betting that people like to share. They are betting that this sharing can be turned into reliable revenue on a massive scale, despite privacy concerns, mobile usage and user fatigue.

The telephone endured because it solved a basic problem without requiring people to perform themselves publicly. Social networks solve some problems but create others. They are useful for invitations, photos and lightweight contact, but their central cultural style — constant display, quantified popularity, publicized emotion — may come to look adolescent. The future may belong less to giant social networks than to messaging, smaller groups and tools that let people communicate without living in a permanent exhibition.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The business model of social networks relies on a lopsided bargain where users provide intimate labor and data in exchange for services that may not be worth the privacy costs.
  2. 02Social networks often damage genuine human connection by collapsing distinct social spheres and forcing users to merge their different identities into a single public profile.
  3. 03The financial expectations for social media companies are overinflated, as they assume user sharing can be infinitely converted into revenue despite growing fatigue and privacy concerns.
  4. 04The future of digital communication likely lies in private messaging and smaller groups rather than the permanent public exhibition required by current social networks.

"Social networks are not permanent utilities like the telephone but are instead historically specific, commercialized environments that may prove to be a transitional craze."

Social networks are not the new telephone. They are closer to the new shopping mall: a privately owned public space where people gather because everyone else is there, until one day they are not.

That does not mean they are trivial. Malls mattered. They shaped adolescence, retail, policing and suburban life. But they were historically specific institutions, not permanent features of human communication. Facebook, Twitter and similar services may prove to be the digital malls of the early twenty-first century: commercially designed environments that feel social but are structured around surveillance, advertising and crowd management.

The comparison with the telephone flatters social networks too much. The telephone was a utility. Its value was direct and durable: one person could speak with another across distance. Social networks add layers of performance, ranking and broadcasting. They encourage users to think of friendship as an audience and identity as a profile. That is a more fragile proposition.

Their political value is similarly overstated. It is plausible that Facebook and Twitter helped Egyptians and others circulate information, shame authorities and organize gatherings. But revolutions are not sustained by status updates. They require unions, parties, courts, newspapers, religious organizations, neighborhood committees, and habits of compromise. A network can help bring people to a square; it cannot decide what they should do after the dictator leaves.

The Facebook I.P.O. has usefully punctured a little of the mystique. A company can have hundreds of millions of users and still face doubts about profits, governance and long-term loyalty. Users are not citizens of Facebook. They are tenants. They can move.

The human desire to communicate will last. Digital tools will last. But the current social network form — one dominant platform asking everyone to maintain a semi-public life inside its walls — may be remembered as a transitional craze. We should not mistake ubiquity for permanence. Pet rocks were obvious fads; more serious fads build office towers before they fade.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Social networks are privately owned spaces structured around surveillance and advertising rather than essential public infrastructure.
  2. 02Unlike the telephone, social networks rely on fragile social performances and the broadcasting of identity to an audience.
  3. 03The political utility of social networks is limited because they cannot provide the institutional 'brick and mortar' necessary to sustain long-term societal change.
  4. 04The commercial instability of social media platforms demonstrates that users are transient tenants rather than permanent citizens of these digital spaces.
  5. 05The current model of maintaining a semi-public life within a single dominant platform is a specific cultural phase rather than a permanent feature of human communication.
