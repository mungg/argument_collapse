"Rereading is a profound and defensible literary pleasure that is particularly suited for the summer season."

The guilty pleasure I would defend is rereading. It has none of the glamour of discovery. It does not help one keep up with prize lists or dinner-party chatter. It can look, from the outside, like retreat. Yet rereading is one of the richest forms of literary pleasure, and summer is its natural season.

A new book often asks, “Do you like me?” An old book asks, “Who are you now?” That is a better question. Return to “Pride and Prejudice” at 18, 35 and 60, and you may find yourself sympathizing with different people each time. Reread “The Great Gatsby” after a recession and its glitter looks harsher. Reread children’s books after having children and the peril changes shape. The words may be fixed; the reader is not.

There is also comfort in knowing the path. During the year, we are trained to value novelty: new messages, new episodes, new scandals, new devices. Rereading resists the churn. It says that depth may come from repetition, that acquaintance can become intimacy. Musicians do not apologize for playing the same score more than once; readers should not apologize for returning to beloved books.

My summer shelf includes books I could almost recite: “To Kill a Mockingbird,” “The Long Goodbye,” “A Wrinkle in Time,” maybe a volume of Sherlock Holmes stories. Are they all perfect? Of course not. Some show their age. So do we. Part of the pleasure is noticing the seams and loving the garment anyway.

If there is guilt in rereading, it is only the guilt of being faithful in a culture that rewards flirtation. I can live with that.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Returning to familiar texts allows readers to track their own personal growth and changing perspectives over time.
  2. 02Rereading offers a necessary respite from a modern culture that overvalues novelty and constant consumption.
  3. 03Revisiting books fosters a sense of intimacy and depth that cannot be achieved through a single reading.
  4. 04There is a unique aesthetic pleasure in acknowledging and accepting the flaws of a beloved book as both the reader and the text age.
Diversified Claude

"The ideal summer reading experience is found in rereading beloved, familiar books rather than tackling demanding new releases."

Summer reading is, for me, almost always rereading. The new book demands too much: a leap into unfamiliar voices, the work of getting one's bearings in the first fifty pages. In December, I have the patience for that. In July, with cicadas going and a sweating glass of iced tea beside me, I want a book I already love.

So my summer stack is more or less the same every year: a Barbara Pym, an Anthony Powell volume (I'm forever halfway through "A Dance to the Music of Time"), a P. G. Wodehouse for when even Pym requires too much social attention, and one of the Patrick O'Brian sea novels for the long afternoon. These are not guilty pleasures. They are companions, and revisiting them in summer is more like a family reunion than an act of consumption.

I suspect this is more common than the new-releases-driven publishing calendar would have us admit. Readers in midlife develop a small canon of beloved books and return to them seasonally. The bookstore tables groaning with new hardcovers in June address a different reader — perhaps a younger, hungrier one, or perhaps a fictional one invented by marketing departments.

If I have a recommendation for the season, then, it is to look at your own shelves before you look at any list, including this one. The best summer book may already be in the house.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01New books require an intellectual effort and patience that is ill-suited for the relaxed, leisurely atmosphere of the summer season.
  2. 02Revisiting familiar authors and stories provides a sense of companionship and comfort akin to a family reunion.
  3. 03Experienced readers often develop a personal canon of books that they find more rewarding to return to seasonally than the latest marketing-driven releases.
  4. 04The most satisfying summer read is likely a book you already own and love rather than something found on a new recommendation list.
