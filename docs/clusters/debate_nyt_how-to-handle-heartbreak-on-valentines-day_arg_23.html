"To effectively handle heartbreak in the digital age, one must proactively remove all digital traces and reminders of the former partner from their devices and social media."

Delete the apps. Delete the texts. Delete, if you can bear it, the photographs.

I realize this is heretical advice in 2015, when our entire emotional lives are archived on glowing rectangles, but the single largest obstacle to recovery from heartbreak is the technology that keeps the wound open. Our grandparents, when their hearts broke, had to actively seek out reminders of the lost beloved. We have to actively avoid them. The default state of a smartphone is to surface, repeatedly and without warning, the face of the person we are trying to forget — in Timehop reminders, in Facebook memories, in the algorithmic suggestions of who to follow.

Heartbreak in the age of social media is like trying to recover from an addiction while living next door to your dealer. Every time you check Instagram you risk seeing them at brunch with someone new. Every shared playlist on Spotify is a landmine. The ambient surveillance we now perform on each other's lives, and that we permit on our own, is corrosive to the natural process of forgetting that healing requires.

So: unfollow. Block, even, if you must. Archive the photo album somewhere you cannot easily reach it. Tell mutual friends you do not want updates. This is not pettiness or denial; it is digital hygiene. The mind cannot heal a wound it keeps picking at, and our phones have become, for most of us, a mechanism for picking at every wound we have ever had.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Modern technology creates a constant, involuntary stream of reminders that prevents the natural process of forgetting necessary for healing.
  2. 02Maintaining digital access to an ex-partner's life functions like an addiction, making it impossible to recover while remaining in close proximity to the source of pain.
  3. 03The ambient surveillance enabled by social media is corrosive to emotional recovery and requires strict 'digital hygiene' to stop the cycle of reopening old wounds.
  4. 04Actively deleting apps, texts, and photos is a necessary act of self-preservation rather than a sign of pettiness or denial.
