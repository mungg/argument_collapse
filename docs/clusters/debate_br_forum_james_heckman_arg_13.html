"While early childhood enrichment programs are beneficial, they are insufficient substitutes for the stability of a married two-parent household, which public policy must prioritize to truly address the roots of social disadvantage."

James Heckman's essay is a powerful testament to the transformative potential of early childhood intervention. His marshaling of evidence from the Perry Preschool and Abecedarian projects, and his emphasis on the critical role of non-cognitive skills, offer a compelling vision for breaking the cycle of disadvantage. Yet in placing his policy bets almost entirely on targeted enrichment programs, Heckman sidesteps a deeper, more uncomfortable truth: the very family environments he seeks to bolster are crumbling, and no amount of government programming can fully replicate the nurturing that a stable, married two-parent household provides. His own data point toward this conclusion, but he pulls back from its logical implications.

Heckman is right that the quality of parenting is the scarce resource, not money. But what fundamentally drives parenting quality? It is not mere lack of information about how to read to a child. The Hart and Risley study he cites showed remarkable word-count gaps between professional and welfare families, but those gaps are symptoms of underlying family chaos, maternal depression, and absent fathers. Heckman acknowledges that the presence of a father can be a net negative if he is antisocial, but that caveat obscures the overwhelming body of social science showing that, on average, children raised by their continuously married biological parents fare dramatically better than those in cohabiting, single-parent, or stepfamily arrangements. A father’s steady involvement—when he is not abusive—brings discipline, economic resources, and a second pair of loving eyes to a child's development.

The decline in marriage, especially among the less educated, is not a peripheral concern. As Heckman notes, well-educated women are marrying later, having children within wedlock, and devoting more time to child enrichment. Less educated women are increasingly having children outside marriage, with the percentage of children under five with a never-married mother reaching 35 percent among dropouts. This is a cultural crisis, not just an economic one. Heckman’s policy solutions—sliding fee schedules for universal programs, home visits, center-based care—are technocratic fixes that, while useful, do little to address the erosion of the norm of married parenthood. They may even inadvertently subsidize family fragmentation by making single parenting less financially daunting.

A predistribution agenda centered only on early enrichment misses the relational roots of disadvantage. The most effective early environment is one in which a child feels the security of a committed parental bond. Public policy should therefore lean much harder into pro-marriage incentives: eliminating marriage penalties in the tax code and safety-net programs, strengthening community-based marriage education, and using public messaging to restore the link between marriage and childbearing. This is not about stigmatizing single mothers; it is about being honest that the data Heckman himself presents point to diverging destinies that track marital status almost as tightly as they track maternal education.

Moreover, the scalability of small, intensive programs like Perry remains deeply uncertain. Perry involved 58 children with an exceptionally low child-to-teacher ratio and a curriculum that required highly trained staff. Attempts to roll out Head Start nationwide have yielded much smaller and often statistically insignificant long-term gains. The interaction effects that made Perry so successful—committed teachers, strong family engagement, a moral framework emphasizing character—are precisely the things that large-scale government bureaucracies tend to flatten out. In contrast, strengthening the natural family unit is a self-sustaining, scalable solution. A married mother and father motivated by love and duty will provide, on average, the optimal levels of cognitive stimulation, emotional support, and moral formation. No intrusive home visitor can compete with that.

Heckman is correct that we need to shift from redistribution to predistribution. But the most powerful predistribution of all is to ensure that children are born into families where two parents have made a binding commitment to care for one another and to pour their lives into their offspring. Early child programs can help at the margins, but they should be seen as a supplement to, not a substitute for, the intact family. In the end, love and parenting are the scarce resources, and public policy should do everything possible to nurture the soil in which that love naturally grows. That means making marriage once again the default setting for American child-rearing, and being unafraid to say so.
