Civil Liberties after 9/11 · Main argument 2

"Some compromise of civil liberties is justified during emergencies."

open-ended · no stance
Lead essay · David Cole · "Their Liberties, Our Security"
Cole argues that post-9/11 security measures targeting noncitizens — selective enforcement of immigration law, secret hearings, preventive detention — have set legal precedents that gradually erode the rights of citizens as well. He traces this dynamic through historical episodes (the Palmer raids, Japanese internment) and the early War on Terror.

When emergency measures are paired with sunset clauses and active judicial review, the structural risks of overreach can be meaningfully contained.

In support of this view, several considerations converge. First, the historical record of sunset enforcement is mixed but not uniformly negative. Second, judicial review under exigency standards has substantially constrained the most expansive proposals. Third, the broader institutional pattern reinforces the same direction.

The implication is that the pragmatic position is to insist on these mechanisms rather than to reject all accommodation categorically.

Practically, this means that the trajectory should be evaluated on substantive outcomes — accountability, public benefit, and durable institutional capacity — rather than on the headline-friendly framing of disruption versus continuity.
