"The vision of participatory democracy presented in the Port Huron Statement is fundamentally flawed because it relies on the centralizing power of the state rather than true individual and local autonomy."

Tom Hayden has given us a touching portrait of a political faith that refuses to die. There is something deeply appealing about his vision of participatory democracy as a big tent, a wide sea where Marxists and libertarians, abolitionists and Internet entrepreneurs can all sail together. It is a vision of radical ecumenism that promises to transcend the factional squabbles of the Left. And yet, when I read his essay, I cannot shake the feeling that for all his talk of “inside-outside” strategy and popular surges, Hayden misunderstands the deepest wellspring of democratic participation: the rejection of remote, unaccountable power, especially when that power wears the mask of benevolence. From a libertarian perspective, the Port Huron Statement’s call for participatory democracy is a call we can half-heartedly echo, but only if we first recognize that the greatest threat to participation in our time comes not from corporate boardrooms alone but from the centralizing state that the New Left has consistently sought to empower.

Let me begin where I agree. Hayden is right to celebrate direct action, independent media, and the grassroots energy of people who refuse to be passive subjects. The libertarian tradition has its own deep history of decentralized resistance, from the anti-draft protests of individual conscientious objectors to the tax revolts and community self-defense networks that have long operated beneath the radar of mainstream politics. When Port Huron denounces the “knowledge factory” and the “arrogant indifference of the mainstream news corporations,” it touches a chord that resonates with any advocate of local autonomy and intellectual freedom. Participatory democracy, properly understood, means bringing decision-making as close to the individual and the community as possible—a principle that militates against authoritarianism of both the corporate and the governmental variety.

But here the agreement ends. Hayden’s canvas of contemporary struggles gives the game away. He praises the “historic election of 2008” and the upswell for Barack Obama—a man who has expanded executive power, waged undeclared wars, overseen the indefinite detention of citizens, and ramped up deportations to record levels. He lauds “courageous politicians” like Russ Feingold and Dennis Kucinich, who are notable not for shrinking government but for wanting to redirect its awesome power toward progressive ends. The movements he celebrates, from the Dream Act to the Wisconsin protests, are overwhelmingly demands for government protection and government-mandated redistribution. The patient is supposed to cure the disease? This is not participatory democracy; it is a competition for control of the commanding heights of the state, a contest in which the most organized interests win and the individual is perpetually subordinated to the collective will.

Hayden’s blindness to the statist dangers latent in his own rhetoric is particularly glaring when he discusses the Occupy movement. He quotes Occupy’s manifesto demanding “direct and transparent participatory democracy,” but he skates over the movement’s deep internal divisions over demands. Many Occupiers shunned specific policy proposals precisely because they recognized that any demand directed at the state would only reinforce the state’s legitimacy as the arbiter of social life. The more radical strands of the movement are explicitly anarchist, rejecting the entire architecture of representative government and the fiction of consent. Yet Hayden assimilates them into a framework that still relies on “candidates and legislation” and the notion that politicians can “open the doors of institutions to the winds of change.” This is a fundamental contradiction, and it reveals that Hayden’s participatory democracy is, at bottom, a rewrite of the old progressive faith in the perfectibility of the administrative state.

What would a genuinely libertarian participatory democracy look like? It would begin by dismantling the regulatory behemoths that suffocate local initiative—the zoning boards, the occupational licensing cartels, the federal education mandates. It would celebrate the mutual aid societies and community land trusts that allow people to manage their own affairs without appealing to distant bureaucrats. It would see the Internet not primarily as a tool for organizing mass protests but as a realm of voluntary association, where individuals can create their own communities of meaning without state permission. Most importantly, it would guard jealously the right of exit, the ultimate check on tyranny. For all its talk of consensus, the Port Huron tradition rarely asks what happens to those who do not consent to the group’s decisions. A true democracy of participation must protect the dissenter not only in his right to speak but in his right to walk away and build something different.

Hayden writes that most people prefer “to steer their boats in a wider sea with many currents.” I agree. But that sea must be one in which each vessel can chart its own course, not one patrolled by a single fleet demanding allegiance. The tragedy of the Port Huron Statement is that it was drafted at the precise moment when the American Left was abandoning the older radicalism of decentralization—of Jeffersonian wards, of mutualist cooperatives, of the IWW’s one big union that prefigured self-governance—and embracing instead the seduction of state power wielded for good causes. That seduction has now produced a warfare-welfare state that is bankrupt, intrusive, and increasingly detached from the consent of the governed.

The spirit of the ’60s that keeps appearing before Tom Hayden is, I suspect, a spirit that he and his colleagues conjured into a bottle that they could not control. The genuine longing for participatory democracy was real, but it was hijacked by the same will to power that has corrupted every progressive movement in modern history. If we want to honor that longing fifty years later, we must have the courage to say that the path forward lies not through more government but through less; not through the election of sympathetic politicians but through the cultivation of self-governing communities that can resist all leviathans, public and private alike. Until Hayden’s followers reckon with that, they will continue to mistake a new master for a liberator.
