"A post-neoliberal economics must prioritize the restoration of worker power and collective organization rather than just correcting market failures or relying on expert-led policy design."

The lead essay rightly announces the end of an era, but from the standpoint of workers it is still too polite. The problem with neoliberalism was not only that it misunderstood market failures. It was that it made working people weaker. Any economics after neoliberalism must begin there.

For decades, American workers were told a story. If productivity rose, wages would follow. If trade expanded, everyone would gain in the long run. If unions declined, labor markets would become more flexible and efficient. If corporations were freed from regulation and taxes, investment would increase. If finance innovated, capital would flow to its best uses. If workers lost jobs, they could retrain. If communities declined, people could move.

That story has collapsed. Productivity and pay have diverged. Trade shocks have left lasting scars. Retraining has been a meager answer to plant closures. Mobility has fallen, not risen. The decline of unions has not produced a paradise of opportunity but a workplace where many employees face unpredictable schedules, weak benefits, mandatory arbitration, noncompete clauses, fissured employment, and little voice. Corporate profits have been strong, but investment in workers has often been weak. Share buybacks and executive compensation have flourished. Working people have been asked to bear risk while capital claims flexibility.

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman understand much of this, and their emphasis on power is crucial. The word “power” was long treated as unscientific in economics, as if wages were set by impersonal forces rather than by bargaining under unequal conditions. But anyone who has tried to organize a union, ask for a raise, refuse an unsafe assignment, or challenge wage theft knows that labor markets are not neutral arenas. They are governed by fear, dependence, and institutional rules.

The decline of unions is the central fact. In the middle of the twentieth century, collective bargaining helped turn productivity growth into shared prosperity. Unions raised wages not only for their members but for nonunion workers whose employers had to match standards. They provided benefits, grievance procedures, apprenticeship systems, political education, and a counterweight to corporate power. They helped build the middle class, though unevenly and often with racial and gender exclusions that must not be romanticized. Their weakening was not an accident of technology. It was the result of employer resistance, hostile law, globalization, fissuring, and political choices.

Economists have too often treated unions as labor market distortions. The modern literature is more nuanced, recognizing monopsony, efficiency wages, bargaining, and inequality. But workers do not need to wait for economists to discover what they have lived. The employment contract is incomplete. The boss has discretion. A worker without collective power may formally be free but practically be dominated.

The lead essay’s discussion of wage boards is therefore welcome. Sectoral bargaining or wage boards could address a central weakness of the American labor system: firm-by-firm organizing is brutally difficult in an economy of franchises, subcontractors, gig work, and high turnover. If standards were set by industry and region, workers would not have to win a separate war at every workplace. Employers would compete on productivity and quality rather than on who can squeeze labor hardest. This is especially important in care work, food service, logistics, retail, hospitality, and other sectors where traditional union density is low but social need is high.

But wage boards cannot be a substitute for unions. Administrative wage-setting without worker organization risks becoming paternalism. Workers need institutions through which they can identify demands, monitor enforcement, protect one another, and exercise voice. A board that sets minimum standards is useful; a democratic labor movement that can contest managerial power is indispensable.

Labor law reform must therefore be more ambitious than small procedural fixes. Workers should have easier paths to recognition, real penalties for illegal retaliation, first-contract arbitration, rights for minority unions, and protections for immigrant workers who face deportation threats when they organize. Misclassification of employees as independent contractors should be aggressively policed. Noncompete agreements for ordinary workers should be banned. Mandatory arbitration clauses that prevent collective legal action should be curtailed. Public procurement should support high-road employers. The federal government should make labor standards a condition of public money.

The authors are also right to connect labor power to trade. For years, trade agreements protected investors, patents, and corporate rights with far more seriousness than they protected workers. Labor provisions were often weak, unenforced, or secondary. Workers were told that globalization was inevitable, while every line of the agreement reflected political choices. If capital can move and labor cannot, bargaining power shifts. If corporations can credibly threaten relocation, even workers who keep their jobs become more cautious. Trade policy must be judged not only by consumer prices but by its effects on bargaining power, regional stability, and democracy.

That said, we should be careful not to let trade become the whole explanation. Many low-wage workers are in non-traded sectors. The home health aide, janitor, warehouse worker, cashier, and child care worker are harmed less by imports than by weak labor standards, racism, sexism, immigration vulnerability, and the low value assigned to care and service work. A worker-centered economics must not focus only on the white male manufacturing worker, however politically salient. Inclusive prosperity must include Black women in hospitals, Latina hotel housekeepers, immigrant farmworkers, adjunct instructors, domestic workers, and gig drivers.

This is where economics needs to learn from labor organizers. The economy is not just a set of markets; it is a set of relationships. Workers want higher wages, but also dignity, predictable schedules, safety, respect, benefits, and a say. These are not easily reduced to income. A worker forced to choose between a child’s emergency and a shift has suffered an economic harm even if a wage equation misses it. A worker subject to harassment or arbitrary discipline is not flourishing simply because employment is technically efficient.

The question of technology should also be framed around worker power. Automation is not a natural disaster. Employers choose technologies in response to wages, labor law, tax incentives, managerial strategies, and worker resistance. A machine that deskills work and intensifies surveillance is not the same as a tool that augments skill and reduces drudgery. Workers should have a voice in technological adoption. If public policy subsidizes innovation, it should demand shared gains: training, shorter hours, worker ownership stakes, or sectoral funds financed by productivity improvements.

Finally, political democracy depends on workplace democracy. People who spend most of their waking hours under authoritarian control are less likely to experience themselves as equal citizens. The decline of unions has not only reduced wages; it has weakened the organizational basis for working-class politics. Moneyed interests fill the vacuum. Campaign finance reform and voting rights matter, but so does rebuilding mass membership organizations that can connect everyday economic life to democratic participation.

The lead essay gives economists a path away from neoliberalism. Workers should welcome that. But the test is whether economists are willing to side with labor not only when the evidence shows a free lunch, but when there is a real conflict over surplus and control. Inclusive prosperity cannot be granted from above by enlightened experts. It must be won by organized people.

Economics after neoliberalism should begin with a simple proposition: no economy is healthy when the people who do its work lack power over its rewards and rules.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The collapse of the neoliberal narrative proves that productivity gains do not automatically benefit workers without institutional mechanisms for shared prosperity.
  2. 02Labor markets are governed by power imbalances and fear rather than neutral exchange, making the decline of unions the central cause of modern economic inequality.
  3. 03While sectoral wage boards are a useful tool, they cannot substitute for the democratic voice and enforcement power provided by independent labor unions.
  4. 04Labor law reform must go beyond procedural fixes to aggressively protect workers from misclassification, mandatory arbitration, and retaliation.
  5. 05Trade policy should be evaluated based on its impact on domestic bargaining power and regional stability rather than just consumer prices or investor protections.
  6. 06Inclusive prosperity must specifically address the vulnerabilities of workers in non-traded service and care sectors, particularly women and people of color.
  7. 07Technological adoption is a choice influenced by policy and management, and workers should have a direct say in ensuring automation augments rather than devalues labor.
  8. 08Workplace democracy is a prerequisite for political democracy, as unions provide the organizational foundation for working-class participation in the broader political system.
