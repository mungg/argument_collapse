"Muslims must engage in an internal reform process to reclaim their faith from extremists by modernizing the interpretation of scripture and institutionalizing moderate teachings."

From an internal reform perspective, the Muslim community must engage in a long-overdue conversation about scripture, authority, and modernity. Extremist groups exploit a literalist reading of religious texts that has been contested for centuries. Progressive Muslim scholars argue that the Quran was revealed within specific historical contexts, and that its universal ethical message—justice, compassion, and stewardship—must be extracted through careful hermeneutics, not blind imitation of seventh-century practices.

This reform is not about abandoning Islam but about reclaiming it from those who would deform it. It requires institutional courage: seminaries and universities must train a new generation of scholars capable of navigating classical sources and modern realities. It also requires lay engagement: ordinary Muslims must feel empowered to question interpretations that seem obsolete or unjust.

Several Muslim-majority countries have begun this journey. Countries like Morocco and Jordan have launched state-sponsored programs to promote moderate Islam (Madrasa), and civil society organizations are fostering interfaith dialogue and community resilience. These efforts must be supported by international donors and praised by media, rather than being overshadowed by sensationalist coverage of extremist atrocities.

The internal reform of Islam is a slow, painstaking process. But it is the most sustainable solution to the crisis of extremist ideology.
