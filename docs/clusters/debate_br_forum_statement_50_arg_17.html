We Are Not Your Inheritance: A Letter from Zuccotti

Dear Mr. Hayden,

I read your essay in the Boston Review with the mixture of gratitude and impatience that I imagine you used to feel when you read tributes from the Old Left. You are kind to us. You see something of yourselves in us. We appreciate it. And we have to tell you, with respect, that we are not your inheritance, and that the frame of participatory democracy you offer us—generous as it is, and intended in friendship—does not quite fit our actual situation.

I was at Zuccotti from late September through the eviction in November. I have been to a dozen general assemblies in three cities. I have been arrested on the Brooklyn Bridge. I am twenty-four years old and have eighty thousand dollars in student debt, a degree the labor market does not want, no health insurance, and no realistic expectation that my life will be materially better than my parents' was. I tell you this not to claim moral authority but to locate the position from which I am writing. The "we" of Port Huron was a generation that grew up under the longest, most equally distributed economic expansion in American history. The "we" of Occupy is a generation that came of age during the worst financial crisis since the Depression, watched our parents lose their homes and their retirements, and inherited a planet whose climate may well be uninhabitable for our children.

This difference matters. The Port Huron Statement opens with an acknowledgment of "modest comfort"—the famous self-recognition of the SDS founders as children of relative privilege whose disquiet was moral rather than material. Whatever Occupy is, it is not that. Many of us are downwardly mobile in ways that the New Left rarely was. Some of us are unhoused. Many of us are saddled with debt so large and so non-dischargeable that any conventional middle-class life trajectory is foreclosed to us. The "alienation" of the comfortable student that animates the Statement is something we cannot really afford. Our complaint is not that the system has not delivered meaning. Our complaint is that the system is actively cannibalizing the conditions of life.

This shapes how we think about participatory democracy. You see, in our general assemblies and our hand signals and our consensus practice, a continuation of your tradition. We see, partly, the same thing—we are not idiots, we have read your books, many of us were in courses taught by your former students. But we also see something else: the general assembly as a form of mutual survival under conditions where the institutions that were supposed to organize collective life have abandoned us. We process by consensus in part because we do not trust hierarchy, and we do not trust hierarchy because every hierarchy we have ever encountered—the university administration, the labor market, the political parties, the police, the financial system—has lied to us and stolen from us. Consensus is not for us primarily a beautiful expressive practice. It is a defensive posture against organizations that we know, from direct experience, cannot be relied upon.

You write about the "inside-outside strategy" and you thank politicians like Kucinich and Feingold. With respect, this is the part of your essay that lands strangest in our ears. Russ Feingold is out of office. Dennis Kucinich is about to be redistricted into oblivion. The institutional Democratic Party that was supposed to be the "inside" of the inside-outside strategy bailed out the banks in 2008, has been mostly absent on housing, has watched twenty-eight states pass voter ID laws without making it a national emergency, and has spent the last three years apologizing to Republicans for having won an election. Many of us went door-to-door for Obama in 2008, the moment you cite as a "moving upswell" of participation. We learned a hard lesson from what happened next. The "inside" did not let us in. The Recovery Act was too small, the public option was abandoned without a fight, Geithner ran economic policy, Holder declined to prosecute, and the foreclosures rolled on. We did not stop participating because we got bored. We stopped participating because the participation did not appear to register.

So Occupy is, in part, what people do when conventional participation has been demonstrated, empirically, not to work. It is also a refusal of the demand to articulate a program in the language of the existing institutions, because we have noticed that articulating a program in that language is the first step toward having one's politics laundered into a fundraising email. The famous "lack of demands" was not an oversight. It was a strategy.

I want to be careful here. I am not dismissing your generation's achievements. The ADA, voting rights, the end of legal segregation, the end of the draft, the slow expansion of LGBT recognition, the partial victories on women's rights—these are real and we are grateful. We also note that these victories were largely achieved by 1975, that the era of major progressive legislative gains has been over for nearly four decades, and that the country we are inheriting is in many measurable ways less equal, less free, and less ecologically viable than the country the New Left set out to reform. This is not an indictment of you. It is an observation about the limits of what your generation's repertoire of action accomplished.

The honest conversation between Port Huron and Occupy would not be about how we are the inheritors of your spirit. It would be about how the strategies that worked for you have stopped working, why, and what we should try next. It would also have to grapple with the fact that some of what your generation produced—the consultant class, the foundation-funded nonprofit sector, the credentialed activist career path—has actively obstructed the radicalism we need. You probably know this. Many of us learned it from sources you helped create.

So accept this, please, as a letter from a younger friend rather than a child. We have read your document. We are not its heirs, exactly. We are people facing a different problem, on a different planet, with different tools and far less time. Participatory democracy is one of the things we have inherited. We will use it where it is useful. We will discard it where it is not. And we will write our own document when we are ready, and we will not be writing it for our fiftieth anniversary, because we will probably not have one in any form you would recognize.

In solidarity, with respect,

A.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Unlike the New Left's origins in 'modest comfort,' the current generation faces downward mobility, insurmountable debt, and an uninhabitable climate that makes their struggle one of survival rather than self-actualization.
  2. 02Participatory democracy and consensus-building are used by Occupy as defensive postures against untrustworthy hierarchies rather than as expressive tools for community creation.
  3. 03The 'inside-outside' strategy of working with institutional politics has been empirically proven ineffective for the current generation, as demonstrated by the perceived failures of the Obama administration and the Democratic Party.
  4. 04The refusal to issue formal demands is a deliberate strategy to avoid having the movement's radicalism co-opted or 'laundered' by existing political and nonprofit institutions.
  5. 05The strategies and institutional structures produced by the New Left, such as the professionalized activist class, have become obstacles to the radical change now required.
