"While the Port Huron Statement’s vision of participatory democracy remains vital, it must be evolved to address the internal power imbalances and exclusions that marginalized groups have historically faced within social movements."

Tom Hayden writes movingly about the Port Huron Statement as a great “coming out by everyone.” That phrase captures something real: the emergence of voices previously trained to silence themselves. But it also risks smoothing over a harder truth. Many people were not simply waiting to come out into a participatory democracy already prepared to receive them. They had to fight within movements that claimed democracy while reproducing the exclusions of the larger society.

The Port Huron Statement was a remarkable document, but it was also a document of its time: male-dominated, largely white, shaped by university life, and limited in its understanding of gender, sexuality, race, colonialism, and domestic labor. Hayden acknowledges some of this by noting that it preceded the modern women’s movement and gay liberation. Still, we should be careful about describing those later movements mainly as fulfillments of Port Huron. They were also critiques of it.

Women in the New Left did not simply expand participatory democracy. They exposed its contradictions. They found themselves typing, cooking, arranging meetings, and providing emotional labor while men gave speeches about liberation. They were told that questions of sexism were secondary or divisive. They watched supposedly anti-authoritarian men reproduce familiar forms of domination through charisma, sexual entitlement, and control of political language. The women’s liberation movement emerged not only from the democratic promise of the New Left, but from disappointment with its failures.

The same is true of gay liberation. Carl Wittman’s “Gay Manifesto” may have carried forward participatory democratic hopes, but gay people did not merely step into a space created by heterosexual radicals. They had to create their own spaces because they were often despised, patronized, or ignored in existing ones. Stonewall was not a seminar in democratic theory. It was a rebellion against police harassment and social humiliation. Its participants included drag queens, street youth, people of color, and working-class queer people whose lives did not fit neatly into the student left’s self-image.

This matters for the present. When Hayden sees the spirit of the 1960s in undocumented students, tuition protesters, climate activists, and Occupy, he is right. But we should ask: whose spirit, and under what conditions? Occupy has opened vital debate about inequality, but many women and people of color have criticized encampments for reproducing racial and gender hierarchies. General assemblies can be alienating for those not trained in activist speech. A supposedly open microphone can favor the loudest, most confident, and most socially secure. Informal camps can become unsafe for women. Police repression affects participants unevenly, especially people of color and immigrants who face consequences beyond arrest.

Participatory democracy must therefore mean more than open process. It must include attention to power before, during, and after participation. Who has time to attend a four-hour meeting? Who can risk arrest? Who has childcare? Who speaks English? Who knows the procedural norms? Who feels safe disagreeing? Who is treated as angry, irrational, or “identity politics” when naming injustice? Who gets quoted by reporters? Who is remembered fifty years later?

The women’s movement contributed one of the most important democratic insights of the twentieth century: the personal is political. This phrase is often misunderstood as a demand to politicize every intimate choice. At its best, it means that structures of power operate through everyday life: marriage, sexuality, housework, reproduction, family violence, wages, language, and bodily autonomy. A participatory democracy that leaves those relations untouched is incomplete. It allows citizens to speak in public while remaining subordinate at home, at work, in clinics, or in the streets.

That is why the current attacks on reproductive rights should be part of any conversation about Port Huron at fifty. Across the country, state legislatures are passing or considering restrictions on abortion, contraception, and women’s health services. The national argument over insurance coverage for contraception has revealed how quickly women’s autonomy can be reframed as a matter of institutional conscience, budgetary concern, or religious offense. If democracy is about participating in the creation of our lives, control over reproduction is not peripheral. It is foundational.

Similarly, immigrant youth fighting for the Dream Act are not only demanding formal inclusion in a national community. They are challenging the politics of fear that makes some people permanently deportable, exploitable, and silent. Their courage is extraordinary because the risks are not evenly distributed. A citizen activist arrested at a sit-in may face a misdemeanor. An undocumented activist may face removal from the country. Participatory democracy becomes meaningful when it recognizes those asymmetries and does not romanticize sacrifice demanded most heavily from the vulnerable.

Race also cannot be folded too quickly into a universal language of participation. The civil rights movement was not simply about letting black citizens participate in existing institutions. It challenged a racial order maintained by law, violence, custom, and economic exploitation. Today, the killing of Trayvon Martin and the national response to it remind us that citizenship is still lived through racial suspicion and unequal vulnerability. Who has the right to walk, gather, protest, occupy, or simply exist without being treated as a threat? Participatory democracy must address the policing of bodies as well as the structure of meetings.

Hayden’s broad coalition—liberals, radicals, libertarians, populists, Marxists, feminists, anarchists, entrepreneurs, poets, unions, NGOs, even conservatives—sounds inviting. But coalitions are not made by listing identities or ideologies. They require conflict. Feminists have learned that unity can be used to silence. Women are told to wait until after the war, after the election, after the class struggle, after the revolution. People of color are told not to divide the movement. Queer people are told not to alienate moderates. Immigrants are told to be respectable. The demand for participation often begins with the refusal to wait.

This does not mean rejecting Hayden’s vision. On the contrary, the best feminist, queer, anti-racist, and immigrant organizing has deepened participatory democracy. Consciousness-raising groups, rape crisis centers, health collectives, ACT UP meetings, community defense networks, worker centers, and domestic violence shelters have all created democratic practices that transform private suffering into collective power. They teach people to trust their experience, analyze institutions, and act together. They are among the richest inheritances of the Port Huron era, even when they arose in criticism of its blind spots.

The challenge in 2012 is to build movements that do not repeat the old pattern: universal language first, marginalized corrections later. Participatory democracy must be intersectional in practice, whether or not every participant uses that word. It must design meetings, campaigns, leadership structures, and agendas around the realities of unequal power. It must value care work as political work. It must understand safety not as comfort for the privileged but as a condition of participation for those most exposed. It must make room for anger without turning anger into a credential. It must develop leaders without creating stars. It must remember its own exclusions.

Hayden is right that the Port Huron Statement should not be treated as catechism. The best way to honor it is not to preserve it unchanged, but to let those once absent from its pages rewrite the meaning of democracy. Participatory democracy is still a beautiful phrase. But it becomes real only when the people invited to participate are also empowered to transform the invitation itself.
