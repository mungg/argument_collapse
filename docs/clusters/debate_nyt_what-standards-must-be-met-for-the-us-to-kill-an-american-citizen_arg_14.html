"Congress must establish a statutory framework and judicial review process for the lethal targeting of American citizens to ensure constitutional due process and democratic accountability."

The debate should not be framed as a choice between trusting the president completely and demanding a public criminal trial for an Al Qaeda commander in Yemen. There is a middle path, and Congress should create it.

The present system is too executive-centered. A confidential Justice Department memo, an internal review and a decision by “an informed, high-level” official are not enough when the consequence is the intentional killing of an American citizen. At the same time, ordinary criminal procedure is not designed for wartime intelligence. The evidence may come from sources who would be killed if exposed. The opportunity to strike may not wait for an adversarial hearing.

Congress should establish a statutory process for lethal targeting of citizens outside traditional battlefields. The statute should define the enemy organizations covered by the 2001 AUMF, require a finding that the citizen is a senior operational leader or directly participating in hostilities, demand evidence of a continuing and imminent threat, and require a determination that capture is infeasible. The president, not an unnamed subordinate, should personally approve the strike.

There should also be judicial review. A special national-security court could review the government’s evidence in classified proceedings before a strike when time permits. In emergency cases, review could occur immediately afterward. To avoid rubber-stamping, the court should hear from a cleared advocate empowered to challenge the government’s claims. Congress’s intelligence and judiciary committees should receive the full legal memos and after-action reports.

This would not be perfect due process. But due process has always depended on context. The process due to a citizen arrested in Chicago is not the same as the process due to a citizen commanding enemy operations from a remote province where the local government cannot arrest him. Still, the Constitution requires some neutral check when the government deliberately targets a citizen.

The larger failure is Congress’s. The Authorization for Use of Military Force was enacted in 2001 against those responsible for September 11. More than a decade later, it is being used against affiliates and successors in places far removed from Afghanistan. If Congress believes that war must continue in this form, it must say so clearly and set rules. Secret law is not law in the republican sense. A democracy cannot outsource life-and-death constitutional judgments to undisclosed executive branch deliberations.
