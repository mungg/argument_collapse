Diversified Claude

"Effective public diplomacy is not merely a matter of better communication or branding, but is fundamentally dependent on reforming the underlying foreign policies that generate global resentment and hypocrisy."

Stephen Walt devotes several sections of his essay to the question of legitimacy and public diplomacy, and as someone who has worked in this field, I want to take up that thread and pull it harder than he does. Walt treats public diplomacy as one element of a broader grand-strategic redesign. I would argue it deserves to be elevated higher — and that, more importantly, the way Walt frames it still understates the depth of the problem.

Walt is right that American public diplomacy is in disrepair. The Council on Foreign Relations task force he cites — the Djerejian report, the Peterson report, and others — have all reached the same conclusion: the United States Information Agency was dissolved in 1999, its functions absorbed into a State Department culture that has never understood communication as a core mission. Funding for public diplomacy is a rounding error in the federal budget. The number of fluent Arabic speakers in our diplomatic corps is embarrassingly low — fewer than a hundred at the working level, by some estimates. We have launched al-Hurra television and Radio Sawa, but their reach and credibility in the region are limited; many Arab viewers see them as obvious propaganda outlets. We have not, since the dissolution of USIA, had a single senior official whose full-time job is to think about how America is perceived abroad and to coordinate the instruments of perception.

But Walt's diagnosis stops short of the harder truth, which he gestures at and then sidesteps: public diplomacy cannot work when the underlying policy generates the resentment. He acknowledges this — "any public diplomacy effort, to be effective, needs a good product to sell" — but he treats it as a caveat rather than the central point. It is the central point. No amount of broadcasting in Arabic will persuade a young man in Cairo or Karachi that the United States is on his side if he turns on al-Jazeera every night and sees images of Marines in Fallujah, of detainees at Abu Ghraib, of bulldozers leveling Palestinian homes. The medium matters; the message matters more.

This is what was so misguided about the Charlotte Beers experiment at State in 2002-03. Beers was a brilliant advertising executive — she had branded Uncle Ben's rice — and the assumption was that the same skills could be applied to "branding America" in the Muslim world. The "Shared Values Initiative" produced glossy video segments of happy Muslim Americans living lives of religious freedom. They were sincere and well-produced. They were also irrelevant. The Arab and Muslim audiences who saw them were not skeptical of whether Muslims could practice their faith in Toledo, Ohio. They were skeptical of American actions in Baghdad and Hebron and Bagram. Advertising cannot fix that gap.

This is why I am sympathetic to Walt's argument that public diplomacy must be paired with policy reform, but I would go further: public diplomacy is downstream of policy in a way that requires us to rethink which decisions we are willing to put on the table. Are we willing to genuinely re-balance our approach to the Israeli-Palestinian conflict? Are we willing to accept higher gasoline prices in exchange for reducing the strategic centrality of Persian Gulf oil? Are we willing to support genuine democratic openings in Egypt and Saudi Arabia even when the likely electoral winners are not pro-American Islamists? These are the policy choices that determine whether any public diplomacy effort has a fighting chance. If we are not willing to revisit them, no broadcaster, no advertising executive, no expansion of Fulbright scholarships will close the credibility gap.

Walt also undersells one specific asset he mentions in passing: American higher education. The international student population in the United States is one of the most powerful instruments of soft power we possess. Foreign students who study here typically return home with a more nuanced view of America — its diversity, its messiness, its capacity for self-criticism — than they could ever acquire from the outside. They become an immune system against the cruder forms of anti-Americanism. They also become, often, the future leaders of their societies. The number of foreign ministers, finance ministers, and central bank governors with American degrees is striking.

Yet since 9/11, we have made it dramatically harder for foreign students to come here. Visa processing times have lengthened; the categories of suspicion have multiplied; brilliant graduate students from Iran, China, Pakistan, and elsewhere have either been turned away or chosen Canada, Britain, or Australia instead. The drop in foreign graduate enrollments in scientific fields is now in the double digits over the past three years, by some estimates. We are, in the name of security, dismantling one of the most effective instruments of American influence ever devised. Walt mentions this briefly. It deserves much more.

A serious public diplomacy reform agenda would include: rebuilding USIA or its functional equivalent as a freestanding agency with cabinet-level access; tripling the State Department's investment in foreign-language training, especially Arabic, Farsi, Urdu, Pashto, and Mandarin; restoring visa policies that welcome foreign students and visiting scholars while screening for actual security risks rather than treating all applicants from certain countries as suspect; investing far more in international broadcasting that does not look or feel like government propaganda; and, perhaps most importantly, training every senior American diplomat to engage local media in the local language as a routine professional expectation rather than an exotic skill.

But none of this will be enough if we cannot also commit to the harder thing: making our policy reflect the values we ask others to admire. Walt is right that hypocrisy is the cardinal sin of American foreign policy in Muslim eyes. We cannot champion freedom while underwriting the dictators of Tashkent and Riyadh. We cannot lecture other states on the rule of law while running offshore prisons that operate outside it. We cannot demand that Iran abandon nuclear weapons while building new ones of our own. The credibility gap is not a communications problem. It is a coherence problem. The fix is not better messaging. The fix is better policy, communicated honestly.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current U.S. diplomatic infrastructure is severely underfunded and lacks the specialized communication expertise lost when the U.S. Information Agency was dissolved.
  2. 02Public diplomacy efforts, such as the 'Shared Values' campaign, fail because they attempt to use advertising techniques to mask the negative impact of controversial military and political actions.
  3. 03Meaningful engagement with the Muslim world requires the U.S. to prioritize difficult policy shifts, such as re-balancing the Israeli-Palestinian approach and supporting democratic openings even when the outcomes are not pro-American.
  4. 04Post-9/11 visa restrictions are self-defeating because they undermine American soft power by discouraging international students who serve as a natural defense against anti-Americanism.
  5. 05The 'credibility gap' in American foreign policy is a problem of incoherence that can only be solved by aligning national actions—such as treatment of dictators and nuclear policy—with the democratic values the U.S. professes.
