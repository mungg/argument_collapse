"While immigration creates competition for specific groups of low-skilled workers, it is not the primary cause of working-class economic insecurity and generally benefits the economy by increasing overall demand."

Immigrants do not “take” a fixed number of jobs, because there is no fixed stock of work to be divided between native-born and foreign-born workers. They are workers, but also consumers, renters, taxpayers and often entrepreneurs. Their presence increases demand for goods and services, and over time that demand supports additional employment. That is why the broad economic consensus has been that immigration raises total output and has small average effects on native wages.

But the word “average” matters. Immigration can create real pressure for particular groups, especially workers with little education who compete most directly with newly arrived laborers. The evidence is mixed, but the best studies suggest the wage effects on most native-born workers are modest, while the largest negative effects tend to fall on earlier immigrants and some native high school dropouts. Those losses should not be dismissed merely because the national gains are larger.

David Frum’s argument about post-recession job growth captures a genuine anxiety but risks drawing too much from a narrow snapshot. If immigrant employment rises faster after a downturn, that may reflect where immigrants live, their age profile, their higher labor-force participation, or the sectors that recovered first, such as hospitality, construction-related services and caregiving. It does not by itself prove that employers replaced citizens with immigrants. During a weak recovery, many Americans left the labor force, retired early, returned to school or were trapped in regions with fewer openings. Immigrant gains can coexist with native hardship without being the main cause of it.

The more serious problem is not immigration in itself but the bargaining power of low-wage workers. If employers can exploit undocumented workers, misclassify employees, evade wage laws or threaten deportation, then standards fall for everyone nearby. The answer is not to pretend immigrants are the root of stagnant wages, but to enforce labor law, regularize workers already embedded in the economy, invest in training, and use immigration rules that reflect actual labor-market needs.

Immigration brings costs and competition, but blaming it for working-class insecurity exaggerates its role and distracts from deeper failures in wages, education and worker protections.

Vanilla Claude

"While immigration is a net economic positive that creates jobs for most Americans, it can negatively impact the wages and employment opportunities of a specific minority of low-skilled native workers."

The claim that immigrants "take" jobs from American-born workers treats the labor market as a fixed pie, which most economists reject. Immigrants are not just workers; they are consumers, renters, entrepreneurs and taxpayers whose spending generates demand for additional labor. The Kauffman Foundation has documented that immigrants start businesses at roughly twice the rate of the native-born, and Census data show foreign-born workers are overrepresented at both the top (engineers, physicians) and bottom (agriculture, home health, hospitality) of the occupational ladder—precisely the segments where native-born labor supply is thinnest.

That said, David Frum is pointing at something real. The bulk of post-2008 employment gains have indeed gone disproportionately to foreign-born workers, and George Borjas's research has long suggested that less-skilled natives—particularly workers without high school diplomas and African-American men—face measurable wage pressure from immigration, on the order of several percentage points over the long run. David Card's studies of the Mariel boatlift and other natural experiments find smaller effects, but the disagreement is over magnitude, not direction at the very bottom of the skill distribution.

The honest answer, then, is "mostly no, but with an important exception." For the economy as a whole and for most native workers, immigration is a net positive: it raises GDP, complements native skills, slows the fiscal squeeze from an aging population, and creates rather than destroys jobs on net. For the shrinking minority of Americans competing directly with low-skilled newcomers, the effect can be genuinely harmful, and dismissing their concerns as nativism is both unfair and politically counterproductive.

The policy response is not to halt immigration but to tilt it toward higher skills, enforce labor standards that prevent undercutting, and invest seriously in adjustment assistance and education for displaced natives.

Vanilla DeepSeek

"While immigration can create short-term competition for the most vulnerable native-born workers, its overall threat to American economic stability is exaggerated because immigrants often complement the native workforce and the primary causes of job instability are structural economic shifts."

The image of immigrants “stealing” jobs from native-born Americans is an enduring political trope, but the reality, as most economists understood it by early 2015, is far more nuanced. David Frum’s observation that post-recession job growth has disproportionately benefited lower-paid immigrants is an important data point, yet it risks confusing a snapshot with the full picture. The labor market is not a fixed pie from which one group’s gain is necessarily another’s loss. While there is evidence of localized and sector-specific pressure, the broad consensus holds that the threat to American economic stability has been exaggerated, and that immigration’s overall effect on native employment is small and often positive over the long run.

The core of the economic argument rests on the fact that immigrants are not simply clones of American workers. They tend to cluster in occupations at both the high and low ends of the skill spectrum, often filling roles that complement rather than compete with the native-born. Research by Giovanni Peri and others has shown that an influx of less-educated immigrants can push native workers toward jobs requiring communication and coordination skills—supervisory or sales roles that are harder to automate and pay better. In this view, immigrants take the manual-intensive jobs, while native-born workers shift into positions that leverage their language and local knowledge, creating a more efficient division of labor.

However, this benign narrative does not apply uniformly. The work of George Borjas has consistently demonstrated that for the most vulnerable native workers—particularly high school dropouts and previous cohorts of immigrants—large inflows of low-skilled labor can depress wages and reduce employment opportunities in the short term. This is precisely the dynamic Frum highlights with his post-recession job growth figures. In a sluggish recovery that was already marked by weak demand and stagnant wages for the working class, it is plausible that native-born workers without a diploma faced stiffer competition for a shrinking pool of entry-level positions, from hospitality to construction. These workers bear the adjustment costs that broader national statistics often mask.

Yet, it is a mistake to attribute these hardships solely to immigration. The 2008 recession accelerated structural changes—automation, the decline of unionization, the offshoring of manufacturing—that had been eroding middle-skill jobs for decades. Immigrants, often more mobile and willing to accept lower wages and erratic schedules, were simply better positioned to snap up the precarious, service-sector jobs that the recovery produced. The problem, then, is less that immigrants are taking jobs that American-born workers are lining up to do, and more that the economy is not generating enough decent, stable employment for anyone, native or foreign-born. Framing this as a zero-sum conflict distracts from the deeper policy challenge of ensuring that aggregate growth translates into broad-based prosperity.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Immigrants often fill roles that complement native-born workers, allowing natives to transition into higher-paying positions that utilize communication and coordination skills.
  2. 02The negative impact of immigration is largely concentrated among specific groups, such as high school dropouts, who face increased competition for low-skilled jobs during periods of weak economic demand.
  3. 03The economic hardships faced by working-class Americans are primarily driven by automation, offshoring, and the decline of unions rather than the influx of immigrant labor.
  4. 04The labor market is not a fixed-sum game, and focusing on job competition between groups ignores the broader failure of the economy to generate stable, high-quality employment for all workers.
Vanilla MiniMax

"The claim that immigrants systematically displace American workers is a simplification that ignores how immigration expands the overall economy and how structural factors like automation are the true drivers of job loss."

The claim that immigrants are systematically displacing American workers from jobs has long circulated in political rhetoric, yet the empirical evidence consistently complicates this narrative. While David Frum's recent analysis deserves serious attention, conflating short-term post-recession dynamics with long-term structural effects risks misdiagnosing what is actually a more nuanced phenomenon.

Economists across the ideological spectrum have largely agreed that immigration expands the overall economy by increasing labor supply, entrepreneurship, and consumer demand. The 2014 Congressional Budget Office report on comprehensive immigration reform projected that legalization of unauthorized immigrants alone would boost GDP by roughly $1 trillion over a decade. This occurs because immigrants do not merely compete for existing jobs—they create economic activity that generates additional employment.

The recession period Frum examines presents a particular analytical challenge. During cyclical downturns, any employment gains may appear concentrated among specific groups, but this reflects matching dynamics rather than displacement. Undocumented workers, often willing to accept less favorable terms, are frequently among the last hired and first fired. Their participation in recovery sectors—construction, food service, hospitality—bears little resemblance to the employment patterns of displaced manufacturing workers in the Midwest, whose losses stem primarily from automation and globalization.

That said, legitimate wage pressures exist in specific labor markets where less-skilled native workers and immigrants directly compete. The empirical evidence suggests these effects are modest and concentrated among recent arrivals to the country rather than established workers. Effective policy addresses such concerns through targeted workforce development rather than blanket restriction.

The framing of this question—as a binary between immigrants either taking jobs or not—obscures the real policy choices involved. Immigration, like trade, generates winners and losers. Acknowledging those losers does not require abandoning the substantial economic benefits that controlled immigration provides, nor does it justify the scapegoating that nativist framing encourages.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02Employment gains among immigrants during post-recession periods reflect specific industry matching dynamics rather than the direct displacement of native-born workers.
  3. 03Job losses among American workers in sectors like manufacturing are primarily the result of automation and globalization rather than immigration.
  4. 04While immigration can create modest wage pressures for specific low-skilled groups, these effects are concentrated among other recent immigrants rather than the broader native-born population.
  5. 05The economic challenges faced by some workers should be addressed through targeted workforce development rather than broad immigration restrictions.
Vanilla Gemini

"The claim that immigrants take jobs from native-born workers is a misconception that ignores the dynamic nature of the labor market and the broader economic benefits of immigration."

David Frum’s recent assertion that post-recession job growth has disproportionately benefited lower-paid immigrants taps into a persistent economic anxiety, but it fundamentally mischaracterizes how the labor market operates. The fear that immigrants "take" jobs from native-born Americans relies on the lump of labor fallacy—the mistaken belief that there is a fixed, finite number of jobs to go around. In reality, the economy is dynamic. Immigrants are not just workers; they are also consumers who buy groceries, rent apartments, and purchase services, thereby generating new demand that creates new jobs across the economic spectrum.

Furthermore, the argument assumes that immigrant and native-born workers are perfect substitutes competing for the exact same positions. Decades of economic research suggest otherwise. Immigrant labor overwhelmingly complements American labor rather than replacing it. At the lower end of the wage scale, immigrants frequently fill physically demanding roles in agriculture, hospitality, and construction that native-born workers generally avoid. Rather than displacing Americans, this influx of labor allows domestic businesses to survive and expand, which in turn generates higher-level managerial, sales, and administrative jobs typically filled by native-born citizens.

This is not to say that labor competition is entirely nonexistent. Some economic studies indicate a slight downward pressure on wages for native-born Americans without a high school diploma. However, this localized effect is heavily outweighed by the broader economic benefits of immigration, including increased innovation, business formation, and lower prices for consumer goods. The genuine threats to working-class economic stability today are deep and structural: increasing automation, the decline of collective bargaining, and the broader forces of globalization. Blaming immigrants misdiagnoses the root causes of wage stagnation and prescribes a restrictionist cure that would ultimately stall overall American economic growth.

  3. 03The minor wage pressures on specific demographics are outweighed by systemic benefits such as increased innovation, business formation, and lower consumer prices.

Diversified DeepSeek

"The threat of immigrants taking jobs from working-class Americans is not exaggerated, as evidence shows that high levels of low-skilled immigration have displaced native-born workers and suppressed their wages."

There is a profound gap between the sanguine view of elite economists and the reality on the factory floors and construction sites where many working-class Americans lost ground. David Frum is right to call out the contradiction: official pronouncements that immigration hurts no one are hard to square with the government’s own job counts, which show that immigrants have captured much of the slow employment growth since 2009 while millions of native-born workers remain sidelined.

The “lump of labor fallacy” is not always a fallacy in the short run, especially in specific sectors and regions. When large numbers of low-skilled immigrants enter a labor market already reeling from a recession, the supply of workers can outstrip demand, driving down wages and reducing job opportunities for the least educated native-born residents. Harvard economist George Borjas, analyzing decades of data, has found that a 10 percent increase in the size of a skill group through immigration reduces the wage of competing native workers by 3 to 4 percent. The effects are most severe for native-born high school dropouts, who saw their weekly earnings stagnate or fall in the years leading up to the financial crisis, even as the immigrant share of the low-skilled workforce climbed.

The recovery years offer a stark illustration. Between 2009 and 2014, the number of foreign-born workers grew by roughly 2.6 million, while the number of native-born workers in their prime working years actually fell. In construction, a traditional pathway to the middle class for Americans without a college degree, immigrant employment rebounded faster, often at lower cost to employers. Native-born teenagers and young adults, meanwhile, faced summer job markets where they now had to compete with immigrant laborers who were often more experienced and willing to work for less. This is not about xenophobia; it is about basic supply-and-demand arithmetic.

Advocates of open immigration policies often point to dynamic benefits that may unfold over decades. But working families at the bottom of the ladder cannot wait a generation for theory to catch up. Until we have policies that tighten labor markets—enforcing existing wage and labor standards, calibrating immigration levels to economic conditions, and investing in the skills of the native-born workforce—the threat to American economic stability Frum describes is far from exaggerated. It is the lived experience of many communities that have seen their bargaining power erode along with their jobs.

Yes, immigrants can and do take jobs from some American-born workers, and the point should not be dismissed as nativism simply because it makes many economists uncomfortable. The labor market is not a seminar room abstraction. If a hotel, meatpacking plant, restaurant kitchen or landscaping company can hire a large number of immigrant workers at low wages, some native-born workers who might have taken those jobs will not be hired, or will leave because the pay has fallen below what they can accept.

The most common answer from immigration optimists is that immigrants also create demand. That is true over the long run, and it is one reason the national effect of immigration may be positive. But averages conceal distribution. The benefits of immigration are spread widely: consumers get cheaper food, child care, construction and services; employers get a larger labor pool; affluent households enjoy more convenience. The costs are concentrated among workers with the least bargaining power, especially those without college degrees.

David Frum’s argument about job growth since the recession should not be treated as definitive proof that immigrants “got” all the new jobs. Demographics, labor-force participation and geography matter. But it should be treated as a warning sign. A recovery in which lower-paid immigrant employment rises while many working-class citizens remain detached from work is not healthy. Telling those citizens that economists have calculated a small net national gain is politically tone-deaf and morally inadequate.

The United States has long relied on immigration, and there is no realistic or desirable path to sealing the country off. But it is equally unrealistic to pretend that labor supply does not affect wages and hiring. If millions of additional workers are available for low-skill jobs, the bargaining position of existing low-skill workers weakens unless institutions intervene. In today’s economy, those institutions are weak: unions have declined, workplace enforcement is thin, and unauthorized workers are easily intimidated.

The right policy is not cruelty toward immigrants. It is a tighter, more deliberate immigration system that gives priority to skills the country genuinely needs, reduces illegal hiring, and avoids using mass low-wage immigration as an anti-poverty substitute. Legalization for those already rooted here should be paired with serious workplace enforcement and a more restrained future flow of low-skilled labor.

Compassion for immigrants and concern for native-born workers need not be enemies. But if progressives and business-friendly conservatives continue to deny that competition exists, they will leave the field to demagogues.

The question is often framed too crudely: “Do immigrants take jobs?” Sometimes they compete for jobs; sometimes they create them; often they change the way work is organized. The real issue is not whether immigrants are good or bad for “the economy,” but which economy we are talking about and under what rules.

Consider three workers. A native-born nurse may benefit when immigrant home health aides make it easier for hospitals and families to provide care. A native-born construction laborer may face direct competition from immigrant workers, especially if contractors pay cash off the books. A software company may hire foreign-born engineers because the skills are scarce, but it may also use visa rules in ways that tie workers to employers and weaken bargaining power. One slogan cannot describe all three cases.

The best research available before 2015 suggests that immigration’s overall effect on native-born wages is modest, and often positive for higher-skilled workers. But “overall” is doing too much work. Low-wage labor markets are local, informal and unequal. The people most likely to be harmed are not the median American worker but those already on the margins: workers without high school degrees, African-American and Latino men in weak urban labor markets, and previous immigrant cohorts. Their losses may be small in national statistics and large in lived experience.

At the same time, restricting immigration will not rebuild the old working-class economy. Factories did not leave Ohio because of Mexican farmworkers. Wages did not stagnate for decades because of Filipino nurses or Indian engineers. The forces reshaping work include trade, automation, financial pressure on firms, declining unions and public policy choices that have favored capital over labor. An immigration debate that ignores these forces becomes a convenient distraction.

A better approach starts with labor standards. Legal status should not be a tool employers can use to frighten workers into silence. Unauthorized workers should be brought into the open, not because this magically helps everyone, but because a shadow labor market drags down standards. Employers who violate wage laws should face real penalties. Guest-worker programs should not bind employees so tightly to one employer that they cannot complain or quit. And immigration levels should be set with attention to labor-market conditions, not only family ties, humanitarian claims or employer demand.

So the honest answer is: immigrants can take particular jobs from particular workers, but immigration is not the central cause of working-class instability. The policy goal should be to prevent employers from using immigration to create a cheaper, more vulnerable workforce. That requires less scapegoating and more power for workers, immigrant and native-born alike.

"Immigration only threatens the economic stability of American-born workers when poorly designed legal frameworks and visa programs grant employers unfair leverage to depress wages and standards."

It's Not Immigrants. It's the Rules We've Built Around Them.

The question "do immigrants take jobs from Americans?" is poorly posed, because it treats immigration as a single phenomenon with a single effect. In reality, the labor-market impact of immigration depends almost entirely on the legal architecture surrounding it. A Salvadoran roofer working off the books, an Indian software engineer on an H-1B, a Filipino nurse on a green card, and a Canadian executive on an L-1 visa are not interchangeable inputs. They affect different labor markets in different ways, and our policy treats them inconsistently—often perversely.

Consider the undocumented workforce, roughly 8 million strong in the labor market. Their illegality is not a bug from the employer's perspective; it is a feature. Workers who cannot complain to OSHA, cannot unionize meaningfully, and cannot demand back wages without risking deportation are cheaper and more pliable than workers who can. This pushes down standards in construction, agriculture, meatpacking, and hospitality—industries where native workers once earned middle-class wages and now often do not. The harm here is real, but its source is the legal limbo, not the immigrants themselves. Legalization would do more to raise wages in these sectors than deportation ever could.

The H-1B program illustrates the same dynamic in a white-collar key. The visa ties the worker to a sponsoring employer, creating a captive labor pool that depresses wages and reduces mobility. Outsourcing firms have learned to game the lottery, importing workers specifically to replace American IT staff—as recent layoffs at Southern California Edison and elsewhere have shown. Again, the issue is not that talented foreigners come to America; it is that we've designed a program that hands employers leverage over them.

So when Frum points to post-recession job gains accruing to immigrants, the right response isn't "build a wall" or "open the borders." It's to ask which immigrants, in which legal status, in which industries—and what features of policy produced that pattern. Enforce labor standards against employers regardless of worker status. Legalize the long-settled undocumented. Replace employer-tethered visas with portable work authorization. Shift toward skill-based selection where the labor market genuinely needs it.

Immigration becomes a threat to working-class stability mainly when policy makes it one. Change the policy, and the supposed dilemma between welcoming immigrants and protecting native workers largely dissolves.

  3. 03Legalizing long-settled undocumented immigrants would raise industry standards and wages more effectively than deportation.
  4. 04Replacing employer-tethered visas with portable work authorization would eliminate the artificial downward pressure on white-collar salaries.
  5. 05Shifting toward skill-based selection and enforcing labor laws regardless of worker status would resolve the perceived conflict between immigrant and native-born labor.

The claim that immigrants “take jobs” from American-born workers is a durable political slogan, but it crumbles under careful examination. David Frum’s essay, while provocative, mistakes correlation for causation. Yes, a disproportionate share of net new jobs since the recession has gone to lower-paid immigrants, but that does not mean they crowded out native workers. Instead, immigrants and the native-born labor force occupy complementary niches in a modern economy, and immigration itself expands the economic pie.

The core economic fallacy at work is the “lump of labor”—the assumption that there is a fixed number of jobs to go around. In reality, immigrants are not only workers; they are also consumers and entrepreneurs. They spend their wages on housing, food, and services, creating demand that requires more workers. They start businesses at higher rates than the native-born: by 2014, immigrants were founders of over a quarter of new firms, from Main Street shops to tech startups. These ventures generate employment that would not otherwise exist, often in precisely the communities where working-class Americans live.
