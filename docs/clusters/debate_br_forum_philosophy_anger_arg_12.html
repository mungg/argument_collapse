"Anger from oppressed groups should be understood primarily as a vital epistemic and expressive tool for disclosing injustice rather than as a form of moral corruption or a desire for revenge."

Agnes Callard’s essay is admirable in its refusal to sentimentalize anger, but troubling in its treatment of the anger of the oppressed. She argues that anger’s grudges and revenge are not pathologies but rational extensions of moral responsiveness. From there she concludes that oppression morally damages its victims: if they are angry, they are corrupted by vengeance; if they are not angry, they acquiesce in evil. “Innocence was not a possibility,” she writes of her childhood encounter with her Holocaust-survivor grandmother.

There is truth here, but the frame is too narrow. It treats anger chiefly as a relation between victim and wrongdoer, organized around the temptation to harm the wrongdoer back. Much political anger, especially anger from subordinated groups, is doing something else. It is not primarily revenge. It is disclosure.

Feminist philosophers and activists have long insisted that anger can reveal what polite society hides. A woman’s anger at harassment, domestic labor, condescension, or exclusion often appears “excessive” because the background conditions have been normalized. The anger says: this is not ordinary; this is an injury. Black anger in the United States has similarly exposed the moral obscenity of institutions that describe themselves in the language of liberty while tolerating segregation, police brutality, housing discrimination, and unequal schools. Anger in such contexts is not merely a wish to make the oppressor suffer. It is a refusal of the world’s description of itself.

Callard’s own account contains the resources for this point. She says anger is “a way of grasping the fact that it is broken.” Exactly. But grasping brokenness is not the same as seeking revenge. Sometimes the chief struggle is to get others to perceive the break at all. The anger of the marginalized is often directed less at a particular offender than at a regime of misrecognition: the boss who says it was a joke, the police report that says reasonable force, the textbook that says westward expansion, the family that says boys will be boys, the colleague who says you are too sensitive. Anger punctures euphemism.

This is why demands for civility so often function as demands for silence. The angry speaker is told that her tone prevents others from hearing her. But often her tone is the evidence. It communicates not only the proposition “this is wrong” but the lived fact that the wrong hurts, repeats, accumulates, and exhausts. A cool statement may be easier for the comfortable to absorb, but ease is not always the measure of moral communication. Sometimes discomfort is the message.

Callard worries that philosophers invent purified anger to avoid confronting rage’s bloodlust. But there is an opposite danger: describing anger so insistently in terms of vengeance that we obscure its epistemic and expressive roles. When an abused person finally becomes angry, the anger may not say “I want to hurt him.” It may say “I am not crazy.” When a worker becomes angry at being called essential but paid poverty wages, the anger may not say “I want the owner ruined.” It may say “My life counts.” When a community protests a police killing, anger may not say “Let us kill in return.” It may say “You will not narrate this death as normal.”

Of course anger can become punitive. Political movements are not immune to cruelty, scapegoating, or sadism. Social media makes this especially visible. The line between accountability and collective humiliation can be crossed in minutes. Callard is right that the line is not secured by vocabulary. Calling anger “righteous” does not make it so. But the answer is not to treat vengeance as anger’s essence. The answer is to ask what the anger is doing in a particular social field.

Power matters. A powerless person’s anger and a powerful person’s anger do not have the same meaning or consequences. When a dominant group is angry, it often has immediate access to police, employers, courts, newspapers, guns, and inherited credibility. Its anger can become policy quickly. When a subordinated group is angry, it is often first dismissed, pathologized, or punished. To describe both as equally tempted by revenge is formally balanced but politically misleading.

This does not mean the oppressed are morally pure. No serious politics should rely on that fiction. Members of oppressed groups can be cruel, prejudiced, self-deceived, or vindictive. Trauma can distort perception. But Callard’s phrase “made morally worse people” is too blunt. Oppression can produce moral injury, and moral injury may include anger. But anger can also be a recovery of moral agency. To be unable to feel anger at one’s degradation may be a symptom of domination. The person who says “I deserved it,” or “that is just how things are,” may be in greater moral danger than the person who says “How dare you?”

The distinction between moral damage and moral defect is essential. Suppose a girl is raised to accept that her brother’s education matters more than hers. If she comes to resent him, her resentment may damage their relationship and distort her view of him. But the first moral event is her dawning recognition that the arrangement is unjust. Her anger is not simply corruption; it is the form her self-respect takes under bad conditions. A better world might allow self-respect without anger. In this world, anger may be the bridge from submission to speech.

Callard’s Holocaust story illustrates this ambivalence, but I read it differently than she does. Her grandmother’s anger at the German child was unjust to that child. Yet it was also a form of historical knowledge. The grandmother knew that what looks like innocent play can be entangled with uniforms, obedience, nationalism, and extermination. The granddaughter knew that children are not their nations and that friendship can cross inherited fences. Both truths mattered. The tragedy was not simply that neither was innocent. It was that there was no shared public world adequate to hold both truths at once. The grandmother’s anger had to do too much because history had done too little.

That is often the case for political anger. When institutions deny injustice, anger becomes archive, alarm, and courtroom. It stores memory, summons attention, and issues judgment. This is burdensome and dangerous. No person or community should have to live permanently in that state. But the danger is not a reason to condemn the anger as corruption. It is a reason to build institutions that can take over some of anger’s work: truthful education, fair courts, reparations, representation, labor protections, health care, and public rituals of remembrance.

The COVID-19 crisis has already revealed how anger discloses hidden structures. Many people now called “essential”—nurses, grocery clerks, delivery drivers, cleaners, home health aides, farmworkers—were treated as replaceable until the emergency made dependence visible. If these workers are angry, should we understand their anger mainly as a wish for revenge? Surely not. Their anger reveals a contradiction: society depends on their labor while denying them security, protection, and respect. That anger may become destructive if ignored or manipulated. But its first function is truth-telling.

Callard is right that anger is not safe. But moral emotions need not be safe to be valuable. Love is not safe; loyalty is not safe; grief is not safe; hope is not safe. Each can mislead and deform. The task is not to purify them into harmlessness, but to cultivate practices that help them serve life rather than death. For political anger, those practices include organizing, democratic deliberation, coalition-building, accountability to those most affected, and a discipline of distinguishing systems from scapegoats.

The best anger of the oppressed is not interested in becoming the mirror image of the oppressor. It does not say, “Your bad is my good.” It says, “Your good has been purchased by my bad, and that arrangement ends now.” That is not revenge. It is justice speaking in a voice loud enough to be heard over centuries of shushing.

If Callard’s essay warns us not to romanticize anger, we should accept the warning. But we should also resist a tragic metaphysics in which the wronged are trapped between acquiescence and moral corruption. Sometimes anger is the first sign that acquiescence has failed. Sometimes it is the beginning of freedom. The moral question is what happens next: whether anger hardens into hatred, dissipates into performance, or organizes itself into a struggle for a world in which fewer people need anger in order to know they matter.
