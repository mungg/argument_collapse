"While social endurance is a necessary component of solidarity, it risks becoming a new form of discipline that ignores the asymmetric costs of risk and harm borne by marginalized bodies in movement spaces."

At the heart of Mie Inouye’s “Solidarity Now” is a powerful tension, one she narrates through her small rebellion against the name tag at a fundraiser. The organizer, Henry, wants her to wear a name tag, to be visible and legible within the organizational space. She resists, not from mere stubbornness, but because the demand feels like a microcosm of a larger political pressure: to explain oneself, to submit to the terms of a group, to be organized. Her honest answer—that she organizes for the meetings—is a confession that she immediately feels is somehow bourgeois, a luxury of the tenured professor. Yet in that confession, Inouye opens the door to a radical rethinking of what brings people together. I want to take her argument seriously but also push it in a direction she only hints at: the ways in which social endurance can become a new form of discipline, one that risks ignoring the asymmetries of risk and harm that different bodies bring into the room.

Inouye is right to critique a one-dimensional deference politics. The logic that tells a white person, a cis person, or a PMC member to simply shut up and follow the lead of those more oppressed is both theoretically flawed and practically corrosive. It assumes that oppression confers automatic strategic wisdom, when we know that trauma can cloud as much as clarify. It also erases the messy interiority of every person, turning social location into a fixed identity that stands in for political judgment. And it often leads, as Inouye notes, to fragile coalitions that dissolve at the first real disagreement because the relatively privileged person’s stakes have been suppressed rather than transformed.

But from my perspective as a Black feminist organizer, Inouye’s proposed alternative—epistemic humility combined with social endurance—sounds a bit like asking the marginalized to do the emotional labor of educating the privileged, again, only this time in the name of “productively working through conflict.” Her reading of Bob Lee organizing white people in the Rainbow Coalition is instructive. Lee’s work was extraordinary, but it occurred in a context where the Black Panther Party had a clear political line and a disciplined cadre structure. He was not simply facilitating white people’s self-discovery; he was recruiting them to a program of revolutionary anti-capitalism and anti-imperialism. That program had a center, not just a vague openness. The conflict he invited was in service of aligning them with that center. Inouye’s suggestion that we need more conflict for its own sake—or even conflict interpreted as a site of personal transformation—can too easily become a justification for demanding that the oppressed endure yet another round of racist, sexist, or classist microaggressions in the name of building solidarity.

What is missing from her account is a frank discussion of safety and the asymmetric costs of endurance. The Mormon Church that taught Inouye to endure three hours of meetings with people she didn’t like was also an institution that enforced a rigid gender hierarchy and, until 1978, an explicitly anti-Black priesthood ban. The endurance she learned was, in part, endurance of patriarchal and white supremacist structures. While she valorizes the friction as transformative, she doesn’t fully reckon with the possibility that for some people—those most targeted by those structures—endurance is not a learning opportunity but simply exhausting, even retraumatizing. To say “keep showing up even when you don’t like the people in the room” is to assume a baseline level of safety that many do not experience. For a trans person in a room of people who deadname them, or a Black person in a mostly white organization that debates their humanity, the cost of staying is not just irritation but a daily assault on dignity.

Inouye might respond that this is why conflict is necessary: to challenge those exclusions. But conflict can be waged more effectively, and with less damage, when there are structures of accountability in place, and when those with less power are not expected to repeatedly relitigate their right to exist. Too often, calls for endurance are used to silence dissent from below: “Just stick with us, we’re all learning together.” That demand can be a way of displacing responsibility for anti-oppressive practice onto those who suffer most from its absence.

I want to be clear: I am not advocating a return to simplistic deference politics. I agree with Inouye and Olúfȧmi Táíwò that we need constructive politics that build new institutions. But those institutions, if they are to be truly liberatory, must be designed with a deep accounting of power asymmetries from the start. They need clear grievance processes, leadership accountability, and a commitment to not simply endure but actively dismantle the hierarchies that reproduce themselves inside movement spaces. Social endurance is not a sufficient condition for solidarity; it is the byproduct of trust, and trust is built when people see that their material and emotional safety is taken seriously, not when they are asked to sacrifice it for some future transformation.

Inouye’s Uncle Gerald story illustrates the limits of endurance from another angle. She wonders what might have changed had her family encountered a solidarity not tied to the American Dream. But Uncle Gerald didn’t need more meetings; he needed a political movement that could reach him on his own terms—a rural doctor, proud and resistant to what he saw as elite moralizing—and show him that his interests aligned with collective demands for universal healthcare, public investment in rural communities, and an end to the predatory capitalism that left him to treat COVID patients with hydroxychloroquine. That kind of organizing requires deep listening, yes, but also a willingness to challenge reactionary ideology head-on, not just to endure it in the hope that proximity will work its magic. Sometimes endurance without confrontation is just enabling.

So I would amend Inouye’s formula: social endurance is necessary but not sufficient. We need political endurance—the capacity to stick with a specific strategic project through difficulties—and that requires more than just showing up. It requires a shared analysis, a commitment to shared action, and a frank, ongoing negotiation of power. Without that, the meeting room becomes a stage for endless processing that rarely gets to the root. I want a left where we can sit with the discomfort of difference, but I also want a left where we don’t mistake discomfort for political work, and where we refuse to ask the most vulnerable to pay the highest price for the group’s education.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02Inouye’s valorization of endurance fails to account for the fact that for many, staying in a room with hostile actors is a retraumatizing assault on dignity rather than a transformative learning opportunity.
  3. 03Social endurance should be viewed as a byproduct of trust built through safety and accountability rather than a prerequisite for participation.
  4. 04Effective organizing requires a willingness to challenge reactionary ideologies head-on rather than simply enduring them in the hope that proximity will eventually lead to transformation.
  5. 05Solidarity requires political endurance centered on a shared strategic project and a frank negotiation of power, rather than just the capacity to show up to meetings.
