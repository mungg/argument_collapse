"While institutional reform is necessary to eliminate crony capitalism and artificial barriers to competition, the primary goal of such reform should be to ensure fairness, productivity, and liberty rather than to achieve a specific egalitarian distribution of income."

David Grusky’s essay is most compelling when it turns the usual inequality debate away from envy and toward privilege. If some people are becoming rich because government protects them from competition, subsidizes their risks, restricts entry into their professions, or allows insiders to bargain with themselves, then reformers of many ideological stripes should object. A free society should not tolerate crony capitalism masquerading as markets.

But Grusky’s argument also illustrates a danger in the current inequality debate: once “inequality” becomes the master problem, every institutional defect is judged by whether it narrows or widens the income distribution. That is the wrong test. The right questions are whether institutions are fair, open, productive, and consistent with liberty. Some reforms that reduce inequality are desirable. Others are not. Some inequalities are troubling. Others are the byproduct of innovation, effort, risk-taking, or differences in preference. A society that focuses too obsessively on the gap between top and bottom may damage the dynamism that makes broad prosperity possible.

Start with the point on which Grusky is clearly right: much of what is called the free market is not free. The financial crisis revealed the problem in dramatic form. Large financial institutions took risks in a system that everyone knew the government could not allow simply to collapse. That implicit guarantee is a subsidy. It encourages leverage, increases profits in good times, and leaves taxpayers exposed in bad times. Ending “too big to fail,” raising capital requirements, simplifying resolution authority, and allowing failure where possible are market reforms, not anti-market reforms.

The same is true of corporate welfare, targeted tax preferences, agricultural subsidies, protectionist measures, and regulations written by incumbents to burden competitors. Occupational licensing has expanded far beyond obvious health and safety cases, often making it harder for lower-income workers to enter trades. Zoning restrictions in prosperous metropolitan areas limit housing supply and keep less affluent families away from good schools and job markets. Patent and copyright rules can encourage innovation, but they can also be stretched to protect incumbents. These are excellent targets for reform, and they do not require a broad assault on high incomes as such.

Where Grusky is less convincing is in his treatment of higher education. He describes elite university admissions as a bottleneck similar to a producer refusing to expand output when demand rises. But universities are not hybrid-car factories. The value of Harvard, Stanford, or Berkeley is not simply the classroom instruction they provide. It includes peer effects, faculty attention, research culture, alumni networks, and reputational signals. These cannot be expanded indefinitely without changing the product. One can favor more access to excellent education without imagining that elite institutions could simply “ramp up production” until the scarcity value disappears.

Nor is it obvious that the college wage premium is mainly a rent. It may reflect real changes in technology and global competition that have increased the productivity of skilled workers relative to less-skilled workers. Computers complement abstract reasoning and management skills while substituting for routine tasks. Trade exposes less-skilled manufacturing workers to more competition while increasing returns to design, logistics, finance, and entrepreneurship. If the premium reflects productivity rather than artificial scarcity, then reducing it by flooding the market with credentials will not solve the underlying problem. We may end up with more indebted graduates and continued weakness among those without strong skills.

The better education agenda is not to equalize outcomes among graduates and non-graduates but to improve human capital at every level. That means early childhood literacy, serious K–12 accountability, alternatives to failing schools, vocational and technical education, apprenticeships, community colleges tied to employers, and lower-cost routes to certification. It also means being honest that not every valuable worker needs a four-year degree. The current obsession with college for all can become its own bottleneck, devaluing practical skills and encouraging credential inflation.

On CEO pay, Grusky again identifies a real governance problem but too quickly assumes that high compensation is evidence of rent. Some executives are overpaid. Some boards are too cozy. Yet large modern firms are enormously complex, and the difference between a good and bad chief executive can be worth billions of dollars. In a global market for managerial talent, it is not absurd that top executives earn much more than they did when firms were smaller, more national, and less exposed to rapid technological change. The solution is not to moralize about ratios but to ensure transparency, shareholder accountability, and genuine board independence. If owners want to pay a fortune for talent with their own money, that is not a public scandal merely because the number is large.

The tax agenda is still more problematic. Raising top marginal rates may reduce rent-seeking, as some economists argue, but it may also reduce entrepreneurship, investment, and the willingness of highly productive people to work, build, and take risks. The United States already faces long-term fiscal pressures from health care costs and entitlement commitments. We should raise the revenue we need with a tax system that is broad-based, simple, and pro-growth, not one designed primarily to express disapproval of the rich. Closing loopholes and taxing consumption more efficiently may do more good than symbolic rate hikes.

The deepest problem with the inequality debate is that it often confuses relative position with absolute well-being. A poor family is not helped if a millionaire becomes less rich while the economy stagnates. What matters most is whether people can find work, start businesses, afford housing, educate their children, obtain health care, and rise through effort and talent. Inequality can threaten those goals when it reflects barriers to entry or political capture. But the target should be the barriers and the capture, not inequality itself.

Grusky’s call for institutional repair is therefore welcome, but it should be detached from the egalitarian mood of the moment. Let us attack bailouts, subsidies, licensing cartels, exclusionary zoning, corrupt procurement, and rigged corporate governance. Let us improve schools and open paths into skilled work. Let us make markets more competitive, not because doing so will guarantee a particular distribution, but because open competition is fairer and more productive than privilege. If the result is still substantial inequality, we should not automatically lament it. Inequality earned in open markets is not a failure of capitalism. It is one of the prices, and sometimes one of the engines, of freedom and growth.

  2. 02Market distortions like 'too big to fail' bailouts, corporate welfare, and restrictive occupational licensing should be abolished because they are unfair subsidies, not because they create high earners.
  3. 03The college wage premium is likely a reflection of increased productivity in a technological and globalized economy rather than a result of artificial bottlenecks in elite university admissions.
  4. 04Education reform should focus on improving human capital and practical skills at all levels rather than pushing a 'college for all' agenda that leads to credential inflation.
  5. 05High executive compensation is not inherently a public scandal if it is determined by transparent, independent boards and reflects the immense value a leader adds to a complex global firm.
  6. 06Using the tax system to express disapproval of the rich or to reduce rent-seeking risks discouraging entrepreneurship and investment.
  7. 07The ultimate objective of policy should be to improve absolute well-being and social mobility by removing barriers to entry, regardless of whether those changes reduce relative inequality.
