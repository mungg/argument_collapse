Diversified MiniMax

"While David Grusky correctly identifies institutional market failures as the root of inequality, his solution is too narrow because it focuses primarily on education reform rather than a comprehensive restructuring of labor market and corporate institutions."

David Grusky's diagnosis of market failure and rent-seeking is largely correct, but his proposed remedies are too narrow. If inequality is generated by distorted institutions, then the appropriate response is to reform those institutions. Yet Grusky focuses almost exclusively on education reform, treating expanded access to college as the key to reduced inequality. This focus misses the broader institutional landscape that shapes economic outcomes, particularly the labor market institutions that determine wages for the vast majority of workers who do not hold elite credentials.

The United States has deliberately weakened the labor market institutions that would protect workers and reduce inequality. Union membership has declined from roughly a quarter of the private sector workforce in the 1970s to barely more than 7 percent today. The minimum wage, in real terms, is substantially lower than it was decades ago. Employment protections have been rolled back. These changes are not incidental; they reflect deliberate policy choices that have increased employer power and reduced worker bargaining power. The result has been stagnant wages for many workers and increased inequality. This is a market distortion, but Grusky's preferred remedy—education reform—does not directly address it.

Consider what institutional repair in labor markets would look like. Restoring the minimum wage to a level that provides a living wage would directly raise earnings for millions of workers. Reviving union membership through supportive legislation would restore bargaining power to workers who currently have none. Strengthening employment protections would reduce the precarity that characterizes so many low-wage jobs. These reforms would attack inequality at its root by restoring competition in labor markets where employers currently exercise substantial monopsony power over workers who have few alternatives.

Grusky would object that such reforms are politically infeasible. And indeed, they face powerful opposition from business interests that benefit from current arrangements. But the same objection applies to his own preferred reforms. Education reform also faces entrenched opposition, and the history of failed reforms in this area is extensive. If we are going to invest political capital in institutional change, we should choose reforms that directly address inequality rather than hoping that expanded college access will eventually trickle down to reduce wage dispersion.

There is also a conceptual problem with Grusky's exclusive focus on education. By emphasizing credential acquisition, he implicitly accepts a framework in which workers compete for positions based on their educational attainments. This framework may justify unequal outcomes if those attainments reflect genuine differences in skills or contributions. But many credentialed workers do not earn returns that reflect their marginal productivity; they earn returns that reflect their position in a credentialed hierarchy. The problem is not simply that some workers lack credentials but that the credential system itself allocates rewards in ways that have little to do with social contribution.

A more fundamental institutional reform would restructure the wage-setting process itself, reducing the power of employers relative to workers regardless of educational attainment. This is what unions historically achieved and what stronger minimum wages would partially restore. The goal is not to equalize credentials but to ensure that workers at all levels of education receive a fair share of the value they create.

Grusky's argument about CEO pay points in a similar direction. He correctly identifies the conflict of interest that arises when boards set executive compensation, but his proposed remedy—reforming corporate governance practices—is limited. A more effective approach would be to tax excess compensation more heavily, to require greater transparency in pay-setting, and to empower shareholders to challenge management proposals. These are institutional reforms, but they operate through tax policy and disclosure requirements rather than through restructuring the internal governance of corporations.

The broader lesson is that institutional reform encompasses many possible interventions. Grusky is right that we should not limit ourselves to after-the-fact redistribution, but we should also not limit ourselves to a single favored reform. Education expansion is valuable, but it is not sufficient. We need a comprehensive agenda that includes labor market reform, corporate governance reform, and yes, tax reform as well. The specific mix will depend on political circumstances, but the overarching principle is clear: because inequality is generated by institutions, it must be addressed by institutional change.

Occupy Wall Street drew attention to the concentration of wealth and power at the top. Grusky's essay translates that attention into a sophisticated diagnosis of market failure. But the prescription that follows—primarily education reform—does not fully exploit the diagnostic insight. A movement that truly aims to reduce inequality must demand change across the institutional landscape, not just in one sector. Anything less leaves the underlying distortions intact.

  3. 03Grusky’s focus on credentials implicitly accepts a flawed hierarchy where rewards are based on educational attainment rather than actual social contribution or productivity.
  4. 04Addressing extreme executive pay requires a broader toolkit than internal corporate governance reform, including tax policy and transparency requirements.
  5. 05A truly effective response to inequality must be a comprehensive agenda that targets distortions across the entire institutional landscape rather than relying on a single sector like education.
