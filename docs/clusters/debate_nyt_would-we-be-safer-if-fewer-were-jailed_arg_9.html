"We can achieve a safer and more just society by implementing targeted, evidence-based reductions in jail populations while replacing cash bail with risk-based supervision and treatment."

Advocates on both sides of the jail debate marshal statistics like weapons, but the honest answer is that the evidence is murky. Would we be safer if fewer were jailed? It depends on a host of variables: who is released, at what stage, under what conditions, and with what supports. Rather than a blanket yes or no, the most responsible position is to embrace targeted, evidence-based reductions while frankly acknowledging the limits of our predictive capacity.

The most powerful argument for reduction comes from the incapacitation literature. Conservative estimates suggest that incarcerating the average inmate prevents a handful of minor property crimes per year, but for serious violent offenses, the effect is much smaller and concentrated among a tiny subset of high-rate offenders. A 2014 Brennan Center analysis found that excessive incarceration beyond a certain tipping point does little to reduce crime and may even increase it in some neighborhoods because of the destabilizing effects on families and labor markets. For low-risk pretrial detainees — those charged with nonviolent misdemeanors or ordinance violations — the crime prevention benefit of jailing them is essentially zero, and the collateral damage is enormous.

But that optimistic framing masks real heterogeneity. Risk assessment instruments, such as the Public Safety Assessment now being piloted in several states, can sort pretrial populations by flight risk and new-crime probability. They show that about 15 percent of defendants score high on indicators like prior violent convictions, recent failures to appear, or age under 23 at first arrest. For this subgroup, pretrial detention may well prevent serious harm. The problem is that these tools are probabilistic, not deterministic. They produce false positives and false negatives. If we release thousands more people based on a risk algorithm that misses, say, 5 percent of high-risk cases, we will inevitably see some serious crimes committed by released defendants. The political and human toll of those "missed calls" can derail rational reform.

The solution is not paralysis but thoughtful iteration. We should eliminate money bail for minor offenses, as Washington, D.C. did decades ago with its pretrial services model, and invest heavily in supervision and treatment alternatives. Early data from the Laura and John Arnold Foundation’s work in places like Charlotte, North Carolina, suggest that risk-based release can reduce jail populations by 15 to 20 percent without increasing failure-to-appear rates. Simultaneously, we must build in rapid-response mechanisms to adjust when something goes wrong, rather than abandoning reform after one headline-grabbing tragedy. No system can eliminate risk, but the current one — which cages the poor for want of cash and ignores the dangerous rich — is so irrational that moderate, well-managed reduction is almost certain to be at least as safe, and far more just. The safest future lies not in fewer jails per se, but in smarter ones.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Excessive incarceration of low-risk and nonviolent offenders provides negligible crime prevention benefits while destabilizing families and local labor markets.
  2. 02Research indicates that the crime-prevention effect of incapacitation is concentrated among a small subset of high-rate offenders, meaning broad incarceration is an inefficient safety strategy.
  3. 03While risk assessment tools are probabilistic and imperfect, they allow for a more rational sorting of defendants than the current wealth-based bail system.
  4. 04Implementing risk-based release models and investing in pretrial supervision can significantly reduce jail populations without increasing rates of defendants failing to appear in court.
  5. 05A smarter jail system must balance population reduction with rapid-response mechanisms to address the inevitable but manageable risks associated with pretrial release.
