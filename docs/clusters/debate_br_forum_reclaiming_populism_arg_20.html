"Instead of reclaiming populism to capture state power, the left should focus on building autonomous, self-organized institutions that operate outside the state-centric paradigm."

The phrase “reclaiming populism” suggested by Adom Getachew’s lead essay is, on its face, a reasonable response to the distortions of mainstream political discourse. Populism has indeed become a slur, used to dismiss any challenge to the status quo as dangerously illiberal. And Getachew is correct that the left must appeal to a broad constituency and build a majoritarian politics capable of countering the rising right. Yet I want to suggest that the entire framework is a detour. The populist tradition, even in its most progressive incarnations, remains wedded to the state form, to electoralism, and to a conception of power as something to be captured at the top. In the midst of a pandemic that has simultaneously revealed the profound inadequacy of state responses and unleashed a wave of popular mutual aid, we might instead ask: What if the path forward is not to reclaim populism but to abandon the state-centric paradigm altogether? What if the left’s energy should be directed toward building autonomous, self-organized institutions that prefigure the world we want, rather than trying to storm the citadel of state power?

Getachew’s essay opens with the Chicago Teachers Union strike, and she rightly celebrates its importance. But she frames the strike primarily as a stepping-stone to electoral power: CTU members have been elected to the Cook County Board and the City Council, forming a Socialist Caucus that can legislate change. This narrative, common among the progressive left, treats the strike as a valuable moment of mobilizing that ultimately finds its fulfillment in the state. I see the CTU strike differently. Its radicalism lay not in its electoral aftereffects but in the act itself: thousands of workers withdrawing their labor, organized through a democratic union, making demands that directly challenge the structure of austerity and privatization, and doing so in alliance with parents and community groups. That is the real power. The electoral victories are supplementary at best and may well become a trap, absorbing the movement’s energy into the very apparatus that has overseen decades of school closures, budget cuts, and privatization. The state in the United States is not a neutral tool that can be wielded for progressive ends once the right people are in charge. It is a deeply embedded structure of racial and class domination, from its origins in settler colonialism and slavery to its contemporary forms of policing, incarceration, border enforcement, and imperial war. Reforming it is not impossible, but any attempt to do so through a populist capture of the executive or legislative branches will inevitably confront the structural limits of capitalist democracy. The trillions of dollars suddenly found to bail out corporations during the pandemic should be a stark reminder: the state serves capital, and it will do so regardless of who holds office, unless the balance of social forces is transformed from below.

The Working Families Party’s project of a “left-led, multiracial working class alignment” is admirable in its inclusivity. But I fear it reproduces a logic of representation in which the alignment is led by a party and its candidates, rather than emerging organically from the self-activity of diverse communities. The language of populism—the people versus the elite—can be useful for rallying masses, but it also tends to homogenize the people, smoothing over the very real tensions and conflicts within the working class, and it directs attention upward, toward the capitol or the White House, rather than outward, toward the neighborhood, the workplace, and the ecological commons. What is happening right now across the country, largely outside the gaze of national politics, is a resurgence of mutual aid. In New York, in Detroit, in Puerto Rico, ordinary people are organizing to deliver food, produce masks, provide childcare, and care for the sick. Tenants are organizing rent strikes. Workers are walking off the job in wildcat strikes for safety protections. These actions are not coordinated by a central committee. They are not framed as a populist struggle against the elite. They are simply people doing what needs to be done because the state and the market have failed. This is the embryo of a different kind of politics—one that does not seek to represent the people but to enable people to govern their own lives directly.

The theorists of the early New Left, drawing on the anarchist and council communist traditions, used to speak of “prefigurative politics”: the idea that the means of struggle must embody the ends, that the forms of organization we build today should mirror the free, egalitarian society we hope to create. The mutual aid networks, the neighborhood assemblies, the worker cooperatives, the community land trusts—these are not merely stopgap measures until we win the next election. They are the building blocks of a new social order that can grow in the cracks of the old. The state will not deliver us universal health care; we must build community clinics and health collectives that operate on principles of solidarity, not profit. The state will not solve the climate crisis; we must build renewable energy cooperatives and transform production at the community level, taking direct action to stop pipelines and fracking. The state will not abolish prisons; we must build networks of transformative justice that resolve harm without cages. All of this requires a massive shift of organizing energy away from the electoral arena and toward the terrain of everyday life.

Getachew’s essay notes the failure of Sanders’s campaign, the ouster of Morales, and the defeat of Corbyn. From my perspective, these defeats are not just contingent; they are indicative of the limits of left populism. In each case, an electoral or party-focused project was unable to withstand the counterattack of capital and the right, because the social movement base that supported it was insufficiently autonomous and self-organized. The rank-and-file did not have the independent power to push back because they had, in effect, delegated their agency to the party and its leader. Corbyn’s Labour membership was vast but largely passive, a cheering section rather than a decentralized force capable of sustained mass action. Sanders’s army of small donors and volunteers could not coordinate a nationwide rent strike or a general strike because the campaign did not build that kind of horizontal infrastructure; it built a vertical one aimed at winning the nomination. And when the nomination was lost, much of that infrastructure dissolved, though some is being repurposed into mutual aid. The lesson is that electoral campaigns are fragile vessels for movement energy. The energy they generate must be anchored in institutions that do not depend on the electoral cycle—unions, yes, but also tenants’ unions, neighborhood assemblies, solidarity networks, and eco-villages.

The pandemic has also made visible the deadly consequences of the global nationalist order. Borders, those instruments of state sovereignty, have been used to block travel and hoard medical supplies. The populist right uses the pandemic to scapegoat immigrants and China. But a left populism that remains within the national frame risks its own forms of exclusion. It tells the story of the American people rising up, but it does not challenge the fundamental legitimacy of the border regime that consigns millions to second-class status or denies them entry altogether. An anarchist politics, by contrast, is inherently internationalist. It sees the global working class as one, and it refuses the very concept of national sovereignty. The mutual aid networks that have emerged in the pandemic are already transnational in spirit: communities learning from one another across borders, sharing knowledge, and sending resources where they are needed. The Rojava revolution in north-east Syria, the Zapatista autonomous municipalities in Chiapas, the worker-recuperated factories in Argentina—these are not examples of populism. They are examples of ordinary people building power without trying to capture the state, and they offer a more compelling vision for the future than any electoral strategy.

I can already hear the objections: this is hopelessly utopian. Mutual aid cannot replace the massive resources that only a state can mobilize. The climate emergency requires a Green New Deal that only a federal government can implement. The right is organized and armed; we need a state to protect us. These are serious arguments, and I do not dismiss them lightly. But I think they overestimate what the state can deliver and underestimate the potential of decentralized, federated power. The New Deal, after all, was a response to the massive wave of strikes, factory occupations, and unemployed councils that threatened the capitalist order from below. It was a concession wrested by a militant movement, not a gift bestowed by enlightened politicians. The Green New Deal will only be won in the same way: through direct action that disrupts the fossil fuel industry, through land occupations that reclaim territory for Indigenous sovereignty, through community-owned energy projects that make large-scale fossil fuel infrastructure obsolete. The federal government may eventually codify these victories into law, but that will be a ratification of a new social reality, not the primary engine of change.

Moreover, the experience of the pandemic suggests that the state is not the reliable guarantor of protection that its defenders claim. It was the state that failed to stockpile ventilators, the state that forced nursing homes to accept COVID-positive patients, the state that continues to deport people and detain migrants in conditions ripe for mass infection. It is mutual aid that is providing masks, food, and emotional support. This is not to romanticize mutual aid; it is meager compared to the need, and it can be exhausting and unsustainable. But it demonstrates that people can organize themselves in the absence of state action, and it builds the kind of solidarity and trust that formal political institutions cannot manufacture. The left’s task should be to strengthen these networks, to connect them into regional and national federations, and to develop the capacity for large-scale coordinated action—general strikes, boycotts, occupations—that can bring the economy to a halt if necessary. That is a politics not of populist representation but of direct, counter-hegemonic power.

In the end, I am not entirely opposed to using the language of populism tactically, in certain contexts. But as a strategic vision, it leads us back into the dead end of state power. The truly emancipatory horizon is not a reclaimed populism but a liberated society: a world without bosses, without landlords, without prisons, without borders. The seeds of that world are being planted right now, in the mutual aid projects, the wildcat strikes, the rent strikes, the community gardens. The left should water those seeds, not transplant them into the sterile soil of the electoral machine. The pandemic has given us a glimpse of both the horror of the current system and the possibility of a different way of life. The choice is ours.
