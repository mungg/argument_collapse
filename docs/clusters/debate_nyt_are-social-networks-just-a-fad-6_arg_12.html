Diversified DeepSeek

"Social networks are not a temporary fad but a permanent shift in communication infrastructure, though their political and economic potential has been significantly overestimated."

Social networks are not a fad, but their political significance has been dangerously overrated, and that mismatch feeds the fad narrative. The telephone was never a fad, but nobody claimed it could spark a revolution by itself. Thomas Friedman is right to question whether Facebook and Twitter can supply “brick and mortar” for change, but the question itself misunderstands the technology. Social networks are communication tools, not political parties. They lower the coordination costs of protest, just as the printing press or the photocopier did before them. That’s a permanent, not a temporary, capability. The candlelit vigils organized via text message in Seoul in 2008, the flash mobs, the Arab Spring eruptions—these aren’t anomalies; they are the natural emergent behavior of a densely connected populace. The fact that they don’t automatically build lasting institutions doesn’t make the networks a fad; it just means revolutions require more than tweets.

What’s striking is how deeply these networks have embedded themselves as social infrastructure regardless of politics. The family members peering at estranged loved ones through a screen are not doing something faddish; they are adapting an ancient pain to a new medium, just as families did with letters and phone calls. The personal use case—maintaining a lifeline, however imperfect—is what guarantees longevity. Facebook’s IPO troubles reflect the difficulty of monetizing that utility, not the utility itself. We don’t call electricity a fad because a power company goes bankrupt. The networks will endure, but our expectations of what they can deliver, from revolution to advertising clicks, will sober up.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Social media functions as a communication tool that permanently lowers the coordination costs for collective action, similar to the printing press or photocopier.
  2. 02The inability of social networks to build lasting political institutions is a limitation of the technology's purpose rather than evidence of its obsolescence.
  3. 03These platforms have become deeply embedded as social infrastructure by facilitating fundamental human interactions and emotional connections.
  4. 04Financial volatility and IPO struggles reflect the difficulty of monetizing social utility rather than a decline in the utility itself.
  5. 05The perceived decline of social networks is actually a necessary correction of unrealistic expectations regarding their ability to drive revolution and advertising revenue.
