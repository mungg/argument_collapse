Diversified Gemini

"The United States should modernize its nuclear arsenal by transitioning from a traditional triad to a more cost-effective and safer "dyad" that excludes land-based intercontinental ballistic missiles (ICBMs)."

As the United States stares down a projected $1 trillion price tag to modernize its nuclear forces over the next three decades, we must confront a harsh budgetary and strategic reality: we are paying for a Cold War architecture that no longer fits the 21st century. We absolutely must maintain a credible nuclear deterrent, but we do not need the traditional "triad" of submarines, bombers, and land-based intercontinental ballistic missiles (ICBMs) to do it.

An appropriate modernization of the nuclear arsenal should begin by excluding the most dangerous and redundant leg of the triad: the ICBMs.

Currently, the Air Force plans to spend tens of billions of dollars replacing the aging Minuteman III missiles scattered across silos in the American Midwest. These land-based missiles are a relic of a bygone era. Unlike submarines, which are virtually undetectable and guarantee a retaliatory second strike, ICBM silos are fixed targets. Their precise coordinates are known to our adversaries.

Because they are vulnerable to a first strike, ICBMs operate on a hair-trigger alert. In the event of a suspected incoming attack, the President would have only minutes to decide whether to launch them before they are destroyed in the ground. This "use them or lose them" dynamic creates an unacceptable risk of an accidental nuclear apocalypse triggered by a false alarm, a computer glitch, or a cyberattack on early warning systems.

By phasing out the ICBM force entirely, the U.S. would save billions of dollars that could be reallocated to conventional military readiness or domestic priorities. More importantly, we would drastically reduce the risk of accidental nuclear war.

Deterrence can be safely and reliably maintained through a "dyad." The stealth capabilities of our strategic bomber fleet offer a visible, recallable deterrent, while the Ohio-class replacement submarines provide an invulnerable, guaranteed second-strike capability. No adversary would dare attack the United States knowing that our silent submarine fleet holds enough firepower beneath the waves to destroy them many times over. Modernization must be an opportunity to streamline our forces, not blindly rebuild the bloated arsenals of the past.

  1. 01ICBMs are strategically redundant because submarines and bombers already provide a credible and sufficient nuclear deterrent.
  2. 02The fixed locations of ICBM silos make them vulnerable targets that create a dangerous "use them or lose them" pressure, increasing the risk of accidental nuclear war.
  3. 03Phasing out the ICBM leg of the triad would save billions of dollars that could be better utilized for conventional military readiness or domestic needs.
  4. 04A dyad consisting of stealth bombers and invulnerable submarines provides a reliable second-strike capability that is more than enough to deter any adversary.
