Diversified Gemini

"The FDA’s approval of genetically engineered salmon without mandatory labeling is a failure of transparency that violates consumer rights and undermines public trust in science."

Regardless of where one stands on the underlying science of genetic engineering, the FDA’s handling of the AquAdvantage salmon approval is a masterclass in how to erode public trust. By approving the first genetically engineered animal for human consumption while simultaneously refusing to mandate clear labeling, the government has shown a profound disregard for the basic rights of consumers.

The FDA justifies its refusal to require labeling by claiming there is no "material difference" between the genetically engineered salmon and conventional Atlantic salmon. In their view, because the nutritional profile is roughly the same, the method of production is irrelevant. This is a patronizing and narrow interpretation of what matters to the American public. A "material difference" should not be strictly limited to protein counts and omega-3 levels; it must encompass the very real ethical, environmental, and personal concerns that guide how people choose to feed their families.

Consumers have a fundamental right to know what is in their food and how it was produced. When people go to the grocery store, they are making purchasing decisions based on a wide variety of values. We label orange juice from concentrate; we label wild-caught versus farm-raised fish. Yet, when a fish has been spliced with the DNA of an ocean pout to grow at twice the normal rate, the FDA insists we are not entitled to that information.

This lack of transparency is inherently dangerous because it breeds suspicion and hostility toward food science. If this genetically engineered salmon is truly the agricultural miracle its creators claim it to be, why hide it? Why not label it proudly and let it compete on the open market? By denying mandatory labeling, the FDA is forcing consumers to play a guessing game, shifting the burden onto traditional seafood producers to proactively label their products as "Non-GMO."

True scientific progress requires societal consent. By slipping GE salmon into the food supply unannounced, the FDA is denying the public the agency to make their own informed choices, effectively prioritizing the commercial viability of a single biotech company over consumer transparency.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The FDA’s definition of 'material difference' is too narrow because it ignores the ethical and environmental concerns that inform consumer choices.
  2. 02Consumers have a fundamental right to know the production methods of their food, similar to existing labels for juice concentrate or farm-raised fish.
  3. 03A lack of transparency creates unnecessary public suspicion and hostility toward beneficial advancements in food science.
  4. 04The refusal to mandate labeling unfairly shifts the burden of proof and costs onto traditional seafood producers to identify their products as non-GMO.
  5. 05True scientific progress requires societal consent and the ability for individuals to make informed decisions about what they eat.
