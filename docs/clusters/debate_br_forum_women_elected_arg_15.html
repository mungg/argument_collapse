Diversified Gemini

"While constitutional reforms face steep hurdles in the U.S., political parties should use their status as private associations to voluntarily implement internal rules and resource allocations that guarantee women’s right to be elected."

Jennifer M. Piscopo’s brilliant analysis of the "right to be elected" exposes a glaring blind spot in American democratic thought. For too long, the United States has hidden behind the polite fiction that removing formal, legal barriers to candidacy is sufficient for achieving equality. Piscopo is entirely correct that leaving women to "pull themselves up by their electoral bootstraps" in the face of structural sexism is a failure of democratic justice. However, while Piscopo points toward the state and the Constitution as the ultimate arbiters of positive rights, the most immediate and pragmatic vehicle for revolutionary change in U.S. politics lies elsewhere: the internal machinery of our political parties.

Because the U.S. relies on a candidate-centric, primary-driven electoral system, applying national legislative quotas à la France or Argentina faces steep constitutional hurdles under current Supreme Court jurisprudence regarding the First Amendment and equal protection. But as Piscopo briefly hints toward the end of her essay, political parties are unique entities. They are private associations with broad First Amendment rights of their own—specifically, the right to free association, which gives them immense leeway in determining how they conduct their internal affairs, allocate resources, and select their standard-bearers.

If we accept Piscopo’s premise that the underrepresentation of women is a crisis of democratic legitimacy, we do not need to wait for a constitutional amendment or a sympathetic Supreme Court. We need the Democratic and Republican parties to voluntarily operationalize positive rights within their own bylaws.

The precedent already exists. As Piscopo notes, the Democratic National Committee (DNC) mandates gender parity for its convention delegates and committee members. This rule, born out of the McGovern-Fraser reforms of the 1970s, fundamentally changed the visual and substantive makeup of the party apparatus. But convention delegates do not pass laws. To achieve actual descriptive representation in the halls of power, parties must extend this logic from the convention floor to the primary ballot.

How could this work in practice? While parties cannot legally bar men from registering for a primary, they can ruthlessly and unapologetically stack the deck in favor of female candidates to compensate for historical inequities.

First, the national and state party committees could adopt binding rules regarding candidate recruitment. The "Rooney Rule" used in the NFL requires teams to interview minority candidates for head coaching jobs. Political parties could institute "Parity Rules," mandating that in any open-seat race (where no incumbent is running), the party’s campaign arm will not provide any institutional support, voter data access, or early funding unless at least 50 percent of the recruited "shortlist" of viable candidates are women.

Second, the parties can weaponize their most potent asset: campaign finance. The Democratic Congressional Campaign Committee (DCCC) and the National Republican Congressional Committee (NRCC) wield massive influence in competitive primaries by signaling to donors who the "establishment" pick is. To implement positive action, parties could create structural financial advantages for women. For instance, party bylaws could stipulate that in any primary featuring a highly qualified woman against a male candidate, the party’s independent expenditure arm will heavily weigh gender as a critical asset for the general election, intentionally offsetting the "boys' club" donor networks that historically favor men.

Furthermore, parties can address the structural barriers Piscopo identifies—such as the prohibitive cost of running and the sexual division of labor—by directly subsidizing the campaigns of women and mothers. The Federal Election Commission has recently allowed campaign funds to be used for childcare. The DNC and RNC could establish dedicated, multi-million-dollar funds exclusively designed to provide living stipends and childcare costs for working-class women running for state and federal office. This is the essence of a positive right: the institution actively provides the material resources necessary for the citizen to exercise their right to be elected.

Some will argue that parties overtly favoring female candidates in primaries subverts the democratic will of the voters. But this assumes the current primary process is a neutral, level playing field. As Piscopo outlines through the "performance premium" and the fact that women are often told to "wait their turn," the current system is already heavily biased toward men. Party intervention would not be corrupting a pure process; it would be applying a necessary counter-weight to a system deeply corrupted by patriarchal norms.

The international examples Piscopo highlights—Sweden, Germany, South Africa—are highly instructive here. In many of these nations, the leap in women's representation occurred not because the federal government mandated it, but because center-left parties voluntarily adopted internal gender quotas. Once one major party achieved gender parity, it became an electoral liability for rival parties to remain entirely male-dominated, forcing a system-wide behavioral shift without a single change to national law.

In the United States, the Democratic Party, which openly champions gender equality and relies heavily on the organizing power of women, is perfectly positioned to lead this shift. The party platform already acknowledges structural racism and sexism; it is time for party operations to reflect that reality.

Piscopo is right: the centennial of suffrage is an opportunity to redefine citizenship to include the power to govern. We do not need to look solely to the courts or the legislature to fulfill this promise. The mechanisms for positive action are already lying dormant within the headquarters of our political parties. It is time for party leaders to stop merely celebrating the women who manage to survive the grueling, unequal gauntlet of American campaigns, and start actively paving the road for them.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The U.S. primary system is not a neutral playing field, and party intervention is a necessary counter-weight to existing patriarchal biases and structural barriers.
  2. 02Political parties can operationalize positive rights by mandating gender parity in candidate recruitment shortlists for open-seat races.
  3. 03Parties should weaponize campaign finance by providing institutional support and financial advantages specifically to female candidates to offset traditional 'boys' club' donor networks.
  4. 04National and state committees should directly subsidize the material costs of running for office, such as childcare and living stipends, to enable working-class women to compete.
  5. 05International precedents show that voluntary party quotas can trigger a system-wide shift in representation by making male-dominated tickets an electoral liability for rival parties.
  6. 06The Democratic Party is uniquely positioned to lead this change by aligning its internal operations with its stated platform of championing gender equality.
