"Instead of retreating into a local, slow-paced, and less acquisitive lifestyle to save the planet, we should leverage technology and green growth to create a future of clean abundance that preserves high-energy pleasures and mobility."

Kate Soper has written a stirring brief against consumer culture, and she is right that the climate crisis forces us to ask uncomfortable questions about how we live. But her answer — a “postconsumerist” vision of slower, more local, less acquisitive pleasures, which she brands “alternative hedonism” — is built on a false dilemma. She asks us to choose between environmental ruin and a retreat from the pleasures of abundance that technology and growth have made possible. The real opportunity of the 21st century is far more exciting: we can have a green planet and a high-energy, high-mobility, high-pleasure life. We don’t need to learn to love less; we need to build clean abundance.

Soper mistakes the historical form of consumer culture for its essence. The polluting, wasteful, car-choked world of the 20th century was the result of a particular fossil-fueled pathway, not an inevitable byproduct of wanting comfort, speed, and novelty. She acknowledges that greener technologies will help, but she dismisses them too quickly, suggesting that electric vehicles will “protract the dangers and spatial monopoly of car culture” rather than moving us beyond its mindset. Yet that mindset is not a fixed car-loving psychology; it is a rational response to a built environment that for decades has been structured around the automobile. Remaking our cities to be greener and more walkable does not require abandoning personal mobility; it requires redesigning streets, building out public transit, and electrifying what remains. The electric scooter and the autonomous electric pod are not evil cousins of the SUV; they are tools that can reclaim urban space for people while preserving the freedom to move swiftly when desired.

The same holds for air travel. Soper paints a grim picture of far-flung vacations as exhausting, high-stakes memory factories. No doubt some are. But the answer is not to shame people into vacationing close to home; it is to develop zero-carbon aviation and high-speed rail networks that make long-distance travel clean and affordable. Synthetic fuels, hydrogen aircraft, and improved battery technologies are advancing rapidly. If we sweepingly declare that the pleasures of seeing the world are a casualty of climate action, we lose a powerful political constituency — and we lose something genuinely valuable about human experience: the thrill of encountering a radically different culture, climate, or landscape. The pandemic lockdowns taught us what enforced localism feels like; many of us missed the wider world deeply.

Soper’s argument about work is similarly tinged with asceticism dressed in hedonist garb. Yes, a shorter workweek could be beneficial for many. But the pleasures she describes — artisanal production, craft, self-provisioning — are not for everyone. Plenty of people find real joy in fast-paced, complex, high-tech work. The satisfaction of a software developer shipping a product used by millions, or a researcher pushing the boundaries of what is known, is not a “bullshit job” merely because it is embedded in a growth economy. Soper’s vision of slow, small-scale making risks projecting a particular aesthetic preference — for the handmade, the local, the tangible — onto a diverse population with wildly different sources of fulfillment. A universal basic income and reduced working hours could certainly free people to pursue these if they choose, but they could also allow people to dive deeper into forms of creative, technological, or intellectual production that require large-scale infrastructure and complex supply chains. The “smartest, greenest technologies” she mentions in medicine and energy will not emerge from artisanal workshops; they require the scale and capital that only a thriving, growth-oriented industrial base can provide, albeit one that runs on green electrons rather than fossil molecules.

Implicit in Soper’s essay is a deep suspicion of technology and mass production as inherently alienating. But technology can also enhance human pleasure enormously. Virtual reality, for example, could reduce the need for physical travel while offering rich, shared experiences. Lab-grown meat could deliver the savors of steak without the misery of factory farming or the methane. High-density, low-carbon cities, served by elevators and AI-managed systems, can create entirely new kinds of urban joy: spectacular vertical gardens, vibrant digital public squares, abundant cultural venues. None of this requires buying endless disposable junk. It requires innovation, investment, and, yes, growth — not growth in material throughput, necessarily, but growth in the capacity to deliver human satisfaction while radically reducing our planetary footprint.

Decoupling economic growth from resource use is not a pipe dream; it is an observable trend in many wealthy economies, where GDP rises while emissions fall, thanks to efficiency, digitization, and service-based expansion. Soper treats the philosophy of “green growth” as a dubious compromise, but it is the only realistic pathway that can bring around the majority of humanity who still aspire to the material security and opportunity that affluence represents. Telling the global middle class that their desire for a bigger home, a holiday in Thailand, or a tablet for their child is a false consciousness is both politically suicidal and, frankly, arrogant. People are not dupes of advertising; they have genuine aspirations that a clean-energy economy can meet far more democratically than our current dirty one.

Let me be clear: I share Soper’s disgust at the exploitative, wasteful, surveillance-driven aspects of modern commerce. But we don’t need to sacrifice pleasure to fix them. We need to build an economy that makes sustainable choices the most enjoyable, convenient, and desirable options — not by nagging people to want less, but by engineering abundance without the carbon. That is a hedonism for everyone, not just for those with a taste for slow food and cycle rickshaws.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The negative aspects of modern consumerism are a result of a specific fossil-fueled pathway rather than an inherent byproduct of human desires for comfort, speed, and novelty.
  2. 02Environmental goals should be met by redesigning infrastructure and developing zero-carbon technologies, such as high-speed rail and synthetic aviation fuels, rather than shaming people for wanting to travel.
  3. 03The preference for artisanal, slow-scale production is a specific aesthetic choice that should not be imposed on a diverse population whose fulfillment often comes from complex, high-tech, and large-scale work.
  4. 04Innovation and economic growth are necessary to provide the capital and infrastructure required to develop the very green technologies that will solve the climate crisis.
  5. 05Technology can enhance human pleasure and well-being through new mediums like virtual reality and lab-grown meat without increasing material throughput or environmental degradation.
  6. 06Decoupling economic growth from resource use is a realistic and observable trend that offers a more politically viable and democratic path to global prosperity than advocating for degrowth.
