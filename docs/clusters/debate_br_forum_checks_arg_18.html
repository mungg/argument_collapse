Diversified DeepSeek

"The critique of checks and balances is incomplete because it treats institutional dysfunction as a procedural flaw rather than a deliberate manifestation of racial capitalism and white supremacy designed into the American constitutional order."

Lisa L. Miller’s critique of checks and balances is a welcome departure from liberal pieties, and her insistence on linking institutional design to elite power is necessary. Yet for all its virtues, her argument remains trapped within the very constitutionalist framework it seeks to transcend. By focusing on veto points as the central problem and constitutional reform as the primary remedy, she misses the deeper structure of racial capitalism that has shaped American governance from its founding. The dead end is not merely procedural; it is a system that has always subordinated both the state and the market to the perpetuation of white supremacy and capital accumulation. Without confronting that reality head-on, tinkering with amendments will only rearrange the furniture in a burning house.

Miller correctly observes that checks and balances have repeatedly allowed powerful minorities to veto policies serving the public good. But she treats these minorities as essentially pluralistic interest groups—the AMA, the health insurance lobby, Southern Democrats—without fully theorizing why such elites consistently prevail. The answer lies not just in the number of veto points, but in the constitutive logic of a settler-colonial, slaveholding republic. The Constitution was not designed primarily to prevent tyranny of the majority in the abstract; it was designed to protect the property interests of a specific class of white male property holders, including human property. The Senate, the Electoral College, the federal structure, and the Bill of Rights all emerged from compromises that explicitly preserved the power of slave states and propertied elites. The “checks and balances” lauded by Schumer and Clinton are, in their origins, instruments of counter-democratic entrenchment.

This historical foundation is not a mere original sin we have overcome. It persists in the structural advantages that former slave states and their political heirs continue to enjoy through Senate malapportionment, the filibuster, and a federalism that has always been a vehicle for racial domination. Miller acknowledges the role of white Southerners in blocking health care and civil rights legislation, but she frames this as a contingent exploitation of checkpoints, not as the system working as designed. The truth is that the veto points themselves were placed there to protect the interests of a racially defined landed oligarchy, and they have been wielded for that purpose consistently through the centuries—from the gag rule on anti-slavery petitions to the contemporary disenfranchisement of Black voters and the gutting of the Voting Rights Act.

This is not simply a story about elite self-interest broadly defined; it is a story about racial hierarchy as a structuring principle of American capitalism. The failure to pass universal health care, for instance, cannot be fully explained without understanding how racial anxieties have been mobilized to fracture working-class solidarity and sustain a for-profit medical system that exploits both Black and white workers unequally. The New Deal itself was a compromise that excluded agricultural and domestic workers—disproportionately Black—from Social Security and labor protections, a concession to Southern Democrats that embedded racial inequality into the welfare state. Miller’s narrative of the New Deal as a triumph of majoritarian responsiveness glosses over this original sin, thereby reinforcing the myth that we can achieve substantive democracy without dismantling the material and ideological structures of white supremacy.

Similarly, her faith in mass mobilization, while inspiring, risks repeating the errors of past progressive movements that subordinated racial justice to class unity. She praises cross-racial organizing and cites broad public support for equality, but the historical record shows that such coalitions have been fragile and vulnerable to racial backlash. The Populist movement of the late nineteenth century, the labor upsurges of the 1930s, and even the New Deal coalition itself eventually fractured along racial lines. Today, the Democratic Party’s reliance on affluent suburban whites and its reluctance to embrace transformative redistribution are partly driven by the fear of losing that demographic. Any mass politics that does not place racial justice at its core will eventually be captured or crippled by white resentment.

So what would a deeper reckoning look like? It would go beyond constitutional reforms, though some of those may be useful. It would demand a public commitment to reparations, not merely as a redistributive policy but as a process of truth and reconciliation that repudiates the legitimacy of the original constitutional compromises. It would require dismantling the carceral state, which, like federalism, is a direct descendant of the slave patrols and Black Codes. It would involve democratizing not just the Senate or the courts, but the workplace, the housing market, and the healthcare system themselves, breaking the link between racialized capitalism and political power. Miller nods toward “anti-oligarchy” and “equality of the law,” but these phrases ring hollow without a specific plan to abolish the racial wealth gap, end mass incarceration, and establish genuine self-determination for historically oppressed communities.

Miller urges us to reject the checks and balances narrative because it obscures elite power. I agree. But replacing it with a narrative of procedural reform and majoritarian mobilization risks obscuring racial power, the deepest and most durable form of elite domination in American history. The system is not broken; it is working exactly as it was built to work. The task, then, is not to repair it but to build anew—and that requires a frank, painful, and radical break with the racial order that the Constitution enshrined. Until we are ready to have that conversation, any reform agenda will only reproduce the dead end of American democracy.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Constitution's veto points were not abstract safeguards against tyranny but were specifically engineered to protect the property interests of a white, slaveholding elite.
  2. 02Racial hierarchy is the foundational structuring principle of American capitalism, explaining policy failures like the lack of universal health care more deeply than mere elite lobbying.
  3. 03The New Deal and other majoritarian triumphs were compromised from the start by the exclusion of Black workers, proving that majoritarianism alone does not resolve systemic inequality.
  4. 04Mass mobilization and cross-racial organizing are historically fragile and will inevitably fail unless they prioritize the dismantling of racial power over simple procedural or class-based reforms.
  5. 05True democratic progress requires a radical break from the constitutional order through measures like reparations and the dismantling of the carceral state rather than just tinkering with amendments.
