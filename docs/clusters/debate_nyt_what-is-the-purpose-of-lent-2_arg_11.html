"The purpose of Lent is to practice spiritual conversion by relinquishing personal appetites to make room for a deeper dependence on God and a greater commitment to one's neighbor."

At its best, Lent is not a religious version of a New Year’s resolution. A resolution says, “I will make myself better.” Lent says, “I have been made for communion, and I keep settling for less.” The old Christian trio—prayer, fasting and almsgiving—is designed to turn that discovery into practice.

There is a point in giving something up, but only if deprivation becomes attention. Hunger, even the small hunger created by skipping meat or dessert, tells the truth about us: we are needy creatures. We do not live by appetite alone, and our appetites are not always trustworthy guides. The forty days recall Jesus in the wilderness, but they also recall Israel’s wandering: a school in dependence, not a heroic contest in self-mastery.

That is why the thing relinquished matters less than the movement it makes possible. Giving up wine but becoming proud and irritable is a failed fast. Giving up an evening of television in order to pray, visit a lonely neighbor, or reconcile with a relative has begun to look like repentance. The money saved by simpler meals belongs, in the logic of Lent, to the poor. Without that turn outward, fasting easily becomes pious dieting.

Lent also rescues Easter from sentimentality. Christians do not arrive at resurrection by denying death; they pass through ashes, temptation, betrayal and the cross. The season gives believers a language for grief and sin that our culture often lacks. It says that something is wrong not only with “the world” but with me, and that grace is not cheap optimism.

For nonbelievers, some of this may still be intelligible. Voluntary limits can expose involuntary bondage: to screens, sugar, shopping, applause. But the Christian purpose is finally not wellness. It is conversion—being re-ordered toward God and neighbor. The point of giving something up is to make room for receiving something deeper than the thing surrendered.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Lent is a school in dependence rather than a contest of self-mastery, revealing that human beings are needy creatures whose appetites are not always trustworthy guides.
  2. 02The act of giving something up is only meaningful if it facilitates a movement toward prayer, repentance, and acts of service for others.
  3. 03Fasting must be paired with almsgiving to ensure the practice is an outward turn toward the poor rather than a form of pious dieting.
  4. 04The season provides a necessary language for grief and sin, preventing the celebration of Easter from becoming shallow sentimentality or cheap optimism.
  5. 05Lent serves to expose involuntary bondages to modern distractions, re-ordering the individual's life toward spiritual communion rather than mere physical wellness.
