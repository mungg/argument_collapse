"The neurodiversity movement should seek to deepen and enforce liberal rights-based frameworks rather than abandoning them for revolutionary anticapitalism."

Robert Chapman offers an eloquent critique of what they call Liberal Neurodiversity, but I worry that the essay understates the radicalism of liberal rights and overstates the promise of revolutionary language. The neurodiversity movement has indeed been co-opted in some settings. Corporate “neurodiversity hiring” can become a way to extract value from autistic workers without changing much about the workplace. “Superpower” rhetoric can be sentimental and exclusionary. But the answer is not to move beyond liberal reformism so much as to deepen it, enforce it, and connect it to practical institutional change.

The distinction matters because liberal neurodiversity has not merely changed attitudes. It has changed lives. The right to reasonable accommodation, imperfect as it is, gives a disabled worker or student a claim that can be made against an institution. It gives parents leverage in schools. It gives adults a language for disclosure and protection. It makes discrimination legible as a public wrong rather than a private misfortune. These are not small achievements. They are not simply “abstract rights.” They are tools, and like all tools they work better for people with resources, knowledge, and support. The solution is to make the tools more accessible, not to dismiss the toolbox.

Chapman recognizes these gains but treats them as inherently constrained by capitalism. There is truth here. Employers often interpret “reasonable” through cost, convenience, and managerial control. People with more complex needs are frequently excluded. Yet this is not unique to disability rights, nor does it prove that rights-based politics is exhausted. Civil rights law, labor law, environmental law, and consumer protection all operate within market societies, and all are vulnerable to capture. Their failures call for stronger public enforcement, legal aid, unions, funding, and democratic accountability. A right that poor people cannot enforce is not an argument against rights; it is an argument for enforcement.

Take the workplace examples Chapman discusses. Neurodivergent workers are often fired, underemployed, or pushed into burnout. A revolutionary critique can name the system, but a worker also needs concrete remedies: flexible scheduling, remote work, quiet rooms, predictable instructions, written communication, job coaching, protection from harassment, modified performance metrics, and managers trained to distinguish disability-related support needs from misconduct. These reforms may sound modest, but for many people they are the difference between independence and poverty. They can be won through law, human resources policy, union bargaining, and professional norms. A movement that disparages them as merely liberal risks alienating the very people who rely on them.

The same point applies to education. Liberal neurodiversity has encouraged schools to reconsider discipline, sensory environments, communication styles, and the assumption that compliance equals learning. Has it gone far enough? Obviously not. Many children still face restraint, seclusion, suspension, bullying, and low expectations. But the remedy is not an abstract call for Neurodivergent Power; it is better teacher training, smaller classes, universal design for learning, speech and occupational therapy access, inclusive classrooms when appropriate, specialized settings when necessary, and due process for families. These require money and political will. They do not require abandoning liberalism.

Chapman’s essay is most persuasive when criticizing “superpower” discourse. It is demeaning to suggest that neurodivergent people deserve inclusion because they might help a company innovate. Many disabled people cannot work, or cannot work consistently, or do not possess marketable savant-like traits. Human dignity should not depend on productivity. But this critique should lead us toward a broader liberal egalitarianism, not away from it. A decent society provides income support, health care, housing, education, and social participation for those who cannot compete in the market. That is not neoliberalism; it is social citizenship.

Indeed, one ambiguity in Chapman’s argument is that “liberal” sometimes seems to mean neoliberal, individualistic, corporate-friendly, and shallowly representational. But the liberal tradition also includes civil liberties, equal citizenship, pluralism, checks on arbitrary power, freedom of conscience, and protection of minorities from coercive majorities. Neurodivergent people need these principles badly. They need protection from forced normalization, abusive institutions, discriminatory employers, punitive schools, and families or states that would make decisions over their heads. Revolutionary movements have not always been kind to those who move slowly, communicate differently, resist discipline, or require care. A politics centered on collective liberation must still preserve individual rights, dissent, and bodily autonomy.

This is why Chapman’s invocation of the Black Panthers and revolutionary party politics gives me pause. The Black Power tradition contributed enormously to American political thought and community self-defense, and Chapman is right that it linked domestic oppression to capitalism and imperialism. But neurodiversity should be cautious about importing models of militancy that may not fit its constituency. Neurodivergent people are not a single class, race, nation, or community. They differ by diagnosis, severity, culture, income, race, gender, and relation to medicine. Some want pride; some want treatment; some want both. Some experience their condition as identity; others experience it as illness. Some are harmed by psychiatry; others credit medication or hospitalization with saving their lives. A movement organized around a maximal ideological line may silence this pluralism.

The essay’s discussion of Palestine and Aaron Bushnell illustrates both the strengths and risks of Chapman’s approach. It is certainly true that war produces disability, trauma, grief, and psychological suffering on a mass scale. It is also true that states pathologize dissent and that military institutions may exploit neurodivergent people’s skills. A neurodiversity politics should oppose the production of debility through war. But when Chapman suggests that pathologizing Bushnell’s self-immolation undermines the significance of his sacrifice, the argument becomes troubling. One can oppose atrocities in Gaza, respect the sincerity of Bushnell’s protest, and still worry about romanticizing self-destruction. Neurodiversity should resist the weaponization of psychiatric labels against political dissent, but it should not imply that mental anguish is irrelevant when someone dies by suicide in public. A humane politics must hold both truths.

Similarly, the call to free people from psychiatric hospitals must be handled carefully. There is a shameful history of institutional abuse, and many people are confined unnecessarily or coercively. But some people in acute psychosis, mania, catatonia, or suicidal crisis need intensive care. The problem is not that hospitals exist; it is that too often they are underfunded, coercive, carceral, and disconnected from community support. We should expand voluntary, humane, well-resourced alternatives, reduce coercion, and protect rights. But abolishing psychiatric hospitals without adequate replacements would abandon some of the most vulnerable people in the name of liberation.

Chapman is right that people with intellectual disabilities, severe mental illnesses, and complex support needs are often excluded from mainstream neurodiversity imagery. Yet their inclusion requires more than anticapitalist analysis. It requires confronting hard questions about guardianship, consent, communication, family caregiving, public finance, medical decision-making, and long-term support. A non-speaking autistic adult with self-injurious behavior may need round-the-clock assistance. A person with schizophrenia may need housing, medication management, peer support, and crisis care. A child with profound intellectual disability may need specialized education and lifelong services. These needs cannot be met by recognition or revolution alone; they require competent institutions.

The future of neurodiversity should therefore be liberal in the best sense: pluralist, rights-based, empirically minded, and committed to equal citizenship. It should reject the thin corporate version of inclusion while defending enforceable protections. It should welcome pride without mandating pride. It should criticize capitalism where markets exclude and exploit, while also working with employers, schools, clinicians, and governments to make immediate improvements. It should treat disabled people not as symbols of a coming revolution but as citizens with diverse needs and views.

A strengthened liberal neurodiversity agenda would include universal health care, robust disability benefits, accessible housing, supported employment, legal aid for accommodation claims, serious enforcement of antidiscrimination law, an end to abusive restraint and seclusion, better transition services, investment in direct care workers, alternatives to police response in mental health crises, and research guided by disabled people. It would also protect the right to seek treatment, the right to refuse treatment when possible, and the right to live without being reduced to either deficit or gift.

Chapman’s warning about co-optation should be heard. But co-optation is not prevented by adopting more radical slogans; radical slogans can be co-opted too. The harder task is building institutions that deliver. Liberal neurodiversity is unfinished, not obsolete. Its future should be less corporate, more egalitarian, more inclusive of severe disability, and more attentive to class and race. But the language of rights, dignity, accommodation, and pluralism remains indispensable. For many neurodivergent people, liberation begins not with the overthrow of liberalism, but with finally receiving the protections liberal societies have long promised and too rarely supplied.

  6. 06Radical calls for total deinstitutionalization or the romanticization of mental anguish risk abandoning the most vulnerable individuals who require intensive, competent medical support.
  7. 07The most effective way to resist co-optation is not through radical slogans, but by building and improving the institutions that deliver promised protections and services.
