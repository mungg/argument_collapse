"News organizations should move beyond the debate over naming shooters and instead reform the entire media script to treat mass shootings as public health emergencies rather than sensationalized spectacles."

The debate over naming mass shooters is important, but too narrow. The real problem is the entire script through which mass killings are covered: breaking-news panic, endless speculation, repeated images of grieving families, obsessive attention to the killer, political shouting, and then a gradual fade until the next atrocity. Whether the name is spoken ten times or not at all, that script can still produce fear, fame and confusion.

News organizations should treat mass shootings less like spectacle and more like a public health emergency. That begins with disciplined early coverage. In the first hours, many facts are wrong: number of shooters, motives, weapons, identities, even casualty counts. Anchors should say less, not more. “We do not know yet” is better journalism than filling airtime with retired profilers and shaky social media claims.

Second, coverage should be proportional. The killer’s name may be reported, but it should not dominate. His writings should be summarized only when they are genuinely newsworthy. If he sought racial terror, misogynistic revenge, anti-religious violence or some other ideological message, the public should know that. But there is no public interest in broadcasting a manifesto line by line or turning a Facebook page into a personality profile.

Third, the press should broaden the frame. Every mass shooting raises several questions at once: How did the shooter obtain his guns? Were there missed warnings? Did institutions have authority to intervene? What patterns exist among prior attacks? What measures have reduced casualties in other settings? How do survivors recover? Which claims about mental illness are accurate, and which merely stigmatize millions of nonviolent people? These questions are harder than displaying a mug shot, but they are more useful.

Fourth, media outlets should avoid creating a scoreboard. Phrases like “the deadliest since…” may be factually relevant, but they also invite comparison. When repeated constantly, they can imply that mass murder is a competition. The same goes for dramatic music, countdown timelines, childhood-to-killer documentaries and full-screen portraits that make the perpetrator the star of his own production.

Finally, editors should distinguish accountability from amplification. Sometimes naming is necessary. Sometimes a photograph helps establish identity or correct false reports. Sometimes a killer’s ideology must be examined. But repetition is a choice. Placement is a choice. Tone is a choice. The news media cannot control whether disturbed or hateful people imitate violence, and it should not pretend otherwise. But it can reduce the rewards of notoriety while increasing the supply of verified, practical information.

The question is not simply “name or don’t name.” It is whether coverage helps the public understand and prevent violence, or whether it merely performs the killer’s drama on a larger stage.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Early coverage should prioritize disciplined silence and verified facts over the speculation and misinformation that often fill the initial hours of a breaking news event.
  2. 02Reporting on the perpetrator should be proportional, focusing on relevant ideological motives rather than broadcasting manifestos or creating detailed personality profiles.
  3. 03The press should broaden the frame of coverage to address systemic questions regarding gun access, institutional warnings, and survivor recovery rather than focusing on the individual killer.
  4. 04Media outlets must avoid using competitive language or dramatic production elements that frame mass murder as a scoreboard or a competition for notoriety.
  5. 05Editors should distinguish between necessary accountability and unnecessary amplification by making deliberate choices about the repetition, placement, and tone of their reporting.
Diversified Claude

"The news media should focus on dismantling the entire ecosystem of sensationalized spectacle surrounding mass shootings rather than simply debating whether or not to name the perpetrator."

Most of the debate over naming mass shooters treats the question as binary: name them or don't. That framing flatters both sides and clarifies nothing. The real problem with mass-shooting coverage isn't the appearance of a name in a news report — it's the entire ecosystem of attention that American media, social platforms, and audiences have built around these events. Fix only the name, and you've fixed almost nothing.

Think about what saturation coverage of a mass shooting actually looks like in 2015. Within an hour, cable networks have cleared their schedules. Within three, an aerial shot of the scene is on a permanent split-screen. By evening, the killer's social media is being read aloud, his selfies blown up to fill a 60-inch monitor, his motives speculated upon by a rotating cast of pundits with no special knowledge. By the next morning, his face is on the cover of every news magazine, and a thousand Twitter accounts are debating whether he was a terrorist, a lunatic, or a victim. The name is the least of it. The spectacle is the problem.

This is why I find both Sheriff Hanlin's request and the standard journalistic rebuttal somewhat beside the point. Suppressing the name while running everything else changes the wrapper, not the package. What needs to change is the disproportion between the rarity of these events (statistically vanishing compared to ordinary gun homicide, which kills far more Americans and gets a tiny fraction of the coverage) and the cultural oxygen they consume.

A more honest reform agenda would address all of it: shorter coverage cycles, no live-airing of manifestos or "martyrdom" videos, no leaderboards comparing body counts, no breathless ranking of "worst shootings in U.S. history," no photographs of the killer in tactical poses. It would also mean covering the unsexy stories — the daily drumbeat of domestic-violence shootings, the suicides that account for two-thirds of gun deaths, the toddlers who find unsecured weapons — with a fraction of the energy currently lavished on the spectacular outlier.

If we did all that, the question of whether to print the name would answer itself. In a sober, proportionate coverage environment, a name is just a name — a fact among facts. It's only in our current carnival that the name becomes a trophy. The "Don't Name Them" campaign is treating a symptom. The disease is the carnival itself, and journalists, audiences, and platforms all own a piece of it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Suppressing a shooter's name is ineffective if the media continues to provide saturation coverage, including social media analysis and constant speculation.
  2. 02The current media environment creates a disproportionate focus on rare mass shootings while ignoring the more common daily realities of gun violence like domestic incidents and suicides.
  3. 03Meaningful reform requires ending the use of 'leaderboards,' the airing of manifestos, and the publication of stylized photos that glorify the killer.
  4. 04A name only becomes a trophy of notoriety because of the 'carnival' atmosphere created by modern media cycles, social platforms, and audience demand.
