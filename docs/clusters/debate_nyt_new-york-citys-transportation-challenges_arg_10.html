"The next mayor should treat bikeshare expansion and pedestrian safety as complementary goals within an integrated transportation system rather than competing priorities."

The debate over bikeshare versus pedestrian safety misses the point. These are not competing priorities but complementary pieces of the same puzzle: creating a transportation network that actually works for all New Yorkers.

Citi Bike's launch earlier this year demonstrated something remarkable—New Yorkers would ride bikes if given safe, convenient infrastructure. Membership numbers have exceeded projections, and usage patterns show workers using bikes for mid-day errands, not just recreational rides. This matters because every bike trip represents one less crowded subway car or congested taxi. Yet the current system barely covers Manhattan below 59th Street, leaving vast swaths of Brooklyn and Queens entirely unserved.

The next mayor should commit to aggressive expansion into underserved neighborhoods. This means physically adding docking stations, but also ensuring bike lanes follow. The success of Copenhagen and Amsterdam didn't come from choosing between bikes and pedestrians—it came from redesigning streets to serve multiple modes safely. New York can do the same.

Critics who worry about pedestrian safety have a point, but their solution points toward better design, not fewer bikes. Protected bike lanes actually calm traffic and make crosswalks safer. The data from cities with robust cycling infrastructure consistently shows improved pedestrian outcomes alongside increased biking.

What about outer-borough residents facing brutal commute times? A expanded, interconnected bikeshare network won't replace the subway, but it can provide first-and-last-mile connections that make the entire system more functional. Someone in Astoria shouldn't need a car to reach the N train.

The real question isn't bikeshare or pedestrians—it's whether the next mayor will treat transportation as an integrated system or continue the siloed thinking that has left New York behind other global cities.
