"While democracies are vulnerable to information attacks, framing the solution as a security problem risks justifying authoritarian-style controls that undermine the very freedoms and anonymity essential to democratic life."

Farrell and Schneier offer a subtle and valuable warning: democracies are vulnerable not only to censorship but also to strategic flooding, deception, and institutional sabotage. Still, I worry about the vocabulary in which the warning is framed. To describe democracy as an information system with an “attack surface” is illuminating up to a point. Beyond that point it may encourage precisely the wrong instincts: control, authentication, monitoring, and expert management of public discourse.

The liberal defense of free speech has never rested on the belief that speech is harmless. John Stuart Mill and Louis Brandeis were not naïve in the way their caricatures suggest. They knew that majorities could be cruel, newspapers irresponsible, and public opinion irrational. Their answer was not simply “more speech” as an algorithmic law of nature. It was a political ethic: coercive control over expression is usually more dangerous than the speech it suppresses, because the power to decide which speech is dangerous will be captured by governments, parties, and majorities. That insight has not been repealed by Facebook.

The authors are careful to reject crude censorship. They do not call for a ministry of truth. They recognize that democracy depends on disagreement and that many destabilizing challenges to established norms have been emancipatory. Yet the security frame can slide easily from protecting democratic procedures to protecting official consensus. The difference matters. Many of the most important democratic advances began as attacks on “common knowledge.” Abolitionists, suffragists, labor radicals, civil rights activists, antiwar protesters, and gay rights advocates all confronted institutions that claimed legitimacy and expertise. They were frequently accused of spreading dangerous misinformation, undermining trust, or aiding foreign enemies.

One need not romanticize every dissident to see the danger. In the United States, the FBI’s surveillance of civil rights and antiwar movements was justified as defense against subversion. During World War I, the Espionage Act was used against dissenters. During the Cold War, the language of security licensed blacklists and loyalty investigations. More recently, after September 11, 2001, the government’s counterterrorism powers expanded dramatically, often with insufficient oversight. If democracy’s current crisis is described primarily as an information war, we should expect agencies and politicians to seek war powers.

The authors’ examples illustrate both the reality of manipulation and the difficulty of remedy. The fake social media operation in the Alabama Senate race was deceptive and corrosive. The fraudulent FCC comments were an abuse of a public process. Efforts to depress census participation may damage representation. These are real problems. But each involves conduct that can be addressed without building a general regime of speech control. Fraudulent use of stolen identities in agency comments is already a kind of fraud. Election-related deception can be regulated when it concerns the mechanics of voting—false claims about polling places or voting dates, for example—without policing ordinary political exaggeration. Census outreach can be improved through trusted community organizations. The more the law tries to govern broad categories such as “disinformation,” the more it will punish controversial opinion.

Authentication is a case in point. Farrell and Schneier suggest that public commenting may require some combination of authentication, random sampling, and auditing, though they note the cost to anonymity. That cost is not incidental. Anonymous and pseudonymous speech have been central to democratic life, from the Federalist Papers to whistleblowing, labor organizing, dissident religious speech, and participation by vulnerable minorities. In contemporary politics, undocumented immigrants, government employees, survivors of abuse, dissidents within tight-knit communities, and workers afraid of employer retaliation may all need anonymity to speak honestly. A verified public sphere may be cleaner, but it may also be more timid and less equal.

Nor should we assume that identity solves deliberation. Much abuse online is committed by people under their real names. Much propaganda is produced openly by campaigns, interest groups, media outlets, and governments. Conversely, anonymous accounts sometimes reveal truths that powerful institutions would prefer to hide. The problem is not simply that we do not know who is speaking. It is that attention is allocated by opaque systems designed to maximize engagement, outrage, and advertising revenue. A verified lie amplified to millions remains a lie; an anonymous truth buried by algorithms remains buried.

This points to a different emphasis: not speech control, but power control. The Internet of the early 2000s was imagined as decentralized and liberating. The Internet of 2019 is dominated by a few platforms that mediate attention at enormous scale. Facebook, Google, YouTube, and Twitter are not merely neutral conduits. They are privately governed public spheres whose ranking systems, advertising markets, and moderation rules shape political reality. The problem with bots and propagandists is inseparable from the business model that rewards virality, emotional intensity, and microtargeted persuasion.

Yet here too we should be cautious. Demands that platforms “take down disinformation” often outsource censorship to unaccountable companies. A public official who cannot constitutionally ban speech may pressure Facebook to do so. A platform fearful of scandal may over-remove lawful dissent, satire, minority speech, or documentation of human rights abuses. Private moderation at global scale is inevitably error-prone. It also tends to favor those with the resources to complain loudly and navigate appeal processes. The cure for platform power cannot simply be more platform power exercised in the name of safety.

Better approaches would reduce the stakes of any one platform’s decisions. Interoperability, data portability, antitrust enforcement, and limits on surveillance advertising could make the public sphere less centralized and less manipulable. If political advertisers could not target citizens with the precision of commercial behavioral advertising, some of the most troubling forms of manipulation would become less attractive. If users had more control over recommendation systems, and if researchers had privacy-protective access to platform data, democratic publics could better understand how information spreads without requiring government control over content.

Transparency is often proposed as a compromise, and rightly so, but it must be carefully designed. Public archives of political advertisements, including information about sponsors, targeting criteria, and spending, would help journalists and watchdogs detect manipulation. Disclosure requirements for bots can be appropriate when automated accounts impersonate humans. But transparency should focus on powerful actors and paid persuasion, not on forcing every citizen to attach a government-verified identity to every political utterance.

The essay’s analogy to the “Dictator’s Dilemma” is provocative. Autocrats need information to govern but fear that information will help citizens coordinate against them. Democracies need information even more, but openness can be exploited. The analogy is useful precisely because it shows why democracies must not borrow authoritarian tools. Flooding, censorship, surveillance, and identity control may stabilize regimes, but they do so by weakening freedom. A democracy that responds to manipulation by narrowing the space for anonymous dissent, protest, and unruly disagreement may become more orderly while becoming less democratic.

The hardest question is what to do about falsehoods that corrode the factual basis of politics: climate denial, anti-vaccine conspiracies, fabricated rumors about candidates, or claims that elections are rigged without evidence. The temptation is to empower referees. But referees require legitimacy, and legitimacy is exactly what is in short supply. Scientific institutions, courts, election administrators, and professional journalism remain indispensable; they should be defended and improved. But their authority cannot be restored by decree. It must be earned through openness, correction, humility, and independence from partisan and corporate capture.

That means the response to falsehood must be plural. Journalists should avoid amplifying manipulative claims merely to debunk them. Schools should teach civic and media literacy without pretending that every problem is solved by individual skepticism. Platforms should reduce algorithmic amplification of demonstrably fraudulent behavior while preserving broad latitude for political speech. Government should punish fraud, hacking, impersonation, and voter suppression, not heterodox opinion. Civil society should build trusted networks that can communicate across lines of class, race, religion, and party.

The distinction between persuasion and manipulation will never be perfectly clean. Politics has always involved selective emphasis, emotional appeal, coalition signaling, and myth. Campaigns do not resemble graduate seminars. A democracy that expects citizens to deliberate only on the basis of pristine facts will be permanently disappointed. The aim should not be to purify politics of rhetoric but to protect the conditions under which citizens can contest rhetoric freely.

Farrell and Schneier are right that inaction is dangerous. But action guided by panic may be dangerous too. The United States has a habit of responding to security crises with institutions that outlast the crises and powers that migrate to new targets. If we define democracy’s problem as hostile information, we may end by treating citizens as endpoints to be secured rather than as agents of self-government.

Democracy does have vulnerabilities. Some are technical. Some are institutional. But one of its noblest vulnerabilities is that it permits people to be wrong, angry, anonymous, impolite, and distrustful. The task is to defend elections, agencies, and public knowledge without domesticating democratic speech into a managed conversation among verified, respectable participants. A secure democracy that has lost its taste for liberty would be a poor victory.

  1. 01The 'security frame' and the use of 'attack surface' vocabulary can lead to expert management and monitoring of public discourse, which historically has been used to suppress legitimate dissent.
  2. 02Anonymous and pseudonymous speech are not mere vulnerabilities but are vital tools for whistleblowers, marginalized groups, and political dissidents to participate in democracy without fear of retaliation.
  3. 03The focus should shift from controlling speech to controlling the power of centralized platforms through antitrust enforcement, interoperability, and limits on surveillance advertising.
  4. 04Authentication and identity verification do not solve the problem of deliberation, as much propaganda and abuse are carried out by verified actors and amplified by engagement-driven algorithms.
  5. 05Addressing institutional failures like fraudulent agency comments or voter suppression can be achieved through existing laws against fraud and hacking without creating broad new regimes to police 'disinformation.'
  6. 06The legitimacy of democratic institutions and shared facts cannot be restored by decree or expert referees but must be earned through transparency, independence, and pluralistic civil society efforts.
