"While the critique of equal opportunity is correct, the ideal alternative is not equal flourishing but economic democracy, which focuses on dismantling the structural power imbalances and private domination inherent in capitalist institutions."

Christine Sypnowich is right to reject the meritocratic romance of equal opportunity, but the strongest alternative is not best described as equal flourishing. It is economic democracy. The central question for egalitarians is not only whether people have opportunities or even whether they flourish, but who has power over the institutions that govern their lives. In capitalist societies, inequality is reproduced through ownership, workplace authority, investment decisions, housing markets, and political influence. Unless these structures are democratized, talk of opportunity will remain evasive and talk of flourishing will remain aspirational.

Opportunity discourse fits capitalism because it leaves the basic organization of production untouched. It says: let everyone compete fairly for jobs, credentials, promotions, and income. Remove discrimination. Improve schools. Offer training. Perhaps soften bad luck with social insurance. But the firm remains hierarchical, capital remains privately controlled, and workers remain dependent on selling their labor to owners or managers whose decisions they do not meaningfully share. Even if the competition were fair, most people would still spend their lives under private government.

This is the blind spot in many liberal theories of equality. They focus on distribution and selection: who gets what, and by what criteria? But work is not merely a source of income. It is a site of command. Employers determine schedules, tasks, pace, surveillance, dress, speech, and sometimes political expression. They can close plants, outsource jobs, impose algorithmic management, or discipline workers for organizing. In low-wage labor markets, the formal freedom to quit often means little when alternative jobs are equally insecure. A person with “equal opportunity” to become a manager may still live in a society where most people are managed without voice.

Sypnowich’s language of flourishing captures some of this. People cannot flourish when work is degrading, precarious, or meaningless. But flourishing can sound too personal, too centered on the quality of individual lives rather than the structure of collective power. The worker’s complaint is not only “my life is going badly.” It is “I am subject to decisions in which I have no say.” The remedy is not only better outcomes but shared authority.

This perspective changes how we view meritocracy. Sypnowich criticizes the “talent plus effort” model and the moralization of choice. She is right. Meritocracy legitimates inequality by suggesting that hierarchy is acceptable if positions are awarded fairly. But the deeper problem is hierarchy itself. Why should so much income, status, autonomy, and security attach to a relatively small number of positions? Why should democratic citizens accept authoritarian relations for eight or ten hours a day? Why should investment decisions that shape whole regions be made by owners seeking returns rather than by workers and communities bearing the consequences?

A society of economic democracy would still need expertise. Hospitals require trained physicians; bridges require engineers; schools require teachers; complex organizations require coordination. But expertise need not entail domination. Democratic workplaces can delegate authority, elect representatives, create professional standards, and hold managers accountable. Political democracy does not mean every citizen decides every administrative detail; it means ultimate power is publicly answerable. The same principle should apply to economic life.

The labor movement has long understood what opportunity talk obscures. Workers need collective power, not just individual chances. A single worker negotiating with an employer is formally free and substantively weak. A union changes the relationship. Sectoral bargaining changes it further by preventing firms from competing by lowering wages and conditions. Codetermination, works councils, employee ownership, and cooperatives go further still, embedding worker voice in governance. These institutions are egalitarian not only because they raise wages, though they often do. They are egalitarian because they reduce subordination.

The decline of unions in the United States and Britain, and the weakening of collective bargaining across many rich democracies, has tracked rising inequality and political disaffection. This is not accidental. When workers lose organizations, they lose bargaining power, civic education, leadership pipelines, and a collective identity capable of contesting capital. Equal opportunity programs cannot substitute for this. A scholarship helps an individual leave a bad labor market; a union changes the labor market.

Sypnowich emphasizes solidarity, and here economic democracy gives solidarity institutional form. Solidarity cannot be only an attitude of generosity by the lucky rich. It must be built into rules that prevent the lucky rich from having unilateral power in the first place. Progressive taxation and welfare states are necessary, but if they operate after markets generate vast inequalities, egalitarians are always repairing damage. Predistribution—shaping wages, ownership, bargaining power, and corporate governance before income is distributed—is essential.

Consider housing. Opportunity discourse might promote fair lending, anti-discrimination law, and savings programs for first-time buyers. A flourishing approach might emphasize stable homes and beautiful neighborhoods. Both are important. But economic democracy asks who controls land and development. If private owners can hoard land, speculate, exclude multifamily housing, and displace tenants, then housing outcomes will remain unequal and communities powerless. Tenant unions, social housing, community land trusts, public development authorities, and democratic planning are power-shifting institutions. They do not merely give individuals opportunities to rent or buy; they change who governs the housing system.

Consider climate policy. Equal opportunity has little to say beyond green jobs training and fair access to new industries. Equal flourishing rightly notes that environmental degradation damages health, beauty, and security. But economic democracy asks who decides investment, energy production, and transition costs. A just transition cannot be delivered by markets alone or by technocrats alone. Workers and communities dependent on fossil fuel industries need voice in planning, guarantees of income and retraining, and public investment. Otherwise climate policy will be experienced as another elite project imposed on people already abandoned by capital.

Consider technology. Platform workers may be told they enjoy flexibility and entrepreneurial opportunity. In reality, many are governed by opaque algorithms, unstable pay, and unilateral deactivation. Flourishing language can describe the stress and insecurity this creates. Economic democracy demands data rights, algorithmic transparency, collective bargaining for gig workers, and perhaps platform cooperatives. Again, the issue is power.

Sypnowich’s critique of luck egalitarianism also looks different from this angle. The obsession with whether outcomes reflect choice or circumstance is a symptom of a society that treats distribution as a moralized aftermath. First markets allocate rewards and risks; then philosophers ask which losers deserve compensation. Economic democracy asks why the allocation process is organized so undemocratically to begin with. If workers collectively governed firms, if communities shaped investment, if social rights insulated everyone from desperation, the question “was your poverty your fault?” would lose much of its grip.

This does not mean outcomes are irrelevant. They are evidence. Persistent racial wage gaps, gendered care burdens, regional decline, disability poverty, and class immobility reveal failures of power and institution. But egalitarians should be wary of making equal flourishing the direct objective. A workplace might be democratic and well-paid, yet some workers find more meaning in family, religion, art, or leisure than in work. Another person may flourish in a demanding entrepreneurial venture with unequal rewards. The point is not that everyone’s subjective or objective well-being must match. It is that no one should be structurally subordinated or denied the material bases of a decent life.

Economic democracy also helps address Sypnowich’s concern about the state promoting valuable pursuits. If public institutions are to shape values, they must themselves be democratized. A centralized state telling citizens how to flourish is rightly suspect. But a network of democratic schools, workplaces, unions, municipalities, cultural institutions, and cooperatives can support plural forms of life while remaining accountable to participants. The choice is not market influence versus state influence. It is oligarchic influence versus democratic influence.

What would this program include? Universal social rights: health care, education, housing support, child care, disability services, and income security. Strong labor rights: card-check recognition or otherwise easier union formation, sectoral bargaining, protection for strikes, portable benefits, and enforcement of labor standards. Workplace democracy: codetermination, works councils, employee ownership funds, cooperative development, and worker voice in technological change. Democratic finance: public banks, green investment authorities, limits on speculative capital, and community control over development. Wealth democracy: inheritance taxation, capital taxation, baby bonds or social wealth funds, and campaign finance reform. Public goods: transit, libraries, parks, broadband, and arts institutions accessible to all. These policies would promote flourishing, but by changing power rather than supervising lives.

The slogan “equal opportunity” asks whether individuals may climb. “Equal flourishing” asks whether individuals live equally well. “Economic democracy” asks whether the structures that shape our lives are under common control. The third question is indispensable. Without it, opportunity becomes a ladder out of subordination for a few, and flourishing becomes a wish for humane outcomes from inhumane institutions.

Sypnowich ends by saying the egalitarian ideal requires socialism. I agree, if socialism means democracy extended into economic life. But socialism should be defended less as the promise of equal flourishing than as the abolition of private domination over the social conditions of flourishing. People do not need the state to make their lives equally fulfilled. They need, together, to govern the workplaces, resources, technologies, and public goods that make fulfillment possible. Equality begins not with a fair race or an equal happiness score, but with power shared among those who must live with its consequences.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The primary failure of liberal egalitarianism is its focus on distribution and selection rather than the hierarchical command structures of the workplace.
  2. 02Flourishing is an insufficient metric because it centers on individual quality of life rather than the collective authority and voice of workers within the institutions that govern them.
  3. 03Meritocracy is fundamentally flawed not just because it mismeasures talent, but because it attempts to justify the existence of undemocratic social and economic hierarchies.
  4. 04Solidarity should be realized through institutional predistribution—such as unions and collective bargaining—rather than through the post-market generosity of the wealthy.
  5. 05Economic democracy addresses the root causes of inequality by shifting control over land, investment, and technology from private owners to democratic communities.
  6. 06A focus on shared power avoids the paternalism of a state-defined 'good life' by allowing pluralistic forms of flourishing to emerge from democratically governed public goods.
