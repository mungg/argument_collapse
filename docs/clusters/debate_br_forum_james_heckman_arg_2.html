"While James Heckman’s evidence for early childhood intervention is empirically and morally compelling, the focus must now shift from proving its value to the practical challenge of designing and funding high-quality, durable institutions that can replicate those results at scale."

James Heckman’s essay makes the strongest possible case for a proposition that ought to unite people who otherwise disagree about nearly everything in social policy: if we are serious about equal opportunity, we must begin much earlier than kindergarten, much earlier than the ninth-grade dropout crisis, and much earlier than the moment when a young adult arrives at a job-training office with weak skills, weak work habits, and a thin record of trust from employers or teachers. The central achievement of Heckman’s argument is to replace a sentimental slogan—“invest in children”—with a disciplined account of why early childhood investment is not merely compassionate but productive.

His most important point is not simply that preschool “works.” It is that human capacities are cumulative. A child who enters school with language, attention, trust, curiosity, and some capacity to regulate frustration is better able to acquire later academic skills. A child who lacks those foundations will find each subsequent institution harder to navigate. This is why the familiar policy sequence—neglect children early, then spend heavily on remediation, policing, incarceration, welfare, and adult retraining—has always been perverse. It is like refusing to maintain a bridge and then congratulating ourselves on our emergency repair crews.

Heckman is also right to insist that cognitive scores are too narrow a measure of human development. One of the unfortunate legacies of No Child Left Behind has been the narrowing of public discussion to reading and math test scores. Those scores matter, but children are not test-taking machines. The traits that parents and kindergarten teachers recognize immediately—persistence, attentiveness, self-control, confidence, sociability—are not ornamental. They are part of the machinery by which learning occurs. A child who can wait, listen, ask for help, recover from disappointment, and cooperate with others is not merely “better behaved”; she is better equipped to learn.

The evidence from Perry Preschool, Abecedarian, and the Nurse-Family Partnership is therefore enormously important. These programs were not casual babysitting arrangements. They were intensive, relationship-based, and attentive to family life. They did not assume that children can be separated from the environments in which they are raised. That is one reason the home-visiting component matters so much. The most effective early childhood programs do not replace parents; they strengthen the daily interactions through which children are formed.

Still, the very strength of Heckman’s evidence raises a practical difficulty. The programs with the most impressive long-term results were small, carefully staffed, and unusually intensive. The danger is that policymakers will cite Perry and Abecedarian while funding something much thinner: overcrowded preschool classrooms, poorly paid staff, little training, and weak links to parents. If we scale the rhetoric but not the quality, we should not expect the same returns. Early childhood policy is not magic. It is labor-intensive work requiring skilled adults, continuity, trust, and accountability.

That means the next phase of debate must focus less on whether early investment is desirable and more on institutional design. Who will staff these programs? How will they be trained? How will quality be measured without reducing the enterprise to another set of crude test scores? How can home visiting preserve trust rather than becoming surveillance? How will programs be coordinated with pediatricians, child welfare agencies, schools, and community organizations? And how do we prevent early childhood systems from becoming a new two-tier institution, with affluent families purchasing enriching environments privately while poor children receive a bureaucratic substitute?

On targeting, Heckman is persuasive but perhaps too cautious. He is right that the highest returns come from children in the most disadvantaged environments. He is also right that income alone is an imperfect measure of disadvantage. But the politics of narrowly targeted programs are often fragile. Programs “for the poor” tend to become poor programs. They are vulnerable to stigma, budget cuts, and administrative suspicion. A sliding-scale universal system, as Heckman suggests, may be the most promising route. Universal access creates a broad constituency; income-based fees concentrate public subsidy where it is most needed. This is not deadweight loss if it builds a durable institution of high quality.

Nor should early childhood policy be understood as a substitute for everything else. Heckman sometimes writes as though later interventions are mostly disappointing. Many are. But early investment will itself create demand for later opportunity. Children who gain from preschool will still need good elementary schools, safe neighborhoods, decent health care, and real pathways into work. “Skill begets skill” is not an argument for abandoning later institutions; it is an argument for sequencing them intelligently.

The most politically powerful part of Heckman’s essay may be his claim that early investment reduces the usual conflict between equity and efficiency. In much social policy, we argue about whether justice requires some sacrifice of growth. Here the categories come together. A society that wastes the talents of children born into adverse circumstances is both unjust and inefficient. It pays twice: first by allowing human potential to wither, then by bearing the costs of that neglect in crime, illness, dependency, and lost productivity.

But the moral case should not be hidden behind the rate-of-return calculation. The return matters because resources are scarce and policy must be responsible. Yet the deepest reason to act is that birth should not so powerfully govern destiny. We do not choose our parents, our first neighborhood, the stress levels in our homes, or the number of words spoken to us before age three. If those accidents shape our capacities before we can exercise agency, then a decent society has reason to intervene early, carefully, and generously.

Heckman has given us a framework that is empirically serious and morally urgent. The task now is to build institutions worthy of the evidence: not cheap preschool, not symbolic pilot programs, not another testing regime for four-year-olds, but sustained, high-quality support for the relationships and environments in which children become capable adults.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The success of early intervention depends on maintaining the high quality and intensity of small-scale models like Perry Preschool, rather than implementing cheaper, diluted versions.
  2. 02Non-cognitive traits like persistence and self-control are essential components of the learning process and must be prioritized alongside academic skills in program design.
  3. 03Effective programs should focus on strengthening the parent-child relationship through home-visiting and support rather than acting as a bureaucratic substitute for the family.
  4. 04A sliding-scale universal system is preferable to narrow targeting because it builds a broader, more stable political constituency and avoids the stigma often associated with programs for the poor.
  5. 05Early childhood investment should be viewed as a necessary foundation for, rather than a substitute for, continued investment in schools, neighborhoods, and healthcare.
  6. 06The convergence of equity and efficiency in early childhood policy provides a powerful economic justification for intervention, but the ultimate motivation remains the moral necessity of ensuring birth does not dictate destiny.
