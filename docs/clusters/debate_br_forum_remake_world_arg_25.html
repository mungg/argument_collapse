"While Walter Johnson correctly identifies the inextricable link between slavery and capitalism, his analysis requires further refinement regarding whether racial subjugation is a necessary or contingent feature of capitalist development."

Walter Johnson's essay accomplishes something essential: it insists that we cannot understand capitalism without understanding slavery, and that any theory of justice adequate to the modern world must begin from the history of racial subjugation rather than from the abstract universals of liberal philosophy. This is a genuinely important intervention, and it succeeds most powerfully when Johnson draws on the work of W.E.B. Du Bois and Cedric Robinson to articulate what he calls "racial capitalism." Yet the essay also raises questions that deserve further exploration—questions about periodization, about the relationship between Du Bois's analysis and contemporary activism, and about the philosophical stakes of Johnson's critique of "dehumanization."

Johnson's most provocative historical claim is that "there was no such thing as capitalism without slavery." This is a bold assertion that challenges the conventional periodization of economic history. The standard account holds that capitalism emerged in Europe through the growth of markets, the enclosure of common lands, and the rise of wage labor—culminating in the industrial revolution of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries. Slavery, on this view, was at most an incidental prelude: a residue of pre-capitalist social relations that would eventually be swept away by the more efficient logic of free labor. Eric Williams challenged this narrative in *Capitalism and Slavery* (1944), arguing that British industrialization was substantially funded by profits from the Atlantic slave trade. Johnson extends this argument, contending that the entire Atlantic economy—Manchester's mills, Manhattan's counting houses, and Mississippi's plantations—constituted a single integrated system.

The case for integration is strong, but we should be careful about what it establishes. The fact that Manchester's mills depended on cotton grown by enslaved laborers does not necessarily mean that capitalism required slavery for its existence. England's industrial revolution occurred largely without enslaved labor on English soil, suggesting that capitalism could, in principle, develop through wage labor. What slavery may have provided was not the conditions for capitalism's emergence but rather a particular distribution of wealth and power: capital that might otherwise have been invested differently, a white working class that was incorporated into the system through the promise of racial superiority, and a set of commercial relationships that shaped the specific form capitalism took in the Atlantic world.

This distinction matters for how we think about the relationship between racial hierarchy and economic systems. If capitalism could have developed without slavery but would have looked different, then racial capitalism becomes one possible path that capitalism took rather than its necessary historical form. If, however, capitalism was constitutively dependent on racial subjugation—embedded in its very structure from the beginning—then the relationship between capitalism and racism is far deeper than even Williams suggested, and the prospects for a non-racial capitalism become correspondingly dimmer.

Johnson's argument leans toward the second interpretation, and his evidence for it is substantial. The plantation system was not a pre-capitalist anomaly but a fully commercial operation oriented toward global markets, and the enslaved were not merely workers but capital itself—invested, depreciated, and leveraged in sophisticated financial instruments. Yet the very sophistication of these instruments suggests a system that was adapting market logic to the control of labor rather than operating outside it. The plantation, in other words, was capitalism with a racial twist rather than capitalism's negation.

The deeper point, however, is that the question "was slavery capitalist?" may be less useful than Johnson suggests. The more important question is how the various forms of capital-labor relationship in the Atlantic world interacted with one another, and how their interaction shaped the development of the global economy. Manchester and Mississippi were not separate worlds but nodes in a single network, and the flows of capital, commodities, and people among them constitute the essential subject of economic history.

Johnson's most original contribution is his analysis of the concept of "dehumanization" and its ideological functions. The standard view holds that slavery reduced enslaved people to subhuman status, and that the violence of slavery was the violence of dehumanization. Johnson argues, convincingly, that this framing is misleading. Slaveholders did not treat enslaved people as less than human; they treated them as valuable property, recognizing and exploiting their full human capacities for labor, reproduction, and suffering. The violence was not the violence of treating people as nonhuman but the violence of claiming the right to treat human beings as things—as means rather than ends.

This reframing has significant implications for how we think about slavery's legacy. If slavery was about the appropriation of human capacities rather than their destruction, then its contemporary echoes are not merely historical curiosities but live continuities. The worker in the Amazon fulfillment center, the inmate in the private prison, and the undocumented immigrant in the agricultural field are not enslaved in any direct sense, but their labor is extracted under conditions of compulsion, and their humanity is acknowledged only insofar as it can be turned to profit. The plantation and the factory are not so far apart after all.

Johnson's turn to Du Bois and Robinson is thus not merely scholarly but political. The critique of racial capitalism is not only a matter of historical accuracy but of contemporary strategy. If capitalism and racism are constitutively intertwined, then neither colorblind policies nor diversity initiatives will suffice. What is required is the transformation of the system itself—a transformation that Du Bois, in his later work, believed would require not only political action but a fundamental rethinking of the categories through which we understand the world.

Du Bois's concept of the "wages of whiteness" deserves emphasis here. Johnson discusses it in terms of the global economy of the nineteenth century, but its logic extends to the present. The white working class in the United States has never been simply a class; it has been a racial formation, incorporated into the system through the promise of superiority over those below. This promise has always been partially false—the white worker has never received the full share of the system's benefits that was dangled before her—but it has been sufficiently real to divide the working class along racial lines and to prevent the formation of the solidarity that would be necessary for transformation.

The contemporary echo is unmistakable. The white working class in the United States today remains racially defined, even as its economic position erodes. Trump's appeal to white nationalism was not merely ideological; it was the promise that whiteness would continue to provide the compensation that wages alone could not. The Movement for Black Lives stands not only against police violence but against this entire structure of racial compensation—and in doing so, it challenges the foundations of the political order that has governed the United States since the compromise of 1877.

Johnson's essay thus points toward a politics as well as a history. The six virtues of justice rooted in the history of slavery and racial capitalism are not only analytical categories but a program for change. They call for beginning from the standpoint of the Global South rather than the Global North, for attending to the distribution of resources rather than merely the distribution of rights, for demanding reparations rather than synchronic remedies, for centering gender and sexuality, for recognizing the unity of race and class, and for connecting the critique of human instrumentalization to the critique of environmental destruction. This is a radical program, and it deserves serious engagement.

Yet the essay leaves room for questioning. The framework is primarily oriented toward the history of African American experience, and while Johnson acknowledges the importance of connecting it to Native American history, the integration remains incomplete. The dispossession of Indigenous peoples and the enslavement of Africans were not separate processes but interlocking ones, and any adequate theory of racial capitalism must account for both. Similarly, Johnson's engagement with questions of gender and sexuality, while important, could be deepened. The sexual violation and reproductive control of enslaved women were not only elements of slavery's violence but crucial to the reproduction of the system itself—and their legacy continues to shape the lives of Black women today.

These are, however, suggestions for further development rather than fundamental objections. Johnson's essay is a major contribution to the literature on slavery, capitalism, and justice, and it deserves to be read carefully by anyone interested in the history of the modern world and its possible futures.

  3. 03Johnson’s critique of 'dehumanization' successfully reframes slavery as the violent appropriation of human capacities rather than their destruction, highlighting continuities in modern labor exploitation.
