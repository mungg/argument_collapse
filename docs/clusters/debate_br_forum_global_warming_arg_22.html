## Beyond the Science: What Policy Makers Should Learn

Kerry Emanuel has provided a clear and accurate summary of climate science as it stood in early 2007. The facts he presents are not in dispute: CO2 concentrations are rising, temperatures are increasing, ice is melting, sea level is rising, and extreme weather events are likely to become more common. What is less clear from his essay is what follows from these facts—what actions policy makers should take and how they should think about the choices before them. That is the gap I want to address.

The first thing to recognize is that the climate change problem is fundamentally an economics problem. The atmosphere is a common resource, and using it to absorb carbon is an externality—a cost imposed on others that is not reflected in the market price of carbon-intensive goods. Left to their own devices, individuals and firms will emit too much carbon because they bear only a fraction of the costs. The solution is to put a price on carbon that reflects the full social cost of emissions. This can be done through a carbon tax or through a cap-and-trade system. Both approaches have advocates and critics, but both internalize the externality in a way that market forces can then respond to.

A carbon price is not sufficient by itself, but it is necessary. Without a price signal, there is no incentive for businesses to invest in lower-carbon technologies, for consumers to choose efficiency, or for entrepreneurs to develop new solutions. We have seen this played out in the electricity sector: when natural gas prices fell in the 1990s, utilities switched from coal because it was economically rational to do so. Market forces responded to price signals. The same would happen with carbon if the price were right.

The challenge, of course, is political. A carbon tax is regressive—it hits the poor harder than the rich because lower-income households spend a higher proportion of their income on energy and transportation. This political economy problem has blocked action for decades. The solution is to return the revenue from a carbon tax as a per-capita dividend, ensuring that low-income households come out ahead. This approach—popularized by economists like James Hansen and Greg Mankiw—would reduce emissions while protecting vulnerable households. It would also build political support for carbon pricing by making it feel like a dividend rather than a tax.

Beyond carbon pricing, government has a role to play in funding basic research and development. The private sector underinvests in energy research because the benefits of successful innovation are widely shared and cannot be fully captured by the innovator. We would not expect the market to develop adequate levels of research on nuclear fusion, advanced solar cells, or carbon capture technologies without public support. The Department of Energy's research programs, the national laboratories, and university research all play essential roles in developing the technologies that will power our future economy.

I am sympathetic to those who argue that international agreements are essential—that climate change is a global problem requiring global solutions. But international negotiations have been slow and will remain so as long as major emitters like the United States and China have incentives to free-ride on others' efforts. The better approach is to lead by example: adopt carbon pricing at home, demonstrate that it is compatible with economic growth, and negotiate agreements from a position of strength rather than hypocrisy. The United States cannot credibly demand sacrifices from developing nations while refusing to take action itself.

Emanuel's essay mentions that media coverage of climate change has been obsessed with sensational debate. I would extend this critique to much of the policy debate as well. We have spent too much time arguing about whether climate change is real (it is) and whether it is caused by humans (it is) and not enough time developing the mechanisms that will actually reduce emissions. The science is largely settled; the policy is not. This is where scholars and commentators should focus their efforts.

One area that deserves more attention is the potential for technological breakthroughs. The same forces that produced the exponential improvement in computing power—the combination of venture capital, public markets, and government research—could work in energy technology. The venture capital industry has already begun to invest in clean energy startups. Regulatory frameworks that allow these investments to flourish will be crucial. We need to reform permitting for transmission lines and nuclear plants, streamline approval processes for new technologies, and create markets for clean energy that do not distort the price signals.

Finally, we should not forget adaptation. Even if we stopped emitting carbon today—which we will not—the atmosphere will continue to warm for decades due to the long lifetime of CO2 already emitted. We need to invest in resilience: seawalls and flood control infrastructure, drought-resistant crops and irrigation systems, early warning systems for extreme weather. Adaptation does not excuse inaction on mitigation, but it is a necessary complement to it.

The science tells us what is happening. Now we need the policies to address it. A carbon price, public investment in research, regulatory reform, and adaptation funding—this is an ambitious but achievable agenda. What we lack is not knowledge but political will.
