"While randomized controlled trials provide valuable data, the authors' push for universal behavioral laws overlooks the fact that cultural context and local meaning are the primary drivers of human decision-making."

Rachel Glennerster and Michael Kremer’s essay, "Small Changes, Big Results," is a clarion call for a new kind of development economics—one grounded in the rigorous evaluation of human behavior rather than abstract, perfectly rational models. The authors compellingly demonstrate that context-specific interventions, tailored to the actual psychological and behavioral constraints of the poor, can produce remarkable improvements in health and education. Yet, beneath their celebration of randomized controlled trials (RCTs) lies a fascinating tension: the authors are simultaneously advocating for a deeply contextualized understanding of human behavior while seeking universal, generalizable laws of economics. From an anthropological perspective, their attempt to extract universal behavioral patterns from highly specific cultural contexts is scientifically precarious and potentially misleads policy.

The authors ask, "can we generalize about human behavior across countries?" They acknowledge that this is an empirical question, but quickly lean toward the affirmative. They draw parallels between American students procrastinating on papers and Kenyan farmers procrastinating on investments. They equate the behavior of Swiss nurses with the poor in the developing world regarding convenience. They assert that "despite our striking surface differences, there are strong similarities in how people make decisions." This conclusion is essential for the RCT movement; if results from Kenya do not generalize to Peru or Indonesia, the entire enterprise becomes impossibly expensive and localized.

However, by focusing solely on the behavioral *outcome* (e.g., did they take the vaccine or not?), the economic model strips away the culturally embedded *meaning* of the action. An economist sees a mother accepting a kilogram of lentils and a set of plates in exchange for immunizing her child and concludes: "Small upfront incentives overcome procrastination." But is it simply procrastination being overcome?

To an anthropologist or a qualitative sociologist, that interaction is thick with local meaning. In rural Rajasthan, where the caste system heavily dictates social interaction, who is handing out the lentils? Are the plates seen as a status symbol? Does the act of receiving food from a government or NGO worker fit into a localized understanding of patronage or religious charity? If the mother took the vaccine not because of a universal human calculation of cost-benefit, but because the specific form of the incentive activated a highly localized cultural norm of reciprocity, then the intervention is *not* generalizable. If you take that exact same incentive structure to a community in West Africa with entirely different norms regarding food sharing, caste, and state authority, it may fail completely.

This is the blind spot of the experimental approach. RCTs treat human communities as black boxes. You input a stimulus (a conditional cash transfer, a free uniform, a chlorine dispenser) and measure the output (enrollment, diarrhea incidence). The RCT tells you definitively *that* the intervention worked, but it is notoriously poor at explaining *why* it worked. Without understanding the "why"—the deeply cultural, qualitative mechanisms driving the behavior—claiming that the result will hold widely across different continents is an act of faith, not science.

Furthermore, the behavioral economist’s definition of "irrationality" or "sub-optimal" behavior is heavily influenced by Western, middle-class norms. The authors suggest that the poor fail to make "even small upfront investments in order to obtain a steady stream of modest future benefits." But in an environment characterized by extreme volatility—where crops can fail, where political violence can erupt, where hyperinflation can wipe out savings—prioritizing immediate consumption or immediate liquidity is not necessarily a cognitive failure or procrastination. It can be a highly rational risk-mitigation strategy. The poor may understand perfectly well that investing tomorrow looks preferable, precisely because tomorrow is deeply uncertain. When external researchers label these survival strategies as "deviations from the rational agent model," they impose an artificial framework that fails to respect the logic of the local environment.

The authors do offer a caveat, noting that "details of institutional circumstance can matter a lot" and that they have not done enough rigorous studies to assert that patterns are "truly universal." But the overall thrust of the essay, up to its concluding quote from a children's book ("How can we feel so different and be so much alike?"), pushes heavily toward universalism.

If development policy in the coming decade relies exclusively on the quantitative outputs of RCTs without integrating deep, qualitative, ethnographic research, it will eventually hit a wall. We will scale up "proven" interventions only to find they mysteriously fail in new contexts, because we copied the mechanics of the intervention without understanding its cultural circuitry. To truly achieve the "big results" the authors promise, development economics must broaden its lens. It must stop treating culture as mere "surface differences" or statistical noise to be randomized away, and start recognizing that context is, in fact, the very substance of human decision-making.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Focusing solely on behavioral outcomes strips away the culturally embedded meanings of actions, making it scientifically precarious to assume that an incentive's success in one culture will translate to another.
  2. 02Randomized controlled trials treat communities as 'black boxes' that measure what works without explaining the qualitative 'why,' leading to a misplaced faith in the generalizability of results.
  3. 03What behavioral economists label as 'irrational' procrastination or sub-optimal decision-making may actually be a rational risk-mitigation strategy for people living in volatile and uncertain environments.
  4. 04Development policy will ultimately fail if it scales interventions based on quantitative mechanics while ignoring the cultural circuitry and ethnographic realities of the target populations.
