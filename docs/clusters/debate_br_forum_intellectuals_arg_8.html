"The responding essay argues that abandoning the commitment to objective truth-telling in favor of partisan 'organic intellectualism' is a dangerous mistake that cedes the very ground necessary to defeat fascism."

Robin D. G. Kelley’s essay is a rich and morally urgent chronicle, but its central intellectual move—the dismissal of truth-telling as an antiquated and inadequate response to fascism—is a profoundly dangerous misstep. In casting Noam Chomsky’s injunction to “speak the truth and expose lies” as “incomplete” because “whatever power truth-telling might have had has been radically diminished,” Kelley not only misreads the history of antifascism but inadvertently cedes the very ground on which a defense of intellectual integrity must stand. The challenge of our moment is not to abandon the universalist commitment to reason and evidence in favor of partisan organic intellectualism, but to recognize that the attack on truth is itself the primary weapon of the fascist right, and that the left’s most powerful countermeasure is a radical recommitment to disciplined, autonomous inquiry that refuses both state propaganda and movement dogma.

Kelley is right to note that the political landscape has shifted since 1967. We live in an era of “post-truth,” where disinformation campaigns, state-sponsored propaganda, and the algorithmic amplification of outrage have degraded public discourse. But that stark reality makes truth-telling more, not less, essential. The fascist movements Kelley rightly identifies—from the Heritage Foundation’s Project Esther to the architects of the MAGA coalition—derive their power precisely from the ability to construct alternative realities sealed off from empirical contestation. When Christopher Rufo wages war on critical race theory by deliberately distorting its meaning, when Project 2025 authors label all critics of Israel as “Hamas supporters” without evidence, when Trump and his allies insist the 2020 election was stolen despite dozens of court rulings to the contrary, they are not merely expressing a different class standpoint. They are engaged in a systematic assault on the very possibility of shared factual reference. The strategic response to such an assault cannot be to shrug and say, “Well, truth-telling is insufficient; let’s choose a side and fight.” That is precisely what the right wants: a political landscape reduced to clashing tribal narratives where power alone decides outcomes.

The tragedy is that Kelley’s own historical examples undermine his argument. The Black antifascist tradition he celebrates was, at its best, profoundly committed to empirical documentation and rigorous argumentation. When W. E. B. Du Bois wrote *Black Reconstruction in America*, he did not simply produce a counter-myth; he marshaled an overwhelming archive of primary sources to demolish the Dunning School’s racist historiography. When *We Charge Genocide* was submitted to the United Nations in 1951, its authors understood that the power of the petition lay in its meticulous documentation—names, dates, medical reports, police records—that made the charge of genocide legally and morally unavoidable, despite the State Department’s attempts to dismiss it. St. Clair Drake and Horace Cayton’s *Black Metropolis* gained its enduring influence not because it was a propaganda tract for a political tendency but because it combined rigorous social science with an openly stated commitment to racial justice, refusing to subordinate the former to the latter. In each case, truth-telling was not a naive supplement to political struggle; it was the very condition of the struggle’s credibility and ultimate influence.

Kelley’s turn to Gramscian organic intellectuals risks collapsing the vital distinction between being *situated* in social relations and being *reducible* to them. Gramsci himself, even as he insisted that all knowledge is embedded in class position, developed a theory of hegemony that relied on the ability of counter-hegemonic intellectuals to produce knowledge that could win over subordinate groups through persuasion, not mere assertion of interest. The fascist right may indeed have its own organic intellectuals, as Kelley points out, but those intellectuals achieve influence precisely because they present their ideology as truth—by fabricating studies, manipulating data, and exploiting the appearance of scholarly credibility. The left’s answer to that cannot be to mimic their instrumentalism, because the left, if it is to remain faithful to its emancipatory promise, cannot survive on a diet of strategic falsehoods. Once intellectuals decide that truth is whatever serves the “insurgent class,” they lose the ability to criticize their own side’s errors, to distinguish between just and unjust tactics, and to hold movements accountable to the very values they claim to fight for.

The argument that truth-telling feels impotent in the face of tanks and deportations is emotionally understandable but politically shallow. The abolitionist movement did not end slavery by “choosing a side” and abandoning reasoned argument; it built a moral consensus over decades through speeches, pamphlets, slave narratives, and newspaper exposés that made the violence of the slave system visible and indefensible. The civil rights movement won landmark legislation not just through direct action but through a carefully orchestrated strategy of media visibility that exposed the brutality of Jim Crow to a national audience. Even in the darkest hours of the 1930s, the antifascist intellectuals Kelley cites—Langston Hughes, Claudia Jones, C. L. R. James—produced works of literature, history, and journalism that sought to persuade, to document, and to bear witness. They did not think of truth as a luxury; they knew that the oppressed need truth as a shield and a sword, even when it seems hopeless.

Today, the assault on truth is not confined to the right. Kelley’s essay acknowledges that liberals themselves bear responsibility for catastrophe by citing former State Department spokesman Matthew Miller’s admission that he had defended Israel’s war crimes while knowing the truth. That is an indictment of institutional hypocrisy, but it does not invalidate the imperative of truth-telling; it merely shows that state intellectuals lie because they are paid to lie. The lesson is not that truth is futile but that we need more intellectuals willing to break with power and speak honestly, not fewer. The collapse of trust in liberal institutions—including universities—is partly due to decades of such hypocrisy, in which academics and experts tailored their findings to the demands of funders and political patrons. The answer is to restore faith in the possibility of honest inquiry, not to jettison it entirely in favor of open partisanship.

There is a deeper peril in Kelley’s framework. By framing the university as merely “contested terrain” and the intellectual’s duty as choosing the right side in a class war, the essay implicitly licenses a kind of epistemological tribalism that corrodes the very possibility of democratic deliberation. If all knowledge claims are ultimately expressions of group interest, then there is no common language in which the pro-Palestine student and the conservative evangelical can engage, no shared criteria of evidence by which the truth of genocide allegations can be adjudicated. That may sound like a radical challenge to power, but in practice it hands the powerful an extraordinary gift: it allows them to dismiss any unwelcome fact as a product of partisan bias and to construct their own alternative “facts” with impunity. This is the logic that has enabled the Trump administration to gut climate science, to rewrite the history of the Civil War, and to slander anti-Zionist activists as terrorists—not by defeating truth with reason, but by asserting that objective truth does not exist and that all that matters is the will to power.

The responsibility of intellectuals in the age of fascism and genocide must therefore include a fierce and unapologetic defense of the very Enlightenment values that the right cynically invokes while the left too often scorns. Freedom of speech, academic autonomy, the commitment to evidence-based argument, the separation of scholarship from state and corporate interests—these are not bourgeois illusions. They are fragile historical achievements, won through centuries of struggle against monarchical, ecclesiastical, and plutocratic power, and they are precisely what the fascist right seeks to destroy. Defending them does not mean pretending the university is an ivory tower free of class bias; it means fighting to make the university live up to its professed ideals, while simultaneously building alternative institutions that can model intellectual integrity more fully.

Kelley’s moving conclusion—the image of Tiffany Willoughby-Herard being handcuffed while crying out for her students’ futures—is not a refutation of truth-telling but its highest form. She did not merely act as an organic intellectual expressing class solidarity; she bore witness, in front of cameras, to the reality of police violence against peaceful protesters and the moral bankruptcy of a chancellor who would sacrifice education for repression. Her words became a truth that could circulate, that could move others to action, that could be entered into the historical record against the lies of the administration. That is exactly the kind of truth-telling Chomsky championed in 1967 and that remains our most potent weapon: not abstract moralizing, but the courageous exposure of concrete crimes, in real time, by those who put their bodies on the line. Kelley’s essay, despite itself, demonstrates that truth still matters. Our task is to amplify it, protect those who speak it, and insist that the right’s house of lies can and must be shattered by the force of evidence and reason, joined with the power of organized resistance. Abandoning that conviction is not a path to antifascist victory; it is the surest route to moral and political defeat.
