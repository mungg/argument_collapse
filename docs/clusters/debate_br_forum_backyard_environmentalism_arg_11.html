"Effective environmental regulation must move beyond end-of-pipe mandates to a performance-based 'rolling-rule' model that integrates environmental stewardship directly into production processes through local operational intelligence and rigorous public accountability."

I read the lead essay less as a theory of democracy than as a description of what many people inside regulated firms have already learned the hard way: environmental performance cannot be bolted onto production at the end of the pipe. It must be built into the way work is organized. On that point, Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen are exactly right. The best environmental regulation is increasingly the regulation that makes companies look carefully at their own processes, compare themselves with others, and involve the people who actually know how the plant runs.

For years, many environmental rules treated firms as if they were machines that could be adjusted by remote control. A permit limit was set; a scrubber, separator, or treatment unit was installed; reports were filed. This approach achieved real improvements and should not be dismissed. But it also encouraged a damaging separation between “production” and “environment.” Production managers made the product. Environmental managers handled the waste. Workers on the line were told to keep output moving. Regulators inspected the paperwork and the discharge point. Everyone played a role, but too few people asked whether the waste had to be created in the first place.

Toxics-use reduction changes that question. Massachusetts’s TURA is important because it forces firms to examine chemical use throughout the production process. In many facilities, the people who can identify practical improvements are not senior executives or outside lawyers. They are operators, maintenance staff, engineers, purchasing managers, and supervisors who know where solvent evaporates, where batches fail, where leaks recur, where materials are over-specified, and where cleaning practices are inherited rather than necessary. A planning requirement can create permission to ask these questions.

The lead essay reports that production-adjusted use of toxic chemicals in Massachusetts fell by 20 percent from 1990 to 1995, while toxic byproducts fell by 30 percent. Those numbers matter, but so does the institutional explanation. Firms often discover that pollution prevention saves money because waste is wasted input. It costs money to buy, store, handle, insure, treat, ship, and report hazardous materials. Reducing use can improve quality and safety. When regulations induce companies to identify such savings, they create a rare alignment between environmental and business goals.

This is not to say industry should regulate itself. Responsible Care shows why a purely private system is insufficient. Trade associations are good at circulating information and creating professional norms; they are poor at disciplining members when discipline threatens commercial interests. A company that invests seriously in pollution prevention has reason to resent a competitor that signs the same code but does little. Without public reporting and credible sanctions, voluntary programs risk rewarding talk over performance.

The solution is not to abandon collaboration but to make it accountable. Public agencies should set minimum requirements, require reporting, audit data, and enforce against laggards. At the same time, they should avoid prescribing detailed technologies when performance can be measured. Plant managers need flexibility because facilities differ enormously. A rule that makes sense for a large continuous chemical process may be absurd for a small batch operation. A technology standard can freeze yesterday’s solution and discourage better ones. Performance targets, planning duties, and public benchmarking can be more effective if they leave room for operational intelligence.

The authors’ “rolling-rule” model captures this dynamic. Local units experiment, report results, and learn from comparison. Central authorities pool information, identify benchmarks, and raise expectations over time. This is close to how serious companies already improve quality and safety. They collect data, investigate deviations, compare plants, circulate best practices, and revise procedures. Environmental regulation that taps those habits will work better than regulation that treats firms only as adversaries.

But the analogy to corporate learning also reveals a danger. Continuous improvement works only when goals are clear and leaders are committed. In a company, if top management treats quality as a slogan, the system decays into paperwork. The same is true in environmental regulation. Planning documents can become shelf documents. Metrics can be selected because they are easy, not because they matter. Benchmarking can become a public-relations exercise. Audits can be staged. Employees can learn that raising environmental concerns is unwelcome. For rolling rules to work, public authorities must verify that learning is real.

Workers should be central to that verification. The lead essay speaks of citizens as deep users, but in industrial environmental protection workers are also deep users. They are exposed first and often most intensely. They see routine deviations that never become reportable incidents. They know when a waste-minimization project has been quietly abandoned. They understand the practical consequences of substituting one chemical for another. A toxics-use plan that excludes workers is missing indispensable knowledge.

This point has implications for regulatory design. Agencies should encourage or require worker participation in facility environmental planning, especially where toxic materials are used. Whistleblower protections must be strong. Unions, where present, should be part of planning and monitoring. Training should not be limited to certified planners and managers. If a firm claims that pollution prevention is integrated into production, the workforce should be able to confirm it.

Small firms deserve special attention. Large companies often have environmental departments, engineers, and access to consultants. Small manufacturers may not know how to identify alternatives or navigate reporting. If regulation simply imposes planning duties, it may produce resentment and poor paperwork. Massachusetts’s Office of Technical Assistance and Toxics Use Reduction Institute are therefore crucial. They show that public authority can be helpful without being lax. Technical assistance is not a subsidy to polluters; it is a way to achieve public goals efficiently.

The lead essay’s broader examples—Chesapeake Bay and HCPs—are farther from the factory floor, but the same lesson applies. People closest to the work often know things central planners miss. Farmers know field conditions. Developers know site constraints. Local conservationists know seasonal patterns. Scientists know ecological relationships. A good process disciplines all these forms of knowledge through monitoring and comparison. A bad process lets each party repeat its preferred story.

Business has reason to support the good version. Regulatory certainty matters, but certainty does not always require fixed technologies. It can also come from clear procedures, transparent benchmarks, and fair treatment across competitors. Companies can live with ambitious environmental goals if they know the rules will be applied consistently and if they have flexibility in meeting them. What they cannot easily manage is arbitrary enforcement, shifting political demands, or local negotiations that reward the most obstructionist participant.

That is why the national and state role remains indispensable. Central agencies should not micromanage, but they must maintain comparability. If one facility invests in genuine reduction while another manipulates estimates, benchmarking fails. If one HCP is rigorously monitored while another is a sweetheart deal, public trust collapses. If one watershed asks farms to make costly changes while neighboring watersheds do nothing, resentment grows. Rolling rules require a center strong enough to prevent free riding.

The lead essay is right to reject the false choice between command and voluntarism. Many in industry have complained about command regulation, but the smarter complaint is not that environmental protection is illegitimate. It is that some rules compel spending without encouraging thought. Performance-based regulation, public disclosure, technical assistance, worker involvement, and enforceable floors can produce better results. They can also reduce the cultural distance between regulators and regulated firms. When both sides focus on measurable improvement, conversation becomes possible.

Still, the public should not be asked simply to trust companies. Trust is earned through data, independent review, and consequences for failure. The best firms should welcome that. A transparent rolling-rule regime can distinguish genuine environmental leaders from companies that hide behind minimum compliance. It can reward innovation and expose evasion. If designed well, it will not be anti-business or pro-business. It will be pro-performance.
