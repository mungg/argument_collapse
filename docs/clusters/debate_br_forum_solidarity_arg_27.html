"While Mie Inouye’s concept of 'social endurance' correctly identifies the need for disciplined togetherness on the left, her proposal fails because it attempts to strip the practice of showing up from the thick theological or strategic contexts that make such endurance meaningful and effective."

Mie Inouye’s “Solidarity Now” is at its most compelling when it draws on her Mormon upbringing to illustrate a mode of togetherness that is neither based on shared material interest nor on abstract moral principle, but on a stubborn practice of showing up. The church, with its three-hour Sunday meetings, youth groups, and early-morning scripture study, taught her “social endurance”—the capacity to stay in a room with people she mostly did not like and allow that friction to transform her. This is a provocative proposal, and it chimes with a growing recognition on the left that something like ritual, discipline, and shared practice might be necessary to hold a fractious coalition together. But I think Inouye misreads the lessons of Mormonism. The endurance she learned was embedded in a thick institutional and theological context that gave it meaning and limit. Without that context, simply telling secular leftists to “keep showing up” risks becoming an empty exhortation—or worse, a recipe for burnout.

What made Mormon endurance possible, for Inouye, was not just the meetings but the entire cosmology of eternal families, the sense of a divine plan, and a hierarchical authority that could adjudicate conflicts. Inouye later in her essay critiques those very elements—rigidity, hierarchy, exclusion—as forms of “antisocial solidarity.” She wants to preserve the endurance while discarding the reactionary scaffolding. But can that really be done? I am not a Mormon, nor a religious believer in any traditional sense, but I have long observed that secular movements that try to adopt religious forms of community without theology often descend into either a cult of personality or a diffuse therapeutic culture. The discipline of endurance requires a reason to endure that transcends personal satisfaction. For the religious, that reason is God or salvation; for the revolutionary, it might be the vision of a radically different world. Inouye gestures toward the latter in her discussion of ideology as a shared vision, but she doesn’t flesh it out. Instead, her practical emphasis is on the process itself: showing up as the end, not the means.

This process-orientation is deeply seductive in a time when the left has lost much of its strategic direction. After the failure of the Bernie Sanders campaigns to capture state power, after the fragmentation of the 2020 uprisings, many activists have retreated to local mutual aid, book clubs, and “transformative justice” circles. Inouye’s essay gives a theoretical gloss to this turn: keep meeting, keep talking, and maybe something will happen. But left history is littered with the bones of groups that met beautifully for years and never built power. The labor movement didn’t win the eight-hour day because workers patiently endured each other’s company; they won because they organized to strike and shut down production. Endurance was a consequence of shared risk and struggle, not a precondition.

Inouye’s reading of Bob Lee’s organizing is again revealing. She focuses on the physicality, the insistence that people speak—the interpersonal techniques. But Lee’s power came from the fact that he represented a political organization that had already demonstrated its willingness to confront the state with armed self-defense and free breakfast programs. The young white people in the room were not just intrigued by Lee’s charisma; they were attracted to the Panthers’ revolutionary potential. If Lee had been just a nice guy asking what they wanted, they might have shared and gone home. The substance of the politics created the context for endurance. Today, we lack that substance. The left offers critique but few compelling visions of a just future that speak to the concrete conditions of working people across race.

Inouye’s concept of “epistemic humility” is another thin replacement. She wants us to hold our beliefs open to challenge, which is fine as far as it goes. But epistemic humility, untethered from a commitment to specific truths—about capitalism, racism, imperialism—can easily slide into a relativism that paralyzes action. If we are all just sharing our limited perspectives, on what basis do we make a decision? At some point, a coalition must decide to fight for a particular policy, or to adopt a particular tactic, and that requires closing down some options and asserting a collective judgment. That process is inherently political, not just procedural, and it will inevitably produce losers and winners within the coalition. Inouye acknowledges the need for conflict, but her framework offers little guidance on how to resolve it beyond “keep meeting.”

I also want to question the unspoken assumption that the Mormons are a model of widespread social endurance. Inouye’s church experience was in the Boston area, where she was a religious and racial minority; that is a specific, often defensive form of endurance. For a white Mormon in Utah, endurance can look more like complacency in the face of a monoculture. The endurance Inouye learned was partly the endurance of a marginal position within a dominant institution. Transferring that to a left coalition where PMC white people might be the dominant group, the dynamic is different. Asking Black and working-class organizers to endure the condescension of highly educated white comrades is not the same as a Japanese American kid in a mostly white Boston Mormon ward learning to get along. Power matters.

So what would a thicker, more substantial solidarity look like? I would argue for a reconstruction of the left as a counter-public with its own distinctive rituals, but those rituals must be tied to a real, ongoing struggle. The movement needs clear political education that teaches not just interpersonal skills but historical analysis. It needs democratic structures that allow for majority decisions without pretending everyone must agree. And it needs to recognize that sometimes endurance is not a virtue but a trap; sometimes leaving and starting something new is the most responsible act. Inouye’s essay is a compassionate plea for patience. I would add that patience without purpose is just waiting, and the world cannot wait.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The social endurance found in Mormonism is inseparable from its hierarchical authority and specific cosmology, making it difficult to replicate in secular, horizontalist political spaces.
  2. 02Prioritizing the process of meeting over strategic goals risks turning organizing into a diffuse therapeutic culture that fails to build actual political power.
  3. 03Endurance in successful historical movements, such as the Black Panthers or the labor movement, was a byproduct of shared risk and revolutionary substance rather than a prerequisite interpersonal skill.
  4. 04The concept of 'epistemic humility' lacks the necessary commitment to objective political truths required to move a coalition from sharing perspectives to taking decisive collective action.
  5. 05Inouye’s framework overlooks how power dynamics and social location change the ethical weight of endurance, as asking marginalized groups to endure the presence of dominant groups is not the same as a minority group's internal cohesion.
