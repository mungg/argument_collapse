Diversified Gemini

"While Chapman’s critique of 'Liberal Neurodiversity' is valid, their proposed 'Neurodivergent Power' framework risks harming the most vulnerable by dangerously collapsing distinct clinical and biological realities into a purely anti-capitalist political identity."

Robert Chapman’s “The Future of Neurodiversity” rightly points out the severe limitations of what they call “Liberal Neurodiversity.” The corporatization of neurodivergence, the extraction of specific “superpowers” for tech-industry profit, and the stark reality that civil rights legislation does little for those locked in prisons or state hospitals are all crucial critiques. However, as we examine Chapman’s proposed solution—a unified “Neurodivergent Power” explicitly modeled on revolutionary Marxist frameworks and the Black Panther Party—we must ask whether this political vision fundamentally misinterprets the nature of severe psychiatric and neurological realities. By attempting to fold all cognitive and emotional differences into a single, anti-capitalist political identity, Chapman risks romanticizing severe mental illness and erasing the profound biological and clinical realities that demand medical, not just political, intervention.

The foundational premise of the neurodiversity movement, originating in the autism community, was that certain neurological differences are natural variations of the human genome, not diseases to be cured. For many autistics, people with ADHD, or those with dyslexia, this framework has been emancipatory. However, as the umbrella of “neurodivergent” has expanded to include psychiatric conditions like schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and severe clinical depression, the socio-political model begins to fracture under the weight of clinical reality.

Chapman writes about individuals prone to psychosis who might fear government spying and need their webcams covered as a “reasonable adjustment.” This example is profoundly telling. Accommodating a delusion in the workplace is not merely a matter of resisting “neuronormativity” or capitalist productivity demands. It touches on the agonizing reality of severe psychiatric illness. For someone experiencing active paranoia or psychosis, the primary issue is not that the capitalist workplace is unaccommodating; it is that their own brain is subjecting them to a terrifying, distorted version of reality that causes immense personal suffering, regardless of the economic system they live under.

When Chapman argues that we must target “the entire economic system that disables and discriminates against us,” they lean heavily into the social model of disability—the idea that people are disabled not by their bodies or brains, but by society’s failure to accommodate them. While this is highly applicable to a wheelchair user facing a staircase, or an autistic person navigating a loud, fluorescent-lit office, its application to severe mental illness is dangerously limited. Schizophrenia, for instance, is a devastating illness that can erode cognitive function, destroy social bonds, and lead to premature death, independent of societal structure. To suggest that the suffering of a person in the throes of a manic episode or deep clinical depression is primarily an artifact of “stifling norms and exclusions” under capitalism is to minimize the intrinsic torment of these conditions.

Historically, the anti-psychiatry movement of the 1960s and 70s—which Chapman briefly mentions—made similar arguments, framing psychiatric diagnoses as mere tools of social control. While that movement exposed very real abuses in psychiatric institutions, its extreme edge, which denied the reality of mental illness entirely, left many individuals without the medical care they desperately needed. There is a real danger that the “Neurodivergent Power” movement, in its zeal to politicize all psychological difference, will repeat these mistakes. If we redefine debilitating psychiatric symptoms as politically oppressed "ways of being," we risk abandoning those who need psychiatric medicine, hospitalization, and clinical care.

Furthermore, Chapman’s grouping of all “neurominorities” into a single oppressed class facing the common enemy of capitalism glosses over the drastically different needs and experiences within this group. The accommodations needed by a highly verbal autistic software engineer are entirely different from the needs of an individual with profound autism who is non-verbal, engages in severe self-injurious behavior, and requires 24-hour medical care. The latter’s condition is not merely a different “disposition” that is rendered “unproductive” by capitalist pacing. It is a profound, life-limiting disability.

The parents and caregivers of those with profound cognitive and psychiatric disabilities often watch with dismay as the neurodiversity discourse is dominated by highly capable individuals who argue against medical research and interventions. Chapman celebrates the boycott of the Spectrum 10k research program, framing it as a victory against potential eugenics. While the ethical concerns surrounding genetic research are valid and must be rigorously monitored, the complete shutdown of such research also halts the search for biomarkers that could lead to better targeted treatments for the excruciating co-occurring conditions (like severe gastrointestinal issues or life-threatening epilepsy) that plague many with profound autism. By viewing all medical research through the lens of a political battle against "neuronormativity," the radical neurodiversity movement often actively hinders the medical advancements that could alleviate tremendous suffering.

None of this is to say that the current mental health and economic systems are without deep flaws. The incarceration of the mentally ill is a national disgrace. The fact that access to care is tied to employment and wealth is a brutal indictment of our healthcare system. Chapman is absolutely correct that poverty, racism, and imperialism exacerbate disability and trauma. But the solution to the medical neglect and social marginalization of the severely neurodivergent is not to declare war on the concept of medical normality itself.

A truly compassionate future for neurodiversity must be bipartite. It must vigorously defend the rights, accommodations, and dignity of those whose neurodivergence is primarily a matter of difference—those who can thrive in society if given the right flexibility and respect. But it must simultaneously recognize the tragic reality of severe neurological and psychiatric *disease*. It must advocate for better hospitals, more effective medications, robust social care systems, and deeply funded medical research.

Revolutionary politics might offer a compelling narrative of collective struggle, but it cannot cure a delusion, stop a seizure, or replace the grueling, everyday reality of medical caregiving. By collapsing the clinical into the political, “Neurodivergent Power” risks leaving the most profoundly disabled behind in a cloud of radical rhetoric. True liberation must include the right to access medical treatment and the acknowledgment that some forms of neurodivergence are, undeniably, illnesses that cause suffering—suffering that we have a moral obligation to treat, not merely to accommodate.
