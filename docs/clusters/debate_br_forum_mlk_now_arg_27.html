"The attempt to recover Martin Luther King Jr.’s radical intellectual legacy is a misguided effort because his core philosophy of nonviolent love ultimately demands black submission and redemptive suffering rather than true liberation from a totalizing white supremacist order."

Brandon Terry’s “MLK Now” mounts an impassioned defense of Martin Luther King Jr. as a serious political thinker whose radicalism has been defanged by commemoration. It is a lucid, carefully argued essay, but ultimately it is an exercise in wishful recovery that misses the deeper truth: King’s thought, even at its most “radical,” was a philosophy of black submission disguised as redemptive love. By holding up King’s ethical and political framework as a model for our own time, Terry not only ignores the profound nihilism that anti-blackness produces but also perpetuates a tradition that has demanded black suffering as the price of a democracy that has never intended to include black people as full humans.

The key to King’s project, which Terry admirably reconstructs, was nonviolent direct action grounded in agape love—a love that insists on the moral equality of the oppressor, a willingness to endure unearned suffering, and a refusal to humiliate the opponent. Terry notes the criticisms this invites from those who find such ethics masochistic, and he acknowledges psychologist Kenneth Clark’s blunt objection that it is “too much” to ask victims of cruelty to love their perpetrators. But he responds by defending the politics of character and the cultivation of virtues, suggesting that King’s ethics are not just about duties but about the good life. This is where the essay parts company with any honest assessment of black life in a white supremacist order. The “good life” that King imagined—a life of dignity, love, and reconciliation—presupposes a world in which white supremacy can be overcome and in which black self-respect is found not in defiance but in virtuous suffering. For those who experience anti-blackness as a total, structuring force, that presupposition is a theological fantasy.

Terry dismisses what he calls “racial pessimism,” singling out Ta-Nehisi Coates’s description of anti-blackness as a “force of nature” and philosopher Calvin Warren’s critique of King’s nonviolence as the offering of the black body as a “vestibule” for a democracy that will never come. Terry argues that such views lead to a politics of fugitive gestures, self-care indulgences, and cynical disengagement. Yet the events of the past five years alone—the acquittal of George Zimmerman, the Charleston massacre, the rapid normalization of white nationalist violence in Charlottesville, and the election of a president who built his campaign on racial animus—are not anomalies to be addressed by more precise diagnoses. They are the predictable, permanent expressions of an anti-black world. King’s “diagnosis of the disease” of racism may help explain some of its mechanisms, but it refuses to confront the possibility that the disease is the body politic itself.

Terry’s retrieval of King’s racism theory insists on the interplay of psychological, sociopolitical, and cognitive factors, and warns against treating everything as “part of a white supremacist totality.” This analytic temperance is precisely the kind of comfort the pessimist finds naive. The demand for precision and nuance in the face of a structure that is inherently totalizing is itself a tactic of liberal management. When King wrote of the “democratic spirit” that has always faced racism, he was invoking a national mythology that has never been more than a cover story for black dispossession. The very documents King appealed to—the Constitution, the Declaration of Independence—were conceived by slaveholders who included blackness only as property. Terry’s hope that civil disobedience can be revitalized for today’s movements ignores the reality that the state has long since learned to absorb and neutralize such protests through police management, corporate allyship, and the hollow rituals of diversity discourse.

The essay’s extended attempt to separate King’s “politics of character” from respectability politics is a distinction without a meaningful difference. Both demand that black people perform a particular set of virtues—humility, self-restraint, non-vengeance—that make white society comfortable. King’s emphasis on not humiliating the opponent, on winning friendship and understanding, is a call to manage black rage so that it does not disturb the sensibilities of those who hold power. The fact that King framed this as an intrinsic part of the good life only makes it more insidious, because it suggests that black well-being is tied to the emotional generosity we extend to our oppressors. This is not a path to liberation; it is a recipe for spiritual exploitation.

The Movement for Black Lives’ turn away from King toward figures like Assata Shakur is not, as Terry implies, a superficial rejection of “uncool” earnestness. It reflects a growing recognition that the love ethic of the southern black church is insufficient to confront a carceral state that kills without compunction. The chant “No justice, no peace” is often misread as a threat; it can also be heard as a statement of fact: in the absence of a meaningful transformation of power, there will be no social peace, and attempts to manufacture peace through moral appeal are a form of violence themselves.

I do not argue that we should ignore King entirely; he is, after all, a central figure in the history of black political thought. But we should read him as a cautionary tale, not as a guide. His life and death demonstrate the limits of a politics that seeks to make black suffering redemptive. The true lesson of King is that the American racial order will accept a black martyr who preaches love and forgiveness far more readily than a black revolutionary who demands immediate, unconditional freedom. If there is a “divine dissatisfaction” to be nurtured today, it ought to be a dissatisfaction with the very idea that we must love our way out of this. Until that is fully grasped, any retrieval of King, however radical, will remain an exercise in managing black disappointment rather than ending black subjugation.
