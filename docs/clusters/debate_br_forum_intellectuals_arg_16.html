Diversified Gemini

"While Robin D. G. Kelley correctly identifies the need for intellectuals to move beyond mere truth-telling, his strategy remains fatally campus-centric and fails to recognize that true organic intellectuals must be rooted in mass working-class labor movements rather than academic institutions."

In his sweeping essay “The Responsibility of Intellectuals in the Age of Fascism and Genocide,” Robin D. G. Kelley provides a necessary intervention into the ongoing debate over academic freedom and political commitment. His central critique of Noam Chomsky—that merely speaking truth to power is insufficient in the face of an organized fascist offensive—is fundamentally correct. Kelley rightfully invokes Antonio Gramsci to argue that intellectuals must become “organic,” deeply embedded within and fighting alongside an insurgent class. However, despite this radical framing, Kelley’s essay ultimately remains trapped within the very ivory tower it seeks to transcend. By conflating the struggles of the professional-managerial academic class with mass working-class insurgency, Kelley presents a deeply warped, campus-centric view of political struggle. If we are truly to defeat the encroaching forces of fascism, we must look beyond the university gates, recognizing that true organic intellectuals are forged not in faculty senates or NGO boardrooms, but on the factory floors, in logistics hubs, and within the broader, multi-racial working class.

Kelley’s invocation of Gramsci is telling, but his application of Gramscian theory is remarkably selective. For Gramsci, the concept of the “organic intellectual” was inextricably linked to the means of production and class formation. In his writings in *L'Ordine Nuovo* and the *Prison Notebooks*, Gramsci envisioned organic intellectuals of the working class as figures who emerged from the proletariat itself—the technicians, the union organizers, the factory council leaders who could articulate the political and economic destiny of their class. Their purpose was to break the ideological hegemony of the bourgeoisie by building a counter-hegemony rooted in the material realities of working people.

When Kelley points to modern examples of “insurgent intellectual work,” however, his horizon abruptly shrinks. He points to the UCLA Institute on Inequality and Democracy, the African American Policy Forum, left-wing think tanks, and the Working Families Party. He centers the struggle on university encampments, the plight of adjuncts, and the unjust firings of tenured radicals. These are certainly important battlegrounds for free speech and civil liberties, but they represent the grievances of the professional-managerial class (PMC), not the mass working class. A university institute funded by foundation grants, regardless of how radical its mission statement may be, is not an organ of proletarian power. It is an apparatus of the progressive elite.

This campus-centric myopia fundamentally limits Kelley’s strategic vision. He paints a terrifying picture of the new fascist political bloc coalescing under the second Trump administration—a coalition of billionaires, tech magnates, Christian nationalists, and white supremacists. He correctly notes that this bloc aims to eliminate trade unions, the social safety net, and affordable healthcare. Yet, his proposed counter-mobilization remains stubbornly focused on the university. He argues that “unionizing all faculty and teaching staff is not just about salaries... but about academic freedom, free speech, and the right to protest.” But how does unionizing graduate students at Ivy League universities build the mass power necessary to stop the deportation of millions of immigrants or the deregulation of fossil fuels? It doesn’t.

To defeat a structurally entrenched, well-funded fascist bloc, the left requires structural, economic leverage. That leverage can only be found at the point of production and distribution. The true organic intellectuals of our moment are not the professors being disciplined for their social media posts; they are the workers organizing Amazon warehouses in Staten Island, the UAW reformers who led the historic stand-up strikes of 2023, the Teamsters preparing to strike at UPS, and the baristas unionizing Starbucks across the country. These working-class leaders are doing the exact work Gramsci described: they are educating their peers, articulating a vision of collective power, and fighting the capitalist class directly. Kelley’s essay barely mentions organized labor, subsuming the working class into a vague litany of “movements to plug into” alongside tenant organizing and NGO campaigns.

By focusing so heavily on the repression of pro-Palestine speech on campus, Kelley inadvertently elevates the intellectual’s own workplace as the primary site of global struggle. The genocide in Gaza is an atrocity of historic proportions, and the student movement protesting it is acting with immense moral courage. The state and administrative repression they face, detailed thoroughly by Kelley, is abhorrent. But we must clearly distinguish between a moral protest movement and a mass movement capable of contesting state power. The campus protests, by their nature, are isolated from the broader economic engines of the country. When police clear an encampment, it is a violation of rights; when workers shut down a port or a supply chain, it is a crisis for capital. The right wing understands this distinction perfectly, which is why they are far more focused on breaking the power of the NLRB than they are on what happens in a sociology department.

Kelley’s historical examples actually undermine his own academic focus. He rightly praises the Black organic intellectuals of the 1930s and 40s—St. Clair Drake, Horace Cayton, Claudia Jones, William L. Patterson. But what characterized these figures was precisely their deep integration with mass, working-class political formations. Claudia Jones and William L. Patterson were leading figures in the Communist Party, an organization fundamentally rooted in labor organizing, unemployed councils, and industrial unionism. They were not merely trying to “carve out spaces for resistance and visionary planning from within” the university, as Kelley suggests modern academics should do. They were building a militant, multi-racial working-class movement outside of it. They faced deportation, imprisonment, and exile not because they published radical academic papers, but because they posed a direct threat to the economic and racial order of American capitalism.

If modern academics wish to emulate these historical figures, they must realize that the university cannot be the center of the revolution. The systemic precarity of higher education—the casualization of labor, the reliance on corporate donors, the administrative bloat—makes it fundamentally compromised terrain. While faculty unions are necessary to protect workers’ rights within that specific industry, the idea that a “new university” can be transformed into the vanguard of antifascist struggle is a utopian fantasy. The university is structurally designed to reproduce the professional classes and manage capital's research and development needs. It will never bite the hand that feeds it.

Therefore, the responsibility of the radical intellectual today is not to retreat further into the defense of the academy, nor to endlessly litigate the boundaries of academic freedom. The responsibility is downward class defection. It requires scholars to take their analytical skills, their historical knowledge, and their access to resources, and place them entirely at the disposal of the organized working class. It means supporting militant labor actions, writing for and with union organizers, and building political formations that are independent of both the Democratic Party establishment and the university-NGO complex.

Kelley closes with the moving image of Professor Tiffany Willoughby-Herard being arrested while defending her students, quoting her poignant question: “What job do I have if the students don’t have a future?” It is a powerful display of solidarity. But we must ask a broader question: What future does anyone have if the working class remains disorganized and politically impotent? The fight against fascism cannot be won by brave professors shielding their students from riot police. It can only be won when the millions of people who make society function decide to withhold their labor and demand a new world. To achieve that, intellectuals must stop viewing themselves as the center of the resistance and start doing the unglamorous, difficult work of building working-class power.
