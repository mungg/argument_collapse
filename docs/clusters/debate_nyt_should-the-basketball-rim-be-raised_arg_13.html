"The basketball rim should be raised only for the All-Star dunk contest to provide novelty and measurable vertical challenges, while remaining at the standard height for regular games."

Raise the rim — for the dunk contest.

If the concern is that the sport’s above-the-rim spectacle has become stale, the All-Star dunk contest is the logical place to experiment. Put the rim at 11 feet for one round. Let players choose between the standard basket and the higher one. Make vertical daring part of the show, the way a high-jump bar makes the achievement visible to everyone in the building.

That would be fun, measurable and reversible. It would create a new challenge without rewriting the sport. A dunk on an 11-foot rim would immediately mean something, even to a casual fan. It might also rescue the contest from the problem of repetition; after decades of windmills, props and teammates standing under baskets, a higher target would provide genuine novelty.

But regular basketball should remain at 10 feet. Games are not laboratory stunts. They depend on rhythm, spacing, touch and the accumulated instincts of players who have practiced millions of shots at the same height. The dunk contest can afford gimmicks. The N.B.A. playoffs cannot.
