Sub-arguments
  1. 01Modern marriage is complicated by a conflict between traditional values of lifelong commitment and the contemporary priority of personal happiness.
  2. 02The viability of a marriage depends on whether an individual fundamentally desires to resolve the conflict or simply wishes to be free from the relationship.
  3. 03Societal narratives unfairly stigmatize ending a marriage by equating the act of leaving with a lack of virtue or effort.

Human Vicki Larson

"The decision to end a marriage is a deeply personal choice that should be based on an individual's own desire to leave rather than external standards or societal pressures."

Read original ↗
Sub-arguments
  1. 01There is no objective 'right' or 'wrong' reason to uncouple because every relationship dynamic and its reasons for forming are unique.
  2. 02Societal pressure and the stigma of failure often force people to remain in miserable relationships longer than they should.
  3. 03External experts and universal 'signs' cannot determine the right time to split because relationship issues are rarely black-and-white.
  4. 04The common narrative that any relationship can be fixed with enough hard work is a fallacy that ignores the reality that some marriages simply cannot be saved.
  5. 05While practical considerations like children and finances are important, they do not invalidate the personal realization that a marriage is over.
Diversified Claude

"A marriage is over the moment one person decides they are no longer willing to be in it, regardless of whether they have a reason that others deem sufficient."

I want to offer a simpler answer than the therapeutic literature tends to provide: a marriage is over when one person has decided it is over. That sounds glib, but it's actually the most respectful framing available, because it takes seriously the autonomy of the person who wants to leave.

We spend enormous cultural energy litigating whether someone's reasons for leaving a marriage are "good enough" — whether the affair was bad enough, whether the neglect was sustained enough, whether they tried therapy long enough. This litigation falls disproportionately on women, who are still expected to justify exits that men are permitted to take without explanation. It also falls on people in marriages that look fine from the outside: no violence, no addiction, no infidelity, just a slow suffocation that the spouse leaving cannot adequately translate into terms a judgmental in-law will accept.

You do not owe anyone a defensible reason to end a marriage. "I am no longer willing" is a complete sentence. The de Botton essay, for all its charm, participates in a long tradition of telling people — historically, women — that their dissatisfaction is a failure of philosophical maturity rather than useful information about their own life.

Of course there are obligations: to children, to honesty during the process, to financial fairness, to not blowing up a partner's life through cruelty or deceit. These obligations are real and they shape how one leaves. But they do not determine whether one is permitted to leave.

The question "when is it time?" presumes there is an external arbiter who will tell you. There isn't. There is only you, paying honest attention to your own life, and deciding whether you can keep living it. That is a heavier responsibility than waiting for a definitive breaking point, but it is also more truthful.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The decision to leave should be framed as an act of personal autonomy rather than a case to be litigated against external standards of what constitutes a 'good enough' reason.
  2. 02Societal pressure to justify ending a marriage disproportionately burdens women and those in relationships that appear functional from the outside.
  3. 03Dissatisfaction in a marriage should be viewed as vital information about one's life rather than a failure of philosophical maturity or endurance.
  4. 04While one has moral and financial obligations regarding how they leave a marriage, those obligations do not dictate whether they are permitted to leave.
  5. 05There is no external arbiter or definitive breaking point to wait for; the responsibility for ending a marriage rests solely on an individual's honest assessment of their own life.
