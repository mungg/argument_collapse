"Mie Inouye’s framework of social endurance and epistemic humility provides a necessary practical alternative to moralism and deference for building the broad, long-term coalitions required to address the climate crisis."

Mie Inouye’s essay is addressed chiefly to racial and economic justice movements, but its argument may be even more urgent for climate politics. Climate change is the great test of solidarity across unequal vulnerability. It affects everyone, but not equally; it is caused by everyone, but not equally; it demands collective action, but the costs and benefits of that action are distributed unevenly. If there is any issue that exposes the limits of both moralism and simple interest-based politics, it is this one.

The climate crisis makes Fred Moten’s formulation—“this shit is killing you, too, however much more softly”—both powerful and complicated. Rising heat, wildfire smoke, floods, storms, crop failures, water stress, and disease risks are already harming people across class and racial lines. In 2023, smoke from Canadian wildfires turned skies orange across parts of the United States, making climate disruption visible to people who had imagined it as distant. Heat waves have strained grids, threatened outdoor workers, and endangered the elderly. Insurance markets are faltering in fire- and storm-prone regions. In this sense, the system really is killing broadly.

But “broadly” is not “equally.” Low-income communities, Indigenous peoples, island nations, outdoor workers, incarcerated people, migrants, the elderly, disabled people, and residents of historically segregated neighborhoods face disproportionate harms. Globally, countries least responsible for cumulative emissions are often most exposed. Within wealthy countries, people with air conditioning, savings, insurance, mobility, and political influence can soften the blows. A climate politics that says “we are all in this together” can become false universalism. A politics that says only the most affected have standing can fail to mobilize the scale of action required. Inouye’s search for solidarity across divergent interests is therefore exactly the climate problem.

Moral commitment has brought many people into climate activism. The image of a burning planet, the injustice to future generations, the devastation of species and ecosystems—these can awaken conscience. Youth climate movements have been especially effective at moral indictment. They ask adults how we can justify sacrificing their future for convenience and profit. This accusation is necessary. Yet moral shock alone has not produced adequate action. Many people agree climate change is real and serious while resisting policies that threaten jobs, raise energy prices, alter landscapes, or challenge consumption patterns.

Material interest is also insufficient. Some climate advocates argue that the clean energy transition will produce jobs, lower costs, and improve health. Often this is true. Solar, wind, batteries, grid modernization, building retrofits, transit, and care infrastructure can create enormous public benefits. But transition also creates losers or perceived losers: fossil fuel workers, communities dependent on extraction, homeowners facing new regulations, consumers worried about prices, people attached to gas stoves or pickup trucks, local residents opposing transmission lines or renewable projects. Pretending that decarbonization is all upside breeds distrust. Solidarity requires conflict over real tradeoffs.

Inouye’s concept of social endurance helps here. Climate politics demands long time horizons and repeated negotiation among people who do not naturally like or trust one another: environmentalists, unions, Indigenous nations, farmers, urban transit advocates, utility workers, engineers, frontline communities, ratepayers, public officials, and private firms. The work is not a single march but decades of siting decisions, permitting fights, labor agreements, adaptation plans, disaster recovery, and public investment. People will need to remain in rooms through boredom, technical detail, betrayal, and grief.

The left has sometimes failed this test. Parts of the environmental movement have treated labor concerns as parochial obstacles to planetary necessity. Parts of organized labor have treated climate demands as elite attacks on working-class livelihoods. Some climate activists have embraced a politics of emergency that undervalues democratic consent. Some local opponents of clean infrastructure use environmental language to defend property values. Meanwhile, fossil fuel companies exploit every division while presenting themselves as defenders of workers and consumers.

A solidaristic climate politics must begin by asking Bob Lee’s question: what do you want? Ask the refinery worker. Ask the child with asthma near a highway. Ask the tribal nation whose land is targeted for mining or transmission. Ask the Appalachian community whose tax base depends on coal. Ask the renter whose apartment overheats. Ask the farmer facing drought. Ask the young person terrified of the future. Ask the ratepayer behind on bills. Ask the undocumented disaster worker cleaning up after storms. Their answers will not harmonize automatically. But without them, climate policy becomes technocratic imposition or moral theater.

The idea of a just transition is supposed to address this. At its best, it means that workers and communities dependent on fossil fuels should not be discarded; they should receive income support, pensions, retraining where appropriate, public investment, and a real voice in shaping local economies. At its worst, “just transition” becomes a slogan invoked at conferences while actual communities decline. Social endurance would mean staying with those communities beyond the press release, through the slow work of building replacement institutions.

The Inflation Reduction Act, passed in 2022, marks an important shift in U.S. climate politics because it ties decarbonization to industrial policy, jobs, and subsidies rather than relying chiefly on carbon pricing or moral appeal. Its incentives for clean energy, manufacturing, electric vehicles, and home energy improvements may accelerate the transition. But it also reveals the limits of policy without deep solidarity. Implementation will require local capacity, permitting, labor standards, attention to environmental justice, and fights over who benefits. A tax credit does not build trust. A factory announcement does not guarantee good jobs. A solar project does not automatically respect land or community.

Climate solidarity must also be international. Wealthy countries built their prosperity through fossil fuels and colonial extraction; poorer countries now demand development, adaptation finance, and loss-and-damage support. It is not enough for Americans or Europeans to decarbonize their own grids while ignoring supply chains, debt, migration, and the right of others to live decent lives. Yet international solidarity is harder than domestic solidarity because the “room” is abstract. Most citizens will never meet those most harmed by their emissions. Here institutions must do what face-to-face organizing cannot: treaties, climate finance, technology transfer, migration protections, and global labor standards. Social endurance at planetary scale requires durable institutions, not just empathy.

Inouye’s critique of deference politics also applies to climate. Frontline communities should have special authority in environmental justice struggles; they know harms that distant experts miss. But no single community can decide the whole energy system. A local veto over every project can block necessary decarbonization, while centralized imposition can reproduce sacrifice zones. Epistemic humility is essential: engineers must listen to residents; residents must have access to technical knowledge; climate modelers must learn from Indigenous ecological knowledge; unions must take seriously the atmospheric stakes; environmentalists must take seriously workers’ fear of abandonment.

The temporal dimension of solidarity is especially acute. Climate politics is intergenerational. The people most affected by decisions made today include children and the unborn, who cannot sit in our meetings. Future generations complicate democratic politics because they cannot express preferences or threaten incumbents. Social endurance must therefore include fidelity to those absent in time. Rituals of memory and responsibility may matter here. Religious traditions often bind people to ancestors and descendants; secular climate politics needs similar practices of obligation.

But endurance must not become patience with injustice. The fossil fuel industry benefits from delay. Calls for civility and togetherness can be weaponized to slow action. There is a place for disruption, litigation, divestment, regulation, and direct confrontation. Coalitions need internal endurance, but they also need external antagonism. The target is not simply individual carbon-intensive behavior; it is a political economy that has allowed companies to profit while obscuring damage and delaying alternatives.

Still, even confrontation requires a positive social basis. People will not accept rapid transformation if they believe they will be abandoned or humiliated. Climate politics must offer belonging as well as warning: public jobs, cleaner air, lower bills, safer homes, better transit, restored lands, and communities with a future. It must make decarbonization feel not like austerity but like repair.

Inouye’s essay reminds us that solidarity is not discovered fully formed in shared danger. Danger can produce panic, scapegoating, hoarding, and authoritarianism. Climate disruption may generate eco-fascism as easily as eco-socialism if institutions of solidarity are weak. The work now is to build organizations capable of connecting unequal harms to common action: unions that bargain for climate, churches that prepare for disasters, schools that teach ecological citizenship, tenant groups that fight for cooling and efficiency, Indigenous-led land defense, public agencies that earn trust, and international movements that link debt, development, and decarbonization.

The climate crisis will force us into relation. The question is whether that relation will be solidaristic or brutal. Social endurance will not lower emissions by itself. But without it, the conflicts of transition may tear apart the coalitions needed to make a livable future.
