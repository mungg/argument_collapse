"Postpartum depression screening should be universal and mandatory for health systems to provide, provided it is implemented as a respectful opt-out process backed by comprehensive treatment infrastructure."

Postpartum depression screening should be universal, but the word “mandatory” needs careful handling. Mandatory for whom? It should be mandatory for health systems to offer screening, explain it and follow up. It should not feel mandatory for a mother to surrender privacy or accept treatment under threat.

The most sensible model is opt-out universal screening at several points: before hospital discharge, at the mother’s postpartum visit, and during early well-baby visits. Depression may not appear in the first week. Some women feel fine at delivery and collapse two months later. Others are screened once, while still surrounded by relatives and nurses, and then disappear into isolation. A single legal requirement is too crude for a condition that changes over time.

The screening tool should be brief and validated, such as the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale, but it should never substitute for clinical judgment. Postpartum mental illness includes depression, anxiety, obsessive thoughts, post-traumatic symptoms after difficult births, and the rare but urgent condition of postpartum psychosis. A mother who says she cannot sleep even when the baby sleeps, hears voices, or fears she may harm herself or the baby needs immediate help, not a routine referral sheet.

Assuring treatment requires building a bridge between maternity care and mental health care. Today that bridge is weak. Obstetricians may not have psychiatrists to call. Psychiatrists may not be comfortable advising about medication during breastfeeding. Pediatricians see the consequences but may not be authorized or reimbursed to treat the mother. Insurers may cover a visit but not the care coordination that makes it happen.

States that require screening should also require a plan: crisis protocols, referral agreements, culturally competent counseling, interpreter services, and coverage for evidence-based treatment. Peer support groups and home visiting nurses can help women who would never walk into a mental health clinic. Fathers and partners should be educated too, because they often see changes first.

The goal is not to label motherhood as pathology. The goal is to stop pretending that a healthy baby automatically means a healthy mother. Universal screening, done respectfully and backed by real services, is a practical and humane standard.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Screening should occur at multiple intervals throughout the postpartum period to account for the fact that depression can emerge or worsen well after hospital discharge.
  2. 02Standardized screening tools must be used as a supplement to, rather than a replacement for, nuanced clinical judgment that can identify a wide range of maternal mental health conditions.
  3. 03Effective screening requires strengthening the currently weak coordination between maternity care, pediatrics, and mental health specialists.
  4. 04Mandatory screening laws must be accompanied by a robust infrastructure of crisis protocols, referral agreements, and diverse support services to ensure identified mothers actually receive care.
  5. 05Universal screening is necessary to dismantle the false assumption that a healthy infant guarantees the well-being of the mother.

"Postpartum depression screening should be mandatory for healthcare systems to provide, provided it is paired with robust treatment infrastructure and respects a mother's right to decline."

The right answer is to make postpartum depression screening mandatory for the health care system, not compulsory for the mother. That distinction matters. Every obstetric, family medicine and pediatric practice serving new families should be required to offer standardized screening at predictable points and to document what happens next. But a woman should retain the ordinary right to decline a medical screen. Public health works best when it normalizes care without turning patients into subjects of enforcement.

The case for routine screening is strong. Postpartum depression affects a substantial share of new mothers, and the Times is right that it is more varied than the stereotype of a sad woman who cannot bond with her baby. It can look like anxiety, rage, numbness, obsessive fear, sleeplessness beyond the baby’s demands, or frightening intrusive thoughts. Many women will not raise these symptoms unless asked directly and without judgment. Pediatric visits are especially important because infants are seen repeatedly in the first months, while mothers may attend only one postpartum visit.

But a screen without a safety net is bad policy. States should treat postpartum depression as a quality-of-care issue. A positive screen should trigger a “warm handoff,” not a photocopied list of therapists. Clinics should know whom to call for urgent psychiatric evaluation, who can provide counseling, which medications are compatible with breastfeeding, and how to follow up if a mother misses an appointment. Peer support and home visiting should be part of the response, particularly for women isolated by poverty, language barriers or lack of transportation.

Assuring treatment also requires payment reform. Mental health parity must be enforced. Medicaid and private insurers should cover screening, consultation, psychotherapy and medication management. Telepsychiatry can help rural areas where specialists are scarce. Training matters too: obstetricians and pediatricians do not need to become psychiatrists, but they do need to recognize risk, discuss treatment without stigma and respond to suicidal thoughts or signs of psychosis as emergencies.

The fear many mothers have is not irrational: they worry that honesty will lead to punishment. Laws should make clear that seeking help is evidence of responsible parenting, not neglect. Mandatory system-wide screening, paired with accessible treatment and strong confidentiality protections, can save lives while respecting mothers’ dignity.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Routine screening is necessary because postpartum symptoms are diverse and many women will not disclose their struggles unless directly prompted by a healthcare professional.
  2. 02Pediatric visits offer a critical opportunity for screening because infants are seen more frequently in the early months than mothers are.
  3. 03Screening must be integrated with a comprehensive 'safety net' that includes immediate referrals, specialized psychiatric knowledge, and peer support services.
  4. 04Effective treatment requires insurance and payment reforms to ensure that mental health services are affordable and accessible, particularly in rural or underserved areas.
  5. 05Healthcare providers must be specifically trained to recognize risks and manage postpartum emergencies without stigmatizing the patient.
  6. 06Legal and confidentiality protections are essential to reassure mothers that seeking help for mental health will not be used as grounds for punishment or claims of parental neglect.
