"The framing of development as a sequential 'turn' for different regions is misleading because progress is an uneven process dependent on specific, non-replicable conditions rather than a predictable historical cycle."

Edward Miguel's essay is a model of careful scholarly analysis, and I mean this as genuine praise. He presents evidence without overclaiming, acknowledges uncertainty, and resists the temptation to reduce complex phenomena to simple causes. This is particularly valuable in a public intellectual context where African development is often debated in hyperbolic terms—either the continent is rising or it is failing, either Chinese engagement is colonial exploitation or it is salvation. Miguel shows us that the truth is more textured and that policy conclusions must respect that texture.

My primary response is to push back gently on the essay's implicit framing. The question "Is It Africa's Turn?" presupposes that development is a discrete event that happens to regions in sequence—first Europe, then North America, then Asia, now perhaps Africa. This framing is seductive but misleading. Development is not a relay race where regions take turns; it is a highly uneven process driven by specific historical, geographic, and political conditions that cannot be replicated by wishing or planning. The question that should matter is not whether it is Africa's turn, but what conditions might allow African countries to achieve sustained economic and political progress if circumstances align favorably.

Miguel himself provides evidence that complicates the turn narrative. China's rise benefits Africa through commodity demand, but Chinese firms are not interested in African development per se; they are interested in profit. Western agricultural subsidies distort markets, but eliminating them would not automatically trigger African industrialization. Foreign aid has proven largely ineffective at driving growth, despite hundreds of billions of dollars invested. Democratic transitions have occurred in dozens of African countries, but electoral democracy without economic security can be destabilizing, as Kenya demonstrates. Climate change threatens the Sahel but its precise impacts remain uncertain. Each of these factors could contribute to African success or derail it, depending on how events unfold.

The honest answer from May 2008 is that we simply do not know whether Africa is on the cusp of transformation or merely experiencing a temporary boom. The indicators Miguel marshals—3 percent per capita growth, improved Freedom House scores, rising commodity prices—are suggestive but not conclusive. Historical precedents are mixed: Latin America's nineteenth-century struggles offer hope but also cautionary tales of prolonged stagnation. East Asia's success was not a predictable outcome in 1960 but rather the result of specific conditions—late development, strong states, favorable Cold War geopolitics—that do not map neatly onto African realities.

What does this uncertainty mean for policy? First, it means avoiding overcommitment to particular interventions based on optimistic scenarios. The Sachs view that dramatically increased aid can break Africa out of poverty traps is not supported by evidence; the Easterly view that aid is largely ineffective is also not supported by evidence. Both camps are claiming certainty that the data do not support. Second, it means prioritizing resilience over optimization. Given uncertainty about which factors will drive future outcomes, African countries and their partners should focus on building diversified economies, strong institutions, and social safety nets that can withstand shocks rather than betting everything on particular sectors or reforms.

The concrete steps Miguel recommends at the essay's conclusion are sound precisely because they are incremental and hedging. Reducing Western farm subsidies would help but won't solve structural problems. Using aid as conflict insurance addresses a real vulnerability without pretending that aid can transform economies. Ending wars in Darfur and Congo is urgent humanitarian work that also happens to serve development ends. Promoting agricultural adaptation to climate change prepares for risks rather than assuming favorable outcomes. None of these steps pretends to guarantee African success; they simply improve the odds.

I would add one recommendation Miguel does not make explicit: invest in African knowledge production. The continent's long-term development depends ultimately on African scientists, engineers, economists, and policymakers who understand local conditions and can generate solutions tailored to African contexts. This means supporting African universities and research institutions, not as charity but as infrastructure essential for development. It means creating conditions where African intellectuals can work in Africa rather than emigrating to Western institutions. It means recognizing that Africa's turn, if it comes, will be built by Africans on African terms, with international support but not international direction.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The indicators of African progress cited by Miguel, such as commodity-driven growth and improved democracy scores, are suggestive of potential but are not conclusive evidence of a permanent transformation.
  2. 02Development policy should prioritize building institutional and economic resilience to withstand shocks rather than overcommitting to singular, optimistic interventions like massive aid infusions.
  3. 03The historical success of other regions, such as East Asia, resulted from unique geopolitical and state conditions that do not neatly apply to the current African context.
  4. 04Sustained progress requires investing in African knowledge production and research institutions to ensure that development is led by local experts rather than external direction.
  5. 05Incremental policy steps, such as reducing trade subsidies and providing conflict insurance, are valuable because they improve the odds of success without pretending to guarantee it.
