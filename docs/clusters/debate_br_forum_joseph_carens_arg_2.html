"Amnesty for settled irregular migrants is a moral necessity because deportation functions as a destructive act against the family unit, harming U.S. citizens and lawful residents who are intimately tied to the migrants."

The case for amnesty looks different when viewed through the lens of family life. Joseph Carens discusses marriage and children, but the full force of the family argument deserves emphasis. Deportation is often described as the removal of an individual noncitizen. In reality, it is frequently an act against a family, including U.S. citizens and lawful residents who have committed no violation at all.

Miguel Sanchez’s story illustrates the point. If he is deported, what happens to his American wife and citizen child? They can remain in the United States without him, in which case the state has separated a spouse from a spouse and a child from a father. Or they can follow him to Mexico, in which case U.S. citizens are effectively pressured to leave their own country to preserve family unity. Neither outcome is compatible with the professed American commitment to family values.

The law often treats this as unfortunate but permissible. Immigration rules assume that the citizen spouse chose the risk by marrying someone without status. That view is morally thin. People do not marry legal categories; they marry persons. The citizen’s interest in living with a spouse in her own country is not trivial. Nor is the child’s interest in daily care from both parents. A liberal democracy should not impose family separation casually to vindicate an immigration violation committed years earlier.

European human-rights law has been more explicit than American law in recognizing a right to family life. The United States, despite its political rhetoric about family, has often been harsher in practice. The 1996 immigration laws, with their three- and ten-year bars and expanded grounds of removal, created traps for mixed-status families. Many people who might otherwise regularize through a citizen spouse cannot do so without leaving the country and triggering long exclusions. The result is perverse: the law recognizes marriage to a citizen as relevant, then makes the path so dangerous that families remain underground.

Carens is right that marriage to a citizen or permanent resident should dramatically strengthen a migrant’s claim to stay. Fraud concerns are real but manageable. Immigration authorities already investigate sham marriages. The existence of fraud cannot justify treating all mixed-status marriages as suspect or disposable. A genuine marriage, especially one with children, should ordinarily bar deportation absent serious criminal conduct or security concerns.

Children create an even stronger claim. The United States grants citizenship by birth on its soil. That means many children of unauthorized migrants are citizens from the moment they are born. Yet their citizenship is devalued if the government can remove their parents without giving serious weight to the child’s interests. A four-year-old cannot exercise citizenship meaningfully, but he can be harmed by its disregard. He needs food, shelter, affection, routines, and the presence of caregivers. Deportation can destroy those goods.

Some respond that parents should not gain legal status through having children. The phrase “anchor baby” captures this suspicion in its ugliest form. But recognizing a child’s interest is not the same as rewarding reproductive strategy. Most parents have children because they are building lives, not because they are calculating immigration benefits. And even if some adults act strategically, the child remains innocent. Public policy should not punish children to express disapproval of parents.

The family argument also helps answer the charge that amnesty is unfair to those abroad. Family unity has long been a central principle of American immigration law. We already prioritize spouses, children, and parents because we understand that intimate relationships generate special claims. Extending protection to mixed-status families is not a radical departure. It is a correction of a system that currently honors family unity only when paperwork is in order.

A family-centered amnesty would include several elements. First, spouses of U.S. citizens and lawful permanent residents who have no serious criminal convictions should be able to adjust status inside the United States after paying reasonable fees and completing background checks. They should not be forced to depart and risk long bars. Second, parents of U.S.-citizen or lawful-resident children should receive provisional legal status, with a path to permanence after continued residence and good conduct. Third, children brought here unlawfully should be legalized quickly, without forcing them to prove exceptional achievement. Fourth, immigration judges should be required to consider the best interests of children before ordering removal.

This would not mean that every family tie defeats enforcement. Recent entrants, sham relationships, and serious offenders present different cases. But the default should shift. The government should bear a heavy burden before separating families that are settled and functioning. Deportation should not be treated as an administrative inconvenience when its effect is the rupture of a household.

The current system also harms families through fear short of deportation. Unauthorized parents may avoid police even when victimized. They may hesitate to seek medical care, attend school meetings, or travel for funerals. Children sense the anxiety. A knock on the door, a workplace raid, or a traffic stop becomes a family emergency. Legalization would restore ordinary life: the ability to drive to another city, board a plane, visit relatives, and plan for the future.

Opponents of amnesty often appeal to responsibility. Adults, they say, must face the consequences of their choices. But family life teaches another form of responsibility: we must consider who else bears the consequences. When the state deports a parent, the punishment falls on children. When it removes a spouse, the citizen partner suffers. When it destabilizes a household, neighborhoods and schools absorb the shock. Immigration enforcement is never as individual as its paperwork suggests.

Carens’s broader principle is that social membership grows over time. Families are among the main ways that growth occurs. Through marriage, parenting, school, caregiving, and neighborhood routines, migrants become woven into the lives of citizens. To tear them out is to tear citizens’ lives as well.

A country that claims to value families should build immigration law around that value. Amnesty for settled migrants is not only an act of mercy toward noncitizens. It is protection for citizen spouses, citizen children, and the ordinary bonds that make a society humane.

  3. 03Current immigration laws create 'perverse' traps that recognize family ties in theory but make the legal path to regularization so dangerous that families are forced to remain underground in constant fear.
  4. 04A family-centered amnesty is not a radical departure from tradition but a necessary correction to align immigration policy with the long-standing American commitment to prioritizing family reunification.

Diversified MiniMax

"While the moral claims of long-term residents are persuasive, any amnesty policy must prioritize the specific legal and moral complexities surrounding the citizen children of irregular migrants rather than relying solely on the passage of time."

Carens is persuasive on the moral claims of long-term residents, but he is less convincing on the specific policy mechanisms he proposes. Let me focus on a narrow but important issue: what happens to the children of irregular migrants under an amnesty regime?

Carens discusses children who arrived in the United States as young children, and he argues that their social formation in this country gives them strong moral claims to remain. This is correct. But what about children who were born in the United States to irregular parents? These children are American citizens by birth, with full constitutional rights. They have never lived anywhere else and have no other citizenship. If we deport their parents, we either separate families or force American citizens to leave the country.

Carens does not address this case directly, but it illustrates a fundamental problem with treating social membership as a criterion for regularization. The children of irregular migrants have not just developed social membership; they were born into it. Their membership is not a matter of degree or time; it is absolute. And yet their fate is tied to their parents' legal status.

Any amnesty policy must grapple with this reality. The options are not comfortable: either we deport the parents and force their citizen children to choose between family and country, or we grant amnesty to parents whose only claim to stay is the citizenship of their children. The first option is cruel; the second undermines the principle that illegal entry should have consequences.

Perhaps the answer is a child-centered amnesty that regularizes the parents of citizen children but not other irregular migrants. This would be less comprehensive than Carens proposes but more targeted toward those with the strongest claims. It would also avoid the more controversial implications of a blanket amnesty for all long-term residents.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The case of U.S.-born children of irregular migrants demonstrates that social membership is often absolute from birth rather than a matter of degree or time.
  2. 02Deporting the parents of citizen children creates an unacceptable moral dilemma by either separating families or effectively forcing American citizens to leave their own country.
  3. 03A child-centered amnesty policy that regularizes parents based on their children's citizenship would be more targeted and less controversial than a blanket amnesty for all long-term residents.
  4. 04Focusing on the rights of citizen children highlights a fundamental flaw in using general social membership as the primary criterion for regularization.
