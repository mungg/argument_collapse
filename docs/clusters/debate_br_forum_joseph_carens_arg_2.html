"Liberal democracies must grant legal status to long-term irregular migrants because social membership is earned through the reality of lived experience and community integration rather than mere official permission."

Joseph Carens’s most important move is to shift the discussion from border control to membership. That shift is both morally clarifying and politically useful. In the United States, debates over unauthorized immigration usually begin with the first act of unlawful entry and then freeze the migrant forever in that moment. Carens asks us to notice what happens next: work, marriage, children, church, mortgage payments, taxes, Little League, English classes, aging parents, neighbors who rely on one another. A society that refuses to see those facts is not defending the rule of law; it is defending a legal fiction against human reality.

The strongest part of Carens’s argument is his insistence that time changes the moral situation. This is not sentimentalism. Law itself recognizes that time matters. Statutes of limitation, adverse possession, residency requirements for voting and tuition, naturalization rules, and common-law doctrines of reliance all reflect the same insight: human beings organize their lives around settled expectations, and at some point the disruption of those expectations becomes too costly to justify. If a state allows a person to live, work, and raise children in its territory for many years, the state cannot plausibly pretend that the person is a stranger.

This point is especially powerful in the United States because unauthorized migration has not occurred in a vacuum. The country has built a labor market that depends heavily on immigrants who lack formal authorization. Agriculture, meatpacking, construction, restaurants, domestic work, hotels, landscaping, and child care have all absorbed unauthorized workers. Employers have often benefited from the workers’ vulnerability. Consumers have benefited from lower prices. Local communities have benefited from taxes, rents, purchases, and labor. Politicians have denounced “illegal immigrants” while tolerating the economic arrangements that draw them here.

That complicity does not erase the fact that immigration laws were violated. But it does make mass deportation morally grotesque. If the country has, in practice, incorporated millions of people into its economy and neighborhoods, then it cannot suddenly describe them as invaders when convenient. A decent society must regularize the people it has already made use of.

Carens proposes an automatic transition to legal status after a fixed period, perhaps five to seven years. I would support such a policy, though I would design it with safeguards that make it politically durable. Applicants should have to show continuous residence, pay assessed taxes or enter a payment plan, pass criminal background checks, and register openly. They should receive probationary legal status first, followed by permanent residence after several years. Those who entered as children, spouses of citizens and lawful residents, and parents of U.S.-citizen children should receive especially generous treatment. The aim should not be punishment for punishment’s sake, but public recognition that regularization is a serious civic act.

The main objection is that amnesty rewards lawbreaking. But that objection assumes a simple moral world in which lawful migration channels are available and unauthorized migrants choose to cheat. For many low-wage workers, there is no realistic line to stand in. The United States admits many family members, some high-skilled workers, refugees, and seasonal laborers. It does not provide anything close to a lawful pathway matching the labor demand that clearly exists. The result is predictable: people move anyway, employers hire anyway, and the law produces illegality rather than order.

Another objection is deterrence. Won’t legalization invite more unauthorized immigration? Possibly, if legalization stands alone. The lesson of the 1986 Immigration Reform and Control Act is not that legalization was wrong, but that legalization without a workable future system is incomplete. IRCA legalized millions, but employer sanctions were weak, document fraud became common, and legal channels for needed workers remained inadequate. A new legalization program should therefore be paired with real labor standards enforcement, reliable workplace verification that does not become a tool for discrimination, and expanded legal avenues for workers whose labor the economy actually uses.

But deterrence cannot do all the moral work its defenders ask of it. To deport Miguel Sanchez after nine years, a citizen spouse, and a U.S.-citizen child would be to sacrifice an actual family to a speculative message. The message might not even be heard by future migrants deciding whether they can feed their families. Meanwhile, the harm to Miguel’s family would be immediate and severe. Liberal democracies should not treat settled families as billboards for border policy.

Carens is right to resist excessive discretion. A system based on proving “exceptional hardship” or persuading an immigration judge that one is sufficiently sympathetic invites arbitrariness. It also favors those with lawyers, English skills, and tidy biographies. A residency-based rule is imperfect, but imperfection is not an argument against rules. Five years may be somewhat arbitrary; so are voting ages and filing deadlines. The relevant question is whether the line reasonably tracks the underlying moral reality. Five to seven years of peaceful residence does.

The deeper question is what kind of country the United States wants to be. We can continue to sustain an underclass that is present but deportable, employed but exploitable, taxed but voiceless, rooted but insecure. Or we can acknowledge that membership grows from life lived together. Amnesty, properly understood, is not forgetting the law. It is law catching up with society.

Carens’s essay is a reminder that immigration policy is not only about who may enter tomorrow. It is also about what we owe to those who are already here, whose lives are interwoven with ours. If liberal democracy means anything, it must mean that human beings cannot be held indefinitely in civic limbo after they have become part of the community. On that central point, the case for amnesty is not merely compassionate. It is just.

"While Joseph Carens is correct that long-term residence creates a moral claim to stay, a just amnesty policy must move beyond sympathetic narratives to establish a rule-bound system that balances social membership with proportional standards for conduct and vulnerability."

The moral force of Joseph Carens’s essay comes from individual stories. Miguel Sanchez, Marguerite Grimmond, and Hiu Lui Ng make visible the human cost of deportation. But the immigration debate cannot be settled by sympathetic cases alone. For every Miguel, opponents imagine a less sympathetic case: someone who crossed recently, used false documents, committed crimes, or imposed costs on public systems. The challenge is to build a policy that neither sentimentalizes nor demonizes.

Carens’s principle—that time creates membership—is a good starting point precisely because it is not dependent on perfect virtue. People do not become members of a society by being saints. Citizens can be lazy, unpleasant, indebted, divorced, unemployed, or guilty of minor offenses without losing the right to remain. If unauthorized migrants must prove exceptional moral worth to avoid deportation, then we are not recognizing membership; we are granting charity.

Still, membership should not be blind to conduct. A legalization program may reasonably exclude people convicted of serious violent crimes, trafficking, or major fraud. The state has a duty to protect residents, including immigrant residents, from predatory behavior. Carens acknowledges criminal convictions as a relevant factor, but the details matter. In American immigration law, the category of “aggravated felony” has expanded far beyond what ordinary language suggests, and minor drug or theft offenses can have drastic consequences. A just amnesty should define exclusions narrowly and proportionately.

The same caution applies to false documents. Carens argues that many unauthorized migrants use false numbers simply to work, often paying taxes without receiving benefits. That is true. But identity misuse can harm real people when Social Security numbers are stolen or credit records affected. The law should distinguish between invented or invalid numbers, borrowed documents, and identity theft that causes financial injury. Survival-based document violations should not bar legalization automatically, but neither should all fraud be waved away. Proportionality is the key.

One weakness in Carens’s essay is that it sometimes suggests deportation after long residence is wrong because the migrant has become harmless and productive. What about the person who has been here ten years but has not worked steadily? What about someone with alcoholism, mental illness, or dependence on relatives? The social membership argument should include them too. Membership is not earned only through economic contribution. A humane society does not exile residents because they are burdensome. The threshold should be residence and non-dangerousness, not market success.

This point matters because pro-immigrant advocacy often leans heavily on the language of contribution: immigrants work hard, pay taxes, start businesses, serve in the military, and strengthen the economy. Much of that is true. But if the right to remain depends on productivity, then vulnerable migrants are left exposed. Children, the elderly, the disabled, caregivers, and the unemployed may all belong deeply without fitting the heroic worker narrative. Carens’s emphasis on ordinary life is therefore welcome. The right to stay should be grounded in human rootedness, not just usefulness.

Opponents of amnesty also make a moral point that deserves engagement: law-abidingness matters. A society that treats unlawful entry as irrelevant from the beginning would weaken legal order. Carens avoids this by saying time matters; recent entrants may be removed. I think that distinction is essential. But the state must act consistently. It is unjust to ignore unauthorized residents for years because their labor is convenient and then suddenly deport them as if discovery occurred at the border. If enforcement is to happen, it should happen early, humanely, and predictably.

The practical implication is a two-tier system. Recent unauthorized entrants could be subject to removal, with asylum and trafficking protections intact. Long-settled residents would be eligible for legalization. The line might be seven years, with shorter periods for childhood arrivals and close family of citizens or permanent residents. This system would preserve the state’s ability to regulate entry while recognizing that failure to act over time changes the moral facts.

A further issue is detention. Hiu Lui Ng’s death shows that deportation policy is not only about legal outcomes but also about the machinery used to produce them. Detention can be dangerous, degrading, and unnecessary, especially for people with families and community ties. Any amnesty debate should include detention reform: medical care standards, judicial review, alternatives to detention, and limits on holding people who pose no danger. A person should not have to die in custody for the public to ask why he was detained at all.

Carens’s proposal would also reduce bad policing incentives. When local police become immigration agents, immigrant communities stop reporting crimes. Victims of domestic violence, wage theft, and assault remain silent. Public safety suffers. Legalization would make cooperation easier. Enforcement should focus on genuine threats, not on making every traffic stop a possible deportation event.

The hardest question remains deterrence. Would a time-based amnesty encourage future unlawful migration? It might at the margins. But deterrence must be weighed against present injustice. We do not deny emergency care to unauthorized migrants to deter border crossing. We do not bar their children from elementary school, thanks to Plyler v. Doe, because punishing children is too high a price. Likewise, keeping millions in permanent fear is too high a price for uncertain deterrence. Better deterrence lies in legal migration channels and employer accountability.

Carens’s essay succeeds because it asks us to treat unauthorized migrants as morally complex human beings rather than symbols. But policy must also treat them as legally varied. Some came as infants, some as adults; some overstayed visas, some crossed deserts; some have citizen families, some do not; some have minor convictions, a few serious ones. A just amnesty should be generous but not indiscriminate, rule-bound but not mechanical, attentive to conduct but not obsessed with purity.

The country needs a way out of the current contradiction: millions of people are socially present but legally absent. Carens is right that time makes deportation increasingly unjust. The task now is to translate that insight into a system that is proportional, humane, and honest about both membership and misconduct.

  3. 03The law must distinguish between survival-based document fraud and identity theft that causes actual financial harm to others.
  4. 04A two-tier enforcement system should be established where the state acts predictably on recent arrivals while granting eligibility for legalization to long-settled residents.

Vanilla GPT

"While the passage of time creates a moral claim to residency, a just immigration policy must balance amnesty for long-settled migrants with a civic bargain that includes earned legalization and institutional reforms to maintain public order."

Joseph Carens is right about the central moral fact: time changes the meaning of illegal presence. A person who crossed a border last month and a person who has worked, paid taxes, raised children, and joined a church or neighborhood for fifteen years are not similarly situated. The law may describe both as removable, but liberal democracies should not pretend that removal imposes the same cost in both cases. Deporting the long-settled is not merely enforcing a rule; it is destroying a life that our society has, in practice, helped make.

That insight is especially compelling for children. Young people brought here by parents did not choose to violate immigration law, and many know no other country in any meaningful sense. To send a high-school graduate “back” to a place whose language she barely reads is not punishment of lawbreaking; it is punishment of childhood dependence. The case for something like the DREAM Act is therefore not a sentimental exception to immigration law but a recognition of membership. The same is broadly true of spouses and parents of citizens, where deportation often punishes the citizen family as much as the noncitizen.

Still, Carens’s proposal of a regular legal entitlement after five to seven years seems too quick to dismiss the institutional problem that amnesty creates. The objection is not simply that legalization “rewards lawbreaking.” Laws often incorporate mercy, limitation periods, and equitable adjustment. The harder problem is that immigration law is a system of expectations. Millions abroad are excluded not because they lack ambition or need, but because the United States has chosen numerical limits, family preferences, labor categories, and refugee rules. If unauthorized residence itself becomes the most reliable path to permanent residence, the system will appear arbitrary even to those who favor generous immigration.

The American experience after the 1986 Immigration Reform and Control Act is relevant. That law legalized roughly 2.7 million people and also promised employer sanctions to deter future unauthorized hiring. The legalization was humane and, for many families, necessary. But the enforcement side was weak, forged documents were common, and the economy continued to demand low-wage labor. The result was not a settled settlement. By 2009 the unauthorized population is again estimated at around eleven or twelve million. This does not prove that legalization caused the new migration; wage gaps, family networks, and labor demand matter more. But it does show that amnesty without functioning institutions invites repetition and political backlash.

For that reason, the moral case for legalization should be joined to a civic bargain. Long-settled unauthorized immigrants should be allowed to come forward, pass criminal-background checks, pay reasonable fines and back taxes where owed, learn English, and receive provisional status leading eventually to permanent residence. The requirements should be real but not designed to fail. They should distinguish ordinary immigration violations from serious crime, and they should not criminalize the necessities of work and family life. But the bargain should also include credible workplace enforcement focused on employers, not spectacular raids that tear apart communities while leaving labor markets unchanged.

Equally important, the United States needs legal channels that correspond to its actual labor needs. It is hypocritical to condemn unauthorized workers while maintaining an economy that quietly absorbs them in agriculture, construction, food processing, child care, restaurants, and hotels. If there is no realistic legal route for needed lesser-skilled workers, then “getting in line” is mostly a slogan. A reformed system should include regulated labor visas with enforceable wages and mobility between employers, so that legality does not become a new instrument of exploitation.

Carens’s deepest contribution is to remind us that sovereignty is not the only value at stake. A border may be lawfully defended, but the people who pass it do not remain strangers forever. Yet membership also requires a public order that citizens and immigrants can regard as fair. The right policy is therefore neither mass deportation nor unconditional rolling amnesty. It is earned legalization for the settled, immediate protection for those brought here as children, family unity where citizens are at stake, and a future immigration system honest enough to reduce the production of illegality.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Deporting long-settled migrants and those brought as children is morally indefensible because it destroys lives that have become inextricably woven into the fabric of society.
  2. 02An unconditional rolling amnesty risks undermining the integrity of the immigration system and creating a political backlash if it is not accompanied by functioning enforcement institutions.
  3. 03Legalization should be framed as a civic bargain where migrants earn their status by meeting requirements like background checks, paying fines, and learning English.
  4. 04The state must implement credible workplace enforcement targeting employers rather than conducting community-disrupting raids on workers.
  5. 05To prevent the future production of illegality, the United States must create realistic legal channels for lesser-skilled labor that correspond to actual economic needs.
Diversified DeepSeek

"Instead of an automatic legal right to stay based solely on the passage of time, liberal democracies should adopt a pragmatic, flexible system of provisional legal status that allows irregular migrants to earn permanent residency through work and integration."

I approach Joseph Carens’s essay from a perspective that might be called humane realism. I share his conviction that it is wrong to deport people who have built their lives in a community over many years, but I think his solution—an automatic legal right to remain after a fixed period—is both politically unattainable and, in its own terms, too rigid. What we need is not a statutory straightjacket but a pragmatic, flexible approach that combines a one-time amnesty for the existing population with a new, robust system of provisional legal status for future migrants who are already here or who may come, allowing them to earn permanent residence through work and integration over time.

Carens’s central insight—that time creates a moral claim—is best realized, not by trying to pinpoint a single point after which deportation is always wrong, but by creating a ladder of legal statuses that reflect the reality of human mobility. The current binary—legal or illegal—is the source of much of the injustice Carens identifies. A person who has lived here for three years and built a life is not, in any meaningful sense, the same as a person who crossed the border yesterday. Yet under current law, both are equally deportable. Instead of waiting for a magic threshold at year five, we should grant provisional legal status to any migrant who can show that he or she has been present and employed for, say, two years, and has not committed serious crimes. This status would not be permanent immediately; it would be converted to permanent residency after an additional period, perhaps three to five years, during which the migrant demonstrates continued employment, payment of taxes, and integration—including learning English. No one would be deported simply for an immigration violation once they have established a foot in the community, but neither would they leap from irregular to permanent overnight.

This approach captures Carens’s moral intuition that the passage of time and the building of ties matter, while also addressing the legitimate concerns of those who worry about incentivizing future flows. A provisional status program, combined with effective workplace enforcement, would make it far more attractive for future migrants to enter through legal channels—an expanded temporary worker program, for instance—rather than to come illegally and wait in the shadows. If the choice is between crossing the desert and entering through a legal guest worker program that provides a clear path to permanent status, most will choose the legal route. That is the lesson of the European guest worker programs, which, though they had flaws, demonstrated that large-scale labor migration can be managed through legal frameworks if those frameworks are designed with human beings in mind.

Carens’s wariness of discretion is understandable, but a provisional status system need not be arbitrary. The criteria can be objective: biometric work authorization cards, employer verification, and regular check-ins. The current hodgepodge of enforcement through raids and I-9 audits is enormously wasteful and cruel; a provisional status system would redirect resources toward ensuring that everyone in the labor market is authorized and that employers follow the rules. This would do far more to protect native workers and legal immigrants than the current practice of tolerating a vast underground workforce while occasionally making examples of some through deportation.

The biggest advantage of this pragmatic approach is political. Carens’s automatic amnesty after five years, no matter how philosophically sound, would be dead on arrival in Congress. The American public, while sympathetic to the plight of long-settled migrants, is deeply opposed to anything that looks like an open-ended entitlement to legalization. A provisional status system, by contrast, can be framed as a “tough but fair” compromise: we will not deport the father of three who has been working here for years, but we will require him to come forward, register, pay a fine, and earn his way to a green card. This is, in fact, the model proposed in various comprehensive immigration bills, including the 2007 Senate bill that, though it failed, garnered significant bipartisan support. Carens’s proposal, by foreclosing fines and probationary periods, would actually be less generous than what even the most liberal Senators were willing to countenance.

In sum, I endorse Carens’s goal but propose a different means. We should give irregular migrants a way up, not out. That means rejecting both the harsh enforcement-only approach and the magical thinking that time alone creates a unilateral right to stay. Instead, let us build an immigration system with multiple rungs on the ladder, so that those who work, pay taxes, and integrate can climb their way into full membership at a pace that reflects their actual contributions. Such a system would be not only more humane than the current mess, but also more sustainable than an amnesty that tries to settle the question with one bold stroke. Let us aim for an immigration policy that is as realistic about human incentives as it is respectful of human dignity.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A ladder of legal statuses better reflects the reality of human mobility than a binary legal/illegal distinction or a single fixed time threshold.
  2. 02A provisional status system combined with robust workplace enforcement would incentivize legal migration over unauthorized entry more effectively than a simple amnesty.
  3. 03Concerns about administrative discretion can be mitigated by using objective criteria such as biometric work authorization and employer verification.
  4. 04A 'tough but fair' earned-legalization model is more politically viable in a democratic context than an open-ended entitlement to amnesty.
  5. 05Requiring migrants to demonstrate ongoing contributions like paying taxes and learning English ensures that legal status reflects actual social integration.
Diversified Gemini

"Legal status for irregular migrants should be granted through a rigorous earned pathway of civic integration rather than an automatic transition based solely on the passage of time."

Joseph Carens makes a compelling case that time spent living in a society generates a moral claim to membership. However, his reliance on the mere passage of time as the sole metric for belonging fundamentally shortchanges the concept of civic integration. By proposing an "automatic transition" to legal status based purely on the calendar, Carens strips the idea of citizenship and permanent residency of its civic responsibilities, reducing membership to mere physical presence.

A liberal democracy is not a hotel where extended stay entitles you to an ownership stake; it is a political community bound together by shared values, language, and civic participation. When we consider bringing millions of irregular migrants out of the shadows, the goal should not simply be to end their vulnerability to deportation, but to integrate them fully into the fabric of the republic. This requires more than just surviving undetected for five or seven years.

Carens dismisses the idea of evaluating an individual's actual ties and capacities, arguing it would be administratively burdensome and potentially discriminatory. But by advocating for a blanket, time-based amnesty, he asks the host society to accept new permanent members without any reciprocal commitment from the migrants themselves. This is politically untenable and socially corrosive.

If we are to grant legal status to those who violated our laws to enter, that status must be earned through active civic engagement, not passively accrued. We should institute a rigorous "earned pathway" to legal status. Yes, time in the country matters, but it must be coupled with concrete steps demonstrating a commitment to the host nation. Migrants should be required to learn the national language, pay back taxes, pass criminal background checks, and demonstrate an understanding of the country's history and civic institutions.

Language, in particular, is the bedrock of civic cohesion. Without a common language, a society fractures into isolated linguistic enclaves, making democratic deliberation impossible. An amnesty that does not mandate language acquisition does a disservice both to the migrant, who will remain economically marginalized, and to the broader society.

Carens worries about the arbitrary power of bureaucrats judging "depth of membership." But requiring a civics and language test is not arbitrary; it is the standard we already apply to legal immigrants seeking naturalization. Why should irregular migrants be held to a lower standard?

Furthermore, Carens’s automatic transition ignores the vital role of the community's consent. Amnesty is a deeply controversial policy that strains the social trust of the native-born population. To rebuild that trust, the public needs to see that those being granted status are making a visible effort to become part of the civic whole. A process that demands English proficiency, payment of fines for the initial lawbreaking, and civic education transforms the migrant from a "lawbreaker" into an active participant earning their place.

Time is a necessary condition for belonging, but it is not a sufficient one. We should embrace the regularization of undocumented immigrants, but we must do so in a way that strengthens, rather than dilutes, our civic bonds.

  3. 03Integration requires concrete requirements such as learning the national language, paying back taxes, and passing criminal background checks to ensure migrants can participate fully in the republic.
  4. 04A common language is essential for civic cohesion and democratic deliberation, preventing the formation of isolated linguistic enclaves.
  5. 05Requiring civics and language tests is a fair and non-arbitrary standard already applied to legal immigrants seeking naturalization.
  6. 06An earned pathway helps rebuild social trust with the native-born population by demonstrating that migrants are making a visible effort to join the civic whole.
