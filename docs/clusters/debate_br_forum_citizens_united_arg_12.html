"While Lawrence Lessig correctly identifies the problem of 'dependency corruption,' his argument for restricting political speech relies on unproven empirical claims about public trust and legislative behavior that do not justify broad judicial deference to campaign finance regulations."

Lawrence Lessig’s essay rests on a proposition that sounds obvious but deserves more scrutiny: that campaign money has weakened public trust in Congress and impaired Congress’s ability to do its work. I share much of his distaste for the fundraising culture of Washington, and I do not doubt that money buys access. But before building a constitutional theory around “institutional corruption,” we should be more careful about the empirical claims doing the work. Low trust in government may have many causes, and campaign finance may be less central than reformers assume.

Start with trust. Americans’ confidence in Congress is indeed dismal. But it has been shaped by Vietnam, Watergate, stagflation, the Iran hostage crisis, recession, deficits, impeachment, the Iraq War, Hurricane Katrina, the financial crisis, bailouts, unemployment, and partisan polarization. In 2010, citizens are angry for concrete reasons: the economy is weak, the unemployment rate is high, the housing market remains damaged, and both parties have supported unpopular rescue measures. It is not surprising that trust is low. To attribute this primarily to campaign finance risks mistaking a background condition for the main cause.

Moreover, Americans often distrust Congress while liking their own representatives. They condemn “special interests” in the abstract but ask their members to bring home benefits, protect local industries, and attend to organized constituencies. A representative who supports dairy programs, sugar protections, defense contracts, or used-car dealer exemptions may be responding not only to campaign contributors but to employers, workers, local businesses, and voters who care intensely. What looks like corruption from a national policy perspective may look like representation from a district perspective.

This does not mean that money is irrelevant. It means causation is complicated. Lessig cites examples in which members appear to betray ideological commitments under pressure from contributors. But anecdotes cannot establish the magnitude of the effect. Legislators’ votes are strongly predicted by party, ideology, constituency, region, and interest-group alignment. Contributions often follow expected votes rather than cause them. A pro-business PAC gives to a pro-business candidate; a labor union gives to a pro-labor candidate. When the candidate later votes accordingly, is that dependence or agreement? The answer varies by case, and constitutional doctrine should not rest on assuming the worst.

Independent expenditures are even more difficult to assess. Campaign consultants believe advertising matters, and it surely can matter at the margins. But American voters are not blank slates. Party identification, economic conditions, candidate quality, incumbency, scandals, and national mood often dominate advertising effects. A flood of outside spending may help define an unknown candidate, but it can also backfire if voters perceive manipulation. The relationship between money and electoral outcomes is real but not linear. Wealthy self-financed candidates often lose. Better-funded candidates often win partly because donors can identify viable candidates, not because money mechanically purchases votes.

Lessig’s “iceberg theory” of threatened expenditures is intriguing, but it too needs evidence. A credible threat to spend against a candidate may influence behavior, but how often does this happen, and how decisive is it compared with other threats—party opposition, activist anger, editorial criticism, primary challenges, presidential pressure? Politics is filled with potential retaliation. Environmental groups threaten scorecards; anti-tax groups threaten pledges; unions threaten mobilization; newspapers threaten hostile coverage; parties threaten committee assignments. The fact that threats exist does not tell us which ones dominate or how law should respond.

This is where I find the Lochner analogy strained. Lessig argues that Justice Kennedy made an unsupported factual assertion when he wrote that the appearance of influence or access will not cause the electorate to lose faith in democracy. Perhaps so. But reformers also make broad factual assertions: that independent corporate expenditures will meaningfully increase corruption, that disclosure cannot work, that public financing will restore trust, that dependency on contributors explains legislative dysfunction. These claims may be plausible, but they are not self-evident. If courts should not rely on armchair sociology, neither should legislatures be granted unlimited deference when restricting core political speech.

A prudent First Amendment doctrine must account for uncertainty. Political speech is not like bakery hours. When government restricts electoral advocacy, the danger of self-dealing is acute. Legislators may sincerely believe they are protecting democracy while also protecting themselves. Incumbents know which speakers are troublesome. They can write rules that burden challengers, outsider groups, and last-minute criticism. The fact that Congress held hearings and compiled evidence does not eliminate the conflict of interest. Judicial skepticism is not Lochnerism in the pejorative sense; it is a recognition that the regulated activity is criticism of the regulators.

Still, Citizens United may have gone too far in declaring independent expenditures incapable of corruption. The world is not so clean. Independence is a legal category that can be manipulated, and gratitude toward outside spenders is predictable. The Court could have acknowledged a broader anti-corruption interest while requiring narrow tailoring and strong evidence. Instead it adopted language that may disable sensible regulation. Lessig is right to object to that rigidity.

But the practical response should be experimental rather than absolutist. We should test reforms that do not suppress speech: real-time disclosure, better databases, clear identification in advertisements, stronger enforcement of coordination rules, shareholder approval for corporate political spending, and voluntary public financing. States can serve as laboratories. If small-donor matching systems increase competition, reduce fundraising time, and improve trust, that evidence will strengthen the case for broader adoption. If they mainly subsidize candidates who would have run anyway or empower ideological extremes, we should know that too.

Public financing deserves particular scrutiny. Reformers often assume that small donors are more representative than large donors. Sometimes they are; often they are not. Small donors may be highly ideological, affluent enough to give, and nationally mobilized by polarizing appeals. Internet fundraising can reward outrage and purity tests. A candidate dependent on thousands of small donors outside the district may still be dependent on non-voters. Democracy vouchers might broaden participation, but only if citizens actually use them. Otherwise politically attentive groups will harvest vouchers and reproduce existing inequalities in another form.

Trust may also fail to improve even after reform. The public’s belief that “money buys results” is part of a broader suspicion of institutions—banks, parties, media, corporations, unions, and government. If Congress adopted public financing tomorrow, citizens might still see lobbying, revolving-door employment, think-tank influence, bureaucratic capture, and partisan gamesmanship. They might conclude that corruption has merely changed channels. Reformers should not promise civic renewal from campaign-finance reform alone.

The better case for reform is more modest: private fundraising consumes legislators’ time, creates access inequalities, and sometimes distorts priorities. These are real costs even if they are not the master explanation for democratic decline. A system that gives candidates incentives to seek many small contributions from constituents is preferable to one that rewards dialing for large checks. But we should pursue it as institutional improvement, not as a constitutional crusade grounded in uncertain claims about public trust.

Citizens United has provoked a panic that may cloud judgment. Corporate independent expenditures may increase; they may also be tempered by reputational risk. Many corporations will hesitate to alienate customers, employees, or shareholders by overtly backing candidates. Trade associations and nonprofits may become more active, but they were already influential. Unions, wealthy individuals, ideological groups, and media corporations remain major players. The post-decision world may be worse, but it will not be wholly new.

Lessig has done an important service by naming dependency corruption. But naming a phenomenon is not measuring it. Before we ask courts to permit restrictions on political speech in the name of institutional integrity, we need clearer evidence about mechanisms, magnitudes, and alternatives. Democracy is harmed by money, but it is also harmed by overconfident reform. The challenge after Citizens United is to improve the campaign-finance system without exaggerating what we know or empowering government to regulate political advocacy on the basis of intuitions no firmer than those we criticize in Justice Kennedy.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Low public trust in Congress is likely driven by major historical and economic events rather than being primarily caused by the influence of campaign money.
  2. 02What appears to be corruption from a national perspective is often a legitimate form of local representation where legislators respond to the intense interests of their own constituents.
  3. 03Legislative voting patterns are more strongly predicted by party, ideology, and constituency than by campaign contributions, which often follow rather than cause political alignment.
  4. 04The 'iceberg theory' of threatened expenditures lacks sufficient evidence to prove that such threats dominate other common forms of political pressure and retaliation.
  5. 05Judicial skepticism of campaign finance laws is a necessary safeguard against incumbents who may use 'reform' to suppress challengers and protect their own power.
  6. 06Reform efforts should focus on experimental, non-speech-restricting measures like small-donor matching and transparency rather than constitutional theories that empower the government to regulate political advocacy.
