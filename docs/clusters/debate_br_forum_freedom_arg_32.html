Diversified DeepSeek

"While Aziz Rana correctly identifies the need for large-scale institution building to reclaim freedom, a truly transformative left project must also prioritize the small-scale, everyday practices of collective agency and the patient interpersonal work of coalition-building."

I read Aziz Rana’s essay as a kind of map. It traces the enormous territory of American freedom—its settler origins, its imperial wages, its twisted capture by a right that gives it a sneer and a fist. The map is painstaking, and I am grateful for it, because I need to know the terrain I am trying to cross. I am an organizer in my late twenties, and I spend my days in the very spaces Rana names: the underfunded community center, the Amazon warehouse parking lot where we try to talk to workers, the crowded Zoom call where we debate the language of a press statement. For me, the question “What does freedom mean?” is not academic. It is the question that keeps me awake after a tough meeting, the one posed by a skeptical co-worker who says “Freedom to me is being left alone.” Rana’s essay offers a coherent, historical answer. But the gap between that answer and the felt reality of organizing is where the real work lives.

Rana says that solidaristic freedom can seem “theoretical and abstract” because there are so few sites in American life where collective agency is a meaningful reality. He is right. But I would go further: even in those sites where we are trying to build that agency, the experience can still feel abstract, because we are so often fighting for things that feel far away—a bill in Congress, a union election two years down the line, a shift in public opinion measured in poll points. The people I work with are not abstract; they are exhausted. They want their rent to stop rising, their manager to stop screaming, their aunt to get insulin. The link between that immediate need and the grand vision of “inclusive solidarity” can feel like a leap of faith that only the already-converted are willing to make.

And yet, I do not think the answer is to abandon the word freedom or to wait for some perfect institutional transformation. The surprising thing I have learned is that freedom becomes real in the small, stubborn acts of refusing to be alone with your problems. When a group of workers in an Alabama warehouse start a group chat to track unfair write-ups, that is a tiny institution of collective self-rule. When a tenants’ union in my city holds a building-wide potluck to plan a rent strike, they are experiencing what it means to have power together, even if the legal structure is stacked against them. These moments are fleeting, often crushed by retaliation or simply by exhaustion. But they are not negligible. They are the micro-institutions that a left freedom project must learn to see, nurture, and replicate. Rana’s call for large-scale institution-building through legislation and reformed labor law is essential, but it must be paired with an equal commitment to these everyday, almost invisible forms of collective practice. Without them, the big institutions risk being hollow shells, run by professionals for a demobilized base.

I also feel a tension Rana acknowledges but perhaps does not sit with long enough: the tension between the radical universalism he wants and the way actual movement energy today arises from specific, often defensive, struggles of marginalized groups. The most powerful invocations of freedom I have witnessed have come from trans youth fighting “Don’t Say Gay” bills, from Indigenous water protectors at Standing Rock, from Black-led protests against police murder. These movements often speak in the language of survival and bodily autonomy, not always in the register of “shared class solidarity.” They do not always see themselves as part of a broad anti-imperial project, even when they are challenging the carceral state or environmental destruction. Rana is correct that these are, in fact, expressions of a deeper solidaristic vision. But the translation work—connecting the specific fight to a universal project of economic and political democracy—is genuinely difficult, and it cannot be accomplished just by building large institutions. It requires the patient, interpersonal work of coalition-building, where trust is forged not through ideological agreement but through shared action over time.

The hardest part of Rana’s argument for me is his insistence on the global dimensions of freedom. I know, intellectually, that my relative comfort as a U.S. citizen is built on an empire that impoverishes others. I know that the garment workers in Bangladesh and the farmers in Honduras are my allies in some profound sense. But in the daily grind of a local campaign, these connections can feel impossibly distant. The left’s anti-imperialism often manifests as a series of statements—condemning this war, that intervention—that feel disconnected from the fight for a city ordinance on fair scheduling. Bridging that gap requires not just a reallocation of the defense budget, as Rana proposes, but a new kind of organizing that deliberately weaves local demands into a transnational fabric. I have seen glimpses of it: domestic workers’ groups building alliances with migrant domestic worker organizations in the Philippines, climate justice activists linking pipeline fights to Indigenous sovereignty across borders. These are fragile, under-resourced, and stunningly beautiful. They are the seeds of an anti-imperial freedom that does not just critique from above but grows from below.

Rana’s conclusion—that there is no end-run around the hard work of movement and coalition building—is the truest thing in the essay. But I would add this: that hard work must include a kind of spiritual or emotional practice, too. We have to help each other believe that freedom is possible, that collective power isn’t just a tool but a way of becoming fully human. The right’s freedom is seductive because it offers a quick-hit satisfaction of grievance and identity. The left’s freedom is harder, because it demands that we give up the illusion of self-sufficiency and embrace the messy, sometimes infuriating, dependence on others. It is a freedom that can only be practiced, not just proclaimed. And it is being practiced, in ways that are rarely visible to the pundits and the academics. I see it when a group of workers, after a long day, still shows up to a meeting to support a fired colleague. I see it when a neighborhood comes together to oppose an eviction despite their differences. That is the freedom Rana is talking about, and it is not a theory. It is a seed pushing through concrete, and our job is to water it with whatever we have.
