"While behavioral economics interventions in the developing world may improve measurable outcomes, they raise serious ethical concerns regarding paternalism, the erosion of agency, and the unequal power dynamics between technocratic experts and the poor."

“Small Changes, Big Results” makes a compelling case that tweaking the way options are presented can dramatically alter the choices of the poor. But what the essay never pauses to ask is: should it? The ethical dimension is almost entirely absent from Glennerster and Kremer’s brisk, technocratic prose. They assume that because an intervention improves some measurable outcome, it is unambiguously desirable. Yet the history of social engineering should give us pause. When we start systematically designing environments to steer the poor toward what we consider better decisions, we tread on morally complex ground. The poor are not lab rats, and good intentions do not inoculate against paternalism’s historical legacy of condescension and control.

Consider the language. The essay is filled with terms like “procrastination,” “fail to save,” “not optimal for them or for society.” The poor are depicted as errant consumers who need to be nudged past their own psychological shortcomings. The parallel drawn with American 401(k) plans is revealing. In rich countries, behavioral nudges are often justified under the banner of “libertarian paternalism”—they guide choices while preserving freedom. But in the developing-world context, where the state is often weak, where NGOs wield significant power over communities, and where many supposed beneficiaries have limited ability to refuse or opt out, the libertarian glaze of “nudge” wears thin. When an NGO places a chlorine dispenser at the only water source, is that really a free choice? When a mother receives lentils only if she immunizes her child, the line between incentive and conditionality blurs.

The authors approvingly note that small fees for preventative care dramatically reduce adoption, and therefore they should be abolished. I don’t necessarily disagree with free distribution of mosquito nets, but the reasoning prompts discomfort. It implies that the poor cannot weigh costs and benefits correctly, and that we know what is best for them. But what if some households decline chlorine because they find the taste unpleasant? What if they believe that water from a certain spring has spiritual properties and must not be tampered with? The essay dismisses the “if you don’t pay for it, you won’t value it” hypothesis with a quick reference to evidence, but it does not engage with the possibility that paying for something might be a meaningful act of agency, a way of expressing resourcefulness rather than passive receipt. Not all cultures share the view that free things are good; some see them as charity that undermines self-respect. The behavioral approach, by focusing on uptake rates, flattens these varied meanings into a single metric.

There is also a risk of mission creep. Once we accept that it is legitimate to use psychological insights to increase immunization rates, why not also nudge the poor to eat differently, migrate less, have fewer children, or adopt whatever behavior experts deem rational? The same behavioral science that gave us the chlorine dispenser could easily inform more intrusive programs. And because these interventions work below the level of conscious reflection, they bypass democratic deliberation. Citizens in poor countries rarely have a say in whether their behaviors are targeted for manipulation by well-meaning outsiders. The essay’s call to “harness these behaviors to improve people’s lives” sounds benign, but history echoes with similar phrases from colonial administrators and missionary reformers.

The problem is compounded by the profound inequality of knowledge and power between the nudgers and the nudged. The researchers and NGO workers who design these interventions typically come from elite institutions in rich countries. They may consult local communities, but the RCT logic itself—with its emphasis on randomization, informed consent for the study, and statistical significance—tends to treat the poor as passive subjects of an experiment. Even when the program is successful, the implicit message is: “We understood your psychology better than you do, and we fixed you.” This is a toxic foundation for genuine partnership.

I am not calling for an end to all behavioral programs. But any serious application of nudges in development must be accompanied by a robust ethical framework. We need to ask not just “Does it work?” but “Would the intended beneficiaries choose this nudge if they understood it and had the power to reject it?” That is the acid test of respect. In some cases, the answer may well be yes: providing free bed nets is not the same as forcing people to use them, and offering lentils for immunization is an incentive, not a command. But the burden of proof should be on the nudgers to demonstrate consent and to explore alternative, non-manipulative ways of achieving the same goals—such as unconditional cash transfers that let the poor set their own priorities. Until the ethics catches up with the evidence, “Small Changes, Big Results” leaves a faint but unmistakable whiff of benevolent control.
