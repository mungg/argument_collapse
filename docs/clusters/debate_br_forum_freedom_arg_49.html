"While Aziz Rana is correct that the left must prioritize institution building over messaging to reclaim freedom, his argument underestimates the extent to which existing political and media structures actively prevent such institutional transformation."

The most provocative claim in Aziz Rana's essay is that the left's primary task is "institution building, reshaping the everyday worlds people inhabit" rather than communication strategy. I want to examine this claim through the lens of recent political history, particularly the Obama and Sanders movements.

The Obama coalition was built on a rhetorical mastery of American political language, including the language of freedom. "Hope" and "change" were the operative terms, and they were deployed with extraordinary skill. But the institutional achievements of the Obama presidency were modest: the Affordable Care Act, which helped millions but left the fundamental structure of American healthcare intact; Dodd-Frank, which regulated banks less than advocates wanted; a failed push for immigration reform. The institutional base of progressive politics was not strengthened; if anything, it continued to erode.

Sanders represents a different approach: a candidate who consistently invoked economic justice and explicitly socialist language, building a movement around Medicare for All, free college tuition, and labor rights. His success in terms of vote totals and movement-building was remarkable. But his institutional achievements are even more negligible than Obama's. Sanders has not passed significant legislation; his influence has been primarily on the Overton window of acceptable political discourse.

Both cases suggest something troubling: neither better messaging nor more radical vision seems to translate readily into institutional change. The political system appears to be structured in ways that make transformation extremely difficult, regardless of the ideas in circulation.

Rana's emphasis on institution building is correct, then, but the essay understates the degree to which the institutions themselves—the Senate filibuster, the Electoral College, the judiciary, the corporate media—operate as guardians of the status quo. We cannot build new institutions without confronting the existing ones that protect the current distribution of power. This is a harder task than the essay sometimes implies.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Obama presidency demonstrates that even masterful rhetorical use of the language of freedom fails to strengthen the institutional base of progressive politics.
  2. 02The Sanders movement shows that shifting the discourse toward radical economic justice does not necessarily translate into tangible legislative or institutional achievements.
  3. 03The current political system is fundamentally structured to protect the status quo, making the task of building new institutions much more difficult than simply shifting focus away from communication strategy.
  4. 04Meaningful institutional change requires a direct confrontation with entrenched 'guardians of the status quo' like the Senate filibuster, the judiciary, and corporate media.
