"The current crisis of democracy is not a general failure of representation or civic virtue, but a structural class war where economic power has been weaponized to ensure political outcomes favor the wealthy over the general public."

For all its historical sweep, Ira Katznelson’s anxious meditation on democracy treads too lightly around the elephant in the chamber: the weaponized conversion of economic power into political domination. He invokes Claus Offe’s “principle of non-convertibility” with approval, but then treats the violation of that principle as one symptom among many—a partisan irritant, a money-in-politics problem. The reality is starker. When policy outcomes across decades systematically align with the preferences of the wealthy and organized business interests while the views of the average citizen have near-zero independent impact, we are not seeing a democracy “distorted by unequal access.” We are seeing a class war waged under a democratic façade. The anxieties Katznelson diagnoses are not a crisis of representation in the abstract; they are the entirely predictable result of four decades of upward redistribution under neoliberal governance.

The landmark 2014 study by Martin Gilens and Benjamin Page is indispensable here. Analyzing nearly two thousand survey questions between 1981 and 2002, they found that economic elites and business interests exert strong influence over U.S. policy, while mass-based interest groups and average citizens have little or none. When majorities of the affluent disagree with the middle class or the poor, the affluent win overwhelmingly. This is not about Citizens United or a few billionaires cutting checks; it is structural. A political economy that has produced staggering wealth concentration—the top 0.1% now hold nearly as much wealth as the bottom 90%—has simultaneously hollowed out unions, eviscerated campaign finance equity, and built a lobbying leviathan that writes legislation. Under these conditions, the “cacophony of partisan argument” and “arcane rules of procedure” that Katznelson laments are not bugs; they are features that protect the status quo from popular demands for redistribution.

Katznelson’s historical touchstones further reveal the blind spot. He praises the New Deal for saving democracy, but the New Deal was a class compromise extracted by massive working-class mobilization and militant labor action, not a gift from enlightened legislators. Its achievements—Social Security, the Wagner Act, progressive taxation—directly attacked the principle of non-convertibility by building countervailing power. Today, that power has been systematically dismantled. The result is a politics in which legislators respond not to the broad public but to a donor class that fundamentally fears a truly egalitarian democracy. When workers’ share of national income stagnates and corporate profits soar, ordinary people rightly sense that the rules are rigged. That is not cynicism; it is accurate perception.

The turn toward plebiscitary, populist, and deliberative alternatives that Katznelson fears is a direct response to this capture. Tea Party anger and Occupy encampments are not cries for authoritarianism; they are desperate attempts to break a system in which voting seems to change nothing. If liberal democracy is to survive, the prescription is not a revival of civic virtue or a new background consensus that papers over class conflict. It is a full-scale assault on the institutional mechanisms that allow economic power to translate seamlessly into political power. That means public financing of elections, a constitutional amendment to overturn Citizens United (which already enjoys broad popular support), a reinvigorated labor movement, and a reassertion of the regulatory state’s capacity to discipline capital. Katznelson asks whether democracies can live without a singular sense of public interest. The more urgent question is whether they can survive when the public interest is defined by a tiny minority at everyone else’s expense. Until we answer that honestly, our anxiety will be entirely warranted.

  5. 05Saving democracy requires a direct assault on the mechanisms of economic capture, including public election financing, overturning Citizens United, and reinvigorating the labor movement.
