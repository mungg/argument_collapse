"While American campuses are not total conformists, there is a troubling trend of students and faculty using institutional power to shield themselves from intellectual discomfort and moral disagreement."

American campuses are not gulags of conformity, but they are showing a troubling habit: turning moral disagreement into a demand for institutional protection. The recent campaigns against commencement speakers—Condoleezza Rice at Rutgers, Christine Lagarde at Smith, Robert Birgeneau at Haverford, and Ayaan Hirsi Ali at Brandeis—were not merely arguments that these figures were wrong. Too often they became arguments that honoring or hearing them would make the campus complicit in injury.

Students have every right to protest speakers and criticize honorary degrees. A university need not pretend that all public lives are equally admirable. But protest crosses into intellectual narrowing when it aims to make exposure itself illegitimate. Commencement is ceremonial, not a seminar, yet universities should be wary of teaching students that the proper answer to objectionable ideas is administrative cancellation.

Trigger warnings raise a different but related issue. In limited cases—graphic material involving rape, torture or war—they can be a humane pedagogical tool, much like noting that a film contains disturbing images. But if warnings become mandatory labels for “troubling” works, they risk implying that discomfort is a harm from which education should shield students. Much great literature and history is unsettling because human beings have done unsettling things.

This is not only a liberal problem. Conservative orthodoxy has long tried to police campuses through attacks on professors, pressure over Middle East politics, suspicion of ethnic and gender studies, or efforts to restrict the teaching of evolution and climate science. The common danger is not left or right but certainty armed with institutional power.

Universities should protect protest, but also protect the conditions that make protest meaningful: open inquiry, difficult texts, unpopular speakers and the discipline of answering argument with argument.

"The mission of universities as spaces for intellectual contestation is being undermined by a bipartisan tendency to treat uncomfortable speech as an injury that must be removed rather than answered."

Universities are supposed to be the place where uncomfortable ideas get aired, contested, and tested. That mission is harder to fulfill when students treat discomfort itself as an injury and when administrators, eager to avoid controversy, accommodate them. The recent cascade of disinvitations—Condoleezza Rice at Rutgers, Christine Lagarde at Smith, Robert Birgeneau at Haverford—suggests that a vocal faction now views the conferral of a podium as an endorsement requiring ideological purity. That is a striking inversion of what a commencement, or a classroom, is for.

Still, it would be wrong to frame this as a uniquely "liberal" failure. Conservative orthodoxy has done its share of stifling: state legislatures policing what can be said about climate change or evolution, donors leaning on trustees, the long campaign to brand criticism of Israel on campus as antisemitism, and the ritual outrage over any professor who says something impolitic on Twitter. The pressures come from both directions, and they meet in the middle on a shared assumption—that speech one finds wrong should be removed rather than answered.

Trigger warnings are a subtler case. A professor flagging that a novel depicts sexual violence is courteous, not censorious; the worry is when warnings harden into a right of avoidance, or when syllabi are pruned to forestall complaint. The distinction matters. The proper response to troubling material is engagement with it, ideally guided by someone who knows the material well. Colleges that forget this are no longer colleges in any serious sense.

"The primary threat to intellectual life on campus is an emerging culture that prioritizes emotional safety and ideological purity over the educational necessity of engaging with challenging or uncomfortable ideas."

The recent wave of trigger-warning requests and commencement-speaker protests has sparked a necessary debate, but framing it as a simple clash of liberal versus conservative orthodoxy misses a deeper problem. The real threat to intellectual life is not one ideology but an emerging campus culture that treats emotional safety as a prerequisite for learning, and political purity as a prerequisite for honor.

Trigger warnings began as a trauma-informed accommodation, rooted in clinical understanding of PTSD. When applied to graphic depictions of sexual violence, for example, they allow survivors to engage with difficult material rather than avoid it. Yet the concept has rapidly expanded. Students now request warnings for classic literature, films, and even historical texts that make them uncomfortable. This shifts the educational aim from grappling with discomfort to sidestepping it. A well-meaning liberal sensitivity can harden into an orthodoxy where any challenging idea is treated as a potential microaggression, and the very act of critical questioning becomes suspect.

The spring’s commencement controversies reveal that this impulse is not limited to one political pole. Students at Rutgers forced Condoleezza Rice to withdraw after protests over the Iraq War, while at Haverford, objections to Robert Birgeneau’s handling of Occupy protests led to a rescinded invitation. In both cases, dissenters judged a speaker’s entire career by a single litmus test—a form of purity politics that conservative student groups have also practiced, though with less media attention. The common thread is an unwillingness to engage with those whose views or records are deemed morally tainted by a vocal segment of the campus.

This dynamic stifles thought precisely because it shrinks the range of permissible discourse. When honor is for the ideologically certified, and warning labels signal that certain ideas are dangerous, the liberal arts’ central mission—to examine messy, contradictory realities—is replaced by an intellectual safe space. The most vital question for universities today is not which orthodoxy is gaining ground, but whether they can reclaim the principle that education means learning to confront, not just to confirm.

The debate over trigger warnings and protest-driven speaker cancellations reflects a genuine tension between competing values that universities must navigate carefully. Students requesting content advisories for works they find distressing are often motivated by legitimate concerns—trauma-informed pedagogy has legitimate psychological grounding, and educators have long understood that student readiness affects learning. However, when such requests become demands that alter curriculum or silence speakers, the balance shifts toward intellectual narrowing.

What concerns me most is the conflation of emotional discomfort with genuine harm. The purpose of higher education is precisely to encounter ideas, texts, and perspectives that challenge and disturb us. This discomfort, while unpleasant, is often where genuine intellectual growth occurs. If we accept that students should be able to opt out of challenging material or veto speakers they find objectionable, we risk creating an educational environment that shields students from precisely the encounters that justify a university education.

That said, the response from critics has sometimes been disproportionate, framing student concerns as merely a generation of hypersensitivity rather than engaging with their substance. There is middle ground: professors can provide context for difficult material without eliminating it; graduation speaker selection can account for community values without becoming a purity test. The solution lies not in uncritical accommodation or dismissiveness, but in honest dialogue about what education is for and what intellectual engagement requires.

If universities continue to prioritize emotional safety over free inquiry, they will fail in their primary duty: preparing students to navigate and engage with a complex, often disagreeable world.

But the dominant pattern in elite American higher education today is leftward, and pretending otherwise out of misplaced even-handedness misses the actual phenomenon. The question is not whether colleges lean liberal—they always have, and that is fine—but whether liberalism on campus has curdled into something allergic to its own foundational commitment to free inquiry. The evidence of this spring suggests it has, and that those who care about the liberal tradition, in both senses, should be the ones most alarmed.

The university should be one of the few places in American life where people learn to argue with the dead, the powerful, the offensive and the wounded all at once. That is why both sides of the current controversy are partly right and partly evasive.

A healthy campus would be neither a safe space nor a gladiatorial arena. "The current debate over trigger warnings and campus expression reflects a fundamental shift in the philosophy of higher education, moving from a model of productive discomfort to one of emotional protection and identity recognition."

We are watching a generational shift in what students think a university is for, and the trigger warning debate is the clearest window onto it.

For most of the postwar period, the liberal arts college presented itself as a place of productive discomfort. You came to be unsettled: to read Nietzsche and feel your faith shake, to read Fanon and feel your nation indicted, to read Nabokov and feel your moral compass spin. The professors were not your therapists; the syllabus was not your safe space. The implicit pedagogy held that growth required exposure to the foreign and the difficult, and that resilience was something you built by encountering material you could not yet handle.

Today's students, or at least the most vocal among them, want something different. They want the university to be continuous with the rest of their lives—a space where their identities are recognized, their wounds acknowledged, their consent solicited before encountering difficult material. There is a real moral seriousness in this. It comes from taking trauma seriously, from recognizing that the old model often worked better for some students (the comfortable, the unmarked) than others.

But it has costs the advocates rarely confront. If "Mrs. Dalloway" requires a warning for suicide, what becomes of the experience of being caught off guard by literature, of having a book do something to you that you did not see coming? If a class on the history of slavery must be prefaced with content notes, what does that imply about a student's capacity to encounter the past on its own terms?
