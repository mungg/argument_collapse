Vanilla GPT

"While Dodd-Frank has significantly improved financial stability by providing regulators with better oversight tools, it has not yet eliminated the systemic risks posed by the size and complexity of major banks."

Dodd-Frank has made the banking system safer, but it has not eliminated its dangers. Nor could any single statute do that. Finance is built around leverage, maturity transformation and confidence; the question is whether the law has reduced the odds that private mistakes become public catastrophes. On that test, the answer is mixed: progress is real, but too-big-to-fail is not gone.

The rejection of the “living wills” of major banks is revealing. On one hand, it is evidence that Dodd-Frank created a process with teeth. Before the crisis, regulators had no credible map for putting a sprawling global bank through bankruptcy without panic. Now the Federal Reserve and F.D.I.C. can demand changes in legal structure, liquidity management and derivatives booking, and eventually impose higher capital or divestitures if banks do not comply. That is not trivial.

On the other hand, the finding also confirms the central worry: years after the crisis, some of the largest institutions still cannot convincingly explain how they would fail safely. JPMorgan Chase and Bank of America are not ordinary companies. Their payment systems, derivatives positions, foreign subsidiaries and short-term funding links make their distress a public problem. If regulators themselves doubt that bankruptcy would work smoothly, markets may still assume that Washington would step in during a panic.

Dodd-Frank’s defenders can point to substantial improvements. Capital requirements are higher. Stress tests have forced banks to hold more loss-absorbing resources and have given supervisors a forward-looking tool. The Volcker Rule, despite its complexity, has restricted some proprietary trading. Derivatives are more often centrally cleared and reported. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau has addressed abuses that helped feed the mortgage bubble. The Orderly Liquidation Authority gives the government a mechanism, outside ordinary bankruptcy, to wind down a failing financial firm without simply bailing out shareholders and executives.

But the law also leaves unresolved problems. Big banks remain extremely large and politically influential. Complexity is itself a source of fragility: a bank that cannot be understood quickly in distress cannot be resolved confidently. Shadow banking risks can migrate outside traditional banks. Central clearinghouses reduce some risks while concentrating others. And regulatory success depends on sustained political will, competent supervision and international cooperation — all of which can weaken over time.

Breaking up the largest banks is not a magic solution; smaller institutions can also fail together if they hold the same bad assets or rely on the same unstable funding. But size and complexity do matter. If a bank is too complicated to fail under bankruptcy, regulators should be willing to force simplification, higher equity funding, and, if necessary, divestitures.

So the honest verdict is: Dodd-Frank has reduced the dangers, exposed some of them, and given regulators better tools. It has not eliminated them. The living-will failures should be treated not as a reason to abandon the law, but as a reason to use it more aggressively.

"While Dodd-Frank has significantly strengthened the financial system's resilience, it has not yet eliminated systemic dangers or the problem of 'too big to fail.'"

The honest answer is: not yet, and perhaps not ever fully — but Dodd-Frank has done more than its critics on both flanks usually admit.

On the positive side, the largest banks now hold roughly twice the high-quality capital they did in 2007, are subject to annual stress tests under CCAR, and must fund themselves with more stable liabilities under the Liquidity Coverage Ratio. The Volcker Rule has curtailed proprietary trading, derivatives are moving toward central clearing, and the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau has reduced the predatory mortgage practices that fed the last crisis. The Financial Stability Oversight Council and the Office of Financial Research, whatever their limitations, at least give regulators a vantage point on systemic risk that did not exist in 2008.

But this week's joint FDIC-Fed rejection of five "living wills" — including those of JPMorgan and Bank of America — is a reminder that the core promise of Title II, an orderly resolution of a megabank without taxpayer support or contagion, remains unproven. If regulators themselves cannot credibly describe how these firms would fail safely, then "too big to fail" is wounded rather than dead. Neel Kashkari, who ran TARP and now leads the Minneapolis Fed, has been saying as much. Meanwhile, much risk-taking has migrated outside the regulated banking perimeter to asset managers, hedge funds, and the repo and money-market plumbing — areas Dodd-Frank addressed only obliquely.

That suggests Sanders and Clinton are framing a real choice imperfectly. Breaking up the biggest banks would simplify resolution but would not by itself tame shadow banking, and it would impose significant transition costs. Defending Dodd-Frank as essentially sufficient understates how much remains contingent on aggressive implementation — capital surcharges, derivatives margining, money-market reform, and tougher living wills.

Dodd-Frank has made the system safer. It has not eliminated the dangers, and the living-wills decision is fresh evidence of that.

Vanilla DeepSeek

"While the Dodd-Frank Act has significantly strengthened the financial system's resilience, it has failed to eliminate the systemic dangers posed by banks that remain too complex and interconnected to fail without taxpayer intervention."

The rejection of five megabanks’ “living wills” by regulators is more than a procedural rebuke; it is a stark admission that, nearly six years after Dodd-Frank’s passage, the core dangers in the banking system have been reduced but not eliminated. The very premise of a living will is that a firm can fail without taxpayer bailouts or systemic chaos. The joint determination by the Federal Reserve and FDIC that these plans are “not credible” confirms that the nation’s largest financial institutions remain, in practice, too complex and interconnected to resolve in an orderly way. If the plans cannot be executed, the implicit government backstop—the too-big-to-fail subsidy—persists.

Dodd-Frank made important strides. Higher capital and liquidity requirements, mandated stress tests, and the Volcker Rule’s restrictions on proprietary trading have forced banks to simplify somewhat and hold larger buffers. The creation of the Orderly Liquidation Authority was meant to provide an alternative to ad-hoc bailouts, shifting the cost of failure onto shareholders and creditors. These measures have undoubtedly strengthened the system against the type of credit and liquidity shocks that triggered the 2008 crisis. Banks today are better capitalized, less leveraged, and hold far more high-quality liquid assets.

But the living-will fiasco exposes the chasm between regulatory intent and reality. To be resolvable, a firm must have a transparent legal structure, separable business lines, and governance that can be unwound quickly. The rejected banks have hundreds of thousands of derivatives contracts booked across thousands of legal entities and operate in dozens of jurisdictions. Their cross-border interdependencies make a single-country resolution fantasy. As long as a single failure could cascade through funding markets—where overnight repurchase agreements and securities lending still create hidden runs—the danger remains. Moreover, Dodd-Frank did not fundamentally alter the business models of the largest dealer banks, which continue to dominate market-making and derivatives intermediation, concentrating risk in a handful of nodes.

The Volcker Rule, though well-intentioned, has proven difficult to enforce, with grey areas around market-making and hedging that allow substantial risk-taking. And Dodd-Frank left the shadow banking system largely untouched: money-market funds, asset managers, and nonbank lenders now harbor the kind of maturity and liquidity mismatches that can ignite panic. The 2008 crisis did not start at a commercial bank but within the unregulated tri-party repo market; that market remains structurally fragile.

Thus, while Dodd-Frank has made the banking system safer—a genuine achievement—it has not eliminated the dangers. The rejected living wills are a public signal that the largest firms are still too big to fail without collateral damage. Breaking them up, as Senator Sanders advocates, would directly reduce complexity and interconnectedness, though at a potentially high cost to the efficiency of global finance. A middle path of much higher capital surcharges and mandatory structural separation of commercial and investment banking activities might force genuine resolvability. But as of now, the danger is dormant, not dead. The next stress test may be political, not financial: whether we have the will to complete what Dodd-Frank started.

"While the Dodd-Frank Act has significantly increased the resilience of the financial system through higher capital requirements and oversight, it has failed to eliminate the systemic dangers posed by institutions that remain too large and complex to fail."

The rejection of the living wills submitted by JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, and other institutions by federal regulators last month provides a timely reminder that Dodd-Frank, however ambitious, has not fully resolved the fundamental tension between the size of major financial institutions and the stability of the broader financial system.

Dodd-Frank accomplished significant things. Capital requirements under Basel III are meaningfully higher than before the crisis. The annual stress tests conducted by the Federal Reserve have forced banks to build more robust balance sheets and have provided unprecedented transparency into bank health. The Volcker Rule has restricted proprietary trading that posed clear conflicts of interest. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau has curbed some of the worst predatory lending practices that contributed to the mortgage crisis. These are genuine achievements that make the financial system more resilient than it was in 2007.

Yet the core vulnerability persists: institutions whose failure could cascade through the economy remain operational. The living will rejections essentially confirmed what critics have long argued — that these firms are so complex, so interdependent, and so globally interconnected that orderly bankruptcy remains theoretically possible but practically unproven. When regulators cannot imagine how a bank could wind down without destabilizing the system, the too-big-to-fail problem has not been eliminated; it has merely been relabeled.

The debate between Sanders and Clinton reflects a genuine policy disagreement about means rather than ends. Both acknowledge the dangers; they differ on whether regulatory vigilance within existing structures can manage them or whether structural reform through breakup is necessary. Clinton's confidence in Dodd-Frank as sufficient is defensible but assumes continued regulatory resolve that history suggests cannot be taken for granted. Sanders' skepticism about regulatory capture has empirical support — the decades of under-regulation that preceded 2008 did not occur because regulators lacked authority but because political and economic pressure diluted their will to use it.

The honest answer in 2016 is that Dodd-Frank reduced dangers substantially without eliminating them, and that ongoing vigilance, not legislative finality, will determine whether the next crisis can be averted.

The recent rejection by federal regulators of the "living wills" submitted by five of the nation’s largest banks is a sobering reality check. It confirms what many financial experts have long suspected: while the Dodd-Frank Act was a historic and necessary response to the 2008 financial crisis, it has not entirely eliminated the systemic dangers inherent in our banking system. "Too big to fail" remains an unresolved threat.

To be fair, Dodd-Frank has made the financial sector undeniably safer. Thanks to the legislation, regulators now enforce significantly higher capital and liquidity requirements, meaning banks have thicker cushions to absorb massive losses. Annual stress tests have forced institutions to prove they can survive severe economic downturns. Furthermore, the creation of the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau has curbed some of the predatory lending practices that fueled the subprime mortgage meltdown. Secretary Hillary Clinton is right to defend these achievements; dismantling them would invite a return to the reckless environment of the mid-2000s.

However, making a system safer is not the same as eliminating its dangers. The failure of these massive institutions—including JPMorgan Chase and Bank of America—to present credible plans for entering bankruptcy without triggering a global panic exposes the limits of the current regulatory framework. Dodd-Frank attempts to manage the immense size and complexity of mega-banks through oversight. Yet, these institutions remain so interconnected and structurally labyrinthine that an orderly wind-down during a crisis is nearly impossible to guarantee. If one of these behemoths were to teeter on the brink of collapse tomorrow, the federal government would still face the agonizing choice between a devastating economic contagion and a taxpayer-funded bailout.

Senator Bernie Sanders’s call to break up the banks highlights a genuine structural flaw, even if his proposed solution glosses over the complexities of global finance. If we are to truly neutralize the dangers in the banking system, regulators must use the tools provided by Dodd-Frank more aggressively. They must force these institutions to simplify their corporate structures, sever highly interconnected liabilities, and hold even more capital. Dodd-Frank laid a vital regulatory foundation, but as the latest rebukes prove, the house is still far from fireproof.

Dodd-Frank has not “eliminated” danger from banking, but that is the wrong standard. No financial law can abolish risk, credit cycles or human folly. The law created an orderly liquidation authority designed to impose losses on shareholders and creditors rather than taxpayers. It also required living wills precisely so regulators could force firms to simplify themselves before a crisis.

That is what is happening now. The government did not rubber-stamp the banks’ plans. It told them their bankruptcy strategies were inadequate and demanded changes. That is a sign of regulatory seriousness. If we had possessed such tools in 2006, the buildup of risk might have been more visible and more costly for firms that relied on fragility.

Breaking up the banks sounds appealing, especially in a campaign season. But it is not a magic solution. Lehman Brothers was not the largest financial institution in America when it collapsed, and its failure still shook the world. Bear Stearns was not a universal bank on the scale of JPMorgan. AIG was an insurance conglomerate with a derivatives problem. Countrywide was a mortgage lender. The crisis was not simply a story of “big banks”; it was a story of leverage, housing mania, shadow banking, bad underwriting, weak supervision and panic-prone funding markets. A breakup could reduce some risks while leaving many others untouched.

Regulators should keep pressing banks to simplify legal structures, improve loss-absorbing capacity, reduce reliance on runnable funding and make derivatives exposures transparent. Congress should resist efforts to weaken the law under the guise of technical fixes. At the same time, regulators should tailor rules so that community banks are not buried under requirements designed for global institutions.

Dodd-Frank is not perfect. No statute of that size could be. But the choice is not between complacency and demolition. The financial system is safer because of the law, and the living-will process is one of the mechanisms making it safer. The answer is to strengthen and enforce Dodd-Frank, not to pretend that a simple breakup slogan can substitute for a durable regulatory regime.

Dodd-Frank has not eliminated the dangers in the banking system, and the rejection this month of the "living wills" submitted by five of the largest banks—JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, State Street, and Bank of New York Mellon—is proof. The whole premise of Title II of Dodd-Frank is that megabanks can be unwound through orderly liquidation rather than disorderly bankruptcy or taxpayer rescue. This is not a small bureaucratic detail. The 2008 crisis was not merely about reckless lending; it was about the impossibility of letting a Lehman or a Citigroup fail without taking the global financial system down with it. That structural reality—interconnectedness, opacity, and the sheer scale of derivatives books—has not been resolved. JPMorgan today has a balance sheet larger than it did in 2008. The top five banks hold a greater share of industry assets than they did before the crisis. Concentration has increased, not decreased.

Defenders of Dodd-Frank point to higher capital ratios, stress tests, the Volcker Rule, and the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau. These are real accomplishments. Common equity at the largest banks is roughly double pre-crisis levels, and the stress-testing regime imposes a discipline that did not exist before. But capital cushions can be eroded quickly in a panic, as 2008 demonstrated, and stress tests model the last crisis rather than the next one. Meanwhile, risk has migrated to the shadow banking sector—money market funds, asset managers, insurance affiliates—where regulation is thinner and where the next run is likely to begin.

The "London Whale" episode at JPMorgan in 2012, the foreign exchange and LIBOR scandals, and the persistent compliance failures across major institutions suggest that the cultural and governance problems that produced the crisis are still alive. A bank that cannot police its own traders cannot credibly claim it could walk itself into bankruptcy court without contagion.

None of this means breaking up the banks is necessarily the right answer—Sanders' plan has serious operational questions of its own. But Clinton's posture, that Dodd-Frank plus tougher enforcement is sufficient, understates the unfinished business. The regulators themselves just said, in effect, that the too-big-to-fail problem is not solved. We should take them at their word and treat the next several years as a period of continued reform, not consolidation.

"While Dodd-Frank cannot eliminate the inherent risks of banking, it has materially reduced systemic fragility and made the financial system meaningfully safer than it was before the 2008 crisis."

The framing of the question—has Dodd-Frank "eliminated" the dangers in the banking system—sets an impossible standard that no regulatory regime has ever met or could meet. Banking is inherently the business of borrowing short and lending long, of transforming maturity and liquidity. That activity will always carry risk, and financial crises will recur as long as human beings borrow money. The relevant question is whether Dodd-Frank has materially reduced systemic fragility compared with the pre-2008 baseline. The answer is yes.

Consider what the law actually did. It forced large banks to hold dramatically more capital and high-quality liquid assets. Tier 1 common equity at the eight U.S. global systemically important banks now stands near 12 percent, compared with roughly 6 percent before the crisis. The Liquidity Coverage Ratio requires enough cash-like assets to survive 30 days of severe stress. Annual stress tests under CCAR have killed dividend plans and forced capital raises. Most over-the-counter derivatives now clear through central counterparties, dramatically reducing the bilateral exposure web that paralyzed markets in 2008. The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau has curbed predatory mortgage practices that fed the subprime bubble.

The recent rejection of the living wills is being read as evidence of failure, but it is actually evidence of the system working. Regulators looked at detailed resolution plans, found them deficient, and publicly demanded improvements with the threat of higher capital requirements or divestiture if banks do not comply. That is the iterative supervisory dialogue Dodd-Frank was designed to create. Before 2010, no such mechanism existed. Lehman went into bankruptcy with essentially no plan at all.

What remains undone is real but specific: the shadow banking sector, particularly money market funds and the tri-party repo market, still has vulnerabilities; FSOC's designations of nonbank SIFIs are being beaten back in court (witness MetLife); and housing finance reform—the unfinished business of Fannie and Freddie—has stalled in Congress. These are legitimate concerns, but they are not arguments for breaking up JPMorgan.

Senator Sanders' proposal to dismantle the megabanks substitutes a slogan for an analysis. The 2008 crisis was not caused by size alone—Bear Stearns and Lehman were smaller than the survivors—but by leverage, opacity, and a regulatory regime designed for the 1930s. Dodd-Frank addressed those underlying problems. The honest verdict is that the law has made the system meaningfully safer without making it perfectly safe. Demanding the latter is a category error.
