"The federal government must maintain a strong presence in drug enforcement to ensure equal protection and prevent the emergence of geographically based racial and legal disparities."

As states like Colorado and Washington move to legalize marijuana, and others emphasize public health approaches to drug use, a chorus of voices is calling for the federal government to step back entirely and leave drug enforcement to the states. While this localist approach is appealing in progressive jurisdictions, it ignores a dark reality of American history: "states' rights" has routinely been a cover for systemic inequality and racial discrimination. We need a strong federal presence in drug enforcement to guarantee equal protection under the law.

The War on Drugs has undoubtedly been plagued by racial disparities. But decentralizing enforcement would likely exacerbate these inequalities, not eliminate them. If the federal government totally withdraws from drug prosecution, we will see the emergence of two Americas. In one America—wealthier, more progressive states—drug use will be treated as a medical issue or a legal recreational activity. In the other America—states with entrenched "tough on crime" mentalities and histories of systemic racism—draconian drug laws will continue to be weaponized against poor and minority communities.

The federal government serves as a crucial civil rights backstop. Federal oversight and federal courts are often the only mechanisms available to challenge deeply biased local policing. If we abandon federal drug statutes entirely, we abandon the citizens living in states that refuse to reform. A young black man caught with a small amount of narcotics in the Deep South could face decades in state prison, while a white college student in the Pacific Northwest faces zero consequences for the same action.

A geographic lottery for fundamental justice is unacceptable in a unified nation. Rather than abdicating its role, the federal government should reform the Controlled Substances Act to mandate fairer sentencing guidelines nationwide. The solution to a flawed federal drug policy is to fix the policy, not to retreat and allow localized injustices to flourish unchecked.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Leaving drug enforcement entirely to the states would allow jurisdictions with 'tough on crime' mentalities to continue weaponizing draconian laws against minority communities.
  2. 02Federal oversight serves as a necessary civil rights backstop to challenge biased local policing and systemic inequalities.
  3. 03A decentralized approach would create an unacceptable 'geographic lottery' where the severity of punishment for the same crime depends entirely on a citizen's location.
  4. 04The federal government should focus on reforming national drug policy and sentencing guidelines rather than abdicating its authority to the states.
