The question is not whether Andi Dorfman is liberating or retrograde. She is both, because “The Bachelorette” is both. The show is a contradictory cultural artifact: it gives a woman power inside a structure that remains obsessed with old-fashioned romantic closure.

That is a meaningful departure from the most passive versions of reality-TV femininity. She seems less interested in being adored in general than in being treated well in particular. That distinction is not trivial.

But the format narrows what her independence can mean. A prosecutor’s judgment becomes, on this show, the judgment of who gets a rose. A woman’s complex adult life is compressed into the question of whether she will be engaged in a matter of weeks. The men compete; the woman presides; the audience judges everyone. This is not a documentary about modern female ambition. It is a romantic game show draped in the language of destiny.

Still, I resist the easy dismissal that any woman seeking marriage on television is somehow harming feminism. That critique can become its own kind of policing. Professional women are often told they must prove their seriousness by downplaying romance, domesticity or vulnerability. The more revealing phrase is “putting her career on hold.” That is where the cultural anxiety lives. We still have not normalized the idea that women’s careers can include pauses, experiments and public detours without being treated as fragile. At the same time, the legal profession and many other workplaces remain unforgiving of exactly those pauses, especially for women. Dorfman’s choice looks glamorous because it is televised; for most women, the collision of love, family and work is far less cinematic.

So is she a new kind of bachelorette? Somewhat. She is a newer type of heroine inside a very old story. The useful response is neither celebration nor scorn, but attention to the tension. Viewers can enjoy watching a smart woman demand respect while also noticing how quickly television converts female agency into a marriage countdown.

It is easy to be snobbish about "The Bachelorette," and the snobbery usually comes packaged with the assumption that the women who appear on it, and the millions who watch, are dupes of a patriarchal entertainment complex. Treating that decision as a betrayal of feminism says more about the critic than about Andi.

Professional women date. They date badly, in fact, by most accounts: the apps are a mess, the bars are worse, and the demands of a career like prosecuting criminal cases do not leave much daylight for meeting eligible partners. "The Bachelorette" offers what is, in its strange way, a rationalized search: a vetted pool, a compressed timeline, an enforced clarity about whether the men are looking for marriage. You can find the spectacle silly without pretending the underlying problem it addresses is silly.

Is the show a fairy tale? Of course. So is most romantic comedy, so is much of how all of us narrate our love lives to ourselves. The question is whether participating in a fairy tale on camera somehow disqualifies a woman from being taken seriously afterward. I don't see why it should. Andi will return to her job, or not, on her own terms. She has not surrendered her bar license to Chris Harrison.

What seems genuinely new this season — modestly new, not revolutionarily so — is the matter-of-factness about her career. Andi has spoken in interviews about her cases, about working in a male-dominated office, about wanting a partner who can handle a woman with a demanding job. Previous bachelorettes have been pitched primarily as romantic protagonists with hobbies attached. Andi is being pitched as a person with a profession who also wants a husband. That is a small shift, but small shifts in mass-market television are how cultural defaults eventually move.

I wouldn't call her revolutionary. I would call her recognizable — recognizable in a way that a lot of women in their late twenties, juggling work they love and a personal life they're still building, might actually appreciate seeing.

The interesting question isn't whether Andi Dorfman represents progress or regression. It's why we keep asking television shows to be referenda on the status of women in the first place. "The Bachelorette" is a competition format borrowed from game shows and dressed up in tulle. Demanding that it inspire a generation of professional women is like demanding that "Deal or No Deal" inspire a generation of statisticians.

Still, since the question is on the table, here's a more useful frame than progress-versus-regression: what does the show actually do to the people inside it, and what does it model for the people watching?
