"The right’s conception of freedom as limited government and private property is not merely a legacy of racial domination, but a necessary defense against the inherent dangers of concentrated state power."

Aziz Rana’s essay is an elegant statement of a familiar left argument: the right has captured the language of freedom by narrowing it into individualism, markets, militarism, and racial exclusion; the left must reclaim freedom as democratic solidarity; and the path to doing so runs through unions, public provision, anti-imperialism, and immigration liberalization. There is much in this account that deserves reflection. American history is indeed marked by slavery, dispossession, racial hierarchy, and wars justified by inflated rhetoric. Conservatives should not deny these facts. But Rana’s argument ultimately misdescribes why many Americans associate freedom with limited government, private property, local control, and national sovereignty. Those associations are not merely the residue of settler domination. They reflect real and continuing dangers posed by concentrated power.

The first problem is Rana’s tendency to treat individual liberty as culturally contaminated because it emerged in a society of conquest. By that standard, no political concept survives. Democracy was practiced in slaveholding Athens. Equality has been invoked by tyrants. Solidarity can justify coercion. National liberation movements have sometimes produced one-party states. The fact that a principle has been used hypocritically does not prove that the principle is rotten. The American idea that citizens should be secure in their property, speech, religion, arms, associations, and private lives against an overreaching state has protected many people, including dissenters on the left.

Rana says settlers did not oppose the state as such; they wanted the state used against outsiders and not against themselves. That is often true. But it does not follow that skepticism of state power is simply a mask for domination. Black Americans in the Jim Crow South had many reasons to distrust local sheriffs, police, licensing boards, school officials, and welfare bureaucrats. Religious minorities have had reasons to distrust majoritarian school boards. Antiwar activists have had reasons to distrust federal surveillance. Parents today may have legitimate concerns about distant administrators making decisions for children without meaningful accountability. Small business owners may reasonably fear regulatory regimes written by large incumbents. The state is not automatically the instrument of solidarity.

Nor is the market simply the enemy of freedom. Markets are imperfect, and they require law, morals, and sometimes regulation. But they also disperse power. A society in which people can start businesses, change jobs, buy property, homeschool, publish, worship, donate, and build associations outside the state is freer than one in which every major institution is subject to political control. Rana’s preferred “democratization” of the workplace sounds attractive until one asks how much of life should be politicized. Must every enterprise become a miniature legislature? Do entrepreneurs who risk savings to build a firm lose the right to direct it once it grows? Can religious schools, family businesses, newspapers, and nonprofits maintain distinctive missions under a regime of pervasive collective governance?

The conservative understanding of freedom begins from a different anthropology. Human beings are social, but they are also partial, ambitious, fallible, and prone to abuse power. That is why freedom requires limits: constitutional limits, market limits, federalist limits, family autonomy, religious liberty, property rights, and civil society independent of government. Rana worries that Americans are not raised in worlds where collective agency feels real. Conservatives worry that Americans are increasingly trapped in systems where collective decisions made by remote elites override local judgment: federal agencies, universities, corporations aligned with political fashions, professional bureaucracies, and courts willing to settle moral questions for the whole country.

This is especially evident in the culture-war conflicts Rana mentions only as reactionary attacks. Take education. Many parents who object to school policies on gender, race, or curriculum are not simply demanding domination over outsiders. They believe that public institutions have become unresponsive to them and that professional classes dismiss their moral concerns as bigotry. One need not agree with every parental-rights bill to see the freedom claim here. If “self-rule” means anything, it must include some authority for families and local communities over the formation of children. A left that defines all resistance as white nationalism will not persuade those people; it will confirm their suspicion that “democracy” means rule by credentialed progressives.

The same is true of pandemic politics. Some opposition to public health measures was irresponsible and conspiratorial. But the experience also revealed how quickly emergency powers can expand, how unevenly rules burden citizens, and how contemptuously experts can treat ordinary tradeoffs. School closures harmed children, especially poor children. Small businesses were shuttered while large retailers operated. Churches faced restrictions that many believers considered unfair. The language of freedom resonated because people experienced commands from institutions that were not always competent, consistent, or accountable.

Rana’s foreign policy critique also needs balance. The Iraq War was a grave mistake, and conservatives should say so plainly. But American power has not only been an engine of domination. It helped defeat fascism, contain Soviet totalitarianism, protect sea lanes, support allies, and create conditions in which many countries prospered. The United States has often acted hypocritically; it has also been preferable to rival hegemonies. A world shaped by Russia’s invasion of Ukraine or China’s coercive power would not be more hospitable to freedom. Anti-imperialism can become a slogan that ignores the agency of smaller nations seeking American support because they fear worse domination.

Rana’s treatment of immigration is similarly one-sided. He calls for presumptive admission and basic decriminalization of entry. That may be morally satisfying, but it evades the relationship between democracy and borders. A political community must have some capacity to decide who enters and on what terms, not because foreigners are enemies but because citizenship, public finance, labor standards, housing, schools, and social trust are not abstractions. A country that loses control of its border will not become more solidaristic; it will fuel backlash and empower demagogues. The right’s rhetoric about immigrants can be cruel and dehumanizing, but the left’s refusal to acknowledge limits is politically reckless.

Still, conservatives should take one lesson from Rana seriously: freedom cannot mean only noninterference. A man addicted to fentanyl, a family crushed by medical bills, a worker whose town has lost its industries, a child in a violent neighborhood, a veteran abandoned after war—these people are not meaningfully free just because taxes are low. The market alone will not restore communities. Family, church, union, local government, charity, and sometimes national policy all have roles. Conservatives who speak of freedom while ignoring economic concentration, corporate dependency, and social breakdown offer only nostalgia.

But the remedy is not a left program of centralized social democracy plus anti-imperial foreign policy. It is a renewed constitutionalism of dispersed power: stronger families, more local authority, plural schools, competitive markets, antitrust enforcement, vocational pathways, serious border policy, civic associations, and a safety net that supports work and family without absorbing society into the state. Freedom is not isolation. It is ordered liberty—liberty sustained by institutions close enough to command loyalty and limited enough to leave room for conscience.

Rana is right that the word “freedom” should not be abandoned. But neither should it be surrendered to a theory in which individualism is presumed guilty and solidarity is presumed innocent. Americans cherish freedom from government because government has often abused them. They cherish property because property can provide independence. They cherish borders because political communities require definition. They cherish local control because distant power is often arrogant. These attachments can be distorted by fear and prejudice. They can also express wisdom. A better debate over freedom would begin by admitting both truths.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The historical misuse of individual liberty by settlers does not invalidate the principle's essential role in protecting citizens from state overreach.
  2. 02The state is not an inherent instrument of solidarity, as evidenced by the legitimate distrust marginalized groups and local communities often have toward bureaucratic and regulatory authorities.
  3. 03Markets and private property serve as vital tools for dispersing power and allowing for institutional diversity outside of political control.
  4. 04True self-rule must include the authority of families and local communities to resist the imposition of values by remote, credentialed elites.
  5. 05A solidaristic vision of freedom requires national sovereignty and border control to maintain the social trust and public resources necessary for a functional political community.
  6. 06While freedom requires more than just noninterference, the solution is a pluralistic system of dispersed power rather than a centralized social democratic program.
