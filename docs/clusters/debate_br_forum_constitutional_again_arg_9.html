Diversified DeepSeek

"The lead essay's call to 're-constitutionalize' progressive politics is a misguided attempt to replace neutral constitutional protections for individual liberty and federalism with an outcome-oriented jurisprudence that serves partisan ends."

The lead essay in this forum is an ambitious effort to reconstruct a progressive constitutional tradition, but it fundamentally misdiagnoses the problem of constitutional law in the United States. The authors argue that conservatives have weaponized the Constitution—especially the First Amendment—to dismantle egalitarian legislation, and they call on progressives to reclaim the “democracy-of-opportunity” tradition to fight back. But their argument rests on a series of flawed premises about both constitutional interpretation and political economy. The conservative judicial project is not, as they claim, a radical departure from neutral principles; it is a principled effort to protect individual rights and structural restraints against an ever-expanding state. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition itself, far from being a lost constitutional truth, is a deeply problematic attempt to bend the Constitution to partisan ends. A sound understanding of constitutionalism requires recognizing that the First Amendment, the Takings Clause, and federalism are not weapons of oligarchy but bulwarks of liberty that benefit all citizens, including the most vulnerable.

Consider the treatment of campaign finance. The authors lament the line of cases culminating in Citizens United, which they claim enables the rich to translate economic power directly into political power. But this framing is misleading. Citizens United did not hold that corporations are citizens or that money is speech in an unqualified sense. It held that the government may not prohibit independent political speech by corporations (and, by extension, unions and other associations) based solely on the identity of the speaker. The law at issue, the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act, banned corporations and unions from using general treasury funds for electioneering communications—even when those communications are wholly independent of candidates. This was a direct restriction on core political speech. The Court’s decision was rooted in a long-standing First Amendment principle: the government cannot suppress speech because it disapproves of the speaker or fears that speech will be too persuasive. As Justice Kennedy wrote, “The First Amendment confirms the freedom to think for ourselves.”

The lead essay’s alternative is telling. It praises public financing systems that match small donations at generous ratios, such as New York City’s 8:1 match. But such systems, however well-intentioned, also raise serious constitutional concerns. By subsidizing some speakers over others, they engage in viewpoint discrimination. The Arizona matching funds scheme struck down in Arizona Free Enterprise Club v. Bennett (2011) was precisely such a law: it triggered additional public funds for a candidate whose opponent spent heavily, effectively penalizing the exercise of First Amendment rights. The Court properly recognized that “leveling the playing field” cannot justify burdening speech. The First Amendment does not exist to equalize influence; it exists to prevent government from silencing voices it does not like. The lead essay’s “democracy-of-opportunity” approach would eviscerate that protection, allowing legislatures to decide which forms of political engagement deserve amplification and which should be suppressed in the name of egalitarian goals.

The Janus decision, similarly, is not an assault on labor but a vindication of individual rights. The Court held that public-sector employees who are not union members cannot be forced to pay agency fees that fund collective bargaining, because bargaining with the government is inherently political speech. In a government workplace, wages, benefits, and working conditions are matters of public concern, decided through the political process. Forcing a dissenting employee to subsidize the union’s advocacy on those issues is compelled speech in violation of the First Amendment. The authors suggest that this ruling undermines countervailing power, but unions remain free to organize and persuade workers to join voluntarily. Janus simply ensures that membership is truly a choice, not a condition of employment. This is a classic liberal principle: the right to associate also includes the right not to associate. It is no more anti-union than the right not to join a church is anti-religion.

The broader narrative of a “Lochner-era” revival is similarly overdrawn. The Lochner Court did not simply strike down “progressive reforms”; it enforced the constitutional right to freedom of contract, which the Fourteenth Amendment’s Due Process Clause protects. While modern jurisprudence has largely abandoned Lochner’s robust economic liberty protections, the core insight—that the Constitution places limits on government’s power to interfere with private ordering—remains vital. The lead essay celebrates antitrust and corporate governance reforms as essential to democracy, but these are precisely the kinds of interventions that can trample property rights and contractual freedom. Mandatory codetermination, for example, forces shareholders to share control of their enterprises with workers, effectively transferring property rights without compensation. This is not a mere regulatory tweak; it is a fundamental restructuring of ownership. The Takings Clause, properly understood, should constrain such schemes, just as it constrains physical expropriations.

The authors’ treatment of race and economic justice also suffers from a selective reading of history. They claim that the Roberts Court’s decisions in NFIB v. Sebelius and Shelby County v. Holder were motivated by a desire to shield the former Confederacy from federal oversight and to perpetuate racial inequality. But the constitutional principles at stake extend far beyond any particular region. In Sebelius, the Court held that Congress cannot coerce states into expanding Medicaid by threatening to withhold all existing Medicaid funds. This is a straightforward application of the Spending Clause, which requires that states’ acceptance of federal funds be voluntary. The “gun to the head” metaphor was dramatic, but the underlying principle—that Congress must respect the states as separate sovereigns—is foundational to the constitutional order. Shelby County similarly was not about race in the way the authors imply; it was about the formula used to determine which jurisdictions were subject to preclearance. That formula was based on data from the 1960s and 1970s, and the Court held that using decades-old data to subject states to extraordinary federal oversight violated the “equal sovereignty” principle and the contemporary realities of voting rights. Congress remains free to enact a new, updated coverage formula. The decisions are less about undermining racial equality than about enforcing constitutional boundaries.

Finally, the lead essay’s underlying economic analysis is contestable. The authors attribute rising inequality and declining entrepreneurship primarily to weakened antitrust enforcement and corporate governance changes. But these trends have many causes, including globalization, technological change, and educational disparities, none of which are easily addressed by breaking up large firms or mandating worker board representation. Moreover, concentrated markets often yield efficiencies that lower prices and improve products for consumers, especially the poor. The Brandeisian vision of small-scale democratic capitalism is romantic but largely incompatible with the realities of modern production. The attempt to constitutionalize that vision risks freezing into law a nostalgic economics that would impoverish millions in the name of a mythical yeoman republic.

In sum, the lead essay’s call to “make progressive politics constitutional again” is an invitation to abandon the Constitution’s actual protections for individual liberty, property, and federalism in favor of a politically convenient outcome-oriented jurisprudence. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition is not a lost constitutional truth; it is a historical artifact of a political movement that, like all such movements, sought to use the Constitution for its own ends. Today’s progressive agenda would be better served by respecting the constitutional limits that protect everyone, and by winning political majorities to enact policies within those limits, rather than trying to erase the limits themselves. The Supreme Court’s conservative majority is not an enemy of democracy; it is a guardian of the constitutional order that democracy requires.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Supreme Court’s decisions in cases like Citizens United and Janus are not weapons of oligarchy but necessary vindications of First Amendment principles against government-compelled speech and viewpoint discrimination.
  2. 02The 'democracy-of-opportunity' tradition ignores the fact that the Constitution's structural restraints, such as the Takings Clause and freedom of contract, are essential bulwarks against the expropriation of private property.
  3. 03Judicial rulings on the Affordable Care Act and the Voting Rights Act are rooted in the foundational principle of state sovereignty rather than a desire to perpetuate racial or economic inequality.
  4. 04The authors' economic diagnosis is flawed because it seeks to constitutionalize a nostalgic, anti-monopoly vision that is incompatible with the efficiencies of the modern global economy.
  5. 05Progressives should focus on winning political majorities to enact policy within existing constitutional limits rather than attempting to redefine the Constitution to bypass those limits.
