Diversified Gemini

"Lawrence Lessig’s theory of 'dependency corruption' is a constitutionally flawed concept that would grant the government dangerous power to suppress political speech and engineer democratic outcomes."

Lawrence Lessig’s diagnosis of “institutional corruption” in the wake of *Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission* is an elegantly constructed, academically rigorous attempt to justify a deeply dangerous proposition: that the government should have the power to ration political speech in order to engineer a preferred democratic outcome. By attempting to expand the definition of corruption beyond the traditional *quid pro quo* to something he calls “dependency corruption,” Lessig seeks to provide a constitutional workaround that would allow Congress to silence its critics. But beneath the polished veneer of his argument lies a fundamental misunderstanding of both the First Amendment and the nature of political power.

Lessig’s central claim rests on a historical analogy. He accuses Justice Anthony Kennedy and the Supreme Court majority in *Citizens United* of engaging in “Lochnerism”—the practice of substituting the Court’s own factual assumptions for those of the legislature. Lessig argues that just as the *Lochner* Court wrongly second-guessed the New York legislature’s health regulations regarding bakers, the *Citizens United* Court wrongly second-guessed Congress’s determination that corporate independent expenditures corrupt the political process. This analogy, however, collapses under the slightest constitutional scrutiny.

The fundamental difference between *Lochner v. New York* and *Citizens United* is the text of the Constitution itself. The *Lochner* Court struck down the Bakeshop Act based on a “freedom of contract” that is nowhere explicitly mentioned in the Constitution; it was inferred from the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. The *Citizens United* Court, on the other hand, struck down the censorship provisions of the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act (BCRA) based on the First Amendment, which states explicitly and unambiguously that “Congress shall make no law… abridging the freedom of speech.” When a legislature regulates the hours a baker can work, it operates within its general police powers, subject only to rational basis review. When a legislature passes a law that explicitly makes it a felony for a group of citizens, organized in the corporate form, to broadcast a film criticizing a candidate for federal office within sixty days of an election, it strikes at the very heart of the First Amendment. It is not “Lochnerism” for the Supreme Court to strictly scrutinize and strike down a law that clearly abridges political speech; it is the Court doing its job.

Lessig’s proposed alternative to the Court’s jurisprudence is the concept of “dependency corruption.” He argues that because members of Congress depend on campaign contributions, their primary loyalty shifts from the voters to the funders. But Lessig defines this dependency as a form of corruption only because he disapproves of the outcomes. In a vast, pluralistic republic of over 300 million people, organizing and amplifying one’s voice costs money. The fact that a politician listens to a constituency that has successfully organized and funded a campaign to promote its views is not corruption; it is representation.

Lessig provides examples to illustrate his point: “free-market” Republicans voting for milk subsidies, or “pro-consumer” Democrats voting to exempt used-car dealers from regulations. He assumes that because these votes contradict the politicians' supposed ideological labels, they must be the result of a corrupt dependency on money. But there is a much simpler explanation: these politicians are representing the economic interests of their constituents. A Republican from a dairy-producing state votes for milk subsidies because the dairy farmers in his district demand it, and they back up that demand with votes and campaign contributions. A Democrat might vote to exempt used-car dealers because those dealerships are significant local employers. Lessig views this as a deviation from the “public interest,” but the “public interest” is not an objective monolith waiting to be discovered by pure-hearted, uncompromised philosopher-kings. It is a messy, contested compromise forged precisely through the clash of competing factions.

By pathologizing the influence of money as “institutional corruption,” Lessig is essentially arguing that any political influence outside of the pure, unadulterated ballot box is illegitimate. But the First Amendment guarantees not just the right to vote, but the right to petition the government for a redress of grievances, and the right to freedom of speech. Money is indispensable to the exercise of both rights. To publish a pamphlet, to air a television advertisement, to rent a hall for a rally—all of this requires money. If the government can restrict the amount of money spent on political advocacy because it fears that such advocacy will be "too persuasive" or will make politicians "too dependent" on the advocates, then the government has granted itself the power to decide who is allowed to speak and how loudly.

Furthermore, Lessig’s reliance on the “iceberg theory” of campaign finance—where the mere threat of independent expenditures exerts an unseen, corrupting gravitational pull on politicians—is a recipe for unlimited legislative suppression of speech. If we accept the premise that unspoken threats of independent advertising constitute “corruption,” how could a court possibly draw a line limiting Congress’s power to regulate speech? Any organization capable of mustering an audience, from a massive labor union to a corporate giant, poses a "threat" to a politician's reelection. Does the *New York Times* exert a corrupting influence because politicians fear its editorial board? Lessig attempts to draw a distinction by suggesting that the *Times* does not coordinate with campaigns. But neither do independent expenditures—that is precisely what makes them independent.

The true danger to American democracy does not lie in the ability of corporations, unions, or advocacy groups to spend their own money to express their political views. The true danger lies in the assumption that the government is capable of objectively managing the "economy of influence" without favoring its own incumbents. Campaign finance laws are almost always written by incumbents to protect themselves from challengers. BCRA, the law struck down in *Citizens United*, was notoriously complex, creating a labyrinth of regulations that only seasoned political insiders and their high-priced lawyers could navigate. By striking down the ban on independent expenditures, the Supreme Court decentralized political power, allowing new voices—albeit well-funded ones—to bypass the incumbent-protecting apparatus of federal election law.

Lessig’s vision of a democracy funded by small-dollar vouchers or strict public financing is undeniably appealing in a utopian sense. If enacted through legitimate legislative means, such as the Fair Elections Now Act, they represent valid public policy experiments. But elevating his theory of “dependency corruption” to the level of constitutional doctrine would require gutting the First Amendment. It would require conceding that the government has a compelling interest in leveling the playing field of political discourse by dragging down the most effective speakers. That is a price far too high to pay for the illusion of a pristine, uninfluenced legislature. In a free society, the cure for speech that represents special interests is not to silence it, but to answer it with more speech.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The analogy between Citizens United and Lochnerism is false because the former protects an explicit First Amendment right against government censorship, whereas the latter relied on an unenumerated right to contract.
  2. 02What Lessig labels as 'dependency corruption' is actually a legitimate form of political representation in a pluralistic society where groups organize and fund campaigns to promote their interests.
  3. 03The 'public interest' is not an objective monolith but a contested compromise between competing factions, making the influence of organized economic interests a natural part of the democratic process.
  4. 04Restricting the money used for political advocacy effectively grants the government the power to decide who is allowed to speak and how loudly, undermining the First Amendment rights to speech and petition.
  5. 05The 'iceberg theory' of campaign finance provides no clear limit to legislative power, potentially allowing the government to regulate any organization capable of influencing a politician's reelection.
  6. 06Campaign finance regulations are often tools used by incumbents to protect themselves from challengers, whereas allowing independent expenditures decentralizes political power.
  7. 07Elevating 'dependency corruption' to a constitutional doctrine would require gutting the First Amendment and accepting the illiberal premise that the government should level the playing field by silencing effective speakers.
Diversified Gemini

"Lawrence Lessig’s theory of 'dependency corruption' is a legally unworkable and subjective standard that would dangerously empower Congress to censor political speech and protect incumbents under the guise of institutional reform."

Lawrence Lessig’s critique of *Citizens United* is built on a foundation of constitutional and historical assertions that, while intellectually provocative, ultimately fail to provide a workable legal standard. Lessig proposes that the Supreme Court should recognize “dependency corruption”—the idea that a legislature acts improperly when it depends on funders rather than voters. He equates Justice Kennedy’s rejection of this theory to "Lochnerism," accusing the Court of substituting its own factual judgments for those of the legislature. While this framing makes for a compelling academic narrative, it fundamentally misreads the role of the judiciary in protecting constitutional rights and invites a dangerous level of legislative self-policing.

Let us first address Lessig’s accusation of "Lochnerism." *Lochner v. New York* is universally reviled in modern legal scholarship because the Court struck down a labor regulation by inventing a "liberty of contract" that is nowhere found in the text of the Constitution. The Court in *Lochner* read its own laissez-faire economic preferences into the Due Process Clause. *Citizens United*, however, is categorically different. The First Amendment explicitly states that Congress shall make no law abridging the freedom of speech.

When a law passed by Congress makes it a criminal offense for a specific type of association (a corporation or a union) to distribute a political film or run a political advertisement in the weeks leading up to an election, that law directly implicates the explicit text of the First Amendment. It is not "Lochnerism" for the Court to demand that the government provide a compelling, rigorously defined justification for such censorship. It is the application of strict scrutiny, a bedrock principle of First Amendment jurisprudence. Lessig complains that Kennedy dismissed the "factual issue" of whether corporate spending causes the electorate to lose faith in democracy. But fundamental constitutional rights are not contingent on public opinion polls. The government cannot ban a book, suppress a religion, or silence a political speaker simply because a majority of the electorate finds their influence distasteful or loses faith in the system because of them.

Lessig’s proposed solution—that the Court should allow Congress to regulate speech to prevent "institutional corruption"—is a cure worse than the disease. Lessig defines this corruption as an institution developing "a dependency that conflicts with its intended dependency." He claims the Framers intended Congress to be dependent "upon the People alone." But who gets to define what constitutes a "conflicting dependency"?

If the Supreme Court adopts Lessig's standard, it must grant Congress the power to define the parameters of its own "proper" dependency and then ban speech that threatens it. This is a breathtaking conflict of interest. Incumbent politicians are the very worst judges of what constitutes healthy political competition. If Congress is empowered to restrict speech that creates an "improper dependency on funders," what stops them from declaring that independent expenditures by labor unions create an improper dependency, while expenditures by corporate PACs do not? Or vice versa? History demonstrates that when legislatures are given the power to regulate political speech to "protect democracy," they invariably write rules that protect their own incumbency and disadvantage their challengers.

The narrow *quid pro quo* definition of corruption that the Supreme Court relies upon—dollars exchanged for political favors—is frustratingly precise, but its precision is its virtue. It provides a clear, objective boundary. Bribery can be proven in a court of law. It requires evidence of an exchange. Lessig’s "dependency corruption," by contrast, is entirely subjective. He points to Congress voting for milk subsidies or exempting used-car dealers from regulations as evidence of corruption. But a senator from Wisconsin voting for milk subsidies is arguably acting in perfect accordance with their dependency on their constituents. To label policy outcomes we dislike as "institutional corruption" is to weaponize campaign finance law for partisan ends.

Furthermore, Lessig’s historical argument regarding the Framers is heavily sanitized. He relies heavily on the idealized rhetoric of Federalist No. 57. But the Framers were not naive idealists; they were pragmatic architects of power who were deeply suspicious of the very "People" Lessig claims they wanted total dependence upon. They constructed a republic, not a direct democracy, specifically to insulate the government from the shifting passions and factions of the populace. James Madison in Federalist No. 10 explicitly recognized that the "unequal distribution of property" is the most common and durable source of political factions. The Framers expected that wealthy interests would attempt to influence the government; they designed a system of checks and balances to prevent any one faction from dominating, not a system that empowers the government to silence the factions.

The reality of the post-*Citizens United* landscape is undoubtedly messy and chaotic. The influx of unlimited independent expenditures from corporations and wealthy individuals can be distorting and alarming. But the First Amendment was not designed to guarantee a neat, tidy, or perfectly egalitarian public square. It was designed to prevent the government from dictating who is allowed to participate in the debate.

If Congress wishes to diminish the relative power of corporate money, it has constitutional avenues available. Lessig's suggestions of small-dollar matching funds or democracy vouchers are excellent policy proposals that do not require censoring anyone's speech. If the government provides candidates with a robust public alternative to corporate funding, it can reduce the dependency on wealthy donors without violating the First Amendment. But we must not ask the Supreme Court to dilute the First Amendment by adopting vague, subjective definitions of "institutional corruption." The freedom of speech is too fragile to be subordinated to Congress’s desire to engineer the precise nature of its own dependencies.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Unlike the 'Lochnerism' Lessig cites, the Court’s decision in Citizens United is rooted in the explicit text of the First Amendment rather than invented economic liberties.
  2. 02Fundamental constitutional rights like free speech cannot be made contingent on public opinion polls or the level of faith the electorate has in democracy.
  3. 03Granting Congress the power to define and regulate 'improper dependencies' creates a conflict of interest where incumbents can silence challengers and favor specific political factions.
  4. 04The narrow 'quid pro quo' definition of corruption is superior because it provides a clear, objective legal boundary, whereas 'dependency corruption' is a subjective label for policy outcomes one dislikes.
  5. 05Lessig’s historical claim that the Framers intended a singular dependency on 'the People' ignores their design of a republic meant to manage, rather than silence, the influence of various factions and property interests.
  6. 06The government can address the influence of money through non-censorial means, such as public financing or democracy vouchers, without diluting First Amendment protections.
