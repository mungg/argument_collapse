"While veganism can be healthy for most people, it is not a universal solution and requires careful planning, nutritional knowledge, and medical stability to be executed safely."

Veganism is not “good for everyone” in the simple sense that one diet can be lifted from a book and applied safely to every human being. But neither is it a diet reserved for a special body type. For most people, a vegan diet can be healthy if it is planned with more seriousness than simply subtracting meat, eggs and dairy from the plate. The key question is not whether a person has the “right” body, but whether the person has the knowledge, access, medical stability and willingness to replace what animal foods had been providing.

The strongest case for veganism is that many Americans eat far too much saturated fat, too few fruits and vegetables, and too many calorie-dense processed foods. A well-designed vegan diet built around beans, lentils, whole grains, vegetables, fruits, nuts and seeds can be high in fiber, low in saturated fat and helpful for cholesterol, blood pressure and weight control. Vegetarian and vegan populations often show lower rates of obesity and heart disease risk factors. For people motivated by animal welfare or environmental concerns, those ethical benefits are also real and not reducible to nutrition.

But “well-designed” is doing a lot of work. Vitamin B12 is the clearest example. It is not reliably supplied by unfortified plant foods, and deficiency can cause anemia and neurological damage. A vegan who refuses supplements or fortified foods is not being purer; he is taking an unnecessary risk. Vitamin D, calcium, iodine, iron, zinc and omega-3 fats also require attention. They are not impossible to get, but they do not take care of themselves. A diet of white pasta, fries, soda and vegan cookies is vegan, but it is not health food.

Life stage matters. The American Dietetic Association has said that appropriately planned vegetarian diets, including vegan diets, can be suitable during pregnancy, lactation, infancy, childhood and adolescence. That is an important corrective to the idea that veganism is inherently dangerous for children or pregnant women. Still, the phrase “appropriately planned” is especially important here. Infants and young children have small stomachs and high nutrient needs; they cannot simply eat bulky adult portions of beans and greens. Pregnant women need reliable B12, iron, iodine, calcium, vitamin D and adequate calories. A vegan pregnancy can be healthy, but casual improvisation is not enough.

Medical history can make veganism more complicated. Someone with a history of an eating disorder may find that a highly restrictive diet gives moral cover to dangerous restriction. A patient with inflammatory bowel disease, severe irritable bowel symptoms or recent gastrointestinal surgery may not tolerate the fiber load that many wholesome vegan diets depend on. People with multiple food allergies — for example to soy, nuts and wheat — may have a harder time meeting protein and calorie needs without monotony or expensive specialty foods. Those with kidney disease may need to monitor potassium, phosphorus and protein sources carefully. None of these situations makes veganism automatically impossible, but they argue for medical and dietetic supervision rather than slogans.

Economics cuts both ways. The cheapest foods in the supermarket include rice, beans, oats, potatoes, peanut butter and frozen vegetables. In that sense, vegan eating need not be elitist. But access is uneven. A person living in a food desert, working two jobs, without good cooking facilities, may find it much easier to obtain cheap fast food than dried lentils and fresh produce. Meanwhile, the fashionable version of veganism — artisanal nut cheeses, fake meats, imported superfoods — can be expensive and misleadingly presented as the norm.

So the honest answer is that veganism can be good for many people, perhaps most, but it is not automatically good for everyone under all circumstances. It is a dietary pattern with potential advantages and predictable pitfalls. The pitfalls are manageable for people who supplement B12, eat varied whole foods, get enough calories and seek guidance when medical issues are present. Veganism should not be dismissed as a fringe experiment, but neither should it be sold as a universal cure. The moral commitment may be simple; the nutritional execution is not.

"While veganism can be safe and beneficial for many, it is not a universal prescription because its success depends on careful planning, supplementation, and an individual's specific physiological and socioeconomic circumstances."

Veganism, properly executed, is a viable diet for most healthy adults. But "most" is doing real work in that sentence. The question of whether a plant-only diet suits everyone is not primarily about willpower or grocery budgets — it is about physiology, life stage, and the often-underappreciated difficulty of replacing nutrients that animal foods deliver in dense, bioavailable forms.

The Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics has long held that well-planned vegan diets are appropriate for all stages of life, including pregnancy, infancy, and adolescence. That position is supported by large cohort studies — the Adventist Health Study and EPIC-Oxford among them — showing vegetarians and vegans tend to have lower rates of obesity, hypertension, type 2 diabetes, and ischemic heart disease. These are real benefits, and they are unlikely to be entirely explained by the fact that committed vegans also tend to exercise more and smoke less.

The catch is in the phrase "well-planned." Vitamin B12 is not produced by plants in forms humans can use, and B12 deficiency — which can cause irreversible neurological damage — is documented at meaningful rates among long-term vegans who do not supplement. Iron from plants is non-heme and less readily absorbed, especially in the presence of phytates and tannins; zinc has similar issues. Omega-3 conversion from plant ALA to EPA and DHA is inefficient, and the efficiency varies genetically. Calcium, iodine, vitamin D, and choline all require deliberate attention. A vegan who eats kale and lentils on purpose will likely thrive; a vegan who lives on pasta and Oreos — which technically qualifies — will not.

Body type and medical history also shape the answer. People with iron-deficiency anemia, particularly menstruating women, may struggle more on a vegan diet without aggressive supplementation. Those with a history of disordered eating should approach any restrictive regime cautiously; the line between principled abstention and orthorexia can blur. Patients on certain medications, people with absorptive disorders like Crohn's or celiac disease, and those who have had bariatric surgery face higher hurdles. Pregnant women and young children require especially careful planning because the costs of a B12 or DHA shortfall are not measured in fatigue but in development. None of this makes veganism impossible for these groups — it makes it harder, and it makes the supervision of a knowledgeable clinician or dietitian closer to a necessity than a luxury.

There is also a cultural caveat. Saying "anyone can do this" elides the reality that in food deserts, in some immigrant family contexts, and on tight budgets, the vegan diet that nutrition journals describe is not the vegan diet on offer.

So: is veganism good for everyone? It can be safe for almost everyone and beneficial for many. But "good for everyone" as a blanket prescription overstates what we know. The honest answer is that veganism is good for people who plan it well, supplement what plants cannot supply, and honestly assess their own bodies before committing.

The debate over whether veganism is uniformly beneficial often centers on willpower and economic access. Tara Parker-Pope is right to note the physical, social, and economic challenges, but the question of body type and medical history pushes deeper. A vegan diet can be nutritionally complete and is associated with lower risks of heart disease, hypertension, and certain cancers. However, the idea that discipline alone can guarantee good health for every person on a vegan diet misunderstands human biology. Individual variation in genetics, digestive function, and medical history means that veganism, while excellent for many, is not a universal prescription.

Advocates often point to the American Dietetic Association’s 2009 position that well-planned vegan diets are appropriate for all life stages. But the operative phrase is “well-planned,” and the planning required is not equally straightforward for everyone. The universal hurdle is vitamin B12, which must be obtained from fortified foods or supplements. Even then, some people have impaired absorption due to pernicious anemia or gastrointestinal conditions, and subclinical B12 deficiency can develop despite careful supplementation. Iron presents another hurdle. The non-heme iron in plants is absorbed less efficiently than heme iron from animals. For menstruating women or individuals with higher iron needs, maintaining adequate ferritin levels on a vegan diet can be a constant struggle, especially for those with undiagnosed absorption issues like celiac disease. Similarly, conversion of alpha-linolenic acid to the long-chain omega-3s EPA and DHA varies significantly; some people carry genetic variants that make this conversion extremely inefficient, potentially compromising brain and cardiovascular health if they rely solely on flax or walnuts.

Medical history matters acutely. For those with irritable bowel syndrome or inflammatory bowel disease, a diet high in legumes, whole grains, and certain fibrous vegetables can trigger painful bloating, gas, or flare-ups. These individuals may find that a vegan diet severely limits their tolerable food choices, making it difficult to meet calorie and protein needs without constant discomfort. A person with a history of eating disorders might also use veganism’s restrictive framework to mask orthorexia or anorexia, and the ethical purity of the diet can make it harder for clinicians to challenge. Furthermore, food allergies—soy, tree nuts, wheat—can carve away the protein and fat cornerstones of a vegan diet, leaving few whole-food alternatives that provide comparable amino acid profiles.

The life stage and physiological context further complicate the picture. During pregnancy and breastfeeding, nutritional demands for choline, iodine, and DHA rise dramatically. While these can theoretically be met with targeted supplementation and careful food selection, the margin for error shrinks, and not everyone has the nutritional literacy or access to the right supplements. Infants and young children raised vegan need meticulously planned diets to support rapid brain growth; a parent who simply eliminates animal products without expert guidance risks deficiencies that can have lasting consequences.

These caveats do not undermine veganism as an ethical or environmental choice, nor do they deny that millions thrive on plant-based diets. But the framing of the question—that discipline and means are the only prerequisites—ignores the messy reality of individual physiology. What is nourishing for one person can be debilitating for another. Veganism is not inherently good or bad, but its goodness is contingent on a person’s unique biological makeup and health status. A truly humane approach to diet must allow room for cases where including animal products is the more medically responsible path. One size never fits all.

While the ethical and environmental arguments for veganism are compelling, treating a strict plant-based diet as a universal biological ideal ignores the complex realities of human physiology. We are not a one-size-fits-all species, and a regimen that leaves one person feeling vibrant can leave another fatigued and malnourished.

The concept of bio-individuality is crucial here. Nutritional science heavily supports eating more plants; research consistently links high vegetable consumption to lower rates of heart disease and type 2 diabetes. However, obtaining adequate nutrients solely from plants requires highly efficient metabolic conversions that not all bodies perform equally well. For instance, plant-based sources of iron are significantly less bioavailable than the heme iron found in meat, making anemia a real risk for certain individuals. Similarly, while flaxseeds provide ALA omega-3 fatty acids, many people possess genetic variations that make them poor converters of ALA into the crucial EPA and DHA forms our brains require. A diet devoid of animal products also entirely lacks naturally occurring Vitamin B12, necessitating careful supplementation.

Beyond genetics, medical history and life stage heavily dictate dietary needs. Pregnant women, growing children, and individuals managing specific metabolic or autoimmune conditions often require dense, easily absorbed nutrients. For these populations, relying entirely on plant sources demands meticulous meal planning that borders on a full-time job.

Furthermore, this biological reality intersects with the socioeconomic privilege mentioned in the prompt. Outside affluent urban centers, acquiring a diverse, balanced vegan diet—complete with the necessary fortified foods and high-quality fresh produce—remains structurally difficult.

Ultimately, while reducing our reliance on industrial meat is an undeniably positive goal for public health, strict veganism is not metabolically or practically feasible for everyone. We should encourage plant-based eating without prescribing it as a universal mandate.

The trouble with the current enthusiasm for veganism is that it confuses “survivable” with “optimal.” Yes, a careful vegan can assemble a diet that meets the major nutrient targets. But the amount of planning and supplementation required should make us pause before declaring veganism good for everyone.

Animal foods are nutritionally dense. Eggs, fish, meat and dairy provide complete protein, vitamin B12, iron, zinc, calcium, vitamin D in some foods, and long-chain omega-3 fats in forms the body can readily use. Plant foods are valuable and should make up a large share of the diet, but they often come with limitations: non-heme iron is less absorbable; zinc can be affected by phytates; plant omega-3s must be converted inefficiently to EPA and DHA; and B12 is essentially absent unless fortified or supplemented.

Vegan advocates often reply that supplements solve the problem. Sometimes they do. But a diet dependent on pills and fortified substitutes is not obviously the universal ideal. Supplementation requires knowledge, consistency and quality control. People forget. Children refuse foods. Teenagers experiment. Poor families run out of money. Elderly people lose appetite. In real life, nutrition is not designed by a dietitian and executed by a robot.

The most vulnerable groups deserve special caution. Pregnancy and early childhood are periods when deficiencies can have lasting consequences. A breastfeeding vegan mother who is B12 deficient may have an infant at risk. Adolescents, who already have high nutrient needs and erratic eating habits, may use veganism as a cloak for restriction. Older adults need enough protein and calories to maintain muscle; bulky high-fiber diets may not always help.

Nor should we pretend that all animal foods are the same. A diet built on processed meats, oversized steaks and sugary dairy desserts is plainly unhealthy. But that does not prove that modest amounts of fish, eggs, yogurt or lean meat are harmful. Traditional diets around the world have often combined grains, legumes and vegetables with small quantities of animal foods. That pattern may be more forgiving than strict veganism while still avoiding the excesses of the American diet.

Veganism can be a principled choice for informed adults. It may improve health when it replaces fast food and processed meat with beans, vegetables and whole grains. But as general advice, it is too restrictive and too dependent on supplementation to be called good for everyone.

A better public message would be less ideological: eat more plants, fewer processed foods, and smaller amounts of better-raised animal products. That recommendation will help far more people than insisting that the healthiest plate must contain no animal food at all.
