"While genetically engineered crops may be biologically safe, the primary controversy surrounding GMOs stems from the aggressive application of utility patents that criminalize traditional farming practices and create dangerous corporate monopolies."

While Pamela Ronald makes a forceful case for the biological safety and agronomic utility of genetically engineered crops, she swiftly brushes past the legal and economic architecture that makes the technology so deeply controversial. For many farmers, advocates, and consumers, the primary issue with GMOs today is not the science of recombinant DNA; it is the aggressive application of intellectual property law to self-replicating organisms. By focusing almost exclusively on molecular biology, Ronald ignores how utility patents have been weaponized to fundamentally alter the relationship between farmers and the seeds they plant, resulting in an unprecedented concentration of corporate power.

Ronald addresses the issue of corporate control by arguing that farmers are smart businesspeople who willingly buy GE seeds because it benefits their bottom line. She writes, "It is unlikely that experienced and skilled farmers would buy GE seeds if their farm operations did not benefit economically." She even cites the recent Supreme Court case, *Bowman v. Monsanto*, as evidence of "the lengths farmers will go to obtain the seed."

This is a profound misreading of the *Bowman* case and the reality of modern agricultural markets. The *Bowman* decision did not simply highlight a farmer's desire for a product; it enshrined the total enclosure of the agricultural commons. Vernon Bowman was an Indiana farmer who bought commodity soybeans from a local grain elevator to use as a late-season second crop. Because those beans were virtually guaranteed to contain Monsanto's patented Roundup Ready genetic sequence, he applied glyphosate to weed his fields. The Supreme Court ruled unanimously that Bowman had infringed on Monsanto’s utility patent simply because the seeds naturally reproduced themselves.

For 10,000 years, the fundamental nature of agriculture has been based on the fact that seeds reproduce. A farmer harvests a crop, saves a portion of the seed, and replants it the following year. The introduction of utility patents on genetically engineered plants in the United States fundamentally criminalized this ancient practice. When a farmer buys GE seed today, they do not own the seed; they are merely licensing the technology for a single season. If they save seeds for replanting, they are subject to devastating lawsuits.

Ronald attempts to mitigate this by pointing out that farmers have bought hybrid seeds for decades, which also lose their traits in the second generation and thus discourage seed saving. But there is a massive legal difference between a biological limitation (hybrids) and a legal prohibition enforced by private investigators and the threat of bankruptcy.

This legal framework has led to massive consolidation in the seed industry. Today, a handful of multinational agrochemical corporations control the vast majority of the commercial seed supply. Because these companies hold patents on the foundational genetic traits, independent breeders and public researchers are heavily restricted from using commercial GE varieties to develop locally adapted crops. This stifles innovation and ensures that the trajectory of agricultural research is dictated solely by what is profitable for shareholders, rather than what is best for the public good.

We can agree with Ronald that the process of genetic engineering is not inherently dangerous to human health. But the legal technology that surrounds GMOs—the utility patent—is highly toxic to a fair and competitive agricultural economy. Until we address the monopolistic practices and the loss of farmer autonomy that define the current biotech industry, assurances about the molecular safety of the crops will continue to ring hollow.
