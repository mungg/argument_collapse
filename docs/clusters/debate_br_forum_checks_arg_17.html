Miller Is Right, But She Doesn't Go Far Enough

Lisa Miller's essay performs a valuable service by puncturing the mystique of constitutional checks and balances at exactly the moment when liberal elites are reaching for it like a security blanket. She is correct that the veto-point structure of American government has historically protected elite minorities far more than vulnerable ones, that federalism is more often a tool of reaction than resistance, and that the courts have spent most of their history defending property against labor and capital against the public. Her account of the long failure to enact universal health care is exemplary political analysis. But her prescription—constitutional reform plus mass mobilization through the Democratic Party—is far too modest given the diagnosis she offers, and it is in tension with her own deepest insights.

Let me start with what Miller gets right and then explain why the implications run further than she acknowledges. The core insight of her piece is that "checks on power" is not a neutral phrase. Every veto point is a structural advantage for whichever party benefits from the status quo. In a society with deeply unequal distributions of wealth and political access, the status quo is by definition the distribution that serves the powerful. Therefore institutional designs that emphasize restraint over capacity will, on average, protect the powerful. This is not a contingent feature of American government; it follows logically from the unequal distribution of political resources. Miller's analysis here is sharper than most political scientists writing about American institutions, who tend to treat veto points as politically neutral.

But notice where this argument leads. If the deep problem is unequal distribution of political resources, then institutional reform that does not address that underlying inequality will produce only modest gains. Eliminate the Electoral College and the same corporate interests will still dominate campaign finance. Abolish the Senate filibuster and the same lobbying apparatus will still dominate legislation. Expand the House and the same gerrymandering technologies will be deployed in new districts. The veto points are not just constitutional artifacts; they are codifications of a society in which a small class possesses overwhelming material, informational, and organizational advantages. You cannot democratize the polity without democratizing the economy.

Miller gestures at this when she notes that "tempering extremism in the United States requires constraining elites more than constraining majorities." Yes—but constraining elites means more than political reform. It means breaking up concentrated economic power: the platform monopolies, the financialized health sector, the private equity machinery hollowing out housing and labor markets. It means restoring countervailing power through mass-membership unions, not just public-sector locals but the kind of cross-sector industrial unionism that briefly disciplined American capital in the mid-twentieth century. It means a tax system that actually taxes wealth, not just income, and that does so confiscatorily at the top. It means the kind of structural reform that the New Deal coalition fought for and that has been steadily unwound since the 1970s.

Miller's frame of "checks and balances" versus "mass democracy" obscures this. The genuine alternative to elite checks on government is not just popular mobilization; it is popular institutions powerful enough to check elites in their actual habitat, which is the workplace, the housing market, and the boardroom, not just the Capitol. The American left's century-long fascination with constitutional politics—first with using courts to advance civil rights, now with reforming the Constitution to make space for redistribution—has consistently underestimated how much political democracy depends on prior economic democratization.

This brings me to my second concern with Miller's prescription. She places her hopes in the Democratic Party as the vehicle for mass democratic mobilization. She acknowledges the difficulties: "deep divisions between liberals and the left," voters who have "opted out of partisan politics altogether." But she does not seriously confront the possibility that the Democratic Party as currently constituted is structurally incapable of being that vehicle. The party's donor base, its strategic class, its think-tank infrastructure, and its electoral coalition are all built around managing rather than transforming the existing political economy. When Bernie Sanders ran a campaign that genuinely mobilized working-class voters across racial lines around an explicitly anti-oligarchic program, the party apparatus closed ranks against him—not because he was unelectable (he polled well against Trump) but because his program threatened the material interests of the people who fund and staff the party.

This is not a complaint about specific personalities. It is a structural observation. The Democratic Party in 2025 is the party of the credentialed professional class, of the metropolitan FIRE sector, of Silicon Valley and Hollywood and pharmaceutical research. Its working-class support is real but residual, and its working-class voters are exiting—first the white ones, increasingly the Latino and Black ones too. A party with this coalition cannot lead an anti-oligarchic mass movement because its core funders and staff are the oligarchy or its functionaries. Asking the Democratic Party to mobilize against elite power is like asking a corporation to abolish profit.

What does this imply? Either a serious effort to capture the Democratic Party from below—which the Sanders campaigns attempted and which the Democratic Socialists of America and various left formations are still pursuing—or the construction of alternative political vehicles outside the party. Both are difficult. Both are necessary. But pretending that the existing Democratic Party can play the role Miller assigns it is wishful thinking of the same kind she diagnoses in the checks-and-balances liturgy.

Finally, a note on time horizons. Miller writes as if constitutional reform is a long but plausible project: "Constitutional change has happened before, and it can happen again." This is true in principle. But the climate crisis, the consolidation of platform monopolies, and the accelerating erosion of democratic norms under Trump's second administration are operating on a timescale that constitutional amendment cannot match. We do not have forty-two years to ratify a national voting rights bill. We have perhaps a decade, optimistically, before climate and political crises foreclose options that are now still open.

This suggests that the strategic emphasis should be less on constitutional reform—important as it is in the long run—and more on building organizations that can exercise power outside the formal political system: militant unions, tenant unions, mutual aid networks, disciplined cadre organizations, and yes, the willingness to use disruptive direct action when the channels of formal democracy are blocked. Miller is right that mass politics is a check on power. But mass politics is not just voting and lobbying. It is also strikes, boycotts, blockades, and the kinds of disruption that historically have forced elites to concede ground.

The lead essay opens a door. We should walk all the way through it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Political and constitutional reforms are ineffective unless they are preceded by the democratization of the economy and the breaking up of concentrated private power.
  2. 02The Democratic Party, as currently constituted and funded by the professional and corporate classes, is structurally incapable of leading a genuine anti-oligarchic mass movement.
  3. 03True popular power requires the cultivation of institutions that can check elites in the workplace and housing markets, such as industrial and tenant unions, rather than just focusing on the Capitol.
  4. 04The urgent timescales of the climate and political crises necessitate a strategy centered on disruptive direct action and extra-parliamentary organization rather than the slow process of constitutional amendment.
