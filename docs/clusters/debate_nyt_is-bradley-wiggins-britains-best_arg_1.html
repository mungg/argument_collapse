The question "Is Bradley Wiggins Britain's best?" is the wrong question. The right one is hiding in the second paragraph of the prompt: shouldn't Britain, having more or less invented modern competitive sport, be producing rather more champions than it does?

What Wiggins's win actually demonstrates is the cure for this disease, and it has nothing to do with sporting heritage. It has to do with money, expertise, and ruthless focus. Team Sky took the same approach to the road and, in three years, produced a Tour winner. The "marginal gains" philosophy is essentially the application of professional management consultancy to athletic performance, and it works.

Compare that with the sports Britain claims to love. The Premier League is awash in money, but almost none of it is spent on developing English players; we import talent and then wonder why the national side underperforms. The LTA spends millions on tennis and produces, in any given decade, roughly one player of genuine quality. County cricket meanders. Rugby union is healthier, but still episodic at international level.

So the answer to capitalising on our sporting history is, ironically, to stop trading on it. Heritage is not a development programme. Wiggins didn't win because Britain invented the bicycle (we didn't, quite) or because he was inspired by Tom Simpson. He won because a serious, well-funded, evidence-based system put him in a position to win. Replicate that across other sports, and the champions will follow. Refuse to, and we'll keep waiting.
