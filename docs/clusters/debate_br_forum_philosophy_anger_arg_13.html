**Anger, Politics, and the Anger of the Oppressed**

Agnes Callard's essay arrives at a striking conclusion: long-term oppression morally damages the oppressed, because the anger of the wronged is itself a form of moral corruption. Either the oppressed acquiesce, which is bad, or they get angry, which is also bad — anger being, on her account, ineliminably linked to grudge-bearing and vengeance. The essay closes with a personal story about her grandmother, a Holocaust survivor whose rage toward a German child was not, Callard says, innocent.

I want to take this conclusion seriously and to disagree with it sharply. I think Callard's framing — for all its philosophical ingenuity — leads her into a politically dangerous place, and that the dangers come from the abstraction at which her argument operates.

Start with the question her essay foregrounds but does not really answer: whose anger are we talking about? The whole tradition Callard surveys — Seneca, Nussbaum, Strawson, Flanagan — treats anger as a generic phenomenon. Someone steals from you; you get angry; we ask whether your anger is rational. But the phenomenology of being wronged once by a peer is very different from the phenomenology of being wronged systematically by an entire social order. Audre Lorde's "The Uses of Anger," Mari Matsuda's work on hate speech, the writings of James Baldwin on Black rage — these are all attempts to think about anger in conditions where the wrongdoer is not a discrete individual who can apologize on Wednesday but a structural feature of the world that cannot be addressed by any single act of restitution.

In such conditions, Callard's Argument for Grudges takes on a different complexion. The "reason to be angry forever" is not a logical artifact about unchanging past facts; it is the entirely live reason that the wrong is still happening. The Black American who is angry about police violence is not nursing a grudge about something resolved on Wednesday. She is responding to a present and ongoing injury. To frame her anger as "moral corruption" — even with the qualification that the alternative is worse — is to misdescribe her situation.

Callard's grandmother provides a different but related case. The Holocaust was not, in any meaningful sense, over for Callard's grandmother. Her son's cerebral palsy, her surviving relatives' nightmares, the destroyed community, the rebuilt synagogue blown up again — these were ongoing presences in her life. Her refusal to allow her granddaughter to march with a German girl was not, I would suggest, best described as colonized fantasy life or as nursing wrath to keep it warm. It was a reasoned (if perhaps mistaken) judgment about the meaning of certain gestures in a world that had not in fact moved past the conditions that produced the Holocaust.

I do not want to deny that political anger can deform those who hold it. It can; the literature on the psychic costs of long-term oppression is extensive. Fanon's writings on colonization, Du Bois on double consciousness, more recent work on racial trauma — all of this is part of the moral landscape Callard is gesturing at. But the response to that literature is not to call the anger of the oppressed "moral corruption" and leave it there. The response is to insist on changing the conditions that produce the anger.

Callard knows this; she would say her point is that, given the conditions, no perfectly clean response is available. Fine. But the framing matters. To name the anger of victims as "corruption" — even reluctantly — places the moral burden in exactly the wrong location. The corruption belongs to the oppressor, who created the situation in which only corrupted responses were available. To redistribute the corruption equally — "oppressors corrupt themselves, but the oppressed are also corrupted, just to a lesser degree" — is to obscure the asymmetry that makes the situation morally legible in the first place.

Here I think Callard's debt to Nietzsche shows. The Genealogy of Morals contains, alongside its real insights, a fundamentally reactionary suggestion: that the morality of the oppressed is constituted by ressentiment, that the slaves invented Christianity to take revenge on the strong, that pity and equality are weapons. This framing has had a long and ugly afterlife. When Callard writes that "the oppressors have made the oppressed morally worse people," she is in Nietzsche's vicinity, and she should be careful about the company she keeps. The claim that suffering ennobles is false; so is the claim that suffering corrupts. Suffering does various things to various people, and the moral assessment of those who suffer should not be loaded onto philosophical schemata about the structure of anger.

Now turn to the politics of Callard's claim about vengeance. She argues that revenge is rational because it "turns the principle of my action into a rule for your conduct toward me." This is a philosophical reconstruction of what cycles of retaliation look like from the inside. It captures something real. But it also abstracts away from the practical question that every political community has to answer: how do you stop the cycle?

Callard's argument suggests that, qua angry person, you have no reason to stop the cycle. Other considerations — prudence, the well-being of others, exhaustion — may give you reasons, but anger itself does not. This is, I think, true in a narrow sense and dangerously misleading in a broader sense. Anger is not the only voice in the wronged person's deliberation; nor is it isolated from the political and ethical resources available to her. Truth and reconciliation commissions, restorative justice practices, the long Christian tradition of forgiveness, the Jewish concept of *teshuvah*, the Islamic *sulh* — these are not external constraints imposed on anger from outside but resources that the wronged themselves draw upon to shape and transform their anger. To treat anger as a freestanding logical structure that "rationally" demands eternal revenge is to abstract it from the cultural and ethical contexts in which it actually lives.

What makes Callard's essay politically dangerous, in my view, is not its diagnosis — much of which is acute — but its conclusion that purification is a fiction. If purification is a fiction, then practical movements like nonviolent resistance, restorative justice, and political forgiveness are exposed as either dishonest (concealing their inner vengefulness) or self-defeating (suppressing the anger that gives them moral force). I think this is just empirically wrong. The civil rights movement was neither dishonest nor self-defeating. The South African Truth and Reconciliation Commission, for all its limitations, was neither dishonest nor self-defeating. These are real human practices that channeled anger into something other than vengeance, without thereby acquiescing in injustice.

I will grant Callard her starting observation: the project of carving anger neatly into "good" and "bad" sides is harder than the philosophical literature makes it look. There is no clean line. The wronged are not innocent. Innocence is not on offer. But the conclusion she draws — that anger is moral corruption all the way down — is too strong, and it lands in a place that disserves the very victims it claims to defend.

The political task is not to declare innocence impossible and shrug; it is to build the practices, institutions, and traditions that allow anger to be morally serious without being eternally vengeful. Those practices exist. They are imperfect. They are not philosophers' fictions. And they are what we need more of, not less.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The essay fails to distinguish between a single interpersonal wrong and systematic, ongoing structural oppression where anger remains a rational response to a present injury rather than a grudge over the past.
  2. 02Defining the anger of victims as corruption unfairly redistributes the moral burden, obscuring the fundamental asymmetry between the oppressor who created the conditions and the oppressed who must navigate them.
  3. 03The claim that suffering and anger necessarily corrupt the oppressed echoes a reactionary Nietzschean framework that risks pathologizing the moral character of those who have been wronged.
  4. 04Treating anger as a freestanding logical structure that demands eternal revenge ignores the cultural, ethical, and religious resources that humans successfully use to shape and transform their anger.
  5. 05The existence of real-world movements like nonviolent resistance and restorative justice proves that anger can be channeled into morally serious protest without descending into the 'limitless violence' or 'corruption' the author posits.
