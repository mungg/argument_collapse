"Anger, including grudges and revenge, should be understood as a rational social-functional tool for bargaining and norm enforcement rather than as a form of moral corruption or a timeless register of brokenness."

Agnes Callard’s essay is philosophical, but one way to test it is to ask what anger is for. Why would creatures like us have such a costly emotion—physiologically arousing, cognitively narrowing, socially risky, sometimes self-destructive—unless it did some work? Callard’s answer is that anger holds others morally responsible through grudges and revenge. I think an evolutionary and social-psychological perspective partly supports her, partly revises her, and partly lowers the temperature of the debate.

Anger is not simply a moral error, as some Stoic accounts suggest. Nor is it simply moral perception, as some sentimentalist accounts imply. It is a bargaining and enforcement emotion. It arises when we perceive that someone has undervalued our interests, violated a norm, or imposed a cost without sufficient justification. Its bodily and expressive features—raised voice, glare, readiness to confront—communicate that the current terms of interaction are unacceptable. Anger says: adjust your behavior, compensate me, show respect, or face consequences.

This helps explain why anger is so closely tied to both justice and revenge. In small-scale social life, norms are only as good as the willingness to enforce them. If theft, insult, betrayal, or free-riding never provokes anger, opportunists learn that violation is cheap. Anger can function as a commitment device: I am so motivated to retaliate that you should not exploit me. Even disproportionate anger may have strategic value if it deters future harm. A person known to “hold a grudge” may be less likely to be cheated.

In this respect Callard is right against those who treat grudges and revenge as mere irrational residues. They can be intelligible. Refusing to forget may protect against repeated exploitation. Retaliation may communicate seriousness when words fail. The desire to make the wrongdoer suffer can be part of a social technology of deterrence. It is not accidental that human beings across cultures have developed practices of punishment, feud, honor, compensation, shaming, and exclusion. These are ways of enforcing norms in creatures who cannot rely on angelic goodwill.

But this perspective also complicates Callard’s claim that once one has a reason for anger, one has a reason forever. The function of anger is not to register timeless propositions. It regulates relationships and expectations. If the offender repairs the damage, apologizes sincerely, accepts lowered status, and credibly commits not to repeat the offense, then many of anger’s functions have been served. Continued anger may still deter, but it may also waste energy, destroy valuable cooperation, and signal unreasonableness. From a practical standpoint, a grudge has costs as well as benefits.

This does not mean that anger is irrational once amends are made. It means its rationality is context-sensitive. If I live among unreliable people who use apologies to reset the clock before exploiting me again, grudges are adaptive. If I live in a community with strong enforcement, trustworthy apologies, and reputational memory, endless anger is less useful and more damaging. The question is not whether the past fact remains true, but what emotional posture best manages present and future interaction in light of that fact.

Callard may object that she is discussing rationality in a moral, not instrumental, sense. But moral life in social animals cannot be cleanly separated from the management of interaction. Norms exist because beings like us must coordinate, cooperate, compete, mate, trade, raise children, share risks, and resolve conflicts. Anger’s moral meaning is entangled with its social function. It is not merely contemplation of brokenness; it is a readiness to renegotiate terms.

The revenge argument also looks different through this lens. Revenge is not primarily “teaching someone a lesson” by universalizing his principle. It is imposing a cost to change incentives, restore status, and communicate commitment. If you steal from me and I retaliate, I show you and others that I am not an easy target. I may also restore my standing in the eyes of third parties. In honor cultures, failure to retaliate can invite further predation because it signals weakness. This does not make revenge morally ideal, but it explains why it feels compulsory.

Modern institutions attempt to replace personal revenge with third-party enforcement. Courts, police, employers, professional associations, religious authorities, and informal reputational networks all say, in effect: you need not retaliate personally; the group will impose costs. When these institutions work, private anger can subside more easily. When they fail, anger intensifies. People cling to grudges when they believe no one else will remember. They seek revenge when they believe no one else will enforce.

This has implications for the anger of oppressed groups. Callard says oppression makes the oppressed morally worse by forcing them into anger or acquiescence. A functional account would say oppression places people in environments where anger remains chronically adaptive because violations are repeated and enforcement is unreliable or hostile. If police will not protect you, if courts will not believe you, if employers can exploit you, if public narratives erase your suffering, then mistrust and anger are not simply moral corruption. They are survival strategies under bad institutional conditions.

That does not make them harmless. Strategies adaptive under threat can become destructive in safer contexts, or can misfire against innocent targets. Hypervigilance may protect and distort. Retaliatory readiness may deter and escalate. Group-based anger may mobilize justice and fuel prejudice. But calling these responses “corruption” may obscure their environmental rationality. The better question is: what conditions keep anger useful, and what conditions would make less anger safe?

The answer is not simply personal virtue. It is reliable accountability. If workers know that unsafe employers will be sanctioned, they need not rely on private fury. If women know harassment will be taken seriously, anger need not do all the work of making harm visible. If racial minorities know the law protects them equally, intergroup anger may diminish. If citizens know officials will tell the truth during a pandemic, conspiracy-minded rage may have less fuel. Trustworthy institutions are anger-reduction technologies.

April 2020 provides a vivid case. The coronavirus pandemic has generated anger at officials, neighbors, foreigners, corporations, journalists, protesters, and experts. Some of this anger tracks real failures: delayed testing, inadequate protective equipment, confused messaging, workers asked to risk illness without support. Some of it is displaced fear. The difference is not always obvious from the inside. Under uncertainty, anger searches for agents. A virus is not morally accountable, but people are, and so the mind gravitates toward blame.

This agent-seeking tendency is useful when there really are negligent agents. It is dangerous when complex systems fail or when information is incomplete. Anger simplifies causal networks into culpable targets. Sometimes simplification is necessary for action; sometimes it produces scapegoats. A mature public culture must distinguish accountable responsibility from the mere need to be angry at someone.

Callard’s essay is strongest as a warning that anger’s internal experience is not a reliable guide to its moral limits. Anger feels like clarity. It often overclaims. It treats the offender’s suffering as informative: if he hurts, justice is being done. But from a social-functional view, suffering is at most a means. The real questions are whether norms are reinforced, victims protected, relationships repaired where possible, and future violations deterred. Revenge often performs poorly by these measures because it escalates conflict, invites counter-revenge, and damages third parties.

Still, anti-anger theorists underestimate the risk of under-enforcement. A world without anger may sound peaceful, but it may also be a world in which the shameless prosper. Some people respond only to credible threat. The challenge is to preserve anger’s signal and enforcement functions while reducing its tendency toward escalation. Human societies have addressed this through proportionality norms, compensation schedules, apology rituals, mediation, courts, cooling-off periods, and moral education. None purifies anger. They regulate it.

I therefore would revise Callard’s conclusion. We can be good in a bad world, but not by relying on unregulated emotional instincts or by pretending to transcend them. We are animals with bargaining emotions trying to build moral orders. Anger is one of those emotions. It detects undervaluation, mobilizes resistance, and threatens costs. It also narrows attention, inflates certainty, and enjoys domination. Grudges and revenge are not irrational mistakes; they are rough tools. But rough tools are not destiny.

The practical lesson is institutional and personal. Personally, when angry, ask: what norm was violated, what repair is needed, what future behavior must be deterred, and am I seeking those ends or merely savoring harm? Institutionally, build systems that make accountability credible enough that victims do not need to keep their own rage alive forever. Anger is not a philosopher’s fiction, but neither is its discipline. It is one of the oldest tasks of human social life.
