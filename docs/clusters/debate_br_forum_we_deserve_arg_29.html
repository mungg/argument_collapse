Diversified Gemini

"The responding essay argues that the decentralized market, rather than state-led planning, is the most effective system for fostering the 'eolithic' or playful discovery-driven potential of generative AI."

Evgeny Morozov’s “The AI We Deserve” is a masterclass in intellectual history, drawing a line from the cybernetics of the 1950s to the large language models (LLMs) of today, all while keeping a critical eye on the political economy that shaped them. His distinction between “instrumental reason”—the teleological, problem-solving mindset of the Cold War military-industrial complex—and the more open-ended, playful “ecological reason” championed by Hans Otto Storm is a brilliant heuristic for understanding what feels so simultaneously magical and hollow about generative AI. However, in his eagerness to rescue the “eolithic” potential of AI from the clutches of neoliberalism, Morozov overlooks the extent to which markets themselves are deeply eolithic systems, and how the very generative AI he critiques is flourishing precisely because it operates within a decentralized, market-driven ecosystem.

Morozov paints the market and the “Efficiency Lobby” as monolithic engines of rigid instrumental reason, forcing users into the narrow roles of “isolated, possibly alienated, postmodern consumers.” He contrasts this bleak, profit-driven grid with the utopian vision of Salvador Allende’s Project Cybersyn, which he holds up as a model of using technology to foster “deliberation on national, industry, and company-wide alternatives.” This framing relies on a profound misreading of both how markets function and the historical reality of top-down state planning.

Let us return to Storm’s prehistoric flâneur, wandering the stonefield, fed, contented, and picking up a stone that suggests a new use. Morozov correctly identifies this as a process of discovery, driven by serendipity and subjective valuation rather than predefined goals. But this is exactly the process that Friedrich Hayek—whom Morozov mentions only in passing—identified as the core mechanism of the free market. A market is not a bureaucracy designed to solve a single, predefined problem; it is a sprawling, emergent network of millions of individuals, each possessing localized, tacit knowledge, engaged in constant discovery and recalibration.

When an entrepreneur in a market economy looks at a new technology—whether it is the internal combustion engine or an OpenAI API—they are engaged in eolithic bricolage. They are asking, “What can this stone do?” The market provides the feedback mechanism (profit or loss) that determines whether their subjective valuation aligns with the needs and desires of society. The sheer explosion of diverse applications built on top of foundation models over the past two years—from Morozov’s own language-learning app to tools for independent filmmakers, accessibility aids for the visually impaired, and decentralized scientific research assistants—is a testament to the staggering eolithic capacity of a decentralized market.

Conversely, the state-socialist projects Morozov lionizes were the very embodiment of rigid, top-down instrumental reason. Project Cybersyn was designed to manage a command economy from a central control room. It was an attempt to replace the decentralized discovery process of the market with a unified, teleological algorithm controlled by political elites. While Morozov praises it for empowering workers to make “informed decisions,” the historical reality of central planning invariably involves suppressing heterogeneous, vernacular experimentation in favor of state-mandated efficiency targets. If the “Efficiency Lobby” has a historical home, it is in the Gosplans and Ministries of Production of the 20th century, not the messy, iterative, bottom-up chaos of Silicon Valley open-source repositories and startup incubators.

Morozov complains that OpenAI charges a fee for API calls and that the energy costs are heavily subsidized by venture capital, arguing that this “privatizes the stonefield.” But this ignores the massive capital expenditure required to create the “stone” in the first place. LLMs do not simply wash up on the beach; they require billions of dollars in research, compute, and human capital to develop. The market mechanism—including venture capital and API fees—is the method by which society allocates resources to undertake these massive risks. Furthermore, the market is already rapidly driving down these costs. The proliferation of powerful, free, open-weights models (like Meta’s LLaMA or Mistral) running locally on consumer hardware proves that the market is democratizing access to the stonefield faster than any state bureaucracy could hope to achieve.

Morozov’s fear that AI under corporate control will fail to solve “big societal problems” misunderstands the nature of progress. Grand societal problems are rarely solved by singular, politically directed technological mandates. They are solved iteratively, through the accumulation of millions of small, eolithic discoveries made by individuals empowered to tinker, fail, and succeed on their own terms. Generative AI is the ultimate tool for this kind of decentralized tinkering. Because it is highly versatile and lacks a built-in telos, it acts as a universal cognitive multiplier for the modern bricoleur.

To subject this technology to the “normative planning” of a political project, as Morozov advocates, would be to strip it of its greatest asset: its unpredictable, emergent utility. We do not need a “Humanity Lobby” to wrest control of AI from the market and place it in the hands of regional planning committees. That would merely replace the dynamic, discovery-driven eolithism of the market with a new, politicized version of bureaucratic instrumental reason. The AI we deserve is one that is accessible to as many flâneurs as possible, allowing them to freely wander the digital stonefield, guided not by state directives, but by their own idle curiosity and entrepreneurial drive.
