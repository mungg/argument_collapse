Diversified Claude

"The United States should proceed with regulated Arctic oil development because it ensures higher environmental standards and supports the economic stability of indigenous communities compared to the alternative of unregulated foreign development."

Much of the commentary about Shell's conditional permit treats Arctic oil development as if it were a moral test the United States is failing. It isn't. It is a question of whether the United States will participate in an industrial activity that the other Arctic littoral states — Russia, Norway, Canada, and Denmark via Greenland — are already pursuing, often with fewer environmental safeguards than U.S. law requires.

Norway's Statoil is drilling in the Barents Sea. Rosneft and ExxonMobil were, until sanctions intervened, partnered on the Kara Sea, and Russia has every intention of resuming. Greenland has actively courted exploration as part of its path toward fiscal independence from Denmark. Canada has issued leases in the Beaufort. If the question is whether Arctic hydrocarbons will be developed, the answer was settled some time ago. The only live question is under whose rules.

That is the strongest argument for U.S. participation. The Bureau of Safety and Environmental Enforcement's post-Macondo regime is the most stringent offshore regulatory framework in the Arctic basin. Shell's Chukchi plan requires a capping stack on site, a same-season relief well capability, and staged drilling that avoids penetrating hydrocarbon-bearing zones late in the season. None of those requirements apply in Russian waters. American withdrawal does not protect the Arctic; it cedes the basin to operators with weaker rules and to states with weaker norms.

There is also a domestic equity dimension that environmental groups headquartered in San Francisco and New York tend to elide. The North Slope Borough's budget, the Alaska Permanent Fund, and the village corporations created under ANCSA are all bound up with resource revenue. Inupiat leaders in Barrow and Wainwright are not uniformly opposed to offshore development; many support it conditionally, because the alternative is a collapsing tax base and out-migration of young people. Treating "Indigenous voices" as monolithically anti-development is itself a kind of colonial projection.

None of this means drilling should proceed without scrutiny. The 2012 season was a fiasco and the Interior Department was right to demand stronger contingency planning. Bonding requirements should be raised. A binding Arctic Council instrument on oil-spill prevention — not merely the 2013 response agreement — should be a priority of the U.S. chairmanship.

But the choice on offer is not pristine wilderness versus industrial development. It is regulated American development versus unregulated development by others. The thoughtful environmentalist should prefer the former.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Arctic hydrocarbon development is already an established reality among other littoral states, meaning the primary concern is which nation's regulatory standards will govern the activity.
  2. 02U.S. participation ensures that drilling occurs under the world's most stringent offshore safety and environmental regulations, whereas American withdrawal would cede the region to states with weaker oversight.
  3. 03Offshore resource revenue is essential for the fiscal health of Alaska's North Slope Borough and the preservation of local indigenous communities.
  4. 04The perspectives of indigenous leaders are diverse and often support conditional development as a necessary means to prevent economic collapse and youth out-migration.
  5. 05The U.S. chairmanship of the Arctic Council should be used to strengthen international oil-spill prevention agreements rather than pursuing a total ban on drilling.
