"Instead of imposing cybersecurity mandates on infrastructure operators, the government should establish software liability laws to hold vendors financially responsible for security defects in their products."

The debate over cybersecurity mandates is looking through the wrong end of the telescope. The problem is not that power companies or banks are too lazy to install firewalls—it’s that the entire digital ecosystem rests on a foundation of insecure code, and the software vendors and systems integrators who build that foundation face virtually no financial consequences when their products are riddled with holes. Before we impose operational mandates on critical infrastructure operators, we should direct regulatory energy at software liability, which would pull the whole market toward more secure design without the government having to dictate a single technical standard.

Today, a typical end-user license agreement disclaims all liability for security flaws. A utility buys an industrial control system, and if a bug in that system allows an intruder to seize control, the vendor typically bears no cost for the resulting outage. That distorted incentive drives a race to the bottom: ship features fast, patch later, and let the customer bear the risk. Even the most security-conscious operator cannot audit millions of lines of source code; they are at the mercy of their suppliers. Mandating that operators achieve a certain level of security is like requiring a building to stand up to hurricanes while the lumber supply is full of rotten timber. You have to fix the supply chain first.

A smarter approach, which does not require a sprawling new regulatory agency, would be to establish clear standards of liability for software defects that cause critical infrastructure failures. If a vendor negligently introduced a vulnerability that could have been found by reasonable quality assurance, that vendor should share in the financial damages. Faced with liability exposure, software companies would immediately invest more in secure development processes, code auditing, and formal verification. This market-driven shift would do more to raise the baseline security of our critical networks than a thousand pages of federal cybersecurity mandates targeted at the end operator.

This is not a deregulatory fantasy. The history of product liability shows that when courts and legislatures establish that defective products carry a cost, manufacturers respond. Automakers didn’t make safety a priority until the law made them pay for unsafe designs. Software as a product is overdue for that same evolution. Importantly, this approach complements rather than conflicts with information-sharing efforts. Once vendors know they could be on the hook, they will insist on timely threat data from government partners, creating a natural alignment of interests.

Opponents will argue that liability will raise software costs and suppress innovation. But the true cost is already being paid in massive losses from breaches and the looming threat of systemic collapse. A liability regime properly scoped to gross negligence would not punish the coder who tries hard but makes a human mistake; it would target the firm that ships code with no security testing whatsoever because it’s cheaper. That distinction is workable. By fixing the incentives of the makers, we can secure our infrastructure from the inside out, avoiding the need for government to inspect every utility’s firewall rules.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current lack of software liability creates a distorted incentive for vendors to prioritize speed and features over security, leaving infrastructure operators at the mercy of flawed products.
  2. 02Infrastructure operators cannot effectively secure their systems if the underlying software supply chain is fundamentally compromised by insecure code.
  3. 03Establishing liability for software defects would use market forces to compel vendors to invest in secure development and rigorous code auditing.
  4. 04Historical precedents in other industries, such as automotive manufacturing, demonstrate that product liability is an effective tool for improving safety and quality standards.
  5. 05A liability regime focused on negligence would internalize the costs of cyber risks that are currently being borne by the public and the economy through breaches and systemic threats.
