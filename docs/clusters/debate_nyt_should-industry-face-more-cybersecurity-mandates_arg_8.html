"Instead of imposing rigid regulatory mandates, the government should foster a voluntary cybersecurity framework that incentivizes information sharing and provides liability protections for private industry."

Secretary Panetta is right to sound the alarm, and the Chamber of Commerce is right to fear clumsy mandates. The solution lies between those poles: a voluntary-but-urgent framework that ties information sharing to strong incentives, rather than imposing a rigid regulatory corset. The blocked Senate bill failed precisely because it tried to command compliance rather than cultivate resilience through collective action.

The core gap today is not that CEOs don’t know they should invest in cybersecurity—it is that no single company can see the full threat landscape. Attackers move across sectors, exploiting interdependencies that only the government’s intelligence apparatus can map. Yet the current liability environment and privacy fears make firms profoundly reluctant to share incident data with the government or with competitors. A company that admits it was breached fears regulatory punishment, customer flight, and shareholder lawsuits. So the intelligence stays in silos, and the next victim learns nothing.

The most powerful move Congress could make would be to pass a bill that offers safe harbor from antitrust and privacy complaints when companies share cyber threat information with a central clearinghouse, and then guarantees liability protection for those that adopt recommended mitigations. This is not deregulation; it is smart governance that flips the incentive structure. Instead of being penalized for openness, firms would be rewarded with legal armor. With that shield, the flow of real-time indicators—malware signatures, command-and-control server IPs, tactics observed in the wild—would increase dramatically, hardening the entire ecosystem.

Mandates alone create a lowest-common-denominator effect, where security becomes a compliance game. However, coupling information sharing with performance-based tax incentives could drive investment in next-generation defenses without prescribing exactly how to build them. A company that meets audited security benchmarks—say, using the NIST framework then under development—could receive a tax credit for part of its spending, making the CFO an ally rather than an obstacle.

Critics will say that nothing short of mandates will force neglectful operators to act. There is merit to that concern. For the truly critical infrastructure—nuclear control systems, dam floodgates, large-scale interconnects—some baseline regulation is warranted. But that must be narrow and outcome-oriented, not a sprawling new rulebook. The centerpiece of our national cyber strategy should be a partnership where government brings threat intelligence to the table and industry brings capital and expertise, with both sides sharing risk. That approach doesn’t make headlines like “cyber-Pearl Harbor,” but it’s the only one that works in the long run.
