"While fostering creative and public-spirited alternatives to corporate AI is valuable, a truly democratic AI agenda must prioritize the establishment of hard legal constraints, state-led regulation, and security protocols to govern the technology's most dangerous and coercive applications."

Morozov’s essay is a bracing reminder that AI is not destiny. Its institutional origins matter, its market form matters, and democratic societies should not accept Silicon Valley’s imagination as the outer limit of the possible. But his emphasis on ecological reason, play, and alternative research agendas leaves too little room for a less romantic fact: some AI systems are dangerous because they are powerful, scalable, and strategically useful. Democracy needs imagination, but it also needs security, law, and state capacity.

The lead essay treats fears about killer robots and artificial general intelligence as partly a refuge for “futilitarians” whose older critiques of AI have been overtaken by generative models. There is a fair point here. Speculation about superintelligence can distract from present harms: labor exploitation, bias, surveillance, fraud, concentration, copyright, environmental costs, and the hollowing of public institutions. But it would be a mistake to swing too far in the other direction. The militarization and weaponization of AI are not future fantasies. They are present realities.

Machine learning already shapes targeting, surveillance, intelligence analysis, drone autonomy, cyber operations, border enforcement, and policing. Generative AI adds new capacities: automated persuasion, synthetic media, code generation, translation, document analysis, and simulation. Even when systems are unreliable, they can be useful enough to accelerate harmful activity. A model does not need to be conscious or generally intelligent to help a malicious actor write phishing emails, generate disinformation variants, summarize stolen data, or assist in vulnerability discovery. Nor does an autonomous weapon need human-like intelligence to create accountability gaps and escalation risks.

Morozov’s historical account of AI’s Cold War entanglements should make us more alert to these dangers, not less. If military and bureaucratic imperatives shaped AI from the beginning, then democratic AI requires more than cultivating eolithic workshops. It requires binding constraints on state and corporate uses of AI in coercive domains. The Efficiency Lobby is troubling when it optimizes office work. It is terrifying when it optimizes suspicion, targeting, and force.

A liberal-democratic AI agenda should therefore begin with prohibitions and hard rules. Some uses should be banned or sharply restricted: real-time biometric mass surveillance in public spaces, social scoring, fully autonomous lethal targeting without meaningful human control, predictive policing systems that reproduce discriminatory enforcement, AI systems that deny essential benefits without due process, and nonconsensual sexual deepfakes. Other uses should require warrants, audits, logging, procurement transparency, and public reporting. The point is not to slow innovation for its own sake. It is to defend the preconditions of democratic life: privacy, accountability, bodily security, equal citizenship, and freedom from arbitrary power.

Here Morozov’s suspicion of “policy tweaks” is understandable but incomplete. Policy can be shallow, captured, and symbolic. But law is also how democracies draw lines. The European Union’s AI Act, for all its compromises, represents an attempt to classify risk, ban certain practices, impose obligations, and create enforcement mechanisms. The Biden administration’s executive order on AI, voluntary commitments from labs, NIST’s risk management work, and debates over compute reporting are imperfect and contested, but they reflect a real need: democratic governments cannot merely sponsor alternative imaginaries; they must govern systems already being deployed.

The challenge is that states themselves are among the most dangerous users of AI. Morozov knows this, which is why he is drawn to more participatory traditions. But the conclusion cannot be anti-statism. Only states, or state-like regional institutions, have the authority to regulate frontier labs, control military procurement, enforce privacy rights, fund public alternatives, inspect data centers, and negotiate international agreements. The question is how to make state capacity democratic rather than technocratic or authoritarian.

That requires institutional design. Independent AI safety agencies should have technical expertise and legal power to inspect high-risk systems. Courts should recognize rights to explanation, contestation, and remedy where AI affects liberty, benefits, employment, housing, credit, education, or migration. Legislatures should create bright-line bans rather than delegating everything to agency discretion. Whistleblower protections should cover employees who expose dangerous AI practices. Public procurement should require impact assessments, auditability, and termination rights. National security uses should be subject to legislative oversight, not hidden behind classification whenever “AI” is invoked.

Frontier model governance also deserves attention. Morozov criticizes the corporate control of generative AI, but public control must grapple with capability thresholds. The most advanced models may lower barriers to cyber operations, biological design assistance, or large-scale manipulation. There is disagreement among experts about the severity and timeline of these risks. As of late 2024, no one can responsibly claim certainty. But uncertainty cuts both ways. When systems are rapidly improving and widely deployed, democratic governments should require pre-deployment testing, incident reporting, cybersecurity standards, model evaluations, and perhaps licensing for the largest training runs. These measures need not entrench incumbents if paired with public compute and support for smaller open systems. The aim should be to govern scale and risk, not to suppress research.

International coordination is equally important. AI competition is already entangled with U.S.-China rivalry, export controls on advanced chips, military modernization, and industrial policy. A purely national democratic AI project will be limited if geopolitical competition pushes every government toward speed and secrecy. We need agreements on autonomous weapons, AI-enabled nuclear command risks, model evaluation standards, incident sharing, and nonproliferation of the most dangerous capabilities. Such agreements will be difficult, especially amid distrust. But arms control was never easy. The alternative is an AI race in which safety and democracy are treated as luxuries.

Morozov’s ecological reason can enrich this security agenda if we interpret it politically. Democratic security is not simply the state optimizing threats. It requires public deliberation about what counts as security. Border AI may make a state feel more secure while making migrants less safe. Predictive policing may promise order while deepening racial injustice. Military AI may protect soldiers while increasing the risk of remote, unaccountable violence. A democratic society must ask whose security is being served and whose insecurity is being automated.

But asking those questions does not eliminate the need for competence. AI-enabled fraud, cyberattacks, deepfake extortion, and foreign influence operations can harm ordinary people and weaken democratic trust. Public agencies need technical capacity to respond. Election offices need tools and protocols to authenticate information without becoming censors. Schools and media institutions need literacy programs. Police need limits on AI, but societies also need enforcement against AI-enabled abuse. A democracy that cannot protect people from new forms of predation will not remain trusted for long.

The AI we deserve, then, is not only playful, public, and emancipatory. It is bounded. It says no. It builds guardrails backed by law rather than corporate terms of service. It distinguishes low-risk experimentation from high-risk deployment. It treats coercive uses as presumptively suspect. It funds public-interest technology while regulating private and state power. It values imagination but does not confuse imagination with governance.

Morozov is right that actual democracy is the prerequisite for democratic AI. But actual democracy includes legislatures, courts, inspectors general, standards bodies, treaties, and enforcement agencies, not only citizen workshops and alternative research paradigms. The Efficiency Lobby must be challenged in the marketplace and the workplace, but also in the police department, the intelligence agency, the border zone, and the battlefield.

The danger of AI is not that machines will become intelligent in some metaphysical sense. It is that institutions will use machine intelligence to act faster, wider, and less accountably than democratic control can follow. The answer is not resignation. Nor is it only utopian reconstruction. It is democratic power in its hardest form: the power to authorize, inspect, limit, and forbid.
