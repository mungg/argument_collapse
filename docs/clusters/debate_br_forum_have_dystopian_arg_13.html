"To ensure AI's future is not dystopian, society must move beyond research funding and norms to fundamentally rebuild the institutional foundations of worker power and democratic accountability."

Daron Acemoglu’s central claim is both modest and radical: AI is not destiny. It is modest because it rejects the familiar science-fiction drama of superintelligence, mass human redundancy, and robot apocalypse. It is radical because it challenges a quieter but more powerful assumption in our political economy: that whatever technology Silicon Valley produces must be accepted as “innovation,” and whatever labor market consequences follow are merely the price of progress.

That assumption has done enormous damage. Over the last four decades, the United States has treated the direction of technical change as if it were a natural phenomenon, like the weather. If a plant closes, a warehouse job becomes more surveilled, a call center is replaced by software, or an app company reclassifies workers as independent contractors, the resulting pain is usually described as “disruption.” The word suggests an impersonal force. But the choices embedded in these systems are made by managers, engineers, investors, procurement officers, and policymakers. Acemoglu is right to insist that AI’s future depends on institutions and power.

The most important contribution of his essay is to connect the labor question to the democracy question. Too often these are treated separately. One debate asks whether robots will take jobs. Another asks whether social media platforms are polarizing the electorate, whether facial recognition should be regulated, or whether governments are building surveillance states. But the two developments are mutually reinforcing. An economy that produces insecurity, humiliation, and regional decline corrodes trust in democratic institutions. A democracy weakened by distrust and plutocratic influence is less capable of regulating firms, strengthening labor, or protecting civil liberties. AI can accelerate both processes if it is deployed mainly to replace workers and monitor citizens.

The American economy already contains many examples of this tendency. The issue is not only that a machine substitutes for a person. It is also that software can reorganize work so that human beings become appendages to algorithmic management. Warehouse workers are directed by handheld devices. Delivery drivers are evaluated by opaque metrics. Call-center workers are timed, scored, and scripted. Retail employees are scheduled by systems that optimize staffing costs while making family life unpredictable. These are not examples of human liberation from drudgery. They are examples of technology being used to transfer discretion upward and risk downward.

Acemoglu’s distinction between automation and the creation of new tasks is therefore indispensable. The question is not “technology or no technology.” The question is what kind. A diagnostic tool that helps a nurse identify deterioration earlier can raise the value of nursing labor. A system that allows a teacher to see which students are confused and why can enrich classroom practice. A translation tool that lets social workers communicate with more clients can expand human service. But an algorithm that simply reduces staffing ratios, deskills workers, or monitors performance minute by minute may raise profits without raising social productivity. Both are “AI.” Only one deserves public encouragement.

The essay’s historical account of the postwar compact is also useful, though it should not be romanticized. The decades after World War II were not a golden age for everyone. Black Americans faced legal segregation, exclusion from many New Deal protections, redlining, and labor market discrimination. Women were often pushed into unpaid domestic labor or low-paid service work. Immigrants and farmworkers remained vulnerable. Still, Acemoglu’s broader point stands: rapid productivity growth, strong unions, rising wages, public investment, and democratic reform combined to create a sense that economic growth could be broadly shared. That combination was political, not automatic.

The decline of that compact was likewise political. It was not inevitable that the minimum wage would erode, that private-sector union density would collapse, that antitrust enforcement would weaken, that corporate governance would become obsessed with shareholder value, or that the tax code would favor capital over labor. The technological path of the last forty years has been shaped by these institutional changes. Firms facing weak unions, cheap capital, permissive labor law, and intense pressure to raise margins naturally invest in labor-saving systems. If the social costs of displacement are borne by workers and communities rather than by firms, the private incentive to automate exceeds the social benefit.

This is why Acemoglu is right to reject the idea that redistribution alone can solve the problem. A stronger safety net is essential. Cash transfers, wage insurance, child allowances, health care, and public employment supports can reduce hardship. But a society cannot build democratic legitimacy solely by writing checks after the labor market has stripped people of bargaining power and social standing. Work is not only income. It is also routine, recognition, skill, solidarity, and a claim on the future. A politics that says “we will let corporations organize production however they wish, then tax some winners to compensate the losers” is unlikely to be stable. The winners will resist taxation; the losers will resent dependence.

Yet Acemoglu’s proposed agenda needs to be pushed further. Redirecting AI research through public funding, norms, and democratic oversight is necessary, but insufficient unless workers themselves gain power inside firms and technology adoption processes. If decisions about AI deployment remain managerial prerogatives, even publicly funded human-complementary technologies may be used in labor-displacing ways. A hospital can use AI to support nurses, or to justify thinner staffing. A school district can use adaptive software to help teachers, or to increase class sizes. A logistics firm can use routing tools to reduce stress, or to intensify quotas. The same tool can be embedded in different labor regimes.

This suggests that labor law must be central to AI policy. Workers should have rights to bargain over algorithmic management, data collection, surveillance, scheduling, and automation. Unions and works councils should have access to technical expertise so they can evaluate employer claims. Public subsidies for AI adoption should require job quality standards, consultation with employees, and transparency about expected labor impacts. If a firm receives public support to deploy AI, it should not be allowed simply to convert that support into layoffs, speedups, or executive compensation.

Tax policy also matters. Acemoglu notes the distortion created by taxing labor more heavily than equipment and software. Correcting that imbalance is a practical step. But we should be careful not to create a crude “robot tax” that discourages genuinely productive investment. The goal should not be to preserve every existing task. Some work is dangerous, degrading, or unnecessary. The goal is to ensure that when technology raises productivity, workers share in the gains and new forms of useful work emerge. That requires stronger wage floors, sectoral bargaining, public training, and regional development—not merely disincentives to automation.

On democracy, the urgency is even greater. AI-powered surveillance and manipulation are not speculative dangers; they are already present. The United States does not have China’s system of authoritarian social control, but it has an enormous commercial surveillance infrastructure. Platforms collect intimate data, infer preferences, rank information, and sell targeted influence. Police departments experiment with facial recognition and predictive tools. Employers monitor workers. Data brokers assemble profiles with little public accountability. A democratic society cannot tolerate a system in which every action becomes a behavioral signal to be monetized, manipulated, or policed.

Here the policy agenda should include strict limits on facial recognition, bans on certain forms of biometric surveillance, data minimization rules, algorithmic transparency, and public-interest auditing. But again, the deeper issue is power. If a few firms control the infrastructure of communication, advertising, cloud computing, and AI research, democratic oversight becomes difficult. Antitrust enforcement, interoperability requirements, and public digital infrastructure should be part of the response.

The hardest question Acemoglu raises is international. If democratic countries restrain surveillance AI while authoritarian states pursue it aggressively, what then? The answer cannot be unilateral innocence. Democracies need technological capacity. They need cybersecurity, intelligence tools, and defense systems. But they also need a clear line between defending democratic institutions and importing authoritarian methods. The United States cannot credibly oppose digital repression abroad while allowing unregulated commercial surveillance and discriminatory policing at home.

AI’s future does not have to be dystopian, but avoiding dystopia will require more than ethical guidelines and well-meaning research grants. It will require rebuilding the institutional foundations that once made technology broadly beneficial: public investment, worker power, competitive markets, civil liberties, and democratic accountability. The lesson of Acemoglu’s essay is that machines do not decide what kind of society we become. Institutions do. If we want AI to complement human beings rather than discipline, replace, or manipulate them, we must give human beings more power over AI.

  3. 03Redistribution and a stronger safety net are insufficient solutions because they cannot replace the social standing, recognition, and bargaining power provided by meaningful work.
  4. 04Redirecting AI requires giving workers legal rights to bargain over algorithmic management, surveillance, and automation processes within firms.
  5. 05Tax policy must be reformed to remove the current bias that favors capital investment over labor without creating crude disincentives for genuinely productive innovation.

  7. 07The United States must establish a clear domestic line against digital repression and commercial surveillance to maintain international credibility in opposing authoritarian AI uses.
