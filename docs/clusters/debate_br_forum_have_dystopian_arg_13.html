"While AI presents real risks to privacy and democracy, the response to these challenges should not be to discourage labor-saving automation, which remains a vital engine of human prosperity and demographic necessity."

Daron Acemoglu is right that AI is not an autonomous force descending upon society from the clouds. Technologies are shaped by incentives, institutions, and values. But his essay risks overcorrecting from technological determinism into technological suspicion. By placing “excessive automation” at the center of the problem, he treats labor-saving innovation too often as a social threat rather than as one of the main engines of human prosperity.

The distinction matters. The history of economic growth is largely the history of doing more with less labor. Agriculture was mechanized; textile production was mechanized; transport, accounting, manufacturing, communications, and retail were transformed by tools that reduced the need for human effort in existing tasks. That process displaced workers, sometimes brutally. But it also made food, clothing, housing, medicine, and information cheaper and more abundant. To describe automation mainly as a handmaiden of inequality is to see only one side of the ledger.

The problem of the last forty years is not that we automated too much. It may be that we automated too little of the right things, diffused productivity-enhancing technologies too slowly, and failed to equip people for a changing economy. Productivity growth in recent decades has indeed been disappointing. But that is an argument for more experimentation, not for steering innovation away from labor-saving tools. If AI can reduce the time doctors spend on paperwork, automate dangerous mining tasks, improve logistics, detect fraud, translate languages, or perform routine legal discovery, society should welcome those gains. The goal should be to make the gains broad-based, not to slow the process because some applications substitute for human labor.

Acemoglu’s own framework acknowledges that automation can be beneficial when it is accompanied by new tasks. But in practice, it is difficult to separate the two. Automating one task often creates demand for another. Spreadsheets reduced the need for armies of clerks doing manual calculations, but they expanded financial analysis, modeling, planning, and small-business management. ATMs reduced the number of tellers required per branch, but banks opened more branches and teller work became more focused on customer service and sales. Industrial robots can displace assembly workers, but they also create jobs in maintenance, programming, supply-chain management, and design. These transitions are uneven, and workers do not automatically move from old tasks to new ones. But the relationship between automation and opportunity is more dynamic than the essay sometimes suggests.

The danger of a policy agenda built around discouraging automation is that it may protect existing jobs at the expense of future living standards. Many jobs should be automated. Repetitive data entry, dangerous warehouse lifting, exposure to infectious disease, long-haul driving fatigue, toxic industrial inspection, and mind-numbing compliance paperwork are not sacred forms of human flourishing. If AI can reduce the need for people to do such work, that is a moral achievement. The right response is to ensure that displaced workers receive support, training, income, and access to new opportunities—not to bias the innovation system against substitution.

This is especially important in an aging society. The United States and many other wealthy countries face rising demand for health care, elder care, and public services, even as labor force growth slows. Japan’s experience is instructive: automation can be a response not only to corporate greed but to demographic necessity. If fewer workers must support more retirees, productivity growth becomes essential. AI tools that allow a nurse, physician, caregiver, or public administrator to serve more people may involve replacing some tasks previously done by humans. That is not automatically a problem. It may be the only way to maintain decent services without unsustainable costs.

Where Acemoglu is strongest is in his critique of market incentives. Markets do not naturally price social cohesion, dignity, privacy, or democratic resilience. Firms may adopt AI in ways that shift costs onto workers or communities. Platforms may optimize engagement at the expense of public reason. Governments may demand surveillance capabilities that threaten liberty. These are real failures. But they call for targeted regulation and social insurance, not a broad presumption against automation.

Take the tax code. Acemoglu argues that labor is taxed more heavily than equipment and software, effectively subsidizing automation. There is a real issue here. Payroll taxes raise the cost of employing workers, while accelerated depreciation and other provisions can favor capital investment. But the solution should be designed carefully. We do not want to tax productivity-enhancing capital so heavily that firms underinvest. A better approach would be to shift taxation away from labor and toward rents, land, monopoly profits, carbon emissions, and high-end consumption. Reduce payroll taxes for low- and middle-wage workers. Expand the earned income tax credit or wage subsidies. Encourage investment, but make sure the returns are shared.

Similarly, redistribution deserves more respect than Acemoglu gives it. He is right that cash cannot replace meaning, status, and participation. But income security is not a consolation prize; it is a foundation for freedom. A person who loses a job because of AI needs money, health care, retraining options, and time. A stronger welfare state can make technological change less frightening and reduce the temptation to preserve inefficient jobs. Denmark and Sweden do not merely rely on good jobs; they combine high labor standards with active labor market policy and robust social benefits. The lesson is not “jobs rather than redistribution,” but “institutions that let people move without falling.”

Universal basic income is not a complete answer, but neither should it be dismissed. A modest income floor, child allowance, or negative income tax could increase bargaining power and support entrepreneurship, caregiving, education, and community work. If AI eventually produces large productivity gains with fewer labor hours, we should not cling to the idea that full-time paid employment is the only legitimate way to distribute income or social status. The postwar compact was built around jobs, especially male industrial jobs. The next compact may need to recognize a wider range of socially valuable activity.

The surveillance half of Acemoglu’s argument is more persuasive and less vulnerable to this critique. AI used to identify faces at protests, manipulate voters, score citizens, or monitor workers minute by minute threatens liberal democracy. Here the problem is not “automation” but domination. A system that predicts behavior can be used to assist people or to control them. The line should be drawn around consent, due process, transparency, and power. Facial recognition in public spaces should be tightly restricted. Government use of predictive policing should face strict scrutiny. Data brokers should be regulated. Platforms should be made more accountable for algorithmic amplification and political advertising.

But even in this domain, the answer is not to redirect AI away from powerful tools. Democracies need powerful tools too: to detect cyberattacks, secure infrastructure, expose disinformation campaigns, improve public health, and identify discrimination. The challenge is institutional design. We need warrants, audits, appeal rights, public procurement rules, and independent oversight. We need to distinguish between AI that expands state capacity under law and AI that enables arbitrary power.

Acemoglu’s call for a change in research norms is welcome but should not become moral theater. It is easy to ask AI researchers to refuse harmful work. It is harder to define harm in advance. A model for image recognition can diagnose disease or power surveillance. A language model can assist disabled users or generate propaganda. A logistics algorithm can reduce waste or intensify labor. Researchers should think ethically, but society cannot outsource political choices to graduate students and engineers. Legislatures, courts, unions, firms, and citizens must set rules.

The better AI future is not necessarily less automated. It is more productive, more competitive, more humane, and more democratic. We should encourage AI that raises output, lowers costs, improves services, and reduces drudgery. We should tax and regulate so that the resulting wealth does not accrue only to shareholders and executives. We should strengthen education and training, but also income supports for those who cannot or do not want to make every transition the market demands. We should protect privacy and civil liberties with hard law, not voluntary principles.

The fear of dystopia is understandable. The current model of platform capitalism has produced too much concentration, too much surveillance, and too little worker voice. But the remedy is not to become wary of labor-saving innovation as such. Human beings have always advanced by inventing tools that do some of our work for us. The question is whether we build institutions that allow everyone to benefit from that emancipation. AI should not be chained to the preservation of existing jobs. It should be harnessed to abundance, security, and freedom.
