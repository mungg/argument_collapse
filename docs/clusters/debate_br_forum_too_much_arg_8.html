"Nancy Hirschmann’s critique of care feminism fails because it treats children as mere policy outputs or moral lessons rather than as human beings with specific developmental and relational needs that must dictate how care is structured."

**The Children Are Not the Point**

There is a strange silence at the heart of Nancy Hirschmann's essay. It is an essay about mothering—a long, careful, ambitious essay about what mothering should look like, who should do it, how it should be valued, and what its political consequences are. And yet the children, the actual subjects of all this mothering, almost never appear except as instruments of an argument. Carla Wagner appears as a moral lesson. Susan and Anthony appear as moral lessons. The children of welfare mothers appear as moral lessons. The unborn children of Hirschmann's students appear as moral lessons. Nowhere in the essay do we encounter a child as a person whose needs and developmental interests might shape what we think mothering should be.

I find this silence revealing. It reflects, I think, a habit in a certain kind of feminist writing of treating children as essentially policy outputs—as future citizens, future workers, future taxpayers—rather than as present beings with developmental and relational needs that the institution of mothering exists, in significant part, to meet. The result is that the essay's argument about the political significance of caregiving never quite touches the question of what caregiving is actually for.

What is it actually for? Children are born helpless. They remain dependent for many years. They develop language, emotional regulation, moral imagination, and the capacity for relationship through sustained interaction with attuned adult caregivers. The developmental literature on this is vast and increasingly converging. The work of Allan Schore, Daniel Stern, and others has documented the neurobiological consequences of caregiver attunement in the first years of life. The work of John Bowlby and Mary Ainsworth on attachment has been refined and extended by subsequent generations of researchers. The basic finding is that children need stable, responsive, primary attachment relationships, and that the quality of those relationships in early childhood has consequences across the lifespan.

None of this requires that the primary caregiver be the biological mother. None of this requires that the primary caregiver be a woman. None of this requires that one adult perform all of the caregiving. But all of this does require that someone, or some small set of someones, be reliably and attentively present to the child—and that this presence requires real time, in real hours, every day, for years.

Hirschmann's essay, focused as it is on the politics of adult labor, never quite confronts the time problem. The actual hours a young child needs are not infinitely flexible. They cannot be reduced by efficiency. They cannot be reallocated to evenings and weekends without significant cost. Daycare, if it is high-quality and well-staffed, can do a great deal of this work, but it cannot replace the primary attachment relationship for very young children, and it cannot do so even in theory unless the staff-to-child ratios are far lower and the wages far higher than the U.S. currently provides. This is the hard knot that the politics of work-and-family keeps trying to untie and cannot.

Once you take the developmental needs of young children seriously, certain questions in the gender debate reformulate themselves. The question is no longer just "how do we redistribute domestic labor between adults?" It is also "how do we ensure that small children get what they need?" These questions can converge: shared parenting between two attuned adults can meet children's needs while distributing labor more equally. They can also diverge: a two-career household with long hours and turnover in paid caregivers may distribute labor more equally between adults but provide less of what young children need. Feminist writing on care has been better at the first question than the second.

I do not raise this to argue for women's full-time mothering. I raise it because the alternative arrangements feminists endorse should be subjected to the same scrutiny applied to traditional ones. If Hirschmann is going to scold the elite stay-at-home mother for failing to produce a public-minded citizen, she should also tell us what arrangement, in her view, does produce one—and on what evidence. The empirical record here is muddled. Studies of maternal employment effects on child outcomes have produced mixed results, with effects that are generally small and that depend heavily on the quality of substitute care. The honest reading is that what matters for children is not whether their mother works but whether their caregiving environment, taken as a whole, is stable, responsive, and well-resourced. This is good news for feminism—it means there is no developmental reason women must be the primary caregivers—but it does not generate the prescriptive conclusion that women should leave the home. It generates a prescriptive conclusion that whoever is doing the caregiving should be supported in doing it well.

A second silence in Hirschmann's essay concerns the relational meaning of caregiving for the caregiver. She mentions, briefly, the goods caregivers receive—love, intimacy, family vacations, holiday gatherings—but she frames these primarily as objects of potential state taxation, a sort of reductio designed to embarrass care theorists. This treatment is, to put it mildly, instrumentalizing. The relationships people form with children they raise are among the deepest and most meaningful relationships available to human beings. To treat these relationships as fringe benefits of an economic role—and then to suggest that if mothers want public support, they should be willing to be taxed for their love—reduces the moral significance of parent-child relationships in a way I find disturbing.

It is also bad strategy. The feminist project has to be plausible to women who care intensely about their children, because that is most women. A feminist analysis that treats these relationships as ancillary, or as ideological constructions to be deflated, will not persuade. A feminist analysis that takes them seriously—that says, yes, this is one of the most important things you will ever do, and the institutional arrangements around you should reflect that importance—will. Care feminism, at its best, did the latter. Hirschmann's critique, in its impatience, occasionally slips into the former.

Let me close with a positive claim. The center of gravity in this debate should be children, not adults. What do children need? They need attuned adults, reliably present, supported by community and resources, freed from desperation. Whatever arrangement of adult labor provides this is the right one. For many families it will be shared parenting between two working adults with public childcare support. For some it will be one parent at home for a stretch of years. For some it will be extended kin networks. None of these arrangements is inherently feminist or anti-feminist. The feminist question is whether the arrangement is freely chosen, supported, reversible, and equitable in its consequences. Hirschmann's essay loses sight of this and ends up scolding women for failing to organize their families around the wrong question.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Children have non-negotiable neurobiological and developmental needs for stable, responsive, and time-intensive primary attachment relationships that cannot be ignored in political debates about labor.
  2. 02The central challenge of family policy is the 'time problem,' as the hours required for attuned caregiving are not infinitely flexible and cannot be reduced by efficiency or simple redistribution between adults.
  3. 03The empirical evidence suggests that child outcomes depend on the quality and stability of the caregiving environment rather than the specific employment status of the mother.
  4. 04Hirschmann instrumentalizes the parent-child bond by framing the emotional rewards of caregiving as taxable fringe benefits rather than as a fundamental source of human meaning.
  5. 05A successful feminist strategy must take the intensity of parental love seriously and advocate for institutional arrangements that support whoever is performing the essential work of caregiving.
  6. 06The moral center of the care debate should be the needs of children rather than the political utility of adult labor arrangements.
