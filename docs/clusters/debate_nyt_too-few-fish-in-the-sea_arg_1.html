The honest answer is: yes, but only if we stop pretending that "wild-caught" and "sustainable" are synonyms, and only if aquaculture grows up fast.

Consider the arithmetic. Global wild capture has been flat at roughly 90 million tons since the late 1980s, while demand keeps climbing. The FAO's most recent assessments suggest nearly 30 percent of stocks are overfished and another 57 percent are fished at their biological limit. There is no remaining slack in the ocean. Every additional pound of seafood the world eats over the coming decade will have to come from farms, not boats.

That makes aquaculture the central question, not a side issue. Done badly — as with open-net Atlantic salmon pens that concentrate waste, spread sea lice, and consume three pounds of forage fish for every pound produced — it simply offloads the overfishing problem onto anchovies and menhaden. On the wild side, the tools exist and we know they work: hard catch limits tied to scientific stock assessments, individual transferable quotas where appropriate, marine protected areas covering meaningful fractions of spawning habitat, and bycatch-reducing gear. The American experience under the reauthorized Magnuson-Stevens Act, and New Zealand and Iceland's quota systems, show that depleted fisheries can rebound within a decade.

What's missing is the consumer-facing connection. Mislabeling studies by Oceana have found a third of fish sold in the US is not what the menu claims. Without traceability — boat to plate — sustainability certifications are theater. Fix labeling, and demand can finally pull in the right direction.
