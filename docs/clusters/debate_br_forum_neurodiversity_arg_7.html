Diversified DeepSeek

"The neurodiversity movement should pursue 'Neuro-Liberty'—a framework of radical pluralism and individual autonomy—rather than the collectivist, revolutionary anticapitalism proposed by Robert Chapman."

Robert Chapman’s diagnosis of what ails the neurodiversity movement—its co-optation by capital, its overemphasis on symbolic representation, its tendency to leave the most vulnerable behind—is thoughtful and in many ways accurate. But the cure they prescribe is worse than the disease. Chapman’s Neurodivergent Power, with its call for a revolutionary party, mass organization, and a unified anticapitalist front, promises liberation but delivers a recipe for subordinating individual neurodivergent flourishing to yet another collectivist abstraction. The future of neurodiversity lies not in mimicking the authoritarian structures of twentieth-century leftism, but in embracing a radical pluralism that maximizes personal autonomy and voluntary cooperation.

At the heart of Chapman’s project is the assumption that neuronormativity is a product of capitalism and that dismantling the latter will undo the former. This historical materialism is far too neat. The urge to enforce behavioral norms and punish cognitive difference predates capitalism and exists in societies organized around very different economic logics. Religious communities, feudal orders, and even anarchist collectives have all, at various points, ostracized or controlled those who think or act strangely. Neurodivergent people have been called holy fools, possessed, or simply insufferable across millennia and continents. The specific ways in which capitalism exploits and abandons us are indeed novel and must be fought, but to imagine that a post-capitalist world would naturally welcome the neurodivergent is a dangerous act of faith. Power concentrations, whether in corporate boards or revolutionary committees, have a tendency to enforce conformity; the content of the conformity changes, but the pressure to mask, to perform, to suppress one’s own cognitive rhythms for the sake of the collective does not.

I propose a different north star: Neuro-Liberty. This is not a return to the identity politics and rights-based reformism of Liberal Neurodiversity, though it shares that tradition’s commitment to protecting individuals from state and corporate coercion. Neuro-Liberty starts from the recognition that neurodivergent people are not a class with a unified interest. Some of us thrive in cooperative, face-to-face communities; some of us are hermits who need solitude to remain sane. Some are verbal processors who need to talk for hours; some communicate best in writing or art. A political project that demands a shared “consciousness” and a common “party” will inevitably privilege some neurotypes over others—probably the hyperlexic, the ideologically fervent, the socially charismatic—while sidelining those for whom meetings and protests are cognitive hell. The history of leftist organizing is littered with disabled bodies that were welcomed as symbols and then discarded as too demanding, too unreliable, too “sick.”

What would Neuro-Liberty look like in practice? It would fight to dismantle the regulations and economic structures that force neurodivergent people into one-size-fits-all institutions, but it would not seek to replace them with another set of collective mandates. The goal is radical choice architecture. This means a universal basic income high enough to allow people to opt out of wage labor entirely, not at the end of some revolutionary process, but as an immediate, non-negotiable demand. It means abolishing compulsory schooling laws that trap children in environments that break their spirits, and replacing them with a genuine marketplace of educational options—from democratic free schools to specialized micro-schools to unschooling—fully funded through flexible, portable public dollars. It means breaking up state-granted medical monopolies so that neurodivergent people can choose their own paths to healing, whether that’s psychiatric medication, neurofeedback, peer respites, or indigenous plant medicine, without the gatekeeping of a bureaucratic licensure system that conflates expertise with conformity.

Crucially, Neuro-Liberty rejects the false binary between medical intervention and identity affirmation. Chapman’s essay, like much of the neurodiversity movement, uses the Spectrum 10k boycott example approvingly, implying that any research into biological markers of autism is inherently eugenic. But for many neurodivergent people, especially those with severe, debilitating conditions, medical research is a lifeline, not a threat. The problem with eugenics is coercion—the state or the medical establishment deciding which lives are worth living and pressuring or forcing people to eliminate certain traits. The solution is not to suppress research, but to ensure that individuals and families have the freedom to use or refuse medical technologies as they see fit. If a future advance allows some autistic people to modulate their sensory processing so they can enjoy a walk without agony, who am I, an ADHD dyspraxic, to tell them they should instead dismantle capitalism? The choice must be theirs, unburdened by either medical paternalism or political purism.

I do not romanticize markets. I know that corporate power can co-opt the language of neurodiversity and exploit workers. But I also know that markets, when genuinely pluralistic and regulated against fraud and predation, can serve niche communities that mass movements ignore. The autistic-run software cooperative, the ADHD leadership coach who works via body-doubling over Zoom, the mutual aid network that uses cryptocurrency to get cash to unhoused psychotic people—all are expressions of decentralized, voluntary association that meet real needs without demanding ideological conformity. These are the seeds of a neurodivergent future that Chapmans’s centralized revolutionary model would likely crush in its demand for unity.

Finally, Neuro-Liberty recognizes that the most profound threats to neurodivergent life often come not from the profit motive, but from the state’s monopoly on violence. Police kill autistic people, and psychiatric incarceration is state violence. The abolition of these institutions does not require a revolutionary party; it requires a persistent, decentralized movement that defunds them, creates alternative crisis responses, and shifts cultural attitudes. That movement will be messy, contradictory, and full of people who disagree on everything else. That is not a flaw; it is evidence that power is being dispersed, not concentrated. The future of neurodiversity should be a tapestry woven from many threads, each neurotype creating its own patterns of life, connected loosely but never forced into a single design.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Enforcing behavioral norms and neuronormativity is a cross-cultural human tendency that predates capitalism and would likely persist under revolutionary or collectivist governance.
  2. 02A unified revolutionary party or shared consciousness would inevitably privilege socially charismatic neurotypes while marginalizing those whose disabilities make collective organizing and meetings difficult.
  3. 03True liberation requires 'radical choice architecture,' such as universal basic income and the abolition of compulsory schooling, to allow neurodivergent individuals to opt out of oppressive systems entirely.
  4. 04The movement must reject the false binary between medical intervention and identity, ensuring individuals have the freedom to pursue medical research and treatments without being accused of supporting eugenics.
  5. 05Decentralized, voluntary associations and pluralistic markets are better suited to serving niche neurodivergent needs than a centralized revolutionary model that demands ideological unity.
  6. 06The primary threat to neurodivergent lives is the state's monopoly on violence, which is best challenged through decentralized abolitionist movements rather than a concentrated revolutionary party.
