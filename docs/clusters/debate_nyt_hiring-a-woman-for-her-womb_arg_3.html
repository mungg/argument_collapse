"To ensure surrogacy is safe and ethical, the medical profession must prioritize the surrogate's health and autonomy by implementing rigorous clinical standards and independent medical advocacy."

As a physician who has cared for surrogates and intended parents, I want to shift the discussion from contracts and ideology toward something that often gets lost: the medicine.

Gestational surrogacy is not a benign procedure. It typically involves hormonal preparation, the transfer of one or more embryos, often a higher-than-average risk of multiple pregnancy, and all the ordinary risks of pregnancy and delivery — hypertension, gestational diabetes, hemorrhage, cesarean section, postpartum depression, and, rarely, death. A surrogate undertakes these risks on someone else's behalf. We owe her, at the very least, the standard of care we would want for ourselves.

Yet current practice falls short in several specific ways. First, single embryo transfer should be the default. Many intended parents, eager to maximize success per cycle and minimize cost, push for double transfers. Twin pregnancies carry several times the maternal and neonatal risk of singletons. The American Society for Reproductive Medicine has issued guidelines, but they are routinely ignored. Make them binding.

Second, mental health support is treated as a formality when it should be central. Carrying a child and relinquishing her is psychologically complex even when the surrogate is committed and well-prepared. Pre-screening should be rigorous, and post-partum counseling should be guaranteed and paid for, not offered as an afterthought.

Third, surrogates need their own physicians, not the intended parents' fertility specialist managing the pregnancy. The conflicts of interest are obvious. A clinic that has been paid by the intended parents has every incentive to recommend interventions — selective reduction, scheduled cesarean — that serve the contract rather than the patient in the room.

Fourth, we should set medical eligibility standards. Many programs allow women to serve as surrogates after multiple prior cesareans, or with conditions that raise the risk of complications. A national medical registry would let us track outcomes and identify practices that are unsafe.

Fifth, the intended parents' medical history should be screened too — both for genetic factors relevant to the embryo and for capacity to parent the child who results. We screen adoptive parents. We do not screen the people commissioning a pregnancy. That is strange.

The ethical debates about commodification will continue, and they should. But while philosophers argue, the women in our clinics are pregnant now. Treating them as patients first — with the autonomy, information, and clinical advocacy any patient deserves — is the most concrete thing the medical profession can offer this debate.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Single embryo transfer should be the binding default practice to minimize the high maternal and neonatal risks associated with multiple pregnancies.
  2. 02Comprehensive mental health support, including rigorous pre-screening and guaranteed postpartum counseling, must be a central requirement of the surrogacy process.
  3. 03Surrogates should be treated by independent physicians to eliminate the conflicts of interest that arise when the intended parents' fertility specialists manage the pregnancy.
  4. 04National medical eligibility standards and a registry should be established to prevent high-risk individuals from serving as surrogates and to track long-term health outcomes.
  5. 05Intended parents should undergo medical and capacity screenings similar to those required in adoption to ensure the well-being of the resulting child.
