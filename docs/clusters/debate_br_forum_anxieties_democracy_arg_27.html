"The crisis of democratic legitimacy is caused by the insulation of professional legislatures from the popular will, and the solution lies in embracing populist and direct-participation mechanisms rather than retreating into mediated representation."

Ira Katznelson’s elegant lament for representative democracy is the sigh of a class that fears the people it claims to champion. His essay is a sustained exercise in liberal gatekeeping: he frets that populist, plebiscitary, and deliberative incursions into legislative authority risk “illiberal authoritarianism,” invoking the specter of Perón and Orbán to warn against any bypassing of mediated representation. But this framing gets things backwards. The crisis of democratic legitimacy today stems not from too much voice or too little deference to legislatures, but from the steady insulation of decision-making from popular will. Katznelson’s solution—a return to “solemn” parliamentary lawmaking bounded by “moral ambiguity”—is precisely what has driven ordinary people to despair. What we need is more populism, not less; more direct control by citizens, not a retreat into the comforting arms of professional lawmakers.

Look at what actually happens inside the legislative arena that Katznelson idealizes. The physics of consent is not a dignified weighing of opinions; it is a cascade of lobbyist-written bills, closed-door leadership negotiations, and omnibus packages that nobody reads. “Poor legislative craftsmanship” is a genteel term for a process in which staffers and interest groups draft provisions that serve narrow ends while the public is shut out. When citizens push back—through protest, through referenda, through disruptive primary challenges—they are not disfiguring democracy. They are trying to reclaim it from a duopoly that has gutted public meaning from elections. The Tea Party’s hostility to the Republican establishment and the Bernie Sanders insurgency both succeed because they speak to a deep desire for representation that actually represents, rather than manages.

Katznelson’s historical contrast between the New Deal’s democratic renewal and the Peronist temptation is revealing. He applauds the New Deal for revitalizing democracy through lawmaking, but that lawmaking was itself a product of massive, often disruptive popular pressure: the Bonus Army, the Flint sit-down strikes, Huey Long’s Share Our Wealth movement. The New Deal worked not because Franklin Roosevelt and Congress coolly deliberated, but because they were terrified of more radical alternatives. Democracy was saved by the threat of populist insurgency, not by its absence. Similarly, the populist energies of today—on left and right—are signals that the system is failing to absorb legitimate grievances. The answer is not to condemn those energies as proto-authoritarian but to channel them into institutional forms that shift power downward.

That means taking seriously reforms Katznelson dismisses. Well-designed referenda and initiatives, like those in California for all their flaws, have sometimes produced policy breakthroughs—on criminal justice, marijuana, environmental protection—that legislatures had refused to touch. Participatory budgeting in New York City and elsewhere gives ordinary people a direct stake in public spending. Deliberative polls and citizens’ assemblies can show that when citizens are given time and information, they are capable of making complex judgments that rival or exceed those of elected officials. These are not replacements for legislatures; they are correctives to a system in which legislatures have become wholly owned subsidiaries of the financial sector.

Katznelson wants citizens to suspend their beliefs and accept provisional outcomes, but he asks too much. Democratic legitimacy requires that people see their deepest convictions reflected, at least sometimes, in public policy. When they do not, and when the institutional gatekeepers tell them to stay patient, they will look elsewhere. If liberal democrats continue to cast populism as the enemy, they may well create the very illiberalism they fear. The alternative is to embrace a democracy in which representation is not an end in itself but a living relationship between institutions and an aroused, participatory citizenry. That is not a disfigurement; it is a homecoming.
