"The survival of American democracy depends on the restoration of legislative institutions and their unglamorous internal structures rather than the pursuit of direct popular sovereignty or alternative bypasses like executive and judicial overreach."

Ira Katznelson is right to begin with the dome. The visible scaffolding around the Capitol is reassuring because it signifies a public commitment to repair. The invisible scaffolding inside Congress—rules, norms, parties, committees, habits of bargaining—is harder to see and easier to neglect. Yet representative democracy depends less on occasional popular arousal than on these unglamorous structures. If they decay, the people do not become more sovereign; they become more available to manipulation by executives, courts, billionaires, and television demagogues.

The central virtue of Katznelson’s essay is its refusal to confuse democratic energy with democratic government. Popular mobilization matters. It has corrected the injustices of legislatures many times, from abolitionism to woman suffrage to the civil rights movement. But mobilization is not a substitute for lawmaking. A march can dramatize a grievance; it cannot by itself write a tax code, administer health insurance subsidies, reform sentencing, or set durable climate policy. Those tasks require institutions capable of turning conflict into binding, revisable rules.

This is why the comparison to the 1930s, though imperfect, is useful. The interwar crisis was not only a crisis of values; it was a crisis of competence. Democracies appeared unable to respond to depression, unemployment, and the demands of mass society. Authoritarian regimes advertised speed, discipline, and unity. Their promises were murderous lies, but their appeal fed on real democratic failures. The New Deal mattered because it answered that challenge in democratic form. It showed that legislatures, parties, agencies, courts, and elections could be assembled into a system capable of action without abolishing criticism.

Our crisis is not as grave, but it is structurally familiar. We have problems that are widely recognized yet poorly addressed: stagnant wages, racialized policing and incarceration, immigration reform, climate change, infrastructure, long-term fiscal commitments, and the governance of national security. The striking fact is not that Americans disagree about them. Democracy presupposes disagreement. The striking fact is that the institutions designed to process disagreement so often convert it into paralysis.

Katznelson emphasizes two sources of this failure: unequal political access and diminished legislative capacity. These are connected. When wealth converts too easily into influence, citizens lose confidence that legislation represents anything more than organized pressure. When legislatures cease to deliberate and legislate competently, organized money becomes still more powerful because complexity favors insiders. The result is a vicious circle: distrust breeds withdrawal, withdrawal strengthens narrow interests, narrow interests deepen distrust.

Campaign finance is therefore not a side issue. The Supreme Court’s post–Citizens United world has not merely permitted more spending; it has changed the psychology of representation. Candidates know that a handful of donors can sustain or destroy campaigns. Officeholders know that policy details invisible to most voters matter immensely to those with resources to monitor them. Even when no explicit bargain is struck, dependence shapes attention. Democracy is distorted not only when votes are bought, but when agendas are quietly narrowed.

Still, money alone cannot explain the present breakdown. Congress has also surrendered too much of its own dignity. Members denounce “Washington” as if they were not part of it. They delegate hard choices to the Federal Reserve, the courts, the bureaucracy, and the president, then complain about unaccountable power. The war power has migrated to the executive; budget decisions lurch from crisis to crisis; oversight often becomes theater rather than discipline. A legislature that does not defend its own role cannot expect citizens to respect it.

Repair will require institutional reform, but also a change in democratic imagination. We should not ask Congress to become harmonious. Harmony is not the democratic ideal. We should ask it to become a forum in which conflict is structured, informed, and consequential. That means reducing the incentives for permanent campaigning, strengthening committees, restoring regular order, curbing the most destructive uses of the filibuster, protecting voting rights, and limiting the capacity of concentrated wealth to dominate elections. It also means reviving parties as responsible governing organizations rather than fundraising brands.

The temptation Katznelson identifies—to bypass legislatures in favor of courts, executives, plebiscites, or movements—is understandable. Many of the twentieth century’s greatest moral advances required pressure from outside Congress and sometimes judicial intervention against legislative injustice. But emergency routes cannot become the main road. A politics that relies permanently on courts will invite counter-courts; a politics that relies on executive orders will invite counter-orders; a politics that relies on plebiscitary mobilization will empower whoever best commands anger.

Representative democracy deserves only two cheers, perhaps, but those two cheers are indispensable. It admits variety and permits criticism, as Forster said. It also does something more prosaic and equally precious: it allows a large, divided people to act together without pretending to be one voice. That is the achievement now under strain. The Capitol dome can be repaired by engineers. Congress must be repaired by citizens who remember that self-government is not the expression of pure popular will, but the patient construction of institutions through which disagreement can govern.
