Diversified Claude

"The lead essay's rejection of neoliberalism is based on a flawed historical and empirical diagnosis, and its proposed alternatives misrepresent economic science as a justification for a specific political manifesto."

In Defense of What They Call Neoliberalism

I want to enter a dissent. The authors of this essay take it as common ground that the policy regime of the last forty years has been a failure, that "neoliberalism" is responsible for the ills of contemporary America, and that the task of economists is to chart an alternative. I think each of these claims is wrong, or at least much more contestable than the authors allow.

Start with the diagnosis. The authors lead with U.S. inequality, wage stagnation, declining life expectancy, and so on. These are real concerns. But the framing of these as the fruits of neoliberalism is a parochial American story dressed up as a global verdict. The period the authors lament has also been the period in which global poverty fell more rapidly than at any time in human history. Roughly a billion people have escaped extreme poverty since 1990. Child mortality has halved. Literacy has soared. Life expectancy worldwide has risen by nearly a decade. If "neoliberalism" means trade liberalization, integration of poor countries into global capital and goods markets, and the spread of property rights and the rule of law, then neoliberalism is the most successful poverty-reduction program in history. To indict it for the relatively modest distributional troubles of the rich world is to weigh a stubbed toe against a cured cancer.

Within the United States, the picture is also more complex than the essay admits. Median household income, properly measured to include in-kind transfers and adjusted for household size, has risen substantially since the 1970s. Consumption inequality has grown much less than income inequality. The share of Americans in absolute poverty as conventionally measured has fallen. Life expectancy declines in the last few years are concerning, but they are driven heavily by opioids and suicide—not phenomena well explained by "market fundamentalism," and not phenomena obviously remediable by better minimum wage policy or financial regulation. The story that everything has gotten worse for ordinary Americans for half a century is rhetorically powerful and substantively misleading.

Now the policy prescriptions. The proposals collected under the EfIP banner are a grab bag of varying quality. Some are sensible (Zucman on multinational taxation, Admati on bank capital). Some are dubious (the suggestion that minimum wage hikes do not reduce employment is true for modest increases at low wage levels and false at higher levels, as the authors surely know but do not say). Some are dangerous (Rodrik on protectionism justified by "social safeguards" is essentially a license for any rich country to block imports whenever a domestic constituency objects, which is to say, always; the casualties of that regime would be poor workers in poor countries, not the constituencies Rodrik wishes to protect).

The deeper problem is the authors' theory of how policy goes wrong. They suggest that neoliberalism succeeded because of intellectual capture: economists became cheerleaders for markets and policymakers listened. This is not a serious history. Neoliberal reforms in the 1980s and 1990s happened because the alternatives had visibly failed. The British state nationalized substantial portions of its economy in the postwar period and the result was relative decline so severe that the United Kingdom went to the IMF in 1976. The Soviet economic model collapsed because it could not produce goods anyone wanted. Latin American import-substitution industrialization produced hyperinflation and debt crises. The "neoliberal" reforms that followed were not imposed by Hayekian ideologues; they were attempts, often grudging, to fix systems that had broken down on their own terms.

The success of the reforms is also clearer than the essay suggests. India's liberalization in 1991 lifted hundreds of millions out of poverty. China's market reforms—which the authors curiously cite as evidence for institutional pluralism—are textbook examples of marketization, including the introduction of private property, profit incentives, foreign investment, and integration into global trade. Eastern European countries that liberalized rapidly outperformed those that did so slowly. The Nordics, often invoked as alternatives to neoliberalism, have actually liberalized substantially since the 1980s while maintaining strong welfare states; they are not models of resistance to markets but of competent markets paired with redistribution.

I am also skeptical of the authors' confidence that they have identified "free lunches." The history of economic policy is largely a history of free lunches that turned out to have prices. Rent control was a free lunch until cities found themselves with chronic housing shortages. Bank regulation was a free lunch until depositors were trapped in failing institutions. The Community Reinvestment Act was a free lunch until subprime lending unwound. Even "no-brainer" interventions usually have second-order effects that economists, including these economists, will discover after the fact and write papers about. Humility, which the authors recommend at one point in the essay, is in short supply when they describe their own proposals.

There is one point at which I want to agree wholeheartedly with the authors. They are right that economics, properly practiced, is not a set of fixed policy conclusions but a framework for analyzing trade-offs. Where I part company is in their suggestion that the framework, when properly applied, more or less supports their politics. It does not. It supports their politics in some cases, opposes it in others, and is genuinely indeterminate in many. The honest version of "Economics After Neoliberalism" would be a much more divided essay, with full-throated defenses of trade liberalization sitting next to wage board proposals, and with serious attention to the costs as well as the benefits of every intervention. What the authors have produced is closer to a manifesto: a presentation of one political vision as the natural conclusion of contemporary economic science. We have seen this move before, from the other side. It was not honest then and it is not honest now.

Vanilla Claude

"While the Economics for Inclusive Prosperity project offers valuable policy alternatives to neoliberalism, its defense of the economics profession fails to fully reckon with the discipline's historical complicity in creating current inequalities or the need for deeper institutional and pedagogical reform."

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman have written something both useful and revealing. Useful, because their Economics for Inclusive Prosperity briefs concretize an emerging center-left policy program at a moment when the U.S. Democratic Party is being pulled, productively, leftward — Elizabeth Warren's wealth tax proposal (built on Zucman's own work with Saez), the Sanders Medicare-for-All bill, Ocasio-Cortez's just-introduced Green New Deal resolution. Revealing, because the essay is in part an act of professional self-defense, and the shape of that defense tells us something about where mainstream economics still resists reckoning with its complicity in the order it now wants to revise.

The defense runs roughly like this: neoliberalism is not economics but its "primitive, simplistic perversion"; the discipline today is empirical, pluralistic, attentive to market failures, and equipped with tools for designing better institutions. The textbook caricature — competitive equilibrium, "markets always work" — is the property of Econ 101 and conservative think tanks, not of the working researcher. Therefore, do not throw the baby out with the bathwater: economists have ideas, and you need them.

Much of this is right. Anyone who has watched the credibility revolution in applied microeconomics, the post-2008 rethinking of macroprudential regulation, the rise of behavioral and historical work, the granular Chetty-style mobility research, or the Card-Krueger-to-Dube reassessment of minimum wages, knows that the discipline has moved. The Diaz-Alejandro line the authors quote is more true than ever: economic theory is now a near-infinite menu, and the empirical literature can sustain almost any ideologically inflected reading. That is precisely why "what does economics say?" is increasingly a question about which economists you ask, and about which findings get picked up by which institutions — that is, a question about politics and sociology, not theory.

And here is where the lead essay is too easy on itself. The claim that neoliberalism is a "perversion" of economics rather than its mainstream expression treats the relationship between disciplinary knowledge and political ideology as one of misunderstanding. But the historical record — readable in Burgin, Mirowski, Slobodian, and Stedman Jones — is that the Mont Pelerin project knew exactly what it was doing, and that its consolidation in U.S. policy circles from the 1970s onward was not a misreading of "true" economics so much as a successful colonization of the discipline's prestige. When the IMF imposed structural adjustment, when Larry Summers signed off on derivatives deregulation, when graduate trade economists asserted that NAFTA's losers could in principle be compensated and then shrugged when they weren't — these were not low-status cranks or Heritage Foundation hacks. They were card-carrying members of the profession in good standing, deploying its tools and authority. The discipline's mainstream cannot simultaneously claim credit for the empirical sophistication of 2019 and disown the policy authority it exercised in 1995.

This is not a gotcha. It is a question about the present. If neoliberalism's grip on policy was made possible in part by features of how economics is done — methodological individualism, a strong prior toward Pareto-style analysis, the habit of holding politics fixed while optimizing within it, the reflex to "take the side of markets because no one else will" — then the corrective is not just better proposals. It is institutional and pedagogical change inside the profession. The authors acknowledge "bad habits" but treat them as foibles around the edges of a fundamentally healthy science. I am less sure. The CORE curriculum project, heterodox programs, and feminist and stratification economists have for years been making more thoroughgoing critiques of how the discipline trains its young, what it counts as rigorous, and which questions get top-five publications. Their absence from this lineup is conspicuous — all the authors of the briefs are, by their own account, "tenured academic economists, working in broadly mainstream subfields." That is a fine bench, but it is not the whole problem.

A related worry concerns the rhetorical center of gravity in the proposals: the "free lunch." The authors emphasize that we are operating well inside the justice-efficiency frontier, so we can have more fairness without sacrificing growth — perhaps even gain on both. This is empirically defensible in many of the cases they cite (monopsony in labor markets, financial-stability gains from higher capital requirements, the deadweight loss of tax competition). And it is rhetorically attractive precisely because it sidesteps the harder distributional fight. But the technocratic move of dissolving political conflict into Pareto improvements is itself a neoliberal habit. Some of what an inclusive economy requires — redistributing wealth, breaking up incumbent firms, reallocating property rights — will not be a free lunch for the losers, who are often powerful and well-organized. The political-economy challenge is then not "how do we identify the free lunches?" but "how do we win the fights that aren't free lunches?" Naidu's union piece and Kaplan's political-economy proposal point in this direction; the framing of the introduction sometimes pulls the other way.

Two more lacunae are worth naming. First, on macroeconomics: the briefs treat capital markets and inequality and financial regulation, which is welcome, but the deeper post-2008 macro debate — about secular stagnation, the appropriate size and composition of public balance sheets, the relationship between fiscal policy and full employment, the conditions under which deficits matter — is barely visible. With unemployment under 4 percent and inflation still subdued, with MMT-flavored arguments forcing their way into mainstream commentary via Stephanie Kelton and the Green New Deal, and with Olivier Blanchard's recent AEA address pushing back on debt panic, the EfIP project's relative silence on aggregate demand and the public sector's macro role is a missed opportunity. "Inclusive prosperity" is much easier to deliver in a hot labor market.

Second, on climate and race. The authors gesture at climate (carbon pricing) and mention persistent racial disparities in the opening, but neither becomes structural to the program. Carbon pricing, as economists have long recommended, is necessary and politically inert; what is now needed is industrial policy, public investment, and distributional design — the substance of the Green New Deal conversation. And on race, the U.S. wealth gap, mass incarceration, and labor market discrimination are not addressable by minimum-wage policy and wage boards alone. Darrick Hamilton, William Darity, and colleagues have been making the case for baby bonds and reparative policy; that intellectual tradition belongs in any serious economics of inclusive prosperity.

None of this is to dismiss the EfIP project. Better to have a group of mainstream economists making this case than not, and several of the proposals — Zucman's formulary apportionment, Admati on bank capital, Dube on wage boards, Rodrik on social safeguards in trade — are concrete, implementable, and overdue. The authors are also right that the discipline contains the resources to do much more than apologize for incumbents. But the deeper task they invite — building a post-neoliberal economic policy paradigm — will require both more humility about how the profession got us here and more political imagination about how to get us out. Tools are not a program. Whose tools, in whose hands, on whose behalf — those are the questions that come next.

  2. 02The project's focus on 'free lunch' solutions—where justice and efficiency are both improved—risks avoiding the necessary political and distributional conflicts required for true structural change.
  3. 03The initiative remains too insular by relying on mainstream tenured academics while overlooking more radical critiques from heterodox, feminist, and stratification economists.
  4. 04The policy framework is incomplete because it lacks a robust engagement with modern macroeconomic debates regarding aggregate demand, fiscal policy, and full employment.
  5. 05The proposals fail to sufficiently integrate industrial policy for climate change or reparative policies to address structural racial wealth gaps.

"While the Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) project correctly identifies empirical inefficiencies, its reliance on a 'free lunch' framing unnecessarily limits the progressive agenda by prioritizing technical efficiency over essential moral and political justifications for redistribution."

Free Lunches and the Question of Values

I want to focus on one revealing phrase in the EfIP manifesto: "free lunch." The authors write that the proposals reflect "the sense that economies are operating well inside the justice-efficiency frontier, and that there are numerous policy 'free lunches' that could push us towards an economy that is morally better without sacrificing (and, indeed, possibly enhancing) prosperity."

This phrasing is meant to disarm critics. The standard objection to egalitarian policy has been that it imposes efficiency costs, and the authors want to push back: no, equality and efficiency can be improved together. The argument is empirically often correct. The U.S. economy is full of inefficiencies that fall disproportionately on the poor and that could be corrected to everyone's benefit. Monopsony in labor markets, predatory lending, the structure of healthcare, the geography of public goods—all of these are sites where redistributive interventions would likely raise aggregate welfare. The authors are right about this.

But the framing has a cost. "Free lunch" frames the project of progressive policy as one of seeking out interventions that are Pareto improvements or close to it. The standard for action is whether we can improve outcomes without anyone losing. This is a stringent standard. It privileges interventions that face minimal opposition and rules out those that require redistribution from concentrated winners to dispersed losers. It implicitly accepts existing distributions of property and power as the baseline against which interventions are to be judged.

The deeper progressive case for economic transformation has never rested on free lunches alone. It has rested on a substantive view of what we owe each other, of what a decent society looks like, of what is unacceptable about the current distribution regardless of whether changing it imposes efficiency costs. The civil rights movement did not justify desegregation primarily on efficiency grounds. The labor movement did not justify the eight-hour day primarily on efficiency grounds. Universal suffrage was not enacted because it was efficient. The case in each instance was a moral case about what people are owed.

When economists frame progressive policy as the discovery of free lunches, they implicitly accept that the burden of proof is on those who would change the status quo to show that the change is costless. This burden is unfair and self-defeating. It is unfair because the status quo is itself the result of countless interventions—property law, contract law, corporate law, intellectual property regimes, financial regulation—whose distributional effects are not subject to the same scrutiny. It is self-defeating because many of the most important progressive interventions will not pass the free-lunch test; they will require somebody to lose, and the case for the policy will rest on the moral importance of what is gained.

Take the case of a wealth tax of the kind proposed by some of the authors' colleagues. The case for such a tax is principally that extreme concentrations of wealth are corrosive of democracy, of equal opportunity, and of social solidarity, and that taxing them is therefore desirable even if it has some efficiency cost. The "efficiency cost" framing—does the tax raise revenue net of behavioral responses, does it distort savings, does it slow growth—is part of the analysis but it is not the heart of the matter. The heart of the matter is the moral and political claim that the current distribution of wealth is incompatible with the kind of society we want to live in.

The EfIP framing makes this kind of argument harder to articulate. By presenting progressive policy as a series of technical corrections to market failures, it strips out the moral content that gives the project its energy. It also limits the range of available policy. Many of the proposals in the project are good; they would not be sufficient even if all adopted, and the next steps will require arguments the free-lunch frame cannot support.

I want to note one place where the authors do break from the free-lunch frame: Rodrik's proposal on trade restrictions justified by "social safeguards." Rodrik explicitly refuses to clothe protectionism in efficiency language. He argues that trade restrictions can be justified by the requirements of the domestic social contract, full stop. This is the right kind of argument. It treats values—the value of social solidarity, of democratic self-government, of egalitarian relationships among citizens—as ends to which efficiency must be subordinated when necessary. The authors note this with approval and connect it to Polanyi.

I would like to see this mode of argument extended through the rest of the project. The case for unions is not just that they correct monopsony; it is that workplaces are sites of human dignity and that workers deserve voice in their conditions. The case for public goods is not just that markets undersupply them; it is that some goods—education, health, security—should be available to all as a matter of citizenship rather than purchasing power. The case for progressive taxation is not just that the marginal utility of money diminishes; it is that great concentrations of wealth distort the political community we share.

Economics does not have to answer these value questions. But it should leave room for them rather than absorbing them into the technical apparatus of welfare maximization. The free-lunch frame, well-meant as it is, narrows the moral imagination of the project. The authors are right that there are many free lunches to be had. They should also remember that the lunches that matter most are sometimes not free, and that the case for them is something the discipline of economics is not equipped to make on its own.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'free lunch' framework implicitly accepts the current distribution of power and property as a baseline, unfairly placing the burden of proof on reformers to show that changes are costless.
  2. 02Major historical progressive achievements, such as civil rights and universal suffrage, were won through moral arguments about what citizens are owed rather than through efficiency-based justifications.
  3. 03Focusing on Pareto improvements or market failure corrections strips policy of the moral energy required to tackle issues like extreme wealth inequality, which may require trade-offs in efficiency to protect democracy.
  4. 04The project should follow the example of Dani Rodrik’s trade proposal by explicitly subordinating economic efficiency to social values like human dignity, citizenship, and democratic self-government.
  5. 05Economics should serve as a tool that leaves space for value-based questions instead of attempting to absorb all social goals into a technical apparatus of welfare maximization.
Diversified MiniMax

"While the Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) project correctly distinguishes economics from neoliberalism, it overestimates the discipline's ability to provide neutral tools for reform without addressing the inherent normative biases and institutional capture of the profession."

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman make a useful contribution by disaggregating economics from neoliberalism and by pointing to the policy-relevant research being produced by mainstream economists. The EfIP project demonstrates that the discipline is not monolithic and that there are economists willing to engage with questions of distribution and power.

But the essay is too optimistic about the capacity of economics to provide the tools needed for inclusive prosperity. The authors write that economics "supplies the tools needed to lay out the trade-offs, thus contributing to a more informed democratic debate." This underestimates the degree to which economic analysis is itself shaped by normative commitments and political positions. The choice of which trade-offs to highlight, which assumptions to make, and which outcomes to prioritize is not a purely technical exercise; it reflects value judgments that are themselves contestable.

The emphasis on market failures is case in point. The authors argue that many policy failures reflect "first-best reasoning" that ignores complications and adverse implications elsewhere. This is a fair critique, but it does not go far enough. The very concept of market failure presupposes a benchmark—a perfectly competitive market economy—that is itself the product of specific theoretical choices. Economists trained to think in terms of market failures will tend to see policy problems as requiring corrections to an otherwise sound market system, rather than as requiring more fundamental transformations of economic organization.

The authors also understate the degree to which economics has been captured by the interests it purports to study. Financial economics, in particular, has developed close ties to the financial industry, and its research priorities reflect the concerns of banks and investment firms more than the concerns of the broader public. The Basel III regulatory framework, which the authors cite approvingly, was itself heavily shaped by the financial industry's lobbying efforts. Similar dynamics operate in other subfields, including trade economics, labor economics, and public finance.

A more radical critique would question whether economics as currently practiced can ever serve inclusive prosperity, or whether fundamental changes in the discipline's structure and priorities are required. The authors seem to believe that economics can be redirected toward more progressive ends without changing the basic framework of academic economics—the journals, the departments, the professional associations, the career paths. I am less confident that such redirection is possible within the existing institutional framework.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Economic analysis is not a purely technical exercise because the selection of trade-offs and assumptions is fundamentally shaped by contestable value judgments and political positions.
  2. 02The 'market failure' framework is limiting because it treats policy issues as mere corrections to a default market system rather than considering fundamental transformations of economic organization.
  3. 03The essay ignores the extent to which various subfields of economics have been intellectually and politically captured by the specific industries and interests they are meant to regulate.
  4. 04Meaningful redirection toward inclusive prosperity may be impossible without radical changes to the discipline’s institutional structures, such as its journals, career paths, and professional associations.
Diversified MiniMax

"While the Economics for Inclusive Prosperity project successfully decouples economics from neoliberalism, it remains overly optimistic about the discipline's capacity to solve societal problems without addressing deep-seated pedagogical and institutional biases."

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman have written a useful essay that makes a strong case against the identification of economics with neoliberalism. The EfIP project demonstrates that mainstream economists can contribute to a more progressive policy agenda, and the specific proposals are generally sound. But the essay is overly optimistic about the capacity of economics to solve the problems it identifies.

The authors write that "contemporary economics is hardly a paean to markets and selfishness" and that "the typical course in microeconomics spends more time on market failures and how to fix them than on the magic of competitive markets." This is true, but it does not address the gap between what economists know and what they teach, between the research frontier and the curriculum, between the sophistication of academic economics and the simplicity of popular discourse.

The introductory courses that shape most students' understanding of economics continue to emphasize the competitive model, the magic of markets, and the efficiency gains from trade. Even when instructors try to complicate this picture, they face constraints from textbooks, course requirements, and the expectations of students who want clear answers. The result is that most people who take economics courses come away with a simplified version of economic theory that bears little resemblance to what professional economists actually believe.

Changing this will require not only better research but also better teaching, better textbooks, and better communication between economists and the broader public. The authors note that "the sociology of the profession—career incentives, norms, socialization patterns—often militates against engagement with the policy world." This is correct, but the same sociology also militates against the kind of pedagogical innovation that would be needed to change how economics is taught.

The authors also underestimate the degree to which economic ideas are shaped by political and institutional contexts. Economics is not a neutral set of tools that can be applied equally well to any policy problem; it is a discipline that has developed in specific historical circumstances and that reflects the concerns and interests of those who have shaped it. The emphasis on efficiency over distribution, the treatment of markets as natural rather than social phenomena, and the focus on individual behavior rather than collective action—all of these reflect choices that have been made by economists operating within specific institutional contexts.

Recognizing this does not mean abandoning economics, but it does mean being more critical about what economics can and cannot do. The tools of economics are valuable, but they are not sufficient. Building a more inclusive economy will require not only better economic analysis but also better politics, better institutions, and better civic engagement. The authors should acknowledge this complexity rather than suggesting that economics can provide the guidance needed for a more inclusive form of prosperity.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A significant gap exists between sophisticated academic research and the simplified, market-centric way economics is taught in introductory courses and communicated to the public.
  2. 02The professional sociology of economics creates incentives that discourage the pedagogical innovation and public engagement necessary for meaningful reform.
  3. 03Economic theory is not a neutral toolkit but a discipline shaped by historical and political contexts that prioritize efficiency and individualism over collective action and distribution.
  4. 04Economic analysis alone is insufficient for building an inclusive economy, as it requires integration with better politics, institutional reform, and civic engagement.
Vanilla Gemini

"While the Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) network correctly identifies the tools within economics to combat inequality, the discipline must acknowledge its own historical role in constructing the neoliberal order and move beyond technocratic models to engage with the raw realities of political power and moral philosophy."

The launch of the Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) network, as outlined by Dani Rodrik, Suresh Naidu, and Gabriel Zucman, represents a vital and overdue intervention in our public discourse. The authors accurately diagnose the cascading crises of our time: historic levels of inequality, wage stagnation, declining life expectancy, and a nativist political backlash fueled by deep economic insecurity. More importantly, they offer a compelling vision for how the discipline of economics can be leveraged to solve these crises rather than exacerbate them. By arguing that contemporary economics is rife with the tools necessary to dismantle the inequities of the current era, they provide a much-needed bridge between academic rigor and progressive policy.

However, in their eagerness to rehabilitate the economics profession and marshal its tools for a post-neoliberal era, the authors let the discipline off the hook a bit too easily. To construct a genuinely inclusive prosperity, we must first be honest about how we arrived at our current gilded age.

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman argue that neoliberalism—with its attendant deregulation, financialization, and hyper-globalization—is not the logical conclusion of modern economics, but rather its “primitive, simplistic perversion.” They lay the blame for this perversion primarily at the feet of conservative foundations, think tanks, and rent-seeking special interests who weaponized a cartoonish version of economics to justify their preferred policies.

This is a comforting narrative for economists, but it is historically incomplete. The architects of the neoliberal order were not merely conservative ideologues operating on the fringes of the discipline; they were often the most celebrated and highly credentialed members of the economics profession itself. From the rational expectations revolution that justified a retreat from macroeconomic management, to the law and economics movement that systematically dismantled antitrust enforcement, to the consensus models of international trade that dismissed the localized devastation of offshoring, mainstream economics actively built the intellectual scaffolding of the last forty years. Policy failures like the repeal of Glass-Steagall, the aggressive push for capital account liberalization in the developing world, and the decades-long insistence that labor unions were mere monopolistic distortions were not forced upon economists by politicians. They were championed by the commanding heights of the academy.

By framing neoliberalism as a mere misunderstanding or hijacking of economics, the authors obscure the ways in which the discipline’s core methodologies inherently bias its practitioners toward certain worldviews. The authors correctly point out that modern economics is highly empirical, and that any bright graduate student can tweak assumptions to yield a variety of policy recommendations. But this defense ignores the immense normative power of the baseline.

In economics, the baseline remains the perfectly competitive market equilibrium. As the authors admit, this is the dominant framework in introductory courses. Even when economists advance to more sophisticated models that account for "frictions," they are still beginning from a paradigm where the unfettered market is the natural, "first-best" state of the world, and government intervention must bear the burden of proof to justify correcting a specific "market failure." When society is viewed through a lens where markets are natural and interventions are artificial, it is unsurprising that generations of students and policymakers have absorbed a default hostility toward the regulatory state.

This is why, despite the brilliance of the EfIP policy briefs, the continued reliance on the language of "market failures" to explain our current dysfunction feels insufficient. The crises we face today are not merely the result of asymmetric information, externalities, or imperfect competition. They are the result of deliberate distributions of power and wealth.

To their immense credit, the authors recognize this. They state explicitly that "power matters enormously in shaping market outcomes," and they highlight power asymmetries between labor and capital, between multinational corporations and sovereign states, and between the wealthy and the disenfranchised. The proposals they cite reflect a genuine desire to rebalance these scales. Arindrajit Dube’s proposal for sectoral wage boards directly tackles the systemic monopsony power of employers. Gabriel Zucman’s ingenious plan for apportioning the global profits of multinationals based on sales offers a tangible mechanism to break the blackmail of international tax competition. Dani Rodrik’s call for "social safeguards" in trade agreements elegantly subordinates textbook efficiency to the preservation of the domestic social contract.

Yet, a tension remains between the profound political shifts these policies require and the technocratic language often used to describe them. Consider the essay’s discussion of organized labor. The authors suggest that mechanism design, behavioral economics, and experiments can be mobilized to "ease the pervasive collective action problem facing unions." While this is undoubtedly true in a technical sense, the decline of the U.S. labor movement was not primarily a failure of mechanism design. It was the result of a coordinated, decades-long political and legal assault by corporate interests. Reviving labor power will require fierce political struggle, mass organizing, and legislative warfare. Economics can offer blueprints for what an optimal union structure might look like, but it struggles to conceptualize the raw, unquantifiable social friction required to build it.

This highlights the ultimate limitation of relying on economics to save us from neoliberalism: economics is fundamentally a science of optimization, but politics is a clash of values and power. The EfIP network’s proposals demonstrate that economics can design fairer, more inclusive institutions. But as the authors note, policies often "break on the rock of government capture by special interests." Ethan Kaplan’s work on democratic political economy is an excellent inclusion to address this, but it underscores a daunting reality. If our political system is already captured by the winners of the neoliberal era, then the most beautifully designed, empirically sound policy proposals will remain locked in academic journals.

Writing in 2019, as we stare down the barrel of an escalating climate crisis and a deeply fractured democracy, the necessity of a new economic paradigm cannot be overstated. The Economics for Inclusive Prosperity project proves that the discipline contains the analytical firepower to help chart a new course. The empirical turn in economics has successfully dismantled the theoretical idols of market fundamentalism, proving that minimum wages do not inherently destroy jobs and that financial liberalization does not automatically generate growth.

However, moving beyond neoliberalism will require the profession to do more than produce better empirical studies and more creative models of market failure. It will require economists to step outside the safety of methodological individualism and engage deeply with history, sociology, and political science—not just as sources of variable data, but as equal partners in understanding human society. It will require abandoning the pretense of the detached technocrat and acknowledging that economic policy is always, inevitably, an exercise in moral philosophy and political power. The authors of this lead essay have bravely taken the first step. The challenge now is for the rest of the discipline to follow them out of the seminar room and into the messy, contested reality of democratic life.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The authors' claim that neoliberalism is a 'perversion' of economics ignores the fact that mainstream economic leaders and academic models actively provided the intellectual scaffolding for deregulation and financialization.
  2. 02The discipline’s reliance on the 'perfectly competitive market' as a baseline creates an inherent normative bias that places the burden of proof on government intervention rather than on the market itself.
  3. 03Explaining societal crises solely through the lens of 'market failures' is insufficient because it obscures the deliberate and systemic distributions of power and wealth that drive inequality.
  4. 04The technocratic language of economics, such as 'mechanism design' for unions, fails to account for the social friction and political struggle necessary to enact structural change.
  5. 05To be truly effective, economists must abandon the pretense of being detached technocrats and treat history, sociology, and political science as equal partners in understanding human society.
