"While the authors of the lead essay correctly argue that economics can be rescued from neoliberalism to serve inclusive prosperity, their project must go beyond technocratic design to confront the political power dynamics and professional biases that have historically sustained the status quo."

Dani Rodrik, Suresh Naidu, and Gabriel Zucman are right about the central fact of our political economy: the old consensus has broken down, and it will not be put back together by repeating the formulas that helped break it. The combination of stagnant wages, extreme concentration of wealth, precarious work, regional decline, unaffordable health care, and ecological danger has made “inclusive prosperity” not a slogan but a necessity. A society that delivers growth without broadly shared security will eventually lose both social peace and democratic legitimacy.

Their most important contribution is to separate economics from neoliberalism. This distinction matters. In public debate, “economics” too often means a handful of policy reflexes: cut taxes, deregulate, liberalize trade, weaken unions, privatize public goods, and assume that whatever happens in markets is efficient unless government has interfered. But this is not economics as a discipline. It is a political program that selectively borrows from economics, strips away the qualifications, and presents contestable assumptions as scientific law.

The authors are also right that contemporary economics contains the resources for a different program. Labor markets are not the frictionless spot markets of textbook diagrams. Employers often have monopsony power. Workers lack information, mobility, savings, and bargaining leverage. Minimum wage research over the last quarter century has shown that modest increases do not necessarily destroy jobs, especially where employers have wage-setting power. Finance is not a neutral plumbing system; it generates leverage, instability, and crises. Trade creates aggregate gains but can devastate particular workers and places, and the political system has repeatedly failed to provide meaningful adjustment. Climate change is perhaps the most obvious market failure in history. Health care and education are riddled with externalities, information asymmetries, and moral commitments that make simple market provision inadequate.

In this sense, the authors’ project is less a revolt against economics than a rescue of economics from caricature. It asks economists to do what the best of them have always done: examine institutions, incentives, power, constraints, and evidence rather than repeat slogans. That is a welcome intervention.

Still, the project will succeed only if it avoids two temptations. The first is the temptation to present inclusive prosperity as a technocratic correction to neoliberalism. The second is the temptation to treat the profession’s past political role as mostly a misunderstanding.

On the first point, one can applaud wage boards, stronger unions, corporate tax reform, financial regulation, social insurance, and pro-worker technology policy while still asking: who will fight for these things? The authors emphasize evidence and institutional design, but inclusive prosperity is not merely a design challenge. It is a conflict over power. The winners of the existing order—multinational corporations, wealthy households, dominant technology firms, financial institutions, and their political allies—will not step aside because better models have been written. They will resist. They already do.

Consider Zucman’s proposal to allocate multinational profits according to sales. It is elegant and promising. It directly addresses the absurdity of a world in which companies book profits in tax havens where little real activity occurs. But the obstacle is not intellectual. Many policymakers already know the current system is indefensible. The obstacle is political: the firms that benefit from tax avoidance have resources, lawyers, lobbyists, and the ability to threaten relocation. Similarly, economists have accumulated evidence that unions reduce inequality and that labor’s decline has contributed to wage stagnation. Yet rebuilding unions requires more than recognizing a collective action problem. It requires changing labor law against intense business opposition.

The authors know this. Their discussion of power is one of the essay’s strengths. But the policy program sometimes sounds as if better economics can make a more equal economy appear through persuasion. In reality, better economics can clarify the stakes and puncture false objections; it cannot substitute for social movements, electoral coalitions, administrative capacity, and political confrontation.

The second temptation concerns the discipline itself. Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman acknowledge that economists have bad habits: excessive faith in first-best reasoning, disdain for other disciplines, overconfidence in markets, and narrowness in policy analysis. But the indictment should go deeper. For decades, many economists did not merely fail to prevent neoliberalism from appropriating their language; they helped legitimate it. When trade liberalization was sold to the public, economists emphasized aggregate gains and treated losses as side payments to be handled later. Later often never came. When financial deregulation advanced, too many economists trusted models that treated markets as self-correcting and risk as efficiently priced. When austerity policies were justified after crises, economists were frequently found on both sides, but the aura of economic expertise helped elevate deficit panic over employment and public investment.

This does not mean economics is inherently reactionary. It means the sociology of the profession matters more than the authors admit. What questions are rewarded? Which assumptions are considered elegant? Which journals define excellence? Which policy institutions hire economists? Which kinds of evidence count? A discipline can contain excellent tools and still have a professional culture that nudges its members toward deference to capital, suspicion of popular demands, and impatience with democratic messiness.

The authors’ own list of proposals is encouraging precisely because it challenges those tendencies. Wage boards would treat labor standards as a matter of institutional bargaining, not merely individual contracting. Stronger union rights would recognize that workers need countervailing power. Profit apportionment would confront tax competition rather than accommodate it. Social safeguards in trade agreements would subordinate certain forms of global integration to domestic social contracts. These are not minor tweaks; they imply a different hierarchy of values.

But that hierarchy should be made explicit. “Inclusive prosperity” should not mean “maximize GDP, then distribute better.” It should mean that the economy is judged by whether people have secure livelihoods, democratic voice, equal citizenship, ecological sustainability, and freedom from domination. Growth matters, but it is not the master value. A coal miner whose community has collapsed, a care worker earning poverty wages, a renter displaced by speculation, or a diabetic rationing insulin cannot be answered with an aggregate welfare theorem.

The next economics should therefore be institutional, empirical, and democratic. Institutional, because markets are made by law, norms, public investment, and social power. Empirical, because we have had enough ideology masquerading as necessity. Democratic, because economics cannot tell us how to weigh liberty, equality, security, dignity, and sustainability. Those are political judgments.

The lead essay opens a door. It tells non-economists that they need not reject economics to reject market fundamentalism. That is true and important. But economists should also hear a message from non-economists: trust will not be restored by saying “you misunderstood us.” It will be restored when economists show, repeatedly and publicly, that they are willing to challenge concentrated power, learn from other disciplines, take social movements seriously, and treat democratic values as more than constraints on efficient policy.

After neoliberalism, economics can help. But it must be an economics that knows its place: indispensable, not sovereign.

"While the lead essay’s call for a post-neoliberal economics is a welcome step, its reliance on mainstream economic tools is insufficient because it prioritizes technocratic inclusion over the fundamental redistribution of power and democratic control."

The call for “economics after neoliberalism” is welcome, but it is too gentle on economics and too optimistic about the capacity of mainstream tools to repair the damage. Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman want to distinguish modern economics from market fundamentalism. They argue that neoliberalism is a “primitive, simplistic perversion” of economics, not its logical expression. There is truth in this. Economics is not reducible to Milton Friedman, perfect competition, or the claim that markets always know best. But it is not enough to say that neoliberal policymakers misused economics. The deeper problem is that the mainstream discipline helped make neoliberalism plausible.

This happened not only through policy advice but through habits of thought. Mainstream economics begins with individuals, preferences, constraints, incentives, and equilibrium. These tools can be powerful. But they also tend to translate social conflict into technical trade-offs, institutions into transaction costs, and power into deviations from a competitive benchmark. Even when economists study market failure, the “failure” is often defined against an idealized market world that never existed and could never exist. The result is a discipline that can analyze inequality while still making capitalism itself appear natural.

The authors insist that economics is full of indeterminacy: “it depends.” That is both a strength and a weakness. If economics can generate many conclusions depending on assumptions, then economists cannot claim special authority over the broad direction of society. They can clarify mechanisms, estimate effects, and warn about unintended consequences. But the choice between a society organized around shareholder value and one organized around social need is not a technical matter. It is a political and moral matter.

The essay’s most promising moments come when it edges toward this recognition. The authors discuss property rights as constructed rather than given. They ask whether jobs belong to firms, workers, or some combination; whether government should retain ownership claims over publicly supported innovation; whether trade rules should be subordinated to social contracts. These are fundamental questions. But if taken seriously, they lead beyond “inclusive prosperity” toward economic democracy.

The phrase “inclusive prosperity” has a technocratic softness. It suggests that the existing economic order can be made more inclusive through better policy design: wage boards here, tax reform there, better social insurance, smarter financial regulation, a more reasonable trade regime. Many of these proposals are good. But inclusion into what? Prosperity for whom, controlled by whom, and on what terms?

A worker can be “included” in prosperity as a better-paid employee who still has no say over investment decisions, scheduling, automation, plant closures, or the distribution of surplus. A community can be “included” through adjustment assistance while still being dependent on mobile capital that may leave when subsidies expire. A citizen can receive tax credits and public benefits while corporations retain enormous authority over production, employment, technology, and politics. Inclusion is not the same as power.

The lead essay acknowledges power but does not fully reorganize economics around it. Power is not merely one variable among others; it is constitutive of markets. Employers’ power over workers is not an imperfection accidentally introduced into an otherwise neutral labor market. It arises from property relations: one side owns productive assets, the other must sell labor to live. Corporate power is not simply a matter of market concentration measurable through markups, though that matters. It is also legal personhood, control over investment, access to the state, intellectual property, and the capacity to shape desires and knowledge. Financial power is not only systemic risk; it is the ability of creditors to discipline households, firms, cities, and governments.

Mainstream economics can study pieces of this, but it often lacks a vocabulary for domination. A model of monopsony may show why wages are below marginal product. Useful. But the lived reality of work includes surveillance, arbitrary scheduling, sexual harassment, immigration status, racial hierarchy, and fear of retaliation. A model of tax competition may show why corporate rates fall. Useful. But the political story includes lobbying, revolving doors, campaign finance, and the ability of wealth to define what counts as “responsible” policy. A model of trade may show localized losses. Useful. But deindustrialization also destroys solidarities, local tax bases, and democratic confidence.

The authors are right that economics has become more empirical. Yet empiricism does not automatically free a discipline from ideology. The credibility revolution in economics has improved causal inference, but it can also narrow the imagination. Economists study what can be identified. Natural experiments, discontinuities, and administrative data are valuable, but the most important questions are not always the most easily identified. What is the long-run effect of union decline on democracy? What forms of ownership best support human flourishing? How does capitalist dependence on growth interact with planetary limits? What is the cumulative political effect of wealth concentration? These questions require history, sociology, political theory, and institutional imagination, not only clever identification strategies.

The policy proposals discussed in the lead essay are strongest when they move toward countervailing power: unions, wage boards, campaign finance reform, tougher corporate taxation. But they should be understood as steps toward a more democratic economy, not merely corrections to market failures. Wage boards are not just a response to monopsony; they are a way of taking wages partly out of unilateral employer control. Unions are not just devices to solve collective action problems; they are schools of democracy and institutions of working-class power. Corporate tax reform is not just an efficient way to raise revenue; it is a challenge to the idea that capital may opt out of social obligation. Trade safeguards are not just exceptions to free trade; they are assertions that democratic communities have the right to shape their economic lives.

A more ambitious post-neoliberal economics would put ownership and control at the center. It would ask how to expand worker cooperatives, public options, municipal enterprise, social wealth funds, codetermination, sectoral bargaining, public banking, and democratic planning for investment. It would revisit not only redistribution after markets generate outcomes, but predistribution: the rules that determine who has claims over income, assets, knowledge, and decision-making before tax and transfer. It would treat the firm not as a production function but as a political institution. It would treat the state not as an external fixer of market failures but as a creator of markets, a coordinator of investment, and a site of democratic struggle.

This does not require abandoning rigor. Quite the opposite. We need rigorous analysis of institutional alternatives. But rigor should not be confused with marginalism. A society facing climate change, mass inequality, and democratic erosion needs structural thinking. Carbon pricing may be useful, but decarbonization will also require public investment, industrial policy, transit, regulation, and planning. Minimum wages help, but rebuilding labor power requires law and organization. Taxing multinationals is necessary, but democratizing investment requires new ownership forms.

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman have written an important essay because they break with complacency. They show that economics need not be an apology for the powerful. But the question “after neoliberalism” should not be answered only by a better economics. It should be answered by a more democratic political economy—one that asks not just how to make markets work better, but which decisions should be made by markets at all, which by democratic institutions, and which by communities and workers themselves.

Economics can contribute to that project. But only if it gives up the desire to be the master discipline of social life. It must become one language among others in a broader struggle for democracy, equality, and ecological survival.

"While economics is an essential tool for reform, a post-neoliberal framework must ensure that economic expertise serves democratic deliberation rather than replacing neoliberal technocracy with a progressive version that still insulates policy from popular control."

The most encouraging feature of the lead essay is also the source of my unease: it places economists back at the center of reform. Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman are among the economists one would want in that position—empirically minded, institutionally aware, skeptical of market fundamentalism, and attentive to power. Yet after decades in which economic expertise helped narrow democratic imagination, we should be cautious about replacing neoliberal technocracy with progressive technocracy.

The authors’ diagnosis is persuasive. The public has come to associate economics with a set of anti-government, pro-market prescriptions. Economists did not always deserve that reputation, but they did plenty to earn it. Too many presented trade liberalization, deregulation, privatization, and fiscal restraint as matters of technical necessity rather than political choice. Too many treated distributional concerns as secondary, to be handled by hypothetical compensation that rarely arrived. Too many spoke in the language of constraints when the constraints themselves were institutionally created.

The authors respond by saying: economics is richer than that. It can justify wage boards, stronger unions, social insurance, tax justice, financial regulation, and democratic reforms. This is true. But it raises a question: if economics can justify so many different institutional arrangements, what democratic role should economists play?

The answer cannot be that economists should design the post-neoliberal order and persuade the public to accept it. That would reproduce a central defect of neoliberalism: the displacement of democratic judgment by expert authority. Neoliberalism was not only a set of policies. It was a style of governance that insulated major economic decisions from popular control. Central banks, trade tribunals, fiscal rules, independent regulators, and global agreements often constrained elected governments in the name of credibility and efficiency. Even when some insulation was justified, the cumulative effect was to tell citizens that the most important economic questions were not really theirs to decide.

A democratic economics after neoliberalism must reverse that presumption. Economists should be servants of democratic deliberation, not managers of society. Their job is to clarify trade-offs, identify mechanisms, test claims, expose hidden costs, and expand the range of feasible options. It is not to decide the values.

This distinction matters because “inclusive prosperity” contains unresolved moral questions. How much inequality is acceptable? What should be guaranteed as a right rather than purchased as a commodity? How should we balance consumption today against climate stability tomorrow? When should communities be allowed to restrict trade or development? How much workplace authority should employers have? Should health care be universal even if taxes rise? Should inheritance be heavily taxed? Should workers have board representation? Should carbon-intensive jobs be phased out even before replacement jobs exist? Economics can inform these debates. It cannot settle them.

The lead essay acknowledges that economics does not provide distributional weights. But the policy discussion sometimes proceeds as if better evidence will reveal win-win reforms and thereby avoid conflict. There are indeed reforms that can improve both efficiency and equality. But democratic politics cannot be built on free lunches alone. Sometimes the rich must lose so the poor can gain. Sometimes consumers may pay more so workers can earn more. Sometimes fossil fuel assets must be stranded to protect the climate. Sometimes corporate discretion must be reduced to increase worker voice. Pretending that all good policy is efficiency-enhancing risks evading the moral core of politics.

This is why the revival of power analysis in economics is so important. Yet power must be understood not only as an object of study but as a condition of policy-making. Who gets invited to the workshop? Who defines the problem? Whose data count? Which communities are treated as cases and which as authors? A policy process dominated by tenured economists, however progressive, will miss knowledge held by workers, tenants, patients, teachers, caregivers, and local officials.

Take labor markets. Economists can estimate the employment effects of minimum wages, model monopsony, and design wage boards. But workers know which scheduling practices make life impossible, which managers retaliate, how subcontracting hides responsibility, why grievance procedures fail, and what forms of voice are credible. A wage board designed without worker participation may be technically sophisticated and socially thin. The same is true in housing, health care, climate policy, and finance. Patients understand insurance denials and medical debt. Tenants understand eviction threats. Farmers understand land and risk. Communities near refineries understand pollution. Expertise must be plural.

This does not mean all knowledge is equal in every respect. Causal inference matters. Budgets matter. Incentives matter. Some policies fail despite good intentions. Economists are right to warn against magical thinking. But democratic knowledge is not the enemy of rigor. It is a source of better problem definition and institutional design.

A post-neoliberal economics should therefore be participatory in method. Before recommending a policy, economists should ask: who is affected, and how have they been consulted? What administrative agency will implement it? What forms of capture are likely? What grievance mechanisms exist? How will the policy be revised when it fails in predictable and unpredictable ways? What data will be collected, and who will own it? Can local variation be used for learning without turning vulnerable communities into experimental subjects without consent?

The authors praise empirical economics as a disciplining device against ideology. It can be. But evidence-based policy has its own politics. Randomized trials and natural experiments often privilege interventions that are small, measurable, and administratively convenient. Structural reforms—unionization, desegregation, decarbonization, democratization of firms—are harder to evaluate with clean designs. They may be undervalued in a culture that prizes identification over transformation. The risk is that progressivism becomes a portfolio of clever tweaks rather than a democratic reconstruction of institutions.

Teaching economics must change accordingly. Introductory courses still shape public understanding. If students leave Econ 101 believing that markets are natural, government is distortionary, and equity is separate from efficiency, later qualifications may never reach them. From the beginning, students should learn that markets require law, power matters, information is imperfect, externalities are pervasive, and distribution is built into institutional design. They should read economic history and competing schools of thought. They should learn humility as a professional norm.

The authors are right that economists have indispensable tools. But indispensability should not become dominance. The social sciences need a new division of labor. Economics brings models of incentives, strategic behavior, general equilibrium, and empirical evaluation. Sociology brings institutions, networks, stratification, and culture. Political science brings collective action, parties, state capacity, and representation. History brings contingency and memory. Law brings rights and institutional architecture. Philosophy brings justice. Public health brings embodiment and population well-being. The people most affected bring lived knowledge. No single discipline can govern the rest.

The challenge after neoliberalism is not only to produce better policies but to rebuild democratic trust. Trust has been damaged by elites who said that globalization would lift all boats, that financial innovation was safe, that austerity was responsible, and that workers should adapt. Progressive economists can help repair that trust, but only if they speak differently: less as priests of necessity, more as translators of possibility.

The lead essay is an important step. It opens economics to institutions, power, and moral purpose. The next step is to open economic policy-making itself to democracy. Inclusive prosperity should not mean prosperity designed for the people. It should mean prosperity designed with them.

"A post-neoliberal economics must prioritize the restoration of worker power and collective organization rather than just correcting market failures or relying on expert-led policy design."

The lead essay rightly announces the end of an era, but from the standpoint of workers it is still too polite. The problem with neoliberalism was not only that it misunderstood market failures. It was that it made working people weaker. Any economics after neoliberalism must begin there.

For decades, American workers were told a story. If productivity rose, wages would follow. If trade expanded, everyone would gain in the long run. If unions declined, labor markets would become more flexible and efficient. If corporations were freed from regulation and taxes, investment would increase. If finance innovated, capital would flow to its best uses. If workers lost jobs, they could retrain. If communities declined, people could move.

That story has collapsed. Productivity and pay have diverged. Trade shocks have left lasting scars. Retraining has been a meager answer to plant closures. Mobility has fallen, not risen. The decline of unions has not produced a paradise of opportunity but a workplace where many employees face unpredictable schedules, weak benefits, mandatory arbitration, noncompete clauses, fissured employment, and little voice. Corporate profits have been strong, but investment in workers has often been weak. Share buybacks and executive compensation have flourished. Working people have been asked to bear risk while capital claims flexibility.

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman understand much of this, and their emphasis on power is crucial. The word “power” was long treated as unscientific in economics, as if wages were set by impersonal forces rather than by bargaining under unequal conditions. But anyone who has tried to organize a union, ask for a raise, refuse an unsafe assignment, or challenge wage theft knows that labor markets are not neutral arenas. They are governed by fear, dependence, and institutional rules.

The decline of unions is the central fact. In the middle of the twentieth century, collective bargaining helped turn productivity growth into shared prosperity. Unions raised wages not only for their members but for nonunion workers whose employers had to match standards. They provided benefits, grievance procedures, apprenticeship systems, political education, and a counterweight to corporate power. They helped build the middle class, though unevenly and often with racial and gender exclusions that must not be romanticized. Their weakening was not an accident of technology. It was the result of employer resistance, hostile law, globalization, fissuring, and political choices.

Economists have too often treated unions as labor market distortions. The modern literature is more nuanced, recognizing monopsony, efficiency wages, bargaining, and inequality. But workers do not need to wait for economists to discover what they have lived. The employment contract is incomplete. The boss has discretion. A worker without collective power may formally be free but practically be dominated.

The lead essay’s discussion of wage boards is therefore welcome. Sectoral bargaining or wage boards could address a central weakness of the American labor system: firm-by-firm organizing is brutally difficult in an economy of franchises, subcontractors, gig work, and high turnover. If standards were set by industry and region, workers would not have to win a separate war at every workplace. Employers would compete on productivity and quality rather than on who can squeeze labor hardest. This is especially important in care work, food service, logistics, retail, hospitality, and other sectors where traditional union density is low but social need is high.

But wage boards cannot be a substitute for unions. Administrative wage-setting without worker organization risks becoming paternalism. Workers need institutions through which they can identify demands, monitor enforcement, protect one another, and exercise voice. A board that sets minimum standards is useful; a democratic labor movement that can contest managerial power is indispensable.

Labor law reform must therefore be more ambitious than small procedural fixes. Workers should have easier paths to recognition, real penalties for illegal retaliation, first-contract arbitration, rights for minority unions, and protections for immigrant workers who face deportation threats when they organize. Misclassification of employees as independent contractors should be aggressively policed. Noncompete agreements for ordinary workers should be banned. Mandatory arbitration clauses that prevent collective legal action should be curtailed. Public procurement should support high-road employers. The federal government should make labor standards a condition of public money.

The authors are also right to connect labor power to trade. For years, trade agreements protected investors, patents, and corporate rights with far more seriousness than they protected workers. Labor provisions were often weak, unenforced, or secondary. Workers were told that globalization was inevitable, while every line of the agreement reflected political choices. If capital can move and labor cannot, bargaining power shifts. If corporations can credibly threaten relocation, even workers who keep their jobs become more cautious. Trade policy must be judged not only by consumer prices but by its effects on bargaining power, regional stability, and democracy.

That said, we should be careful not to let trade become the whole explanation. Many low-wage workers are in non-traded sectors. The home health aide, janitor, warehouse worker, cashier, and child care worker are harmed less by imports than by weak labor standards, racism, sexism, immigration vulnerability, and the low value assigned to care and service work. A worker-centered economics must not focus only on the white male manufacturing worker, however politically salient. Inclusive prosperity must include Black women in hospitals, Latina hotel housekeepers, immigrant farmworkers, adjunct instructors, domestic workers, and gig drivers.

This is where economics needs to learn from labor organizers. The economy is not just a set of markets; it is a set of relationships. Workers want higher wages, but also dignity, predictable schedules, safety, respect, benefits, and a say. These are not easily reduced to income. A worker forced to choose between a child’s emergency and a shift has suffered an economic harm even if a wage equation misses it. A worker subject to harassment or arbitrary discipline is not flourishing simply because employment is technically efficient.

The question of technology should also be framed around worker power. Automation is not a natural disaster. Employers choose technologies in response to wages, labor law, tax incentives, managerial strategies, and worker resistance. A machine that deskills work and intensifies surveillance is not the same as a tool that augments skill and reduces drudgery. Workers should have a voice in technological adoption. If public policy subsidizes innovation, it should demand shared gains: training, shorter hours, worker ownership stakes, or sectoral funds financed by productivity improvements.

Finally, political democracy depends on workplace democracy. People who spend most of their waking hours under authoritarian control are less likely to experience themselves as equal citizens. The decline of unions has not only reduced wages; it has weakened the organizational basis for working-class politics. Moneyed interests fill the vacuum. Campaign finance reform and voting rights matter, but so does rebuilding mass membership organizations that can connect everyday economic life to democratic participation.

The lead essay gives economists a path away from neoliberalism. Workers should welcome that. But the test is whether economists are willing to side with labor not only when the evidence shows a free lunch, but when there is a real conflict over surplus and control. Inclusive prosperity cannot be granted from above by enlightened experts. It must be won by organized people.

Economics after neoliberalism should begin with a simple proposition: no economy is healthy when the people who do its work lack power over its rewards and rules.

Or consider the treatment of corporate power. Zucman's proposal on apportionment is excellent and I support it. These options are not on the EfIP menu. The menu stays within the bounds of what is sayable in a faculty seminar at Harvard.

The framework presupposes the legitimacy of the underlying market and treats deviation from competitive equilibrium as a tractable engineering problem. This is fine if you think most existing markets are basically legitimate and competently designed. It is inadequate if you think the basic shape of contemporary capitalism is the problem—if you think, for example, that the conversion of housing from shelter into a financial asset is a categorical mistake, or that the patent system is a giant rent extraction machine that we would never adopt if we were designing the institutions of innovation from scratch.

The authors actually have one excellent example of the more ambitious mode: Korinek's observation that the direction of technological change is itself a policy choice, and that we routinely intervene to shape it. This kind of thinking points beyond market-failure economics toward what we might call constitutive policy—policies that do not just correct outcomes within given institutions but design the institutions themselves. The same logic could be applied much more broadly. Who owns data? Who owns biotech patents developed with public money? Who owns the upstream platforms that mediate most of contemporary commerce? These are not market failure questions. They are ownership questions, and the existing distribution of ownership is the result of political choices that can be remade.

The rise of right-wing authoritarianism in the United States and across Europe is not a problem that can be solved by better tax design and better bank regulation. Recovering legitimacy will require a politics that visibly transforms the relations of economic power, not one that tweaks them. EfIP supplies the tweaks. Someone else will need to supply the vision.

In Defense of What They Call Neoliberalism

I want to enter a dissent. The authors of this essay take it as common ground that the policy regime of the last forty years has been a failure, that "neoliberalism" is responsible for the ills of contemporary America, and that the task of economists is to chart an alternative. I think each of these claims is wrong, or at least much more contestable than the authors allow.

Start with the diagnosis. The authors lead with U.S. inequality, wage stagnation, declining life expectancy, and so on. These are real concerns. But the framing of these as the fruits of neoliberalism is a parochial American story dressed up as a global verdict. The period the authors lament has also been the period in which global poverty fell more rapidly than at any time in human history. Roughly a billion people have escaped extreme poverty since 1990. Child mortality has halved. Literacy has soared. Life expectancy worldwide has risen by nearly a decade. If "neoliberalism" means trade liberalization, integration of poor countries into global capital and goods markets, and the spread of property rights and the rule of law, then neoliberalism is the most successful poverty-reduction program in history. To indict it for the relatively modest distributional troubles of the rich world is to weigh a stubbed toe against a cured cancer.

Within the United States, the picture is also more complex than the essay admits. Median household income, properly measured to include in-kind transfers and adjusted for household size, has risen substantially since the 1970s. Consumption inequality has grown much less than income inequality. The share of Americans in absolute poverty as conventionally measured has fallen. Life expectancy declines in the last few years are concerning, but they are driven heavily by opioids and suicide—not phenomena well explained by "market fundamentalism," and not phenomena obviously remediable by better minimum wage policy or financial regulation. The story that everything has gotten worse for ordinary Americans for half a century is rhetorically powerful and substantively misleading.

Now the policy prescriptions. The proposals collected under the EfIP banner are a grab bag of varying quality. Some are sensible (Zucman on multinational taxation, Admati on bank capital). Some are dubious (the suggestion that minimum wage hikes do not reduce employment is true for modest increases at low wage levels and false at higher levels, as the authors surely know but do not say). Some are dangerous (Rodrik on protectionism justified by "social safeguards" is essentially a license for any rich country to block imports whenever a domestic constituency objects, which is to say, always; the casualties of that regime would be poor workers in poor countries, not the constituencies Rodrik wishes to protect).

The deeper problem is the authors' theory of how policy goes wrong. They suggest that neoliberalism succeeded because of intellectual capture: economists became cheerleaders for markets and policymakers listened. This is not a serious history. Neoliberal reforms in the 1980s and 1990s happened because the alternatives had visibly failed. The British state nationalized substantial portions of its economy in the postwar period and the result was relative decline so severe that the United Kingdom went to the IMF in 1976. The Soviet economic model collapsed because it could not produce goods anyone wanted. Latin American import-substitution industrialization produced hyperinflation and debt crises. The "neoliberal" reforms that followed were not imposed by Hayekian ideologues; they were attempts, often grudging, to fix systems that had broken down on their own terms.

The success of the reforms is also clearer than the essay suggests. India's liberalization in 1991 lifted hundreds of millions out of poverty. China's market reforms—which the authors curiously cite as evidence for institutional pluralism—are textbook examples of marketization, including the introduction of private property, profit incentives, foreign investment, and integration into global trade. Eastern European countries that liberalized rapidly outperformed those that did so slowly. The Nordics, often invoked as alternatives to neoliberalism, have actually liberalized substantially since the 1980s while maintaining strong welfare states; they are not models of resistance to markets but of competent markets paired with redistribution.

I am also skeptical of the authors' confidence that they have identified "free lunches." The history of economic policy is largely a history of free lunches that turned out to have prices. Rent control was a free lunch until cities found themselves with chronic housing shortages. Bank regulation was a free lunch until depositors were trapped in failing institutions. The Community Reinvestment Act was a free lunch until subprime lending unwound. Even "no-brainer" interventions usually have second-order effects that economists, including these economists, will discover after the fact and write papers about. Humility, which the authors recommend at one point in the essay, is in short supply when they describe their own proposals.

There is one point at which I want to agree wholeheartedly with the authors. They are right that economics, properly practiced, is not a set of fixed policy conclusions but a framework for analyzing trade-offs. Where I part company is in their suggestion that the framework, when properly applied, more or less supports their politics. It does not. It supports their politics in some cases, opposes it in others, and is genuinely indeterminate in many. The honest version of "Economics After Neoliberalism" would be a much more divided essay, with full-throated defenses of trade liberalization sitting next to wage board proposals, and with serious attention to the costs as well as the benefits of every intervention. What the authors have produced is closer to a manifesto: a presentation of one political vision as the natural conclusion of contemporary economic science. We have seen this move before, from the other side. It was not honest then and it is not honest now.

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman have written something both useful and revealing. Useful, because their Economics for Inclusive Prosperity briefs concretize an emerging center-left policy program at a moment when the U.S. Democratic Party is being pulled, productively, leftward — Elizabeth Warren's wealth tax proposal (built on Zucman's own work with Saez), the Sanders Medicare-for-All bill, Ocasio-Cortez's just-introduced Green New Deal resolution. The defense runs roughly like this: neoliberalism is not economics but its "primitive, simplistic perversion"; the discipline today is empirical, pluralistic, attentive to market failures, and equipped with tools for designing better institutions. The textbook caricature — competitive equilibrium, "markets always work" — is the property of Econ 101 and conservative think tanks, not of the working researcher. Therefore, do not throw the baby out with the bathwater: economists have ideas, and you need them.

Much of this is right. Anyone who has watched the credibility revolution in applied microeconomics, the post-2008 rethinking of macroprudential regulation, the rise of behavioral and historical work, the granular Chetty-style mobility research, or the Card-Krueger-to-Dube reassessment of minimum wages, knows that the discipline has moved. The Diaz-Alejandro line the authors quote is more true than ever: economic theory is now a near-infinite menu, and the empirical literature can sustain almost any ideologically inflected reading. That is precisely why "what does economics say?" is increasingly a question about which economists you ask, and about which findings get picked up by which institutions — that is, a question about politics and sociology, not theory.

And here is where the lead essay is too easy on itself. The claim that neoliberalism is a "perversion" of economics rather than its mainstream expression treats the relationship between disciplinary knowledge and political ideology as one of misunderstanding. But the historical record — readable in Burgin, Mirowski, Slobodian, and Stedman Jones — is that the Mont Pelerin project knew exactly what it was doing, and that its consolidation in U.S. policy circles from the 1970s onward was not a misreading of "true" economics so much as a successful colonization of the discipline's prestige. When the IMF imposed structural adjustment, when Larry Summers signed off on derivatives deregulation, when graduate trade economists asserted that NAFTA's losers could in principle be compensated and then shrugged when they weren't — these were not low-status cranks or Heritage Foundation hacks. They were card-carrying members of the profession in good standing, deploying its tools and authority. The discipline's mainstream cannot simultaneously claim credit for the empirical sophistication of 2019 and disown the policy authority it exercised in 1995.

This is not a gotcha. It is a question about the present. If neoliberalism's grip on policy was made possible in part by features of how economics is done — methodological individualism, a strong prior toward Pareto-style analysis, the habit of holding politics fixed while optimizing within it, the reflex to "take the side of markets because no one else will" — then the corrective is not just better proposals. It is institutional and pedagogical change inside the profession. The authors acknowledge "bad habits" but treat them as foibles around the edges of a fundamentally healthy science. I am less sure. The CORE curriculum project, heterodox programs, and feminist and stratification economists have for years been making more thoroughgoing critiques of how the discipline trains its young, what it counts as rigorous, and which questions get top-five publications. Their absence from this lineup is conspicuous — all the authors of the briefs are, by their own account, "tenured academic economists, working in broadly mainstream subfields." That is a fine bench, but it is not the whole problem.

A related worry concerns the rhetorical center of gravity in the proposals: the "free lunch." The authors emphasize that we are operating well inside the justice-efficiency frontier, so we can have more fairness without sacrificing growth — perhaps even gain on both. This is empirically defensible in many of the cases they cite (monopsony in labor markets, financial-stability gains from higher capital requirements, the deadweight loss of tax competition). And it is rhetorically attractive precisely because it sidesteps the harder distributional fight. But the technocratic move of dissolving political conflict into Pareto improvements is itself a neoliberal habit. Some of what an inclusive economy requires — redistributing wealth, breaking up incumbent firms, reallocating property rights — will not be a free lunch for the losers, who are often powerful and well-organized. The political-economy challenge is then not "how do we identify the free lunches?" but "how do we win the fights that aren't free lunches?" Naidu's union piece and Kaplan's political-economy proposal point in this direction; the framing of the introduction sometimes pulls the other way.

Two more lacunae are worth naming. First, on macroeconomics: the briefs treat capital markets and inequality and financial regulation, which is welcome, but the deeper post-2008 macro debate — about secular stagnation, the appropriate size and composition of public balance sheets, the relationship between fiscal policy and full employment, the conditions under which deficits matter — is barely visible. With unemployment under 4 percent and inflation still subdued, with MMT-flavored arguments forcing their way into mainstream commentary via Stephanie Kelton and the Green New Deal, and with Olivier Blanchard's recent AEA address pushing back on debt panic, the EfIP project's relative silence on aggregate demand and the public sector's macro role is a missed opportunity. "Inclusive prosperity" is much easier to deliver in a hot labor market.

Second, on climate and race. The authors gesture at climate (carbon pricing) and mention persistent racial disparities in the opening, but neither becomes structural to the program. Carbon pricing, as economists have long recommended, is necessary and politically inert; what is now needed is industrial policy, public investment, and distributional design — the substance of the Green New Deal conversation. And on race, the U.S. wealth gap, mass incarceration, and labor market discrimination are not addressable by minimum-wage policy and wage boards alone. What "Neoliberalism" Means, and Why It Matters

The authors use the term "neoliberalism" with a kind of weary frequency, occasionally accompanied by parenthetical synonyms—market fundamentalism, market fetishism, market worship. The term does considerable work in their essay: it names the enemy, identifies the past forty years as a coherent period, and locates the source of contemporary discontent. But the term is doing rather more work than its history can bear, and the slippage matters for the argument.

A brief reminder of the history. "Neoliberalism" was coined in the 1930s by a group of liberals who met at the Colloque Walter Lippmann in Paris in 1938, including Hayek, Mises, Lippmann himself, and others. Their concern was that classical liberalism had failed to defend itself against socialism and fascism, and that a renewed liberalism—a "neo" liberalism—needed to embrace an active state that would construct and maintain the legal and institutional conditions of competitive markets. The neoliberals were not advocates of laissez-faire. They were advocates of a particular kind of state, one strong enough to defend the price system from democratic interference.

This intellectual lineage matters because the actual neoliberal tradition, as documented by historians like Quinn Slobodian and Philip Mirowski, was not principally about deregulation or shrinking the state. It was about insulating market mechanisms from democratic politics by embedding them in transnational legal structures—the EU's internal market rules, the WTO's trade disciplines, central bank independence, investor-state dispute settlement, constitutional debt brakes. The neoliberal project was constitutional, not just policy-level. Its great victory was not in any particular tax cut but in constructing an architecture in which whole domains of economic policy were placed beyond the reach of ordinary politics.

Why does this matter for Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman? Because their critique of "neoliberalism" is in part a critique of laissez-faire economics, and laissez-faire was not what neoliberalism principally delivered. The state did not shrink. Public spending as a share of GDP in OECD countries has been remarkably stable or rising. What changed was the character of state action: it became more friendly to capital, more disciplinary toward labor, more committed to the protection of property and contract, more willing to use coercion in some domains (criminal justice, border control) and more reluctant in others (industrial policy, labor regulation). The neoliberal state is a strong state, just one organized around different priorities.

This means that "more state" is not, by itself, an alternative to neoliberalism. The contemporary right understands this perfectly well: Viktor Orban and Donald Trump are perfectly happy to use state power, including against markets, in pursuit of ethnonationalist projects. The state has been wielded against migrants, against political opponents, against the press. None of this contradicts neoliberalism in the historically specific sense. It accompanies it.

What would actually contradict neoliberalism would be the re-democratization of economic decision-making: the return of disputes about wages, prices, investment, and credit to ordinary political contestation rather than to courts, central banks, and international institutions. This is a much more demanding project than the EfIP proposals suggest. It would require not just better technical policy but the dismantling of significant portions of the constitutional architecture that the neoliberal project built. It would require, for example, rethinking central bank independence, rethinking trade dispute settlement, rethinking the protection of capital mobility, rethinking the legal status of corporate personhood and free speech.

The EfIP project, to its credit, gestures at some of this. Rodrik's social safeguards in trade are a step toward giving democratic publics a claim against transnational disciplines. Zucman's formulary apportionment is a step toward asserting public taxing authority against transnational capital mobility. These are real interventions. But the framing of the essay—as a contest between "neoliberal" policies and better economic ideas—obscures the deeper struggle, which is about who decides, not just what is decided.

I want to add one more historical observation. The authors note that conservatives have monopolized the banner of economics in policy circles. This is true but incomplete. The actual story of the last forty years is that center-left parties—the Democrats, New Labour, the SPD—largely accepted the neoliberal consensus on economic management while differentiating themselves on social issues. Clinton, Blair, Schröder were enthusiastic privatizers and globalizers. The economists who advised them—Summers, Rubin, Mandelson—were not conservative. They were mainstream. They were drawing on the same discipline the authors of this essay come from. The neoliberal era was a bipartisan achievement, and the bipartisanship was held together by a set of beliefs about what economic science permitted.

The authors are right that the science permits more than this. But the asymmetry between what the science permits and what the policymakers believed was sociologically constructed and politically enforced. Changing it will require not just better ideas but a different relationship between economic expertise and democratic publics—one in which economists are visibly accountable to constituencies other than their professional peers and elite policymakers.

I am hopeful about the EfIP project as a contribution to that change. The point is not to replace bad expert rule with good expert rule. The point is to subordinate expertise to politics, while keeping it competent. That is harder than the essay suggests.

The Limits of the Credibility Revolution

The EfIP manifesto leans heavily on a specific story about contemporary economics: that the discipline has become much more empirical since the 1990s, that systematic evidence has displaced ideological priors, and that the new evidence supports a substantially more interventionist policy stance than the old conventional wisdom. I want to push on each of these claims as someone who works inside this empirical tradition.

The empirical turn is real. The share of empirical papers in top journals has risen substantially, and the methodological standards for credible causal identification have tightened. Where once a regression with a few control variables passed as analysis, the modern paper requires natural experiments, instrumental variables, regression discontinuity designs, or randomized controlled trials. This is progress. Many findings that survived for decades in the textbooks—negative employment effects of moderate minimum wages, large growth effects of trade liberalization, neutral distributional effects of capital account opening—have not held up under more rigorous scrutiny. The authors are right to celebrate this.

But the celebration should be more guarded than the essay allows. The credibility revolution has its own pathologies, and they matter for the political project the authors describe.

First, the credibility revolution biases the discipline toward questions that admit of clean identification, often at the expense of larger and more important questions. We have a great deal of careful evidence about the effects of small changes in policy parameters at the margin of existing institutional arrangements. We have much less evidence about the effects of large institutional changes, because such changes do not produce the kind of variation that credibility-focused designs require. The minimum wage literature is illustrative: we know a great deal about the local effects of $1-$2 minimum wage hikes from neighboring-county designs. We know much less about what a $15 federal minimum wage would do, because such a change is outside the support of the available data. The EfIP project includes proposals (wage boards, contingent debt contracts) that are similarly outside the support of clean U.S. evidence. We can extrapolate, we can use Australian or German comparisons, but the confidence the empirical literature affords for marginal interventions does not carry over to structural ones.

Second, the credibility revolution has a partial-equilibrium bias. The cleanest identification strategies isolate local effects, often by exploiting the fact that one place got a policy and a similar place didn't. But many of the most important effects of policy are general-equilibrium effects that wash out in local comparisons. A minimum wage that raises wages in restaurants might lower wages in agriculture as displaced workers flow elsewhere; a local design won't catch this. A trade liberalization that decimates manufacturing communities might still raise national income on net through gains in other sectors; a place-based design will document the losses but not the offsetting gains. The empirical literature is good at the parts of policy questions that admit of local variation and worse at the parts that don't. The authors lean on the empirical literature without acknowledging that the parts of their proposals that are well-supported empirically are often not the parts that matter most.

Third, the credibility revolution has done less than it claims to discipline ideological priors. The same methods can be applied with different framings, different samples, and different control variables. Studies of the same question often reach divergent conclusions. The minimum wage literature is again illustrative: there is now a large body of credible studies, and they continue to disagree. The Card-Krueger tradition finds small or zero employment effects. The Neumark-Wascher tradition finds meaningful negative effects. Both use modern methods. The disagreement is not resolved by better identification; it is reproduced inside better identification. The authors' confidence that the empirical evidence has moved the field is, in part, a selection effect: they cite the findings consistent with their politics and elide the rest.

Fourth, the credibility revolution has been less successful at the macroeconomic questions that matter most for the EfIP project than at microeconomic questions. We do not have good causal estimates of the effects of capital account liberalization, or of corporate tax cuts, or of public investment, because the relevant variation is at the country-year level and there are not many countries. The authors' confidence that "financial liberalization produces crises rather than faster economic growth" rests on findings that, while suggestive, do not have the identification credentials of the minimum wage or trade literatures. This is not a fatal problem—we do the best we can with the data we have—but it is a reason for more caution than the essay exhibits.

I want to be clear: I support the EfIP project. I think the proposals are mostly good. I think the empirical literature, properly handled, leans in the direction of more interventionist policy than the conventional wisdom of the 1990s. But the rhetorical move the authors make—from "economics has become more empirical" to "economics now supports our policy program"—is faster than the evidence warrants. The empirical literature is a tool, not a verdict. It supports some of the EfIP proposals more than others, and it leaves many of the most important questions open.

The honest version of the empirical case for inclusive prosperity would acknowledge the gaps. It would distinguish between proposals that are well-supported by clean evidence (Dube on local minimum wage variation, perhaps), proposals that are supported by reasonable extrapolation (wage boards informed by Australian evidence), and proposals that rest mostly on theory and analogy (Korinek on directing technological change). All of these can be defended, but the defenses look different. Treating the whole portfolio as if it is uniformly underwritten by the credibility revolution does the project a disservice and gives critics an easy target.

If economics is to play the role the authors hope for it, it needs to be honest about what it knows and doesn't. The credibility revolution is a powerful tool but it does not magically deliver progressive policy conclusions. It delivers, mostly, more cautious and more local conclusions than the old confident generalities. Sometimes those are progressive, sometimes they are not, and sometimes they leave the big questions unresolved. EfIP should embrace this uncertainty rather than paper over it.

I want to focus on one revealing phrase in the EfIP manifesto: "free lunch." The authors write that the proposals reflect "the sense that economies are operating well inside the justice-efficiency frontier, and that there are numerous policy 'free lunches' that could push us towards an economy that is morally better without sacrificing (and, indeed, possibly enhancing) prosperity."

This phrasing is meant to disarm critics. The standard objection to egalitarian policy has been that it imposes efficiency costs, and the authors want to push back: no, equality and efficiency can be improved together. The argument is empirically often correct. The U.S. economy is full of inefficiencies that fall disproportionately on the poor and that could be corrected to everyone's benefit. Monopsony in labor markets, predatory lending, the structure of healthcare, the geography of public goods—all of these are sites where redistributive interventions would likely raise aggregate welfare. The authors are right about this.

But the framing has a cost. "Free lunch" frames the project of progressive policy as one of seeking out interventions that are Pareto improvements or close to it. The standard for action is whether we can improve outcomes without anyone losing. This is a stringent standard. It privileges interventions that face minimal opposition and rules out those that require redistribution from concentrated winners to dispersed losers. It implicitly accepts existing distributions of property and power as the baseline against which interventions are to be judged.

The deeper progressive case for economic transformation has never rested on free lunches alone. It has rested on a substantive view of what we owe each other, of what a decent society looks like, of what is unacceptable about the current distribution regardless of whether changing it imposes efficiency costs. The civil rights movement did not justify desegregation primarily on efficiency grounds. The labor movement did not justify the eight-hour day primarily on efficiency grounds. Universal suffrage was not enacted because it was efficient. The case in each instance was a moral case about what people are owed.

When economists frame progressive policy as the discovery of free lunches, they implicitly accept that the burden of proof is on those who would change the status quo to show that the change is costless. This burden is unfair and self-defeating. It is unfair because the status quo is itself the result of countless interventions—property law, contract law, corporate law, intellectual property regimes, financial regulation—whose distributional effects are not subject to the same scrutiny. It is self-defeating because many of the most important progressive interventions will not pass the free-lunch test; they will require somebody to lose, and the case for the policy will rest on the moral importance of what is gained.

Take the case of a wealth tax of the kind proposed by some of the authors' colleagues. The case for such a tax is principally that extreme concentrations of wealth are corrosive of democracy, of equal opportunity, and of social solidarity, and that taxing them is therefore desirable even if it has some efficiency cost. The "efficiency cost" framing—does the tax raise revenue net of behavioral responses, does it distort savings, does it slow growth—is part of the analysis but it is not the heart of the matter. The heart of the matter is the moral and political claim that the current distribution of wealth is incompatible with the kind of society we want to live in.

The EfIP framing makes this kind of argument harder to articulate. By presenting progressive policy as a series of technical corrections to market failures, it strips out the moral content that gives the project its energy. It also limits the range of available policy. Many of the proposals in the project are good; they would not be sufficient even if all adopted, and the next steps will require arguments the free-lunch frame cannot support.

I want to note one place where the authors do break from the free-lunch frame: Rodrik's proposal on trade restrictions justified by "social safeguards." Rodrik explicitly refuses to clothe protectionism in efficiency language. He argues that trade restrictions can be justified by the requirements of the domestic social contract, full stop. This is the right kind of argument. It treats values—the value of social solidarity, of democratic self-government, of egalitarian relationships among citizens—as ends to which efficiency must be subordinated when necessary. The authors note this with approval and connect it to Polanyi.

The case for unions is not just that they correct monopsony; it is that workplaces are sites of human dignity and that workers deserve voice in their conditions. The case for public goods is not just that markets undersupply them; it is that some goods—education, health, security—should be available to all as a matter of citizenship rather than purchasing power. The case for progressive taxation is not just that the marginal utility of money diminishes; it is that great concentrations of wealth distort the political community we share.

Economics does not have to answer these value questions. But it should leave room for them rather than absorbing them into the technical apparatus of welfare maximization. But this defense ignores the immense normative power of the baseline.

In economics, the baseline remains the perfectly competitive market equilibrium. As the authors admit, this is the dominant framework in introductory courses. Even when economists advance to more sophisticated models that account for "frictions," they are still beginning from a paradigm where the unfettered market is the natural, "first-best" state of the world, and government intervention must bear the burden of proof to justify correcting a specific "market failure." When society is viewed through a lens where markets are natural and interventions are artificial, it is unsurprising that generations of students and policymakers have absorbed a default hostility toward the regulatory state.

This is why, despite the brilliance of the EfIP policy briefs, the continued reliance on the language of "market failures" to explain our current dysfunction feels insufficient. The crises we face today are not merely the result of asymmetric information, externalities, or imperfect competition. They are the result of deliberate distributions of power and wealth.

To their immense credit, the authors recognize this. They state explicitly that "power matters enormously in shaping market outcomes," and they highlight power asymmetries between labor and capital, between multinational corporations and sovereign states, and between the wealthy and the disenfranchised. The proposals they cite reflect a genuine desire to rebalance these scales. Arindrajit Dube’s proposal for sectoral wage boards directly tackles the systemic monopsony power of employers. Gabriel Zucman’s ingenious plan for apportioning the global profits of multinationals based on sales offers a tangible mechanism to break the blackmail of international tax competition. Dani Rodrik’s call for "social safeguards" in trade agreements elegantly subordinates textbook efficiency to the preservation of the domestic social contract.

Yet, a tension remains between the profound political shifts these policies require and the technocratic language often used to describe them. Consider the essay’s discussion of organized labor. The authors suggest that mechanism design, behavioral economics, and experiments can be mobilized to "ease the pervasive collective action problem facing unions." While this is undoubtedly true in a technical sense, the decline of the U.S. labor movement was not primarily a failure of mechanism design. It was the result of a coordinated, decades-long political and legal assault by corporate interests. Reviving labor power will require fierce political struggle, mass organizing, and legislative warfare. Economics can offer blueprints for what an optimal union structure might look like, but it struggles to conceptualize the raw, unquantifiable social friction required to build it.

This highlights the ultimate limitation of relying on economics to save us from neoliberalism: economics is fundamentally a science of optimization, but politics is a clash of values and power. The EfIP network’s proposals demonstrate that economics can design fairer, more inclusive institutions. But as the authors note, policies often "break on the rock of government capture by special interests." Ethan Kaplan’s work on democratic political economy is an excellent inclusion to address this, but it underscores a daunting reality. If our political system is already captured by the winners of the neoliberal era, then the most beautifully designed, empirically sound policy proposals will remain locked in academic journals.

Writing in 2019, as we stare down the barrel of an escalating climate crisis and a deeply fractured democracy, the necessity of a new economic paradigm cannot be overstated. The Economics for Inclusive Prosperity project proves that the discipline contains the analytical firepower to help chart a new course. The empirical turn in economics has successfully dismantled the theoretical idols of market fundamentalism, proving that minimum wages do not inherently destroy jobs and that financial liberalization does not automatically generate growth.

However, moving beyond neoliberalism will require the profession to do more than produce better empirical studies and more creative models of market failure. It will require economists to step outside the safety of methodological individualism and engage deeply with history, sociology, and political science—not just as sources of variable data, but as equal partners in understanding human society. It will require abandoning the pretense of the detached technocrat and acknowledging that economic policy is always, inevitably, an exercise in moral philosophy and political power. The authors of this lead essay have bravely taken the first step. The challenge now is for the rest of the discipline to follow them out of the seminar room and into the messy, contested reality of democratic life.
