"The climate movement must adopt a strategic hierarchy that prioritizes the democratic seizure of state power to abolish fossil capital, as the state is the only institution capable of coordinating a transition at the necessary scale and speed."

Olúfẹ́mi Táíwò is right to insist that the climate movement needs a strategic hierarchy. Not a moral hierarchy: no serious emancipatory politics should tell people facing police violence, occupation, hunger, misogyny, caste domination, or anti-migrant terror that their struggles are secondary in worth. But politics is made not only of values; it is made of sequencing, leverage, timing, and institutional terrain. The climate crisis is not simply another issue on a long progressive list. It is becoming the operating system within which all other struggles will be fought. If fossil capital remains politically dominant for another generation, the space for humane politics will shrink everywhere.

The strongest part of Táíwò’s essay is his refusal of two comforting myths. The first is the myth that the state is either simply the problem or simply the solution. The second is the myth that technological improvement by itself will retire coal, oil, and gas. Both myths have a partial truth. States have been engines of colonial extraction, border violence, police repression, and ecological destruction. And technological progress in renewables has been extraordinary: solar and wind are now, in many contexts, the cheapest sources of new electricity; batteries have improved quickly; electric vehicles are moving from novelty to mass market. But neither fact tells us how power will be reorganized. Cheap clean energy does not automatically dislodge entrenched dirty energy. And suspicion of the state does not answer the question of who will plan, finance, permit, build, and democratically govern the new energy system.

The phrase “dethrone fossil capital” is useful because it names the conflict that polite climate discourse often evades. Fossil fuel companies are not just suppliers responding to consumer demand. They are political institutions. They fund parties, shape trade associations, litigate against regulation, bankroll misinformation, influence universities, cultivate unions and local governments through jobs and tax revenue, and press states to treat continued extraction as synonymous with national prosperity. In the United States, fossil interests have spent decades weakening environmental regulation and blocking climate legislation. In Europe, the energy crisis after Russia’s invasion of Ukraine gave fossil companies a renewed argument for gas infrastructure. In many poorer countries, oil and gas are presented as the last available path to development, debt repayment, and fiscal stability. This is not a market gently awaiting substitution. It is an organized bloc defending a material order.

Táíwò’s two-step argument captures something many climate advocates have learned through defeat. One cannot simply propose the beautiful end state: abundant public transit, retrofitted housing, clean public power, shorter workweeks, regenerative agriculture, and democratic planning. One must first weaken the institutions that make that future structurally implausible. Fossil capital has veto points everywhere. It sits in the tax base of regions, in pension portfolios, in state-owned enterprises, in pipelines and terminals with multidecade lifespans, in campaign finance networks, and in the energy security doctrines of great powers. If that power is not confronted directly, “green transition” becomes additive rather than substitutive: more renewables, yes, but also more oil and gas; more electric cars, but also more highways; more corporate sustainability reports, but also more liquefied natural gas export terminals.

The strategic implication is that public power should be central to climate politics. This does not mean romanticizing every existing public utility or assuming that state ownership is inherently democratic. Publicly owned fossil companies can be as destructive as private ones. Norway’s oil wealth, Saudi Aramco, and many national oil companies show that “public” can mean the public absorption of risk and the elite capture of reward. But public power matters because the energy transition is a coordination problem at civilization scale. It requires building transmission, storage, generation, electrified transport, and efficient buildings in an order and at a speed that private profit signals will not reliably deliver.

The examples Táíwò cites—municipal solar funding teacher raises, Los Angeles’s renewable target, New York’s Build Public Renewables Act—are not marginal anecdotes. They are glimpses of a different political economy. The usual climate bargain offered to working people is sacrifice now for atmospheric benefits later. Public power can reverse that bargain: cheaper bills, union jobs, cleaner air, revenue for schools, community control, and resilience during disasters. It can make decarbonization visible as an improvement in daily life. That is essential because fossil capital’s deepest political strength is not merely money; it is its ability to pose as the defender of ordinary security. “We keep the lights on.” “We provide jobs.” “We protect you from volatile prices.” “We stand between your family and decline.” A democratic clean energy state must defeat that claim in practice, not just in rhetoric.

This is where labor politics becomes indispensable. A climate movement that treats workers in fossil-dependent regions as obstacles will lose, and deserve to lose. The point is not to flatter every union position or give veto power to incumbents. Some unions have aligned themselves with pipelines and drilling in ways that are shortsighted and damaging. But workers’ fear is rational. Mines close, refineries automate, plants shut down, and communities are abandoned. The United States has a long record of promising “transition” and delivering ruin. If climate policy is to dethrone fossil capital, it must also dethrone the corporate power that holds workers hostage. That means wage guarantees, pension protection, public employment, union standards, community reinvestment, and worker-led planning. A “just transition” cannot be a grant program attached to an otherwise neoliberal energy policy. It must be one of the central mechanisms by which fossil capital is politically isolated.

Still, I would sharpen Táíwò’s argument in one respect. The first step cannot be “energy transition” in a narrow sense; it must be fossil abolition through democratic state capacity. Those are not identical. A country can add vast amounts of renewable energy while leaving fossil production intact for export. It can electrify cars while permitting petrochemical expansion. It can subsidize clean technology while allowing utilities to retain gas assets and pass the cost of stranded infrastructure to ratepayers. The climate movement should therefore judge policy not only by green additions but by fossil retirements: no new extraction leases, no new long-lived fossil infrastructure, managed decline of production, accelerated coal retirement, methane regulation, public buyouts where necessary, and legal accountability for deception and damage.

This is why the state cannot be bypassed. Permits are state instruments. Mineral rights are state-defined. Utility regulation is state-based. Public debt, trade rules, central bank policy, industrial standards, and disaster planning are state-mediated. Even the decision to let private companies dominate is a political decision enforced by law. The state creates markets; it does not merely supervise them. A movement that refuses to contest state power leaves those tools to fossil capital and its allies.

But there is also a danger in speaking as if “the state” were a single lever to be seized. States are internally divided: courts, legislatures, bureaucracies, municipal agencies, public utilities, central banks, regulators, militaries, and subnational governments often move in conflicting directions. Climate strategy should exploit these fractures. A city can build public solar while a national government stalls. A state legislature can mandate clean public power while federal courts narrow agency authority. A public pension fund can divest or, better, use ownership to force phaseout plans. A procurement agency can create markets for low-carbon steel and cement. A central bank can treat fossil assets as systemic risk. The two-step strategy will not be one dramatic capture of power; it will be a long campaign to convert many institutions from fossil dependence to public purpose.

Internationally, the same point applies. Dethroning fossil capital cannot mean rich countries shutting down their own production while outsourcing extraction, manufacturing, and sacrifice elsewhere. The atmosphere has been colonized, as the Cochabamba language suggests, and any serious climate politics must include debt cancellation, technology transfer, adaptation finance, and room for poorer countries to build dignified lives. But that strengthens rather than weakens Táíwò’s point. Without defeating fossil interests in wealthy countries, those countries will continue to export both emissions and obstruction. They will lecture the Global South about responsibility while expanding LNG terminals, protecting oil majors, and underfunding climate finance.

The case for state-centered climate politics is not a case for moderation. It is a case for choosing the battlefield where fossil power can actually be broken. Street protest, direct action, electoral campaigns, litigation, union organizing, municipal experiments, and international treaty work are not rivals; they are components of a single struggle if they are oriented toward the same objective: making fossil extraction politically, legally, financially, and morally untenable.

Táíwò’s essay is valuable because it restores a word that climate politics often lacks: enemy. Not an enemy in the sense of individual hatred, but an organized force whose continued power is incompatible with a livable and democratic future. Fossil capital will not be persuaded into graceful retirement. It must be defeated. The state, for all its violence and compromise, remains one of the few institutions capable of doing that at the required scale. The question is not whether we can keep our hands clean by avoiding it. The question is whether we can make it answer, finally and materially, to the people whose future it claims to represent.

Diversified MiniMax

"Olúfẹ́mi O. Táíwò’s 'two-step' strategy provides a necessary and historically grounded framework for climate politics by prioritizing the defeat of fossil capital through state power as the prerequisite for all other social transformations."

Táíwò's essay arrives at precisely the right moment to inject much-needed strategic clarity into climate politics. The fragmentation of the contemporary left—split between street mobilizers and electoral reformers, between those who view the state as adversary and those who see it as the essential lever of change—has produced a movement rich in passion but poor in the kind of unified analysis that historical moments of genuine transformation have required. The "two-step" framework he articulates, while presented as a twenty-first-century adaptation, deserves recognition as what it actually is: a resuscitation and modernization of the most successful political strategy ever devised by movements seeking fundamental change.

Consider the historical record without ideological blinders. Every significant advance in human freedom—from the abolition of slavery to the eight-hour workday, from civil rights to decolonization—occurred not through the pure expression of moral will or the spontaneous combustion of popular sentiment, but through the patient, difficult work of building institutional power capable of compelling concessions from those who held the guns, owned the factories, and controlled the treasuries. The state was not incidental to these victories; it was central. Laws were changed, not merely attitudes. Power was seized or wrested away, not simply hoped for.

Táíwò is correct that the climate crisis has introduced a temporal urgency that distinguishes our moment from previous struggles. The Marxist concept of "stagism" assumed that the road to liberation wound through necessary historical stages, each with its own duration. But greenhouse gases are indifferent to such patience. The carbon we emit today will shape the climate of 2050 and beyond. This temporal dimension demands that we think about sequencing with a new rigor: what must we accomplish first, and what can only follow?

The answer, as Táíwò argues, is fossil capital. Not because other struggles lack value or urgency—racial justice, gender liberation, and workers' rights each command moral authority that requires no external justification—but because the continued dominance of fossil fuel interests renders all other progress precarious and reversible. What use is a workplace safety regulation if climate-driven crop failures produce famine? What value is voting rights if rising seas swallow the communities where those votes are cast?

The essay's treatment of energy democracy movements in Arkansas and New York demonstrates that the "two-step" strategy is not merely theoretical. These are real instances of public power being wielded to advance renewable energy while simultaneously serving broader social goals—better salaries for teachers, union jobs with living wages. They show that the state, or more precisely public institutions operating through the state, can be made to serve purposes other than capital accumulation. The question has never been whether the state can be used for progressive ends; history answers that question affirmatively. The question is whether we can build the organized power necessary to make it do so.

Some will object that the state has always been an instrument of class domination, that any attempt to wield it for liberation reproduces its fundamentally coercive character. This objection carries theoretical weight but historical weakness. The state in liberal democracies was indeed constructed to protect property and maintain order for the benefit of those with property. But it was also constructed through class struggle, and its character has changed—imperfectly, incompletely, but genuinely—in response to organized pressure from below. The welfare state was not a gift from enlightened elites; it was extracted through decades of organizing, striking, and voting.

Similarly, an energy transition achieved through state action would not represent the perfection of state power but its transformation. Táíwò acknowledges that even a state aligned with public interests faces challenges: capital flight, investment strikes, the resistance of entrenched fossil fuel industries. But these obstacles are not unique to climate politics; they have been faced and partially overcome before. The New Deal, the National Health Service, the Scandinavian welfare states—each represented a moment when the organized left forced the state to serve broader purposes than capital preferred.

The path forward requires electoral engagement, legislative struggle, and mass mobilization in careful combination. It requires supporting candidates and policies at every level of government. It requires building unions capable of compelling employer compliance and public officials responsive to constituent demands. It requires the patient, unglamorous work of politics as it has always been practiced by those seeking change without the advantages of wealth and established power.

Táíwò's essay should be read as a call to strategic unity. The left cannot afford the luxury of mutual incomprehension between those who march in streets and those who stuff envelopes for campaigns. Both tactics serve a single strategic purpose: building the organized power necessary to defeat fossil capital and open space for broader transformation. The climate crisis has given us clarity about what that purpose must be. Now we must build the movements capable of achieving it.
