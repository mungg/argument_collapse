Diversified GPT

"The climate movement must adopt a strategic hierarchy that prioritizes the democratic seizure of state power to abolish fossil capital, as the state is the only institution capable of coordinating a transition at the necessary scale and speed."

Olúfẹ́mi Táíwò is right to insist that the climate movement needs a strategic hierarchy. Not a moral hierarchy: no serious emancipatory politics should tell people facing police violence, occupation, hunger, misogyny, caste domination, or anti-migrant terror that their struggles are secondary in worth. But politics is made not only of values; it is made of sequencing, leverage, timing, and institutional terrain. The climate crisis is not simply another issue on a long progressive list. It is becoming the operating system within which all other struggles will be fought. If fossil capital remains politically dominant for another generation, the space for humane politics will shrink everywhere.

The strongest part of Táíwò’s essay is his refusal of two comforting myths. The first is the myth that the state is either simply the problem or simply the solution. The second is the myth that technological improvement by itself will retire coal, oil, and gas. Both myths have a partial truth. States have been engines of colonial extraction, border violence, police repression, and ecological destruction. And technological progress in renewables has been extraordinary: solar and wind are now, in many contexts, the cheapest sources of new electricity; batteries have improved quickly; electric vehicles are moving from novelty to mass market. But neither fact tells us how power will be reorganized. Cheap clean energy does not automatically dislodge entrenched dirty energy. And suspicion of the state does not answer the question of who will plan, finance, permit, build, and democratically govern the new energy system.

The phrase “dethrone fossil capital” is useful because it names the conflict that polite climate discourse often evades. Fossil fuel companies are not just suppliers responding to consumer demand. They are political institutions. They fund parties, shape trade associations, litigate against regulation, bankroll misinformation, influence universities, cultivate unions and local governments through jobs and tax revenue, and press states to treat continued extraction as synonymous with national prosperity. In the United States, fossil interests have spent decades weakening environmental regulation and blocking climate legislation. In Europe, the energy crisis after Russia’s invasion of Ukraine gave fossil companies a renewed argument for gas infrastructure. In many poorer countries, oil and gas are presented as the last available path to development, debt repayment, and fiscal stability. This is not a market gently awaiting substitution. It is an organized bloc defending a material order.

Táíwò’s two-step argument captures something many climate advocates have learned through defeat. One cannot simply propose the beautiful end state: abundant public transit, retrofitted housing, clean public power, shorter workweeks, regenerative agriculture, and democratic planning. One must first weaken the institutions that make that future structurally implausible. Fossil capital has veto points everywhere. It sits in the tax base of regions, in pension portfolios, in state-owned enterprises, in pipelines and terminals with multidecade lifespans, in campaign finance networks, and in the energy security doctrines of great powers. If that power is not confronted directly, “green transition” becomes additive rather than substitutive: more renewables, yes, but also more oil and gas; more electric cars, but also more highways; more corporate sustainability reports, but also more liquefied natural gas export terminals.

The strategic implication is that public power should be central to climate politics. This does not mean romanticizing every existing public utility or assuming that state ownership is inherently democratic. Publicly owned fossil companies can be as destructive as private ones. Norway’s oil wealth, Saudi Aramco, and many national oil companies show that “public” can mean the public absorption of risk and the elite capture of reward. But public power matters because the energy transition is a coordination problem at civilization scale. It requires building transmission, storage, generation, electrified transport, and efficient buildings in an order and at a speed that private profit signals will not reliably deliver.

The examples Táíwò cites—municipal solar funding teacher raises, Los Angeles’s renewable target, New York’s Build Public Renewables Act—are not marginal anecdotes. They are glimpses of a different political economy. The usual climate bargain offered to working people is sacrifice now for atmospheric benefits later. Public power can reverse that bargain: cheaper bills, union jobs, cleaner air, revenue for schools, community control, and resilience during disasters. It can make decarbonization visible as an improvement in daily life. That is essential because fossil capital’s deepest political strength is not merely money; it is its ability to pose as the defender of ordinary security. “We keep the lights on.” “We provide jobs.” “We protect you from volatile prices.” “We stand between your family and decline.” A democratic clean energy state must defeat that claim in practice, not just in rhetoric.

This is where labor politics becomes indispensable. A climate movement that treats workers in fossil-dependent regions as obstacles will lose, and deserve to lose. The point is not to flatter every union position or give veto power to incumbents. Some unions have aligned themselves with pipelines and drilling in ways that are shortsighted and damaging. But workers’ fear is rational. Mines close, refineries automate, plants shut down, and communities are abandoned. The United States has a long record of promising “transition” and delivering ruin. If climate policy is to dethrone fossil capital, it must also dethrone the corporate power that holds workers hostage. That means wage guarantees, pension protection, public employment, union standards, community reinvestment, and worker-led planning. A “just transition” cannot be a grant program attached to an otherwise neoliberal energy policy. It must be one of the central mechanisms by which fossil capital is politically isolated.

Still, I would sharpen Táíwò’s argument in one respect. The first step cannot be “energy transition” in a narrow sense; it must be fossil abolition through democratic state capacity. Those are not identical. A country can add vast amounts of renewable energy while leaving fossil production intact for export. It can electrify cars while permitting petrochemical expansion. It can subsidize clean technology while allowing utilities to retain gas assets and pass the cost of stranded infrastructure to ratepayers. The climate movement should therefore judge policy not only by green additions but by fossil retirements: no new extraction leases, no new long-lived fossil infrastructure, managed decline of production, accelerated coal retirement, methane regulation, public buyouts where necessary, and legal accountability for deception and damage.

This is why the state cannot be bypassed. Permits are state instruments. Mineral rights are state-defined. Utility regulation is state-based. Public debt, trade rules, central bank policy, industrial standards, and disaster planning are state-mediated. Even the decision to let private companies dominate is a political decision enforced by law. The state creates markets; it does not merely supervise them. A movement that refuses to contest state power leaves those tools to fossil capital and its allies.

But there is also a danger in speaking as if “the state” were a single lever to be seized. States are internally divided: courts, legislatures, bureaucracies, municipal agencies, public utilities, central banks, regulators, militaries, and subnational governments often move in conflicting directions. Climate strategy should exploit these fractures. A city can build public solar while a national government stalls. A state legislature can mandate clean public power while federal courts narrow agency authority. A public pension fund can divest or, better, use ownership to force phaseout plans. A procurement agency can create markets for low-carbon steel and cement. A central bank can treat fossil assets as systemic risk. The two-step strategy will not be one dramatic capture of power; it will be a long campaign to convert many institutions from fossil dependence to public purpose.

Internationally, the same point applies. Dethroning fossil capital cannot mean rich countries shutting down their own production while outsourcing extraction, manufacturing, and sacrifice elsewhere. The atmosphere has been colonized, as the Cochabamba language suggests, and any serious climate politics must include debt cancellation, technology transfer, adaptation finance, and room for poorer countries to build dignified lives. But that strengthens rather than weakens Táíwò’s point. Without defeating fossil interests in wealthy countries, those countries will continue to export both emissions and obstruction. They will lecture the Global South about responsibility while expanding LNG terminals, protecting oil majors, and underfunding climate finance.

The case for state-centered climate politics is not a case for moderation. It is a case for choosing the battlefield where fossil power can actually be broken. Street protest, direct action, electoral campaigns, litigation, union organizing, municipal experiments, and international treaty work are not rivals; they are components of a single struggle if they are oriented toward the same objective: making fossil extraction politically, legally, financially, and morally untenable.

Táíwò’s essay is valuable because it restores a word that climate politics often lacks: enemy. Not an enemy in the sense of individual hatred, but an organized force whose continued power is incompatible with a livable and democratic future. Fossil capital will not be persuaded into graceful retirement. It must be defeated. The state, for all its violence and compromise, remains one of the few institutions capable of doing that at the required scale. The question is not whether we can keep our hands clean by avoiding it. The question is whether we can make it answer, finally and materially, to the people whose future it claims to represent.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Fossil fuel companies function as organized political blocs that use their structural power to ensure green transitions are merely additive rather than substitutive of dirty energy.
  2. 02Public power and state-owned utilities are essential because they can provide immediate material benefits like union jobs and lower bills, making decarbonization visible as an improvement in daily life for working people.
  3. 03Labor politics must be central to the strategy, ensuring that workers in fossil-dependent regions are protected through wage guarantees and public employment to isolate fossil capital politically.
  4. 04The movement must focus specifically on fossil abolition—the managed decline and retirement of existing extraction—rather than just the addition of renewable technology.
  5. 05Strategic climate action should exploit internal divisions within the state, using municipal and subnational agencies to build public alternatives when national governments stall.
  6. 06International climate justice requires defeating fossil interests in wealthy nations to prevent them from exporting emissions and obstruction to the Global South.

"While the strategy to defeat fossil capital through state power is correct, the energy transition must prioritize a planned reduction in aggregate material throughput rather than a like-for-like substitution of green energy for fossil fuels."

**Yes, And: A Friendly Amendment from the Degrowth Left**

Táíwò's essay is the best short statement I have read of a position I largely share: that the climate crisis is, in the first instance, a political problem about the organized power of fossil capital, and that defeating that power requires using, rather than abandoning, the state. I want to push the argument further in a direction Táíwò gestures toward but does not fully embrace: the energy transition cannot be a like-for-like substitution of clean kilowatt-hours for dirty ones. It must involve a substantial reduction in aggregate energy and material throughput in the rich world. Without that, the "two-step" politics he proposes will fail not because it loses the political fight but because it wins the wrong one.

Start with the arithmetic. Global primary energy consumption in 2023 was around 620 exajoules, of which roughly 80% was fossil. The remaining 20% includes hydro, nuclear, biomass, and the still-modest renewable share. To replace the fossil share at current consumption levels with wind and solar would require building several times the entire existing renewable fleet, plus storage, plus transmission, plus the mining capacity to source the lithium, cobalt, nickel, copper, and rare earths required, plus the industrial capacity to manufacture and install the panels, turbines, batteries, and HVDC lines. The IEA's own scenarios concede that some of these material requirements have no precedent. Some categories—copper especially—face physical depletion of high-grade ores within a few decades.

This is not a counsel of despair. It is a counsel of honesty. The energy transition that is physically possible in the next two decades is one in which absolute energy demand in the rich world falls substantially while clean supply ramps up. "Green growth" at current global GDP growth rates is not a serious proposal; it is a marketing slogan attached to the same growth model that produced the crisis.

Táíwò knows this. He cites the degrowth literature respectfully. But his strategic argument tilts away from its implications. The "two-step" formulation suggests that once fossil capital is defeated, we will have the political space to debate growth, distribution, and ecological limits. I worry this gets the sequencing backwards. The political coalitions capable of defeating fossil capital are precisely those that will be unwilling to accept a stationary or contracting economy. The labor unions that Táíwò rightly centers, the social-democratic parties that might build "big green states," the renewable-energy capitalists who could be peeled off from the oil majors—all of these constituencies have a material interest in a high-throughput green economy. None has an interest in a low-throughput one.

What follows is that "first dethrone fossil capital, then transform the world" risks producing a transformation that consolidates rather than dissolves the social relations of capitalism. We get electric SUVs, AI data centers running on solar, and lithium extracted from Chilean salt flats and Indonesian rainforests at colonial terms of trade. Carbon dioxide concentrations stabilize, but every other planetary boundary keeps blowing through its threshold. This is not a hypothetical. This is the trajectory the existing "green industrial policy" of the U.S., E.U., and China is on.

The honest answer is that we need a "two-and-a-half-step" politics: dethrone fossil capital; simultaneously contest the material composition of the replacement; then transform the rest. The middle step cannot be deferred because the infrastructure being built now will lock in patterns of consumption for forty to sixty years. A solar-powered colonial extraction regime is a different and slightly less terrible thing than an oil-powered one, but it is not what climate justice means.

Where I do agree with Táíwò, emphatically, is on the centrality of state power to this project. Degrowth has too often been articulated as a politics of personal renunciation or local self-sufficiency. That tendency is rightly criticized as politically empty. You cannot localize semiconductor supply chains. You cannot prefigure a high-speed rail network. You cannot, by lifestyle choice alone, decommission a coal plant. The reduction of throughput must be planned, coordinated, and democratically authorized—which is to say, it must be statist, even as it expands democratic control over what states do.

This is why I think the most important debates in climate politics are not between degrowthers and Green New Dealers but between both camps and the financialized "transition" agenda that dominates official policy. Daniela Gabor's work, which Táíwò cites, is essential here. The "de-risking state" is the actual political form of the energy transition under current conditions, and it is structurally incapable of either rapid decarbonization or just distribution. Public power—as both ownership and ethos—is the alternative.

One last point. Táíwò argues that fossil capital must be defeated because it is the structural barrier to other justice projects. I would invert the framing slightly: fossil capital must be defeated, but defeating it is not sufficient to achieve any of those other projects, including the ecological project. The same political-economic logic that produced fossil capital—endless accumulation organized through commodified labor and nature—will produce its successor monster if not itself dismantled. Lithium capital, data-center capital, geoengineering capital. The names change; the function does not.

What this means practically is that the movements building toward the first step have to keep one eye on the second. A worker-led nationalization of an oil company is good; a worker-led nationalization that maintains the firm's drilling schedule under public branding is bad. A green industrial policy that creates union jobs is good; a green industrial policy that locks in suburban sprawl, private car dependence, and tropical mineral extraction is mostly bad. The criteria for evaluating "two-step" tactics cannot be reduced to "does this hurt fossil capital." They must include "does this build the political and material capacity for the second step."

Táíwò's essay opens this conversation rather than closes it. The strategic clarity he insists on—that we must concentrate fire on the fossil bloc rather than dispersing it across every justice front—is correct, and the left is much weaker than it admits for failing to internalize it. I want only to add: concentration of fire is not the same as narrowing of vision. We can know what we are aiming at and still know why we are aiming.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The physical and material constraints of mineral extraction and industrial capacity make a high-growth 'green' economy an ecological impossibility.
  2. 02The 'two-step' strategy risks failure if it defers the debate on growth, as the coalitions needed to defeat fossil capital often have a material interest in maintaining high-throughput capitalism.
  3. 03Infrastructure built during the transition will lock in consumption patterns for decades, meaning the material composition of the new energy system must be contested simultaneously with the dethroning of fossil capital.
  4. 04State power is essential for a successful transition because the reduction of throughput requires large-scale planning and democratic coordination that localist or individualist approaches cannot provide.
  5. 05Defeating fossil capital is insufficient for climate justice if the underlying logic of endless accumulation is simply transferred to new sectors like lithium or data-center capital.
Diversified MiniMax

"Olúfẹ́mi O. Táíwò’s 'two-step' strategy provides a necessary and historically grounded framework for climate politics by prioritizing the defeat of fossil capital through state power as the prerequisite for all other social transformations."

Táíwò's essay arrives at precisely the right moment to inject much-needed strategic clarity into climate politics. The fragmentation of the contemporary left—split between street mobilizers and electoral reformers, between those who view the state as adversary and those who see it as the essential lever of change—has produced a movement rich in passion but poor in the kind of unified analysis that historical moments of genuine transformation have required. The "two-step" framework he articulates, while presented as a twenty-first-century adaptation, deserves recognition as what it actually is: a resuscitation and modernization of the most successful political strategy ever devised by movements seeking fundamental change.

Consider the historical record without ideological blinders. Every significant advance in human freedom—from the abolition of slavery to the eight-hour workday, from civil rights to decolonization—occurred not through the pure expression of moral will or the spontaneous combustion of popular sentiment, but through the patient, difficult work of building institutional power capable of compelling concessions from those who held the guns, owned the factories, and controlled the treasuries. The state was not incidental to these victories; it was central. Laws were changed, not merely attitudes. Power was seized or wrested away, not simply hoped for.

Táíwò is correct that the climate crisis has introduced a temporal urgency that distinguishes our moment from previous struggles. The Marxist concept of "stagism" assumed that the road to liberation wound through necessary historical stages, each with its own duration. But greenhouse gases are indifferent to such patience. The carbon we emit today will shape the climate of 2050 and beyond. This temporal dimension demands that we think about sequencing with a new rigor: what must we accomplish first, and what can only follow?

The answer, as Táíwò argues, is fossil capital. Not because other struggles lack value or urgency—racial justice, gender liberation, and workers' rights each command moral authority that requires no external justification—but because the continued dominance of fossil fuel interests renders all other progress precarious and reversible. What use is a workplace safety regulation if climate-driven crop failures produce famine? What value is voting rights if rising seas swallow the communities where those votes are cast?

The essay's treatment of energy democracy movements in Arkansas and New York demonstrates that the "two-step" strategy is not merely theoretical. These are real instances of public power being wielded to advance renewable energy while simultaneously serving broader social goals—better salaries for teachers, union jobs with living wages. They show that the state, or more precisely public institutions operating through the state, can be made to serve purposes other than capital accumulation. The question has never been whether the state can be used for progressive ends; history answers that question affirmatively. The question is whether we can build the organized power necessary to make it do so.

Some will object that the state has always been an instrument of class domination, that any attempt to wield it for liberation reproduces its fundamentally coercive character. This objection carries theoretical weight but historical weakness. The state in liberal democracies was indeed constructed to protect property and maintain order for the benefit of those with property. But it was also constructed through class struggle, and its character has changed—imperfectly, incompletely, but genuinely—in response to organized pressure from below. The welfare state was not a gift from enlightened elites; it was extracted through decades of organizing, striking, and voting.

Similarly, an energy transition achieved through state action would not represent the perfection of state power but its transformation. Táíwò acknowledges that even a state aligned with public interests faces challenges: capital flight, investment strikes, the resistance of entrenched fossil fuel industries. But these obstacles are not unique to climate politics; they have been faced and partially overcome before. The New Deal, the National Health Service, the Scandinavian welfare states—each represented a moment when the organized left forced the state to serve broader purposes than capital preferred.

The path forward requires electoral engagement, legislative struggle, and mass mobilization in careful combination. It requires supporting candidates and policies at every level of government. It requires building unions capable of compelling employer compliance and public officials responsive to constituent demands. It requires the patient, unglamorous work of politics as it has always been practiced by those seeking change without the advantages of wealth and established power.

Táíwò's essay should be read as a call to strategic unity. The left cannot afford the luxury of mutual incomprehension between those who march in streets and those who stuff envelopes for campaigns. Both tactics serve a single strategic purpose: building the organized power necessary to defeat fossil capital and open space for broader transformation. The climate crisis has given us clarity about what that purpose must be. Now we must build the movements capable of achieving it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Historical progress in human freedom has consistently relied on seizing or compelling state power rather than relying solely on moral expression or spontaneous sentiment.
  2. 02The unique temporal urgency of the climate crisis necessitates a rigorous sequencing of political goals where the removal of fossil fuel interests must come first to prevent all other social gains from being reversed.
  3. 03Successful local initiatives in energy democracy prove that public institutions can be effectively repurposed to serve social needs like fair wages and renewable infrastructure.
  4. 04While the state has historically protected property and capital, its character is not fixed and can be fundamentally transformed through organized class struggle and pressure from below.
  5. 05The obstacles of capital flight and investment strikes are not insurmountable, as evidenced by past achievements like the New Deal and the creation of welfare states.
  6. 06Strategic unity is required between street mobilizers and electoral reformers to build the institutional power necessary to dismantle the influence of fossil capital.
Diversified Gemini

"While Táíwò correctly identifies the state as the necessary tool to defeat fossil capital, his strategy will fail unless it centers the organized working class as the primary political agent capable of exerting the material leverage required to capture and discipline the state."

In his essay “Climate, State, and Utopia,” Olúfẹ́mi O. Táíwò provides a sobering and necessary intervention into left-wing debates over state power. His argument that the climate crisis necessitates a "two-step" strategy—first utilizing the state to break the back of fossil capital, then pursuing broader emancipatory goals—is a vital corrective to the anti-statist paralysis that grips much of the contemporary environmental movement. Táíwò correctly identifies that only the massive, coordinating power of the state can manage the logistical and financial scale of a planetary energy transition. However, while Táíwò meticulously theorizes *what* the state must do, his essay suffers from a glaring omission: he fails to identify the political agent capable of actually seizing the state to enact this program.

Táíwò speaks of "the left," "antisystemic movements," and "activists" as if they possess the inherent structural power to discipline the capitalist state. But the state is not a neutral vessel waiting for smart activists to occupy it; it is a battleground of class forces, currently thoroughly dominated by capital. If we are to achieve Táíwò’s first step—the total defeat of the organized political interests of oil, gas, and coal producers—we must answer a fundamental question: who has the material leverage to force the state to act against its most powerful capitalist benefactors? The answer, conspicuously sidelined in Táíwò’s analysis, is the organized working class.

Any climate strategy that relies on state capture without placing the labor movement at the center of its theory of change is doomed to political irrelevance. Fossil capital derives its power not just from lobbying and campaign contributions, but from its control over the physical infrastructure of the economy: energy production, logistics, transportation, and manufacturing. The only social force capable of disrupting that control, and thereby altering the balance of power within the state, is the workers who operate those systems.

Táíwò mentions workers' unions and labor parties in passing as "the usual organizational subjects of the left," and he rightly praises the New York Build Public Renewables Act for its "gold-standard labor language." But labor cannot simply be a constituency that is pandered to with good contract language after the activists have drafted the policy. Labor must be the tip of the spear.

The transition away from fossil fuels represents a profound threat to the livelihoods of millions of workers globally. Extractive industries, despite their ecological devastation, have historically provided some of the highest-paying, most heavily unionized blue-collar jobs available. When climate politics is framed as an environmental imperative driven by NGOs, academics, and enlightened policymakers, it is easily weaponized by the political right as a cosmopolitan attack on working-class prosperity. We see this dynamic playing out across the globe, from the Yellow Vest protests in France—which Táíwò mentions but mischaracterizes as merely an anti-state movement, when it was largely a working-class revolt against a regressive carbon tax—to the right-wing populism courting autoworkers and miners in the United States.

To execute Táíwò’s "two-step" maneuver, step one actually requires a preliminary step: building a mass, majoritarian coalition rooted in working-class material interests. The state will only move against fossil capital when the political cost of inaction outweighs the cost of action. This means climate policy must be fundamentally rebranded as an economic populist project. The demand cannot merely be "emissions reductions" or "planetary delinking"; the demand must be a massive, state-led reindustrialization that guarantees millions of unionized, high-wage jobs in manufacturing, construction, and public transit.

We can see the glimmers of this potential in the recent militancy of the United Auto Workers (UAW). In their 2023 strike, the UAW did not oppose the transition to electric vehicles; rather, they used their structural leverage to demand that the state-subsidized transition to EVs not be used as an excuse by automakers to bust unions and slash wages. This is the exact kind of material contestation that actually disciplines capital. If the environmental movement tries to bypass this kind of messy, shop-floor class conflict by appealing directly to "policymakers" or engaging in symbolic civil disobedience like Extinction Rebellion, it will fail to build the requisite power to capture the state.

Furthermore, Táíwò’s reliance on historical analogies to national liberation movements (Samir Amin’s "delinking") obscures the domestic class conflict required. The national bourgeoisie of the Third World were fighting against external colonial powers. In the fight against fossil capital, the enemy is deeply embedded within the domestic ruling class. Defeating them requires splitting the capitalist class, yes, but more importantly, it requires a mobilized working class willing to strike, disrupt, and vote in disciplined blocks.

Táíwò is right that we cannot wait for perfect ideological purity before acting. We must use the state we have. But the state will only enact the radical decarbonization he envisions if it is pushed by a coalition anchored in labor. We do not just need to dethrone fossil capital; we need to empower the working class to govern the transition. If climate action remains an elite or purely activist project, it will be crushed by a reactionary, fossil-fueled right wing that successfully appeals to the very workers left behind by a poorly executed transition.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The state is not a neutral vessel but a battleground of class forces that will only act against fossil capital if forced by the social power of workers who control the economy's physical infrastructure.
  2. 02Climate politics must be rebranded as an economic populist project to prevent the political right from weaponizing the energy transition as an attack on working-class livelihoods.
  3. 03A successful 'two-step' strategy requires a preliminary step of building a majoritarian coalition that links decarbonization to state-led reindustrialization and high-wage, unionized jobs.
  4. 04Relying on symbolic civil disobedience or appeals to policymakers is insufficient because only shop-floor class conflict and labor militancy can effectively disrupt the power of the domestic ruling class.
  5. 05The environmental movement must empower the working class to govern the transition to ensure it is not crushed by a reactionary, fossil-fueled right-wing backlash.
