"Progressives should stop trying to reclaim the Constitution through legal interpretation and instead focus on building the political power necessary to confront and transcend its inherently anti-democratic structures."

The lead essay’s proposal to “make progressive politics constitutional again” is seductive in its ambition. It promises a way to fight the conservative judicial juggernaut without abandoning the Constitution to the right. By reviving the democracy-of-opportunity tradition, progressives are urged to argue that egalitarian legislation is not just permitted but constitutionally required—that the First Amendment protects collective action by workers, that the Reconstruction Amendments demand economic justice, and that antitrust law is a constitutional necessity. This is a well-intentioned project, but it is also a dangerous trap. The Constitution, as both text and institutional practice, is fundamentally hostile to substantive equality. Trying to reclaim it for progressive ends is like trying to reclaim the master’s tools to dismantle the master’s house; it may yield temporary rhetorical victories, but it ultimately reinforces the very structures that block transformative change.

The authors are correct that the conservative legal movement never accepted the postwar liberal settlement, which separated law from politics and economics from democratic contestation. The right has always understood that the Constitution is a weapon for protecting property and hierarchy. But what the lead essay does not fully acknowledge is that this is not a perversion of the Constitution; it is the Constitution working as designed. The document ratified in 1787 was, from the start, a counter-majoritarian framework engineered to protect creditors, slaveholders, and propertied elites from the unruly passions of democratic assemblies. Its vaunted checks and balances were explicitly designed to filter and blunt popular will. The Senate, the Electoral College, the life-tenured judiciary, and the amendment process were all intended to insulate governance from the kind of broad, redistributive majorities that the democracy-of-opportunity tradition purports to celebrate. The Reconstruction Amendments expanded the franchise and promised equal protection, but they were quickly hollowed out by a judiciary that protected corporations as “persons” while abandoning Black citizens to Jim Crow. The New Deal’s constitutional revolution, far from fundamentally altering this architecture, was a political accommodation that left the underlying property regime intact, merely adding a modest welfare state on top.

In light of this history, the call to re-constitutionalize progressive politics risks legitimizing an illegitimate system. Every time progressives make a constitutional argument, they implicitly accept the authority of the courts to adjudicate the boundaries of the possible. They signal to the public that what matters is what the Constitution means, rather than what the people need. This is a gift to the right, which has spent decades populating the bench with true believers who will not be swayed by even the most eloquent progressive briefs. The Roberts Court is not interested in a dialogue. It is intent on imposing a radical vision of economic liberty and state sovereignty, and it is doing so with breathtaking speed. The leaked draft opinion in Dobbs v. Jackson Women’s Health Organization, which would overturn Roe v. Wade, is only the most visible example. Behind it lies a whole docket of cases that will dismantle the administrative state, eviscerate labor protections, and lock in the political power of minority rule. Against this onslaught, the lead essay’s proposal is to argue back—to say that the Constitution really means the opposite of what the Court says it means. That is like bringing a historical monograph to a gunfight.

The democracy-of-opportunity tradition itself is far from the unalloyed good that the authors paint. As Aziz Rana acknowledges in a passing quote, the “historical high tides” of that tradition occurred during periods of territorial expansion and empire. Jacksonian democracy was built on the genocide of Native Americans and the expansion of slavery. The New Deal’s labor protections were explicitly structured to exclude agricultural and domestic workers—disproportionately Black—as a concession to Southern segregationists. And the postwar liberal consensus that the authors criticize was itself a product of the Cold War, in which any hint of economic radicalism was purged in the name of anti-communism. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition has always been deeply compromised by racial exclusion and imperialism. To resurrect it as a template for today is to whitewash its sins and to invite a politics that again subordinates the most marginalized to the interests of a relatively privileged labor elite.

The alternative to constitutional reclamation is not passivity. It is to treat the Constitution as what it has always been: a site of political struggle, not a source of binding authority. The right has already abandoned any pretense of constitutional fidelity when it suits them. They packed the courts through cynical procedural maneuvers, most famously by refusing to hold a hearing for Merrick Garland while rushing through Amy Coney Barrett. They use the filibuster to block majority-supported legislation while carving out exceptions for tax cuts and judicial appointments. They embrace “constitutional hardball” while lecturing liberals about norms. The proper response is not to play the constitutional argument game better; it is to change the rules of the game itself.

Progressives should be explicit that the current Supreme Court is a political entity with no legitimate claim to the final word on constitutional meaning. Congress has the power, under Article III, to strip the Court of jurisdiction over whole categories of cases. It can expand the lower federal courts and the Supreme Court itself. It can refuse to enforce judicial rulings, as President Andrew Jackson allegedly did in Worcester v. Georgia. These are not extra-constitutional measures; they are within the constitutional text, but they require the political courage to confront the judiciary directly. The lead essay flirts with this idea when it mentions that legislators must “contest hostile judges’ visions,” but it pulls back from the logical conclusion: that the judiciary must be broken as a veto point on democratic governance.

More radically, progressives should consider that the entire framework of constitutionalism may be an obstacle to the kind of transformation needed to forestall climate catastrophe, build racial justice, and end oligarchic rule. The Constitution’s amendment process is so onerous as to be a near-impossibility in an era of deep polarization. Its structural features—equal state representation in the Senate, the electoral college—systematically favor rural, white, conservative populations. No amount of clever constitutional interpretation can overcome the fact that Wyoming has the same number of senators as California. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition cannot fix this; it can only offer consoling fables about a Constitution that never truly existed.

A truly emancipatory politics would focus not on reinterpreting the Constitution but on building the extra-constitutional power to force change. That means mass strikes, civil disobedience, mutual aid networks, and the creation of alternative institutions that prefigure a more democratic society. It means treating election victories as opportunities to pass transformative legislation and daring the courts to strike it down, using noncompliance as a tool of political mobilization. It means, in the long run, constitutional reconstitution—a new founding that replaces the eighteenth-century framework with a genuinely democratic republic. The Movement for Black Lives has called for a “people’s constitution”; that is the right direction, not a retreat to a mythical anti-oligarchy past.

The lead essay is a serious and important contribution. Its authors know their history, and their frustration with liberal timidity is well placed. But their solution—making progressive politics constitutional again—is a strategy for a bygone era. We no longer live in a time when Supreme Court justices can be shamed into moderation or when eloquent briefs can sway the course of law. We live in an era of constitutional crisis, where the old rules no longer bind. In such moments, it is not the best arguments that win, but the side with the power and the will to impose its vision. Progressives should spend less time trying to reclaim the Constitution and more time building the power to transcend it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The U.S. Constitution was intentionally designed by the founders as a counter-majoritarian framework to protect property and elite interests from democratic will.
  2. 02Engaging in constitutional debate legitimizes the Supreme Court's authority and traps progressives in a rhetorical game they are destined to lose against a radicalized right-wing judiciary.
  3. 03The 'democracy-of-opportunity' tradition is a flawed historical model because it was historically predicated on racial exclusion, settler colonialism, and imperial expansion.
  4. 04Rather than reinterpreting the law, progressives should use 'constitutional hardball' tactics such as jurisdiction stripping, court expansion, and direct noncompliance with judicial rulings.
  5. 05The structural features of the Constitution, such as the Senate and the amendment process, are insurmountable obstacles to justice that require a new founding rather than a return to the past.
  6. 06True transformative change will come from extra-constitutional power, including mass strikes and civil disobedience, rather than from winning arguments in court.
