"Aziz Rana correctly argues that reclaiming freedom requires moving beyond messaging to address the structural links between American imperialism and exclusionary liberty by building institutions that foster collective agency."

Aziz Rana's essay is a welcome intervention into what has become a stale debate about American political language. For too long, the left has treated the right's dominance over "freedom" as a communication problem—something to be solved with better slogans and more persuasive messaging. Rana correctly identifies this framing as inadequate. But his analysis goes further, and I think more usefully, by tracing the deep structural connections between American imperialism and the hollowed-out notion of liberty that dominates our political imagination.

What strikes me most is his argument that settler colonialism didn't just incidentally involve violence against Indigenous peoples—it was constitutively tied to white freedom. Land theft wasn't a byproduct of liberty; it was the mechanism through which liberty was produced. This is a crucial insight that many on the left have been reluctant to engage with directly, preferring instead to treat American racism as a distortion of otherwise sound liberal principles. But Rana's framework suggests something more radical: that the very definition of freedom available to white Americans was constructed through exclusion and domination.

The essay's policy implications are also compelling. Rana rightly insists that organizing the workplace is essential—not because it provides a messaging backdrop, but because it creates the material conditions under which solidaristic freedom can be experienced and understood. You cannot argue people into believing in collective agency; you have to build institutions that make collective agency real. This is why the assault on unions over the past fifty years has been so devastating: it didn't just reduce workers' bargaining power, it eliminated one of the primary sites where democratic freedom could be practiced.

I would push Rana further, however, on the question of how exactly these new institutions should be built. The essay gestures toward the "Freedom Budget" and labor law reform, but doesn't fully address the political economy of transformation. How do we win the structural changes Rana envisions when the very institutions that would enable such changes have been systematically dismantled? This is not a rhetorical question—I genuinely don't know the answer, and I wish Rana had engaged with it more directly. What strategies have existed for rebuilding labor movement infrastructure in contexts of deep neoliberal defeat? What can we learn from successful cases like the recent Amazon labor organizing or the幼教师 union victories?

None of this diminishes the essay's contribution. Rana has given us a framework for understanding both the problem and the direction of travel. That the destination remains uncertain is not his failure; it's the nature of the political moment we inhabit.
