"The lead essay’s focus on defeating authoritarianism as the primary goal for Democrats is a flawed normative framing that may fail to resonate with voters more concerned with immediate material hardships."

I want to engage with the lead essay not on the empirical questions—which Bonica and Grumbach handle with admirable rigor—but on the normative framing they employ.

The essay presupposes that Democrats should be primarily concerned with defeating authoritarianism, or at least with winning elections to prevent authoritarian consolidation. This framing shapes their recommendations. If the goal is to prevent authoritarianism, then mobilization and moral clarity are paramount; moderation appears insufficient.

But what if we question this framing? Authoritarianism is not the only threat facing American democracy. Other threats include economic precarity, environmental catastrophe, declining life expectancy, and social fragmentation. These are not less serious than authoritarianism; in some readings, they are more fundamental.

A party organized around opposition to authoritarianism will prioritize institutions, procedures, and norms. A party organized around material improvement will prioritize economic policy. These organizations will look different and make different trade-offs.

The authors assume that democratic preservation is the prerequisite for all other goods. This is a reasonable position, but it is not self-evident. Many voters experience daily economic hardship that feels more immediate than abstract threats to democracy. For these voters, a party that promises to fight authoritarianism while failing to address their immediate needs will seem irrelevant.

This suggests that the mobilization strategy the authors recommend may be less effective than they assume. Anti-authoritarian energy can mobilize those already engaged with the issue, but it may not resonate with voters who are primarily concerned with economic survival.

Democrats should not assume that their preferred framing—democracy vs. authoritarianism—will mobilize voters. They should test alternative framings, including economic populism and social solidarity. The goal is to find what actually motivates the coalition they need.

The authors are right that the moderation debate has been insufficiently attended to. But they may not fully appreciate that the framing of the debate itself may be part of the problem.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Authoritarianism is not the only existential threat to American democracy, as issues like economic precarity and social fragmentation are equally or more fundamental.
  2. 02Prioritizing the defense of democratic norms and institutions over material improvements creates a party structure that may seem irrelevant to voters facing daily economic struggles.
  3. 03A mobilization strategy centered on anti-authoritarianism risks only engaging those already concerned with the issue while failing to reach the broader coalition needed for victory.
  4. 04Democrats should move beyond the democracy-versus-authoritarianism binary to test alternative framings such as economic populism and social solidarity.
