Diversified Claude

"While Daniel Richman’s theory of 'negotiated federalism' correctly identifies the leverage local police hold, his cautious optimism is misplaced because the window to prevent the hardening of an unaccountable, permanent domestic intelligence apparatus is rapidly closing."

Reading Daniel Richman's essay, I find myself agreeing with most of its specific claims while disagreeing with its overall tone of cautious optimism. Richman has correctly identified a real dynamic: federal counterterrorism efforts depend on local police, local police are answerable to local politics, and that dependence creates leverage that may, under favorable conditions, produce a more accountable national intelligence network than would otherwise emerge. What I want to add is that the conditions are not particularly favorable, and that several developments in the last year give us reason to expect things to get worse before they get better.

Consider the trajectory of the past twelve months. The CLEAR Act and its Senate companion would, if enacted, force local police to enforce federal civil immigration law on pain of losing federal funds. Richman notes the pending legislation but treats its future as "unclear." That may be technically true, but the political momentum behind it has only grown. The bill has acquired co-sponsors. A coalition of restrictionist groups is pushing it hard. The administration, while officially neutral, has not opposed it. The bill's underlying premise — that the "inherent authority" of states extends to civil immigration enforcement and that the federal government can compel its exercise — has been blessed by the Office of Legal Counsel in a 2002 memo whose reasoning has not been formally disowned. If CLEAR or something like it passes, the local-police-as-accountability-mechanism story Richman tells collapses immediately, because local police will have been conscripted into precisely the immigration-enforcement role the IACP rightly says destroys community cooperation.

Consider also the rapid build-out of state fusion centers, with federal funding and federal guidance, over the past eighteen months. These centers — there are now several dozen in various stages of development — aggregate criminal, intelligence, and increasingly private-sector data into common platforms accessible by state, local, and federal personnel. Their governance is murky, their privacy protections weak, their mandates expansive. They are being built faster than the policy framework around them. By the time the kind of negotiated federalism Richman envisions matures, the fusion-center architecture may already have locked in a model of information sharing that bypasses local political control entirely.

Consider, third, the steady erosion of statutory and policy constraints on FBI domestic intelligence collection. The Ashcroft guidelines of May 2002 lifted the Levi/Smith guidelines' predication requirements for surveillance of religious and political organizations. The administrative subpoena power, the use of National Security Letters, the relaxed FISA standards under the PATRIOT Act — each individually defensible to some, cumulatively they amount to a significant expansion of authority. Local police participating in JTTFs operate inside this expanded authority, often with less oversight than they have under their own state laws. The "voice" Richman wants local police to exert against federal practice is increasingly being exerted inside a federal practice that has loosened its own constraints.

Consider, finally, the political mood. The 9/11 Commission's recommendations have been received by Congress with strong support for the structural moves (DNI, NCTC) and almost no engagement with the civil-liberties balancing the Commission itself called for. The intelligence-reform bills now moving through Congress are heavily weighted toward the integration side of the ledger and lightly weighted toward the oversight side. The election that just concluded did not, despite the hopes of some, produce any significant change in the political coalition around counterterrorism policy. The next four years are likely to see continued expansion of the apparatus Richman is trying to keep accountable, with no countervailing political pressure to constrain it.

In this environment, the modest mechanisms Richman pins his hopes on — local police voice, JTTF protocols, the National Criminal Intelligence Sharing Plan — are likely to be overwhelmed. Big-city chiefs can grumble, but their grumbling is reliably outweighed by federal funding pressure, by the cooperative habits of working detectives, and by the political costs of being seen as obstructing the War on Terror. Mayors can pass resolutions, but, as Richman notes, most of those resolutions are costless and the ones that bite — like Portland's — are exceptional. The system that is being built does not, on current evidence, much care what local politics has to say about it.

I would push, then, for a more pessimistic stance, and a correspondingly more aggressive political strategy. The window in which the architecture of post-9/11 domestic intelligence can still be shaped is closing. Once fusion centers, expanded watchlists, JTTF expansion, the NCISP, and (perhaps) CLEAR-style local-immigration cooperation are all in place, the institutional inertia behind them will be very difficult to reverse. Now is not the moment for genteel federalism arguments about voice. Now is the moment for sharper political demands: kill CLEAR, impose statutory privacy rules on fusion centers, restore predication requirements for FBI domestic surveillance, sunset more PATRIOT Act provisions and let them lapse, and establish a serious congressional oversight regime for domestic intelligence.

This is not the agenda Richman articulates, and it is not really compatible with his essay's tone, which assumes that the relevant institutional choices are open and the conversation is ongoing. I think the conversation is closing. The architecture is hardening. The window for the kind of federalism solution Richman wants is roughly the window for any reform at all, and that window will not stay open indefinitely.

The right fight is, in the end, not against poorly designed federal-local cooperation. It is against the construction of a permanent domestic intelligence apparatus inadequately constrained by law, in which the post-9/11 emergency is reified into a permanent institutional landscape. Richman's essay is a thoughtful contribution to a conversation that may already be too narrow. The broader conversation — about whether we are building the right kind of state at all — is the one I would prefer to have, and the one I suspect we will all wish, in a few years, we had had more loudly while it was still possible.
