Diversified MiniMax

"CVS should continue to sell tobacco products because it is not the role of a commercial retailer to act as a moral gatekeeper or to restrict the legal choices of informed adult consumers."

The CVS tobacco decision is a mistake, and it sets a troubling precedent regardless of whether you think cigarettes are bad. The company has essentially decided that it will no longer sell legal products to adults who want to buy them because the company — and presumably a board of directors and a PR team — has decided those products are unhealthy. That is not CVS's role.

Retailers do not exist to certify which consumer choices are virtuous. CVS is not a doctor's office, a hospital, or an insurer. It is a commercial enterprise that happens also to offer health-related services. The presence of a walk-in clinic in the building does not give CVS the right to impose its moral judgments about consumption on customers who make different choices. Adults have the capacity and the responsibility to make their own decisions about risk.

If we accept that CVS should stop selling tobacco because it's unhealthy, we must accept the logic that CVS should also stop selling alcohol, processed foods, sugary drinks, and eventually perhaps anything that contributes to climate change, factory farming, or any other concern that captures the imagination of a company's board. The corporate ethics committee becomes the arbiter of daily life.

More fundamentally, the people who buy cigarettes at CVS are not making uninformed decisions. The health risks are among the most thoroughly documented in human experience. If information-based approaches to reducing smoking have failed, the answer is better public health policy, not private retail boycotts that reduce adult consumer choice. CVS should sell tobacco. Adults should make their own decisions. Government can regulate. Companies can inform. But CVS should not become a moral gatekeeper.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01CVS is a commercial enterprise rather than a medical institution, and its provision of health services does not grant it the authority to judge or restrict customer consumption habits.
  2. 02Adult consumers have the personal responsibility and capacity to weigh health risks and make their own decisions regarding legal products.
  3. 03Removing tobacco based on health concerns creates a slippery slope that would logically require the removal of alcohol, processed foods, and other products deemed unhealthy or unethical by a corporate board.
  4. 04Since the health risks of smoking are already well-documented and known to consumers, public health issues should be addressed through government regulation rather than private retail boycotts.
