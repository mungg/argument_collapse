"The climate crisis requires a pragmatic 'one-step' strategy of co-opting and steering private capital toward green infrastructure rather than the ideologically pure but time-consuming 'two-step' goal of first defeating fossil capital through public ownership."

In “Climate, State, and Utopia,” Olúfẹ́mi O. Táíwò delivers a sweeping, historically grounded argument for what he calls a twenty-first-century “two-step” strategy: first use the state to defeat fossil capital, and only then proceed to transform the broader socioeconomic order. It is an elegant framework, and his critique of the paralyzing skepticism the global left harbors toward state power is a necessary corrective. However, while Táíwò is entirely right that state power is the indispensable engine of the energy transition, his conception of how that power must be deployed—specifically his insistence on the total political defeat of "fossil capital" as a prerequisite for climate action—suffers from a fatal misreading of the temporal and economic realities of the climate crisis.

Táíwò’s argument rests on a zero-sum battle between the public interest and private capital. He laments the Biden administration’s approach—what Daniela Gabor calls “bribing private capital”—as a failure, advocating instead for mass public ownership and direct state planning of the energy sector. But in our fiercely constrained timeline to prevent catastrophic tipping points, Táíwò's "step one" is an ideological luxury we simply cannot afford. If we wait to build a political coalition capable of outright expropriating and dethroning private capital, we will cross every planetary boundary in the process. We need a one-step strategy: co-opting private capital to build out green infrastructure at breakneck speed.

The core vulnerability in Táíwò's argument is time. As he rightly notes, emissions are a stock, not a flow. The carbon we have already pumped into the atmosphere is locking in devastating warming, making speed the absolute paramount virtue of climate policy. Building a "big green state" capable of entirely supplanting private investment in the energy sector would require decades of capacity building, institution creation, and brutal, attritional political warfare against deeply entrenched financial interests. The political preconditions for the complete socialization of the energy sector do not exist in the United States, nor in most of the major emitting nations. Attempting to force them into existence as a prerequisite for decarbonization is a recipe for catastrophic delay.

Contrast Táíwò’s utopian public power vision with the messy, pragmatic reality of the Inflation Reduction Act (IRA). Is the IRA a concession to capital? Absolutely. Does it rely on tax credits that enrich private developers and asset managers? Yes. But it is also driving the fastest deployment of renewable energy in American history. By aligning the profit motive of capital with the survival motive of the species, the state has fundamentally altered the trajectory of emissions. Battery manufacturing plants are breaking ground, solar deployment is skyrocketing, and electric vehicle adoption is climbing the S-curve.

Táíwò is dismissive of this approach, citing the European Union's struggles with intermittency and the fact that asset managers like BlackRock still profit from fossil fuels. He demands a clean break, a "delinking." But the history of rapid industrial transformations—from the mobilization for World War II to the postwar economic booms—shows that the state rarely achieves massive infrastructural build-outs by abolishing private capital. Instead, the state acts as the conductor, using subsidies, guarantees, and regulations to force private capital to perform the labor of transition.

Furthermore, Táíwò’s conflation of "fossil capital" with all private capital is an analytical error. Capital is not a monolith. The climate transition is currently creating deep fissures within the capitalist class itself. There is now a burgeoning "green capital" faction—comprising renewable developers, electric vehicle manufacturers, critical mineral miners, and tech companies desperate for clean energy to power their data centers. This faction's material interests are increasingly diametrically opposed to those of ExxonMobil and Chevron. A smart, pragmatic state strategy does not seek to fight both fossil capital and green capital simultaneously in the name of public power. Instead, it seeks to ally with green capital to out-compete and bankrupt fossil capital.

Táíwò points to the New York Build Public Renewables Act as a template for his vision. While a noble and useful piece of legislation, relying on state-run utilities to build out the entirety of the necessary grid at the scale of the North American continent ignores the sheer magnitude of the capital required. Global transition costs are estimated in the trillions of dollars annually. No state balance sheet, not even that of the United States, can internalize that entire cost without triggering massive inflation or crowding out every other social priority. Leveraging private finance through public-private partnerships is not a neoliberal conspiracy; it is a mathematical necessity.

Of course, a strategy of co-optation comes with immense risks. Private capital will seek to extract maximum rents, pass costs onto consumers, and avoid liability for stranded assets. Warren Buffett’s maneuvering, which Táíwò highlights, is a real threat. The state must be ruthless in its regulation, enforcing stringent labor standards, protecting consumers from price gouging, and ensuring a just transition for fossil fuel workers. But regulating capital is an infinitely more achievable political project than abolishing it.

We do not have the time to fight the final battle against capitalism before we save the physical habitability of the Earth. Táíwò’s two-step approach is beautifully reasoned, but structurally backwards for the moment we find ourselves in. We must use the state not to defeat capital, but to steer it, bribe it, and harness its relentless capacity for scale and deployment. If we manage to stabilize the climate through a messy, contradictory, state-subsidized green capitalism, we will live to fight another day. We can debate the utopian horizon of a fully decommodified society once the floodwaters have receded and the fires are put out.
