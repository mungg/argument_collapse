"While effective altruism provides a vital, evidence-based corrective to sentimental and ineffective philanthropy, it must evolve to account for the systemic harms of certain careers and incorporate a stronger respect for individual rights and human dignity."

Peter Singer’s essay has the rare merit of making a moral claim that is both radical and immediately actionable. One need not wait for Congress, a revolution, or the purification of one’s soul. One can, this afternoon, move money from a low-impact use to a high-impact use. In a world where children still die of malaria, diarrhea, measles, and other preventable causes, that matters. Singer’s central point—that affluent people ought to ask not merely whether their giving expresses concern, but whether it actually helps—is difficult to reject without retreating into sentimentality.

The most powerful part of the effective altruist argument is not that it has solved every problem in moral philosophy. It has not. The most powerful part is its comparative discipline. If $7,500 can either provide a magical day for one sick child in San Francisco or prevent several children from dying of malaria, the burden of justification falls on those who choose the wish. It is not enough to say that Batkid moved us, or that the child was visible, nearby, and charming. Those facts explain the donation; they do not justify it.

This distinction between explanation and justification is where effective altruism performs a public service. Most charitable giving in affluent societies follows psychological grooves laid down by proximity, identity, storytelling, and institutional habit. Alumni give to already wealthy universities. Museum patrons give to museums they enjoy attending. Disaster relief surges after televised catastrophes and collapses when the cameras leave. None of this proves that these gifts are worthless. But it does show that our giving often resembles consumption: we buy the moral experience we want. Effective altruism asks us to treat beneficiaries, not donors, as the center of the transaction.

Singer is also right to resist the charge that effective altruism must be joyless. One of the oddest objections to utilitarian giving is that it drains life of meaning. In fact, many people find modern affluence draining precisely because it gives them so few purposes beyond acquisition, display, and private comfort. The decision to give substantially and intelligently can be liberating. It converts a salary into a tool. It makes ordinary professional life part of a larger project. The young graduate who donates half his Wall Street income may be less trapped by money than the lawyer or professor who earns less but spends every marginal dollar preserving a status position.

Still, the example of Matt Wage also points to the most serious internal challenge for effective altruism: the difference between doing good with money and doing good through institutions. Earning to give is morally attractive when the work itself is neutral or only mildly regrettable. But not all high-paying work is neutral. Finance can allocate capital productively; it can also extract rents, increase systemic risk, and encourage talented people to design socially useless instruments. A person who earns a fortune by producing harm and donates a fraction to repair the damage cannot claim victory simply because the donated dollars are measurable. Effective altruism needs a rigorous account of the negative externalities of careers, not only the positive externalities of donations.

That is not a refutation. It is a demand that effective altruism apply its own standards consistently. If a hedge fund job indirectly worsens market instability or political inequality, those effects should count. If a pharmaceutical career helps bring useful drugs to market, that should count too. If a civil servant improves vaccine delivery for millions, the value of that career may exceed anything she could donate from a higher salary elsewhere. “Earning to give” should be one option in a broader menu of high-impact lives, not the movement’s signature myth.

Singer’s discussion of rights and rules is reassuring but too brief. He says effective altruists know that violating basic moral constraints will “almost always” have worse consequences. That is true, but it understates the reason many of us prize rights. Rights are not merely useful rules of thumb that happen to maximize welfare most of the time. They also protect persons from being treated as raw material for other people’s projects. Effective altruism will be stronger if it can speak fluently in the language of dignity, consent, and democratic equality, not only in the language of aggregate benefit.

Yet this criticism should not obscure the achievement. Effective altruism has already changed the moral conversation. It has made it harder for donors, foundations, and universities to speak as though every cause were equally urgent. It has exposed the evasiveness of “follow your passion” philanthropy. Passion is not a moral compass; it is, at best, fuel. The compass must be evidence, impartial concern, and a willingness to change one’s mind.

Singer’s essay should be read less as a complete ethical system than as a corrective to complacency. It reminds affluent people that our ordinary decency is often compatible with preventable death. We can pay our taxes, love our children, refrain from cruelty, and still ignore opportunities to save lives at little cost to ourselves. Effective altruism gives that uncomfortable fact a practical form. We do not have to become saints. But we do have to stop pretending that all generosity is morally equal. Some gifts save lives. Some mostly flatter the giver. The difference is real, and it is our responsibility to know it.
