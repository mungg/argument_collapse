"The retraction of the Rolling Stone article must not be used to undermine campus sexual assault reform or the civil rights protections afforded to survivors under Title IX."

The unraveling of the Rolling Stone narrative is a tragedy on multiple fronts, but the greatest danger it poses is the weaponization of its retraction to silence survivors. Opponents of campus sexual assault reform have immediately seized upon this single journalistic failure to suggest that the epidemic of campus rape is a myth, and that universities are plagued by false accusations. We must firmly reject this backlash.

Statistically, false accusations of rape remain remarkably rare, hovering between 2 and 8 percent according to extensive criminological research. In contrast, the Department of Justice estimates that nearly one in five women will experience an attempted or completed sexual assault during their college years. The overwhelming majority of these crimes go unreported because survivors fear precisely what we are witnessing now: public skepticism, victim-blaming, and institutional betrayal.

Universities have a legal and moral obligation under Title IX to investigate these claims. Suggesting that we leave it entirely to the police ignores the reality of the criminal justice system, which historically mistreats rape victims, subjects them to secondary trauma, and operates under a "beyond a reasonable doubt" standard that is nearly impossible to meet in cases involving alcohol and private encounters.

Title IX is not about criminal punishment; it is about a student's civil right to equal access to education. If a student is forced to share a dorm or a classroom with her rapist, she is being denied that right.

Treating accusers supportively does not mean abandoning fairness; it means reforming a historically biased system. A trauma-informed approach dictates that when a student reports an assault, she is met with belief and support rather than suspicion and interrogation. Providing interim measures—such as moving the accused out of a shared class or dormitory during the investigation—is not a violation of due process; it is a standard safety protocol.

Fairness for the accused is preserved through impartial fact-finding, transparent communication, and equal access to the appeals process. But we cannot allow the demand for "fairness" to become a Trojan horse for re-traumatizing victims with aggressive cross-examination or impossibly high burdens of proof. The Rolling Stone story may have been flawed, but the reality of campus sexual violence is not. We must not let one retracted article derail a desperately needed civil rights movement.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Statistical evidence shows that false accusations are rare while sexual assault remains an epidemic that is significantly underreported due to fear of public skepticism.
  2. 02Universities have a unique legal and moral obligation under Title IX to ensure equal access to education, which necessitates an administrative response separate from the criminal justice system.
  3. 03The criminal justice system is often ill-equipped to handle sexual assault cases due to a history of mistreating victims and a burden of proof that is difficult to meet in private encounters.
  4. 04Adopting a trauma-informed approach and providing interim safety measures for accusers does not violate the due process rights of the accused.
  5. 05Fairness for the accused can be maintained through impartial fact-finding and transparent appeals without subjecting victims to re-traumatizing cross-examinations.
