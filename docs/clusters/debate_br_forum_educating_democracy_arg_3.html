The Romance of the Small School

Deborah Meier's "Educating a Democracy" is one of the most eloquent statements of the progressive case against standards-based reform that I have read. It is also, I want to suggest, a deeply nostalgic document — one that imagines a future for American schooling by misremembering its past and overgeneralizing from a small set of unusually successful institutions. I want to take both Meier and her standards-movement opponents to task, because I think this debate, as currently staged, is impoverishing our public conversation about education.

Let me start with the standards reformers, since they make up the side currently winning. Meier is right that the rhetoric of "world-class standards" has run far ahead of any actual evidence. The empirical foundation for A Nation at Risk was always wobbly, as Richard Rothstein and others have shown. The link between school test scores and national economic performance is loose at best, as Meier notes by pointing out that our economy is booming while our TIMSS rankings are middling. And the implementation of standards-based reform in places like Texas, Chicago, and now Massachusetts has been crude, punitive, and frequently incoherent — long lists of skills no real teacher could teach in a year, tests of dubious validity, cut scores set by political feel, and "accountability" that falls almost entirely on the powerless. On all of this, Meier is correct, and the standards advocates owe the public a much more humble account of what they know and don't know.

But Meier's alternative also has problems, and I want to name them. Her argument rests, again and again, on the demonstrated success of a particular kind of school: Central Park East, Mission Hill, the Boston Pilot schools, the small alternative schools that flowered in New York between 1975 and 1995. These are real institutions with real outcomes, and they deserve celebration. But they share a set of features that should make us cautious about generalizing.

First, they are schools of choice. Families who attend them have actively opted in, which means the parent population is, on average, more engaged, more aspirational, and more willing to put up with institutional eccentricities than the average urban parent. This selection effect is large. We know from countless studies — including studies of magnet schools, Catholic schools, and early charter schools — that simply moving children from assigned schools to chosen ones produces meaningful gains in outcomes, holding everything else equal.

Second, they are founder-led. Meier herself launched Central Park East; the Mission Hill model bears her imprint; the Pilot schools were created by teams of educators with strong shared visions. Such schools are not reproducible at scale because charismatic founders are not reproducible at scale. Every successful small-school network in American history has hit a ceiling at exactly the point when it ran out of true believers to staff its expansion.

Third, they are populated by self-selected faculty. Teachers come to Mission Hill because they want to teach at Mission Hill. They accept long hours, intense collaboration, and constant scrutiny because they are bought into the mission. Most American teachers are not in self-selected faculties. They are assigned to schools by districts; they work alongside colleagues they did not choose; and many would actively resist the kind of intensive, mission-driven culture Meier describes.

Fourth, they are small. This is the easiest variable to scale, and to her credit, Meier emphasizes it. But even here, building 90,000 small schools out of America's current stock of medium-to-large ones is not a trivial undertaking, and the evidence on what happens when you simply break a big school into smaller administrative units, without changing anything else, is mixed.

I dwell on these features not to disparage Meier's schools, which I admire, but to point out that the policy question is not "should we have more Mission Hills?" — to which the answer is obviously yes — but "what should we do about the 50 million children who will not attend a Mission Hill?" Meier's essay offers surprisingly little guidance on this. Her implicit answer seems to be: make every school more like Mission Hill. But the conditions that make Mission Hill work — choice, founder vision, self-selected faculty, scale — cannot all be reproduced by policy fiat. Some of them are in tension with public schooling itself.

The standards movement, for all its flaws, is at least an attempt to answer the question of what to do about ordinary schools — the ones with assigned populations, average teachers, average principals, and no charismatic founder. Its bet is that clear public expectations, plus measurement, plus consequences, will move the median school forward. It may be a bad bet, but it is a bet about the actual distribution of American schools, not about the best ones.

A second concern about Meier's argument is its treatment of democracy. She insists that "in fundamental questions of education, experts should be subservient to citizens," and that local communities should make the key decisions about what their children learn. This sounds democratic, but it conceals a sleight of hand. Whose local community? American schools have always been governed locally; that is precisely how we ended up with Jim Crow schooling, with creationism in Kansas, with grossly unequal per-pupil spending across district lines. Local democratic decision-making has produced some of the worst outcomes in American educational history, and "deference to the community" has often meant deference to its most powerful or most fearful members.

Meier knows this. At one point she concedes that "the only local powers we seem to be interested in expanding are those that allow us to re-segregate our schools by race or gender." But this concession is fatal to her argument. If local control produces bad outcomes when uncorrected by external pressure, then external pressure — including the kind embodied in standards, tests, and federal civil-rights enforcement — is part of how democracy actually works for vulnerable minorities. Meier wants the protective external pressure (civil-rights regulations, anti-segregation rules) but not the academic external pressure (standards, tests). The distinction is hard to defend on principle.

A third concern is her diagnosis of the deeper crisis. Meier argues that the real American crisis is one of human relationships — adolescents disconnected from adults, communities frayed, voter turnout low. I think she is right that something has gone wrong with American adolescence, and that schools are part of the picture. But the causal chain from "make schools smaller and more loving" to "fix democracy" is a long one, and Meier asks us to take a great deal of it on faith. Voter turnout in the United States has been declining since the 1960s; large high schools are one of many possible explanations and probably not the largest. Civic disengagement has structural causes — money in politics, the decline of unions and mass-membership organizations, residential segregation, media fragmentation — that no school reform, however humane, will touch.

What would I propose instead? A modest synthesis. Yes to common standards, but written by working teachers and disciplinary experts together, kept short and clear, and revised on a regular cycle. Yes to public assessments, but multiple kinds — including performance tasks and school-quality reviews, not just bubble tests. Yes to accountability, but aimed at adults and institutions, not at individual children, and yes especially to accountability for the inputs we know matter (teacher quality, class size, curricular resources). Yes to small schools of choice — fund them, replicate them, free them from regulations that don't make sense — but do not pretend they are a system-wide answer. And yes to local democratic voice, but always in dialogue with the wider public interest, including the interests of children whose own communities may not be willing or able to give them what they need.

Meier's essay is a useful corrective to standards-movement triumphalism, and I am grateful for it. But the answer to a bad theory of school reform is not a romantic theory of school reform. It is a sober one.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The success of Meier’s model schools relies on unique conditions—such as self-selected families, charismatic founders, and mission-driven faculty—that are not reproducible by policy fiat for the majority of students.
  2. 02Meier’s emphasis on local control ignores the historical reality that local democratic decision-making has frequently produced inequitable outcomes, such as segregation and curricular censorship.
  3. 03The standards movement, despite its flaws, attempts to address the needs of 'ordinary' schools with average resources and assigned populations, whereas Meier’s vision offers little guidance for schools lacking her specific institutional advantages.
  4. 04The causal link between creating smaller, more personal schools and fixing broad structural crises in American democracy, such as low voter turnout and civic disengagement, is speculative and unsupported by evidence.
  5. 05A more effective approach would be a modest synthesis that maintains common standards and public assessments while incorporating multiple forms of accountability and supporting small schools of choice as part of a larger system.
