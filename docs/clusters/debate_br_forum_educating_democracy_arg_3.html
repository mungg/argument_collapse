"While Deborah Meier correctly identifies the human and democratic risks of rigid standardization, the solution lies not in a total retreat to local control, but in a balanced system that combines clear, universal academic expectations with the flexibility and relationship-rich environments of small schools."

Deborah Meier's essay is a compelling portrait of what's wrong with American education, but I fear it may be fighting yesterday's battle. By December 1999, the standards-based reform movement has already evolved considerably from its Reagan-era origins. While Meier correctly identifies the dangerous implications of zero-tolerance expulsion policies and the cynical use of "standards" rhetoric to mask segregation and privatization, she sometimes conflates the worst manifestations of standards-based reform with its core principles. A more discriminating analysis would serve us better.

Meier is absolutely right that the crisis narrative of A Nation at Risk was overblown. The American economy in 1999 is strong, our universities remain globally competitive, and international literacy comparisons, while imperfect, hardly justify the alarm bells that have echoed for sixteen years. This point deserves fuller acknowledgment than she gives it—many of us who support educational improvement have long since moved past the crisis rhetoric. The question now is not whether our schools are in crisis but whether they are adequate to a rapidly changing world.

Where I part ways with Meier is on the question of expertise and external standards. She argues that "experts should be subservient to citizens" and that locally determined standards will produce better outcomes than externally imposed ones. This romanticizes local communities in ways that ignore their very real limitations. Let me offer an example from my own experience: I have worked with schools in rural Mississippi and urban Louisiana where local "standards" meant keeping textbooks from the civil rights era out of classrooms, where science curricula were modified to undermine evolution, and where tracking systems ensured that African American students were disproportionately placed in lower academic tracks. Local control, in these contexts, was precisely the problem.

The question is not whether communities should have voice in their children's education—they absolutely should. The question is whether communities should have veto power over universally applicable academic expectations. Meier's alternative assumptions seem to assume that local communities, given proper resources and freedom, will naturally converge on wise educational choices. But communities have values, biases, and interests that sometimes conflict with the needs of children who will live in a diverse, interconnected nation and world.

I do agree with Meier on several crucial points. Her critique of large, impersonal schools is well-founded, and the evidence from small schools like Central Park East is genuinely impressive. Her insistence that equity requires resource equality, not just uniform standards, is essential and too often ignored. And her concern about the disconnection between young people and mature adults is perceptive and important.

But small schools and local flexibility are not inherently opposed to clear expectations about what students should know and be able to do. The most effective small schools I've observed actually have very clear standards—they've just developed them collaboratively rather than having them imposed from above. Mission Hill's approach, which Meier describes, is not anti-standards; it's anti-imposed-standards. That's a meaningful distinction.

The challenge for American education is to develop expectations that are rigorous, clear, and widely shared, while preserving the flexibility and relationship-rich environments that Meier rightly values. This is not impossible, but it requires us to think more carefully about institutional design than either the standardization enthusiasts or the local-control romantics typically manage.

What would such an approach look like? First, state or national frameworks could establish the domains of knowledge and skill that all students should encounter, without mandating how teachers must teach or what resources must be used. Second, assessment systems could include multiple measures, including portfolios and performance tasks, not just standardized tests. Third, school-level flexibility could be built in, with real autonomy over curriculum implementation, staffing, and scheduling. Fourth, accountability systems could focus on growth and progress, not just absolute performance on standardized measures.

Meier's real contribution is reminding us that education is not merely a technical enterprise but a fundamentally human one, shaped by relationships and embedded in communities. This insight should inform how we design standards and assessments, not lead us to abandon standards altogether. In 1999, we have both the knowledge and the opportunity to build educational systems that combine rigor with humanity, common expectations with local creativity. Let's not waste that opportunity by retreating into familiar ideological camps.

  2. 02Romanticizing local control ignores the reality that local communities can sometimes use their autonomy to enforce biases, maintain inequity, or exclude essential scientific and historical knowledge.
  3. 03Universal academic frameworks are necessary to ensure that all children, regardless of their specific community's values or limitations, are prepared for life in a diverse and interconnected nation.
  4. 04Small, successful schools like Mission Hill demonstrate that the real conflict is not with standards themselves, but with how they are imposed and assessed.
  5. 05A viable middle ground involves state frameworks that define broad domains of knowledge while granting schools autonomy over curriculum implementation, staffing, and diverse assessment methods like portfolios.
  6. 06True educational equity requires addressing resource disparities alongside standards, as uniform expectations alone cannot compensate for unequal funding and support.
Diversified MiniMax

"While Deborah Meier correctly identifies that rigid standardization undermines the cultivation of democratic character, her rejection of external standards is overly broad and fails to provide a complete solution for balancing local autonomy with the need for shared educational expectations."

Deborah Meier's essay is best understood not as a contribution to technical debates about educational policy but as a statement of democratic philosophy. Her argument is that standardization undermines the conditions for democratic life, and that schools in a democracy should above all else model and transmit the practices of self-governance. This is a serious argument that deserves a serious response.

Meier's core claim is that standardization turns teachers and parents into "the local instruments of externally imposed expert judgment," thereby depriving young people of the opportunity to observe adults exercising genuine judgment in the face of disagreement. This argument has considerable force. Democratic citizenship requires more than knowledge of facts and procedures; it requires the habits of mind and character that enable citizens to deliberate together about the common good. These habits cannot be transmitted through standardized curricula; they must be learned through participation in communities that actually deliberate.

But Meier's argument also proves too much. If external standards are incompatible with democratic education, then most of what we call curriculum is already problematic. Textbooks, which Meier herself notes dominate American education, are externally authored documents that teachers are expected to implement. Graduation requirements are externally imposed standards. Even the most progressive schools have expectations about what students should learn and be able to do. The question is not whether external standards exist but whose standards they are and how they are developed and implemented.

Meier's alternative assumptions are more nuanced than simple localism. She argues for "multiple, legitimate definitions of a good education" developed through dialogue among educators, families, and communities. This is a plausible vision of democratic curriculum development, but it raises difficult questions about whose voice prevails when definitions conflict, and how we ensure that all students receive educations that prepare them for participation in a diverse and interconnected society.

The deeper issue is whether democracy requires a shared conception of the good life or merely procedural rules for living together despite our differences. Meier seems to assume the former—that democratic education must transmit shared values and practices. But if democracy is fundamentally about accommodating difference, then perhaps the goal of education should be to prepare students to negotiate their differences rather than to agree on a common vision.

This ambiguity in Meier's argument reflects a tension in democratic theory itself. Should schools aim to transmit the traditions and practices that constitute a community, or should they aim to develop the critical capacities that enable citizens to examine and revise those traditions? Both goals are legitimate aspects of democratic education, and they sometimes conflict.

I do not pretend to resolve this tension here. But I want to suggest that Meier's argument illuminates a genuine problem in American education without offering a complete solution. The standardization movement has often ignored the formative purposes of education—the cultivation of character, citizenship, and community—in favor of purely instrumental goals. This is a real failure that deserves critique.

But the solution to this failure is not to abandon expectations or accountability but to reconceive them in light of broader purposes. What would standards look like if they aimed at developing democratic character, not just academic competence? How would we assess schools if we cared about the quality of community life, not just test scores? These are the questions that Meier's essay invites us to confront.

The schools Meier describes—small, reflective, community-centered—offer partial answers to these questions. But they are answers that must be elaborated and extended, not merely celebrated. The challenge for democratic education is to create institutions that are both excellent and inclusive, both demanding and supportive, both accountable and free. This is a harder task than either standardization or localism alone can accomplish, and it requires us to hold multiple values in tension.

Meier's essay is a valuable contribution to this ongoing project. It reminds us that education is not merely preparation for economic success but formation for democratic life. This insight should inform our reforms, even as we struggle to implement it in institutions that serve millions of diverse students with diverse needs and aspirations.

  3. 03Meier’s vision of multiple, localized definitions of education raises unresolved questions about how to handle conflicting community values and ensure all students are prepared for a diverse society.
  4. 04The tension in Meier's argument reflects a fundamental democratic conflict between transmitting community traditions and developing the critical capacities to revise those traditions.
  5. 05The true solution to the failures of the standardization movement is not to abandon accountability, but to reconceive standards and assessments to prioritize democratic character and community quality over purely instrumental academic goals.
