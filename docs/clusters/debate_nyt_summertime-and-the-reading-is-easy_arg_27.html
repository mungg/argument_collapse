"The concept of "guilty pleasures" in reading should be embraced rather than abolished because it reflects the honest, multifaceted nature of human interest."

I am not ready to abolish the phrase “guilty pleasure.” It names something real: the gap between the reader we advertise and the reader we actually are.

There is comedy and honesty in that gap. The novelist who publicly praises Proust but privately devours a rock star’s memoir is not necessarily a hypocrite. She is human. We read for many reasons: beauty, gossip, consolation, speed, nostalgia, instruction, escape. A good literary culture should be elastic enough to admit all of them.

The guilt, lightly held, can even be part of the fun. It acknowledges that taste is social. We know certain books carry prestige and others do not. Pretending otherwise can become its own form of snobbery. Better to confess: yes, I wanted the backstage stories, the bad behavior, the absurd clothes, the rise and fall of a person I watched on television in 1983.

Besides, today’s trash is often tomorrow’s evidence. Pop memoirs, romances, thrillers and forgotten best sellers tell us what people feared, desired and admired. They preserve the texture of an era more vividly than many official documents.

So let there be guilty pleasures — but with the emphasis on pleasure, not guilt.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The gap between our public literary persona and our private reading habits highlights a healthy and human diversity in the reasons why we seek out books.
  2. 02Acknowledging the social prestige associated with certain books prevents a reverse form of snobbery and allows for a more honest engagement with popular culture.
  3. 03Lighthearted guilt can enhance the reading experience by acknowledging the social dynamics of taste.
  4. 04Popular and commercial literature serves as valuable historical evidence that preserves the cultural texture and desires of an era better than official documents.
