Diversified Gemini

"While Evgeny Morozov correctly identifies AI’s ideological capture by 'instrumental reason,' his critique is limited by a Eurocentric framing that fails to address the colonial foundations of AI data regimes and the epistemic violence they inflict on the Global South."

Evgeny Morozov’s “The AI We Deserve” offers a sweeping, erudite critique of artificial intelligence, diagnosing its flaws not in its code, but in its soul. By tracing the lineage of AI back to the “instrumental reason” of the Cold War and contrasting it with the playful “eolithism” of Hans Otto Storm, Morozov argues that we have built machines that understand efficiency but are blind to context, imagination, and purpose. His call to wrest AI from the corporate “Efficiency Lobby” and redesign it to serve “ecological reason” is a powerful provocation. Yet, while his diagnosis of AI’s ideological capture is razor-sharp, his essay suffers from a pervasive Eurocentric framing. In his search for alternatives, Morozov briefly gestures toward Latin America, but he ultimately fails to reckon with the colonial foundations of AI’s data regimes and the ongoing epistemic violence perpetrated by these systems against the Global South.

Morozov frames the original sin of AI as its teleological obsession with problem-solving, born from the U.S. military-industrial complex. But there is a deeper, unacknowledged sin: AI, from its cybernetic origins to today’s Large Language Models (LLMs), has always been a project of universalizing a specifically Western, hegemonic worldview while actively erasing peripheral knowledges. The “problems” that early AI sought to solve, and the “data” upon which modern neural networks are trained, are not neutral. They are steeped in coloniality.

Consider the foundation of modern generative AI: the training dataset. LLMs like ChatGPT are trained on vast scrapes of the internet, which disproportionately reflect the languages, cultures, and values of the Anglophone Global North. The result is an intelligence that defaults to Western norms of propriety, history, and logic. When a user in Nairobi or Jakarta interacts with an LLM, they are forced to conform to a digital architecture designed in Silicon Valley, trained on the digital detritus of the Global North, and fine-tuned by poorly paid annotators to align with California’s legal and cultural sensitivities. This is not just a problem of “instrumental reason”; it is an ongoing project of digital colonialism.

Morozov’s concept of “ecological reason”—the ability to interact contextually with one’s environment—is deeply relevant here. But true ecological reason must recognize that the “environment” is not a monolith. Indigenous and subaltern communities possess profound, non-instrumental knowledge systems—ways of relating to land, community, and time—that are fundamentally illegible to the statistical flattening of LLMs. Generative AI does not just fail to embody ecological reason; its deployment actively destroys it by flooding the global digital ecosystem with homogenized, synthetic Western discourse, drowning out vernacular languages and local epistemologies.

To his credit, Morozov brings Latin American examples—Stafford Beer and Fernando Flores’s Project Cybersyn in Chile, and Oscar Varsavsky’s work in Argentina—into his narrative. He praises these efforts for attempting to decouple national technology projects from the efficiency agenda of the Global North. However, he treats these examples primarily as political curiosities, historical evidence that “another world is possible,” rather than engaging deeply with the decolonial theory that animated them. Varsavsky, for instance, was deeply concerned with how imported science and technology perpetuated dependency and cultural imperialism in the Third World. Morozov glosses over this anti-imperialist core, adapting Varsavsky’s “normative planning” merely as a tool for public deliberation in a generalized (and presumably Western) democratic context.

If we want the “AI we deserve,” we must look beyond the paradigm of merely socializing the infrastructure within Western democracies. A truly decolonial AI agenda would challenge the very imperative to build massive, universalist foundation models. The assumption that a single model should be able to answer any question, in any language, for any person on Earth, is inherently imperial. It assumes a "view from nowhere" that always, inevitably, turns out to be a view from the metropole.

Instead of trying to redirect the “eolithic potential of generative AI toward public... alternatives,” as Morozov suggests, we should be asking how to dismantle the monopoly of the foundation model entirely. A decolonial approach would prioritize the development of small, localized, sovereign AI systems. These would be models built by and for specific communities, trained on proprietary local datasets that are governed by data trusts, not scraped indiscriminately. For indigenous communities, for example, AI could be used to revitalize endangered languages and archive oral histories, but only if the community retains absolute sovereignty over the data and the weights of the model.

Furthermore, we must address the material extraction that sustains the “Efficiency Lobby.” Morozov mentions the energy costs of LLMs, but a decolonial critique must center the fact that the physical infrastructure of AI is built on the extraction of minerals from the Global South and the exploitation of “ghost labor”—the thousands of data annotators in Kenya, the Philippines, and Venezuela who perform the traumatic work of filtering out toxic content to make AI palatable for Western consumers.

Morozov is right that we need a radical political project to rethink AI. But that project cannot simply be a New Deal for the digital age, focused on domestic public goods and participatory budgeting. It must be an internationalist, anti-colonial project. The AI we deserve is one that refuses the universalizing gaze of the metropole, respects digital sovereignty, and recognizes that true “ecological reason” is plural, local, and firmly rooted in the defense of marginalized lifeworlds.
