"The only way to meet global seafood demand while protecting marine life is to transition from ocean-based fishing and farming to land-based, closed-containment aquaculture systems."

If we want to eat fish without destroying the seas, we must stop looking to the sea as our primary source of protein and start looking inland. Aquaculture can fully decouple seafood demand from ocean depletion, but only if we invest in technologies that leave the wild oceans alone. The era of offshore net-pen farming, with its pollution, disease transfer, and reliance on wild fishmeal, is a dead end. The future is in closed-containment systems on land.

Recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS) now allow us to raise species from barramundi to Atlantic salmon in gigantic indoor tanks, miles from the nearest coastline. These systems filter and reuse over 99 percent of their water, capture all waste for fertilizer, and eliminate escapes and parasite interactions with wild stocks. Crucially, they also allow for much tighter control over feed. Researchers at the University of Maryland and elsewhere have already demonstrated salmon thriving on diets that replace the vast majority of fishmeal with algae-based oils and soy. When the price of wild forage fish inevitably rises, these plant- and microbe-based feeds will become the economic norm.

Skeptics say it’s too expensive. But we pay massive hidden subsidies for wild fish in the form of depleted common resources and degraded ecosystems. A level playing field—where we actually account for the environmental cost of trawling and open-net farming—would make RAS instantly competitive. Already, companies like Australis Aquaculture are marketing their sustainably raised barramundi to Whole Foods at a price premium. As energy costs for climate control drop and scales increase, that premium will shrink.

This doesn’t mean we should abandon wild fisheries. Wild salmon and tuna can become the equivalent of grass-fed beef: a high-value, occasional luxury harvested under strict quotas. But the daily fish that anchors the Mediterranean diet—the fillets, the fish sticks, the sushi rolls—will come from factories, not the ocean. That is the only plausible path to giving the sea a break while still feeding a fish-hungry world.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS) eliminate environmental hazards like pollution, disease transfer, and species escapes that plague traditional offshore net-pen farming.
  2. 02Advancements in feed technology allow land-based farms to replace wild fishmeal with sustainable algae and plant-based alternatives, decoupling seafood production from ocean depletion.
  3. 03Land-based aquaculture becomes economically competitive when the hidden environmental costs and subsidies of wild fishing and open-net farming are factored into the market price.
  4. 04Shifting mass-market seafood production to land-based factories allows wild fisheries to be managed as high-value, low-volume luxuries under strict conservation quotas.
