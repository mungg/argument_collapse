**Smart Cities Are the Prize Worth Fighting For**

The most exciting frontier of the Internet of Things isn't in our homes—it's in our streets. Consider what becomes possible when a city's infrastructure becomes sensor-rich and networked. Parking spaces that report their occupancy can eliminate the estimated 30 percent of urban traffic that consists of drivers circling for a spot. Streetlights that dim when nobody is present can cut municipal energy budgets significantly. Trash bins that report when they're full let sanitation crews skip the empty ones. Air-quality sensors at the block level let asthmatic residents plan their commutes. Bridges with strain gauges can warn engineers before they fail.

Cities like Santander in Spain and Songdo in South Korea are already experimenting with this. Closer to home, Chicago's planned array of environmental sensors is a modest version of the same idea. The aggregate gains—in energy, in time, in safety, in quality of life—dwarf anything we will get from a smarter refrigerator.

But cities are also where the privacy stakes become collective rather than individual. You can decline to buy a connected thermostat. You cannot decline to walk down a street that is densely instrumented with cameras, microphones, and Bluetooth sniffers. The data generated by smart-city infrastructure is data about everyone who passes through it, and the governance questions—who holds it, who can subpoena it, how long it is retained, whether it can be combined with face recognition—are public questions that cannot be resolved by clicking "I agree."

This argues for a different kind of approach than we use for consumer products. Smart-city deployments should be subject to mandatory privacy impact assessments before deployment, with public comment. Data retention should be short by default. Identifying data should be discarded at the point of collection wherever possible; aggregate counts, not individual traces, satisfy most legitimate municipal uses.

We can have the efficiency and the freedom both, but only if cities are forced to design for both from the start. Retrofitting privacy onto a surveillance system that already exists has, historically, never worked.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Networked urban infrastructure can drastically improve municipal efficiency, public safety, and environmental quality of life.
  2. 02Smart-city technologies pose a unique privacy threat because individuals cannot opt out of being monitored in public spaces.
  3. 03The governance of urban data should be a matter of public policy rather than private user agreements.
  4. 04To protect civil liberties, cities must implement mandatory privacy impact assessments and prioritize the collection of aggregate rather than identifiable data.
  5. 05Privacy protections must be integrated into the initial design of smart-city systems because they cannot be effectively added after a surveillance infrastructure is already in place.
