Diversified Claude

"The defense of campus protests should be grounded in the coercive logic of the labor picket line rather than the expressive logic of free speech, as protest is a tool for the powerless to impose costs on institutional behavior."

**The Picket Line Test**

Alex Gourevitch is right that the campus crackdowns of the last eighteen months represent a generational rollback of the right to protest, and right again that the most insidious justification has been the conflation of subjective discomfort with objective hostility. But his essay, for all its clarity, soft-pedals the strongest argument for an expansive right to protest, which is not a free-speech argument at all. It is the argument from the picket line.

Gourevitch notes in passing that, "unlike, say, a labor picket," the Gaza encampments did not actually try to prevent anyone from getting where they were trying to go. He intends this as a concession to critics: see, the protests were less disruptive than even a routine union action. But the comparison cuts the other way. A picket line works precisely because it imposes a cost—social, moral, sometimes physical—on crossing it. The right to picket is the right to make business-as-usual harder. If we accept that workers can lawfully obstruct foot traffic into a struck workplace, shout at strikebreakers, and call their coworkers scabs, then we have already accepted that the right to collective protest includes the right to be aggressive, uncivil, and inconvenient. Once that is granted, the entire edifice of "I felt unsafe walking past the encampment" collapses. Of course you felt unsafe. That is what political pressure feels like from the receiving end.

The reason this framing matters is that Gourevitch's "right to be hostile" rests, in his telling, on the civic value of disagreement: democracy is demanding, encountering opposition to one's views is uncomfortable, etc. This is true but thin. It treats protest as essentially expressive—a form of speech to be tolerated because speech is good. The picket-line tradition treats protest as essentially coercive—a form of pressure that the powerless use against the powerful when other channels fail. Once you see protest this way, the question of whether the encampments "interrupted" anyone's day becomes almost beside the point. The protesters were trying to change institutional behavior; making the institution feel the cost of its choices is the whole game.

This matters for the Gaza encampments specifically because the targets were not abstract political views but concrete institutional entanglements: endowments invested in arms manufacturers, research partnerships with Israeli military contractors, study-abroad programs at universities built on occupied land. These were demands for divestment and disclosure—precisely the kind of demands South African anti-apartheid activists made in the 1980s, and which universities, after years of resistance, ultimately conceded. Treating the Gaza protests as primarily a free-speech matter, as Gourevitch tends to, lets administrators off the hook for the substantive demands they refused to engage.

I would push Gourevitch one step further than he goes. He wants administrators to tolerate disruption short of "serious" disruption, and to mete out proportionate punishment for the rest. Fair enough. But the standard he proposes still leaves enormous discretion to deans and provosts who have demonstrated, over the past two years, that they cannot be trusted with such discretion. A stronger principle would be: when protest is directed at institutional policy, the institution forfeits its role as neutral arbiter and should not be punishing protesters at all. Discipline in such cases is self-dealing. Let the criminal law handle genuine violence; let everything else go.

This sounds radical only because we have forgotten how universities used to function. The encampments at Columbia in 1985 against apartheid lasted three weeks; the building was renamed Mandela Hall; nobody was expelled. That precedent was not "lenience." It was an acknowledgment that the university itself was the party being protested, and that you do not get to be both defendant and judge.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The discomfort felt by those passing the encampments is a legitimate and necessary feature of political pressure, analogous to the social and moral costs imposed by a labor picket.
  2. 02Framing protest as primarily expressive speech is insufficient because it ignores the essential role of protest as a coercive mechanism used when formal channels of power fail.
  3. 03The Gaza protests were specifically targeting concrete institutional entanglements like endowments and research partnerships, making them more like a labor dispute than an abstract debate over ideas.
  4. 04Universities should not act as both defendant and judge by disciplining students who are protesting the institution's own policies.
  5. 05Administrators cannot be trusted with the discretion to define 'serious disruption,' and therefore should forfeit their disciplinary authority over nonviolent protests directed at institutional policy.

**Yes, And: A Labor Perspective**

Reading Gourevitch's essay as someone who has spent a career organizing campus workers, I want to say two things. First: he is exactly right, and the analysis cannot stop where his does. Second: the people most likely to be crushed by the new repressive apparatus on campus are not the tenured professors or even the protesting students, but the workers who hold campuses together—custodians, food service workers, graduate employees, postdocs, adjuncts.

On the first point. Gourevitch's defense of an absolutist right to protest, including the right to be hostile, including the right to make people uncomfortable, is the only defense compatible with anything we would recognize as labor's traditional understanding of collective action. Every major workplace gain in American history was achieved by people who broke rules, made noise, refused to disperse, and generally inflicted on bosses and bystanders the precise sort of "subjective discomfort" that today's administrators treat as a Title VI violation. The sit-down strikes at Flint, the Memphis sanitation strike, the UC Berkeley clerical workers' strike in 1980—these were "disruptive." That was the point.

The collapse of the distinction between hurt and harm is therefore not just a campus problem or even a free-speech problem. It is the legal architecture that, if extended, makes labor militancy impossible. Already we are seeing employers argue that union-organizing speech creates a "hostile work environment" for anti-union workers; that picket lines amount to harassment; that disruptive collective action violates the rights of customers and clients to access services without distress. The same logic Gourevitch dissects on campus is being deployed to neuter the NLRA. If feelings of vulnerability suffice to shut down protest, then the strike weapon is dead.

On the second point. Gourevitch's framing tends to treat "students and faculty" as the campus constituency whose rights matter. This is understandable—they are the most visible protesters—but it is incomplete. Campus workers are the canaries in this coal mine. When Columbia called in the NYPD, the students who got arrested were eventually released and went back to class. The graduate workers and the contingent faculty who joined them face firings that have no due process at all. The food service workers who refused to cross picket lines have already been disciplined. The undocumented workers in campus dining halls live in a state of perpetual exposure that no Title VI argument will ever reach.

This is why I think Gourevitch's prescription, while correct, is too narrow. He wants administrators to apply proportionate, individualized discipline and to distinguish discomfort from harm. Fine. But the deeper problem is that the disciplinary apparatus itself—the deans of students, the campus police, the surveillance cameras, the keycard systems, the conduct codes—was built over the past forty years for the express purpose of producing a docile workforce and a consumerist student body. Reforming its standards will not change what it is for.

The labor answer is collective: campus unions, student-worker solidarity, and concrete material demands that connect the right to protest to the right to organize. The University of California academic workers' strike of 2022, which the UC system tried to suppress in part through "hostile environment" arguments against picketers, is a model. The Rutgers AAUP-AFT strike in 2023 is another. These struggles do not just defend speech; they build the kind of countervailing power without which speech rights are a paper guarantee.

So I would say to Gourevitch: take your argument further. The right to be hostile is the right to inflict the costs of disagreement on those who would otherwise ignore us. That right has always been most fully realized in labor, and labor's institutions—unions, councils, picket lines—are the only mechanism robust enough to defend it against an administrative state, public or private, that would prefer we be polite. Campus protesters who built encampments instinctively understood this. They borrowed forms from Occupy, from the South African divestment movement, and from the long history of working-class self-organization. The next step is to make the connection explicit and organizational. Otherwise, we will spend the next decade arguing about civility while the actual machinery of repression grows.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The conflation of subjective discomfort with objective harm creates a legal precedent that threatens the fundamental viability of labor strikes and union militancy.
  2. 02Campus workers, including graduate employees and contingent faculty, are more vulnerable to the university's disciplinary apparatus than tenured professors or students.
  3. 03The existing administrative and surveillance infrastructure on campuses was specifically designed to enforce docility among workers and students, making simple policy reform insufficient.
  4. 04True protection of speech and protest rights requires the building of countervailing power through unions and student-worker solidarity rather than relying on administrative benevolence.
  5. 05The right to be hostile is essentially the right to impose the costs of disagreement on those in power, a principle historically rooted in working-class self-organization.

**Where Gourevitch's Symmetry Breaks**

Gourevitch closes his essay with a principle that, he insists, applies regardless of one's views on Gaza: "whatever right protesters claim for themselves is a right they are also claiming for their enemies." This is the moral hinge of the argument. It is also where I want to press.

The principle is logically sound. If you want a robust right to occupy a quad in protest of Israeli policy, you must also tolerate a quad occupation by, say, an anti-trans group, an anti-abortion group, or a group celebrating the deportation of undocumented students. The right is either viewpoint-neutral or it is not a right. Gourevitch knows this; his examples of pro-life "Genocide Awareness" displays and conservative bake sales show that he is willing to bite the bullet.

But the symmetry breaks down at the level of institutional practice in ways that Gourevitch's essay does not fully address. Consider three asymmetries.

First, asymmetry of administrative response. Gourevitch notes that pro-Palestinian protesters have been punished while pro-Israel counter-protesters have largely not been, even when they engaged in similar behavior (and in some cases, like the UCLA attack on the encampment, considerably worse). He treats this as evidence that administrators are applying their standards unevenly under political pressure. True. But the question is what to do about it. A purely formal commitment to viewpoint neutrality will not, by itself, produce viewpoint-neutral outcomes when administrators are subject to asymmetric pressures from donors, trustees, and federal agencies. You can have the most beautiful First Amendment doctrine in the world and still find that, in practice, only one side gets prosecuted.

Second, asymmetry of social cost. Gourevitch's "right to be hostile" assumes that the discomfort of being protested is, in some rough sense, equally distributed across political positions. But protest targets the powerful, or at least it is supposed to. When pro-Palestinian students protest a Hillel speaker, they are exercising countervailing pressure against an institution backed by enormous resources and political weight. When pro-life activists post graphic images outside a Planned Parenthood, they are exercising countervailing pressure against a vulnerable population (women seeking abortions, many of them already in distress) backed by far less institutional support. The "right to be hostile" looks different in the two cases. Gourevitch's formal symmetry obscures this material asymmetry.

Third, asymmetry of consequence. Gourevitch wants us to tolerate protest because the alternative is administrative overreach. Fair. But some protests are themselves attempts to bring about administrative overreach. The "antiwoke" right has spent five years using protest, complaint, and public pressure to demand the firing of professors, the cancellation of courses, the elimination of DEI offices, and the legislative gutting of academic freedom. Should we tolerate these protests on the same terms as we tolerate Gaza encampments? In one sense yes, because viewpoint neutrality. In another sense, no, because the content of one protest is "stop investing in this war" and the content of the other is "fire that professor for what she said in class."

A consistent absolutism on the right to protest cannot distinguish these cases at the level of the right. It can only distinguish them at the level of substance: this protest is correct, that one is wrong. Gourevitch is uncomfortable with this and tries to abstract from it. But the abstraction will not hold. When pro-life protesters at South Carolina display lynching photos to make their case against abortion, they are not just exercising a right; they are making a substantive moral argument that is, in my view, deeply wrong and that causes real harm to Black students and to women on that campus. Defending their right to do this is one thing. Pretending that defending their right is morally equivalent to defending the Gaza protesters is another.

This is where Gourevitch's essay, for all its rigor, performs a familiar liberal sleight of hand. It defends an absolutist right at the level of formal entitlement while quietly counting on substantive agreement at the level of which protests are worth defending. I share his substantive sympathies. I think the Gaza protests were morally serious and that the crackdown was a disgrace. But I would prefer an argument that owns its substance rather than launders it through procedural neutrality.

What would that look like? It would say: the right to protest is not viewpoint-neutral in the way liberal speech doctrine pretends, because some protests aim at justice and some aim at domination, and a healthy university distinguishes between them. The distinction will be contested, sometimes wrongly. But pretending we can do without it has produced a regime in which the only protests anyone defends in practice are the ones that confirm the priors of whoever happens to be in power. Gourevitch's essay is, in the end, a brief for substantive judgment dressed in proceduralist clothing. I would rather wear the substance plainly.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Formal viewpoint neutrality fails to produce equitable outcomes when university administrators are subject to lopsided political and financial pressures from donors and government agencies.
  2. 02The social cost of hostile protest is not equally distributed, as protests targeting vulnerable populations carry different moral weight than those targeting powerful institutions.
  3. 03An absolutist right to protest fails to distinguish between movements seeking justice and those specifically designed to trigger administrative overreach and the suppression of academic freedom.
  4. 04The lead essay's reliance on procedural neutrality is a 'liberal sleight of hand' that avoids the necessary task of making substantive moral judgments about the goals of specific protests.
  5. 05Universities should move away from pretending to be neutral and instead openly distinguish between protests that aim at justice and those that aim at domination.

"Framing the campus crisis as a violation of the "right to protest" is a strategic mistake because it reduces a fundamental political struggle over institutional power to a procedural legal debate that favors the university administration."

**Against the Right-to-Protest Frame**

I want to make an argument that, I suspect, will be unpopular with most readers of Gourevitch's essay and with Gourevitch himself: the entire "right to protest" frame, as deployed in this debate, obscures more than it reveals, and the campus crisis it is meant to address will not be resolved at the level of rights at all.

Let me start with what Gourevitch gets right. The crackdown on Gaza protesters has been excessive, hypocritical, and often nakedly political. The Trump administration's deployment of antidiscrimination law as a weapon against universities is a transparent abuse, and the universities that have folded under pressure deserve every bit of contempt they receive. The Khalil case is a constitutional disgrace. The disparity between the punishment of anti-war protesters and the impunity of their attackers (most spectacularly at UCLA) demonstrates that we are not living under viewpoint-neutral rules. None of this is in dispute, at least not from me.

What I dispute is the frame in which Gourevitch's essay places these facts. The frame is: there is a right to protest, this right has been violated, the violation must be reversed. The frame's grammar is liberal-individualist; its hero is the individual protester confronting an overbearing institution; its remedy is procedural (better rules, more proportionate discipline, clearer standards). And the frame's promise is that, if we can just get the rules right, the underlying conflict will become manageable.

I do not believe this. The conflict on campus is not, fundamentally, about whether protesters have rights. It is about what universities are for and to whom they belong. The Gaza protesters were not just expressing views; they were trying to force their institutions to stop being complicit in mass killing. The donors, trustees, and federal officials cracking down on them are not just enforcing rules; they are reasserting that universities exist to serve their preferences, not the preferences of students and faculty. This is a political conflict over institutional control, dressed up as a legal debate about speech and discipline.

When you frame a political conflict as a rights conflict, several things happen, none of them good for the weaker party.

First, you concede the terrain of legitimacy to the institution. The very act of demanding that the institution recognize your right is the act of accepting its authority to grant or deny rights. The South African divestment movement of the 1980s did not primarily win through arguments about the right to protest. It won through years of disruptive, sometimes unlawful collective action that eventually made the cost of refusing divestment higher than the cost of conceding it. The Black liberation movement did not win the Civil Rights Act by establishing its right to peaceful assembly; it won by making segregation ungovernable.

Second, you displace organizational power-building with juridical argument. Once the question becomes "what are our rights and how should they be adjudicated?" the actors are no longer collectives but lawyers, plaintiffs, and amici. The relevant venues become courtrooms and editorial boards. The relevant skills become litigation and op-ed writing. The protest movement that built the encampments was, at its best, a genuine collective political formation; the post-encampment phase, dominated by disciplinary hearings and federal investigations, has dispersed that formation into atomized individuals defending themselves.

Third, you create a discursive trap where every defense of protest must be coupled with a denunciation of "bad" protest. Gourevitch's essay performs this ritual carefully: yes, some statements were genuinely antisemitic, yes, some celebrations of violence were repugnant, yes, the universities had a legitimate interest in preserving order. These concessions are not strategically wrong. But they reflect a defensive posture that the original protesters did not occupy. The protesters were not asking permission. They were trying to make a divestment movement that universities could not ignore.

Fourth, and most importantly, the rights frame mistakes the audience. Gourevitch's essay is addressed, ultimately, to administrators, faculty colleagues, and liberal opinion. It is an argument for restraint, proportionality, and principle. But the people imposing the crackdown are not bad-faith liberals who can be persuaded by better arguments; they are political actors pursuing political objectives, and they will continue pursuing them regardless of what Boston Review publishes. The audience that matters is the next cohort of would-be protesters, and to them the rights frame is, frankly, demobilizing. It tells them that their political action depends on institutional permission, that they should be prepared to argue their case in a hearing, that the limits of acceptable protest are negotiable with deans and lawyers. None of this is how political movements actually win.

What would I propose instead? Three things, none of which Gourevitch's essay precludes but none of which it foregrounds.

First, organize. The campus actors who have weathered the crackdown best are those embedded in unions, formal coalitions, and durable political organizations. Atomized students, however brave, are crushed. Organized students have leverage, even now.

Second, build solidarity outside the university. The reason South African divestment eventually succeeded was that it operated simultaneously inside campuses and in city councils, state legislatures, churches, and labor halls. The Gaza movement has had less of this, partly because the targets are different (universities and federal policy rather than corporate investments), partly because the political coalition is harder. Building outward is the work of the next phase.

Third, abandon the assumption that universities are reformable on their own. Gourevitch flirts with this realization—he notes that universities are "deeply undemocratic and donor-driven" and that we cannot expect administrators to behave well. But he does not draw the conclusion: that the project of reforming university discipline from within is largely hopeless and that the energy of the protest movement should be aimed at forcing structural change (governance reform, donor accountability, faculty power) rather than at adjusting the disciplinary calculus at the margin.

I will close with a partial concession. Gourevitch's essay does work that needs doing. Someone has to make the principled case for tolerance of disruptive protest; someone has to articulate why the hostile-environment standard, as currently deployed, is a menace. He does this with care and erudition. My quarrel is not with the essay's content but with the implicit theory of change it offers. Rights arguments matter, but they are downstream of power. The Gaza protesters discovered, painfully, that they did not have the power they imagined themselves to have. The recovery of that power will not come from a better articulation of their rights. It will come from building, slowly and unfashionably, the kind of collective political capacity that makes rights enforceable in the first place. That is the work in front of us, and the right-to-protest frame, valuable as it is in defensive moments like this one, will not be sufficient to that work.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The rights-based frame reinforces the legitimacy of the university's authority by asking the institution for permission rather than challenging its control.
  2. 02Focusing on legal rights shifts the movement's energy away from collective political organizing and toward atomized litigation and academic debate.
  3. 03A rights-centric approach forces protesters into a defensive posture where they must constantly distance themselves from "bad" or controversial speech to maintain legitimacy.
  4. 04The rights frame misidentifies the audience, attempting to persuade bad-faith political actors with principled arguments rather than building the power necessary to force structural change.
  5. 05Real progress for campus movements depends on building durable organizational capacity and external solidarity rather than reforming internal university disciplinary codes.
