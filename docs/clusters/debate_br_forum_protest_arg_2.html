"A durable defense of campus liberty must protect the right to offensive protest while maintaining viewpoint-neutral rules of order that prevent disruption from swallowing the university's educational mission."

Alex Gourevitch has written the free speech essay many conservatives used to write and too few now seem willing to endorse. His central point is correct: a right to protest that disappears when protest becomes offensive, hostile, or emotionally unsettling is not a right. Universities that call police on peaceful demonstrators because donors, politicians, or administrators dislike the message have betrayed their mission.

Yet Gourevitch’s argument also exposes a problem he does not fully confront. The campus left helped build the machinery now being used against it. For years, many students and administrators treated speech as harm, demanded disinvitations, shouted down speakers, expanded bias-reporting systems, and insisted that members of marginalized groups should have broad authority to define what counts as violence. Now the same logic is being applied to pro-Palestinian protest by people with more political power. The result is ugly, but it is not surprising.

This does not justify the crackdown. Hypocrisy is not a civil liberties principle. If a student once favored deplatforming a conservative speaker, that student does not thereby forfeit protection against expulsion for peaceful protest. Rights are not rewards for consistency. Still, any durable defense of campus liberty must admit that the old progressive speech regime was wrong before it was turned against progressives.

Gourevitch says as much, and he deserves credit for it. But his remedy risks replacing one special pleading with another. He wants an “absolutist” defense of protest, including disruptive protest, and he suggests that encampments on campus commons should be broadly tolerated. I agree up to a point. But conservatives, libertarians, and old-fashioned liberals should be wary of granting protest a privileged status that swallows ordinary rules.

The university commons is not a public park in the full sense, especially at a private university. It is a shared institutional space devoted to education. Students should be able to rally there, leaflet there, hold vigils there, argue there, and display messages others hate. But they should not acquire the right to indefinitely occupy it, control access, establish checkpoints, or make other students run a gauntlet of political confrontation as the price of attending class. Property rules are not always pretexts for repression. Sometimes they are the rules that allow people with competing commitments to share a space.

That is where some defenders of the Gaza encampments have been evasive. It is not enough to say most people could still get where they needed to go, or that obstacles were erected for self-defense. The question is whether a university may impose generally applicable rules against camping, structures, amplified sound, and disruption. Of course it may. The issue is whether those rules are clear, viewpoint-neutral, and enforced proportionately.

This is why police raids and expulsions were often indefensible. If a university had long tolerated encampments for other causes and suddenly discovered a no-camping rule only when the cause was Gaza, that is viewpoint discrimination in spirit even if lawyers can dress it up otherwise. If students were suspended or denied degrees for nonviolent conduct that would previously have received a warning, that is punitive overreach. If the Trump administration is threatening funds to force universities into ideological conformity, conservatives should oppose it as an abuse of federal power.

Unfortunately, many conservatives have abandoned their own stated principles. After years of denouncing “cancel culture,” some now cheer the cancellation of anti-Israel protesters. They once argued that students must hear ideas they find hateful; now they argue that Jewish students must be shielded from anti-Zionist slogans. They once mocked “safe spaces”; now they demand them, only for different constituencies. They once warned that DEI bureaucracies would become ideological police; now they want to redirect the police rather than dismantle the ideology.

This is not conservatism. It is factional opportunism.

A principled conservative response would say three things.

First, antisemitism should be punished when it takes the form of threats, vandalism, assault, discriminatory exclusion, or targeted harassment. Jewish students have the same right as anyone else to attend school without being menaced. Universities that ignored swastikas, slurs, or intimidation would be failing in their duties.

Second, criticism of Israel, including harsh criticism and anti-Zionism, must be protected. The state should not define the boundaries of acceptable opinion on foreign policy. Nor should federal agencies adopt expansive definitions of antisemitism that effectively make one side of a geopolitical debate legally hazardous.

Third, universities may enforce neutral rules of order. They can ban camping on quads, limit amplification, keep entrances clear, and remove occupiers from buildings. But penalties should be modest unless there is violence or serious obstruction, and rules should apply to everyone: pro-Palestinian activists, pro-Israel activists, climate protesters, anti-abortion demonstrators, and anyone else.

Gourevitch is right that subjective fear is a poor guide. The examples he quotes—students frightened by keffiyehs, chants, or the need to walk near a protest—cannot justify suppression. If conservatives allow such claims to control, they will have conceded every premise they used to oppose progressive speech codes. The answer to offensive protest is counterprotest, argument, social criticism, and, where necessary, narrow discipline for actual misconduct.

But Gourevitch should be more candid about the limits of disruption. Civil disobedience has consequences. If students deliberately violate a neutral rule, they may accept a proportionate penalty as part of the protest. That is different from persecution. Expulsion for peaceful trespass may be excessive; a citation or probation may not be. The moral seriousness of a cause does not give activists unilateral authority to decide which rules bind them.

The best campus speech regime is neither therapeutic nor anarchic. It protects speech and assembly. It rejects heckler’s vetoes. It allows offensive displays, whether anti-abortion images, anti-Israel slogans, anti-affirmative-action stunts, or radical critiques of America. It punishes threats and violence. It enforces neutral rules about space and noise. It does not ask administrators to decide which political emotions deserve validation.

The Trump administration’s campaign against universities should make defenders of limited government uneasy. Federal research money should not become a tool for forcing ideological reforms through executive threat. Universities have earned criticism for ideological conformity, administrative bloat, and double standards. But the cure for campus illiberalism is not federal illiberalism.

Gourevitch’s essay is valuable because it calls everyone’s bluff. If you defended free speech only when conservatives were unpopular, now is the test. If you defended student safety only when progressives invoked it, now is the test. If you said universities should be places of fearless inquiry, then you must tolerate students saying things about Israel, Palestine, America, policing, abortion, race, and religion that you find repugnant.

The right to protest is real. It is not unlimited. It should not depend on whether I like the protesters. That simple proposition has become rare enough to sound radical.

"While the lead essay correctly identifies the dangers of university overreaction and political interference, a sustainable right to protest requires a framework of clear, viewpoint-neutral rules that balance the right to dissent with the university's obligation to maintain its educational operations."

The best way to destroy the right to protest is to pretend that protest presents no hard administrative problems. Alex Gourevitch does not quite do that, but he comes close. His essay powerfully indicts the overreaction of universities and the opportunism of the Trump administration. Yet it gives too little attention to the mundane obligations that make a university different from a town square: classes must meet, laboratories must operate, libraries must remain usable, dormitories must be livable, and students who want no part in a demonstration must still be able to go about their lives.

The issue is not whether students have a right to protest. They do. Nor is it whether universities should have called armed police on peaceful encampments as readily as many did. They should not have. The issue is whether Gourevitch’s near-absolutist defense of disruptive protest gives institutions enough guidance to distinguish tolerable disruption from coercive occupation.

Universities failed this test in both directions. Before the Gaza protests, many had cultivated expansive, selectively enforced speech norms. Administrators routinely sent moral pronouncements on public events, policed interpersonal conduct through broad harassment rules, and encouraged students to understand emotional injury as a matter for bureaucratic resolution. Then, when protests over Gaza erupted, the same institutions abruptly rediscovered property rights, order, and neutrality. That hypocrisy helped create the crisis.

But the remedy cannot be simply to say that encampments belong in the campus commons until they become violent or directly threatening. Space is scarce. Time matters. Scale matters. Duration matters. A twelve-hour rally is not a three-week encampment. A symbolic sit-in is not the indefinite seizure of a building. A march through campus is not a blockade. A protest that creates ambient noise on the quad is not one that makes a classroom unusable. Rules have to account for those differences.

Gourevitch argues that campus commons are special, not like classrooms or libraries. That is right. But “less governed” cannot mean “governed by whoever can mobilize enough people to hold the space.” A commons exists for the whole community. If one group may convert it into a semi-permanent political camp, others must have the same right. In theory, Gourevitch accepts that symmetry. In practice, simultaneous encampments by mutually hostile groups can make the commons unusable for everyone and potentially dangerous. Administrators cannot avoid responsibility by invoking protest rights in the abstract.

The answer is not repression but constitutionalism for campus life: clear, public, viewpoint-neutral rules adopted through legitimate processes and enforced with restraint.

A reasonable protest code would protect rallies, marches, pickets, vigils, symbolic displays, and short-term encampments in designated public areas. It would permit noise but regulate amplification during class hours and quiet hours. It would allow signs, chants, and offensive messages but prohibit targeted threats, discriminatory exclusion from campus facilities, harassment directed at specific individuals, and obstruction of entrances. It would distinguish occupying an administrative building from sitting on a lawn. It would provide escalating consequences: warning, citation, modest disciplinary sanction, and only in the most serious cases suspension or police referral.

The most important feature would be due process. Students should not face suspension, expulsion, degree withholding, or arrest through improvised procedures created under donor, congressional, or executive pressure. Evidence should be individualized. Charges should be specific. Hearings should be timely and fair. Sanctions should be proportionate. Collective punishment should be forbidden.

On this point Gourevitch’s critique is devastating. The extraordinary punishments imposed on many Gaza protesters look less like discipline than deterrence. Universities were not simply enforcing rules; they were signaling to politicians, trustees, and donors that they had regained control. That is an institutional failure of the first order.

Yet defenders of protest should also be honest about the concept of civil disobedience. Civil disobedience often involves breaking rules to expose injustice. Its moral power does not depend on pretending no rules were broken. If students occupy a building, refuse lawful instructions, or establish an encampment in violation of a clear time limit, they may be disciplined. The question is whether discipline is modest and whether the institution recognizes the conscientious nature of the act. A warning or probation may be appropriate where expulsion is outrageous. A trespass arrest may be disproportionate where an internal sanction would suffice. But “protest” cannot be a universal exemption from rules.

The hostile environment question is harder. Gourevitch is correct that subjective fear cannot be enough. If every group can shut down protest by saying it feels unsafe, no protest will survive. At the same time, administrators cannot ignore patterns of intimidation simply because they are politically framed. If students are followed, filmed, surrounded, spat on, denied access, or targeted by slurs, the university has an obligation to intervene. If Jewish, Israeli, Palestinian, Arab, or Muslim students are treated as legitimate objects of contempt because of identity, that is not merely robust politics.

Here too the answer is specificity. “I felt afraid walking past a protest” is not, by itself, evidence of a hostile environment. “A group blocked me from entering a building while calling me a terrorist” is different. “I dislike that slogan” is not the same as “students surrounded me and shouted it in my face.” “This movement’s demands make me feel unwelcome” is different from “this organization excludes people of my national origin from events.” Universities should investigate conduct, not adjudicate metaphysical debates about whether anti-Zionism is always, sometimes, or never antisemitism.

The Trump administration’s intervention makes this disciplined approach harder but more necessary. The federal government has legitimate authority to enforce civil rights laws. It does not have legitimate authority to use Title VI as a pretext for ideological takeover. Conditioning hundreds of millions of dollars on sweeping political demands threatens the independence of universities and the integrity of civil rights enforcement. Administrators should resist such coercion. But they will resist more credibly if their own procedures are fair, transparent, and serious.

Gourevitch’s broader diagnosis of the university is also right: boards are too powerful, faculty governance too weak, donor influence too pervasive, and student life too bureaucratized. The crisis over protest has exposed institutional rot. Presidents who spent years issuing statements on every public controversy found themselves unable to articulate principled neutrality. Offices built to manage harm lacked the conceptual tools to defend liberty. Trustees treated universities as brands to protect rather than communities to govern.

Still, a university cannot be governed by maximalist protest rights alone. It must protect dissent and continuity at once. It must allow students to disrupt ordinary complacency without allowing them to make ordinary education impossible. It must take fear seriously without letting fear rule. It must punish misconduct without persecuting movements.

The right standard is not hostility without limits. It is liberty under fair rules. Universities need fewer emergency decrees, fewer police raids, fewer donor-driven investigations, and fewer therapeutic abstractions. They need stable norms that everyone can understand before the next crisis arrives.

If they do not build those norms now, the next wave of protest—whether over Gaza, climate change, abortion, immigration, policing, or something not yet visible—will produce the same cycle: administrative improvisation, accusations of selective enforcement, outside political pressure, excessive discipline, and deeper distrust. Gourevitch is right that democracy requires discomfort. But democracy also requires institutions capable of channeling conflict without either smothering it or surrendering to it. That is the work universities have avoided for too long.
