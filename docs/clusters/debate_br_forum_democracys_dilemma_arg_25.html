"The primary threats to American democracy are long-standing structural and analog issues—such as voter suppression, gerrymandering, and the influence of money in politics—rather than the novel digital information vulnerabilities emphasized by Farrell and Schneier."

It is easy to be seduced by the drama of information warfare. Russian hackers, viral disinformation, deepfakes—these make for gripping headlines and lend themselves to the language of cybersecurity. Farrell and Schneier’s essay is a sophisticated example of this tendency, recasting democratic decay as a failure to manage information flows and proposing solutions drawn from systems engineering. But this focus is disproportionately concentrated on the novel and the cybernetic, while the most serious threats to American democracy remain stubbornly analog. Malapportioned legislatures, racialized voter suppression, campaign finance corruption, and an erosion of civil society organizations do far more daily damage than bots and trolls. By elevating informational vulnerabilities to the center of democratic theory, the authors risk diverting scarce political energy into technical reforms that, at best, tinker at the margins and, at worst, create new obstacles to democratic participation.

The concept of Democracy’s Dilemma is appealing, but it obscures the fact that democracies have always faced a version of this tension. The Federalist Papers warned about the dangers of faction and demagoguery; propaganda, yellow journalism, and paid misinformation are as old as mass media itself. What has supposedly changed is the scale and efficiency of manipulation enabled by digital technologies. Yet the evidence that these new tools are systematically destabilizing advanced democracies is, as of 2019, far from conclusive. The 2016 election saw Russian efforts that were certainly alarming, but their actual effect on voting behavior remains debated among political scientists. The vast majority of voters’ opinions are shaped less by obscure Facebook ads than by long‑standing party loyalties, local economic conditions, and the messaging of mainstream media. The notion that the American body politic is an “attack surface” easily exploitable by a few thousand rubles’ worth of ads risks giving Russian intelligence too much credit and stripping American voters of their agency.

The three examples the authors provide actually illustrate the opposite: that informational problems are inseparable from deeper power struggles. The Doug Jones manipulation was a domestic campaign tactic, not a foreign assault. Political campaigns have always experimented with underhanded methods; if anything, the Internet has made it easier to expose them, as the quick reporting on the fake Facebook page showed. The FCC’s comment fraud was a weapon deployed by corporate lobbyists, a symptom of the pervasive influence of money in politics. The census citizenship question is a partisan power play, one of many attempts by the Republican Party to entrench its advantage in a demographically changing country. In each case, the “informational” dimension is secondary to the raw exercise of political power. To solve these problems, we need to redistribute power, not perfect our comment systems.

Farrell and Schneier’s proposed institutional reforms reflect this misdiagnosis. Paper ballots and post‑election audits are sensible and widely supported; they are not novel ideas born of the cybersecurity age but standard practices in many mature democracies. Endorsing them is hardly a new framework. The more distinctive proposals—commenter authentication, enforcement against deceitful political communication, insulating the census from political interference—are either impractical or dangerous. Authentication systems would require a national digital identity scheme, which carries enormous privacy risks and would almost certainly be used to suppress turnout among marginalized communities, mimicking the effects of strict voter ID laws. Attempts to police political deception would run headlong into the First Amendment; our courts have long recognized that the cure for falsehood is more speech, not government regulation of truth. And while insulating the census from partisan manipulation sounds admirable, it is impossible to depoliticize an inherently political process. The census is a fight over resources and representation; the solution is to win that fight through democratic mobilization, not to hand it to an unaccountable technocratic body.

What, then, are the real negative feedback loops that could pull democracy back from the brink? The essay gestures toward them but never fully commits. The most powerful equilibrating force in a democracy is electoral accountability: the ability of voters to throw out rascals. That mechanism is currently broken not because of misinformation, but because of deliberate institutional design. Partisan gerrymandering means that most House seats are safe for one party, freeing representatives from the need to appeal to the broader public. The Senate gives wildly disproportionate power to rural, predominantly white states. The Electoral College can override the popular vote. Voter ID laws, purges of voter rolls, and the disenfranchisement of ex‑felons systematically exclude millions from the franchise. Add to this the Supreme Court’s dismantling of campaign finance regulations, which allows a tiny plutocratic class to dominate political spending, and you have a system that has been meticulously rigged long before any Russian bot entered the chat.

No amount of information security wizardry can compensate for these structural deficits. If we want to reduce mutual distrust and the sense that the game is rigged, we should make the game clearly fair. That means automatic voter registration, a constitutional amendment to abolish the Electoral College, independent redistricting commissions, public financing of elections, and statehood for Washington, D.C. and Puerto Rico. It means aggressive enforcement of the Voting Rights Act, which was gutted by the Supreme Court in 2013. These are familiar items on the democratic reform agenda, but they have been sidelined in the recent frenzy about information warfare. Farrell and Schneier’s essay, for all its subtlety, inadvertently reinforces that sidelining by implying that the new, tech‑enabled threats require a fundamentally new kind of expertise.

The real danger is not that we will ignore the “attack surface” of democracy, but that we will pour our energies into cybersecurity while the ancient pillars of self‑government crumble. The Internet may have created new avenues for mischief, but the cure for democracy’s ills remains what it has always been: more democracy. Let us focus our attention there, and not be dazzled by the latest digital fears.
