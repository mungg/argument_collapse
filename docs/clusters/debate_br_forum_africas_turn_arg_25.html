"The true driver of Africa's development is the internal agency and grassroots creativity of ordinary Africans rather than the external policy interventions and macroeconomic factors emphasized by Edward Miguel."

Edward Miguel’s essay, for all its careful balance, remains a view of Africa from a distance — a sympathetic but ultimately external gaze that sees the continent as a problem to be solved by better policies, more sensitive aid, or more responsible trade. Missing from this frame is the most powerful force now reshaping the continent: the agency of ordinary Africans themselves, expressed not so much in national growth rates or Freedom House scores as in the inventive, provisional, and often informal ways they are building livelihoods, communities, and new cultures. If there is an African turn, it is being made by city dwellers in Lagos, smallholder farmers pooling savings in a Kenyan village, and young producers in Nollywood and the music studios of Accra. These stories do not always show up in the macro data, but they are the continent’s true pulse.

Consider the informal economy, which Miguel entirely ignores. Across sub-Saharan Africa, the vast majority of urban workers are self-employed or working in micro-enterprises that operate beyond formal regulation. Street vendors, motorcycle taxi drivers, hair braiders, scrap-metal dealers — these are often dismissed as “survivalist” activities, but they are also sites of remarkable dynamism. In many countries, the informal sector is where new technologies are first adopted, where credit innovations appear, and where young people acquire skills. The spread of mobile phones, which Miguel mentions in the context of grain markets, is intimately linked to the informal sector. M-Pesa, the mobile money transfer service launched in Kenya in 2007, is a case in point. Within a year, it has signed up millions of customers, enabling even unbanked rural dwellers to send and receive money, pay bills, and save small sums. This is not a government program or a World Bank project; it is a commercial innovation that works because it meshes with the way people actually live — in networks of mutual support, moving constantly between city and countryside. M-Pesa is just one example of a broader phenomenon: African adaptation and creativity in the face of failing formal institutions.

That creativity extends to culture. Nollywood, Nigeria’s film industry, now produces more movies each year than Hollywood, employing thousands of actors, directors, and technicians, and generating a homegrown aesthetic that circulates across the continent and its diaspora. The music scene, from Congolese soukous to South African kwaito to Ghanaian hiplife, not only provides livelihoods but also shapes political commentary and social critique. These cultural industries are part of a broader re-imagining of what it means to be African in the twenty-first century — an imagination that refuses to be defined by poverty, war, or aid dependency.

Miguel worries that Africa’s growth is not driven by manufacturing transformation, echoing a classic development narrative. But that narrative itself may be dated, rooted in the experience of East Asia in a different technological era. Africa’s path might bypass the factory floor and leapfrog into services, creative industries, and high-value agriculture linked to global niche markets. The growing demand for organic and fair-trade products, for instance, is opening spaces for African cooperatives. The diaspora, with its significant financial remittances and growing interest in investing back home, is another force for change that does not depend on World Bank conditionality. In 2007, remittances to Africa were estimated at over $30 billion, far outstripping foreign aid, and they flow directly into households. That money builds houses, pays school fees, and funds small businesses, often informed by local knowledge that outside experts lack.

This is not to romanticize poverty or ignore the very real constraints Miguel identifies: climate vulnerability, conflict, and the depredations of authoritarian leaders. But it is to insist that the central protagonists of Africa’s story are Africans, not Chinese investors, western aid donors, or even well-intentioned academics. The development that endures is the development that grows from within, from the countless daily choices of people who are already proving that they can build viable, modern lives under immensely difficult conditions. Policymakers in Washington, London, and Beijing would do better to listen to those choices and support them, rather than trying to design the future from on high.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The informal economy is a site of remarkable dynamism and technological adaptation that provides livelihoods and innovation outside of formal state regulation.
  2. 02Commercial innovations like M-Pesa demonstrate how African creativity solves problems by aligning with the social networks and practical needs of the population.
  3. 03The rise of homegrown cultural industries like Nollywood and diverse music scenes reflects a self-determined African identity that rejects narratives of aid dependency.
  4. 04Africa may bypass traditional manufacturing-led growth by leapfrogging into services, creative industries, and high-value niche agriculture.
  5. 05Financial remittances from the African diaspora provide a more direct and effective source of development capital than traditional foreign aid.
  6. 06Sustainable development must be rooted in the internal choices and local knowledge of Africans rather than top-down designs from international powers.
