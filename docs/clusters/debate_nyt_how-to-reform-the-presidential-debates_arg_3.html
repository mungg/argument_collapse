Diversified Claude

"To improve the quality of presidential debates, the current moderator-driven format should be replaced with a Lincoln-Douglas style structure that prioritizes direct candidate exchange over media-driven spectacle."

The simplest fix to the current debate format would be the most radical: get rid of the moderators. Or rather, demote them. The role of a moderator should be that of a timekeeper and traffic cop, not a co-star. What has made the early Republican debates feel less like policy seminars and more like reality television is not the candidates so much as the gotcha questions, the prosecutorial framing, and the moderators' obvious hunger to generate viral moments.

Replace this with a Lincoln-Douglas style format. Pair candidates off. Give each one ten minutes to lay out a position on a single issue — taxes, immigration, ISIS — and the opponent ten minutes to rebut. Then a free exchange. No bell, no buzzer, no anchor asking whether Donald Trump thinks Carly Fiorina has a pretty face.

Yes, this would be less entertaining. Ratings would fall. CNN and Fox would hate it. But the parties — not the networks — control whom they sanction as official debates, and there is no reason a serious party committed to governing should outsource its candidate selection process to a medium whose business model depends on conflict. If the RNC and DNC want substance, they have the leverage to demand it. So far, they have not used it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current debate format suffers because moderators prioritize viral moments and confrontational framing over substantive policy discussion.
  2. 02A Lincoln-Douglas format would allow candidates sufficient time to present detailed positions and rebuttals on specific issues without constant interruption.
  3. 03Political parties should use their leverage to reclaim control of the debate format from television networks that prioritize ratings over governance.
  4. 04Demoting moderators to the role of simple timekeepers would eliminate the 'gotcha' questions that turn debates into reality television.
Diversified Claude

"The presidential debate format should be reformed to allow candidates to question each other directly while minimizing the role of moderators."

Let the candidates question each other.

Strip out the moderator panel — or reduce it to one neutral timekeeper — and instead give each candidate, in turn, five minutes to question another candidate of their choosing. The questioner controls the topic. The respondent must answer. Then they switch.

This format would accomplish several things at once. It would surface the genuine policy disagreements within each party, which moderators often miss because they are looking for theatrical conflict rather than substantive difference. It would test the candidates' actual command of policy detail, because a rival who has spent months preparing on health care can ask sharper questions than a journalist with twenty other things to research. And it would expose evasion in real time: a candidate who refuses to answer a direct question from a peer cannot hide behind a moderator's willingness to move on.

The Republican debates this year have produced their most illuminating moments when candidates have engaged each other directly — Christie scolding Paul on surveillance, Bush and Rubio over Senate attendance. These exchanges happened despite the format, not because of it. Build the format around them.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Direct questioning would highlight genuine policy disagreements that moderators often overlook in favor of theatrical conflict.
  2. 02A peer-to-peer format tests a candidate's command of policy detail more effectively than questions from journalists.
  3. 03Allowing candidates to control the questioning prevents rivals from using evasion tactics that moderators typically permit.
  4. 04The most illuminating moments of past debates have occurred during spontaneous direct engagement rather than through structured moderator questions.
Diversified GPT

"Presidential debates should be restructured to focus on direct candidate-to-candidate debate over specific policy motions rather than moderator-led questioning."

The best reform would be to make the candidates debate one another, not the moderators.

Much of the present format is built around the moderator as prosecutor. This produces some useful moments, but it also encourages candidates to complain about biased questions, to attack the press and to avoid sustained engagement with rivals. The moderator asks, the candidate pivots, the next candidate attacks, the moderator moves on. Nobody is forced to defend a claim for more than a few seconds.

A more substantive format would borrow from parliamentary debate. The evening would be divided into several motions: “The United States should provide a path to legal status for most undocumented immigrants”; “The next president should increase ground operations against ISIS”; “Tax reform should be revenue neutral”; “Social Security benefits should be reduced for future retirees.” Candidates would be assigned to speak for or against positions consistent with their stated views, and then they would cross-examine one another.

This would clarify real differences. Many candidates can say they favor “strong borders,” “growth,” “peace through strength” and “entitlement reform.” Those phrases conceal more than they reveal. A motion forces the question: yes or no? If a candidate rejects the wording, he or she must explain why. That explanation itself is substantive.

The moderator’s job would be narrower but more important: enforce time, require answers, and prevent filibustering. Instead of asking clever “gotcha” questions, the moderator would ensure that the exchange stays tied to the motion. Candidates would have opening statements, rebuttals and brief cross-examination periods.

This format is especially valuable in primaries, where the policy differences inside a party are often understated. Republican voters deserve to know which candidates favor a more interventionist foreign policy and which are more restrained; which would cut taxes even if deficits rise and which would not; which oppose any legalization of unauthorized immigrants and which allow exceptions. Democratic voters deserve the same kind of clarity in their debates.

Television producers may fear that structured argument is dull. They are wrong. Real disagreement is more compelling than rehearsed one-liners. The drama of politics should come from conflict over choices, not from who can produce the sharpest insult.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current moderator-as-prosecutor format encourages candidates to evade questions and attack the press rather than engage with their rivals' positions.
  2. 02Adopting a parliamentary style with specific policy motions would force candidates to move beyond vague slogans and clarify their actual stances on complex issues.
  3. 03A structured debate format would empower moderators to act as neutral enforcers of time and relevance rather than sources of 'gotcha' questions.
  4. 04Primary voters would benefit from a format that highlights the nuanced policy disagreements that exist within a single political party.
  5. 05Substantive policy conflict is more inherently compelling for a television audience than rehearsed insults and one-liners.
Diversified Gemini

"To improve the substance of presidential debates, journalists should be removed as moderators and replaced with a format where candidates question each other directly."

We complain about the lack of substance in debates, but we ignore the root of the problem: the moderators. In their quest to create compelling television, media personalities dominate the stage, asking questions designed to trap candidates, provoke bickering, or generate viral clips for the next day's news cycle. The candidates are merely reacting to the media's agenda.

If we want a substantive exchange of ideas, we should get the journalists out of the way and let the candidates question each other.

Imagine a format where the first hour consists of a round-robin. Candidate A gets three minutes to ask a question of Candidate B and debate the response. Then Candidate B questions Candidate C, and so on.

This format would reveal far more about a candidate's priorities and depth of knowledge than any journalist's question could. What a candidate chooses to ask their opponent tells us what they believe is the most critical issue facing the nation. When forced to cross-examine a rival, candidates would have to rely on their own grasp of policy details, exposing who has done their homework and who is relying on superficial talking points.

Furthermore, this removes the "media bias" excuse that candidates frequently use to deflect difficult questions. If a conservative candidate is grilled by a fellow conservative on their tax plan, they cannot brush it off as a liberal media attack; they must answer the substance of the critique. True political leadership requires the ability to confront opponents directly, defend one's record, and argue a case. Let the candidates run the debate, and we will finally see who is truly prepared for the Oval Office.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Current moderators prioritize viral moments and conflict over substantive policy discussion.
  2. 02The questions a candidate chooses to ask reveal their true priorities and depth of knowledge more effectively than media-driven questions.
  3. 03A peer-to-peer format forces candidates to demonstrate a personal grasp of policy details rather than relying on prepared talking points.
  4. 04Direct cross-examination eliminates the ability for candidates to deflect difficult questions by claiming media bias.
Diversified GPT

"To make presidential debates more substantive, the format should be restructured to encourage direct, adversarial conflict between candidates rather than relying on stage-managed moderator questions."

The mistake is assuming that “substantive” means “orderly.” Some of the most revealing moments in presidential debates occur when the format breaks open: when one candidate challenges another’s record, when a rehearsed talking point meets a hostile question, when a politician must decide whether to interrupt, concede or counterpunch.

So I would not try to turn the primary debates into graduate seminars. I would make them more genuinely adversarial. Moderators should ask fewer questions, not more. Give each candidate a set amount of time to question rivals directly. A governor who claims executive experience should be pressed by a senator who thinks the record is inflated. A hawk on Iran should be challenged by a noninterventionist. A tax-cutter should have to answer another Republican who asks which deductions will disappear.

The current “circus” is unsatisfying partly because it is a fake circus: crowded, noisy, but still tightly stage-managed by television producers looking for viral moments. A better circus would force actual conflict over ideas. Not “raise your hand if you will support the nominee.” Not “say something nice about the person next to you.” Instead: “You have accused Senator X of supporting amnesty. Ask him your question.” Then let the answer and rebuttal run long enough for viewers to see whether there is anything behind the accusation.

This approach has risks. Candidates may grandstand. The loudest personality may dominate. But those things already happen. At least in a more open format, the grandstanding would be tested by rivals who know the weaknesses in one another’s records.

Voters do not need candidates to recite white papers. They need to see how candidates think under pressure, whether they understand the consequences of their own proposals, and whether they can defend their beliefs against serious opposition. Politics is conflict. A debate that hides that fact in the name of decorum is not more substantive; it is merely duller.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Substance in debates is revealed through spontaneous moments of pressure and challenge rather than orderly, academic-style discussions.
  2. 02Moderators should facilitate direct cross-examination by allowing candidates to question one another's specific policy records and ideological positions.
  3. 03Extending the time for rebuttals would allow viewers to discern whether a candidate's accusations or defenses have actual merit.
  4. 04An open, adversarial format better tests a candidate's ability to defend their beliefs and think under pressure than a traditional moderated Q&A.
  5. 05The current debate format fails because it prioritizes artificial decorum and viral moments over the inherent and necessary conflict of politics.
