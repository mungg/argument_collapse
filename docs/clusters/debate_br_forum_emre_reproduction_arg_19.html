"While artificial womb technology offers the potential to liberate people from the physical burdens of gestation, it must be governed by a feminist legal framework to prevent it from being weaponized by the state to erode abortion rights and reproductive autonomy."

The return of the artificial womb in feminist argument is both thrilling and dangerous. Thrilling, because gestation is work—difficult, risky, painful work—and any technology that could reduce coerced pregnancy or distribute reproductive labor more fairly deserves serious attention. Dangerous, because the artificial womb has always tempted lawmakers, doctors, and moralists to imagine that pregnancy can be separated from pregnant people. The lead essay understands this history. Its opening account of incubators, ectogenesis, and Sherri Finkbine reminds us that the same device can be described as lifesaving, emancipatory, or coercive depending on who controls it.

That question of control is especially urgent in 2018. Abortion rights are under siege. States continue to pass waiting periods, clinic regulations, mandatory ultrasounds, gestational bans, and restrictions justified by dubious concern for women’s health. The Supreme Court has protected abortion in Whole Woman’s Health v. Hellerstedt, but the retirement of Justice Kennedy has made the future of Roe and Casey uncertain. In this context, artificial wombs are not merely speculative. Even partial ectogenesis—the ability to sustain extremely premature fetuses outside the body—could alter legal and moral arguments about viability. The technology need not be perfected to be politically weaponized.

Abortion law has long treated viability as a threshold. Before viability, the state’s ability to prohibit abortion is limited; after viability, the state may regulate more aggressively, provided exceptions remain for the pregnant person’s life and health. But viability is not a fixed biological fact. It depends on technology, hospital resources, geography, and medical judgment. A fetus “viable” in a major neonatal intensive care unit may not be viable elsewhere. If artificial wombs eventually move viability earlier, anti-abortion advocates will argue that abortion is no longer necessary: simply transfer the embryo or fetus to an external womb. This argument will be presented as a compromise. It is not.

The right to end a pregnancy is not only the right to avoid raising a child. It is the right to stop gestating. It is the right not to have one’s body used by another. But it is also the right not to become a genetic parent against one’s will. Artificial wombs might address the first interest if transfer were safe, accessible, and noncoercive. They do not automatically address the second. A person who seeks an abortion may not want a child genetically related to them existing in the world, perhaps because of rape, abuse, poverty, health, age, family circumstances, or simply because they do not want to reproduce. The state should not be able to say: we have removed the fetus from your body, so your autonomy problem is solved.

The lead essay’s claim that all reproduction is assisted helps clarify the issue. Assistance is not neutral. An incubator assists a premature infant and parents who want that infant to live. An artificial womb used after miscarriage or extreme prematurity could be an extraordinary gift. But an artificial womb used to deny abortion would assist the state in commandeering reproduction. The moral status of the machine depends on whether it expands or contracts the freedom of the person whose body began the pregnancy.

This is why Firestone remains both useful and insufficient. She was right to see that pregnancy is not sacred merely because it is biological. The idea that women must discover liberation in nausea, risk, pain, and dependency has always been suspect. If ectogenesis could make gestation optional, it might transform gender relations. It might allow women who cannot safely carry pregnancies to have genetically related children. It might allow gay male couples, trans women, and people without uteruses to participate in reproduction in new ways. It might reduce the demand for commercial surrogacy. It might make premature birth less deadly.

Yet Firestone underestimated the state and the market. Technologies do not arrive “in the right hands” by default. They arrive through research funding, patents, hospitals, insurance codes, religious lobbying, court cases, and profit. In the wrong hands, ectogenesis could become a tool for fetal personhood, embryo rescue, and maternal surveillance. Pregnant people could be pressured to undergo risky extraction procedures rather than abortion. Miscarriages could become scenes of legal investigation: could the fetus have been transferred? Did the pregnant person act quickly enough? Did she harm a fetus that technology might have saved? Poor women, women of color, immigrants, and drug users would likely bear the brunt of this scrutiny.

We already know how this works. Pregnant women have been prosecuted for drug use, stillbirths, and alleged harm to fetuses. Hospitals have sought court orders for cesarean sections. Women have been shackled during labor in prisons. The ideology of fetal protection has never been evenly applied; it intensifies where women have the least power. An artificial womb in such a legal culture could become not a liberation from reproductive labor but an expansion of reproductive policing.

A feminist legal framework for ectogenesis should therefore be established before the technology matures. First, no embryo or fetus should be transferred to an artificial womb without the informed and ongoing consent of the pregnant person. Consent must include the right to refuse extraction, especially if the procedure carries any medical risk. Second, the availability of artificial gestation should not limit the right to abortion. If a person wants to terminate a pregnancy and does not consent to genetic parenthood, the state should not impose it. Third, parentage rules must be clear. If artificial gestation is used by intended parents, law should recognize them. If it is proposed after an unwanted pregnancy, the pregnant person must not be saddled with legal or financial obligations against their will.

Fourth, public funding and access matter. If artificial wombs are available only to the wealthy, they will deepen reproductive inequality. If they are publicly funded only for fetal rescue but not for maternal health, queer family formation, or infertility, they will reflect anti-abortion priorities rather than reproductive justice. Fifth, research ethics must include women, queer people, trans people, disability advocates, neonatologists, abortion providers, and reproductive justice organizations. The governance of reproductive technology cannot be left to inventors and judges.

The lead essay also invites us to rethink the rhetoric of “saving babies.” Incubators did save premature infants. Neonatal intensive care is one of modern medicine’s great achievements. But the phrase can obscure the pregnant person, the parents, and the conditions into which a child is born. Saving a fetus by coercing a woman is not a feminist triumph. Saving a premature infant while denying the family paid leave, health care, or disability support is incomplete. A politics that values life must value the social world that sustains life after birth.

There is a promising version of artificial womb technology. It would be used to help wanted pregnancies when gestation becomes dangerous or impossible. It would reduce maternal mortality. It would support premature infants without turning pregnant people into legal containers. It would be governed by consent, equality, and public accountability. It would be one reproductive strategy among many, not a weapon against abortion.

But there is also a nightmare version. In that version, anti-abortion legislatures declare that no pregnancy need be terminated because technology can preserve fetal life. Employers and insurers prefer extraction to maternity leave. Courts treat embryos as patients with independent rights. Women who miscarry are interrogated about whether they failed to seek artificial gestation. The natural womb is romanticized until technology can be used to bypass the woman; then technology is suddenly sacred.

“All reproduction is assisted” is true. But assistance can become domination when the assisted party is not the one who asked for help. Feminism should welcome technologies that free people from forced reproductive labor. It should fight, with equal force, any technology used to make reproductive labor more compulsory. The artificial womb will not decide between those futures. Politics will.
