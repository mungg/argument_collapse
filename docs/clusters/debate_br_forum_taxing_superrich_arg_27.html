"The proposed radical wealth tax is an economically destructive and morally flawed policy based on a zero-sum misunderstanding of wealth creation that would stifle innovation and undermine property rights."

Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman’s essay on taxing the superrich rests on a deeply flawed, zero-sum view of the American economy. In their telling, extreme wealth is inherently the result of "rent extraction," an "unmerited accumulation," and a toxic force that impoverishes the rest of society. To cure this perceived disease, they propose a "radical" wealth tax designed not merely to raise revenue, but to actively confiscate and destroy the fortunes of the most successful entrepreneurs in the country. This approach is economically ruinous, morally questionable, and fundamentally misunderstands how wealth is created and sustained in a free-market capitalist system.

The foundational error in Saez and Zucman's argument is the assumption that wealth is a fixed pie, and that the billions held by Jeff Bezos, Bill Gates, or Mark Zuckerberg were somehow taken from the middle class. This ignores the reality of economic value creation. Jeff Bezos did not become a centibillionaire by stealing from his workers or his customers; he became wealthy because he built a logistics and technology network that revolutionized global commerce, creating hundreds of billions of dollars in consumer surplus. The market value of Amazon stock—which constitutes the vast majority of his wealth—is simply the collective estimate of millions of investors regarding the future value the company will produce.

When Saez and Zucman argue for a 10 percent annual wealth tax on billionaires, they are not asking the wealthy to pay a fair share of their income; they are demanding the forced liquidation of the underlying productive assets of the American economy. A wealth tax does not tax consumption or idle cash; it taxes capital. And capital is the lifeblood of economic growth, innovation, and wage gains. When you tax capital heavily, you reduce the incentive to invest, save, and build.

Consider the practical implications of a 10 percent annual wealth tax on a successful entrepreneur. If a founder builds a company that becomes wildly successful, they will not have enough liquid cash to pay a 10 percent tax on the paper value of their stock. They will be forced to sell a significant portion of their ownership stake every year just to pay the IRS. Over a decade, the founder will lose control of the company they built. Control will be transferred from the visionary entrepreneurs who created the value to Wall Street asset managers, index funds, or foreign investors who buy the liquidated shares. Punishing success by forcing founders to surrender control of their enterprises is a surefire way to kill the entrepreneurial dynamism that makes the United States the world's leading economic engine.

The authors seem to recognize this liquidity problem, but their proposed solution reveals the startling radicalism of their agenda. They suggest that if an owner of a private company cannot pay the tax in cash, the government should allow them to pay "in kind"—handing over shares of their business to the federal government. The IRS would then sell these shares to the highest bidder.

This proposal is breathtaking in its economic recklessness. It essentially calls for the creeping nationalization of American private enterprise. The federal government would become the largest shareholder in thousands of private businesses, constantly intervening in the market to auction off corporate equity. The authors blithely assume that a robust market will magically appear to purchase these government-held shares. In reality, forced sales by the state will result in massive fire-sale discounts, destroying shareholder value and disrupting corporate governance. Furthermore, what happens during an economic downturn, when there are no buyers for these private shares? Does the government simply hold onto them, gradually accumulating controlling stakes in the nation's largest private industries? This is a recipe for state-directed capitalism, not a free and prosperous market economy.

Saez and Zucman also draw a false equivalence between the "quasi-confiscatory" income tax rates of the mid-twentieth century and their proposed wealth tax. It is true that top marginal income tax rates were very high in the 1950s, but effective rates were much lower due to a labyrinth of deductions and loopholes. More importantly, high income taxes encourage wealthy individuals to reinvest their earnings back into their businesses rather than taking high salaries or dividends, which actually spurs corporate growth. A wealth tax does the exact opposite: it taxes the accumulated capital itself, draining resources out of the business regardless of whether it is profitable or reinvesting in its future.

The authors also lean heavily on James Madison, selectively quoting him to suggest the Founders would have supported confiscatory wealth taxes. But Madison and the Founders were fiercely protective of property rights. The "inequality of property" Madison warned against was primarily the result of government-granted monopolies and aristocratic privilege, not the hard-earned wealth of self-made entrepreneurs in a competitive market. A true Madisonian approach to inequality would focus on eliminating crony capitalism, regulatory capture, and corporate subsidies, not imposing punitive taxes on the fruits of free enterprise.

If we want to improve the lot of the working and middle classes, the answer is not to tear down the wealthy. Leveling down does not lift up. We should focus on policies that promote robust economic growth, increase workforce participation, and improve education and skills training. We can debate the proper level of income or capital gains taxes, but a punitive wealth tax designed to actively destroy capital formation is a dangerous ideological experiment. A society that views success as a negative externality to be taxed out of existence will soon find itself with far fewer innovators, less capital investment, and a stagnant economy that harms everyone, most of all the vulnerable workers Saez and Zucman claim to champion.
