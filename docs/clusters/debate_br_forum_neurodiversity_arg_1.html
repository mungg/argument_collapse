"The neurodiversity movement must evolve into a pluralist disability politics that balances the pursuit of social power with an honest acknowledgment of profound medical impairment and the necessity of accountable care."

The neurodiversity movement has done something morally necessary: it has taught many people to stop treating autism, ADHD, dyslexia, Tourette’s, and other neurological differences as shameful deviations from a single proper way to be human. Robert Chapman rightly honors that achievement. “Don’t Mourn for Us” remains powerful because Jim Sinclair named a wound inflicted by pity: when parents say they wish autism would disappear, autistic children may hear that they themselves should disappear. A politics that begins by refusing that message is indispensable.

But Chapman’s essay, while careful in places, still moves too quickly from the limits of liberal neurodiversity to a sweeping politics of Neurodivergent Power. What is missing is sustained attention to people for whom neurodivergence is not only a stigmatized identity or a mismatch with capitalist norms, but also a source of profound suffering, impairment, danger, and dependence. Any future for neurodiversity must be honest about them. If it is not, the movement will reproduce the very exclusion it condemns.

Consider the autistic person who cannot communicate pain in conventional speech and therefore goes years with untreated medical problems. Consider the teenager with intellectual disability and epilepsy whose seizures are not socially constructed. Consider the adult who smears feces, bangs his head against walls, or bolts into traffic. Consider the person with bipolar disorder who, during mania, destroys relationships and finances, or the person with obsessive-compulsive disorder trapped for hours in rituals they despise. Consider schizophrenia when voices command terror, or severe ADHD when impulsivity leads repeatedly to accidents, addiction, and jail. Environment matters enormously. Stigma matters. Poverty, racism, educational exclusion, and trauma intensify all of it. But the impairment itself also matters.

Some neurodiversity rhetoric has struggled to say this plainly. It has sometimes suggested that disability lies mostly in social barriers, or that autism is simply a “way of being,” or that medical intervention is suspect because it aims at normalization. These claims are liberating for some people and inadequate for others. Many families and disabled people are not mourning difference; they are trying to survive exhaustion, self-injury, sleeplessness, seizures, aggression, elopement, or psychosis. They may want acceptance and treatment. They may want society to accommodate their loved one and also want research that reduces suffering. They may reject eugenics and still hope for therapies that improve communication, sleep, anxiety, motor control, or dangerous behavior.

Chapman is rightly alarmed by genetic research that could facilitate selective abortion. Disability history gives every reason for vigilance. Down syndrome screening already raises painful questions about a society that claims to value disabled people while preventing many from being born. Autism biomarker research can be entangled with eugenic fantasies of prevention. But it does not follow that all biological research into autism or other neurodevelopmental conditions is suspect. Families facing epilepsy, gastrointestinal distress, severe anxiety, catatonia, or intellectual disability need knowledge. Autistic adults themselves often want better medical care, not less. The ethical line should not be between medicine and liberation. It should be between research that seeks to eliminate people and research that seeks to relieve suffering, expand autonomy, and improve quality of life under the guidance of those affected.

The same distinction applies to cure. Some neurodivergent people experience talk of cure as a threat to identity. Others would gladly cure parts of their condition if they could: panic, executive dysfunction, hallucinations, compulsions, sensory pain, suicidal depression, insomnia. A humane movement should not impose one answer. It should defend the person who says “my autism is inseparable from me” and the person who says “my disorder torments me.” It should oppose coercive normalization while supporting voluntary treatment. It should be possible to say, without betrayal, that a trait can be part of someone and still be disabling.

Chapman criticizes “superpower” narratives, and here I strongly agree. The superpower trope is not merely silly; it is a moral trap. It reassures society that inclusion is worthwhile because the included person might be unusually useful. It also lets the public focus on articulate, often white, often middle-class people whose differences can be made charming or profitable. Meanwhile people with profound intellectual disability, non-speaking autism, severe mental illness, or complex medical needs vanish. They are not easily turned into diversity consultants or innovation assets. If neurodiversity cannot speak for them, it becomes a boutique identity for the relatively advantaged.

Yet I am not convinced that anticapitalism by itself solves this exclusion. Market logic certainly devalues people who cannot produce profit. But socialist and revolutionary societies have also institutionalized, neglected, and hidden disabled people. The problem is deeper than capitalism alone: it includes fear of dependency, disgust at bodily and cognitive vulnerability, impatience with those who require time, and the human tendency to rank lives by convenience. Any political economy can fail disabled people if it does not build care into its foundation.

Care is the word that should stand beside power. Chapman calls for Neurodivergent Power, but many neurodivergent people cannot exercise power in familiar political forms. They may not attend meetings, write manifestos, vote in assemblies, or articulate demands. Some will need others to interpret their preferences, protect their interests, and provide intimate support. This reality makes activists understandably nervous, because “care” has often been used to justify control. Parents, doctors, and institutions have spoken over disabled people in the name of their best interests. But the answer to paternalism cannot be pretending that everyone can self-advocate in the same way. The answer is accountable care: supported decision-making, communication access, independent advocacy, family support, transparency, and safeguards against abuse.

Parents and caregivers also need a more honest place in neurodiversity politics. The movement emerged partly against parent organizations that portrayed autism as tragedy and autistic people as burdens. That critique was necessary. But many caregivers are not enemies of neurodivergent liberation. They are exhausted people doing unpaid or underpaid work that society refuses to share. A mother sleeping three hours a night because her child is in distress is not simply an agent of neuronormativity. A sibling worried about what happens when parents die is not promoting capitalism. A direct support worker paid poverty wages to manage complex needs is not the oppressor. A serious movement must organize them too, while insisting that the disabled person’s dignity remains central.

What would such a movement demand? First, a massive public investment in lifelong disability support: in-home services, respite, supported living, day programs that are not warehouses, communication technology, behavioral supports that are ethical and non-abusive, specialized medical care, and well-paid direct care workers. Second, a ban on cruel practices such as electric shocks, unnecessary restraint, and seclusion, coupled with real alternatives for families and schools. Third, research agendas shaped by neurodivergent people across the spectrum of support needs, including those who communicate nontraditionally. Fourth, education systems that include where possible but do not make inclusion an ideology indifferent to individual needs. Fifth, housing models that offer safety and community without recreating institutions. Sixth, protection from poverty for disabled people and caregivers alike.

Chapman’s attention to prisons, policing, and homelessness is essential. Many people with cognitive and psychiatric disabilities are punished because society has no patience for dysregulated behavior. Jails have become mental health facilities by default. Schools call police when they need specialists. Homelessness is treated as disorder rather than abandonment. Here the neurodiversity movement should join abolitionist and disability justice campaigns in demanding non-police crisis response, supportive housing, diversion, and community-based care. But again, the word “care” must be concrete. A psychotic person sleeping under a bridge is not liberated because no hospital bed confines him. A person with intellectual disability cycling through jail because no supported housing exists is not freed by anti-institutional rhetoric. Freedom requires somewhere to go.

The future of neurodiversity should therefore be neither a return to the old medical model nor a leap into a politics that treats medicine mainly as discipline and capitalism as the master explanation. It should be a pluralist disability politics capable of holding hard truths together. Neurodivergence can be identity, culture, disability, illness, gift, burden, and ordinary human variation—sometimes all in the same life. Social arrangements can disable people, and bodies and minds can disable them too. Acceptance can coexist with treatment. Pride can coexist with grief. Rights can coexist with care. Self-advocates, families, clinicians, and support workers can be allies if power is shared and abuse is confronted.

Chapman is right that we should stop asking which neurodivergent people are useful enough to include. But we should also stop asking neurodivergent people to fit a single political story. Some are oppressed primarily by stigma; some by poverty; some by institutions; some by symptoms; many by all at once. A movement worthy of them must be broad enough for the proud autistic lecturer, the non-speaking adult in supported living, the child with seizures and self-injury, the veteran with traumatic brain injury, the person hearing voices, and the caregiver afraid of the future. Its promise should not be that neurodivergence is always beautiful, or always socially produced, or always a revolutionary standpoint. Its promise should be that every life, including the most dependent and difficult, will be met with dignity, resources, and love.

"The future of neurodiversity politics should not be a choice between liberal rights and revolutionary power, but rather an additive approach that combines radical systemic critique with the practical, material infrastructure of disability policy."

Robert Chapman is right that neurodiversity has reached a point at which vocabulary can outrun transformation. A company can celebrate “different minds” while refusing flexible hours; a university can publish an inclusion statement while making disabled students fight semester after semester for the same accommodations; a school can praise autism acceptance while suspending the child whose distress is read as defiance. The language of neurodiversity is now familiar enough to be marketed, and that familiarity creates a real danger: the movement’s most palatable claims—creativity, innovation, resilience—are absorbed, while its harder demands are ignored.

But the future of neurodiversity should not be framed as a choice between “liberal” rights and revolutionary power. The people most likely to be harmed by that opposition are often the very people Chapman wants to center: those with significant intellectual disabilities, people in institutions, people who need daily support, people without income, people in prisons, people whose communication is not easily legible to professionals or activists. For them, rights are not mere liberal decoration. They can be the difference between a segregated classroom and a public education, between forced institutionalization and community living, between unemployment and supported work, between medical neglect and enforceable care standards.

The problem is not that rights-based politics has gone too far. It is that it has usually not gone nearly far enough, and that rights without material support become hollow. The Americans with Disabilities Act did not make disabled people equal, but it changed the terrain on which disabled people could fight. The same is true of special education law, Medicaid-funded home- and community-based services, supported decision-making reforms, and the Supreme Court’s Olmstead decision affirming the right to receive services in integrated settings. These are imperfect, uneven, bureaucratic, and often humiliating systems. Yet they are also tools people use to get out of institutions, keep housing, obtain communication devices, challenge exclusion, and survive.

A more radical neurodiversity politics should therefore be additive rather than dismissive. It should ask: what would it take to make rights real? The answer is not only consciousness-raising, though that matters. It is public money, trained support workers, accessible housing, universal health care, income supports that do not punish people for working or marrying, transportation, respite, peer-run crisis services, non-police emergency response, and schools designed around regulation and communication rather than compliance. These are not glamorous demands, and they do not fit easily into either corporate diversity language or romantic images of revolt. But they are the infrastructure of freedom.

Chapman’s strongest point is that the “superpower” version of neurodiversity is too narrow. It reassures employers that autism may produce good coders, ADHD may produce energetic entrepreneurs, and dyslexia may produce visionary CEOs. Sometimes such reframing helps individuals shed shame. But as a political story it fails because it makes acceptance contingent on usefulness. It leaves out the nonspeaking autistic adult who needs round-the-clock assistance, the psychotic person who does not become more employable through positive branding, the child whose learning disability is not secretly genius, the person with traumatic brain injury cycling between homelessness and jail. A movement that cannot speak honestly about dependency, pain, ambivalence, and support needs will reproduce the hierarchy it claims to oppose.

Still, in rejecting deficit narratives, neurodiversity advocates should be careful not to create a new orthodoxy in which treatment, diagnosis, or even the wish for relief is treated as betrayal. Sinclair’s insistence that autism is not separable from the person remains morally important. But many neurodivergent people experience aspects of their conditions as disabling in themselves, not only because society is hostile. Severe anxiety, self-injury, catatonia, seizures, insomnia, executive dysfunction, addiction, depression, mania, or sensory pain are not made irrelevant by the social model. A liberatory politics should defend people against coercive normalization while also defending access to wanted care. The line between support and suppression is not always simple, but the answer cannot be to deny either side of the truth.

This is where the analogy to Black Power is illuminating but limited. “Neurodivergent Power” can name collective refusal: refusal of eugenics, of segregated institutions, of poverty wages, of carceral responses to distress, of workplaces that burn people out and discard them. Yet neurodivergence is not one people in the way Black Americans have been made a people through a shared history of racial domination. It is a broad and internally varied category, spanning autism, ADHD, dyslexia, intellectual disability, Tourette’s, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, epilepsy, trauma-related conditions, and more, depending on usage. Some neurodivergent people are disabled; some do not identify that way. Some want cure for particular symptoms; others fear cure as erasure. Some can organize publicly; others need advocates, family members, or support staff to make participation possible. A politics built in their name must be unusually pluralist.

That pluralism also requires taking families and caregivers seriously without returning to parent-dominated paternalism. Early autism politics rightly challenged organizations that spoke about autistic people while excluding them. But many people with high support needs rely on circles of care, including parents, siblings, attendants, teachers, and direct support workers. Those relationships can be controlling or abusive; they can also be the condition of agency. The slogan “nothing about us without us” remains indispensable, but “us” must include people who communicate unconventionally and people whose preferences emerge through trusted relationships rather than formal meetings, manifestos, or social media.

Chapman is also right that capitalism disciplines minds and bodies through productivity. But neurodivergent liberation cannot wait for capitalism’s end, nor can it be reduced to capitalism alone. State socialist societies have also institutionalized disabled people, medicalized dissent, and valued citizens according to productivity. Families, schools, clinics, prisons, and bureaucracies can be cruel under many economic systems. The deeper target is not only the market but any social order that grants dignity conditionally: if you work, if you behave, if you speak, if you recover, if you become manageable.

The future of neurodiversity should combine the impatience of radical politics with the specificity of disability policy. It should fight eugenic research agendas and also fight Medicaid waiting lists. It should oppose police violence and also build crisis alternatives. It should criticize corporate appropriation and also help workers get accommodations tomorrow morning. It should build culture, scholarship, unions, and mutual aid, while defending the legal levers disabled people already possess.

Neurodiversity began as a refusal to mourn the existence of people whose minds diverge from the norm. Its next stage should be a refusal to ration belonging. Not only the brilliant, not only the employable, not only the articulate, not only the proudly identified, not only those whose differences can be redescribed as gifts. Everyone. That will require power, yes—but also care, law, money, patience, and institutions remade so that needing support is no longer treated as a failure to be human.

Against the Neuro

Robert Chapman wants a more radical neurodiversity politics. I want to ask whether the neurodiversity framework is the right vehicle for radical politics at all. My worry is not that Chapman is too revolutionary; my worry is that the prefix "neuro-" smuggles in exactly the kind of biologized, individualizing assumptions that a genuinely emancipatory disability politics should be trying to displace.

The neurodiversity movement made its peace, early on, with the idea that the differences it sought to defend were brain-based. "Neurological pluralism," in Harvey Blume's phrase. The framework's strategic genius was to take a medicalized vocabulary — the same vocabulary that had been used to pathologize — and reclaim it in the spirit of disability pride. Autism is in the brain, yes, but so what? The brain that produces autism is no worse than the brain that produces neurotypicality. This move did real work, and I do not want to dismiss it. But it has consequences that Chapman's essay does not fully reckon with.

First, by anchoring identity in the brain, neurodiversity discourse imports the assumptions of contemporary neuroscience — a field that is, despite its prestige, in serious methodological crisis, with replication failures across major findings and ongoing debates about the validity of its core categories. We do not actually have reliable neural signatures for autism, ADHD, dyslexia, or most of the conditions the movement defends. The "neuro" in neurodivergent is doing rhetorical work that the underlying science does not yet support. This is a problem for the movement's credibility, but more importantly it is a problem for the movement's politics. When researchers eventually pin down whatever heterogeneous biological processes underlie these categories, will the political identities survive? Will autism even remain a single thing? The history of psychiatric classification suggests it will not. Categories that seem stable today look quaint in a generation.

Second, the "neuro" framing tends to locate the difference inside the individual, even as the movement insists that disability emerges from the interaction between individuals and environments. The contradiction is structural. To say "I am neurodivergent" is to make a claim about one's brain. To say "society is neuronormative" is to make a claim about the social. The movement wants both claims at once, but the rhetorical center of gravity tends to drift toward the individual, because brains belong to people. This is why the discourse has been so easily absorbed by corporate diversity programs: the unit of analysis is already the individual employee with their distinctive cognitive profile, which is exactly the unit capitalism wants to manage.

Chapman sees this drift and tries to correct it by adding a structural anticapitalist analysis on top. But the structural analysis is grafted onto a fundamentally individualist categorical scheme, and the grafting does not take. You can see the strain in the essay's argument about Gaza. Chapman wants to describe the production of disability in Palestine as a "mass-disabling and neurodivergifying event," using the vocabulary of neurodiversity to politicize a colonial atrocity. But the violence done to Palestinians is not really illuminated by the framework of neurological difference. It is illuminated by the framework of colonialism, settler violence, and political economy. Calling Palestinians "neurodivergent" because they have been traumatized is at best a category error and at worst a kind of imperial appropriation, where a Western diagnostic vocabulary is extended over a situation it did not arise to describe and cannot really illuminate.

There is a deeper alternative available, and it is one Chapman alludes to but does not embrace. The disability studies tradition — particularly its more materialist strands, drawing on work by Marta Russell, Liat Ben-Moshe, and others — has long argued that disability is produced by political economy, that the category "disabled" is a category of those whose bodies and minds do not fit the labor demands of capital. From this angle, the question is not whether neurodivergent people deserve recognition for their cognitive differences, but how capitalism produces and discards human variation. The political subject is not the neurodivergent individual but the disabled class, in something close to a Marxist sense: those expelled from or marginalized within the wage relation by the structure of work itself.

This framework has several advantages over the neurodiversity framework. It does not require us to wait for neuroscience to validate diagnostic categories. It does not split the disabled population into the neurodivergent and the physically disabled in a way that disadvantages coalition. It does not get co-opted as easily, because its central claim — that capital itself produces disability — is harder to absorb into corporate diversity programs. And it generates political demands that are continuous with the broader left: universal income, decommodified care, public housing, decarceration, a labor movement that fights for shorter hours and a slower pace.

Chapman, I suspect, would not disagree with most of this. The concrete demands at the end of their essay are essentially these. But they arrive at these demands through the wrong door. By preserving the neurodiversity frame and trying to radicalize it, Chapman ends up with a politics that is both ambitious and oddly narrow: ambitious in its rhetorical scope, narrow in its political subject. A genuinely radical disability politics would let go of the "neuro-" prefix entirely, or at least demote it from the organizing concept to one descriptive vocabulary among several. It would organize the disabled, broadly construed, around the political economy that disables them, and would treat the question of which diagnostic categories apply to whom as secondary.

This is not to deny the importance of cognitive difference, or the lived realities that autistic, ADHD, and other neurodivergent people share with one another. It is to say that those realities are not the foundation of a political program; they are reasons to participate in one. The foundation has to be elsewhere — in the analysis of how value, labor, and care are organized, and how human variation is sorted into categories of usefulness and uselessness by an economic system that does not care about any of us.

Chapman's essay is closer to this analysis than most. But it stops short. The future it imagines is a more radical neurodiversity. The future I want is one in which the prefix "neuro-" is one tool among many, and the organizing principle is the disabled class — a class of which neurodivergent people are part, but not the center.

In Defense of Reform

Robert Chapman's essay performs a familiar move on the left: take a reform tradition that has won meaningful victories, label it "liberal" in a tone that suggests embarrassment, and then propose a more radical successor that will accomplish what the reformers could not. The successor is sketched in broad strokes — a revolutionary party, mass organization, cultural institutions, neurodiversity studies departments, an "intercommunal" politics modeled on the Black Panthers — and the suggestion is that this is what the moment requires. I am unpersuaded. The "Liberal Neurodiversity" Chapman wants to move beyond is, on the evidence Chapman themselves marshals, the most successful disability politics in American and British history. Before we declare it inadequate, we ought to be sure that what replaces it would do better, and on this question the essay is, to put it mildly, vague.

Consider what Chapman concedes. Reasonable accommodations under the ADA have transformed access to workplaces, schools, transportation, and public space for millions of people. The Autistic Self Advocacy Network helped end the practice of paying autistic workers below minimum wage and helped ban the use of electric shocks at the Judge Rotenberg Center. Cultural representations have shifted from tragedy and freakery toward more humane depictions. Sensory-friendly retail hours and airport sensory rooms now exist. Workplace coaching programs help neurodivergent adults find and keep jobs. Pride-based identity work has improved mental health outcomes for many. Each of these is a real change in a real person's life. The case against "Liberal Neurodiversity" cannot be that it has failed, because it has not failed; it has won, repeatedly, against tremendous odds. The case has to be that its victories are insufficient. And here is where the argument gets thin.

Chapman's central indictment is that the reformist movement leaves the most marginalized behind. This is true. It is also true of every successful reform movement in history. The expansion of LGBT civil rights left poor trans women of color disproportionately exposed to violence. The Civil Rights Act did not end the school-to-prison pipeline. The welfare state did not abolish poverty. Reform movements, by their nature, move outward from those best positioned to claim rights. The right question to ask is whether the alternative would actually reach the people the reformist movement has failed to reach. Chapman's answer is that a revolutionary anticapitalist movement would do so. But the historical record on revolutionary anticapitalist movements and their treatment of disabled people is, to put it gently, mixed. The Soviet Union institutionalized psychiatric patients on a massive scale and used psychiatric diagnoses against dissidents. Maoist China was hardly a paradise for the mentally ill. Cuba, often invoked as a counterexample, has its own complicated record. There is no historical example I am aware of in which a revolutionary movement organized around anticapitalism actually liberated the most cognitively marginalized people. The reformist tradition Chapman dismisses is the tradition that has, in practice, produced what improvements exist.

Then there is the question of the historical analogy. Chapman's invocation of Kwame Ture and the Black Panthers is meant to inspire, but the Black Panther Party is a peculiar model to recommend. The Panthers did real and admirable community organizing, but they were also crushed within a decade, partly by state repression but partly by their own internal contradictions, factionalism, and inability to sustain a political base. Their "revolutionary party" model did not transform American capitalism. The civil rights movement, including the legislative achievements Chapman implicitly disparages by calling them mere "antidiscrimination legislation," did more to change the actual conditions of Black life in America than the Panthers did. This is not to denigrate the Panthers' contribution to political consciousness; it is to ask whether the strategic lesson Chapman draws — that we should imitate the Panthers rather than the civil rights movement — is supported by the historical record. It is not.

I also want to push back on the framing of co-optation. Chapman writes that capitalism is "adapting to neutralize the mass of people fed up with its stifling norms and exclusions, using superficial moves toward greater inclusion." This is true. Robert Chapman is asking the right question. Start with the analogy. Chapman draws a parallel between the proposed shift from "Liberal Neurodiversity" to "Neurodivergent Power" and the shift from civil rights to Black Power in the 1960s. Black Power did not arise as a strategic upgrade to civil rights; it arose from a community with deep institutions — Black churches, HBCUs, mutual aid traditions, an actual press, decades of labor organizing, the long memory of slavery and Reconstruction — that already constituted a people in something close to the political sense. The people in a position to articulate, lead, and represent such a movement are precisely the most articulate — and they are disproportionately white, college-educated, and located in the relatively privileged sectors of the neurodivergent population. This is not a knock on them; it is a structural feature of any identity politics organized around a heterogeneous category. The Black Panthers had this problem too, but they had a community base that pulled against it. A movement built primarily around the discursive identity "neurodivergent" has no such ballast.

I think Chapman's instincts are better than the framework. The concrete demands at the end of the essay — free the incarcerated, house the homeless, end imperialist war — are demands shared with the broader socialist left. Many neurotypical workers also experience these conditions as crushing, which is why disability accommodations historically have been won most effectively by union shops with strong floors and seniority protections, not by individual rights-based claims. The path forward is to build labor power and use it for everyone — including neurodivergent workers — rather than to construct a parallel structure that mirrors capital's tendency to fragment workers along identity lines.

There is a deeper theoretical point here that I think Chapman's essay flirts with but does not quite confront. If, as Chapman suggests near the end, "neurodivergent people are not oppressed by neurotypicals; rather, both neurotypicals and neurodivergents are harmed by our market-oriented system" — then the framework of neurodivergent identity is doing a lot of work that the framework of class would do better. The reason "reasonable accommodation" gets interpreted to mean "does not interfere with profit" is that capital sets the terms. The reason intellectually disabled people die young is that public health is rationed by class. The reason the school-to-prison pipeline disproportionately captures neurodivergent kids of color is the carceral state, which is itself an instrument of racialized class control. These are problems of political economy, and identity-based mobilization can illuminate but not fundamentally challenge them. Class can.

I am not saying neurodivergence should be abandoned as a political category. I am saying it works best as a lens, a way of seeing how capitalism produces and discards certain forms of life, rather than as the organizational basis of its own movement. The strongest moments in Chapman's essay are precisely the analytic ones, where they show how the production of debility in Palestine generates profits for arms manufacturers, where they connect autistic affect to the policing of acceptable responses to atrocity, where they trace the logic of extractive abandonment through ADHD employment statistics. This is the work of theory, and it serves the work of organizing in a much wider field.

So my response to Chapman is: keep the diagnosis, keep the critique, but rejoin the broader socialist movement rather than building a parallel one. The future of neurodiversity is not a neurodivergent party. It is a labor movement, a housing movement, a tenants' movement, an abolition movement, and a socialist movement in which neurodivergent people show up as themselves and bring the analytic clarity their experience affords. That, I think, is what Black Power, at its best, actually did — it did not separate from the broader left; it pulled the broader left toward a sharper analysis of empire and capital. We can do the same, without inventing a constituency that does not yet exist.

The Inflation of Identity

There is a particular kind of essay that has become familiar over the last decade. It begins by celebrating the achievements of an identity-based movement, pivots into a critique of how those achievements have been "co-opted" by capital, and concludes by proposing that what is needed is a more radical, anticapitalist version of the same movement, modeled on the Black Panthers or some other touchstone of 1960s radicalism. Read closely, however, the essay raises questions that go beyond its own argument and bear on the broader expansion of identity categories in contemporary politics. I want to raise some of those questions, in a spirit of friendly skepticism, because I think the neurodiversity discourse is at a turning point and the direction it goes from here matters.

The first question is about the breathtaking expansion of who counts as neurodivergent. Twenty years ago, "neurodiversity" was a term used by a small number of autistic activists. Today, it covers autism, ADHD, dyslexia, dyspraxia, dyscalculia, Tourette's, bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, OCD, sometimes depression and anxiety, sometimes the effects of trauma. In some popular formulations, anyone whose brain does not produce a perfectly neurotypical experience — and who knows what that even means — can claim the identity. Diagnostic rates for autism and ADHD have risen severalfold in a generation. TikTok, as Chapman notes, has become a major vector for self-diagnosis. Whether this expansion reflects genuine progress in recognizing previously hidden conditions, a generation more attuned to its own psychology, or something more like a diagnostic fashion, is an open question. Chapman treats it as straightforwardly good. I am not so sure.

The reason I am not so sure is that political identities have a way of becoming less politically powerful as they expand. The Black Power analogy Chapman invokes is instructive here, but not in the way Chapman intends. Black identity in 1966 was rooted in a specific, historically produced social position. The Black Panthers could organize around it because it had a determinate content. "Neurodivergent" does not have a determinate content. It is a discursive category that has expanded to cover a substantial fraction of the population — by some estimates, fifteen to twenty percent of people in wealthy countries, and rising — and it includes such heterogeneous experiences that any political program built around it has to either remain extremely abstract or quietly privilege one constituency within the umbrella over others. Chapman's program does both. The abstract calls for liberation are universal; the concrete examples — autistic activists protesting eugenics research, ADHD adults navigating workplaces, autistic teenagers in Israeli military intelligence — privilege a particular kind of articulate, relatively able neurodivergent person.

The second question is about the relationship between identity and pathology. The neurodiversity movement has always wanted to have it both ways: these conditions are identities, not pathologies, and yet they should also count as disabilities for purposes of legal protection. This combination has been strategically clever — it has secured both pride and accommodations — but it is intellectually unstable, and Chapman's essay shows the strain. Chapman wants psychosis to count as a form of neurodivergence whose accommodations should include things like covering up workplace webcams to address paranoid concerns about government surveillance. Set aside whether this is a reasonable accommodation; ask instead what is being claimed. It is being claimed that paranoid delusions are not symptoms of an illness to be treated but expressions of an identity to be accommodated. Many psychiatric service users would reject this characterization of their own experience. They want their delusions to stop, not to be accommodated. The framework that treats every cognitive difference as a valuable form of human variation has trouble making sense of suffering that the person experiencing it wants gone.

This is not a small matter. It bears directly on the question of treatment. Chapman, channeling Jim Sinclair, suggests that autism cannot be "treated" without erasing the person, and the same logic is implicitly extended to other conditions. But for many people with these diagnoses, treatment is what makes their lives livable. The ADHD adult who can finally focus on medication, the bipolar patient stabilized on lithium, the schizophrenic person whose voices are reduced by antipsychotics — these people are not erased by their treatment; they are enabled by it. A framework that codes treatment as a form of normative violence does these people a disservice, and the movement's ambivalence about psychiatry has, in my view, been one of its real weaknesses.

The third question is about the political analogy itself. Chapman wants to model neurodivergent politics on Black Power and on revolutionary anticapitalist movements more generally. This is a long-running tic of contemporary identity politics — every category aspires to its own version of the civil rights movement, with itself in the lead role. But the analogy is rarely as tight as it is meant to be, and in this case it is particularly loose. Neurodivergent people, broadly defined, are not a population subject to a coherent system of domination analogous to American racial caste. They are a diagnostically heterogeneous group whose social fortunes vary enormously, including by race, class, and the specifics of their condition. To call for a revolutionary movement to overthrow "neuronormative" capitalism on their behalf is to assume a unity of interest that does not exist and to import a movement template that does not fit. The result is rhetorically heated but practically thin: lots of calls for solidarity and consciousness, very few specific institutional proposals, and a closing list of demands (free housing, decarceration, end imperialist war) that are basically the platform of the contemporary anglophone left and are not specifically about neurodiversity at all.

The fourth question — and the one I want to leave readers with — is whether the proliferation of identity categories like "neurodivergent" has been good or bad for the project of building a politics that can actually make life better for the people the category claims to represent. There is a reasonable case, which I am inclined to make, that it has been mostly counterproductive. The expansion of diagnostic categories has medicalized ordinary human variation in ways that have empowered the pharmaceutical industry and the diagnostic establishment more than it has empowered the people diagnosed. The shift from a politics of common citizenship to a politics of cognitive identity has made it harder, not easier, to build the kind of broad coalitions that produce universal goods like better schools, better public mental health services, and a more humane workplace. The "superpowers" discourse, even when intended emancipatorily, has accelerated the absorption of cognitive difference into the logic of human capital.

A different politics — one that pursued the substantive goods Chapman wants (housing, decarceration, fair labor, peace) through the framework of common citizenship and material redistribution rather than through the proliferation of identity categories — would, I suspect, do better by neurodivergent people than the movement Chapman wants to build. The neurodiversity framework was useful for a particular historical task: rescuing the dignity of autistic people from a clinical establishment that pathologized them. That task is largely done. What remains is the broader work of building decent societies, and for that work, identity categories are at best a partial tool and at worst a distraction. Chapman, gifted writer that they are, has produced an essay that asks us to double down on a framework that has reached the limits of what it can do. I would urge the opposite: let the framework recede, keep the gains, and rejoin a politics organized around what we owe one another as human beings, not as members of cognitive subgroups.

It has helped win accommodations, improve representations, and challenge cruel practices. But the person who can ask for noise-canceling headphones at a tech company is not the whole movement. The person restrained in a psychiatric ward, excluded from school, sleeping rough, cycling through jail, or dying early from preventable illness is also neurodivergent. A politics that cannot reach them is not yet a politics of liberation.

Still, the path from liberal neurodiversity to Neurodivergent Power needs more specification. “Power” can mean many things: community control, organized labor, disability justice, mutual aid, abolition, institutional redesign, or revolutionary party politics. Chapman invokes the Black Panthers and Kwame Ture to suggest a militant shift from rights to collective self-determination. The analogy is useful insofar as it reminds us that antidiscrimination law can coexist with continuing domination. But the analogy also raises questions. Black Power emerged from communities with shared histories of racialization, segregation, and colonial violence. Neurodivergence is a looser category: autism, ADHD, dyslexia, intellectual disability, epilepsy, Tourette’s, bipolarity, schizophrenia, traumatic brain injury, and dementia do not map neatly onto one shared experience or one political constituency. Some people embrace neurodivergent identity; others experience their condition primarily as illness, terror, pain, or loss. Some require quiet and predictability; others need stimulation and flexibility. Some demand freedom from institutions; others need intensive lifelong support.

This diversity is not an argument against Neurodivergent Power. It is the reason such power must be built around material guarantees rather than a single cultural identity. Neurodivergent politics should not depend on proving that we have “superpowers,” but neither should it depend on proving that all of us share the same oppression in the same way. It should start from a simpler claim: every person is entitled to the conditions necessary for a livable life, whether or not they can be productive, charming, articulate, independent, employable, or intelligible to bureaucracies.

That means Chapman’s list of demands—freeing people from prisons and psychiatric hospitals, housing for all, ending imperial wars, unionizing against neuronormativity—is a good beginning. But we should add a disability justice infrastructure that is less glamorous and just as radical: public care work paid at living wages; accessible communication supports; respite without abandonment; supported decision-making; non-police crisis response; guaranteed income; desegregated education with real staffing; assistive technology; peer-run spaces; transportation; sensory-accessible public design; and medical care that does not treat cognitively disabled people as disposable. These are not mere “services.” They are the material base of freedom.

The strongest part of Chapman’s critique is the exposure of “strengths-based” rhetoric as a double-edged sword. It is understandable that autistic people, dyslexic people, and others have wanted to contest deficit-only accounts. Many of us grew up hearing only what was wrong with us. To learn that our forms of attention, perception, memory, honesty, or pattern recognition can be valuable is not trivial. Pride can be lifesaving. But the corporate version of this message narrows dignity into utility. Autism is welcomed when it helps quality assurance. ADHD is praised when it produces entrepreneurial energy. Dyslexia is celebrated when it belongs to a CEO who can tell a triumph-over-adversity story. The person who cannot work a forty-hour week, who melts down, dissociates, hears voices, needs help bathing, or communicates through behavior rather than speech remains outside the frame.

This is why the movement should retire “superpower” as its dominant public metaphor. Not because neurodivergent people lack gifts, but because gifts are not the basis of rights. A person’s claim on the world does not increase when their difference becomes profitable. We do not need to show that autism improves innovation or ADHD increases creativity in order to deserve housing, care, love, education, and freedom from violence. Indeed, one of the deepest forms of neuronormativity is the assumption that human worth must be translated into contribution.

Chapman is also right that incarceration must be central, not peripheral, to neurodiversity politics. Schools often punish disability before they name it. Children who cannot sit still, regulate emotion, process language quickly, or comply with arbitrary authority are suspended, restrained, isolated, or handed to police. Later, prisons fill with people marked by learning disabilities, traumatic brain injuries, psychosis, addiction, ADHD, and autism. Liberal inclusion in elite workplaces does little for those trapped in this pipeline. If the movement’s public image remains the quirky coder, it will fail those most harmed by neuronormativity.

Yet I would complicate Chapman’s call to free people from psychiatric hospitals. The institution is often violent; coercive psychiatry has a long record of abuse; confinement can strip people of autonomy and community. But many families and disabled people also know crises in which there is no safe place to go. The demand should not be simply “close institutions” unless it is inseparable from building robust alternatives: housing with supports, peer respite, crisis sanctuaries, mobile teams that do not involve police, long-term accompaniment, and noncoercive treatment options. Otherwise abolition becomes a slogan borne by those with the fewest resources, while middle-class people quietly purchase private care.

A politics of Neurodivergent Power must therefore be prefigurative: it must build the world it demands. The early autistic online communities mattered because they created spaces where autistic people could speak to one another without translation through parents or clinicians. The next stage requires more durable institutions: cooperatives, unions, independent media, accessible assemblies, legal defense funds, research collectives, and schools of political education. Chapman’s call for neurodiversity studies departments is valuable, but universities are not enough. Some of the most excluded neurodivergent people will never enter a seminar room. Movement knowledge must be produced in day programs, prisons, group homes, kitchens, Discord servers, union halls, clinics, libraries, and encampments.

The question of capitalism cannot be avoided. A market society measures minds by speed, flexibility, emotional performance, and output. It creates scarcity, then requires people to prove impairment to survive scarcity. It celebrates independence while depending on underpaid care labor. It turns accommodations into individualized exceptions rather than redesigning the pace and purpose of work. In that sense Chapman’s anticapitalism is not ornamental; it follows from the problem. If neurodivergent people are valued only when we can be efficiently exploited, then liberation requires changing what value means.

But anticapitalism must also become practical enough to organize around. Many neurodivergent people need immediate protection from eviction, benefit cuts, workplace discipline, school exclusion, and medical neglect. Rights-based tools remain useful in those fights. The ADA, the Equality Act, individualized education plans, disability benefits, and antidiscrimination law are limited, but limits are not uselessness. A mature movement should not counterpose rights and power as if one must choose. Rights without power are fragile; power without rights can become rhetorical. We need both: litigation and direct action, accommodations and universal provision, pride and care, identity and class struggle.

Chapman ends with the phrase “individually, we are disabled; united, we hold a form of strength.” I would amend it only slightly. Individually, many of us are disabled by environments, institutions, economies, and sometimes by our own bodies and minds. United, we can demand not that every mind be celebrated, but that no mind be abandoned. That is the future of neurodiversity worth fighting for: not a more inclusive labor market for the exceptional few, but a society organized around interdependence, where even those who cannot be made useful are understood as fully, nonnegotiably human.
