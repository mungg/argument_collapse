Diversified DeepSeek

"While the call for a radical, anticapitalist neurodiversity movement is welcome, it risks reproducing existing inequities unless it moves beyond generic revolutionary rhetoric to explicitly center the specific material realities of anti-Blackness, settler colonialism, and intersectional oppression."

I come to Robert Chapman’s essay as a Black, autistic, and chronically ill woman—a multiply neurodivergent person who has spent years in spaces that claim to speak for me. I appreciate Chapman’s ambition to push the neurodiversity movement beyond its liberal comforts and their insistence that any liberatory politics must grapple with racial capitalism, imperialism, and the carceral state. The call to “connect the oppression of neurodivergent people to anti-imperialist, decolonial, and abolitionist efforts globally” is one I have long made from the margins of predominantly white neurodiversity activism. Yet I leave the essay with a familiar ache: the sense that my specific embodiment is being conscripted into someone else’s analogy without my consent.

Chapman invokes the Black Power movement as a historical parallel for what neurodivergent organizing might become. The comparison is not new; I’ve lost count of how many times white disabled activists have gestured toward Black liberation as a model, a metaphor, or an aspiration. But analogies can wound as much as they illuminate, and this one demands an honesty the essay doesn’t provide. When Stokely Carmichael called for Black Power, he was speaking from within a community forged by the Middle Passage, chattel slavery, Jim Crow, and ongoing state-sanctioned murder. That history produced a collective identity—not a diagnostic one—that was, and remains, a source of both profound trauma and world-making resilience. Neurodivergence, as Chapman acknowledges, is a sprawling category invented by the psy-disciplines, and while many of us have reclaimed our diagnoses as identities, the material conditions of that reclamation differ radically across race, class, and gender. To speak of “Neurodivergent Power” without first interrogating how whiteness structures the neurodiversity movement is to risk building a revolutionary house on a cracked foundation.

What do I mean by whiteness structuring the movement? Consider the public face of neurodiversity: the prominent advocates, the most-shared blog posts, the keynote speakers at neurodiversity conferences. They are overwhelmingly white, and their analyses frequently center workplace inclusion and sensory-friendly shopping hours—issues that reflect a particular class and racial position. Meanwhile, Black autistic children are still being handcuffed by school resource officers. Black mothers with mental health disabilities are having their children taken by the family regulation system. Black people in psychiatric crisis are shot by police. These are not side issues to be tacked on via a nod to “intersectionality.” They are the heart of any neurodiversity politics that claims to care about the marginalized. Chapman rightly notes that autistic people are incarcerated at twice the rate of the general population, but does not disaggregate that statistic by race. Ask yourself why. In the United States, disability and race are so intertwined that you cannot understand the school-to-prison pipeline without understanding how Black children are disproportionately labeled with “oppositional defiant disorder” and shunted into segregated special education classrooms, a modern form of ableist and anti-Black social control.

Chapman’s anticapitalist analysis, while welcome in a movement allergic to class critique, also needs a racial lens. The “extractive abandonment” that Adler-Bolton and Vierkant describe operates differently on the racialized body. The recognition of neurodivergent “superpowers” has, in practice, created a two-tier system: white and Asian neurodivergent people are sometimes admitted to the realms of “competitive advantage,” while Black and Indigenous neurodivergent people are more likely to be classified as “behaviorally disordered” and disciplined or incarcerated. The tech companies that pitch their “autism hiring initiatives” aren’t scouring the juvenile detention centers where Black boys with undiagnosed disabilities languish. They recruit from elite universities and white-dominated maker spaces. If Chapman’s Neurodivergent Power does not explicitly center a critique of anti-Blackness and settler colonialism within its revolutionary program, it will simply reproduce these inequities under a red flag.

So I am not satisfied with a generic call to “end imperialist wars.” I want to know what a neurodivergent-led antiwar movement would do about the fact that American empire has produced disabled veterans and disabled civilians abroad in racially uneven patterns, and that the U.S. psychiatric system was built in part on the dispossession of Indigenous land. I want a movement that sees the ongoing crisis of missing and murdered Indigenous women and girls as a neurodiversity issue, given the high rates of fetal alcohol spectrum disorders and intergenerational trauma in those communities. I want a movement that fights for Black trans women with ADHD and PTSD who are being killed and homeless, not abstractly, but by building the mutual aid and legal defense funds they have asked for. This is not a request to add more items to a coalitional checklist. It is a demand that we refuse to organize around “neurodivergence” as a master identity that erases the specific gravity of racism, transmisogyny, and colonization.

None of this means I reject the idea of collective power. I am a child of the Black radical tradition, and I believe in revolutionary transformation. But I believe that transformation must be built in the kitchen, not just the party hall. I have been part of Black-led, neurodivergent-centered healing justice circles that weave together ancestral practice, harm reduction, and disability justice. These spaces do not look like a vanguard party; they look like people feeding each other, naming our triggers, creating safety plans for psychotic episodes that do not involve the police. They look like the Combahee River Collective’s insistence that if Black women are free, everyone will be free—not because Black women are a revolutionary class, but because we sit at the nexus of interlocking oppressions and our liberation requires dismantling them all. Perhaps Neurodivergent Power, if it is to be worthy of the name, should follow that model: center those of us who are most impacted, not as a symbolic gesture, but by letting us set the agenda, control the resources, and define the vision. That would be a genuine departure from Liberal Neurodiversity, and a much harder one than adopting revolutionary rhetoric.

  3. 03An anticapitalist neurodiversity framework must recognize that 'extractive abandonment' and the 'superpowers' narrative operate through a racial lens that benefits white neurodivergent people while further pathologizing and disciplining Black and Indigenous bodies.
  4. 04True liberation requires moving away from 'neurodivergence' as a master identity and instead adopting a disability justice model that centers the leadership and specific needs of those at the nexus of interlocking oppressions.
  5. 05Revolutionary neurodivergent power should be modeled on the Black radical tradition of mutual aid and community care rather than the hierarchical structures of a vanguard party.
Diversified MiniMax

"While Robert Chapman correctly identifies the failures of liberal neurodiversity, a truly liberatory movement must move beyond general anti-capitalist coalitions to adopt a 'disability justice' framework that centers the leadership and specific needs of the most multiply marginalized neurodivergent people."

I want to approach Chapman's essay from the angle of disability justice, a framework developed by activists like Leah Lakshmi Piepzna-Samarasinha that centers the experiences of disabled people at the multiply marginalized intersections of race, class, gender, and disability. Chapman's essay gestures toward this framework when he discusses incarcerated neurodivergents, the Global South, and intersectionality, but I think disability justice offers resources his argument doesn't fully exploit.

The disability rights movement that Chapman's neurodiversity movement builds upon—and critiques—was itself largely shaped by a particular historical moment and constituency. The ADA and its equivalents were won primarily by and for people with visible, physical disabilities who could participate in visible, organized activism. This legacy is a strength in some ways—we have legal frameworks that would not exist without this activism—but it also means that the most marginalized disabled people have always been partially outside these frameworks. Psychiatric survivors, people with intellectual disabilities, people whose disabilities are episodic or fluctuating, people whose disabilities are seen as "too expensive" to accommodate: these groups have always faced exclusion from the movement even as they were invoked by it.

Chapman's analysis of "extractive abandonment" is valuable here. He correctly identifies how capitalist societies selectively incorporate neurodivergent people only to the extent we can be profitable. But disability justice asks us to go further and ask: what would it mean to build a movement led by the most disabled? This isn't a rhetorical question. The most marginalized neurodivergent people—institutions, prisons, psychiatric hospitals, homeless shelters—are often exactly those whom existing frameworks cannot reach or represent. A politics that centers their experiences might look very different from what Chapman proposes.

Consider abolition. Disability justice frameworks have long connected disability to carceral systems, arguing that the institutions we use to "contain" disability are part of the same apparatus of control that contains criminalized people, undocumented immigrants, and others deemed undesirable by society. Chapman's essay calls for "freeing those of us forcibly incarcerated," which I support, but disability justice asks whether "freeing" people into existing communities and housing markets is enough. Perhaps what we need is not just decarceration but the abolition of the institutions themselves—the group homes, the sheltered workshops, the psychiatric hospitals—and their replacement by genuinely supportive communities.

Similarly, disability justice centers interdependence and care. Chapman's essay, like much neurodiversity discourse, implicitly assumes that the goal is independence and productivity. But many disabled and neurodivergent people have always known that we depend on each other and on non-disabled caregivers, and that this dependence is not shameful but fundamental to human existence. A politics that centers the most marginalized neurodivergent people might center care, mutual aid, and community support rather than competitive advantage and workplace inclusion.

I don't disagree with Chapman that capitalism is the enemy. But I think we need a politics that starts from the experiences of the most marginalized, not one that tries to build coalitions at a more general level and then hopes the marginalized will be included. The structural changes Chapman calls for—free housing, decarceration, anti-imperialism—are necessary, but they will only happen if the movement is led by those most affected, not used as coalition-bait for more privileged neurodivergents. Disability justice demands nothing less.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The historical legacy of disability rights has structurally excluded psychiatric survivors and those with intellectual disabilities, necessitating a movement led by those whom existing legal frameworks cannot reach.
  2. 02True liberation requires the total abolition of carceral and 'containment' institutions—such as group homes and psychiatric hospitals—rather than merely releasing individuals into existing, unsupportive social structures.
  3. 03A radical neurodiversity politics must replace the capitalist focus on independence and productivity with a framework that centers interdependence, mutual aid, and the fundamental necessity of care.
  4. 04Structural changes like decarceration and anti-imperialism will only be achieved if the movement is led by those most affected rather than being used as rhetorical tools to build broad coalitions for more privileged neurodivergents.
