"New York should implement compulsory voting to ensure that the government reflects the entire public rather than a narrow slice of the electorate."

New York should consider the reform Americans usually dismiss before discussing seriously: compulsory voting.

The phrase sounds harsh, but the practice need not be. Australia has long required citizens to vote, with modest penalties and broad acceptance. The point is not to drag people into ideological enthusiasm. It is to treat voting the way we treat jury duty: a basic obligation of democratic citizenship. A voter should remain free to cast a blank ballot, write in a protest or choose “none of the above.” What should not be optional is participation in choosing the government.

This idea is especially relevant in New York City’s Democratic congressional primaries. In many districts, the primary effectively determines the representative. When only a tiny fraction of eligible voters participates, organized interests and habitual voters gain disproportionate power. That does not make their votes illegitimate; they followed the rules. But it does mean that the resulting government reflects a narrow slice of the public. Mandatory participation would force candidates to speak to the whole district rather than only to the most reliable primary electorate.

Compulsory voting would also change campaign strategy. Today, campaigns spend enormous energy identifying likely voters and ignoring everyone else. If most citizens were expected to vote, the incentive would shift from turnout micro-targeting to persuasion. Candidates would have to communicate with younger voters, renters, immigrants who are citizens, working-class residents and people alienated from politics. The electorate would look more like the city.

Of course, compulsory voting cannot stand alone. It would be unfair to require voting while maintaining New York’s outdated barriers. A mandate would have to be paired with same-day registration, no-excuse absentee ballots, early voting, language access and a professional Board of Elections. Election Day should be made easier, perhaps with weekend voting or a holiday. If the state asks citizens to participate, the state must make participation simple.

Critics will argue that forced voting produces uninformed ballots. But our current system does not guarantee wisdom; it guarantees overrepresentation of those with the time, habit or organizational connection to vote in low-salience elections. A broader electorate may include some poorly informed voters, but it will also include many thoughtful citizens who are now excluded by inconvenience, cynicism or the sense that politics is not meant for them. Moreover, compulsory voting would likely create demand for better voter guides, debates and civic education.

Americans tend to define freedom as the right to abstain. That right has value. But democracy also depends on shared obligations. We require taxes to fund public goods and jury service to administer justice. Representatives chosen by the public are a public good too. If New York is serious about making its elections representative, it should put compulsory voting on the table — not as a punishment, but as a civic norm.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Treating voting as a civic obligation, similar to jury duty, would force candidates to address the needs of the whole district rather than just organized interests and habitual voters.
  2. 02Mandatory participation would shift campaign strategies from turnout micro-targeting to broad persuasion, incentivizing candidates to engage with marginalized and alienated groups.
  3. 03Compulsory voting would necessitate the removal of outdated barriers to voting, such as the lack of same-day registration and no-excuse absentee ballots.
  4. 04A broader electorate would likely increase the demand for better civic education, voter guides, and debates to inform the public.
  5. 05Just as taxes and jury service are shared obligations required to maintain public goods, participation in choosing a representative government should be a requirement of citizenship.

"New York should implement compulsory voting to transform participation from an optional errand into a fundamental civic duty and ensure a more representative democracy."

The most effective way to get more New Yorkers to vote is to stop treating voting as an optional errand and start treating it as a civic duty. New York should consider compulsory voting.

That phrase sounds un-American to many ears, but the idea is neither exotic nor authoritarian. Australia has required voting for decades and regularly achieves turnout above 90 percent. The ballot is secret, and citizens may cast a blank ballot if they wish. What is required is not a government-approved opinion; it is participation in the common decision. We already require jury service, school attendance for children and taxes. Voting for the people who write the laws is at least as central to self-government.

Compulsory voting would change campaign behavior immediately. Today, campaigns spend much of their energy identifying “likely voters,” then speaking almost exclusively to them. In a low-turnout Democratic primary, that can mean older, more ideological and more organized slices of the electorate. If everyone were expected to vote, candidates would have to speak to renters as well as homeowners, young voters as well as retirees, the disengaged as well as the party regulars. A broader electorate would produce a broader campaign.

Of course, compulsory voting cannot be imposed on top of a cumbersome system. It would have to come with automatic registration, early voting, no-excuse absentee ballots, clear notices and strong language access. The state cannot fairly fine people for failing to vote if it has not made voting easy. The penalty should be modest, more like a parking ticket than a criminal sanction, and exemptions should be generous.

The common objection is that compulsory voting would bring uninformed citizens to the polls. But low-turnout elections are not necessarily models of wisdom. They overrepresent people with the time, habit or material interest to participate. A universal electorate would force schools, media organizations and campaigns to do more civic education. It might also reduce the incentive for campaigns to depress turnout through negativity, since everyone is coming anyway.

New York’s congressional primaries now allow a tiny fraction of voters to choose representatives for millions. That is legal, but it is not healthy. Democracy should ask something of citizens, and citizens should be able to expect something in return: a system that welcomes them, informs them and counts them. Compulsory voting would not solve every problem, but it would finally take political equality seriously.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Compulsory voting is a proven democratic tool used in other nations that aligns with existing civic requirements like jury duty and tax obligations.
  2. 02Mandatory participation would force political campaigns to engage with a broader, more diverse cross-section of the electorate rather than focusing only on reliable, ideological voters.
  3. 03The implementation of compulsory voting would necessitate and drive essential reforms to make the registration and voting process easier and more accessible for all citizens.
  4. 04A universal electorate would incentivize better civic education and discourage negative campaigning tactics designed to depress voter turnout.
  5. 05Moving to a compulsory system would address the unhealthy reality where a tiny fraction of voters currently determines representation for millions of residents.
