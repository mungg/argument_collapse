"The American system of checks and balances is a vital safeguard against the overreach of a progressive elite establishment and should be preserved rather than dismantled to facilitate majoritarian rule."

Lisa L. Miller’s essay reads like a perfect encapsulation of the progressive left's current identity crisis. Faced with a second Donald Trump term and an electorate that continues to reject their grand, centralizing visions, liberal academics have decided that the problem isn't their policies—it’s the Constitution itself. In "The Dead End of Checks and Balances," Miller posits that a cabal of wealthy elites uses the Constitution's veto points to thwart the progressive, socialist-lite utopia that the "mass public" supposedly craves. Her solution is to strip away the structural guardrails of our republic to impose unfiltered majoritarian rule. But Miller’s argument rests on a profound, almost willful misunderstanding of both the American electorate and the true nature of the elite establishment she claims to oppose.

Miller’s foundational premise is that "majorities of Americans across the political spectrum" want the left-wing agenda: universal healthcare, massive environmental regulation, and federal takeovers of local zoning and elections. She claims that if only the filibuster, the Supreme Court, and federalism were neutralized, a benevolent Democratic majority would sweep in and uplift the working class.

This is a coastal, faculty-lounge fantasy. It requires completely ignoring the reality of the 2024 election and the shifting demographics of the populist right. The working-class voters Miller claims are being stymied by "elites" did not vote for a massive expansion of the administrative state; they voted for Donald Trump. They voted for border security, a return to domestic manufacturing, deregulation to lower energy costs, and an end to the hyper-progressive cultural mandates radiating from Washington, D.C., and corporate boardrooms.

Miller complains about the "self-dealing and repressive actions of elites," yet she spectacularly misidentifies who those elites are today. In 2025, the institutional establishment—the legacy media, the university systems, the tech monopolies, the federal bureaucracy, and the massive philanthropic NGOs—is overwhelmingly aligned with the Democratic Party. When Miller attacks checks and balances, she is not attacking a system that protects conservative holdouts; she is attacking the only mechanisms that allow middle-American voters to resist the consolidated cultural and economic power of the progressive ruling class.

Take, for example, her frustration with the courts. Miller writes with disdain about a Supreme Court that has "weakened congressional legislation on health care, voting rights, labor, campaign finance, environmental protections, and gun safety." What she calls "weakening," millions of Americans call "constitutional fidelity" and "protecting civil liberties." When the Biden-Harris administration attempted to mandate experimental vaccines for private-sector workers via OSHA, or when it tried to unlawfully bypass Congress to cancel hundreds of billions in student debt (effectively a wealth transfer to college-educated elites paid for by the working class), it was the courts that stopped them. The judiciary did not act to protect a narrow plutocracy; it acted to protect the separation of powers against an overzealous administrative state that viewed the consent of the governed as an annoying speed bump.

Similarly, Miller’s attack on federalism betrays a deep authoritarian impulse masquerading as majoritarianism. She brings up the legacy of slavery to delegitimize any state-level resistance to federal mandates. This is a tired rhetorical trick. The fact that states’ rights were abused in the 19th century does not invalidate the genius of decentralization in the 21st. The vast, diverse expanse of the United States—spanning 330 million people with wildly different economic realities, religious convictions, and cultural traditions—cannot be micro-managed from a single ZIP code in Washington.

When Miller demands a system without veto points, where a bare 51% majority can impose its will without compromise, she is advocating for a system of raw coercion. Under her vision, if the progressive coalition manages to cobble together a slim victory, they would have the unchecked power to force Wyoming ranchers to abide by the environmental dictates of Brooklyn activists, or compel religious hospitals in Oklahoma to perform procedures that violate their consciences, without any recourse to the courts or state legislatures.

Miller claims that "tempering extremism in the United States therefore requires constraining elites more than constraining majorities." I agree that elites need constraining. But Miller's proposed constitutional reforms—such as eliminating the Electoral College or centralizing election laws—would do the exact opposite. They would permanently shift political power away from rural and working-class communities and concentrate it entirely in the densely populated, elite-managed coastal megacities. It would effectively render half the country politically voiceless.

The American system of checks and balances was designed with a realistic, unromantic view of human nature. The Founders knew that majorities can be swept up in passions, that factions will seek to dominate others, and that centralized power is inherently dangerous, regardless of who wields it. The veto points Miller hates so much are not "dead ends"; they are the vital friction that forces a divided nation to seek compromise rather than submission.

If the Democratic Party wants to pass its agenda, it shouldn’t try to rewrite the Constitution or pack the courts. It should try something truly revolutionary: listening to the working-class voters it has alienated, dropping its obsession with radical cultural engineering, and crafting policies that actually appeal to a broad, durable consensus of the American people. Until they do, we should thank God for the filibuster, the Electoral College, and the constitutional friction that keeps the progressive elite in check.
