"The failure to achieve inclusive prosperity is primarily a result of extractive financing structures and ownership models rather than a poor choice of innovation stages."

A Hidden Argument About Finance

What I find most striking about Dan Breznitz's essay is what it almost says but does not quite say. He correctly identifies the venture capital model as a driver of inequality and concentration, and he correctly observes that the Israeli case shows how a country can become a global tech hub without sharing the proceeds. But he frames the problem as one of innovation strategy—choose stage three instead of stage one—when the more direct lesson is about financing structures. The deepest difference between Taiwan and Israel is not which stage of innovation they emphasize. It is who owns the companies, who gets the cash flows, and what time horizons the dominant investors operate on.

Consider what venture capital actually is. It is a financing instrument designed for power-law returns from a portfolio of high-risk bets, with capital recycled through liquidity events—either acquisition or IPO—that return cash to limited partners on a roughly ten-year cycle. Every feature of the model is calibrated to that exit. Founders accept dilution and board control in exchange for the prospect of a windfall. Employees accept below-market salaries plus stock options that vest on a similar timeline. Customers and communities are not parties to the deal. When the exit comes, the value created flows to the LPs, the GPs, the founders, and the early employees, in roughly that order, and very little of it stays with the locality unless those individuals choose to keep living there.

This is not a problem of which industry the model is applied to. It is a problem of the model itself. Apply the same exit-oriented financing to a Brenta-style luxury shoe cluster and you would get the same hollowing-out: roll-up acquisitions by private equity, brand consolidation, gradual offshoring of the lower-margin steps, eventual sale to LVMH. We already know what this looks like because it has happened to many craft industries. The reason Brenta has survived as an ecosystem is partly that its firms have remained closely held, often family-owned, with cash flows reinvested rather than extracted. Taiwan's electronics firms similarly grew under financing arrangements—bank lending, government-directed credit, retained earnings—that allowed them to plow profits back into capacity rather than distributing them to dispersed equity holders.

Once you see this, the policy implications shift. The question is not just "which innovation stage should we pursue" but "what financing instruments do we want to be dominant in our economy." The United States has spent forty years progressively eroding the alternatives to public equity and venture capital. Patient bank lending to mid-sized firms has shrunk as commercial banks consolidated and focused on consumer lending and large corporates. Cooperative financing structures, mutual insurance, and community development financial institutions have been chronically underfunded. The mid-twentieth-century landscape that produced firms like Hewlett-Packard, which could grow for decades on retained earnings before going public, has been replaced by a structure that funnels promising ideas through Y Combinator and out the other end as acquisition targets.

Breznitz's tech teens are precisely the firms that fall through the gap. They are too established for seed funding, too small for the public markets, too unlikely to deliver a ten-bagger to interest a VC. The financing instruments suited to them—long-duration debt, patient equity from family offices and pension funds, cooperative ownership transitions—exist but are vanishingly small parts of the American financial system. If we wanted to fix this, we would not start with another science park. We would start with the tax code, with the regulatory framework around small-business banking, and with the rules governing pension fund investment mandates.

There is also an institutional ownership story to tell. One of the quiet differences between Germany and the United States is the prevalence of family-owned Mittelstand firms with multi-generational time horizons, supported by Sparkassen and Landesbanken that are explicitly mandated to serve regional economies. These firms behave very differently from publicly traded American competitors precisely because they are not under quarterly earnings pressure. The result is exactly the sustained, incremental, locally embedded innovation Breznitz celebrates in his Taiwanese examples. It is not an accident that German manufacturing has held up against globalization while American manufacturing collapsed. The financing structure was different, the ownership structure was different, and the political economy of regional banking was different.

So when Breznitz says we need to invest in a "local innovation ecosystem" including "specialized production facilities" and "collaborative public spaces," I agree, but I want to add: we also need to invest in local financing ecosystems. Capital allocation is at least as important as skill formation, and arguably more so, because no amount of training will save a firm that cannot raise patient capital. The interesting policy experiments of the last decade—the rise of revenue-based financing, employee ownership trusts, Calvert and other mission-oriented investment vehicles, public banks like the Bank of North Dakota—deserve far more attention than they get.

I also want to register a small objection to Breznitz's celebration of Operation Warp Speed and SiO2. The case is real and the contract was well-structured. But singling out one company benefiting from a $143 million directed contract is not yet evidence of a scalable policy. The interesting question is whether the underlying procurement and contracting capabilities of the U.S. government can sustainably channel capital to tech teens at scale, or whether SiO2 will turn out to be a fortunate one-off. The historical track record of American industrial policy is decidedly mixed precisely because we lack the institutional infrastructure—the bureaucratic autonomy, the long-term contracting relationships, the willingness to absorb visible failures—that Taiwan's ITRI or Germany's Fraunhofer Institutes embody.

Innovation strategy is downstream of financing strategy, and financing strategy is downstream of ownership structure, and ownership structure is downstream of political choices about who gets to own what and on what terms. Breznitz's essay points us in the right direction by exposing the inequality embedded in the VC model. But the cure is not just to substitute one innovation stage for another. It is to rebuild the patient-capital ecosystem that once made America's mid-sized industrial firms the envy of the world, and that still makes Germany and Taiwan formidable. Innovation taxonomies are useful. Financial architectures are decisive.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The venture capital model is inherently designed for short-term exits and wealth concentration rather than long-term community stability.
  2. 02Successful innovation hubs like Taiwan and Brenta thrive because their financing—such as bank lending and family ownership—prioritizes reinvesting profits over extracting them for shareholders.
  3. 03The decline of 'tech teens' in the United States is caused by a financial gap where patient, long-duration capital has been replaced by equity models that demand rapid growth and acquisition.
  4. 04Inclusive growth requires rebuilding local financial ecosystems, including regional banking and mission-oriented investment vehicles, to support firms that are not suited for public markets or venture capital.
  5. 05Innovation strategy is ultimately downstream of political and regulatory choices regarding ownership structures and capital allocation.
