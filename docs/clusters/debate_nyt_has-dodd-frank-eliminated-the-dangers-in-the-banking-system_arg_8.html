"Dodd-Frank has failed to eliminate the dangers in the banking system and has instead codified the 'too big to fail' status of large financial institutions into law."

Dodd-Frank has not eliminated the dangers in the banking system; it has solidified them into law. The government’s formal rejection of the living wills is a Kabuki dance—a show of toughness that obscures the legislation’s real legacy: the official codification of “too big to fail.”

Consider the logic. Title I of Dodd-Frank designates a set of firms as “systemically important financial institutions,” or SIFIs, and subjects them to special regulatory oversight and Federal Reserve liquidity access. The government is explicitly identifying the companies it believes could trigger a meltdown if they went under. Far from eliminating the problem, this label tells the world: these firms are special. Creditors and counterparties, knowing the government is watching closely and stands ready to support liquidity, lend to these institutions more cheaply than to their smaller rivals. The safety net, once implicit, is now a branded product.

The living will process is equally perverse. It imagines a calm, orderly bankruptcy of a trillion-dollar derivatives book that spans the globe. No such thing is possible. In a crisis, derivatives contracts would be frozen by legal crossfires across jurisdictions, and central banks would be forced to intervene to prevent a cascade of defaults. The “resolution authority” created by Dodd-Frank—Orderly Liquidation Authority—simply replaces bailouts with a government-run bankruptcy that would still rely on taxpayer-funded backstops. It is bailout by another name, without reference to the moral hazard it creates.

Meanwhile, the regulatory burden has crushed smaller banks. Community banks, which had nothing to do with the crisis, are drowning in compliance costs from rules designed for giants. The number of new bank charters has plummeted since 2010, and consolidation is accelerating. We are left with an even more concentrated, less competitive banking system, dominated by a handful of behemoths that are now officially partnered with Washington.

A genuinely safe system would be much simpler: drastically higher equity capital requirements (as advocated by economists Anat Admati and Martin Hellwig), a clear prohibition on any government support, and no special designations. It would let failure happen, forcing losses on shareholders and bondholders, which would impose market discipline. Instead, Dodd-Frank has built a high fortress around the very firms it purported to tame. The dangers have not been eliminated; they have been bureaucratized and deferred, growing more dangerous as the memory of 2008 fades.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The designation of 'systemically important financial institutions' provides a government-branded safety net that allows large firms to borrow more cheaply than smaller competitors.
  2. 02The living will process and Orderly Liquidation Authority are unrealistic mechanisms that would still rely on taxpayer-funded backstops during a global financial crisis.
  3. 03The legislation's heavy regulatory burden has disproportionately harmed community banks, leading to a more concentrated and less competitive banking system.
  4. 04The law fails to impose market discipline through high equity capital requirements and the credible threat of unassisted failure for large institutions.
