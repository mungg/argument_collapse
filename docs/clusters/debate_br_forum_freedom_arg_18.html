What Land Back Means for Rana's Argument

Aziz Rana's essay does something I have rarely seen in left American political commentary: it places settler colonialism at the analytical center of its account of American freedom-talk. For an Indigenous reader, this is welcome. Too often the left version of American history treats Native dispossession as a regrettable backstory, mentioned briefly before the analysis moves on to its real subject (class, race-meaning-Black-and-white, or empire-meaning-overseas-projection). Rana refuses this evasion. He insists that "Indigenous conquest and Native expropriation" were "the basic engine of liberty" in the American settler tradition, and that the cultural inheritance of that engine continues to shape what freedom means in the United States today.

I want to honor this analysis while pressing on what it might require if taken fully seriously. My concern is that Rana's prescriptions, despite the radicalism of his diagnosis, do not adequately reckon with what an Indigenous politics of freedom and self-determination actually demands. The gap between diagnosis and prescription, in his essay, is wide enough that the diagnosis ends up being mostly rhetorical — a frame for an otherwise familiar social-democratic program — rather than a guide to the difficult institutional and political work an anti-settler politics would actually involve.

Begin with what Rana proposes for Native nations specifically. He mentions Indigenous freedom three times. He calls for "redistributive and reparative policies that commit to Indigenous freedom alongside genuine global well-being and equality." He notes Puerto Rico as a "still-colonized territory." He acknowledges that domestic projects of expansion remain part of the contemporary picture. These mentions are gestures, not programs. There is no engagement with treaty rights, with the land back movement, with the federal-tribal trust relationship, with tribal sovereignty as a distinct legal and political category, with the Indian Child Welfare Act recently before the Supreme Court, with the legacy of boarding schools, with the missing and murdered Indigenous women crisis, with the politics of recognition versus refusal that animates contemporary Indigenous thought.

This is not pedantic citation-tracking. It points to something substantive. If settler colonialism is the engine of American freedom-talk, then dismantling that engine requires more than adding Indigenous concerns to a broader progressive agenda. It requires reckoning with the fact that the United States is, as a polity, constituted on stolen land, that its territorial sovereignty is in significant respects illegitimate from an Indigenous perspective, and that no amount of social democratic redistribution within the existing federal system can substitute for the actual return of land, jurisdiction, and political authority to Indigenous nations. This is uncomfortable. It is also what Native scholars and activists have been saying with increasing clarity over the past two decades — Glen Coulthard, Audra Simpson, Leanne Betasamosake Simpson, Nick Estes, the Red Nation, and others.

Coulthard's Red Skin, White Masks (2014) argues that the politics of recognition through which states like Canada and the United States have incorporated Indigenous claims since the 1970s has been a mechanism of continued dispossession, not its remedy. To be recognized by the settler state — granted limited self-governance, protected cultural rights, included in liberal multicultural narratives — is to accept the legitimacy of the settler state's underlying jurisdiction over land that was never legitimately ceded. Coulthard advocates a politics of refusal: refusing recognition on settler terms, building Indigenous political authority on its own terms, and insisting that land relationship rather than recognition is the foundation of Indigenous freedom.

Rana's program, with its emphasis on building institutions of "inclusive solidarity" within the existing American polity, has no good answer to this Indigenous critique. The inclusion he proposes is inclusion into a continuing settler state. The solidarity he envisions is solidarity among the populations the settler state has assembled on Indigenous land. These can be good things on their own terms. They are not the same thing as decolonization, and conflating them risks reproducing exactly the recognition-as-dispossession dynamic Coulthard identifies.

A second concern. Rana's framework places immigration at the heart of his anti-settler politics: he wants decriminalized entry, presumptive admission, an end to border enforcement as a freedom issue. This is admirable, and I share his commitments. But from an Indigenous perspective, "open borders" of the United States is not quite the same thing as decolonization. The border is itself an artifact of settler conquest, drawn across Indigenous nations whose territorial relationships preceded and exceed the US/Mexico/Canada cartography. Decriminalizing the crossing of that border is good. It is not the same as restoring Indigenous jurisdiction over lands the border crosses. The Tohono O'odham, the Yaqui, the Mohawk, and many other nations have territories bisected by colonial borders and have their own positions on what justice in border policy would look like — positions that do not always align with the "open borders" framework progressives often imagine.

A third concern. Rana's emphasis on workplace democracy and labor reform as the central institutional sites for reconstituting freedom locates the project in the wage-labor economy. For many Indigenous communities, the relationship to wage labor is itself part of the problem — a form of integration into the settler economy that displaces other ways of relating to land, kinship, and provisioning. Land-based Indigenous economies, traditional harvest practices, and the political authority that comes from sustained relationship with specific territories are not adequately captured by a labor-democracy framework. This is not to dismiss the workplace reforms Rana proposes; many Indigenous people work for wages and would benefit. It is to say that a politics genuinely committed to undoing the settler structure of American freedom-talk would also have to make space for Indigenous economic and political forms that exceed the wage-labor frame.

What would an Indigenous-centered program look like? It would begin from treaty obligations. Many of the treaties between the United States and Native nations have specific provisions — for land, for hunting and fishing rights, for federal trust responsibilities — that have never been adequately honored. Enforcing treaty obligations as a baseline is the minimum any serious anti-settler politics requires. It would include support for the land back movement: the return of federal lands, of national parks established on Native territory, of lands held by extractive corporations, to the Indigenous nations from whom they were taken. It would include genuine tribal sovereignty over criminal jurisdiction, child welfare, environmental policy, and economic development on tribal lands, free from the constant constraints imposed by federal preemption and Supreme Court attacks. It would include reparations, including monetary reparations, for the specific historical injuries of removal, boarding schools, forced assimilation, and the ongoing extraction of resources from Indigenous lands.

None of this is in Rana's program. He could include it. He should include it. Until then, his settler-imperial framework remains a diagnostic with rhetorical force but limited prescriptive bite for the people whose dispossession he names as foundational.

The deeper point is this: a different freedom, the freedom Rana wants, cannot be built on Indigenous land without Indigenous consent. That consent has not been given, and the project of obtaining it would require political and material concessions far beyond anything currently on the American left's agenda. Acknowledging this is the beginning of an honest reckoning. Rana has taken the first step. Many more are required.
