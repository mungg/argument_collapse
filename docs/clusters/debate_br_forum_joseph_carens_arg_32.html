"The United States should grant immediate, unconditional amnesty to all irregular migrants because the current enforcement system is a humanitarian disaster that inherently violates human dignity regardless of a migrant's length of residence."

Joseph Carens’s essay is a model of lucid moral argumentation, but for all its sophistication it remains curiously indifferent to the lived experiences of irregular migrants themselves—particularly their suffering under current enforcement practices. By focusing on an abstract ideal of social membership that matures over time, Carens inadvertently lends legitimacy to a system that routinely violates human dignity long before his five- or seven-year threshold is reached. The real urgency is not when to grant amnesty, but how to stop the ongoing humanitarian disaster unfolding at borders and in detention centers. We should grant amnesty now, unconditionally, to all irregular migrants present in the United States, not because they have become members after a certain number of years, but because the very attempt to deport them is causing indefensible harm.

Consider the reality of enforcement in 2009. Under the Bush administration, worksite raids and home invasions have become commonplace. People are snatched from their families without warning, their children left stranded at school. Detention facilities are overcrowded, and medical neglect is rife—the death of Hiu Lui Ng from untreated cancer in immigration custody is not an anomaly but a symptom of a punitive system that treats migrants as disposable. The human costs of these policies are borne disproportionately by people of color, reinforcing racial hierarchies in ways that should shame a liberal democracy. Carens acknowledges these harms in passing, but by accepting the state’s right to deport in the early years of residence, he effectively condones them. The moral calculus must change. It is not enough to say that deportation is wrong after five years if the very process of deportation—the raids, the detention, the separation of families—is itself a form of state violence.

Carens’s careful parsing of how many years are needed to establish social membership can feel like a luxury to those who are living in constant fear now. For a Miguel Sanchez, whether he is in his third year or his ninth year, the terror of a traffic stop is the same. The moral claim to be free from that terror does not depend on a calendar; it inheres in his humanity. Deportation is not merely an administrative inconvenience; it is exile, often to a place the person barely knows or where poverty and violence await. The state should bear an extremely heavy burden of justification before it inflicts that on anyone. In practice, our immigration laws fail to meet that burden, not only because they are enforced cruelly, but because they are themselves rooted in a system of global economic inequality that the United States actively perpetuates.

Carens’s complicity argument gestures toward this, but he is too cautious. The United States has negotiated trade agreements that have devastated Mexican agriculture, pushing millions northward. It has supported repressive regimes in Central America that have generated refugee flows, then denied asylum to the victims. It has built a vast immigration enforcement apparatus while quietly relying on cheap undocumented labor in key industries. This is not inadvertent complicity; it is a deliberate, if hypocritical, structural arrangement. To then impose a probationary period of illegality before granting amnesty is to add insult to injury. The only morally coherent response is an immediate, unconditional amnesty for all irregular migrants now in the country, coupled with an end to the militarization of the border and the abolition of detention as a tool of immigration enforcement.

Some will say that an unconditional amnesty would be a magnet for future migration. But the magnet argument has been oversold. The surge in irregular migration in recent decades occurred despite, not because of, amnesties; it was driven by economic push-factors in sending countries and pull-factors in the U.S. labor market. Moreover, if we are serious about preventing future flows, the answer is not to punish those already here but to create legal, safe avenues for migration. Let workers come with visas, not through deserts. Let families be reunited. Invest in the economic development of sending nations, not as an afterthought, but as a matter of mutual obligation. The enforcement-only approach has not only failed; it has produced a humanitarian crisis. Carens is right that we need a new framework, but his insistence on a waiting period reproduces the logic of enforcement-first that has done so much damage.

Ultimately, I am not content with Carens’s “minimalist” argument because minimalism, in the face of ongoing injustice, is complicity. He wants to carve out a space of moral decency within a system he accepts as basically legitimate. I think the system is rotten to the core, and decency demands its transformation. Let us have amnesty—full, immediate, and without apology. Let us also begin the harder work of building an immigration system that treats migrants not as criminals-in-waiting but as human beings with rights that no border can erase.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The process of deportation—including worksite raids, family separation, and medical neglect in detention—constitutes a form of state violence that is morally indefensible.
  2. 02The moral right to be free from the terror of enforcement inheres in a person's basic humanity and does not depend on a specific calendar of social membership.
  3. 03The state's right to deport is undermined by its active role in creating the global economic inequalities and political instabilities that drive migration.
  4. 04Amnesty should be viewed as a response to structural complicity and systemic injustice rather than a reward for a probationary period of 'illegality.'
  5. 05The 'magnet' argument against amnesty is overstated, as migration is primarily driven by economic push-and-pull factors rather than the prospect of future legalization.
  6. 06True moral decency requires the total transformation of a 'rotten' immigration system and the abolition of detention rather than minimalist reforms that accept the state's right to exclude.
