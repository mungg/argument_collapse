Vanilla Claude

"While the moral case for amnesty based on social membership is sound, a humane amnesty policy must be integrated into a broader reform of the legal immigration system and labor enforcement to be effective and sustainable."

Joseph Carens has written the most morally serious case for amnesty I have read, and on the core question—whether long-settled irregular migrants have claims of membership that override the state's interest in enforcement—I think he is right. The Grimmond and Ng cases are not edge cases dressed up as principles; they expose what is actually at stake when a country deports someone who has lived among us for decades. A society that can do that to its own neighbors has confused legal categories with moral ones. Carens's "social membership" framework names the confusion well.

But I want to press him on three matters where I think his minimalist argument is too minimal: the incentive objection, the threshold question, and the political economy that surrounds any amnesty rule.

First, the incentive objection. Carens dispatches it quickly: prospective migrants think near-term, not about regularization "years down the road." This is plausible at the margins, but the United States already has empirical experience that complicates the claim. The Immigration Reform and Control Act of 1986 legalized roughly 2.7 million people through its general and agricultural amnesties. It also imposed employer sanctions that were almost immediately gutted in practice by the easy availability of false documents and the absence of a verification system. In the two decades since, the unauthorized population has grown from perhaps three million to the eleven million Carens cites. One cannot run the counterfactual cleanly—NAFTA, demographic pressures in Mexico and Central America, and demand for low-wage labor all matter—but the experience suggests that an amnesty unaccompanied by credible enforcement and credible legal channels does not stabilize. It primes the next cycle. Carens's argument is for a permanent legal rule, not a one-off, which is in some ways better; but that only sharpens the question of what surrounds the rule.

This leads to my second point: the threshold. Carens proposes five to seven years, with ten as a maximum. I think he has the morally compelling end of the spectrum (Grimmond's seventy-eight years, Ng's fifteen) doing too much work for the lower end. Five years is a remarkably short period in which to say that the state has forfeited its right to enforce its own admissions policy against an adult who entered knowing he was violating it. Many of the most poignant features in his examples—children educated in U.S. schools, marriages, deep neighborhood ties, mortgages, careers—generally accrue over longer horizons. France's old ten-year rule and the U.S. cancellation-of-removal provision he cites both used a decade. I would defend ten as the right anchor: long enough to register that the state has tolerated the migrant's presence in a way that approaches complicity, short enough that a child who arrived at eight is regularized before adulthood, and consonant with what most liberal democracies have historically practiced when they have practiced anything at all.

Third, and most importantly, Carens underplays his own complicity argument. He treats it as a supplementary consideration that "reinforces" the case for amnesty but can be "overused." I think he has the dialectic backwards. In the American case, the question is not whether the state has been complicit; the question is how anyone could believe otherwise. The 1986 sanctions regime was deliberately designed to be unenforceable. The Bracero program's end was followed by decades in which agricultural, construction, hospitality, and meatpacking industries built their business models on irregular labor, with periodic ICE raids functioning more as political theater than as deterrence. Congress has repeatedly declined to mandate E-Verify or to expand legal visas for low-skilled work to anything like the levels the labor market absorbs. The eleven million did not slip past us; we hired them, housed them, taxed them, and educated their children. The moral force of Carens's membership claim is multiplied by the fact that this membership was solicited, not merely tolerated.

If complicity is taken seriously, the policy package looks different from what Carens sketches. An automatic regularization at, say, ten years is necessary but insufficient. It must be paired with two reforms his essay largely brackets: a substantial legal channel for the unskilled labor the U.S. economy actually demands, and a verification system that makes off-the-books hiring genuinely costly to employers rather than to workers. Without the first, the next eleven million are already on their way. Without the second, the rule of law that immigration restrictionists invoke remains the fiction it has been for thirty years.

None of this is a rebuttal of Carens. It is a friendly amendment: his moral argument is sound, but a humane amnesty rule is not a freestanding intervention. It is one piece of a settlement in which the state stops pretending it does not want the workers it has spent decades quietly welcoming.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A permanent amnesty rule unaccompanied by credible enforcement and expanded legal channels risks creating cycles of unauthorized migration rather than stabilizing the population.
  2. 02The threshold for amnesty should be set at ten years rather than five to seven, as a decade better reflects the time required for deep social ties to accrue and for the state's inaction to constitute complicity.
  3. 03The state's active complicity in soliciting irregular labor through unenforceable sanctions and inadequate visa quotas significantly multiplies the moral force of the membership claim.
  4. 04To uphold the rule of law, amnesty must be paired with a substantial legal channel for unskilled labor and a verification system that penalizes employers who hire off-the-books.
