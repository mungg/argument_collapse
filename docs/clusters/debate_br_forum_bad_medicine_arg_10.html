Angell Is Right, and the Rot Goes Deeper Than She Says

Marcia Angell's indictment of the entanglement between academic medicine and the pharmaceutical industry is largely correct, and as someone who has spent a career in primary care, I would only add that her account, if anything, understates the problem on the ground. She writes from the vantage point of a journal editor and elite faculty member. From the vantage point of an ordinary internist seeing thirty patients a day, the corruption is even more naked, because what trickles down to us is not the data but the marketing residue: the glossy reprint, the dinner talk, the drug rep with the latest "outcomes" study tucked into a sample bag.

Consider the recent history that should haunt anyone who prescribes. Vioxx, which she mentions, was not a freak accident. Merck had internal data suggesting cardiovascular risk for years before withdrawal, and the published trials were carefully framed. The reanalysis of Study 329, which compared paroxetine to placebo in adolescents, showed that what GlaxoSmithKline marketed as a positive trial was, on the actual data, a negative one with serious safety signals. Avandia is now embroiled in a similar controversy, with Steven Nissen's meta-analysis pointing toward elevated cardiovascular risk that GlaxoSmithKline's own internal analyses had apparently flagged. The pattern is so consistent that one has to stop calling it a series of scandals and start calling it the business model.

Where I would push Angell is on her diagnosis of academic complicity. She frames it as a story of medical centers becoming "supplicants" because of shrinking reimbursements. That is too kind. The senior faculty I have watched build consulting empires were not driven into industry's arms by hunger; they were drawn by ambition and the lifestyle that a $500,000 supplemental income provides. The deans and department chairs who sit on corporate boards are not victims of austerity. They are participants in a class-formation process within medicine — the rise of a physician-entrepreneur stratum whose interests have diverged sharply from those of working clinicians and from those of patients. Conflict-of-interest rules are weak because the people who would write them are the people who would lose money under them.

Her three reforms are necessary but not sufficient. Banning personal payments to clinical trialists, banning gifts and industry-funded CME, and stripping the windfall from Bayh-Dole patents would all help. But none of these touches the central problem, which is that we ask a profit-maximizing industry to generate the evidence base for medical practice. The structural fix is to require that pivotal trials of any drug seeking FDA approval be conducted by an independent body — funded by industry fees, modeled perhaps on how the SEC funds itself, but with no contractual relationship between the sponsor and the investigator. Industry would still pay; industry simply would not choose the design, the comparator, the dose, or the publication strategy. Until that separation is made, disclosure is window dressing.

I also want to defend one of Angell's points that draws the most predictable scorn: the claim that there is "very little innovative research in the modern pharmaceutical industry." Critics will say this is unfair, pointing to imatinib, the statins, the protease inhibitors. But the question is not whether industry has ever produced an important drug; it is what fraction of current pipeline activity addresses unmet medical need versus me-too variation, lifestyle indications, and patent-extension strategies. Anyone who reads the trade press honestly knows the answer. The blockbuster model rewards drugs for chronic conditions in wealthy populations, taken daily for life. It does not reward narrow-spectrum antibiotics, vaccines for diseases of the poor, or treatments for rare conditions absent special incentives. The opportunity cost Angell mentions is not hypothetical; it is the reason we are running out of antibiotics while the eighth statin comes to market.

The objection I anticipate is that Angell's reforms would slow drug development and harm patients. This is the industry's standard rhetorical move, and it should be retired. The counterfactual is not "no drugs"; it is "drugs whose efficacy and safety we can actually trust." A health system that approves fewer drugs but knows what they do is more useful to patients than one that approves many drugs through a corrupted evidence pipeline and then withdraws them after the body count rises. The Vioxx episode alone is estimated to have caused tens of thousands of excess cardiovascular events in the United States. That is the price of the current arrangement, and it does not appear on industry's balance sheet.

A final point. Angell ends by reminding researchers that they are not entitled to whatever they want just because they are very smart. She is too polite. The deeper issue is that academic medicine has, over thirty years, internalized a worldview in which any restriction on commercial activity is treated as an affront to scientific freedom. This is ideology, not science. The medical profession used to understand itself as a guild with duties — to patients, to the public, to the integrity of its own knowledge base — that took precedence over the personal enrichment of its members. We have nearly lost that self-understanding. Angell is trying to recover it, and she deserves more than respectful murmurs at ethics conferences. She deserves to win the argument.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The corruption of medical practice is even more pervasive at the clinical level than in academia, as doctors are bombarded with marketing masquerading as scientific data.
  2. 02Academic complicity is driven by the personal ambition and greed of a physician-entrepreneur class rather than by institutional financial necessity.
  3. 03The only way to ensure the integrity of medical evidence is to mandate that clinical trials be conducted by independent bodies rather than the drug sponsors themselves.
  4. 04The pharmaceutical industry's focus on 'me-too' drugs and patent-extension strategies creates a massive scientific opportunity cost by neglecting urgent, non-profitable medical needs.
  5. 05The industry's claim that reform would slow innovation is a rhetorical tactic that ignores the immense human cost and safety risks inherent in the current corrupted system.
  6. 06The medical profession has replaced its traditional duty to the public with a market ideology that views restrictions on commercial activity as an affront to scientific freedom.
