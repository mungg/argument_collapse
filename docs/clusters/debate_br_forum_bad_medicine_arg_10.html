"Medical knowledge and discoveries derived from public funding and trust carry inherent public obligations that must be protected through stronger public-interest conditions and the elimination of commercial bias."

Marcia Angell’s essay is about conflicts of interest, but beneath it lies an even larger question: who owns the benefits of medical knowledge? Her answer, echoing Jonas Salk’s “Could you patent the sun?” is that discoveries made possible by public trust and public money carry public obligations. On this point she is exactly right, and the access question should be placed at the center of the debate.

The public pays for biomedical innovation in multiple ways. Taxpayers fund the National Institutes of Health, which supports the basic science on which many therapies rest. Patients volunteer for clinical trials, often accepting inconvenience and risk for the sake of knowledge. Universities and teaching hospitals receive tax exemptions because they are supposed to serve public purposes. Then, once a product reaches the market, the public pays again through Medicare, Medicaid, the Veterans Health Administration, private insurance premiums, and out-of-pocket costs. If publicly supported research becomes privately controlled medicine sold at whatever price the market will bear, the public has been converted from sponsor to captive customer.

Angell’s criticism of Bayh-Dole should be read in this light. The issue is not merely whether technology transfer increased patenting. It is whether universities have behaved as stewards of public knowledge or as speculative investors. A patent license from a university is not morally identical to a patent developed entirely within a private firm. When the underlying science was publicly funded, the university’s bargaining power is held in trust. Licensing terms should reflect that trust.

This does not mean every academic discovery must be given away. Development is costly and uncertain. A company asked to conduct large trials and scale manufacturing may need exclusivity. But exclusivity should not be a blank check. Universities can and should require reasonable pricing commitments, access provisions for low-income countries, research-use rights, sublicensing obligations when a company fails to develop a product, and transparency about the public contribution to the invention. If a licensee benefits from taxpayer-funded science, “reasonable terms” should be more than a forgotten phrase in a statute.

The pricing problem is not abstract. Patients routinely face high costs for new brand-name drugs, and public programs strain under the expense of therapies that may offer only marginal advantages over older alternatives. Angell is right that the most commercially attractive products are not always the most medically important. A pill taken daily by millions for a chronic condition is a better business proposition than a short-course antibiotic held in reserve for resistant infections. A drug that slightly differentiates itself from a blockbuster nearing patent expiration may be more lucrative than a treatment for a neglected tropical disease. Markets reward purchasing power, not suffering.

That reality should temper the common claim that high drug prices are simply the necessary cost of innovation. Some high prices may indeed finance valuable research. But the industry also spends heavily on marketing, promotion, and the defense of market share. It devotes enormous energy to shaping prescribing behavior, extending patent life, and distinguishing new products from older drugs even when comparative evidence is thin. When companies say prices must remain high to fund research, the public is entitled to ask: research into what, for whose benefit, and under whose control?

Conflicts of interest matter because they help sustain this misalignment. If academic experts with financial ties write guidelines that expand the population eligible for drug treatment, the result is not only a scientific concern but a distributive one. If continuing medical education teaches doctors to favor the newest brand-name product over an inexpensive generic without strong evidence of superiority, patients and insurers pay. If negative trials are unpublished, patients may take drugs whose harms or limitations are hidden. Bias is not merely a violation of professional etiquette; it moves money and risk onto patients.

A patient-centered reform agenda would therefore combine Angell’s conflict-of-interest proposals with stronger public-interest conditions on the entire drug pipeline.

First, all clinical trials involving human subjects should be registered, and results should be reported in a timely public database, whether favorable or not. Publication in journals is not enough. Patients who volunteer for trials do not consent to have their data buried because the results are commercially inconvenient.

Second, trials intended to influence practice should compare new drugs with the best existing therapy when such therapy exists, not only with placebo. Placebo-controlled trials can establish efficacy, but they often do not answer the patient’s question: is this drug better, safer, or worth more than what is already available? Comparative effectiveness research deserves public funding precisely because manufacturers may not ask these questions.

Third, guideline panels should include patient representatives and independent methodologists, not only subspecialists. Patients have a stake in how benefits, harms, inconvenience, and cost are weighed. A cholesterol target, a psychiatric diagnosis, or a pain-treatment recommendation can reshape millions of lives. Those decisions should not be dominated by experts entangled with manufacturers.

Fourth, universities should publish the terms of major exclusive licenses for medically significant inventions. Confidentiality may be convenient, but secrecy prevents the public from knowing whether a tax-exempt institution has protected access. At minimum, universities should disclose whether licenses contain affordability provisions, global access plans, and safeguards against shelving.

Fifth, public funders should invest more in areas markets neglect: antibiotics, vaccines, prevention, rare conditions without profitable markets, and diseases concentrated among the poor. The absence of a lucrative market is not evidence of low medical value. It is evidence that public priorities and private incentives diverge.

Angell’s essay may strike some readers as nostalgic for a pre-commercial age that was never as pure as imagined. Academic medicine has always had hierarchies, ambitions, and blind spots. But nostalgia is not the core of her argument. Her core claim is that medicine cannot be governed primarily by commercial incentives without compromising its public mission. That claim is difficult to refute.

The challenge is not to punish companies for being companies. It is to stop universities from behaving as though their highest calling is to become minor pharmaceutical firms. It is to stop physicians from allowing marketing to masquerade as education. It is to stop public discoveries from becoming private tollbooths without accountability.

Patients do not care whether a therapy emerged from a university lab, a start-up, or a multinational corporation. They care whether it works, whether its risks are honestly described, whether their doctor’s advice is independent, and whether they can afford it. A drug-development system that cannot answer those concerns has lost sight of its purpose.

The sun cannot be patented, Salk said. Perhaps some molecules can be. But the moral light by which medicine sees its obligations must remain public.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The public acts as both the primary sponsor and the captive customer of medical innovation, paying for basic research through taxes and then again for the resulting products through high healthcare costs.
  2. 02Universities should act as stewards of public knowledge rather than speculative investors by including reasonable pricing and access provisions in their patent licensing agreements.
  3. 03Market incentives frequently misalign research priorities, favoring lucrative 'me-too' drugs for chronic conditions over urgent but less profitable needs like new antibiotics.
  4. 04Financial conflicts of interest among academic experts and guideline panels result in distributive harms by inflating healthcare costs and shifting risks onto patients.
  5. 05Clinical trial integrity must be restored by requiring the registration and reporting of all results and mandating comparisons against existing therapies rather than just placebos.
  6. 06Public funders should actively invest in medical areas that private markets neglect, such as vaccines and rare diseases, to bridge the gap between profit motives and human suffering.
  7. 07Transparency in university licensing and the inclusion of patient representatives on medical guideline panels are essential to ensure that medical practice serves the public mission.
