A Conservation Biologist's Doubts About Adaptive Management as Magic

I read this essay as someone who has spent twenty years doing conservation biology in working landscapes, much of it on issues directly relevant to Habitat Conservation Plans and watershed-scale planning. I am sympathetic to many of the authors' instincts, but I want to register serious doubts about how the science is actually working out in the regulatory experiments they celebrate. The phrase "adaptive management" appears in this essay with the magical valence it has acquired in policy discourse over the past decade, and I think the time has come to take some of the shine off it.

Let me begin with the California HCPs, which the authors hold up as exemplary. The 1996 Natural Communities Conservation Plan for coastal sage scrub in San Diego County is, I agree, an ambitious and in some ways admirable experiment. It identified large reserve areas, set aside substantial acreage, and committed signatories to a multi-decade implementation framework. But the biological foundations of the plan were and remain shakier than the policy enthusiasm would suggest.

The California gnatcatcher, the listed species at the center of the plan, has a poorly understood demography. The reserve design relied on habitat models calibrated against a few years of survey data, in a system that experiences high inter-annual variability driven by rainfall and fire. Coastal sage scrub is a fire-dependent ecosystem; the plan does not adequately address fire regime management, partly because fire is politically toxic in suburban-adjacent landscapes. The reserves are fragmented, edge effects are substantial, and connectivity is constrained by the very development the plan accommodates. The "No Surprises" assurances locked in landowner obligations on the assumption that the reserve configuration would suffice for fifty to seventy-five years, a forecast no responsible ecologist would endorse given what we know about regional climate variability, much less plausible climate change scenarios.

The authors note in passing that HCPs include "adaptive management" provisions allowing adjustments to be made in light of monitoring. The reality of these provisions is considerably less robust than the term suggests. Most HCPs have weak or undefined adaptive management commitments. Monitoring budgets are typically inadequate. There is no enforceable mechanism by which monitoring data trigger management changes. The Kareiva et al. report from the National Center for Ecological Analysis and Synthesis that the authors cite—"Using Science in Habitat Conservation Plans"—was scathing on these points, finding that most HCPs failed to articulate testable biological goals, failed to specify monitoring protocols capable of detecting change, and failed to define the management responses that monitoring would prompt. The authors mention this report in a footnote but do not reckon with its implications.

This matters because the entire rolling-rule architecture, in the ecosystem-management domain, depends on the assumption that monitoring will reveal problems in time for management adjustments to correct them. If monitoring is weak, slow, or unenforceable, the adaptive feedback loop is broken, and what remains is unilateral concession to development with a fig leaf of scientific language.

I want to make a related point about the timescales involved. Ecosystem responses to management interventions often play out over decades to centuries. The recovery of a depleted fish population, the re-establishment of a forest canopy, the response of riparian systems to changed hydrology—these are slow processes. The rolling-rule regime envisions iterative learning cycles in which monitoring informs revision. But if the relevant biological signals emerge over fifty-year timescales, the regime cannot in fact learn from them within human policy time. We are committing to management trajectories whose consequences we will not detect until they are largely irreversible.

This is not an argument against adaptive management as a discipline. Carl Walters and others developed adaptive management precisely as a way to embrace uncertainty in resource management, and the framework has produced real insights in fisheries, forestry, and waterfowl management. But "adaptive management" as practiced in fisheries (Walters' Pacific salmon work, for example) is much more rigorous than "adaptive management" as it appears in most HCPs. It requires explicit hypothesis-testing, controlled experimentation, and willingness to act on findings even when they are politically inconvenient. Almost none of these conditions obtain in HCP implementation.

The Chesapeake Bay Program, which the authors also celebrate, has a better record but its limitations are illuminating. The 1987 commitment to reduce nutrient loadings by forty percent by 2000 has not been met. The program's monitoring has indeed produced an impressive understanding of the Bay's nutrient dynamics, and the tributary strategies represent real institutional creativity. But the underlying problem—non-point source pollution from agriculture, especially poultry operations on the Delmarva peninsula—has resisted the program's deliberative approach. Voluntary best management practices have been adopted unevenly. Regulatory authority over agricultural runoff remains weak under federal and most state law. The Bay continues to experience large summer dead zones. The tributary teams are an interesting institutional experiment, but they have not solved the underlying political-economy problem of who bears the costs of nutrient reduction.

What worries me about the essay's framing is that it treats institutional innovation as if it could substitute for hard substantive choices. The Chesapeake's nutrient problem is not, fundamentally, an information problem or a deliberative problem. It is a problem of allocating economic costs across an industry (agriculture) whose political organization protects it from those costs. No amount of pooled monitoring will solve this. Solving it would require either explicit subsidies or explicit regulation, both of which run into political constraints the rolling-rule regime does not relax.

Similarly with endangered species: the binding constraint on species recovery is usually not insufficient deliberation but insufficient habitat. The HCP framework, however cleverly designed, cannot create habitat that does not exist. It can only allocate take among competing claims on shrinking habitat. The most rigorous ecological assessments of HCPs—the Kareiva report, John Kostyack's National Wildlife Federation analyses, the work of the Society for Conservation Biology working group—suggest that many HCPs are accommodating more take than the listed species can sustain. We will only know which were wrong in retrospect, when the species cross thresholds from which they cannot recover.

I want to make one constructive suggestion. The rolling-rule architecture would be much improved by what fisheries managers call "precautionary defaults": substantive presumptions, embedded in law, that hold absent affirmative scientific demonstration to the contrary. The default for an HCP affecting a listed species, for instance, might be no take above a specified threshold, with the burden on the proponent to demonstrate that higher take is sustainable. The default for a watershed plan might be no degradation of existing ambient quality, with the burden on the plan to demonstrate that proposed activities will not degrade. Precautionary defaults preserve the deliberative architecture the authors value while protecting against the slow-feedback problems that adaptive management cannot solve.

The essay before us is intellectually exciting, but it leans too heavily on a confidence in monitoring and adjustment that the actual scientific track record does not yet warrant. We are making large, often irreversible commitments under the banner of "adaptive management," and we will be living with the consequences long after the academic literature has moved on to its next paradigm.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The biological foundations of celebrated experiments like the California Habitat Conservation Plans are shaky, relying on inadequate data and failing to account for long-term ecological variables like fire regimes and climate change.
  2. 02Adaptive management in practice often lacks the rigorous hypothesis testing and enforceable feedback loops necessary to actually correct failing environmental policies.
  3. 03The mismatch between slow ecological timescales and rapid policy cycles means we may not detect the failure of a management trajectory until the damage is irreversible.
  4. 04Institutional and deliberative innovations cannot substitute for hard political-economic decisions regarding the allocation of costs for pollution reduction and habitat loss.
  5. 05The rolling-rule framework should be anchored by 'precautionary defaults' that place the burden of proof on developers to demonstrate sustainability before allowing environmental degradation.
  6. 06Current Habitat Conservation Plans often serve as a 'fig leaf' for development because they lack the funding and scientific protocols required to meet their stated biological goals.
