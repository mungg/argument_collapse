Diversified Gemini

"While Chapman’s critique of 'Liberal Neurodiversity' is valid, their proposed 'Neurodivergent Power' framework risks harming the most vulnerable by dangerously collapsing distinct clinical and biological realities into a purely anti-capitalist political identity."

Robert Chapman’s “The Future of Neurodiversity” rightly points out the severe limitations of what they call “Liberal Neurodiversity.” The corporatization of neurodivergence, the extraction of specific “superpowers” for tech-industry profit, and the stark reality that civil rights legislation does little for those locked in prisons or state hospitals are all crucial critiques. However, as we examine Chapman’s proposed solution—a unified “Neurodivergent Power” explicitly modeled on revolutionary Marxist frameworks and the Black Panther Party—we must ask whether this political vision fundamentally misinterprets the nature of severe psychiatric and neurological realities. By attempting to fold all cognitive and emotional differences into a single, anti-capitalist political identity, Chapman risks romanticizing severe mental illness and erasing the profound biological and clinical realities that demand medical, not just political, intervention.

The foundational premise of the neurodiversity movement, originating in the autism community, was that certain neurological differences are natural variations of the human genome, not diseases to be cured. For many autistics, people with ADHD, or those with dyslexia, this framework has been emancipatory. However, as the umbrella of “neurodivergent” has expanded to include psychiatric conditions like schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and severe clinical depression, the socio-political model begins to fracture under the weight of clinical reality.

Chapman writes about individuals prone to psychosis who might fear government spying and need their webcams covered as a “reasonable adjustment.” This example is profoundly telling. Accommodating a delusion in the workplace is not merely a matter of resisting “neuronormativity” or capitalist productivity demands. It touches on the agonizing reality of severe psychiatric illness. For someone experiencing active paranoia or psychosis, the primary issue is not that the capitalist workplace is unaccommodating; it is that their own brain is subjecting them to a terrifying, distorted version of reality that causes immense personal suffering, regardless of the economic system they live under.

When Chapman argues that we must target “the entire economic system that disables and discriminates against us,” they lean heavily into the social model of disability—the idea that people are disabled not by their bodies or brains, but by society’s failure to accommodate them. While this is highly applicable to a wheelchair user facing a staircase, or an autistic person navigating a loud, fluorescent-lit office, its application to severe mental illness is dangerously limited. Schizophrenia, for instance, is a devastating illness that can erode cognitive function, destroy social bonds, and lead to premature death, independent of societal structure. To suggest that the suffering of a person in the throes of a manic episode or deep clinical depression is primarily an artifact of “stifling norms and exclusions” under capitalism is to minimize the intrinsic torment of these conditions.

Historically, the anti-psychiatry movement of the 1960s and 70s—which Chapman briefly mentions—made similar arguments, framing psychiatric diagnoses as mere tools of social control. While that movement exposed very real abuses in psychiatric institutions, its extreme edge, which denied the reality of mental illness entirely, left many individuals without the medical care they desperately needed. There is a real danger that the “Neurodivergent Power” movement, in its zeal to politicize all psychological difference, will repeat these mistakes. If we redefine debilitating psychiatric symptoms as politically oppressed "ways of being," we risk abandoning those who need psychiatric medicine, hospitalization, and clinical care.

Furthermore, Chapman’s grouping of all “neurominorities” into a single oppressed class facing the common enemy of capitalism glosses over the drastically different needs and experiences within this group. The accommodations needed by a highly verbal autistic software engineer are entirely different from the needs of an individual with profound autism who is non-verbal, engages in severe self-injurious behavior, and requires 24-hour medical care. The latter’s condition is not merely a different “disposition” that is rendered “unproductive” by capitalist pacing. It is a profound, life-limiting disability.

The parents and caregivers of those with profound cognitive and psychiatric disabilities often watch with dismay as the neurodiversity discourse is dominated by highly capable individuals who argue against medical research and interventions. Chapman celebrates the boycott of the Spectrum 10k research program, framing it as a victory against potential eugenics. While the ethical concerns surrounding genetic research are valid and must be rigorously monitored, the complete shutdown of such research also halts the search for biomarkers that could lead to better targeted treatments for the excruciating co-occurring conditions (like severe gastrointestinal issues or life-threatening epilepsy) that plague many with profound autism. By viewing all medical research through the lens of a political battle against "neuronormativity," the radical neurodiversity movement often actively hinders the medical advancements that could alleviate tremendous suffering.

None of this is to say that the current mental health and economic systems are without deep flaws. The incarceration of the mentally ill is a national disgrace. The fact that access to care is tied to employment and wealth is a brutal indictment of our healthcare system. Chapman is absolutely correct that poverty, racism, and imperialism exacerbate disability and trauma. But the solution to the medical neglect and social marginalization of the severely neurodivergent is not to declare war on the concept of medical normality itself.

A truly compassionate future for neurodiversity must be bipartite. It must vigorously defend the rights, accommodations, and dignity of those whose neurodivergence is primarily a matter of difference—those who can thrive in society if given the right flexibility and respect. But it must simultaneously recognize the tragic reality of severe neurological and psychiatric *disease*. It must advocate for better hospitals, more effective medications, robust social care systems, and deeply funded medical research.

Revolutionary politics might offer a compelling narrative of collective struggle, but it cannot cure a delusion, stop a seizure, or replace the grueling, everyday reality of medical caregiving. By collapsing the clinical into the political, “Neurodivergent Power” risks leaving the most profoundly disabled behind in a cloud of radical rhetoric. True liberation must include the right to access medical treatment and the acknowledgment that some forms of neurodivergence are, undeniably, illnesses that cause suffering—suffering that we have a moral obligation to treat, not merely to accommodate.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The socio-political model of neurodiversity fractures when applied to severe psychiatric conditions like schizophrenia or bipolar disorder, which cause intrinsic suffering independent of societal or economic structures.
  2. 02By framing debilitating symptoms as politically oppressed 'ways of being,' the movement risks repeating the mistakes of the anti-psychiatry movement and abandoning those who require essential medical intervention and clinical care.
  3. 03The 'Neurodivergent Power' framework glosses over the drastically different needs within the community, falsely equating the experiences of high-functioning individuals with those of people with profound, life-limiting disabilities.
  4. 04Politicizing all medical research as a fight against 'neuronormativity' can actively hinder scientific advancements that could alleviate excruciating co-occurring conditions and improve quality of life.
  5. 05A truly compassionate approach must be bipartite, defending the rights of those with neurological differences while simultaneously acknowledging and treating the reality of neurological and psychiatric diseases.
Diversified Gemini

"While Robert Chapman correctly critiques the corporate co-optation of neurodiversity, his proposed radical anti-capitalist framework still fails to address the material needs and medical realities of individuals with profound cognitive and intellectual disabilities."

Robert Chapman’s essay, "The Future of Neurodiversity," lays bare a crucial tension within the modern disability rights movement: the disconnect between the glossy, corporate-friendly version of neurodivergence and the grim reality faced by those who are severely marginalized. Chapman is entirely correct that "Liberal Neurodiversity"—with its emphasis on "superpowers" and Harvard Business Review-endorsed competitive advantages—leaves behind those who cannot be easily assimilated into the capitalist workforce. However, Chapman’s proposed alternative, "Neurodivergent Power," pivoting toward a revolutionary, anti-capitalist movement, still fundamentally ignores the most vulnerable population within this sphere: those with profound cognitive and intellectual disabilities who require intensive, lifelong care.

Both Liberal Neurodiversity and Chapman’s radical Neurodivergent Power share a common blind spot: they are theories built by and for individuals who possess the cognitive capacity, communication skills, and executive function to advocate for themselves, whether that means asking for a sensory room at work or organizing a mass boycott of a university research project.

Let us ground this theoretical debate in material reality. Consider an autistic adult who is non-speaking, experiences profound intellectual disability, requires assistance with toileting and feeding, and engages in severe self-injurious behaviors (such as head-banging or biting) when distressed. How does this individual fit into the paradigm of Liberal Neurodiversity? They do not possess a "superpower" that can be monetized by a tech company. They will not be celebrating Autistic Pride Day at an open mic night. The liberal framework of "reasonable accommodations" in the workplace is entirely irrelevant to someone who will never enter the workforce.

Now, how does this same individual fit into Chapman’s vision of Neurodivergent Power? Chapman calls for "mass organization," "unionizing against neuronormativity," and building a "revolutionary party." These are activities that require immense cognitive and communicative resources. To suggest that profound cognitive disability is merely a variation of human experience that has been rendered "unproductive" by capitalism is a profound distortion of reality. Severe self-injury is not a protest against "neuronormativity"; it is a sign of deep, neurological distress that requires intensive medical and behavioral intervention.

Chapman critiques the medical model and the search for biomarkers (such as the Spectrum 10k project), framing such research as inherently eugenicist. This is a common talking point among neurodiversity advocates, but it is deeply alarming to parents and caregivers of the profoundly disabled. While no one wants a return to the dark days of institutionalization and eugenics, the complete rejection of the medical model denies the reality that for some, neurodivergence is accompanied by severe, debilitating co-occurring conditions, such as intractable epilepsy, severe gastrointestinal disorders, and sleep disorders. We need medical research to understand these conditions and develop treatments that can alleviate suffering. By framing all neurological research as an act of oppression, the radical neurodiversity movement actively campaigns against the very science that could improve the quality of life for the most severely affected.

The true crisis facing the most vulnerable neurodivergent individuals is not merely capitalism or imperialism; it is the crisis of care. Across the world, families are collapsing under the weight of providing 24/7 care for adults with profound disabilities. When aging parents can no longer care for their children, these individuals often end up in underfunded state facilities, or worse, the criminal justice system, which, as Chapman rightly points out, is disastrously unequipped to handle them.

The liberation of this specific population will not be achieved through a vanguardist political party or an alliance with anti-imperialist struggles. It will be achieved through the unglamorous, resource-intensive work of building a robust, fully funded social care infrastructure. We need highly trained, well-paid direct support professionals. We need accessible, specialized medical care that understands profound autism and intellectual disability. We need comprehensive respite care for families.

Chapman argues that "changing economic relations is unlikely to be achieved through liberal, rights-based reformism alone." But it is precisely through the levers of the state—taxation, legislation, and public funding—that complex care infrastructure is built. In their rush to dismantle the "neoliberal order," Chapman fails to articulate how a post-capitalist society would actually manage the highly specialized, medically complex, and constant care required by those with profound disabilities. History shows us that revolutionary movements are often ruthlessly utilitarian; those who cannot contribute to the struggle or the new society are often the first to be discarded.

Furthermore, Chapman's assertion that "individually, we are disabled; united, we hold a form of strength" is a beautiful sentiment, but it relies on a capacity for unity and collective action that some individuals simply do not possess. An individual with profound intellectual disability cannot "unite" in the political sense Chapman describes. They rely entirely on proxies—parents, caregivers, and advocates—to represent their needs.

The future of neurodiversity must indeed move beyond the superficial corporate inclusion that Chapman so effectively critiques. But the answer is not a retreat into abstract Marxist theory. The answer is a radical recentering of the movement around vulnerability, care, and dependence. We must build a society that values individuals not because they have hidden "superpowers," nor because they are potential comrades in a revolutionary struggle, but simply because they are human beings who deserve safety, comfort, and dignity. Until the neurodiversity movement can adequately address the realities of profound intellectual disability and the staggering demands of lifelong caregiving, its visions for the future—whether liberal or radical—will remain critically incomplete.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Both liberal and radical neurodiversity paradigms are designed by and for high-functioning individuals, effectively excluding those who lack the cognitive or communicative capacity for self-advocacy and political organization.
  2. 02The radical rejection of the medical model and neurological research ignores the necessity of scientific intervention to alleviate the suffering caused by severe co-occurring conditions like self-injury and epilepsy.
  3. 03Profound disability is not merely a social construct of capitalist 'unproductivity' but a biological reality that requires intensive, specialized medical and behavioral support.
  4. 04The liberation of the most vulnerable neurodivergent people depends on a robust, state-funded social care infrastructure rather than abstract revolutionary struggle or anti-imperialist alliances.
  5. 05Chapman’s vision of collective power fails to account for individuals who cannot participate in political unity and must rely on caregivers and proxies to represent their interests.
  6. 06A truly inclusive neurodiversity movement must center itself on the ethics of care, vulnerability, and lifelong dependence rather than economic utility or political agency.
