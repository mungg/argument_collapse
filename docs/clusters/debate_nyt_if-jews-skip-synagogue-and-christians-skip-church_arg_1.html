Religious leaders should resist the most comforting interpretation of these numbers: that people are still “basically ours” and need only be nudged back into the pews. The Pew findings suggest something more serious and also more promising. Many Americans still want inherited identity, moral vocabulary, ritual at life’s thresholds, community in crisis and a story larger than the self. For Jews, the lesson is especially clear. Jewish identity has never been reducible to synagogue attendance or doctrinal belief. Culture, peoplehood, memory, family, Israel, ethics, food, humor and learning all carry Jewishness. If many Jews say they are proud to be Jewish while also saying religion is not central to their lives, synagogues should not respond by scolding them for inconsistency. For Christians, the challenge is parallel. Nominal affiliation remains high, but regular participation is much lower, and younger Americans are increasingly comfortable saying they have no religious affiliation. Some have drifted because Sunday morning is crowded with work, sports and exhaustion. A congregation cannot program its way around a loss of trust.

The answer, then, is not simply better marketing, livelier music or more aggressive outreach, though hospitality and competence matter. Religious communities need to make a stronger case that participation changes a life. That means serious teaching for adults, not only children; worship that is beautiful, intelligible and demanding; communities small enough that people are missed when absent; and public service that is not a recruitment gimmick but an expression of faith.

Leaders should also lower unnecessary barriers. They come, and stay, when a community offers depth, obligation, friendship, consolation and a credible path toward becoming better human beings.

The “lost sheep” may not be lost. Many are waiting to see whether religious institutions can speak with humility, courage and substance.

"Religious leaders must address the decline in attendance not as a loss of faith, but as a failure of traditional institutions to adapt to modern sociological and spiritual needs."

The Pew survey should not be read as a crisis of belief but as a crisis of belonging. Roughly 22 percent of American Jews now describe themselves as having no religion, yet they still claim Jewish identity through ancestry, culture, and ethics. Among Christians, weekly attendance has been sliding for decades even as self-identification holds. The pattern is consistent: Americans have not stopped finding meaning in their inherited traditions; they have stopped finding meaning in the institutions that house them. Religious leaders who treat the numbers as a marketing problem—better music, hipper sermons, livelier websites—will misdiagnose what is happening.

The deeper issue is that synagogues and churches were built for a sociological world that no longer exists. Postwar congregations functioned as ethnic anchors, civic clubs, marriage markets, and schools all at once. Suburbanization, intermarriage, dual-career households, the cost of dues and Hebrew school, and the rise of online community have stripped away most of those secondary functions. What remains—worship, study, ritual—is precisely the part that requires the most from members and offers the least obvious "return." It is no wonder that people who still treasure the holidays, the Seder, the Christmas Eve service, or the ethical tradition find weekly attendance optional.

Three responses seem more honest than the usual hand-wringing. First, leaders should stop measuring success by pews filled on Saturday or Sunday morning. By Pew's own data, Jews of no religion still light candles, fast on Yom Kippur, and care intensely about Israel and anti-Semitism. Christians who skip church still pray, read scripture, and baptize their children. A congregation that meets these people where they actually are—through life-cycle events, holiday gateways, social-justice work, adult learning, online study—will reach more souls than one that demands weekly attendance as the price of admission.

Second, clergy should take seriously that many Americans find their institutions theologically thin or politically compromised. Younger Americans in particular tell pollsters they leave because churches seem judgmental, anti-science, or partisan; Jews under 30 cite irrelevance more than disbelief. The remedy is not to soften everything into therapeutic vagueness, which only accelerates the drift, but to offer something distinctive—serious texts, demanding ethics, real community obligations—that secular life cannot replicate.

Third, congregations should lower the financial and bureaucratic walls. Mandatory dues, building campaigns, and pay-to-pray High Holiday tickets send a message that membership is a consumer transaction. Free or sliding-scale access, smaller minyanim, house churches, and partnerships with Hillel or campus ministries can rebuild the muscle of showing up.

The "lost sheep" are not lost. They are standing outside the gate wondering whether what is inside is worth the cost of entry. The answer must come from the shepherds.

The latest Pew survey on Jewish Americans confirms what many religious leaders already know in their bones: identity is not the same as practice. A robust 94 percent of Jews surveyed are proud to be Jewish, even as secularism rises and synagogue attendance falls. Similarly, the vast majority of Americans still call themselves Christian, but only a minority warm a pew on Sunday. The impulse to ask how we might recapture these “lost sheep” is understandable, but it misreads the moment. People aren’t lost; they’re quietly renegotiating what faith means to them, often outside the walls of traditional congregations.

The first takeaway is that belonging still matters deeply. The Pew data showed that Jewish identity is increasingly rooted in culture, ancestry and ethics rather than in belief in God or adherence to religious law. Many Jews who intermarry raise children with some Jewish consciousness, and most celebrate a Passover seder. Among Christians, the rapid rise of the “nones”—one in five Americans overall, and a third of adults under 30—has not erased a stubborn, diffuse sense of being Christian. This suggests that the craving for community, story and moral horizon endures, even when institutional religion feels irrelevant or alienating.

If congregations hope to reverse the drift, they should stop seeing the problem as a marketing failure or a need for more entertainment. The people who stay away often do so not out of laziness but out of a conviction that religious bodies are too dogmatic, too politicized or too inward-looking. A 2012 Pew study found that most unaffiliated Americans see religious institutions as overly concerned with money and rules rather than with justice. The path back, then, is not to lower the bar but to raise the table: to become places where honest doubt is welcomed, where interfaith families aren’t seen as a crisis but as a fact of life, and where action on poverty, immigration or the environment makes the ancient texts feel urgently moral.

The metaphor of “lost sheep” hints at a passive flock waiting to be herded home. But today’s seekers are often more like pilgrims who have left a town that no longer nourishes them. They won’t be scolded back. They can, however, be drawn toward communities that practice what they preach—that combine contemplative depth with a tangible commitment to healing the world. Jewish denominations that fully embrace patrilineal descent, and churches that halt culture wars over sexuality, remove obstacles that no longer make sense to younger generations. At the same time, vibrant, small-group initiatives—home-based Bible studies, social-justice collectives, young-adult Shabbat dinners—often outpace formal services because they foster intimacy and shared purpose.

In the end, the numbers challenge religious leaders to make a hard distinction between fortress religion and living faith. The fortress demands conformity and rewards attendance; living faith gathers around a fire that warms both insider and stranger. The challenge is not to recapture the departed but to become the kind of community they would freely choose.
