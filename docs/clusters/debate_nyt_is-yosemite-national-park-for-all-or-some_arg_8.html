"Yosemite and other national parks should transition to a management model that balances ecological restoration with historical honesty regarding Indigenous displacement and proactive measures to ensure social accessibility for underrepresented communities."

“Is Yosemite for all or some?” is a necessary question, but it is incomplete. Yosemite was never simply empty nature waiting for tourists or preservationists to decide its purpose. It is also a homeland from which Native people were violently displaced, and a landscape whose meaning was narrowed when Americans began treating it as scenery.

That history matters for the current plan. Restoring meadows and reducing traffic may be good policy, but the debate should not be limited to environmental groups, campers, concessionaires and state officials. If the park is to be “for all,” then tribal voices should have a central role in deciding what restoration means. Ecological health is not only the absence of tourists. It may include cultural burning, traditional plant gathering, interpretation that tells the harder parts of Yosemite’s story, and employment or co-management arrangements that respect long-standing relationships to the land.

The same point applies to visitors. A park can be overrun and still not be democratic. If the people most able to enjoy Yosemite are those with cars, leisure time, camping know-how and money for lodging, then simply preserving the valley from crowds will not solve the access problem. The Park Service should pair environmental limits with outreach, affordable transportation, multilingual education and programs that welcome communities historically underrepresented in the parks.

So, yes, Yosemite should reduce activities that damage the Merced River, meadows and wildlife. It should not be managed as a theme park. But neither should it become a museum of pristine nature curated mainly for affluent hikers and photographers. The better model is a living public landscape: ecologically repaired, historically honest and socially accessible.

Other national parks should follow that broader lesson. Protection is essential, but protection alone is not justice. The parks are at their best when they preserve land, tell the truth about it and invite a wider public into responsibility for its future.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Tribal voices must have a central role in park management and restoration to honor the land's history as a displaced homeland and to incorporate traditional ecological practices.
  2. 02Environmental protection measures must be paired with affordable transportation and outreach programs to ensure the parks are democratically accessible rather than reserved for affluent visitors.
  3. 03National parks should move away from being treated as either theme parks or pristine museums, instead becoming living landscapes that integrate ecological health with cultural responsibility.
  4. 04True justice in the national park system requires moving beyond simple land preservation to include truthful historical interpretation and the active inclusion of a wider public.
