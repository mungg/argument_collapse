"While amnesty for long-settled unauthorized migrants is a moral necessity, it must be part of a broader reform that expands legal migration pathways to ensure justice for those currently excluded from the system."

Joseph Carens writes as if the central question is when unauthorized migrants become members of the receiving society. That is indeed an important question. But another question must be asked alongside it: what do receiving societies owe to outsiders who never manage to enter? Carens’s answer risks making physical presence the key to moral priority. The result is compassionate toward those already here but troublingly arbitrary from a global point of view.

Consider two workers. One crosses the U.S.-Mexico border without authorization, finds work, and remains for six years. The other, equally poor and equally hardworking, stays in Oaxaca, Manila, Port-au-Prince, or Accra because she cannot afford a smuggler, cannot risk the journey, must care for a parent, or chooses not to violate another country’s laws. Under Carens’s proposal, the first worker gains a right to legal residence. The second gains nothing. What moral principle justifies this difference?

Carens would say that the first worker has become a social member through residence. That is true as far as it goes. Human relationships generate claims. But if we focus only on social membership within the receiving state, we may reward morally accidental advantages: proximity to the border, access to migration networks, physical stamina, money for smugglers, or willingness to take legal risks. The very poorest and most constrained are often least able to migrate. An amnesty based solely on presence may therefore favor the relatively mobile among the global poor.

This objection should not be confused with the familiar “waiting in line” complaint. Carens is right that for many low-wage migrants there is no real line. My point is different. The absence of a line is itself a moral failure. If the United States and other wealthy democracies need immigrant labor and if migration can dramatically improve human lives, then they should create lawful channels that are fair, transparent, and accessible. Amnesty for those who have already arrived may be necessary, but it should not become a substitute for justice toward those excluded.

The danger is that receiving countries will continue the cycle: restrict legal entry, tolerate unauthorized labor, periodically legalize the settled population, and then repeat. This cycle benefits employers and the migrants lucky enough to survive it, but it is not a principled immigration system. It is a lottery conducted through illegality. Carens’s time-based amnesty would reduce cruelty at the back end, but unless paired with front-end reform it would leave the global distribution of opportunity largely untouched.

A more complete approach would combine legalization for long-settled unauthorized migrants with expanded legal migration pathways for future workers. The United States should admit more low-wage workers through visas that protect rights and allow mobility between employers. Temporary-worker programs have a bad history, including the Bracero Program, because tying workers to employers invites abuse. But the lesson is not that low-wage migration must be illegal. The lesson is that visas should be portable, wages enforceable, recruitment regulated, and workers given a path to permanence if they put down roots.

Family immigration also needs attention. Long backlogs create incentives for unlawful presence and impose years of separation. If the law says family unity matters, it should not make spouses and children wait in limbo for unreasonable periods. Likewise, students educated in American schools should not be blocked from building lives because of decisions made by their parents.

Carens’s essay is strongest when he describes people like Miguel Sanchez and Hiu Lui Ng. It would be morally obtuse to deny their ties. But stories can narrow vision as well as expand it. The person standing before us naturally commands sympathy. The person who never crossed the border remains invisible. A just policy must care about both.

This matters especially because liberal democracies often justify exclusion through formal equality: everyone abroad is equally unauthorized unless admitted. In practice, however, exclusion is not equal. Geography matters. Mexicans and Central Americans can attempt land crossings, though at great danger. Others must obtain visas or fly, and visa regimes screen heavily by wealth and nationality. Border enforcement pushes migrants into deserts, increasing deaths. Smuggling networks profit. A system that makes unlawful entry physically possible for some and impossible for others cannot claim that subsequent amnesty simply recognizes neutral facts.

Still, the right response is not to reject amnesty. Deporting settled migrants will not help those abroad. It will merely add suffering. Moreover, once people have lived in a society for years, their claims are real even if others also have claims. Moral life is full of such layering. Parents owe special duties to their children without denying duties to strangers. Communities owe something to residents because residence creates interdependence.

But Carens’s proposal should be embedded in a broader ethic of mobility. Wealthy states should ask not only, “When do unauthorized migrants become ours?” but also, “Why do we provide so few lawful opportunities to people whose labor we welcome and whose poverty is partly shaped by global inequalities?” Trade policies, agricultural subsidies, demand for cheap labor, and historical ties all complicate the idea that migration is simply an individual choice to break another country’s law.

An amnesty worthy of support should therefore include three commitments. First, legalize those who have become members through long residence, family, work, or childhood arrival. Second, create future legal channels that reduce the need for unlawful migration. Third, strengthen labor rights so that migration does not become a race to the bottom.

Carens gives us a necessary principle: time and social membership erode the moral force of deportation. But taken alone, that principle is too inward-looking. It tells us how to treat those who got in, not how to structure opportunity for those kept out. Liberal democracies should grant amnesty, but they should do so with humility. Legalization repairs one injustice. It does not exhaust the demands of immigration justice.
