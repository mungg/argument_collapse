"The United States should approve the Smithfield acquisition only if it uses the deal as leverage to demand reciprocal market access and transparency from China."

The Smithfield deal should be viewed less as a referendum on pork than as a test of reciprocity. The United States remains one of the world’s most open markets for foreign investment. China, by contrast, often restricts foreign ownership, pressures companies to form joint ventures, favors national champions and uses regulation as an instrument of industrial policy. If Chinese firms can buy major American companies while American firms face closed doors in China, then the system is not free trade. It is unilateral openness.

That does not automatically mean Washington should block Shuanghui. Openness is a strength. Foreign capital can keep American companies competitive, reward shareholders, preserve jobs and expand exports. Smithfield has struggled with debt and volatile commodity prices, and access to China’s enormous pork market could benefit American producers. A flat ban on Chinese acquisitions would be crude, self-defeating and likely to provoke retaliation.

But neither should the United States pretend that all investors operate under the same rules. When a Chinese company makes a multibillion-dollar bid, regulators should examine not only the immediate transaction but also the institutional environment behind it. Who finances the purchase? What role do state banks play? Are there hidden subsidies? Would an American meat company be allowed to make a comparable acquisition in China? If the answer is no, that fact should matter.

The right response is a more hardheaded form of openness. CFIUS should review genuine security concerns, including supply-chain vulnerabilities and sensitive agricultural technology. Antitrust and food-safety regulators should do their jobs without deference to diplomatic sensitivities. At the same time, trade officials should use the moment to press China for reciprocal investment rights. Approval, if granted, should come with clear conditions and a public explanation of why the transaction does not compromise American interests.

This approach avoids two mistakes. One is xenophobia dressed up as prudence: the idea that Chinese ownership is inherently illegitimate. The other is naïveté dressed up as globalization: the idea that capital flows exist in a world free of state power. The United States should not close its market, but it should stop giving away the advantages of openness for free. If Shuanghui wants the benefits of the American system, China should be expected to move toward the same standards for American investors.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current investment relationship between the U.S. and China is characterized by a lack of reciprocity, where Chinese firms enjoy open access to American markets while American firms face significant restrictions in China.
  2. 02A total ban on Chinese acquisitions would be counterproductive because foreign capital can preserve American jobs, reward shareholders, and expand export opportunities.
  3. 03Regulators must investigate the institutional environment behind Chinese bids, including the role of state-sponsored financing and hidden subsidies.
  4. 04The Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS) and other regulators should prioritize national security, supply-chain integrity, and food safety over diplomatic sensitivities.
  5. 05U.S. trade officials should utilize major acquisition attempts as diplomatic pressure points to force China to adopt the same investment standards required of American companies.
