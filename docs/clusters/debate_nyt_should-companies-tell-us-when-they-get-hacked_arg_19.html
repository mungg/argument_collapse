"The federal government should implement a clear, scaled, and uniform mandatory breach disclosure law to protect consumers while ensuring the requirements are practical for businesses of all sizes."

For small and midsize companies, “mandatory breach disclosure” can sound like another expensive legal trap. A national retailer can hire forensic consultants, outside counsel and a crisis communications firm. A local medical practice, online shop or software start-up may have one overworked administrator and a shared hosting provider. Any disclosure law that ignores that difference will be resented and evaded.

Still, customers should not bear the cost of silence. Even a small business can hold information that matters: payment card numbers, passwords reused on other sites, home addresses, medical details, tax forms or employee records. If that information is exposed, the person affected needs notice. A stolen password can be changed. A compromised bank card can be replaced. A Social Security number cannot be made secret again, but a consumer can at least monitor accounts and place fraud alerts.

The best answer is a clear federal standard that replaces the current maze of state rules. Today, a company with customers in many states may have to interpret dozens of different breach notification laws. That does not help consumers; it mainly helps lawyers. A uniform rule would be easier for honest small businesses to follow and harder for large companies to game.

The rule should be scaled and practical. Notice should be required when sensitive personal information was acquired or is reasonably likely to have been acquired by an unauthorized person. Encrypted data should generally qualify for a safe harbor if the key was not compromised. The law should allow a brief investigation period to determine what happened, but it should impose outer limits so companies cannot delay indefinitely. Notices should be simple and inexpensive: e-mail may be sufficient in many cases, especially for online services.

The law should also avoid turning every breach into a lawsuit lottery. Companies that had reasonable security, discovered the problem, reported promptly and helped affected customers should not face ruinous penalties merely because they were attacked. Companies that were reckless, ignored warnings or covered up breaches should face serious consequences.

Mandatory disclosure, done badly, becomes a burden. Done well, it creates a fair rule of the road: if you collect people’s data, you must protect it; if you lose control of it, you must tell them.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Consumers have a right to be notified of security breaches so they can take immediate action to mitigate identity theft and financial fraud.
  2. 02A single federal standard should replace the current patchwork of state laws to reduce legal complexity and increase compliance for small businesses.
  3. 03Disclosure requirements should be practical and scaled, allowing for brief investigation periods and inexpensive notification methods like email.
  4. 04The law should distinguish between companies that maintained reasonable security and those that were reckless or attempted to cover up a breach.
  5. 05Mandatory disclosure creates a necessary accountability framework where the responsibility to protect data includes the responsibility to report its loss.
