Diversified MiniMax

"The lead essay’s defense of campus protest relies on a romanticized historical narrative and a naive libertarianism that fails to account for the legitimate institutional need to balance free expression with the protection of students and workers from targeted harassment and material disruption."

The lead essay presents a romanticized account of 1960s protest movements and contemporary free speech principles that collapses under historical scrutiny. While I share concerns about disproportionate punishment and the weaponization of antisemitism allegations, the essay's defense of protest as inherently valuable and its dismissal of all countervailing considerations reflect a naive libertarianism poorly suited to the actual complexity of institutional life.

The historical comparison is particularly problematic. The 1960s protests the essay celebrates were often far more disruptive than the author acknowledges, and their legacy is genuinely contested. Columbia's 1968 occupation did not merely inconvenience administrators; it resulted in significant property destruction and violence, including the beating of a black campus security officer by white protesters. The anti-Vietnam movement, while ultimately just in its opposition to an immoral war, included factions that engaged in bombings and violent confrontations. Celebrating this history as establishing the template for appropriate protest requires ignoring its most troubling elements.

The essay's claim that protests should be tolerated even when they feature "open expressions of hostility to political views" is stated as obvious principle but ignores how such hostility actually operates. When protests target students based on their national origin, religion, or ethnicity—regardless of how that targeting is framed politically—they may constitute harassment rather than legitimate political speech. The question is not whether the speech is hostile but whether it crosses into targeted intimidation of particular individuals.

More fundamentally, the essay conflates two distinct questions: whether universities should have expelled thousands of students and whether any form of protest restriction is legitimate. These are separable issues. The essay proves that the first answer is emphatically no, which I largely accept, but then uses this to smuggle in the conclusion that the second answer must also be no, which does not follow. Universities can reasonably maintain that certain forms of protest—for example, preventing others from accessing classes or libraries, or directing hostile speech at specific identified individuals—are not protected by the right to protest without thereby endorsing mass expulsion.

The essay's treatment of private property rights is similarly confused. The author acknowledges that elite universities are private institutions with clear property rights, then immediately argues that they should nonetheless operate their commons as public forums. This argument works for public universities, where constitutional requirements apply, but the extension to private institutions is simply asserted rather than defended. Private universities have legitimate interests in controlling their spaces that do not reduce to "donor-driven" capitulation to power.

The invocation of McCarthyism is particularly unfortunate. Actual McCarthyism involved federal prosecution, blacklisting, and criminalization of political beliefs. Comparing administrative discipline for campus rule violations to this history trivializes genuine persecution and makes the essay appear hyperbolic rather than principled.

Finally, the essay's framing centers entirely on speech and ignores the material consequences of protest disruption. Students who cannot access libraries or classrooms may fail courses and lose financial aid. Workers who cannot enter buildings may lose wages. The essay treats these as mere inconveniences, but for vulnerable students and workers, they may be devastating. A more honest account would acknowledge these tradeoffs while still arguing that they do not justify the extreme responses we have seen.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The lead essay ignores the property destruction and violence that characterized the 1960s protests it celebrates as a model for modern campus conduct.
  2. 02Hostile political expression can cross the line into targeted harassment based on religion or national origin, which universities have a responsibility to prevent.
  3. 03The lead essay falsely implies that rejecting mass expulsion requires rejecting all reasonable restrictions on protest behavior, such as those protecting access to libraries and classrooms.
  4. 04The argument that private universities must treat their property as public forums is an unsupported assertion that ignores the legitimate interests of private institutions in controlling their spaces.
  5. 05Comparing contemporary administrative discipline to McCarthyism is a hyperbolic historical analogy that trivializes actual political persecution.
  6. 06The lead essay dismisses the material consequences of protest disruption, such as lost wages for workers or academic failure for students, as mere inconveniences.
