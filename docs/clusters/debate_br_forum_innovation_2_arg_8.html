"While Dan Breznitz correctly warns against copying Silicon Valley's flaws, the stage-one innovation model of high-growth entrepreneurship remains essential for solving global crises and should be reformed through better institutions rather than dismissed."

Dan Breznitz is right to warn cities against trying to copy Silicon Valley. But in correcting one myth, he risks encouraging another: that the venture-backed, novelty-focused model is mostly a distraction from the real work of inclusive prosperity. The truth is more complicated. Silicon Valley’s problems are real—housing unaffordability, inequality, racial exclusion, monopoly power, tax avoidance, and a financial culture obsessed with exits. But the lesson should not be that stage one innovation is inherently thin, elitist, or locally destructive. It should be that powerful innovation ecosystems require complementary institutions. Silicon Valley has too few of them, but that does not mean the underlying model of high-growth entrepreneurship has nothing to teach.

The first point to make in defense of Silicon Valley is that the world actually needs novelty. It is easy, and often justified, to praise incremental innovation: the better gear system, the cheaper component, the more reliable production process. Much of human welfare does depend on continuous improvement. But we should not minimize the importance of radical invention, especially in moments of crisis. The mRNA vaccine platforms that have become central to the pandemic response did not come from bicycle-style incrementalism alone. They emerged from decades of risky scientific work, biotech entrepreneurship, public funding, private capital, and regulatory adaptation. The same can be said for many technologies needed for decarbonization: long-duration storage, advanced nuclear designs, carbon-free industrial heat, green hydrogen, direct air capture, and better grid software. Some will fail. Some may prove overhyped. But societies that cannot generate and scale novelty will be badly equipped for the twenty-first century.

Breznitz acknowledges system-wide stage one innovation, especially in decarbonization, but his essay’s rhetorical center of gravity lies elsewhere. The cautionary tale of Israel is meant to show that a thriving high-tech sector can coexist with poverty and stagnant wages. That is true. Yet Israel’s experience does not prove that venture-backed innovation is a poor development strategy in all cases. It proves that a high-tech sector, if poorly connected to the rest of the economy and heavily oriented toward foreign exits, will not by itself produce inclusive prosperity. That is an institutional failure, not a necessary property of start-ups.

A similar point applies to Silicon Valley. The region’s inequality is not simply the result of venture capital funding software firms. It is also the result of land-use policy that restricts housing supply, fragmented local governance, weak American labor protections, underinvestment in transit, exclusionary school systems, and a tax structure that lets enormous capital gains coexist with public scarcity. If the Bay Area had built housing at anything like the rate demanded by its job growth, if it had stronger wage floors and sectoral training, if public institutions captured more of the gains from platform monopolies, and if transportation connected lower-cost communities to job centers, the local distributional picture would look different. Venture capital did not force Palo Alto, Cupertino, and San Francisco to make it nearly impossible to build enough homes. The innovation model and the governance model must be analytically separated.

This matters because many places that hear “don’t copy Silicon Valley” may conclude that they should avoid high-growth entrepreneurship altogether. That would be a mistake. The United States has an unusual ability to create new firms that scale quickly. This capacity is one reason it remains technologically powerful. Venture capital is imperfect, faddish, and often socially wasteful. It funds too many delivery apps, advertising technologies, speculative financial products, and companies designed to arbitrage labor law. But it also funds companies that banks would never touch because they lack collateral and predictable cash flow. A society that wants new therapeutics, robotics, climate technologies, and advanced software needs risk capital.

The problem is that venture capital is too narrow a form of risk capital. It seeks extraordinary returns, prefers winner-take-all markets, and pushes firms toward rapid scaling and exit. For some sectors, that works. For others—advanced manufacturing, hardware, clean energy, medical devices, industrial processes—it is often a poor fit. Rather than rejecting the Silicon Valley model, we should pluralize it. We need venture capital, but also patient capital, public equity stakes, revenue-based financing, cooperative finance, procurement-backed loans, and regional development banks. We need more ways for innovative firms to grow without being forced into the unicorn-or-bust path.

Breznitz’s concept of “tech teens” is helpful here, and it may be where defenders and critics of Silicon Valley can meet. Many of the most important firms are not two founders with a pitch deck, and they are not giant incumbents. They are companies that have found a market, built a product, hired a team, and now need capital and coordination to expand. Traditional VC may ignore them because they are not likely to produce a hundredfold return. Banks may ignore them because their assets are intangible. Public policy should fill that gap. But some of the skills these firms need—product management, recruiting, technical sales, organizational scaling, connections to investors and customers—are precisely the skills Silicon Valley developed unusually well. The answer is not to sneer at start-up culture but to adapt its useful parts to more sectors and regions.

The same is true of universities. Many cities have tried to turn research universities into start-up engines with disappointing results. But universities remain crucial. They train people, generate ideas, convene networks, and provide legitimacy. The mistake is believing that a university plus an incubator equals an ecosystem. Successful regions require experienced managers, early customers, specialized lawyers, suppliers, mentors, technical workers, and investors with domain knowledge. These are not created overnight. Silicon Valley’s advantage is not only venture capital; it is a dense labor market and a culture in which people move among firms, carry knowledge with them, and start again after failure. That culture cannot be photocopied, but its components can be studied.

Breznitz’s stage framework is valuable because it broadens the menu of possible strategies. Yet the stages should not be treated as separate ladders, with communities choosing one and staying there. The most resilient regions connect stages. Taiwan’s semiconductor strength is not merely stage three production innovation; it depends on design, equipment, materials, process engineering, customer relationships, and research. Shenzhen’s manufacturing ecosystem increasingly feeds back into design and product development. Silicon Valley itself, despite decades of offshoring, still benefits from proximity to specialized hardware, biotech labs, prototyping shops, and advanced research. The future likely belongs to places that can recombine stages, not merely occupy one.

This is especially important for the United States as it debates industrial policy. The semiconductor shortage has reminded policymakers that design leadership without fabrication capacity creates vulnerability. But rebuilding fabs is not enough. The country needs the whole ecology: equipment, chemicals, substrates, technicians, engineers, process knowledge, and customers. Public subsidies for one large plant will not recreate a missing ecosystem. On that point Breznitz is exactly right. Still, it would be equally foolish to imagine that production capacity can thrive without close links to frontier research and entrepreneurial experimentation. Advanced manufacturing is not a nostalgic return to the midcentury factory; it is deeply technological and often software-intensive.

The deeper issue is not Silicon Valley versus “real” innovation. It is whether innovation systems are governed for public purpose. A venture-backed software company that improves medical diagnostics, reduces energy waste, or expands access to education can contribute to welfare. A manufacturing cluster that pays poverty wages and pollutes nearby communities cannot be rescued by the label “stage four innovation.” What matters is the alignment of technology, business models, labor institutions, and public goals.

So let us retire the fantasy that every city can become the next Silicon Valley. But let us not replace it with a reverse snobbery that treats Silicon Valley mainly as a cautionary tale. The region’s achievements are substantial: it has generated companies and technologies that changed the world, attracted extraordinary talent, and created a model of rapid experimentation that remains powerful. Its failures are also substantial: it has distributed gains badly, empowered monopolies, inflated housing costs, and encouraged a shallow ideology of disruption.

The task is synthesis. We need Silicon Valley’s appetite for risk without its indifference to place. We need Taiwan’s production learning without assuming every region can become a semiconductor hub. We need Italy’s craft-based specialization without romanticizing small firms. We need public procurement, patient capital, housing reform, labor standards, and antitrust enforcement. We need radical invention and incremental improvement.

Innovation is not a single road. Breznitz is right about that. But one of the roads still runs through the territory Silicon Valley mapped. The challenge is not to abandon it; it is to civilize it.
