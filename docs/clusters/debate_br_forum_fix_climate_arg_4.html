"While the experimentalist governance framework correctly identifies that climate progress depends on sectoral learning and technical feasibility rather than abstract global treaties, it must be supplemented by active movement-building and the symbolic legitimacy of the Paris Agreement to create the political space necessary for ambition."

Victor and Sabel have produced an essay of genuine intellectual distinction. Their experimentalist governance framework offers something increasingly rare in climate policy discourse: a pragmatic, empirically grounded approach that actually engages with how governance works in practice rather than how idealists imagine it should. I find myself largely persuaded by their analysis and want to extend it in a direction they only hint at.

The core insight—that climate diplomacy has been paralyzed by the assumption that solutions must be global from the outset—is exactly right. The Paris Agreement represents the logical endpoint of this assumption: a framework so accommodating that it allows every nation to do essentially nothing while maintaining the appearance of serious commitment. The NDC system is a masterpiece of diplomatic theater but a failure as policy architecture. When the measure of success is whether emissions peak, not whether they fall, you've already conceded the argument to the obstructionists.

The Montreal Protocol's success, by contrast, demonstrates what becomes possible when governance is organized around learning rather than predetermined outcomes. The sectoral approach is crucial here. Organizing problem-solving by industry—solvents, foams, refrigerants—allows regulators to engage directly with the technical realities that global frameworks obscure. When assessment panels review what's actually working in pharmaceutical propellants versus industrial refrigeration, they generate the kind of granular knowledge that enables genuine progress. This is fundamentally different from asking nations to commit to abstract percentage reductions with no mechanism for determining feasibility.

What strikes me as most important, and underemphasized in Victor and Sabel's account, is how experimentalism creates political space for ambition. The "penalty default" mechanism they describe—where early innovators make it risky for laggards to resist—isn't just an enforcement tool. It's a political strategy. When California proved that vehicle emissions standards could work, it shifted the terrain of debate. When EU demonstrated that renewable integration was feasible, it became harder for other nations to claim impossibility. Each successful experiment doesn't just solve a piece of the problem; it changes what seems politically possible.

This suggests a more active role for advocates than experimentalism might initially imply. Rather than waiting for solutions to emerge organically from local experimentation, there is value in deliberately creating the conditions for visible, high-profile successes. If shipping emissions become the site of a Maersk-led demonstration program that proves zero-carbon fuels viable, that changes the politics of maritime regulation everywhere. The experimentalist framework provides the governance architecture, but movement-building remains essential to populate that architecture with ambitious experiments.

The distinction Victor and Sabel draw between globalized, concentrated industries and place-based sectors is useful, but I think they understate the importance of the former. Deep decarbonization requires transforming the sectors they identify as "globalized and concentrated"—steel, cement, shipping, aviation. These industries are responsible for a substantial fraction of remaining emissions, and their global reach means that standards set in one jurisdiction ripple outward. The International Maritime Organization's slow progress on shipping emissions reflects precisely the dysfunction that experimentalism aims to address, but it also shows how high the stakes are. If Maersk and similar firms can be brought into sectoral demonstration programs with credible penalty defaults for nonparticipation, the effects could be transformative.

I want to push back, however, on the essay's treatment of the Paris Agreement. Victor and Sabel argue that Paris "has little to contribute" to the actual work of decarbonization. This seems to me to underestimate its political utility. Paris provides the legitimacy framework within which activist pressure operates. When Extinction Rebellion disrupts airline operations or when shareholders file climate resolutions, they invoke Paris. When journalists cover climate policy, they frame it against Paris commitments. This symbolic architecture matters politically, even if the agreement itself has no enforcement teeth. Paris is, as the essay acknowledges, the most legitimate venue for global climate discussion—a role that cannot easily be replicated by sectoral institutions no matter how technically sophisticated.

The essay's invocation of John Dewey is apt. Dewey's pragmatism emphasized learning through experience, adapting institutions to new circumstances, and integrating expert knowledge with democratic participation. Victor and Sabel's synthesis of these ideas with Montreal's institutional innovations points toward a genuinely post-ideological approach to climate governance—one that abandons both the fantasy of binding global commitments and the despair of "every little bit helps" incrementalism. Instead, it offers a pathway for ambitious action that remains responsive to technical reality and political constraint.

What remains underspecified is how to scale this approach. Montreal took over a decade to demonstrate success and required favorable geopolitical conditions—the Cold War's waning, American leadership, manageable industry structure. Climate change demands faster transformation across more diverse and politically powerful sectors. The experimentalist framework needs supplementation with strategies for accelerating the pace of institutional development. Perhaps that means more aggressive use of penalty defaults—immediate trade sanctions for noncompliance rather than graduated responses. Perhaps it means larger multilateral funds to make alternatives economically compelling for developing countries. Perhaps it means building experimentalist institutions in parallel with rather than after securing political commitments.

Regardless, Victor and Sabel have identified the right problem and pointed toward promising solutions. The world does not lack for ambitious climate targets; it lacks for governance mechanisms capable of achieving them. Experimentalist governance, properly understood and aggressively pursued, offers a way forward that neither corporate voluntarism nor global treaty-making has provided. The essay deserves the serious engagement it has already received and should inform how policymakers think about institutional design for the decades of difficult work ahead.

Diversified MiniMax

"While experimentalist governance offers a superior framework for climate action compared to traditional diplomacy, its success depends on the deliberate construction of political preconditions and the strategic utilization of the Paris Agreement's legitimacy."

Victor and Sabel's essay is the best synthesis I have read of how governance actually works in complex policy domains. Its application to climate change is illuminating and I largely endorse its conclusions. But I want to push on two points: the essay's underestimation of the political preconditions for experimentalism, and its too-quick dismissal of the Paris Agreement.

On the first point: experimentalist governance requires that actors have incentives to participate in good faith, that monitoring mechanisms can distinguish genuine failure from willful obstruction, and that penalty defaults are credible and enforceable. None of these conditions emerges automatically. They must be constructed through political effort that the essay does not adequately address.

The Montreal Protocol's success depended on American leadership—a "hegemon" that Victor and Sabel rightly identify as no longer available. In the 1980s, the United States was willing and able to impose costs on recalcitrant actors: trade sanctions threatened countries that violated the treaty, diplomatic pressure kept developing countries in compliance, and domestic politics pushed even resistant industries toward alternatives. This political scaffolding is what made the governance mechanisms function. Remove it, and the same institutions might produce the same gridlock we see in climate diplomacy.

For experimentalist climate governance to work, similar political conditions must be rebuilt. This is not primarily an institutional design problem but a political one. It requires that the United States, Europe, China, and other major powers agree on the urgency of action and the willingness to impose costs on laggards. It requires that domestic political coalitions in major emitting countries support aggressive climate policy. And it requires that the fossil fuel industry be neutralized as a political force—either through successful transition to clean energy or through sustained political pressure that makes obstruction costlier than cooperation.

None of this is assured. Victor and Sabel document the political dysfunction that has paralyzed climate policy without providing a theory of how to overcome it. Their "penalty default" mechanism assumes that once leaders demonstrate alternatives, laggards will face exclusion from markets. But markets do not automatically exclude laggards; they respond to regulations, tariffs, and other political signals. Without the political conditions that make penalty defaults credible, experimentalist governance collapses into another form of ineffective coordination.

On the Paris Agreement, I think the essay is too dismissive. Victor and Sabel argue that Paris "has little to contribute" to the actual work of decarbonization. This seems to me to confuse the agreement's substantive provisions with its political functions.

Paris does serve a crucial legitimizing role. It provides the framework within which climate activists operate, the language in which demands are made, and the benchmarks against which progress is measured. When activists pressure companies to set net-zero targets, they invoke Paris. When journalists cover climate policy, they cite Paris commitments. When diplomats negotiate sectoral agreements, they reference Paris goals. This legitimizing function is not trivial; it shapes what is politically possible.

More specifically, Paris creates institutional infrastructure for coordination that experimentalist governance might exploit. The NDC system, whatever its substantive weakness, provides a framework for tracking national commitments. The periodic review process creates opportunities for pressure and learning. The agreement's explicit recognition of climate finance creates leverage for demanding increased funding. None of this is sufficient, but it is not nothing.

Victor and Sabel might respond that these functions could be served by other institutions—that Paris is not necessary for climate governance. Perhaps. But institutional innovation is costly and uncertain. Paris exists; it has legitimacy; it has infrastructure. Experimentalism could work within and through Paris rather than around it.

I also want to note what I find most valuable in the essay: its account of how learning actually occurs in governance systems. The description of Montreal's sectoral committees—how they organized working groups, assessed alternatives, scaled successful pilots, and exempted critical uses—is the clearest synthesis I have seen of how regulatory learning can work in practice. This is not just historically interesting; it provides a roadmap for how climate governance might actually function.

The essay's distinction between globalized, concentrated industries and place-based sectors is similarly valuable. It clarifies that different governance approaches suit different contexts—that the shipping sector might be amenable to international coordination while power grid integration requires place-based solutions. This is the kind of practical nuance that climate discourse often lacks.

In the end, I read Victor and Sabel as offering a framework that is both accurate and incomplete. Accurate because the diagnosis of global climate diplomacy's failure is on point and the alternative of experimentalist governance points in the right direction. Incomplete because experimentalism requires political conditions it does not itself generate. The essay implicitly assumes a political context that is more favorable than the one we actually have. Building that context—through organizing, advocacy, and electoral engagement—is the work that remains.

Sub-arguments

  3. 03The Paris Agreement is more valuable than the lead essay suggests because it provides the essential legitimizing framework and institutional infrastructure that activists and diplomats use to demand change.
  4. 04The political power of the fossil fuel industry must be neutralized through external pressure for experimentalist 'penalty defaults' to become effective in the market.
  5. 05Experimentalist governance should be viewed as a process that works through and within existing structures like Paris rather than as a separate alternative that renders them obsolete.
