"The United States should pursue a deliberate military disengagement from Afghanistan because the population-centric counterinsurgency (COIN) strategy is fundamentally unworkable given the absence of a legitimate local partner and the institutional failures of the Afghan state."

Nir Rosen’s essay is most powerful not when it argues against counterinsurgency in the abstract, but when it shows what counterinsurgency becomes in the hands of tired men, frightened policemen, corrupt institutions, and villagers who have learned that every armed actor is dangerous. The details from Highway 601 do more to refute Washington’s Afghanistan debate than another stack of strategy papers. A doctrine that sounds humane in a field manual—protect the population, subordinate military action to politics, understand local grievances—becomes, in practice, a convoy, an interpreter, a handful of Afghan police who cannot read a map, and American soldiers who do not know whether the man in front of them is Taliban, farmer, victim, opportunist, or all four depending on the week.

Rosen is right that the United States is trying to create “something out of nothing.” That phrase may sound too sweeping. Afghanistan is not nothing: it has communities, clerics, markets, tribal and non-tribal networks, armed factions, memories, customs, and forms of authority that predate the American republic. But the “nothing” Rosen identifies is the thing Washington keeps pretending exists: a legitimate national partner capable of converting foreign military success into Afghan political order. That is what is missing. The central state is not merely weak; in many places it is predatory, irrelevant, or alien. The police do not merely need training; they often embody the insecurity from which villagers seek protection. Elections do not merely suffer fraud; they consecrate a political class that many Afghans experience as thieves with international backing.

The counterinsurgency debate in Washington often turns on a misleading question: can COIN work if done correctly? In principle, perhaps. If an outside power possesses deep local knowledge, decades of patience, fluency in local languages, disciplined troops, a legitimate indigenous partner, a functioning justice system, and a population that wants protection from insurgents more than it resents the protector, population-centric counterinsurgency may have a chance. But that list is not a strategy; it is a confession. The United States has almost none of these things in Afghanistan, and where it has fragments, they are not scalable. A good captain such as Westby can apologize to elders, share watermelon with policemen, and try to prevent arbitrary killings. But he rotates out. His replacement relearns the same village from scratch. The elders remain, as does the Taliban shadow structure, as do the police who steal sheep.

The defenders of the new strategy answer that Rosen’s evidence proves not that counterinsurgency is impossible but that it has not yet been tried with adequate resources. More troops, they say, will allow Americans and Afghans to live among the population, secure roads, prevent Taliban intimidation, and create space for governance. But Rosen’s reporting shows why this argument is circular. More troops require more Afghan partners; more Afghan partners require a state capable of recruiting, paying, disciplining, and legitimating them; that state does not exist; therefore the foreign troops must stay longer to build it. Each missing condition becomes a reason for deeper commitment.

The Iraq analogy is especially dangerous. The United States has turned the “Surge” into a parable about willpower: change generals, protect civilians, buy time, and violence declines. But Iraq’s violence had its own trajectory. The Sunni insurgency was exhausted by sectarian defeat and horrified by al Qaeda’s brutality. Shia parties had captured much of the state. Baghdad had been violently sorted. The United States did not create these facts, though it exploited them. Afghanistan offers no comparable moment. The Taliban have not been decisively defeated by a rival Afghan force; they are not generally hated in the way al Qaeda in Iraq came to be hated in Anbar; and the population is dispersed across rural terrain where “protecting the people” would require an impossible density of knowledge and presence.

Nor can the United States define the enemy coherently. Rosen notes that “Taliban” is often a category of convenience. It can mean ideological militants, local fighters, drug networks, angry relatives, paid gunmen, or men resisting abusive police. The broader the category becomes, the more enemies we create; the narrower it becomes, the less our military operations appear relevant to the political disorder we claim to be fixing. A young man with a ringtone can be sent to prison. A farmer whose water supply has been blocked must petition a foreign captain. A village becomes “bad” because insurgents pass through it at night. This is not population protection. It is armed confusion.

The strongest case for remaining in Afghanistan is that leaving may be ugly. The Taliban may take more districts. Afghan women, journalists, teachers, and local officials may be threatened. Men who worked with Americans may be killed. Pakistan may become more unstable. These are not trivial fears. But they should not be transformed into a blank check for a strategy that cannot deliver what it promises. The moral burden of withdrawal is real; so is the moral burden of staying. Every additional year of occupation means raids, air strikes, detainees, empowered warlords, corrupt police, and families forced to choose between collaboration and survival.

Rosen is also right to challenge the assumption that defeating the Taliban is necessary to defeat al Qaeda. The 9/11 attacks were planned by a transnational network that used Afghanistan as one sanctuary, not by the Taliban as a national government pursuing global war. Al Qaeda’s most important sanctuary now lies in Pakistan, where American troops cannot conduct a Helmand-style counterinsurgency. If the core American interest is preventing attacks on the United States, then the relevant tools are intelligence, police cooperation, financial tracking, border diplomacy, limited strikes when necessary, and a foreign policy that reduces rather than multiplies recruits for anti-American violence. None of that requires building a centralized Afghan state at gunpoint.

The United States should therefore begin a deliberate military disengagement, not a theatrical abandonment. It should stop promising Afghans a state we cannot build. It should pursue regional diplomacy that includes Pakistan, Iran, India, Russia, China, and Afghanistan’s neighbors, all of whom have interests in preventing total chaos. It should support negotiations among Afghan factions, including Taliban elements willing to accept local power-sharing arrangements. It should redirect aid away from large contractor-driven projects and toward modest, accountable humanitarian assistance, especially in health, agriculture, and refugee support. It should offer asylum or protection to Afghans most endangered by their association with us. And it should maintain the capacity to disrupt transnational terrorist plots without pretending that every Taliban checkpoint is a threat to New York.

The hardest thing for Washington to admit is that some problems are not soluble by American power. Afghanistan is not a blank slate awaiting our doctrine. It is a country whose people have endured monarchy, communism, jihad, civil war, Taliban rule, invasion, and international reconstruction schemes. They will survive our departure, though not without suffering; they are also suffering through our presence. Rosen’s essay forces us to look at the war as Afghans on Highway 601 might see it: not as a contest between enlightened counterinsurgency and extremism, but as a bewildering succession of armed men demanding loyalty and promising protection they cannot provide. That is not a war we can win. It is a war we should stop enlarging.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'nothing' the U.S. is trying to build upon is the lack of a legitimate national partner, as the central Afghan state is predatory, irrelevant, or alien to its own people.
  2. 02The requirements for successful COIN—such as deep local knowledge, linguistic fluency, and a functioning justice system—are not present and cannot be scaled by the U.S. military.
  3. 03The Iraq 'Surge' is a misleading analogy because Afghanistan lacks the specific conditions, such as population exhaustion and sectarian sorting, that allowed for temporary stability in Iraq.
  4. 04The U.S. military cannot coherently define the enemy, leading to 'armed confusion' where civilians and local actors are incorrectly categorized as Taliban.
  5. 05Defeating the Taliban is not a prerequisite for defeating al-Qaeda, as the latter is a transnational network that can be managed through intelligence and diplomacy rather than state-building at gunpoint.
  6. 06The moral burden of continuing an occupation that empowers warlords and corrupt police is as significant as the potential risks associated with withdrawal.
  7. 07U.S. policy should shift toward regional diplomacy, factional negotiations, and modest humanitarian aid rather than attempting to solve insoluble problems through military power.
