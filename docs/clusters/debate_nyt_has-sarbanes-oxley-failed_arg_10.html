"The Sarbanes-Oxley Act has failed because it created an expensive, burdensome compliance machine that has not significantly improved investor safety beyond what existing laws already provided."

Yes, Sarbanes-Oxley has failed in a broad economic sense. It was enacted in anger after genuine scandals, but it answered those scandals with a Washington compliance machine that has made being a public company more expensive without proving that investors are safer from catastrophe.

The most revealing fact is that the major Enron-era wrongdoers were pursued under laws that already existed. Fraud was illegal before 2002. Lying to investors was illegal. Conspiracy, obstruction and false filings were illegal. Arthur Andersen was destroyed as a business even though its criminal conviction was later overturned. The system did not lack legal tools; it lacked vigilance, skepticism and timely enforcement.

Sarbanes-Oxley’s signature achievement, Section 404, illustrates the problem. Internal controls matter, but the law turned them into a costly certification exercise. It empowered auditors and consultants, not entrepreneurs or shareholders. Large companies can absorb the expense. Smaller companies cannot. The burden has helped make public listing less attractive and has encouraged some firms to stay private longer, sell themselves, or look to markets where the costs of being public are lower. Sarbanes-Oxley is not the only reason for the weak I.P.O. market, but it is one reason policymakers are now trying to revive public offerings through the JOBS Act.

The law also entrenched the dominance of the largest audit firms. If compliance becomes more elaborate, companies naturally turn to the few firms with the staff and reputation to satisfy regulators. That does not necessarily produce better judgment. It can produce box-checking by very expensive professionals.

Defenders say Bear Stearns and Lehman Brothers are irrelevant because Sarbanes-Oxley was not meant to prevent financial collapses. But that defense concedes too much. If the law’s benefits are narrow while its costs are broad, then it is a poor bargain. Investors need honest disclosure, but they do not need Congress to prescribe layers of process for every public company.

The better approach is to repeal or sharply limit Section 404’s auditor-attestation mandate, enforce anti-fraud laws aggressively, and let exchanges, institutional investors and insurers demand governance protections suited to each company. Sarbanes-Oxley promised restored trust. What it delivered was expensive paperwork and a false sense of security.

  3. 03The law has entrenched the dominance of the largest audit firms, prioritizing expensive 'box-checking' over the exercise of better professional judgment.
  4. 04The narrow benefits of the law's disclosure requirements do not justify the broad economic costs and layers of process imposed on all public companies.
  5. 05The act has failed to deliver on its promise of restored trust, providing instead expensive paperwork and a false sense of security.
