"The tax code should aim to reduce the sharpest marriage penalties for low- and moderate-income families while accepting that a perfectly neutral system is impossible if we wish to maintain progressive taxation and treat married couples as single economic units."

The phrase “marriage penalty” makes the issue sound simpler than it is. In fact, the tax code is trying to reconcile three goals that cannot all be perfectly satisfied: progressive taxation, equal treatment of married couples with the same total income, and neutrality between marriage and nonmarriage. Pick any two, and the third becomes difficult.

If we want a progressive income tax, higher incomes must face higher rates. If we also want married couples with the same total income to pay the same tax, then a couple earning $100,000 should owe the same whether the income is split $50,000-$50,000 or $100,000-$0. But once that principle is adopted, marriage will sometimes create bonuses and sometimes penalties compared with remaining single. That is arithmetic, not moral confusion.

Joint filing reflects a defensible idea: marriage is an economic partnership. Most married couples pool resources, share housing, make joint decisions and enjoy economies of scale. A spouse with no market income is not necessarily poor if married to a high earner. Taxing that couple as though the earner were a single person with no one else in the household would miss something real about ability to pay.

That does not mean the current system is ideal. Congress has repeatedly patched the code to reduce penalties for middle-income couples, but the patches are uneven. The standard deduction and the lower brackets for married couples should be kept close to twice the levels for singles where possible. Policymakers should also be attentive to interactions with the earned income tax credit, child credits and phaseouts, where penalties can be more severe for working-class families than for the affluent couples who usually dominate the discussion.

But abolishing joint filing would create its own unfairness. Consider two couples with the same total income, one with two earners and one with a stay-at-home parent caring for young children. Under pure individual taxation, the one-earner couple could pay substantially more, even though the household has no greater ability to pay and may be purchasing less market child care precisely because one parent is providing it. That is not obviously fair.

The goal should be modest: reduce the sharpest penalties, especially for low- and moderate-income couples, while recognizing that no tax system can be perfectly neutral about marriage unless it gives up either progressivity or household taxation. The present mixed message is not elegant, but some mixture is unavoidable.
