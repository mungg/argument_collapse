"While effective altruism offers a compelling vision for helping others, its underlying philosophical framework is too narrow because it prioritizes the maximization of consequences over the cultivation of virtue, the importance of relationships, and the inherent dignity of persons."

Peter Singer's effective altruism presents a compelling vision of ethical life, but it rests on philosophical foundations that many people, myself included, find deeply problematic. The framework implicitly assumes that morality is primarily about maximizing consequences—that the right action is whichever one produces the best outcomes. This is a particular philosophical stance, not a neutral description of ethics, and it conflicts with approaches to moral life that emphasize character, virtue, and relationship.

Consider the Matt Wage story from a different angle. A young man takes a job on Wall Street specifically to earn more money for donation. His motivation is admirable, but what does this career choice do to him? Does it cultivate virtues that will serve him well as a moral agent? Does it connect him to communities and relationships that give his life meaning? Singer treats these questions as secondary—the point is to save lives, and the rest is commentary. But this approach misses something essential about what it means to live ethically.

Classical virtue ethics, from Aristotle through Aquinas, holds that ethical life is about developing good character traits—courage, temperance, justice, prudence—and living in accordance with them. We become good by doing good actions, but the actions matter not only for their consequences but for how they shape our souls. A person who gives money to the poor but is consumed by pride or anxiety about their giving has not fully achieved moral goodness, even if their donations save lives.

Effective altruism also tends to treat recipients as means rather than ends. Singer calculates how many lives can be saved, how much suffering reduced, how much happiness increased. These are important considerations. But the recipients of our charity are not merely data points in our utility calculations; they are persons with their own dignity, agency, and conceptions of the good life. A truly ethical relationship to others involves respect, not just efficiency.

The Christian tradition offers another perspective. Moral life is fundamentally about love—specifically, love of neighbor as oneself, grounded in the love of God. This love is not primarily a calculation but a transformation of the heart. It is expressed in generosity, yes, but also in forgiveness, patience, and the willingness to suffer with others rather than merely solve their problems from a distance.

These traditions would not dispute that we should help those in need. But they would insist that how we help matters, not just how much good we do. They would ask whether our giving builds relationship or creates dependency, whether it respects the dignity of recipients or treats them as objects of our charity, whether it flows from genuine love or from a desire to optimize our moral performance.

I am not suggesting that effective altruists are bad people or that their work is worthless. Many effective altruists are motivated by genuine concern for others and have done impressive research on how to help. But the philosophical framework that underlies the movement is too narrow. Ethics is more than consequences, moral agents are more than calculators, and love is more than efficient allocation of resources.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The movement's focus on career choices based solely on earning potential ignores how such paths affect the moral character and spiritual well-being of the individual.
  2. 02Effective altruism reduces moral agents to calculators and recipients to data points, failing to treat individuals as persons with their own agency and dignity.
  3. 03A truly ethical life involves developing internal virtues and a transformation of the heart, rather than just optimizing the external outcomes of one's actions.
  4. 04The framework overlooks the importance of the nature of the relationship between the giver and the receiver, which should be grounded in mutual respect and love rather than mere efficiency.
  5. 05Moral traditions like virtue ethics and Christianity suggest that how we help is as significant as the amount of good we achieve.
