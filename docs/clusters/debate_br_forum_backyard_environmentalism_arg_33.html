Diversified Gemini

"The authors' claim of a radical shift toward a unified 'rolling-rule regime' is historically and conceptually flawed because it overstates the novelty of these programs while ignoring that they are actually pragmatic administrative adaptations grafted onto existing federal laws."

In “Beyond Backyard Environmentalism,” Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen provide a sweeping and optimistic narrative of institutional evolution. They claim that the United States is quietly moving away from both centralized command-and-control regulation and market-based mechanisms, toward a "rolling-rule regime" characterized by localized deliberation, continuous monitoring, and centralized data pooling. They root this development in the historical traditions of Jacksonian populism and Progressive expertise, suggesting we have finally found a way to synthesize the two. As a historian of environmental policy, I find their narrative intellectually thrilling, but historically premature and conceptually oversimplified.

The authors set up a rigid dichotomy: the old world of inflexible commands and the new world of pragmatic deliberation. In doing so, they drastically understate the degree to which "command and control" has always incorporated negotiation, learning, and local adaptation, and they overstate the novelty of the programs they describe.

Consider the Clean Water Act (CWA) of 1972, often held up as the quintessential rigid command-and-control statute. The CWA established the National Pollutant Discharge Elimination System (NPDES), requiring a permit for any point-source discharge. The authors suggest such systems demand "immodest omniscience" and freeze technology in place. But historically, NPDES permits are renewed every five years. The effluent limitations are based on "Best Available Technology" (BAT), a standard that explicitly evolves. As new pollution control technologies are developed, the EPA updates the BAT guidelines, and the new standards are incorporated into the next cycle of permits. This is, in fact, a form of a "rolling rule." It has always required continuous data gathering and adaptation. What Fung et al. describe as a radical new architecture is often just a maturation of the existing administrative state.

Furthermore, the authors draw a false equivalence between the different programs they highlight to build their grand theory. The Toxics Release Inventory (TRI) is fundamentally different from a Habitat Conservation Plan (HCP).

TRI is an information disclosure mandate layered on top of a robust system of federal environmental laws. It works because the "shadow of the law" is long. Companies reduce their toxic emissions when the data is published not merely because of neighborhood deliberation, but because they fear the EPA will step in with traditional, rigid regulations if they do not, or that trial lawyers will use the data for toxic tort litigation.

HCPs, on the other hand, are exemptions from a strict federal prohibition (the ESA’s Section 9 take provision). They are complex, negotiated contracts regarding land use. Combining TRI, HCPs, the Chemical Manufacturers Association's voluntary Responsible Care program, and the Chesapeake Bay Program into a single "emergent regulatory regime" is an exercise in academic lumping that obscures massive differences in statutory authority, enforcement mechanisms, and political dynamics.

The authors also invoke the Progressive tradition, noting its reliance on "stewards of the common good who would themselves be disciplined by rigorous inquiry." They claim their new regime transcends the Progressive limitation of excluding the public. But a close reading of their examples shows that expertise still dominates. In the Chesapeake Bay Program, citizens are taught to "mimic the monitoring and reporting protocols developed by scientific experts." This is not the democratization of science; it is the outsourcing of data collection. The fundamental parameters of what constitutes a healthy bay and how to measure it are still dictated by hydrologists and marine biologists, not by "lunch-pail environmentalism."

This is not necessarily a bad thing. Complex ecological systems require expert management. But we should be clear-eyed about what is actually happening. We are not entering an era where ordinary citizens and experts merge into a unified, problem-solving collective. We are simply developing more sophisticated administrative interfaces between the regulatory state and civil society.

Finally, the authors predict that this decentralized regime will eventually require "open validation—probably through Congress," citing the Miller Bill as an example of how pluralist politics will institutionalize directly deliberative frameworks. Writing today, in the fall of 1999, with a deeply polarized Congress that has been unable to pass major environmental legislation for years, this seems like wishful thinking. The shift toward HCPs and localized agreements is happening precisely because legislative action is paralyzed. The administration is using executive discretion to create flexibility because it knows Congress cannot agree on how to rewrite the ESA or the CWA.

Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen have accurately identified a trend toward flexibility, localized negotiation, and data-driven management. But they have mistaken administrative coping mechanisms for a coherent, unified democratic philosophy. The "rolling-rule regime" is not a master plan replacing the old order; it is a patchwork of pragmatic fixes grafted onto a statutory framework built in the 1970s. Whether it can sustain itself without the rigid backbone of that original framework remains the unanswered question of the coming century.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Traditional 'command-and-control' regulations, such as the Clean Water Act, have historically incorporated the same iterative learning and technological adaptation that the authors characterize as a new architecture.
  2. 02The authors incorrectly group fundamentally different programs, such as information disclosure mandates (TRI) and land-use exemptions (HCPs), into a single regime, thereby obscuring critical differences in enforcement and statutory authority.
  3. 03The programs described do not truly democratize science but rather outsource data collection to citizens who must still follow expert-dictated protocols and parameters.
  4. 04The shift toward localized, flexible agreements is a symptom of congressional paralysis and executive discretion rather than a coherent new democratic philosophy or a master plan for reform.
  5. 05The success of these flexible mechanisms often depends on the 'shadow of the law' provided by the very rigid federal frameworks the authors claim are being replaced.
