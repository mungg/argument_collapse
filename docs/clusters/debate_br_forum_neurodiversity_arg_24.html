Diversified Gemini

"The shift from practical, rights-based advocacy to a radical anti-capitalist agenda threatens to alienate supporters and dismantle the significant progress made by the neurodiversity movement."

Robert Chapman’s essay is a textbook example of how the logic of radical intersectionality inevitably consumes and destroys specific, practical advocacy movements. What began decades ago as a focused, compassionate, and highly successful effort to ensure that individuals with autism, ADHD, and other cognitive differences could participate fully in society is now being hijacked by a maximalist, Marxist political agenda. In demanding that the neurodiversity movement abandon "Liberal Neurodiversity" for a revolutionary anti-capitalist crusade, Chapman threatens to unravel the immense progress that has been achieved and alienate the very people the movement is supposed to protect.

Chapman is remarkably candid about their ultimate goal: the destruction of the capitalist system. They argue that the successes of the neurodiversity movement—workplace accommodations, the Americans with Disabilities Act, sensory-friendly hours in supermarkets, and the destigmatization of neurological differences—are merely forms of "elite capture." In Chapman's view, capitalism only accommodates neurodivergent people to extract profit from them, and therefore, true liberation requires the total overhaul of our economic and political relations, drawing explicit inspiration from the Marxist-Leninist Black Panther Party.

This argument rests on a profound misunderstanding of both human nature and economics. The very concept of "accommodations"—whether it is a specialized education program, a sensory room, or flexible work hours—requires surplus resources. It is no coincidence that the most robust protections for disabled and neurodivergent individuals exist in wealthy, capitalist democracies. The free market system, despite its flaws, is the greatest engine for wealth creation in human history. It is this wealth that funds medical research, builds accessible infrastructure, and allows society to prioritize inclusion over mere survival.

Chapman casually dismisses the fact that tech companies and other businesses are recognizing the "superpowers" of neurodivergent individuals. They view this as exploitative. But what is the alternative? In a free society, the alignment of an individual's unique skills with a productive role in the economy is the foundation of independence and self-worth. When a software company hires an autistic individual because their attention to detail makes them an exceptional coder, this is a triumph of mutual benefit. The individual gains financial independence, purpose, and community, while the company gains a valuable employee. To frame this as sinister "extractive abandonment" is to view all human cooperation through a deeply cynical, ideological lens.

Furthermore, Chapman’s proposed alliance with global anti-imperialist and anti-capitalist movements represents a dangerous mission creep. What does the tragic and complex conflict between Israel and Hamas have to do with whether a dyslexic child receives reading support in a public school? By demanding that the neurodiversity movement adopt a stance on "imperialist wars" and the abolition of prisons, Chapman is forcing a deeply diverse group of people into an ideological straitjacket.

Neurodivergent people are not a monolith. They are conservative and liberal, religious and secular, business owners and laborers. A movement that requires its members to subscribe to revolutionary socialism will instantly shed the majority of its supporters. Parents advocating for their neurodivergent children, or adults seeking workplace adjustments, do not want to be foot soldiers in a Marxist vanguard; they want practical solutions to navigate their daily lives.

Chapman’s critique of "Liberal Neurodiversity" also fundamentally mischaracterizes the nature of rights in a liberal democracy. The ADA is not a perfect instrument, but it relies on a framework of individual rights and equal opportunity. It demands that barriers be removed so that individuals can compete and thrive on their own merits. Chapman, conversely, seems to desire a system of equal *outcomes*, which historically has only been achieved through intense state coercion.

The historical parallel Chapman draws with the Black Power movement is instructive, but perhaps not in the way they intend. While the Black Panthers highlighted genuine injustices, their embrace of Marxism-Leninism and political violence alienated broad segments of the American public and ultimately fractured their own movement. In contrast, it was the "liberal reformism" of the Civil Rights Act and the Voting Rights Act that dismantled institutional segregation and created lasting legal frameworks for equality.

If we look at history, regimes that have attempted to abolish capitalism and enforce the kind of collective, anti-market systems Chapman advocates have had catastrophic records on human rights, particularly regarding the disabled and the non-conformist. The Soviet Union, for example, notoriously institutionalized political dissidents and those who did not fit the mold of the "New Soviet Man" under the guise of psychiatric treatment. A system that demands total ideological conformity and collective sacrifice is inherently hostile to the very concept of neurodiversity, which celebrates the individual and the atypical.

The challenges facing neurodivergent people are real. The incarceration of those with mental illness is a tragedy that requires serious policy reform, specifically regarding the funding of mental health facilities and the training of law enforcement. But these are complex policy issues that require targeted, pragmatic solutions, not the overthrow of the economic order.

"Liberal Neurodiversity" has succeeded precisely because it appeals to our shared humanity and our shared desire for a fair, meritocratic society. It asks institutions to be flexible so that individuals can contribute. Chapman’s "Neurodivergent Power" asks us to burn the institutions down. We must reject this radicalization. The future of neurodiversity lies in continuing the hard, practical work of expanding access, understanding, and opportunity within the framework of a free and prosperous society.
