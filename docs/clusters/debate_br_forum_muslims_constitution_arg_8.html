"While the Trump administration's policies intensified anti-Muslim prejudice, they are rooted in a long-standing, bipartisan security infrastructure that treats Muslim belonging as provisional and requires constant, performative proof of loyalty."

The most painful sentence in Akbar and Theoharis’s essay is not about surveillance, courts, or war. It is the question about Khizr Khan: why did it take a Muslim family’s sacrifice for them to be celebrated? That question lingers because many Muslim Americans know the answer before it is asked. We have been trained to understand that our belonging is provisional. We may be neighbors, classmates, doctors, cab drivers, teachers, soldiers, lawyers, store owners, and children born in New Jersey or Michigan or Texas. But when the country becomes frightened, we are asked to introduce ourselves again.

This repeated reintroduction is exhausting. After every attack somewhere in the world, Muslims are asked to condemn. After every arrest, Muslims are asked whether our mosques are doing enough. After every political controversy, Muslims are asked whether Islam is compatible with democracy, women’s rights, free speech, gay rights, modernity, America. The questions are posed as if they are new. They are not. They are rituals of suspicion, and participation is compulsory.

The travel ban intensified this ritual but did not create it. The executive order made airports into scenes of open humiliation: families waiting for relatives who did not come through the doors, lawyers sitting on floors drafting habeas petitions, translators searching for frightened travelers, protesters chanting outside glass walls. But for many Muslims, the airport had long been a place where citizenship thinned. You learn to leave extra time. You learn to unlock your phone or wonder if you must. You learn that a beard, a name, a stamp in a passport, a place of birth, or a visit to family can become an invitation to questioning. You learn to smile carefully.

The lead essay’s account of surveillance captures something that ordinary policy language misses: surveillance is not only information gathering; it is atmosphere. It changes how people breathe. When a community suspects that informants may be present, conversations shift. A young person who wants to talk about Syria or Palestine or police violence may stay quiet. A convert may be viewed with unfair suspicion because people have heard too many stories of informants. An imam may avoid controversial subjects not because he lacks courage but because he must protect a congregation. Parents may warn their children not to post, not to argue, not to attend that meeting, not to say that phrase. A political identity becomes a risk assessment.

This is why Countering Violent Extremism programs have been so damaging. Their language is soft: partnership, resilience, engagement, prevention. But the premise is hard. Muslim communities are treated as spaces where danger incubates and where signs must be detected before they become crimes. Religious practice, adolescent alienation, political anger, mental health struggles, and moral outrage at war are folded into a grid of possible radicalization. The result is not safety. It is mistrust.

Defenders of CVE often say that communities asked for resources and that some Muslim organizations participated. That is true. Muslim communities are not monolithic, and they make difficult choices under pressure. If a city offers youth programs, mental health funding, or dialogue with officials, some leaders may accept because the needs are real. But a program does not become non-stigmatizing simply because some of the stigmatized participate. Poor communities have long accepted police-sponsored programs, school resource officers, or gang-prevention grants while also experiencing over-policing. Consent under conditions of unequal power is complicated.

Akbar and Theoharis are also right to place Muslim experiences within broader histories of racial control. This is not always easy to discuss because American Muslims are racially diverse: black Muslims whose families have been here for centuries, Arab immigrants and their children, South Asian professionals, refugees from Somalia or Iraq, white converts, Latino Muslims, Iranians, Bosnians, Turks, West Africans, and many others. Some are wealthy; many are not. Some are citizens; others are undocumented or on temporary visas. Anti-Muslim policy touches them differently.

But the war on terror has racialized Islam in a particular way. The imagined terrorist has a body, an accent, a beard, a gender, an origin story. Sikhs have been attacked by people who think they are Muslims. Arab Christians have been profiled. South Asians of different religions have learned that brownness can be read as Muslim threat. Black Muslims experience anti-Muslim suspicion layered on top of anti-black policing. Somali communities face counterterrorism scrutiny, refugee politics, and ordinary racism simultaneously. The state may say it targets conduct, but the social field on which suspicion operates is racial.

That is why alliances with Black Lives Matter and immigrant-rights movements are not optional. They are not fashionable add-ons. They are analytically necessary and politically vital. Stop-and-frisk, gang databases, deportation raids, FBI visits, no-fly lists, school surveillance, prison isolation, border interrogations—these are not identical practices, but they share logics. They treat certain communities as problems to be managed before any individual wrong has been proven. They create categories of people who must live transparently before the state while the state remains opaque to them.

Still, I would add one caution to the lead essay. In criticizing the “good Muslim/bad Muslim” binary, we should not create another binary between radical critique and compromised community life. Many Muslims respond to suspicion through patriotism, interfaith work, military service, law enforcement careers, or public declarations of loyalty. Some do so strategically; some sincerely; often both. They should not be dismissed as dupes of empire. A refugee who flies an American flag because this country gave her safety is not responsible for the Pentagon. A Muslim police officer who believes he can serve justly is not reducible to the security state. A Gold Star family’s grief should not be treated only as evidence of national pathology.

The point is not to judge how Muslims survive conditional belonging. The point is to end the conditions. Some will wave the Constitution; some will quote the Qur’an; some will march against war; some will enlist; some will build local mutual aid; some will litigate; some will write poetry; some will keep their heads down. A movement spacious enough to defend Muslim life must make room for all of these without allowing the state to decide which ones count as acceptable.

The question of foreign policy cannot be separated from this. Many American Muslims have relatives in countries bombed, occupied, sanctioned, or destabilized by the United States. When public officials say “we are fighting them over there so we do not fight them here,” Muslims hear the geography of kinship turned into a battlefield. When drone strikes kill civilians, the grief travels through diaspora communities. When refugees are described as threats after fleeing wars in which the United States has played a role, cruelty is compounded by denial.

Yet Muslims are also not merely victims of American policy. They have political opinions about Assad, ISIS, Saudi Arabia, Iran, Egypt, Pakistan, Palestine, Kashmir, drones, dictators, sectarianism, feminism, secularism, and religious authority. These debates are vibrant and sometimes fierce. Surveillance flattens them. It makes internal community disagreement risky because a position may be overheard as extremism. One of the great harms of anti-Muslim policing is intellectual: it narrows the space in which Muslims can argue with one another about the world.

What should be demanded now? First, the travel ban must be defeated not only because it is badly drafted but because religious exclusion is intolerable. Second, watch lists must be made subject to meaningful due process. Third, CVE programs that single out Muslims should be rejected, and resources for youth, mental health, and anti-violence work should be provided without intelligence strings. Fourth, material support laws must be narrowed so that charity, speech, and political association are not chilled. Fifth, mosques and Muslim student groups should not be mapped or infiltrated absent individualized suspicion of crime. Sixth, opposition to anti-Muslim hate must include opposition to wars that render Muslim lives abroad invisible.

Finally, non-Muslim allies must resist the urge to defend Muslims only by saying “they are just like us.” Sometimes that phrase is meant kindly. But Muslims do not need to be just like anyone else to have rights. Some Muslims are secular and some are devout; some are feminist and some patriarchal; some are liberal and some conservative; some are queer; some are homophobic; some are generous; some are ordinary and flawed. A community’s humanity cannot depend on its public relations department.

The airport protests suggested that many Americans are ready to say no to the most blatant forms of exclusion. The next task is harder: to say no when exclusion wears the language of expertise, when suspicion is hidden in databases, when informants call themselves friends, when courts defer, when bombs fall far away, when the person targeted is not a perfect symbol. Akbar and Theoharis ask us to look beyond Trump. That is the right demand. The ban is a crisis, but the deeper crisis is the notion that Muslims must be managed into belonging. We do not need management. We need freedom.
