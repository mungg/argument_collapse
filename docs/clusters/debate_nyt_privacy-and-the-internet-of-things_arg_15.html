"To ensure safety and accountability in the Internet of Things, connected devices should be subject to strict product liability and federal safety regulations rather than the lenient legal standards applied to software."

**Treat Connected Devices Like Cars, Not Like Software**

When Microsoft ships a buggy version of Word, the consequence is annoyance. When Ford ships a defective brake system, the consequence is death—and we hold automakers to a standard of strict liability, mandatory recalls, and federal safety regulation. The Internet of Things sits awkwardly between these two regimes, and right now it is being treated as software when it should be treated as a physical product.

This matters because the software industry has been allowed to externalize the costs of its insecurity for decades. End-user license agreements disclaim all warranties. Breaches are met with apologies and credit monitoring. The legal architecture assumes the user accepts the risk. That arrangement was tolerable, barely, when the worst consequence was a hijacked email account. It is intolerable when the device in question is the lock on your front door, the insulin pump in your body, or the camera over your child's crib.

The framework already exists. The Consumer Product Safety Commission regulates dangerous goods. The NHTSA mandates auto recalls. The FDA has begun, tentatively, to assert authority over networked medical devices—witness its draft guidance this June on cybersecurity for medical equipment. Extending product-liability principles to connected consumer goods would shift the incentives dramatically. If a manufacturer were on the hook for foreseeable harms caused by their unpatched device, they would patch it. If they could be forced to recall a vulnerable product, they would design it to be patchable in the first place.

Yes, this raises costs. Yes, it might slow the breathless pace of product launches. Good. The promise of the Internet of Things is genuine, but it will only be realized if consumers can trust that the objects in their lives are not also weapons aimed at them. Liability is how we have always built that trust for physical things. Connected objects are physical things.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current software industry model allows manufacturers to unfairly externalize the costs of insecurity through liability disclaimers.
  2. 02The physical nature of connected devices means that security failures can result in life-threatening consequences that far exceed the risks of traditional software bugs.
  3. 03Existing regulatory frameworks, such as those used by the NHTSA and CPSC, provide a proven model for managing the risks of dangerous consumer goods.
  4. 04Imposing legal liability would create necessary financial incentives for manufacturers to prioritize security patches and safer product designs.
  5. 05Establishing consumer trust through accountability is essential for the long-term success and adoption of Internet of Things technologies.
