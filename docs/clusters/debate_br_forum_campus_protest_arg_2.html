Diversified Claude

"Student activists should leverage the political vulnerability of universities to force structural institutional change rather than withdrawing into 'fugitive' study or settling for superficial reforms."

I want to come at Robin Kelley's essay from an angle he does not quite address: the institutional question of what universities should actually do in response to the protests of the last year. Kelley is suspicious of the reformist demands; he thinks they will be absorbed and defanged. He is also suspicious of expecting universities to be the engines of social transformation. The implied conclusion is that universities are not really the right object of radical political action at all—that the work has to happen elsewhere, in freedom schools and study groups and movement organizations.

This is a coherent position, but it has costs that I do not think Kelley fully accounts for. Universities are enormous institutions in American life. They employ millions of people, sit on hundreds of billions of dollars in endowments, train the professional and managerial classes, conduct most basic research, hold significant real estate in dozens of cities, and shape the credentialing structures that govern access to economic security. They are also, in many cases, the dominant employer and landlord in their host communities. To treat them as too peripheral to be worth contesting—or as too compromised to be capable of meaningful change—is to write off a significant share of contemporary institutional life.

The students who occupied administrative buildings at Mizzou and threatened to strike at Yale were doing something that I think Kelley underweights: they were demonstrating that these institutions are politically vulnerable. The Mizzou president did not have to resign. The threat from the football team made him resign. This is a small lesson but a real one: institutions that look like immovable bureaucracies can be moved by the right kind of pressure at the right moment. The lesson is not "now we can transform the university into a socialist commune." The lesson is "we have leverage we did not know we had."

What you do with that leverage is the political question. Here I think Kelley's worry about reformist demands has more bite. If the leverage is spent on getting a building renamed, the institution will absorb the change and move on. If it is spent on getting a counseling office expanded, the institution will absorb that too. The harder demands—tuition reform, divestment, worker pay, admissions overhaul—are the ones that would actually restructure the institution. These are the demands worth making with the leverage one has.

But making them requires staying in the institution, not withdrawing from it. The undercommons framework Kelley invokes is, I think, the wrong move at this particular juncture. Harney and Moten wrote in a period of perceived hopelessness about institutional change—the late Bush years, the depths of the financial crisis. Their counsel of fugitive withdrawal made sense then. But 2015 was a year of significant institutional vulnerability, and the right response to institutional vulnerability is institutional pressure, not fugitivity. The students who chose to engage rather than withdraw chose correctly.

This is also where I would push back on Kelley's invocation of the Mississippi Freedom Schools as a model. The Freedom Schools were not an alternative to institutional engagement; they were embedded in the larger SNCC project, which was very much about transforming institutions—first the Mississippi Democratic Party, then the federal voting system, then the entire architecture of American apartheid. The Freedom Schools served the movement; they did not substitute for it. To invoke them as a model for autonomous study divorced from institutional contestation is to misread the historical record.

What I take from Kelley, in the end, is something narrower than what he intends. I take from him a healthy skepticism about which demands are absorbable and which are not, and a healthy respect for the political education work that has to happen in addition to—not instead of—institutional struggle. I do not take from him the conclusion that universities are essentially hopeless objects of political contestation, or that students should be in but not of them. The students should be in, of, and against them. That is a more complicated stance than fugitivity, but it is the stance the moment actually calls for.

The endowments are real. The labor relations are real. The admissions criteria are real. These things can be changed. They will not be changed by students reading together in a park. They will be changed by students reading together in a park and then occupying the registrar's office. Both halves matter, but the second half is the one that universities will respond to.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Universities are too economically and socially powerful to be treated as peripheral or beyond the reach of meaningful political contestation.
  2. 02Recent campus protests demonstrated that even entrenched university bureaucracies possess significant leverage points that can be exploited through strategic pressure.
  3. 03Activists must prioritize 'hard' demands that restructure the institution, such as tuition reform and divestment, over 'absorbable' symbolic reforms like building renamings.
  4. 04The 'undercommons' framework of fugitive withdrawal is an inappropriate strategy for a moment characterized by institutional vulnerability and the potential for real power shifts.
  5. 05Historical models like the Mississippi Freedom Schools were successful because they were embedded in projects of institutional transformation rather than serving as autonomous alternatives to them.
  6. 06Effective activism requires a dual approach of collective political education and direct institutional struggle, such as occupying administrative offices.
Diversified Claude

"While Robin Kelley correctly identifies the university as a corporate entity that produces inequality, his advocacy for 'fugitive study' risks encouraging a withdrawal from institutional struggle at a moment when students should instead be pushed to transition from demands for recognition to demands for the redistribution of power."

Robin Kelley's essay is principally about black students and black study, and I do not want to displace that focus. But I want to read it through a slightly different lens—the lens of political economy—because I think the most powerful claim in the essay is one Kelley makes almost in passing: that the contemporary university is not a neutral institution that has been corrupted by racism, but a corporate entity whose normal functioning is bound up with the production of inequality.

This claim deserves more elaboration than Kelley gives it. The university of 2016 is not the university of 1968. Tuition has risen by orders of magnitude faster than inflation. Student debt has crossed $1.3 trillion. The administrative class has grown enormously while tenure-track faculty positions have stagnated and adjunctification has spread. Endowments have become major financial actors, with university money managers indistinguishable in practice from hedge fund operators. The relationships between research universities, the defense industry, the pharmaceutical industry, and Silicon Valley have deepened into structural dependencies.

In this context, the campus protests of 2015 take on a different cast. The students at UNC demanding $25/hour for campus workers and pay parity between administrators and workers are not simply asking for a kinder workplace; they are challenging the basic compensation structure of the contemporary university, in which presidents make seven figures while groundskeepers struggle. The Mizzou demand for free tuition for black and indigenous students is not merely a reparative gesture; it is a challenge to the tuition model itself, which has functioned to transfer the cost of higher education from public revenue to private debt over the last forty years.

These are not therapy demands. They are political-economic demands, and they would, if met, materially restructure the institutions in question. Kelley is right that the institutions will not meet them in full. But he is wrong, I think, to treat this as evidence that the institutions cannot be sites of meaningful struggle. The history of American institutional change suggests that institutions can be forced into significant transformations by pressure—particularly pressure that exposes the gap between their stated values and their actual practices. The civil rights movement did this to American liberalism; the labor movement did it to American capitalism; the women's movement did it to nearly every institution in American life. None of these movements achieved full transformation, but the changes they did achieve were real.

What worries me about Kelley's essay, despite my deep agreement with much of it, is that the rhetorical pull of "fugitive study" and the Undercommons can underwrite a kind of withdrawal from institutional struggle precisely when institutional struggle is most needed. The Harney and Moten line that one can "only sneak into the university and steal what one can" is poetically arresting, but as a political program it leaves the actual structure of the university untouched. The dean keeps her salary. The endowment keeps its prison investments. The adjunct keeps adjuncting. The students stealing time from the institution to read together are doing valuable work, but they are not changing the institution.

The students Kelley criticizes—the ones demanding diversity training and inclusion programs—are at least, on his own account, getting the attention of the trustees. The fact that the trustees respond with cosmetic concessions is a critique of the trustees, not of the students who forced them to respond. The work of pushing past cosmetic concessions to substantive ones is the work of the next phase of the movement. It is not aided by suggesting to the students that they should have spent that energy on autonomous study groups instead.

Kelley's essay would be stronger, I think, if it framed the choice not as one between reform-on-campus and study-off-campus, but as one between two kinds of on-campus struggle: the kind that asks the institution to recognize you and the kind that asks the institution to redistribute power. Mental health services and renamed buildings fall into the first category. Pay parity and divestment fall into the second. The students are pursuing both, often simultaneously. The task of the older radical intellectual is not to scold them for the first but to clarify and reinforce the second.

This, I think, is the work Kelley is uniquely equipped to do, and the work I hope his next essay will take up. The undercommons is beautiful. The picket line is essential.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The contemporary university has evolved into a financialized corporate actor where rising tuition, student debt, and administrative expansion are structural features rather than accidental corruptions.
  2. 02Radical student demands, such as pay parity and free tuition, are material challenges to the university's political economy rather than mere 'therapy' or 'trauma' demands.
  3. 03History shows that institutions can be forced into significant, if incomplete, transformations when pressured to bridge the gap between their stated values and actual practices.
  4. 04The concept of the 'undercommons' is poetically compelling but politically insufficient because it leaves the exploitative structures of the university, such as endowments and adjunctification, entirely intact.
  5. 05The role of the radical intellectual should be to help student activists pivot from cosmetic reforms like diversity training toward substantive institutional restructuring and the redistribution of resources.
