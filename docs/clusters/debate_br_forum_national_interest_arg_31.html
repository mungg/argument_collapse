"The strategy of offshore balancing is a dangerous and outdated approach that fails to address the modern existential threat posed by the convergence of radical terrorist movements and weapons of mass destruction."

Stephen Walt's essay is a sophisticated restatement of a worldview that has consistently misjudged the nature of the threats facing America. His offshore balancing strategy would leave the United States vulnerable to precisely the dangers he acknowledges but fails to address adequately: the spread of weapons of mass destruction to terrorist movements and hostile states.

The central flaw in Walt's analysis is his implicit assumption that great power competition poses the primary danger to American security. This may have been true during the Cold War, but the strategic environment has fundamentally changed. The Soviet Union is gone, but a new threat has emerged: the convergence of terrorism with weapons of mass destruction. Terrorist groups like al-Qaeda have demonstrated both the capability and the willingness to attack the American homeland on a catastrophic scale. Their ultimate aim is not territorial aggrandizement but the infliction of maximum damage on American society. Nuclear weapons in terrorist hands would represent an existential threat qualitatively different from anything posed by great power competition.

Walt's response to this threat—securing loose nuclear materials and negotiating arms control agreements—is necessary but insufficient. By the time terrorists acquire nuclear weapons, it will be too late to negotiate. The only effective response is to eliminate the regimes that might provide terrorists with nuclear weapons or materials. This means regime change in Iran and North Korea, not engagement. Walt's approach of offering concessions in exchange for nuclear abandonment misunderstands the nature of these regimes. They are not rational actors calculating costs and benefits; they are ideological movements driven by religious or nationalist fervor. No amount of American restraint will satisfy them, because their grievances against America are not based on American behavior but on American existence.

The Iraq war, despite its difficulties, demonstrates the value of preventive action. Saddam Hussein was a genuine threat—a regime that had used chemical weapons against its own people, that had invaded two neighboring countries, and that was working to acquire nuclear weapons before 1991. Removing that threat was worth the cost, even if the post-war reconstruction has been more difficult than anticipated. Walt's critique mistakes execution for strategy; the problem in Iraq was not that we overthrew Saddam but that we failed to plan adequately for the aftermath.

On the Middle East conflict more broadly, Walt's analysis is skewed by his apparent belief that American support for Israel is the primary source of anti-American terrorism. This conflates the symptom with the cause. Anti-American terrorism predates Israeli occupation of the West Bank; it is rooted in a genuine clash of civilizations between radical Islam and Western modernity. America is hated not because of its policies but because of its values—its commitment to individual liberty, gender equality, and secular government. No amount of American concessions on Israeli settlements will satisfy those who wish to destroy the West.

Walt's strategy would withdraw American power precisely when it is most needed. The United States must remain engaged, both diplomatically and militarily, to prevent the emergence of a new great power threat and to combat the terrorist movements that threaten American lives. Restraint is not wisdom; it is capitulation.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The primary threat to American security has shifted from great power competition to ideological terrorist groups whose goal is the catastrophic destruction of American society rather than territorial gain.
  2. 02Diplomatic engagement and arms control are insufficient because hostile ideological regimes in places like Iran and North Korea are not rational actors and must be met with regime change to prevent nuclear proliferation.
  3. 03The Iraq War was a justified and necessary preventive action against a proven threat, where the flaws lay in post-war execution rather than the strategic decision to remove a dangerous dictator.
  4. 04Anti-American terrorism is rooted in a fundamental clash of civilizations over Western values like liberty and secularism, rather than being a mere reaction to U.S. policy toward Israel.
  5. 05Withdrawing the American military footprint would constitute a capitulation that leaves the United States vulnerable at a time when active global engagement is most required to combat modern threats.
