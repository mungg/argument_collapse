"To ensure presidential primary debates prioritize substantive policy over entertainment, political parties must reclaim control of the debate process from television networks."

The networks should not be allowed to design presidential primary debates as if they were sweeps-week entertainment specials. Their incentives are obvious: conflict, suspense, ratings, and memorable clips. The parties’ incentive, at least in theory, should be different: to help voters choose a nominee capable of governing. Reform should begin with the parties taking back control.

The Republican National Committee has already tried to limit the number of debates after the long 2012 primary season, but quantity is only one issue. The more important question is who sets the rules. A party-sponsored debate commission for the primaries could license the broadcast feed to any network willing to carry it under common conditions. That would mean no network-specific gimmicks, no arbitrary “main debate” designed around early polling, no emphasis on dramatic camera shots and audience reaction.

The rules should be public before the campaign begins. Qualification standards should combine several measures: polling, fundraising from small donors, ballot access, prior elected office or demonstrated campaign organization. No formula will be perfect, but it should not be invented by television executives a few weeks before the event.

The format should be built around accountability. If a candidate says he will cut taxes by trillions and balance the budget, the next question should be: which programs shrink, and by how much? If a candidate promises to defeat the Islamic State, the follow-up should ask whether that means ground troops, a no-fly zone, cooperation with local forces, or something else. Moderators should be chosen for subject expertise, not just celebrity.

There is a tradeoff. A quieter, more structured debate may attract fewer casual viewers. But the purpose of a primary debate is not merely to maximize audience size. Parties are private associations performing a public function. They owe their voters more than spectacle.

The candidates also benefit from clearer rules. A serious contender should want the chance to explain a governing program. If he cannot do that in a format designed for substance, voters have learned something important.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Political parties should establish their own debate commissions to set standardized rules and licensing conditions that eliminate network-specific gimmicks and arbitrary polling-based tiers.
  2. 02Qualification standards for candidates should be based on a transparent, multi-factor formula established well before the campaign begins rather than being invented by TV executives.
  3. 03The debate format should prioritize accountability by requiring specific follow-up questions on policy implementation and selecting moderators based on subject expertise rather than celebrity status.
  4. 04The primary goal of debates should be to inform voters on a candidate's ability to govern rather than maximizing audience size through conflict and spectacle.
