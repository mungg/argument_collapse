"The current crisis of American democracy cannot be understood or resolved through procedural restoration alone because representative institutions have historically been defined by racial exclusion and distortion."

The phrase “anxieties of democracy” invites a question: whose anxiety, and about what? Ira Katznelson writes eloquently about the fragility of representative institutions, the dangers of money, and the temptation to bypass legislatures. But American democracy has always been anxious, especially for those whom the polity has excluded, surveilled, segregated, deported, or imprisoned. The present crisis cannot be understood apart from race.

The history of American representation is also the history of racial distortion. The Constitution counted enslaved persons for purposes of representation while denying them personhood. The Senate protected slave power and later segregationist power. After Reconstruction, southern states used terror, literacy tests, poll taxes, white primaries, and administrative tricks to eliminate black voting. For decades, one-party white supremacist rule in the South shaped Congress, blocked labor and civil rights legislation, and limited the New Deal. This was not a failure outside representative democracy. It was built into representative democracy as Americans practiced it.

Katznelson knows this history well, and his work has shown how the New Deal’s achievements were purchased in part through accommodation with Jim Crow. That makes the New Deal a more ambiguous model than the lead essay sometimes suggests. It proved that democratic government could regulate capitalism and provide social security, but it also proved that legislative compromise can entrench racial hierarchy. Agricultural and domestic workers—disproportionately black—were excluded from key protections. Local administration allowed discrimination. The welfare state was racially stratified from its beginning.

This matters because calls for a restored legislative center can sound different depending on one’s historical position. For many black Americans, “regular order” has often meant delay. “States’ rights” meant domination. “Civic virtue” meant white respectability. “Background consensus” meant agreement among whites about the boundaries of black citizenship. The problem was not that consensus disappeared; it was that the wrong consensus lasted too long.

In 2015, the racial dimension of democratic anxiety is again unmistakable. The Supreme Court’s decision in Shelby County v. Holder weakened a central provision of the Voting Rights Act at precisely the moment when many states were adopting voter ID laws, cutting early voting, or changing registration rules. These policies are defended as neutral protections against fraud, but their burdens fall unequally. Felony disenfranchisement continues to exclude millions, disproportionately African American. Districting practices dilute minority influence. Equal citizenship remains contested not only in rhetoric but in election administration.

Policing has made the gap between formal democracy and lived citizenship painfully visible. Ferguson was not just about one killing. It revealed a municipal order in which black residents were treated as revenue sources through fines and warrants, and as threats through aggressive policing. When citizens experience local government primarily as surveillance, extraction, and force, voting every few years is a thin form of democracy. The protests that followed Ferguson and other deaths are not symptoms of anti-institutional impatience; they are demands that institutions recognize black life as politically equal.

Katznelson warns against populist claims to embody “the people.” That warning is important, for racial nationalism has often used democratic language to exclude. But the United States also has a long tradition of movements by excluded groups claiming the peoplehood they were denied. Frederick Douglass, Ida B. Wells, Martin Luther King Jr., Fannie Lou Hamer, and contemporary activists all challenged the nation to expand its understanding of who counts. Their democratic speech was disruptive because the official democracy was too narrow.

The question, then, is not simply how to protect representation from populism or wealth. It is how to make representation genuinely multiracial. That requires more than civility and procedural repair. It requires a renewed Voting Rights Act, automatic or easier registration, restoration of voting rights to former prisoners, fair districting, and protection against local practices that suppress participation. It requires campaign finance rules that do not allow affluent white networks to dominate political agendas. It requires parties to organize in communities they have neglected, not merely advertise to them.

It also requires policy responsiveness. Racial democracy cannot be separated from schools, housing, health, employment, criminal justice, and environmental exposure. Segregated neighborhoods and unequal schools shape political voice long before election day. Mass incarceration removes citizens from communities and marks them after release. Immigration enforcement produces a different but related democratic vulnerability, creating millions who live under law without a secure voice in making it. A polity with large numbers of residents who work, pay taxes, raise families, and fear deportation cannot be complacent about consent.

Katznelson’s defense of criticism is valuable here. Liberal democracy’s great virtue is that it permits those injured by the polity to indict it publicly. But criticism must be more than permitted; it must be empowered. A democracy that tolerates protest while ignoring its claims is not healthy. Nor is one that praises diversity while structuring elections to minimize the power of the diverse.

The anxiety of American democracy is not new. What may be new is that demographic change, protest, and legal contestation have made old contradictions harder to hide. Some citizens experience this as loss: loss of cultural centrality, loss of uncontested authority, loss of a familiar “we.” Others experience it as the unfinished promise of democracy finally pressing its claim. The task is not to recover a consensus that often rested on exclusion. It is to build institutions capable of governing without racial hierarchy.

The Capitol dome belongs to all citizens, but American history has never made that ownership easy. Reconstruction, in the deepest sense, remains unfinished.

  5. 05Achieving a healthy democracy requires moving beyond procedural civility to actively dismantle racial hierarchies in voting access, criminal justice, and economic policy.
  6. 06The goal of democratic reform should not be to recover a past consensus based on exclusion, but to complete the unfinished work of building a genuinely multiracial representative system.
