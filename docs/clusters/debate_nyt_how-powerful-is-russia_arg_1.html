Vladimir Putin’s return to the Kremlin does not make Russia a superpower again, but it does confirm that Russia remains a power the United States cannot safely ignore. The right question is not whether Moscow can rival Washington across the board, as the Soviet Union once did. It cannot. The question is whether Russia has enough capacity, geography and will to affect American interests in important places. It plainly does.

Russia’s strengths are real. It possesses the world’s largest or second-largest nuclear arsenal, a permanent seat and veto at the United Nations Security Council, vast territory across Europe and Asia, major arms industries, and a formidable intelligence and security apparatus. It is central to questions involving Iran, Syria, Afghanistan, missile defense, nonproliferation and European energy security. It can help implement sanctions or weaken them; it can permit supply routes to NATO forces in Afghanistan or make them more difficult; it can sell weapons and diplomatic cover to governments Washington wants isolated. That is not global dominance, but it is meaningful leverage.

Energy is part of this leverage, though often overstated. Russia’s oil and gas exports give Moscow influence, especially in parts of Europe that depend heavily on Gazprom supplies. Past disputes with Ukraine and Belarus showed that energy can be used politically. But the “energy weapon” is constrained by Russia’s own dependence on revenue. The Russian budget relies heavily on oil and gas income, and Moscow needs European customers as much as many European states need Russian supplies. Pipelines create mutual dependence, not one-way control. In the long run, this dependence is also a weakness: it ties Russian prosperity to commodity prices and discourages economic diversification.

Russia’s weaknesses are equally significant. Its economy is large but not dynamic in the way China’s, India’s or Brazil’s are. It suffers from corruption, capital flight, weak rule of law and a business climate that too often depends on political connections. Its population faces troubling demographic and public health trends, even if the worst predictions of collapse have not materialized. Its military has improved since the chaotic 1990s, and the 2008 war with Georgia showed Moscow’s willingness to use force in its neighborhood. But that war also exposed serious deficiencies in command, equipment and logistics. Russia can dominate smaller neighbors; it cannot project power globally on an American scale.

The election and the protests matter because they shape how Putin is likely to use the power he has. The large demonstrations after the parliamentary elections and now after the presidential vote reveal a legitimacy problem among urban, educated Russians. Putin can respond by allowing more political competition and legal accountability, or by tightening control and portraying dissent as foreign-inspired. His campaign already leaned heavily on suspicion of the United States, including accusations that Washington encourages opposition activity. That suggests U.S.-Russian relations may become more brittle.

The Obama administration’s “reset” produced some tangible results: New START, cooperation on Afghanistan transit, Russian support for tougher Iran sanctions, and progress on Russia’s entry into the World Trade Organization. But the reset always rested on a narrow bargain, not a broad strategic partnership. Missile defense, NATO enlargement, human rights, Georgia, and now Syria remain deep points of disagreement. Russia’s recent veto, with China, of a U.N. resolution on Syria reflected not only loyalty to an arms client but also Moscow’s fear that Western-backed humanitarian intervention is becoming a tool for regime change.

The United States should therefore neither inflate Russia into a revived Soviet menace nor dismiss it as a declining petro-state. Rising powers, especially China, deserve more long-term strategic attention. But Russia is still unusually able to frustrate U.S. policy at low cost, particularly in Eurasia and at the United Nations. It is also able to cooperate when interests overlap. A sensible American approach would be firm about democratic values and the sovereignty of Russia’s neighbors, pragmatic on arms control and supply routes, and realistic about Moscow’s insecurities. Russia is powerful enough to matter, but not powerful enough to be treated as America’s central rival.

"The United States should treat Russia as a transactional spoiler power rather than a peer competitor, recognizing its capacity for obstruction despite significant structural weaknesses."

Russia in 2012 is best understood as a spoiler power rather than a rival great power. It retains three genuine sources of leverage — a nuclear arsenal comparable to America's, a permanent Security Council seat, and a near-monopoly on pipeline gas to much of Europe — but each of these is more a veto on others' ambitions than a platform for projecting Russian ambitions of its own. That asymmetry should shape how Washington thinks about Moscow.

Consider the structural weaknesses. Russia's GDP, around $1.9 trillion, is smaller than Italy's and roughly an eighth of America's. Hydrocarbons supply more than half of federal budget revenue, and the IMF has estimated the fiscal break-even oil price has climbed past $110 a barrel — meaning Putin's social bargain depends on prices staying high. Demographically, the population is still shrinking and aging; capital flight ran above $80 billion in 2011; and the much-touted Skolkovo and modernization agenda has produced little. The Customs Union with Belarus and Kazakhstan is real but small. Beyond the post-Soviet space, Russia has no meaningful alliance network. The "gas weapon" cuts both ways: the 2009 Ukraine cutoff accelerated European diversification toward LNG, Nord Stream alternatives, and shale exploration in Poland.

So no, the United States should not treat Russia as a peer competitor on the order of a rising China. China's economy is roughly seven times larger, its growth trajectory transformative, and its strategic reach in Asia, Africa, and Latin America genuinely new. India, Brazil, and Turkey matter more to the shape of the coming international order than Russia does.

But "worry less" is not the same as "ignore." Russia can block what the U.S. wants to accomplish: in Syria, where Moscow and Beijing have already vetoed two Security Council resolutions; on Iran sanctions; on missile defense in Europe; on further arms reductions after New START. The reset produced real gains — the Northern Distribution Network into Afghanistan, the 123 agreement, WTO accession — and Putin's return makes preserving them harder, given his preferred narrative of Western encirclement and his suspicion that the State Department instigated December's protests.

The sensible posture is therefore to deal with Russia transactionally, issue by issue, without inflating it into something it is not — a declining petro-state with nuclear weapons, capable of obstruction but not of leadership.

Russia’s power is real but brittle, a fact laid bare by the street protests greeting Vladimir Putin’s tainted re-election. The regime leans on a narrowing base of legitimacy, and that domestic fragility limits its global reach.

Militarily, Russia retains a vast nuclear arsenal and a Security Council veto—instruments of disruption, not construction. But its conventional forces are corrupt, poorly maintained, and unable to project power far beyond its borders. The 2008 Georgia war exposed glaring operational flaws. Russia can menace neighbors and prop up clients like Syria’s Assad, but it cannot sustain expeditionary campaigns.

The energy “weapon” is equally double-edged. Moscow can—and does—cut gas supplies to extract political concessions, yet Gazprom is an inefficient monopoly and Europe responds by accelerating diversification. The U.S. shale gas boom, though still in early stages, is already loosening Russia’s pricing leverage. Russia’s own dependence on hydrocarbon revenues to fund budgets and patronage makes it vulnerable to price swings.

Economically, Russia is a petro-state with a shrinking, unhealthy population and a stifling corruption problem. WTO accession will expose uncompetitive industries. Its economy is too narrow to underpin a genuine superpower.

So the United States should neither ignore nor obsess over Russia. Moscow can spoil and obstruct, but it is a regional power with global pretensions, not a peer competitor. The real urgency lies in managing the rise of China, which is reshaping trade, finance, and military balances across the Pacific. Russia merits a pragmatic, limited-engagement strategy, while Washington devotes its strategic attention to Asia and the emerging powers that will define the century.

Vladimir Putin’s predictable return to the presidency, accompanied by the most significant domestic protests Russia has seen since the fall of the Soviet Union, forces a recalibration of how we view Russian power. The tens of thousands of citizens braving the Moscow cold to protest widespread electoral fraud highlight a growing fracture in Putin’s carefully managed social contract. For the United States, this domestic unrest—and Putin's reliance on fierce anti-Western rhetoric during his campaign—raises a critical question: How much does Russia still matter on the global stage, and should Washington worry more about unequivocally rising powers like China?

The reality is that Russia is no longer a global superpower, but it remains a formidable regional hegemon and an indispensable geopolitical spoiler. Its power does not stem from a dynamic, modernizing economy or soft-power appeal, but rather from a potent combination of legacy Soviet assets and geographic luck. Foremost among these is its permanent, veto-wielding seat on the United Nations Security Council. As we are currently witnessing with the tragic crisis in Syria, Moscow retains the absolute ability to paralyze international consensus. When combined with the world’s largest nuclear arsenal and a massive geographic footprint spanning Europe and Asia, Russia’s capacity to demand a seat at the diplomatic table is impossible to ignore.

At the same time, the geopolitical utility of Russia’s energy “weapon” is often overstated. It is true that Moscow supplies a vital percentage of Europe’s natural gas, a reality that allows it to exert intense political pressure on its neighbors. However, this sword is undeniably double-edged. The Russian state budget is dangerously dependent on these hydrocarbon revenues. Moscow cannot simply shut off the taps without inflicting severe fiscal damage on itself, threatening the very economic stability that Putin has used to justify his authoritarian grip for the past twelve years.

Beneath its aggressive geopolitical posture, Russia suffers from profound, perhaps terminal, structural weaknesses. The country faces an ongoing demographic crisis, characterized by poor public health metrics and a shrinking working-age population. Its economy remains remarkably undiversified, plagued by endemic corruption, crony capitalism, and a dismal investment climate that drives capital and educated professionals abroad. The current wave of protests is not just about rigged ballots; it is the voice of an emerging, tech-savvy urban middle class that recognizes the limitations of a stagnant petro-state and is deeply frustrated by a political system seemingly incapable of modernization.

Therefore, American foreign policy must thread a careful needle. We should not treat Russia as a peer competitor on the level of China, whose massive economic engine and global integration pose a profoundly different, long-term structural challenge. A rising China requires a strategic pivot; a declining but proud Russia requires diligent, unsentimental management. Washington must accept that the era of the Obama-Medvedev "reset" is likely over. Yet, we cannot afford to simply ignore Moscow. From nuclear nonproliferation to the logistics of supplying forces in Afghanistan and managing Iranian nuclear ambitions, Russian cooperation remains a frustrating necessity.

Ultimately, Russia is powerful enough to disrupt, but too fragile to dominate. The United States must remain vigilant against Russian overreach while recognizing that Moscow's greatest long-term vulnerabilities are now entirely domestic.

The Wrong Question

"How powerful is Russia?" is the kind of question that produces unsatisfying answers because it lumps too many different forms of power into a single bucket. Russia is enormously powerful in some domains and pathetically weak in others, and the policy implications differ in each case.

Militarily, Russia retains the world's second-largest nuclear arsenal and a conventional military that, while a shadow of the Soviet force, has been modernizing with serious investment since the embarrassing 2008 Georgia war revealed its deficiencies. In its near abroad, it remains the dominant military actor. Beyond that perimeter, it has limited expeditionary capacity.

Economically, Russia is a one-trick pony, and the trick is hydrocarbons. High oil prices have funded Putinism; a sustained drop below $80 a barrel would expose the fragility of the social contract he has built. The state's failure to diversify the economy, despite twelve years of opportunity, is the single most important fact about Russia's future trajectory.

Diplomatically, Russia punches above its economic weight because of the Security Council veto, the energy relationship with Europe, and a foreign service that is, frankly, better than ours at playing weak hands well. The Lavrov-Clinton dynamic, the deft maneuvering on Syria, the patient cultivation of ties with China, India, and Iran — these reflect skill, not just resources.

Soft power is where Russia is weakest. Nobody wants to immigrate to Russia (except from poorer post-Soviet states), nobody learns Russian for opportunity, and the Putin model attracts imitators only among kleptocrats. The protests on Bolotnaya Square show that even at home, the regime's legitimacy is contested.

So how should the U.S. think about this? Worry about Russian behavior, not Russian power. Engage on arms control and counterproliferation where interests align. Support civil society and the rule of law where we can do so without backfire. And recognize that the China question is bigger — but a botched Russia policy can still cost us dearly.
