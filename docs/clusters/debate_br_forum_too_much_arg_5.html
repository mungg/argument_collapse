Diversified Gemini

"Nancy Hirschmann’s critique of care feminism is based on a fundamental mischaracterization of the theory, wrongly conflating the ethical responsibility of care with the narcissistic class privilege of the upper-middle class."

Nancy J. Hirschmann’s critique of care feminism relies on a fundamental mischaracterization of what theorists of care have actually argued over the past three decades. By equating the profound, systemic ethic of care with the selfish, indulgent behaviors of privileged parents, she throws the baby out with the bathwater. Hirschmann points to a few egregious examples of parental enabling—the mother of a plagiarist, the parents of a drunk driver—and suggests these represent the logical endpoint, or at least a dangerous byproduct, of feminist efforts to validate care work. But to conflate the defense of an underappreciated human necessity with the narcissistic protectionism of the upper-middle class is a profound theoretical misstep.

Care feminism, as developed by thinkers like Carol Gilligan, Nel Noddings, and Joan Tronto, never posited that any action taken in the name of a child constitutes “care” in the ethical sense. Care, as an ethic, is inherently relational and grounded in responsibility. It is not merely the biological fact of mothering, nor is it the blind defense of one’s progeny against the just consequences of their actions. The mother who calls a professor to bully them into changing a grade for a plagiarizing son is not practicing an “ethic of care”; she is exercising class privilege and teaching her child entitlement. She is operating under an ethic of domination and market competition, ensuring her son secures his place at a "high-profile investment firm" by any means necessary. To lay this behavior at the feet of care feminism is to misunderstand the theory entirely.

Hirschmann notes with dismay that her students plan to become stay-at-home mothers, using the language of care to justify what she sees as a retreat from feminist progress. She is right to be frustrated by young women who embrace a highly individualized, consumerist version of domesticity while looking down on welfare mothers who are structurally forced into different choices. However, the problem lies not with the theory of care, but with how it has been co-opted by a neoliberal, hyper-individualized culture. These students are not reading Gilligan and concluding they must abandon the public sphere; they are reading a cultural landscape that makes combining work and family agonizingly difficult, and they are retreating into the private sphere where they feel they have control. They are using the vocabulary of "care" to mask a retreat into class-based isolationism.

Hirschmann’s proposed solution—that women must stop caring so much, gain more economic clout, and use that leverage to force men to change—is surprisingly retrograde. It resurrects the 1970s liberal feminist model that measures women’s worth and power primarily through their participation in the capitalist wage-labor market. This model effectively cedes the moral high ground to the market, accepting the premise that unpaid relational work is inherently disempowering and secondary to "productive" labor.

But the original insight of care feminism remains true: a society that values only wage labor and individual autonomy is a fundamentally broken one. If we urge women to care less so they can compete more effectively with men in the workplace, we are not changing the rules of the game; we are merely asking women to play a rigged game more aggressively. Who, in this scenario, will do the actual work of caring? Hirschmann hopes that by dropping the ball, women will force men to pick it up. But the more likely outcome, in a society that already systematically devalues dependency, is that the ball will simply be dropped. Children, the elderly, and the sick will suffer, or the labor will be outsourced to marginalized women who will perform it for poverty wages.

Hirschmann also warns that if care is treated as a public good deserving of public subsidy, the state will inevitably demand to oversee and regulate how mothers raise their children. She evokes the specter of the state judging whether mothers are producing "good citizens." But this fear-mongering ignores the fact that the state already heavily regulates and oversees marginalized families. The child welfare system constantly polices poor women, particularly women of color, judging their parenting against middle-class norms. Middle-class and wealthy families are largely exempt from this surveillance not because they don't receive public subsidies (as Hirschmann acknowledges, they receive massive subsidies through the tax code), but because of their class power. Recognizing care as a universal public good, akin to public education, would ideally democratize this support, rather than increasing punitive oversight.

The failure to achieve an equal division of labor in the home is indeed one of the great unfinished tasks of the feminist movement. But we cannot achieve equality by devaluing the very work that sustains human life. We need a political movement that demands the restructuring of the workplace to accommodate human vulnerability and caregiving needs for all workers, male and female. We need universal childcare, paid family leave, and a shorter workweek. Relying on individual women to wage a private strike in their marriages, armed with the "leverage" of their salaries, is a recipe for endless marital conflict and burnout, not systemic revolution. Care feminism does not need to be abandoned; it needs to be radicalized.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The behavior of over-involved parents who shield their children from accountability is an exercise in class entitlement and market competition rather than a practice of the relational ethic of care.
  2. 02The retreat of young women into stay-at-home mothering is a response to a neoliberal cultural landscape that makes balancing work and family difficult, not a logical outcome of care theory.
  3. 03Hirschmann’s solution of 'caring less' to gain economic leverage is a retrograde return to liberal feminism that accepts the market's devaluation of essential, unpaid relational work.
  4. 04Urging women to drop caregiving responsibilities in hopes that men will pick them up is more likely to result in the suffering of dependents or the exploitation of marginalized women.
  5. 05Recognizing care as a universal public good would democratize support for all families rather than simply increasing the punitive state oversight that already targets poor mothers.
  6. 06True gender equality requires a radical restructuring of the workplace and public policy to accommodate caregiving for everyone, rather than individual women waging private strikes within their marriages.
Diversified Gemini

"The problems Nancy Hirschmann identifies in modern parenting stem not from an excess of care, but from a philosophical conflation of care with indulgence and narcissism."

In "Mothers Who Care Too Much," Nancy J. Hirschmann presents a compelling, if deeply troubling, portrait of how the feminist "ethic of care" has been distorted. She observes that students and mothers use the language of care to justify what is, in reality, a retreat into class privilege, enabling behavior, and an abdication of civic responsibility. Hirschmann’s diagnosis of the political and economic implications is astute, but her philosophical framing of the problem reveals a significant misunderstanding of the concept of care itself.

The crux of Hirschmann’s argument relies on the premise that the behavior she describes—the mother defending her plagiarizing son, the parents sending their vehicular-manslaughter-committing daughter to Paris, the stay-at-home mother isolating her children from the diversity of public schooling—actually constitutes "care." She writes, "We should resist the temptation to claim that this is not genuine care. It is one part of the reality of care, as distinct from the idealized image."

Here, Hirschmann makes a profound philosophical error. She conflates "care" with "indulgence," "enabling," and "attachment." By accepting these toxic behaviors as "genuine care," she allows the concept to be degraded, which in turn leads her to the flawed conclusion that women need to "care a little less."

Drawing on the tradition of virtue ethics, we must insist that "care" is not merely an emotion, an intense biological attachment, or the act of expending effort on behalf of one's offspring. Care, in a moral and ethical sense, is a virtue. And like all virtues, it is defined by its end: the flourishing of the individual being cared for. True human flourishing is not achieved by insulating a child from the consequences of their actions, nor is it achieved by securing their unearned admission into an investment bank. Flourishing requires the development of character, integrity, resilience, and a sense of justice.

When the mother of Anthony, the plagiarist, calls the professor to demand leniency because cheating "had nothing to do with anything" regarding her son's future in finance, she is not exhibiting care. She is exhibiting narcissism. She views her son not as an independent moral agent whose character must be formed, but as an extension of her own ego and status. By shielding him from the consequences of his dishonesty, she is actively harming his moral development. She is teaching him that rules do not apply to him, that truth is subordinate to success, and that his wealth shields him from accountability. This is not "caring too much." This is a failure to care at all about the boy's soul.

Similarly, the parents of Carla Wagner, who request she spend the summer in Paris after killing a girl while driving drunk, are committing an act of profound moral neglect. True care for a child who has committed a terrible wrong involves guiding them through remorse, restitution, and accountability. It requires the painful work of helping the child face the gravity of their actions. Sending her to Paris is an act of erasure. It is a refusal to parent.

Hirschmann’s reluctance to judge these acts as anything other than "genuine care" stems from a modern, subjectivist view of morality, wherein if a mother *feels* she is caring, her actions must be classified as care. But virtue ethics demands an objective standard. If an action demonstrably corrupts the moral character of the recipient, it cannot be called care.

Because Hirschmann accepts this corrupted definition of care, she struggles to find a way out of the feminist dilemma. If care naturally leads to this kind of selfish, insular protectionism, then of course it undermines "basic political values as freedom, equality, and engaged citizenship." And if this is what care is, then her solution makes sense: women must do less of it. They must step back, become more individualistic, and focus on their own economic power.

But if we reclaim the true definition of care, the solution changes entirely. We do not need women to care less; we need parents to care *better*.

The ethic of care, properly understood, is not antithetical to justice; it is the foundation of it. As Carol Gilligan herself argued, an ethic of responsibility must include both self and other. It requires recognizing the interconnectedness of all people. A mother who truly understands the ethic of care knows that she cannot raise a good child in a vacuum. She knows that her child's flourishing is ultimately bound up in the flourishing of the community. Therefore, instilling a sense of civic duty, respect for others, and an understanding of structural inequality is not something external to care; it is the very substance of it.

Hirschmann complains that feminists focus too much on the "burdens" of mothering and ignore the private "goods" (laughter, intimacy) that parents receive. She wonders if the state should tax these emotional goods. This bizarre calculus reveals the limits of trying to understand human relationships through the lens of pure political economy. The joy a parent feels in raising a child is not a taxable commodity; it is the natural byproduct of virtuous action.

Furthermore, Hirschmann's ultimate prescription—that women must use economic leverage to force men to do more housework—reduces the family to a site of endless transactional warfare. It assumes that human beings only respond to power and incentives, completely writing off the possibility of moral persuasion, mutual love, and shared commitment to the virtue of care.

If feminists want men to share the work of raising the next generation, we must not adopt the tactics of the marketplace. We must instead articulate a compelling moral vision of what fatherhood and motherhood ought to be. We must insist that care is not a weak, emotional indulgence that traps women, but a rigorous, demanding moral discipline that is required of all adult human beings. It is the hard work of shaping raw human potential into decent, responsible, and just citizens.

The students in Hirschmann's class who want to be stay-at-home mothers are not wrong to think that raising children is "the most important job." They are only wrong if they define that job as merely providing comfort and advantage to their own biological offspring at the expense of the wider world. The task of feminism is not to disabuse them of the importance of motherhood, but to elevate their understanding of what true mothering—and true fathering—demands.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Care should be defined as a virtue aimed at the moral flourishing and character development of the individual, rather than the mere shielding of a child from consequences.
  2. 02The toxic behaviors Hirschmann describes, such as enabling plagiarism or vehicular manslaughter, constitute moral neglect and a failure to care rather than 'genuine care.'
  3. 03Hirschmann’s suggestion that women should 'care less' is a flawed solution based on a corrupted definition of the term; the actual necessity is for parents to care better.
  4. 04A proper ethic of care is the foundation of justice and civic responsibility, as it recognizes that an individual's flourishing is inextricably linked to the flourishing of the community.
  5. 05Reducing family dynamics to a struggle for economic leverage and power ignores the potential for moral persuasion and a shared commitment to care as a rigorous discipline for both genders.
