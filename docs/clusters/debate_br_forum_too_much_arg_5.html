"The problem with modern mothering is not that women care too much, but that American society assigns moral value to care based on class, allowing affluent families to use the rhetoric of 'care' to privatize advantage and hoard opportunity."

The most revealing moment in Nancy Hirschmann’s essay is not the anecdote about plagiarism or the drunken-driving case, though both are memorable. It is the classroom contrast: students who celebrate affluent married women staying home with children but condemn poor single mothers for not working. That double standard is the key to the whole discussion. The trouble is not simply that some mothers care too much. It is that American culture assigns moral meaning to mothering through class.

A married middle-class woman at home is described as making a sacrifice. A poor mother at home is described as avoiding work. A professional woman’s dependence on a husband is called family teamwork. A welfare mother’s dependence on public assistance is called dependency. A suburban mother’s advocacy for her child is called involvement, even when it becomes bullying. A poor mother’s advocacy may be treated as aggression or pathology. The same act—staying home, asking for help, defending a child—changes moral color depending on the mother’s class, race, marital status, and proximity to respectability.

Hirschmann knows this, but her argument sometimes drifts toward making stay-at-home mothers as such the problem. I would put the emphasis elsewhere. The problem is the privatization of advantage through the family. Affluent parents do not merely love their children. They purchase neighborhoods, schools, test preparation, lessons, internships, safety, legal representation, and second chances. They also cultivate habits of entitlement: the assumption that institutions exist to serve their family’s trajectory. When they invoke care, it is often in defense of inherited privilege.

This phenomenon is not limited to mothers. Fathers call coaches, hire lawyers, donate to schools, and trade on professional networks. But mothers are often the day-to-day managers of class reproduction. They fill out applications, monitor homework, arrange enrichment, volunteer strategically, intervene with teachers, and polish children into competitive applicants. The contemporary ideal of “intensive mothering,” analyzed by sociologists such as Sharon Hays, demands extraordinary investments of time and emotional energy. It is not simply a patriarchal relic; it is also an adaptation to inequality. When the distance between success and failure widens, parents with resources feel compelled to pour everything into ensuring that their children land on the winning side.

That is why telling women to “care a little less” is both right and insufficient. Individual mothers may need to stop treating every institutional rule as negotiable when their own child is inconvenienced. But the pressure will remain as long as childhood is organized as a high-stakes competition for scarce security. If health care, decent schools, safe neighborhoods, and dignified work were less unequally distributed, parental panic would diminish. Instead, we have built a society in which parents are told that a child’s future depends on constant management. Under such conditions, the family becomes a private rescue boat. Those with better boats praise themselves for devotion and blame those who drown.

Care feminism is vulnerable to this dynamic when it speaks of care without speaking of inequality. But an anti-care feminism is vulnerable too, especially if it imagines that paid employment straightforwardly liberates women. For poor women, paid labor has never been the badge of feminist modernity. Black women, immigrant women, and working-class women have always worked, often caring for other people’s children while being judged deficient in caring for their own. Welfare reform in the 1990s did not need Linda Hirshman to tell poor mothers to resist domestic dependency; it forced them into low-wage work under threat of losing aid. Many had to leave children in unstable care arrangements to prove their moral worth through employment.

That history should make us cautious about any feminist rhetoric that treats wage work as civic virtue and full-time caregiving as withdrawal. Paid work can be a site of independence, solidarity, and public contribution. It can also be degrading, underpaid, dangerous, and incompatible with family life. The question is not whether women should work, but what kind of work, under what conditions, with what supports, and with what alternatives.

Hirschmann’s examples come largely from elite education, and they show a genuine pathology: privileged families using maternal devotion to excuse dishonesty. But the public conversation about bad mothering rarely targets those families. It targets poor women, especially women of color. The “welfare queen” stereotype did enormous political work in the late twentieth century, helping justify cuts, surveillance, work requirements, and moral lectures. Meanwhile, public subsidies for affluent families remain hidden: mortgage interest deductions, tax advantages, school funding tied to property wealth, employer-provided health insurance exclusions, dependent spouse benefits, college legacy preferences, and informal professional networks.

If care is a public good, Hirschmann asks, shouldn’t there be public oversight? The answer depends on who is being overseen and how. The United States already subjects poor mothers to extensive oversight: caseworkers, eligibility rules, child welfare investigations, school discipline systems, housing regulations, drug testing proposals, and criminal courts. Affluent parents receive a presumption of privacy. A mother who hires a lawyer for her son is caring; a poor mother who misses a welfare appointment because childcare fell through is irresponsible. Before feminists worry that recognizing care will invite surveillance, we should notice that surveillance is already here, but unevenly distributed.

A democratic politics of care would reverse the gaze. It would scrutinize not the poor mother’s refrigerator but the institutions that allow wealthy families to monopolize opportunity. It would ask why public schools are so unequal, why childcare workers are so badly paid, why paid leave is scarce, why wages at the bottom are inadequate, why prisons have become warehouses for the failures of education and employment policy, and why the tax code subsidizes some family forms while stigmatizing others. It would also ask why men across classes are permitted to treat care as optional.

Hirschmann is correct that men must change. Yet men will not change merely because individual women bargain harder, particularly when class inequality lets some families outsource the conflict. A professional mother may keep her career by hiring a nanny, often a poorer woman who leaves her own children with relatives or in cheaper care. The husband has not necessarily become more egalitarian; the family has purchased another woman’s labor. This arrangement can be better than forcing the professional woman out of work, but it is not feminist justice unless the caregiver herself has decent wages, benefits, respect, and time for her own family.

The best response to the failures Hirschmann identifies is not to devalue mothering but to de-privatize both care and opportunity. Universal early childhood education, paid family leave for mothers and fathers, health care independent of marriage and employment, living wages, predictable schedules, stronger public schools, and caregiver credits in Social Security would help. So would reforms that reduce the arms race of elite parenting: fairer school funding, less reliance on private credentials, and more transparent paths into employment. These policies are not sentimental. They are ways of preventing the family from becoming the primary engine of inequality.

We also need a moral vocabulary that distinguishes care from capture. Genuine care prepares children to live among equals. It teaches accountability, reciprocity, and respect for the claims of others. It does not teach that rules are for people without connections. The mother who refuses to lie for her daughter, who lets her son face consequences for cheating, who tells a child that another child’s life matters as much as his own, is not caring less. She is caring democratically.

Hirschmann has opened an important argument, but we should be careful where we aim our criticism. The target should not be mothers who spend time with children, nor even mothers who find deep meaning in domestic life. The target should be a class society that converts maternal love into competitive hoarding, then congratulates the winners for devotion and punishes the losers for dependency. Feminism should challenge that bargain at every level: in marriages, workplaces, schools, welfare offices, tax policy, and the habits of privileged families. Mothers do not need to care less about their children. They need to care more about everybody else’s children too.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A pervasive double standard exists where the same acts of care—such as staying home or advocating for a child—are praised as sacrifice in middle-class women but condemned as dependency or pathology in poor women.
  2. 02Intensive mothering among the affluent is an adaptation to a high-stakes, unequal society where parents feel compelled to use their resources to ensure their children land on the winning side of a widening success gap.
  3. 03The focus on wage work as a primary feminist virtue ignores the reality that for poor and minority women, labor has often been coercive and used as a tool of moral judgment rather than liberation.
  4. 04Public oversight and surveillance of mothering are already heavily and unfairly concentrated on poor families, while affluent families enjoy a presumption of privacy even when undermining social rules.
  5. 05True gender equality cannot be achieved through individual bargaining alone because class privilege allows wealthy families to outsource care work to poorer women rather than forcing men to change.
  6. 06Feminism should aim to de-privatize care and opportunity through universal social policies that reduce parental panic and prevent the family from serving as the primary engine of social inequality.
  7. 07Democratic care should be redefined as a moral practice that teaches children accountability and respect for others rather than the pursuit of competitive advantage through parental influence.
