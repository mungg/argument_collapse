Diversified Claude

"The only genuine hope for defeating the Pakistani Taliban lies in a sustained and vocal mobilization of civil society to reclaim political space from extremists."

The most important thing that happened in Pakistan this week was not the attack itself, terrible as it was. It was the candlelight vigil in Lahore, the protests outside Lal Masjid in Islamabad demanding that its cleric Maulana Abdul Aziz condemn the killings, the spontaneous gatherings of ordinary Pakistanis from Karachi to Quetta refusing to absorb this as just one more atrocity. Whatever hope exists against the Pakistani Taliban lives in those crowds, not in the military's press briefings.

For years, the standard analysis has been that Pakistan's jihadist crisis is a problem of state policy — of generals, of the ISI, of the cynical doctrine of strategic depth. All true. But state policy in Pakistan has been able to persist because civil society has been fragmented, intimidated, and often complicit. Liberal voices have been picked off one by one — Salmaan Taseer in 2011, Shahbaz Bhatti weeks later, journalists like Hamid Mir nearly killed this past April. The religious right has held the streets because the rest of the country has ceded them.

The Peshawar massacre has the potential — only the potential — to break that paralysis. When the All Pakistan Ulema Council issues a fatwa against the attackers, when the Jamaat-e-Islami's leadership is forced into uncomfortable condemnations, when even Imran Khan ends his talks-with-the-Taliban rhetoric, the political space shifts. The job of those who want a different Pakistan is to widen that space and prevent it from closing again in three weeks, as has happened after every previous atrocity.

Concretely: this means sustained pressure on the government to deregister and prosecute groups that glorify the TTP; it means demanding that media outlets stop hosting apologists like Aziz as legitimate commentators; it means supporting the schools, especially girls' schools, that the Taliban targets precisely because they represent the alternative future; it means standing visibly with the Hazara, Ahmadi, and Christian communities who have borne the brunt of sectarian violence while the majority looked away.

Military operations have their place. So do madrassa reform and a strategic rethink toward India. But none of those happen without political will, and political will in Pakistan has been absent because the constituency for it has been silent. The children of the Army Public School deserve, at minimum, that their countrymen finally find their voice. That is where hope begins.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01State policy has historically failed to address the jihadist crisis because civil society has been too fragmented and intimidated to challenge the status quo.
  2. 02The Peshawar massacre has created a rare moment of national unity that has forced religious and political leaders to shift their rhetoric away from Taliban apologia.
  3. 03To prevent a return to paralysis, the public must exert continuous pressure on the government to prosecute extremist groups and deny media platforms to their supporters.
  4. 04True resistance involves actively supporting the institutions and marginalized communities that the Taliban targets, such as girls' schools and religious minorities.
  5. 05Military and strategic reforms are ineffective without the underlying political will generated by a vocal and active citizenry.
