"Sarbanes-Oxley has not failed because it has successfully improved the reliability of public-company financial statements and increased executive accountability, even if it cannot prevent all financial crises."

Sarbanes-Oxley has not failed, but it has often been judged against the wrong test. It was not designed to prevent every corporate collapse or every financial crisis. Bear Stearns and Lehman Brothers were, above all, failures of leverage, liquidity, risk management and regulatory oversight in the shadow banking system. Sarbanes-Oxley was aimed at something narrower: making public-company financial statements more reliable after Enron, WorldCom and Tyco exposed fraud, auditor conflicts and supine boards.

On that score, the law has had real value. Chief executives and chief financial officers now must personally certify accounts. Audit committees are more independent. The Public Company Accounting Oversight Board ended the old system in which the auditing profession largely policed itself. Section 404’s internal-control requirements forced companies to document and test systems that many executives had barely understood. Restatements and control weaknesses did not disappear, but investors have better tools for detecting them, and managers have stronger incentives not to look away.

Still, the critics were not inventing the costs. The first years of Section 404 compliance were expensive, especially for smaller public companies, and often encouraged box-checking rather than judgment. For a young manufacturer or software firm, audit fees and consultant costs can be a serious burden. Congress and regulators were right to scale requirements for smaller issuers and to push auditors toward risk-based testing rather than exhaustive paperwork.

The deeper weakness is that Sarbanes-Oxley improves disclosure without guaranteeing wisdom. A bank can disclose numbers more accurately and still take disastrous risks. Compensation incentives, off-balance-sheet exposures, derivatives, credit ratings and capital regulation require other tools, including parts of Dodd-Frank.

So the answer is not repeal but refinement. Keep the core: independent auditing, executive accountability and credible internal controls. Reduce needless compliance costs. And stop expecting an accounting statute to do the work of financial regulation.

"The Sarbanes-Oxley Act has been a qualified success that fulfilled its primary mandate of improving financial reporting and auditor oversight, though it required adjustments for smaller firms and remains weak in specific enforcement areas."

Sarbanes-Oxley has neither failed nor fully succeeded; it has done roughly what it was designed to do, while being asked to do things it was never meant to address. The law was a response to accounting fraud—cooked books, captured auditors, and CEOs who claimed ignorance of their own financials. On those specific problems, the record is reasonably good. Restatements rose sharply in the years immediately after passage as firms cleaned house, and have since stabilized. The Public Company Accounting Oversight Board has ended the era of auditor self-regulation, and Section 302 certifications have made "I didn't know" a much harder defense, as Jeff Skilling and Bernard Ebbers discovered.

Pointing to Bear Stearns and Lehman as evidence of SOX's failure misreads the statute. Those firms did not collapse because their 10-Ks misstated earnings; they collapsed because of leverage, maturity mismatch, and opaque derivatives exposures—problems of prudential regulation, not financial reporting. Lehman's Repo 105 maneuver is the partial exception, and it suggests the audit and disclosure regime still has gaps around off-balance-sheet treatment.

Where SOX is fairly criticized is Section 404(b)'s internal-controls audit, which imposed compliance costs wildly disproportionate for smaller issuers. Congress and the SEC have already responded—exempting non-accelerated filers in Dodd-Frank, and the JOBS Act this spring carved out emerging growth companies. That iteration seems right.

The weaker aspects are enforcement-adjacent: whistleblower protections under Section 806 have produced few wins for employees, and clawbacks under Section 304 have been sparingly used. Ten years in, SOX deserves a passing grade on its actual mandate, with targeted reform rather than repeal or radical expansion.

Vanilla DeepSeek

"Sarbanes-Oxley has succeeded in its primary mission of restoring integrity to financial reporting and increasing executive accountability, even if it was not designed to prevent broader systemic financial crises."

The tenth anniversary of Sarbanes-Oxley invites a natural audit of its legacy. Critics point to the implosions of Bear Stearns and Lehman Brothers as proof that the law failed to prevent corporate collapse. That charge mistakes the statute’s purpose. Sarbanes-Oxley was a direct response to massive accounting fraud—Enron, WorldCom—where executives systematically falsified financial statements. It was never designed to avert a credit and liquidity crisis driven by mortgage-backed securities. Conflating the two is like blaming a seatbelt for not preventing a bridge collapse.

By its own logic, the law has delivered meaningful, if imperfect, improvements. The core of SOX—Section 404’s requirement for management to assess internal controls—dramatically reduced the incidence of material financial restatements. Studies from the Government Accountability Office and academic research by 2012 show a sharp decline in fraud-related restatements among public companies. The mere threat of CEO and CFO certification under criminal penalty has changed executive behavior, elevating the seriousness of financial reporting. Moreover, auditor independence rules and the creation of the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board ended the self-regulatory model that had clearly failed. While no legislation can eliminate greed, the systemic opportunity for Enron-style deception has been substantially narrowed.

That said, a decade of experience reveals areas too onerous and others too weak. The initial compliance burden, particularly for smaller public companies, was steep and may have chilled innovation or encouraged firms to stay private. Subsequent SEC guidance and the 2010 Dodd-Frank permanent exemption for non-accelerated filers acknowledged that one-size-fits-all was costly. On the weak side, SOX did not address the complexities of modern finance—the very leverage and off-balance-sheet vehicles that felled Lehman. Its focus remained squarely on historical financial statements, not forward-looking risk. Sarbanes-Oxley has not failed; it has succeeded in its primary mission of restoring integrity to financial reporting and amplifying accountability. To ask it to prevent all corporate failures is to confuse an accounting fix for a systemic regulator. The law needs careful calibration, not a repeal, to remain relevant without stifling enterprise.

Vanilla MiniMax

"The Sarbanes-Oxley Act has not failed, as it successfully restored the integrity of financial reporting and auditing, though it was never designed to address the broader systemic risks that led to the 2008 financial crisis."

The tenth anniversary of Sarbanes-Oxley offers a fitting moment to assess whether this landmark legislation has succeeded or failed. The evidence suggests a more complicated verdict than either champions or critics often allow: SOX has achieved significant objectives while leaving certain critical gaps.

The law's core achievements are real. Before 2002, auditor independence had become largely illusory as accounting firms collected lucrative consulting fees from the same companies they audited. SOX's creation of the PCAOB fundamentally changed this dynamic, establishing real oversight of audit firms for the first time. The certification requirements forced senior executives to pay closer attention to financial reporting, and the enhanced penalties created genuine deterrence against fraud. The large-scale accounting scandals that prompted the legislation—Enron-style deception hiding from shareholders—have not recurred on that scale.

Yet the financial crisis of 2008 exposed SOX's limitations. Bear Stearns, Lehman Brothers, and numerous other institutions collapsed not because their accounting was fraudulent, but because of unsustainable leverage, risky assets, and inadequate risk management. SOX addressed the accounting dimension of corporate governance but left untouched broader questions about systemic risk, executive compensation incentives, and the adequacy of capital requirements. The law's focus on individual company behavior could not compensate for regulatory failures elsewhere.

On burden, the critics have a point, though it is overstated. Section 404 compliance has imposed substantial costs, particularly on smaller companies, potentially discouraging some firms from going public. Yet evidence suggests these costs have been overstated, and many compliance expenses represent legitimate improvements in internal controls rather than pure overhead.

SOX has not failed outright—it helped restore confidence in financial reporting that had been badly damaged. But it was never sufficient to prevent all corporate misconduct or systemic financial instability. Its weakness was not that it was too tough, but that it addressed only part of the governance problem. The next decade should focus on complementary reforms rather than dismantling what works.

"Sarbanes-Oxley has largely succeeded in its specific goal of improving accounting transparency and corporate governance, even if it was not designed to prevent broader financial crises caused by poor business judgment."

To judge Sarbanes-Oxley a failure because it did not prevent the 2008 financial crisis is to misunderstand what the law was designed to do. Passed in the wake of the Enron and WorldCom scandals, SOX was fundamentally an accounting and corporate governance measure. Its primary goal was to prevent executives from cooking the books and to restore investor confidence in the accuracy of corporate financial statements.

By that specific metric, the legislation has largely succeeded. We have not seen a repeat of the massive, systemic accounting frauds that defined the early 2000s. Provisions requiring CEOs and CFOs to personally certify their financial reports—under threat of criminal penalty—have fundamentally changed boardroom culture. Audit committees are now more independent, and external auditors face stricter, necessary oversight through the Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB).

However, Sarbanes-Oxley was never a panacea for poor business judgment or excessive risk-taking. The implosions of Lehman Brothers and Bear Stearns were driven by catastrophic bets on toxic mortgage-backed securities and extreme leverage, not by the type of deceptive accounting that characterized Enron. You cannot legislate away bad strategy or market bubbles.

That said, the criticisms regarding the law’s burden are not without merit. Section 404, which requires management to assess internal controls, proved vastly more expensive and time-consuming than anticipated, particularly for smaller firms. The passage of the JOBS Act earlier this year, which smartly scales back some of these compliance requirements for emerging growth companies, is a sensible course correction.

Ultimately, Sarbanes-Oxley succeeded in making corporate ledgers more transparent and reliable. It simply could not protect the financial market from its own hubris.
