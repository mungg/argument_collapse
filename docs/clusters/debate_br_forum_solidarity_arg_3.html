"While Mie Inouye correctly identifies the interpersonal virtues needed for solidarity, her theory fails to address the macro-level structural and economic barriers of neoliberal capitalism that systematically undermine the material preconditions for collective action."

Mie Inouye's essay performs a valuable service by challenging deference politics and articulating an alternative rooted in epistemic humility and social endurance. But I want to argue that the essay, despite its many virtues, retreats from the harder questions that a theory of solidarity must address. By focusing on the micro-level dynamics of organizing encounters, Inouye largely sidesteps the macro-level structural analysis that might explain why solidarity is so difficult to achieve and sustain under contemporary capitalism.

The core of my concern is this: social endurance and epistemic humility are individual-level virtues. They describe the dispositions that individual organizers should cultivate and the norms that organizations should promote. But the barriers to solidarity are not primarily individual; they are structural. Neoliberal capitalism does not merely produce individual subjects who lack the moral development or epistemic humility to recognize their stake in multiracial struggle. It produces entire social structures that systematically undermine the material preconditions for solidarity.

Consider what happened to the labor movement over the past fifty years. Union density in the private sector fell from roughly 35 percent in the 1950s to under 7 percent today. This collapse was not primarily a failure of moral education or organizational culture. It was the result of systematic legal warfare, capital mobility, deindustrialization, and political assault. The workers who might have been available for multiracial coalitions were not merely missing in consciousness; they were removed from the equation entirely. When the factories closed, the possibility of solidarity closed with them.

Inouye's account of the George Floyd uprisings is instructive here. She notes that despite remarkable displays of interracial solidarity in the summer of 2020, we did not see the emergence of "a sustained multiracial movement against policing and mass incarceration." She attributes this failure partly to the limitations of deference politics and the absence of organizations capable of holding diverse interests together. These are real factors. But she does not adequately address the structural context that made organizational development so difficult.

The COVID-19 pandemic, which created the conditions for the 2020 uprisings, also devastated the organizational infrastructure that might have channeled protest energy into sustained movement building. Community organizations closed or went virtual. Mutual aid networks formed and dissolved. The workers who flooded into the streets were, in many cases, the same workers who had been atomized by pandemic isolation and economic precarity. Building durable multiracial coalitions under these conditions was nearly impossible, regardless of whether organizers practiced epistemic humility or deference politics.

A more adequate theory of solidarity would locate the problem at the level of political economy, not individual psychology or organizational culture. Jared Clemons, whose work Inouye discusses, comes closer to this analysis when he argues that neoliberal capitalism has "privatized racial responsibility" and undermined the material conditions for cross-class, multiracial coalition. But even Clemons does not go far enough. The problem is not only that white workers do not recognize their stake in racial justice. The problem is that contemporary capitalism systematically destroys the material basis for any form of working-class solidarity, racial or otherwise.

This structural analysis suggests that organizing strategies focused on changing individual consciousness or organizational culture are insufficient. We need a political program capable of transforming the material conditions that make solidarity so difficult to achieve. This means fighting for labor rights, universal healthcare, housing as a human right, and the reconstruction of the social safety net. It means building organizations that can contest political power at the state level, not merely cultivate solidarity at the interpersonal level.

Inouye's personal narrative about her family and the Mormon Church is moving and revealing. But the story of one family's assimilation into "Caucasian democracy" cannot substitute for a systematic account of how racial capitalism structures consciousness and creates obstacles to solidarity. The question is not merely what kinds of organizational practices can cultivate social endurance, but what political and economic transformations would create conditions in which social endurance might actually achieve something.

I do not want to suggest that Inouye's insights are wrong or unimportant. Epistemic humility, conflict tolerance, and organizational cultures that promote endurance do matter. But they matter most when they operate within a broader political framework that addresses the structural sources of disunity. Without such a framework, we risk reproducing the very individualism that the left purports to critique, attributing failures of solidarity to failures of individual virtue rather than to the political economy that systematically undermines collective action.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Social endurance and epistemic humility are individual-level virtues that cannot overcome structural obstacles like capital mobility, deindustrialization, and the legal erosion of labor unions.
  2. 02The failure of the 2020 George Floyd uprisings to produce a sustained movement was due less to organizational culture and more to the atomizing effects of economic precarity and the pandemic's destruction of social infrastructure.
  3. 03A viable theory of solidarity must prioritize a political economy analysis over individual psychology to explain why contemporary capitalism destroys the material basis for any form of working-class coalition.
  4. 04Organizing strategies should focus on a political program that transforms material conditions—such as fighting for universal healthcare and labor rights—rather than merely cultivating interpersonal dispositions.
  5. 05Focusing on individual virtues without a broader framework to contest state power risks reproducing the very neoliberal individualism that the left aims to critique.
Diversified MiniMax

"While social endurance and organizational culture are important for solidarity, they cannot succeed without addressing the broader political and economic conditions that systematically atomize potential coalition members."

The historical record of multiracial coalition in the United States offers both inspiration and warning. Mie Inouye draws on the original Rainbow Coalition to illustrate how solidarity can emerge across racial and class differences when people recognize shared stakes in challenging systemic oppression. But history also teaches us that such coalitions are exceptionally difficult to build and sustain, and that their failures often reveal as much as their successes.

The original Rainbow Coalition of the late 1960s emerged in Chicago under specific historical conditions: the Black Power movement was at its height, the Vietnam War was fueling anti-establishment sentiment, and poverty was a visible and undeniable feature of American life. The alliance between the Black Panthers, Young Patriots, and Young Lords was not simply the product of shared consciousness; it was the product of intensive organizational work, geographic proximity, and external pressure that made coalition survival a matter of practical necessity.

When Fred Hampton was murdered by Chicago police in 1969, the coalition began to fragment. When the Nixon administration's War on Drugs and Law and Order politics intensified, the coalition lost political space. When internal disagreements about strategy and purpose emerged, the organizations that might have resolved those disagreements were under attack from all sides. The Rainbow Coalition did not fail because of insufficient recognition or insufficient deference; it failed because the political conditions that made it possible were being systematically destroyed.

This historical lesson should inform our assessment of contemporary possibilities. Inouye writes that "the gap between the interests of participants in recent movements for racial justice is just too wide." She is right to be concerned, but the gap is not primarily a product of consciousness or organizational failure. It is a product of political conditions that have systematically atomized potential coalition members: deindustrialization, suburbanization, the destruction of labor unions, mass incarceration, and the fragmentation of public space.

The question is not merely how to cultivate solidarity across difference but how to create political conditions in which solidarity becomes possible. This is a different and harder question. It requires engaging with the political economy that structures our possibilities, not merely the organizational cultures that shape our interactions.

Inouye's emphasis on social endurance points toward part of the answer. Movements need to persist through difficult periods if they are to build the infrastructure necessary for coalition building. But persistence alone is not sufficient. Movements also need to win concrete victories that demonstrate the possibility of change and create stakes in continued struggle. They need to develop organizational forms that can survive the inevitable repression and fragmentation that powerful movements always face.

This is a long-term project. The coalitions that transformed American life in the mid-twentieth century—the CIO, the civil rights movement, the anti-war movement—did not emerge overnight. They developed over decades of patient organizing. Inouye's essay is a contribution to that longer project, offering conceptual resources for the organizational work that must precede and accompany coalition building.

But we should not mistake the essay for a program. The theory of social endurance is a beginning, not an end. It points toward the kinds of practices that solidarity organizations might cultivate. But it cannot substitute for the harder work of building the political conditions in which solidarity becomes possible.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The historical failure of movements like the original Rainbow Coalition was driven more by the systematic destruction of political conditions than by internal failures of recognition or deference.
  2. 02The current gap between participants in social movements is primarily a product of structural forces like deindustrialization and the destruction of labor unions rather than a lack of shared consciousness.
  3. 03Solidarity requires more than just persistence; it necessitates winning concrete victories that demonstrate the possibility of change and create tangible stakes for continued participation.
  4. 04The focus of organizing must shift from internal organizational dynamics to engaging with the political economy that structures the very possibility of coalition building.
