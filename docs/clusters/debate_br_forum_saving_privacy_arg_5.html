"While privacy is a vital democratic value, the pursuit of digital anonymity must be balanced against the state's legitimate obligation to protect citizens from networked, transnational threats through accountable and transparent institutions."

Reed Hundt offers an eloquent defense of privacy, but his argument risks underestimating the real difficulty of governing in a world where danger is networked, transnational, and technologically sophisticated. It is easy to say that “privacy versus security” is a false choice. In some philosophical sense, it is. A free society needs both. But policymakers do face tradeoffs, and pretending otherwise can make democratic deliberation less honest, not more.

The first obligation of government is to protect citizens from violence. This does not erase the Bill of Rights, and it certainly does not justify every program adopted after September 11. But it does mean that intelligence collection cannot be treated as a regrettable hobby of power-hungry officials. There are people and organizations actively trying to kill civilians. They use mobile phones, email, Web forums, encryption, disposable accounts, and global financial networks. A government that failed to adapt to those facts would be negligent.

Hundt acknowledges this, but much of his essay frames the state as if it were primarily a menace and technology firms as reluctant accomplices. That is too simple. The same state that conducts surveillance also funds the courts, prosecutes cybercriminals, builds parts of the privacy infrastructure, and is accountable—however imperfectly—to voters, inspectors general, Congress, and judges. Private companies, by contrast, are accountable chiefly to markets. If we are worried about concentrations of information power, we should worry at least as much about companies whose business models depend on tracking, prediction, and behavioral influence.

Nor should we assume that the government’s use of data is uniquely dangerous because commercial false positives merely produce irrelevant advertisements. Commercial errors can deny credit, raise insurance costs, shape employment opportunities, and expose intimate facts. A bad government watch list can be devastating, but so can a private dossier that follows a person through opaque systems of scoring and risk assessment. The line between state and market power is real, but it is not as clean as the essay sometimes suggests.

On the specific question of NSA metadata collection, the debate should be more careful than much of the post-Snowden rhetoric has allowed. Metadata can be revealing, and the legal doctrine that treats all third-party records as unprotected is due for reconsideration. Still, there is a difference between collecting the content of calls and querying a database of call records under restrictions. The difference is not morally decisive, but it matters. A program can be too broad without being “Orwellian” in the full sense. If we exaggerate, we make reform harder by convincing security professionals that civil libertarians do not understand the tools they condemn.

The Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board’s criticism of the telephone metadata program deserves serious attention, especially its claim that the program was not essential to stopping attacks. Effectiveness matters. Intrusive programs should have to prove their value, not merely invoke fear. President Obama’s proposal to end government storage of phone records and require court approval for access moves in the right direction. So do proposals for greater transparency and a more adversarial process before the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court. These are practical reforms, and they are preferable to broad denunciations of intelligence work as such.

Where Hundt is most vulnerable is in his enthusiasm for encryption, Tor, Bitcoin, and pseudonymous digital communities. He is right that encryption is necessary. The government should not weaken standards or require universal backdoors. In a world of identity theft, industrial espionage, hostile foreign intelligence services, and ordinary criminal hacking, strong encryption protects the public. But privacy technologies are not simply instruments of democratic renewal. They also make it easier to launder money, distribute child pornography, hire criminals, evade sanctions, and coordinate terrorist activity. The Silk Road example is not incidental; it is a warning about how quickly high-minded architecture can be repurposed.

Hundt says warrants and traditional criminal process should govern access to encrypted spaces. In principle, yes. But a warrant is useful only if it can be executed. If data are technically inaccessible, if identities are impossible to link to real persons, or if networks are deliberately designed to resist all lawful process, then “get a warrant” becomes a slogan rather than a solution. We need to preserve the possibility of targeted investigation. That does not mean building a master key into every device. It does mean being wary of a politics that treats perfect anonymity as a constitutional ideal.

A better digital settlement would combine several commitments. First, surveillance authorities should be clearer, narrower, and publicly debated to the maximum extent compatible with operational needs. Secret legal interpretations should be rare and temporary. Second, bulk programs should be judged by demonstrated necessity, not hypothetical usefulness. Third, the FISA process should include more independent advocacy when novel legal questions arise. Fourth, companies should be permitted to publish more information about government demands, but they should also be subject to stronger privacy obligations in their own collection and retention practices. Fifth, encryption should be protected, while law enforcement retains tools for targeted investigation through endpoints, undercover work, financial tracking, and compelled disclosure where constitutionally permissible.

The aim should not be to make the Internet either a glass house or a fortress. A glass house would chill speech, expose citizens to abuse, and invite authoritarian habits. A fortress would create zones where law cannot reach, weakening the state’s ability to protect the vulnerable and punish the violent. Democratic government requires something more difficult: accountable power.

Hundt asks, “Who rules?” The answer cannot be simply “individuals,” if that means each person or each digital collective decides when law applies. In a constitutional democracy, the people rule through institutions—legislatures, courts, executives, elections, and public argument. Those institutions are flawed, sometimes badly. They require reform after the Snowden disclosures. But they should not be displaced by faith in code. Code can protect liberty, but it cannot deliberate, balance competing goods, or answer to victims. Privacy is worth saving; so is the capacity of democratic government to defend the public. The hard task is to preserve both without romanticizing either technology or the state.
