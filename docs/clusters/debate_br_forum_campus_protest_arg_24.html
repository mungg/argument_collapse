Diversified Gemini

"While Kelley’s call for radical resistance is historically grounded, his critique of student activists' focus on trauma fails to recognize that anti-Blackness is an ontological reality that cannot be resolved through traditional political struggle or Marxist-inflected optimism."

In "Black Study, Black Struggle," Robin D. G. Kelley offers a compelling, historically grounded plea for Black student activists to move beyond the language of trauma and embrace a radical politics of resistance, solidarity, and "love." Grounded in a Marxist-inflected black radical tradition, Kelley insists that structural oppression can be mapped, understood, and ultimately dismantled. However, in his eagerness to rescue Black agency from the despair of Ta-Nehisi Coates’s *Between the World and Me*, Kelley inadvertently glosses over the profound and totalizing nature of anti-Blackness in civil society. From the perspective of Afropessimism, the students’ focus on trauma and the destruction of the Black body is not a neoliberal trap, but an accurate, if incomplete, apprehension of their ontological reality.

Kelley critiques Coates for suggesting that it is traditional in America to "destroy the black body" and that this violence is our "heritage." Kelley argues that this framing reduces Black people to "fungible bodies" and ignores the dialectic of resistance. "Resistance is our heritage," Kelley declares, pointing to the ways enslaved people loved, worshipped, and defended themselves.

But Kelley’s insistence on this dialectic fundamentally misunderstands the nature of anti-Black violence. The violence enacted upon the Black body is not merely a tool of capitalist discipline or a method to extract labor, which could theoretically be resolved through a socialist revolution. As thinkers like Frank B. Wilderson III and Jared Sexton have argued, anti-Blackness is the foundational architecture of the modern world. Civil society defines itself in opposition to the Black subject; human coherence requires Black subjection. When Coates writes of the casual wrath and random manglings of the Black body, he is naming a gratuitous violence that exceeds economic utility.

Therefore, when Black students arrive at elite universities and immediately feel an overwhelming sense of alienation, isolation, and trauma, they are not simply experiencing a lack of "cultural competency." They are encountering the reality that the university, as a quintessential institution of civil society, is inherently and irredeemably anti-Black. Kelley acknowledges that the university cannot be transformed by "simply adding darker faces" and agrees that it will never be an engine of social transformation. But he still holds out hope for the "undercommons" and the possibility of creating a utopian future that "overthrows the logic of neoliberalism."

This is where Kelley’s optimism becomes a burden. By insisting that "resistance is our healing" and that collective struggle can "eviscerate the source of trauma" and "make ourselves whole," Kelley relies on a narrative of eventual triumph that historical evidence simply does not support. We have been resisting for four hundred years, yet the gratuitous violence continues—looping on our smartphones, as Kelley himself notes, with terrifying regularity. Telling Black students that they can "make themselves whole" through study groups and political education places an impossible burden on them to fix an ontological tear that they did not create and that cannot be repaired through traditional political or economic organizing.

The language of trauma, PTSD, and triggers that the students use is certainly co-opted by neoliberal administrators who prefer to treat systemic violence as an individual psychological issue. Kelley is brilliant in his dissection of how "tolerance" and "diversity" depoliticize structural antagonism. However, the students' insistence on their pain is a rupture. It is a refusal to perform the resilience that the American project demands of Black people. When a Black student says, "I am traumatized, I am not safe here," they are exposing the lie of the liberal institution. They are saying that no amount of diverse reading lists or Black faculty can shield them from the inherent violence of the world.

Kelley’s directive to "love all"—even those who oppress us, following James Baldwin—feels particularly out of step with the current moment. Baldwin’s love was a heavy, agonizing demand, but it was still rooted in a belief that the American soul could be redeemed. In 2016, watching the fires of Ferguson and Baltimore, the demand that Black youth lead with love and assume the moral responsibility for liberating their oppressors is exhausting.

We must consider the possibility that Coates is right: the spirit and the soul *are* the body and the brain, and they are destructible. The emphasis on the Black body is necessary because it is the site of absolute vulnerability. If we take this seriously, then the role of "Black study" is not to prepare for a revolution that will finally bring us into the fold of humanity. It is to study the mechanisms of our exclusion with unsparing clarity, without the comforting illusions of guaranteed progress, and to care for one another in the dark. The students are not wrong to center their trauma; they are simply realizing that the world, and the university within it, was built on their destruction.
