"The crisis of modern democracy stems from a territorial mismatch where national legislatures are held accountable for global forces—such as capital flight, climate change, and migration—that exceed their jurisdictional reach."

Ira Katznelson’s essay is framed around national representative institutions, above all legislatures. That focus is understandable: democracy has historically been organized through the nation-state, and parliaments remain the chief sites of legitimate lawmaking. But one reason legislatures now appear weak is that many of the forces shaping citizens’ lives no longer operate primarily within national borders. Democracy’s anxiety is not only institutional. It is territorial.

The lead essay notes this concern but does not make it central enough. Citizens suspect that national borders no longer define a zone of security or control. They are right. Capital moves faster than tax authorities. Carbon emissions disregard jurisdiction. Migrants flee wars, poverty, and climate stress produced partly by global systems. Terror networks operate transnationally. Supply chains diffuse responsibility. European states negotiate domestic budgets under pressure from markets, Brussels, Frankfurt, and Berlin. Trade agreements constrain regulation. Even the most competent legislature faces problems whose causes and solutions exceed its reach.

This does not mean national democracy is obsolete. It means national democracy is overburdened. Voters hold representatives accountable for wage stagnation, deindustrialization, migration pressures, financial crises, and environmental risks that representatives can influence only partially. The resulting gap between responsibility and capacity breeds cynicism. Politicians promise control they cannot deliver; citizens conclude that politics is fraudulent.

The eurozone crisis is the clearest current example. Greece has held elections, changed governments, and voted in a referendum, yet its policy space remains sharply limited by creditors, European institutions, and the architecture of the euro. One may fault Greek governments for corruption, tax evasion, and fiscal irresponsibility, and still see the democratic problem. If voters cannot choose a meaningfully different economic path without risking financial collapse and expulsion from the currency union, then formal democracy becomes constrained by technocratic and market imperatives. The same tension, though less dramatic, affects Spain, Portugal, Ireland, and even France.

The European Union was created partly to tame nationalism and bind democracies together through law. It has achieved much. But its democratic deficit is not a slogan invented by extremists. The European Parliament has gained power, yet many crucial decisions are made through councils, commissions, central bankers, and negotiations remote from ordinary citizens. National politicians then blame “Europe” for constraints they helped create. This dynamic feeds parties of the radical right and left, from the National Front to Syriza to Podemos to UKIP. Their ideologies differ, but they draw energy from the perception that democracy has migrated away from the voter.

Globalization produces a related challenge in the United States. Congress can debate trade agreements, but once supply chains and investor expectations are entrenched, communities have limited recourse. A factory closure is experienced locally, justified globally, and addressed politically through promises of retraining that often ring hollow. Immigration debates become proxies for wider anxieties about sovereignty, labor competition, and cultural change. Climate policy is delayed because unilateral action seems costly unless others act too. National representatives are blamed for failing to master interdependence.

The answer cannot be simple withdrawal. Autarky is neither feasible nor desirable. Nationalist fantasies of sealed borders and restored economic independence are likely to produce poorer, meaner societies. But neither can democrats accept a world in which economic integration outruns political accountability. If markets are global and democracy remains national, markets will often win.

We need a multi-level democratic imagination. Some powers should be restored to national legislatures where international agreements have become too insulated from revision. Trade deals should be more transparent and more open to labor, environmental, and consumer interests, not merely corporate advisers. Investor-state dispute settlement deserves skepticism when it allows private tribunals to challenge public regulation. Central banks, while needing independence from short-term electoral manipulation, must be subject to clearer democratic mandates and oversight.

At the same time, some problems require stronger transnational democratic institutions. Climate change cannot be solved by national legislatures acting alone. Financial regulation requires coordination to prevent races to the bottom. Tax avoidance by multinational corporations demands international cooperation. Refugee policy cannot be humane if every state tries to shift burdens elsewhere. The democratic question is how to make such cooperation accountable rather than technocratic.

This is difficult because there is no global demos comparable to a national citizenry. Solidarity thins across borders; languages differ; media systems remain national; redistribution is politically hard even within states. But democracy has always been an institutional achievement, not a natural fact. National peoples were made through schools, parties, armies, newspapers, and social policy. Transnational publics may be weaker, but they can be strengthened through parliamentary networks, transparent negotiations, civil society participation, and cross-border party cooperation.

Katznelson’s defense of representation remains essential. The danger is that citizens, feeling dispossessed by global forces, will embrace illiberal leaders promising national rebirth. Viktor Orbán’s “illiberal state” is one answer to globalization: defend the nation by weakening liberal constraints. Similar temptations exist elsewhere. But liberal democrats need more than warnings. They need to show that democracy can regain control without retreating into xenophobia.

The Capitol dome can be repaired in Washington, but democratic capacity must also be repaired in Brussels, Athens, Beijing, Geneva, and the treaty rooms where rules are written. Representation must follow power. If consequential decisions are transnational, democratic accountability must become transnational too. Otherwise national elections will increasingly become rituals of frustration, and frustration will seek harsher remedies.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The gap between the domestic responsibilities of politicians and their actual capacity to control globalized systems breeds public cynicism and fuels the rise of illiberal movements.
  2. 02The eurozone crisis demonstrates how formal democratic processes become hollow when national policy choices are constrained by transnational technocratic and market imperatives.
  3. 03National democracy is overburdened by interdependence, as issues like trade and environmental policy cannot be effectively managed by unilateral legislative action.
  4. 04To restore democratic legitimacy, accountability must follow power through a multi-level approach that strengthens transnational democratic institutions while returning specific regulatory powers to national legislatures.
  5. 05While a global citizenry does not yet exist, transnational publics can be intentionally cultivated through transparent negotiations, civil society participation, and cross-border political cooperation.
