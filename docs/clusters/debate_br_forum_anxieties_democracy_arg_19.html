**The Schmittian Moment**

There is something quietly alarming in the way Katznelson invokes Carl Schmitt only to dismiss him. Schmitt, he tells us, was wrong: postwar legislative democracies disproved the claim that representative regimes possess "intrinsic and unresolvable contradictions requiring a radical shift toward executive power buttressed by the legitimacy of unmediated popular sovereignty." This is the right historical judgment about 1945–1989. I am less sure it is the right judgment about 2015. Schmitt's questions, if not his answers, have returned, and we should sit with them rather than wave them away.

Schmitt's core challenge to liberal parliamentarism, articulated most sharply in *The Crisis of Parliamentary Democracy* (1923), was that the institution's foundational assumptions—rule by discussion, the conversion of disagreement into provisional consensus through open debate, the belief that truth emerges from the competition of arguments—had become hollow. Parliaments, he claimed, no longer practiced genuine discussion. Decisions were made by party caucuses and interest groups before they reached the floor; speeches were ritual performances; the supposedly truth-seeking process was a façade over what was in fact a calculus of force among organized blocs. He concluded that the legitimacy claim of parliamentarism had collapsed, and that some other principle—he proposed plebiscitary acclamation of an executive—must take its place.

The horror of where this argument led Schmitt should not blind us to the empirical force of his diagnosis. Look at the contemporary American Congress. Genuine deliberation occurs rarely on the floor. Speeches are written for C-SPAN clips and fundraising emails, not for persuasion of colleagues. Decisions are made in caucus meetings, by leadership, in conference reports negotiated in private. The myth of legislative deliberation is one many participants no longer bother to maintain. When Mitch McConnell declares that the Senate's purpose is to deny the President a second term, or when bills are passed without anyone reading them, the gap between parliamentarism's ideological self-presentation and its actual operation is what Schmitt was describing.

The same is true elsewhere. The European Parliament, despite its growing formal powers, struggles to constitute itself as a forum of genuine pan-European political contestation. National parliaments across the Eurozone have lost meaningful control over fiscal policy to the troika and the ECB. The British Parliament, sovereign in theory, has delegated wide swaths of policy to administrative bodies and the courts. The institutional fact is that genuine deliberation about major political questions has migrated away from parliaments to other sites—executive councils, central banks, courts, regulatory agencies, transnational bodies.

Katznelson sees this. He mentions "delegation to courts and executive agencies" among the symptoms of legislative dysfunction. But he treats it as a remediable problem—reform the legislature, restore its function—rather than as a structural shift. I worry that the shift is deeper than that. The complexity of modern policy, the speed of modern decision-making, the technical expertise required for financial regulation or climate policy or cyber security, may be genuinely incompatible with parliamentary deliberation as classically understood. If so, the question becomes not how to restore deliberation to its traditional seat but how to democratize the new sites where consequential decisions actually occur.

This is where Schmitt's solution—plebiscitary executive acclamation—becomes seductive, and dangerous. Across the established democracies, we see strong executives increasingly bypassing legislatures: Obama's executive actions on immigration and climate, his predecessors' use of the war powers, the routine resort to executive orders and signing statements. The justifications are often plausible: legislatures cannot act, decisions must be made, leaders elected on democratic mandates have authority to lead. But each instance erodes the principle that consequential lawmaking belongs in the representative branch.

Katznelson is right to warn against this. But the warning needs to be more than exhortation. If the structural conditions for parliamentary deliberation have eroded—if Schmitt's diagnosis applies even though we reject his prescription—then defending parliament requires reconstructing those conditions, not just praising the institution.

That reconstruction would need to address: the integration of expertise with democratic deliberation, the relationship between national parliaments and transnational policy, the temporal mismatch between legislative cycles and policy problems like climate change that operate on multi-decade scales, and the role of parties in mediating between unorganized publics and structured decision-making. These are hard problems, and no postwar institutional settlement adequately resolved them. The Schmittian moment is the moment when these problems become unavoidable. Katznelson signals the moment without quite confronting it.
