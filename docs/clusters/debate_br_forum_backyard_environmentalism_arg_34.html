Diversified Gemini

"While the proposed performance-based regulatory framework correctly identifies the flaws of command-and-control systems, it is practically unworkable without a massive increase in administrative funding and technical infrastructure that currently does not exist."

As a state-level environmental regulator tasked with implementing the laws Congress passes and the rules the EPA promulgates, I read Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen’s “Beyond Backyard Environmentalism” with a mixture of profound validation and deep anxiety. The authors accurately diagnose the rigidities of the old command-and-control system. We see it every day in our offices: federal regulations that make no sense for our specific topography, and permitting processes that focus on checking boxes rather than actually improving environmental quality. The shift toward performance-based, collaborative regulation is indeed happening, and in many ways, it is a vast improvement. But the authors drastically underestimate the sheer administrative and financial burden of the "rolling-rule regime" they propose.

The core mechanism of their new architecture is information. Local units experiment, set targets, and then "provide detailed reports on actual performance and possible improvements." Central authorities then monitor performance, pool experiences, and reformulate standards. This sounds elegantly simple in an academic essay. In the real world, it requires a monumental administrative infrastructure that currently does not exist, and for which no one wants to pay.

Take the Massachusetts Toxics Use Reduction Act (TURA), which the authors highlight as a success. TURA works because Massachusetts invested heavily in it. The state created the Toxics Use Reduction Institute at UMass Lowell, established an Office of Technical Assistance, and funded a peer inspectorate. This requires millions of dollars in state funding and a large staff of highly trained professionals. It is not something that happens organically just because we ask industries to collaborate.

Now, scale this up to the entire country, across every environmental domain. The authors envision local "tributary teams" managing watersheds and local coalitions designing Habitat Conservation Plans (HCPs). Who is collecting the baseline data for these localized plans? Who is paying the biologists, hydrologists, and toxicologists needed to understand the "continuous change in the nature of risks"? Currently, state environmental agencies are facing budget cuts, not expansions. We are struggling to maintain basic compliance inspections under the Clean Water Act.

The authors praise the Chesapeake Bay Program, noting that it shifted toward teaching large numbers of volunteers to "mimic the monitoring and reporting protocols developed by scientific experts." This is a wonderful civic exercise, but as a regulator, relying on volunteer data for enforceable regulatory standards is a nightmare. Volunteers have turnover; their training varies; their equipment is not always calibrated. When a multi-million dollar wastewater treatment plant upgrade hinges on data showing nutrient overloading in a specific stream, that data will be challenged in court. If the data was collected by well-meaning weekend volunteers, it will not withstand legal scrutiny.

Furthermore, the authors talk about "centralized pooling of experience." As of 1999, the state and federal governments do not have the IT infrastructure to seamlessly pool, analyze, and disseminate this massive volume of dynamic data. The EPA’s databases are notoriously fragmented and outdated. Building the kind of responsive, real-time feedback loop the authors describe requires a level of technological and bureaucratic integration that is years, if not decades, away.

The danger of the "rolling-rule regime," from a practical standpoint, is that we will dismantle the rigid command-and-control rules before we have built the capacity to implement the new system. We will end up with the worst of both worlds: the devolution of authority to local actors without the rigorous monitoring, scientific support, and centralized pooling necessary to ensure they are actually protecting the environment.

If Congress and the public truly want a performance-based system that relies on continuous local deliberation and adaptive management, they must be prepared to fund it. Deliberation is incredibly labor-intensive. It requires state agencies to transition from being police officers to being facilitators, scientists, and data managers. I welcome that transition. But we cannot build a 21st-century, data-driven, collaborative regulatory state on a 1970s bureaucratic budget. Until the financial and administrative realities of this new architecture are addressed, the rolling-rule regime will remain an inspiring theory rather than a workable reality.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'rolling-rule regime' requires a monumental administrative infrastructure and high levels of state funding to support the technical institutes and professional staff necessary for its success.
  2. 02State environmental agencies are currently facing budget cuts that make it impossible to hire the specialized scientists needed to manage localized, data-heavy conservation plans.
  3. 03Relying on volunteer-collected data for localized monitoring is legally and scientifically insufficient for enforcing high-stakes regulatory standards.
  4. 04Current government IT infrastructure is too fragmented and outdated to facilitate the seamless, real-time pooling and analysis of data required for a feedback-driven system.
  5. 05Dismantling existing regulations before building the capacity for the new system risks a dangerous regulatory vacuum where local autonomy exists without necessary oversight or scientific support.
  6. 06The transition from a punitive 'police' model of regulation to a collaborative 'facilitator' model is an incredibly labor-intensive process that cannot be achieved on a traditional bureaucratic budget.
