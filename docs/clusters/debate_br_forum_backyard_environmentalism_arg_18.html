"The proposed 'rolling-rule regime' of experimentalist governance is fundamentally incompatible with American administrative law and threatens to undermine the rule of law and democratic accountability."

The environmental policy innovations surveyed by Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen are undeniably intriguing. They suggest that government can be more adaptive, more collaborative, and more effective. But in their haste to praise the pragmatist philosophy of experimentalist governance, the authors sidestep a more fundamental tension: the “rolling-rule regime” is on a collision course with the basic structures of American administrative law. The new architecture, if allowed to expand without a corresponding legal framework, will undermine the rule of law, erode democratic accountability, and ultimately delegitimize the very environmental gains it seeks to achieve.

The traditional environmental regulatory state is built on a foundation of legislative delegation and procedural regularity. Congress passes a statute—the Clean Air Act, the Clean Water Act, the Endangered Species Act—that sets forth broad goals and authorizes an agency to issue rules. Those rules are developed through notice-and-comment rulemaking under the Administrative Procedure Act (APA), subject to judicial review to ensure they are not arbitrary and capricious and stay within the bounds of the statute. This system, for all its sluggishness, provides a clear chain of legal authority and public accountability. When the EPA sets a national ambient air quality standard, it must justify that decision based on a public rulemaking record, and anyone aggrieved can challenge it in court. The result is a body of law that is stable enough for regulated entities to rely on, yet open to revision through prescribed, transparent processes.

The rolling-rule regime, as described, unravels this web. In the Chesapeake Bay Program, the Executive Council issues “directives” that the authors frankly acknowledge are of “dubious legal pedigree and status.” They are treated as “morally binding commitments” by the governors and the EPA administrator. But a moral commitment is not a law. If a governor decides that the tributary team’s plan is too onerous for his state’s farmers and withdraws his support, under what legal authority can he be forced to comply? The Clean Water Act gives the EPA authority to regulate point sources, but not to mandate specific non-point source pollution reductions by states, which was precisely the legislative compromise that created the Bay’s problems in the first place. The program’s elaborate governance structure is essentially an elaborate, extra-statutory framework, operating in the shadow of the law but without the law’s binding force. It works because of the goodwill of the current participants. A change in administration in Richmond or Washington, or a lawsuit by a determined developer, could expose its legal frailty overnight.

The Habitat Conservation Plan process under the Endangered Species Act is similarly adrift. The FWS issues “incidental take” permits based on negotiated HCPs that incorporate all manner of requirements—monitoring regimes, adaptive management triggers, habitat set-asides—that go far beyond anything explicitly authorized by Section 10 of the ESA. The Act says the Secretary can permit a taking if it will not “appreciably reduce the likelihood of the survival and recovery of the species.” It does not say the Secretary can convert the permitting process into a de facto regional land-use planning authority, bundling multiple species, unlisted species, and vast acreages into a comprehensive deal. The Babbitt administration’s aggressive use of HCPs is, in many ways, a heroic effort to make a broken statute work. But it is an effort that operates in a legal gray zone. When a conservation group sues, claiming an HCP does not adequately ensure recovery—as has happened repeatedly in California—the courts are left to navigate a situation where there is almost no hard law to apply, only a mass of agency guidance, informal promises, and complicated local documents. The very flexibility and context-sensitivity that the authors praise is a source of legal arbitrariness. A developer denied a generous permit in one county may point to a more lenient deal struck in another and claim unequal treatment under the law.

The Toxics Release Inventory and TURA raise a different set of legal concerns. TRI requires disclosure, not pollution reduction. But as the authors note, the data is then used “as a lever by which to apply public pressure for improvements,” and often to support citizen suits under other statutes. The line between a neutral information-gathering statute and an informal regulatory scheme that coerces firms through reputational sanctions without due process is dangerously thin. When the EPA issues press releases highlighting the “Dirty Dozen” worst polluters based on TRI data, it is effectively imposing a penalty—stock price depression, consumer boycotts—without any adjudicatory proceeding. The agency disclaims any responsibility for the accuracy of the data, and does not verify it. Yet the practical effect is a powerful regulatory instrument that circumvents the procedural safeguards built into enforcement actions. If a rolling-rule regime extends this logic, establishing “minimum performance standards” based on data pooled from local participants, what recourse will a firm have if it believes the pooled data is flawed, or the benchmark is set unfairly? The APA’s protections against arbitrary government action are largely sidelined in a system where standards “emerge from practice” rather than being promulgated as rules.

The authors’ preferred solution is for Congress to pass something like the Miller Bill, giving explicit statutory blessing to the new architecture. They imagine Congress shifting its role from “setting some relatively circumscribed public goal” to “authorizing and conferring pluralist political legitimacy on the constitutive framework” of experimentalist governance. This is a profound redefinition of the legislative function. It would mean that Congress no longer decides what level of protection is required—that the air be safe for asthmatics, that rivers be fishable and swimmable, that species be pulled back from the brink—but instead merely sets the rules of a game in which those substantive questions are perpetually up for local renegotiation. Such a framework law would raise serious constitutional questions under the nondelegation doctrine, which holds that Congress cannot delegate its legislative power without providing an “intelligible principle” to guide its exercise. “Thou shalt run a collaborative process with good monitoring and public participation” may not be a sufficiently intelligible principle to determine whether a species goes extinct or a community’s water is poisoned.

Moreover, a framework that relies on local negotiation and benchmarking radically reduces the transparency and accountability that the APA was designed to ensure. The public can read a rule in the Federal Register and know what the law requires. How does an ordinary citizen know what standards apply under a rolling-rule system? She would have to study the local HCP, the tributary plan, the pooled benchmarks, and the guidance documents. This information is scattered, often highly technical, and not easily accessible even on the Internet. The promise of “deep use” is that citizens will become co-authors of regulation. The reality is that the power to author will accrue to those with the resources to master an extraordinarily complex, non-public system of governance, while the rule of law, which protects the rest of us by insisting that the rules be publicly known, stable, and equally applied, will be fatally weakened. We should not dismantle the hard-won procedural safeguards of the administrative state in pursuit of a theoretical ideal of deliberation.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The informal 'directives' and 'moral commitments' of collaborative programs lack the binding legal force and statutory authority necessary to ensure long-term compliance and stability.
  2. 02The high degree of flexibility and context-sensitivity in negotiated agreements like Habitat Conservation Plans creates legal arbitrariness and unequal treatment under the law.
  3. 03Information-based regulatory levers, such as the Toxics Release Inventory, bypass the procedural safeguards and due process protections built into traditional enforcement actions.
  4. 04Shifting the role of Congress from setting substantive goals to merely authorizing procedural frameworks may violate the constitutional nondelegation doctrine.
  5. 05The complexity and localized nature of the rolling-rule architecture reduce transparency, making it difficult for ordinary citizens to hold regulators accountable compared to the traditional rulemaking process.
