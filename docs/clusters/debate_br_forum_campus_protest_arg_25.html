Diversified Gemini

"While Robin D. G. Kelley correctly identifies that universities are not engines of social transformation, his focus on internal academic subversion fails to recognize that the campus movement's preoccupation with university reform is a distraction from the urgent, material struggles of the Black working class."

In "Black Study, Black Struggle," Robin D. G. Kelley offers a necessary reality check to the student activists who brought their righteous anger to college campuses in the fall of 2015. His central assertion—that the university "will never be engines of social transformation" and that such work ultimately belongs outside the academy—is the most urgent takeaway of his essay. Yet, despite making this point, Kelley spends the bulk of his piece debating the ideological fissures *within* the campus movement, validating the "undercommons" as a site of radical potential. As a community organizer working in the neighborhoods surrounding these towering academic institutions, I argue that we must take Kelley’s premise further: the intense focus on reforming the university is a massive distraction from the life-and-death struggles happening in the streets.

The campus uprisings were, as Kelley notes, ignited by the fires of Ferguson and the relentless police murders of Black Americans. But as the movement migrated from the streets of our cities to the quads of Yale, Mizzou, and Claremont McKenna, the demands fundamentally shifted. What began as a desperate outcry for Black survival—a demand to stop the state-sanctioned execution of Black people—was translated into the language of the academy. Suddenly, the national discourse was consumed by debates over Halloween costumes, the names of residential colleges, trigger warnings, and the racial composition of reading lists.

Kelley eloquently critiques the neoliberal co-optation of these demands, pointing out how cultural-competency training and diversity initiatives personalize and depoliticize systemic racism. But the deeper problem is not just *how* the students are fighting the university; it is *that* they are fighting the university while the world burns outside its gates.

For the Black working-class residents living in the shadows of these multi-billion-dollar institutions, the campus debates over "safe spaces" feel painfully insular. A revised syllabus does not stop a police bullet. A newly appointed dean of diversity does not stop gentrification, much of which is driven by the very universities these students attend. While activists exhaust themselves occupying administration buildings to secure a new cultural center, community members just blocks away are fighting evictions, mass incarceration, and the gutting of public schools.

Kelley holds up the UCLA graduate students’ "undercommons" as a radical model, praising their outdoor meetings and integration of activists from Black Lives Matter and Critical Resistance. It is certainly a step up from demanding better PR from the administration. But we must be honest: an outdoor seminar at UCLA is still UCLA. It is still a highly exclusive space, gated by academic privilege. To tell young, brilliant Black minds that their highest political calling is to become "subversives in the academy"—sneaking around to steal university resources—is to trap them in a cloistered game of academic hide-and-seek.

The historical models Kelley invokes, like the Mississippi Freedom Schools and the Mt. Vernon collective, were powerful precisely because they were deeply embedded in the community, not the university. They were addressing the immediate, material needs of sharecroppers, domestic workers, and welfare mothers. The education was a tool for the people, not a way to endure life in the ivory tower.

If students want to honor the legacy of the Black radical tradition, they need to recognize the immense privilege of their position and direct their energy outward. We do not need students to spend four years trying to make the university "love" them. We need them to leverage the immense resources of the university—its money, its legal clinics, its research capabilities, its prestige—and funnel them directly into local, grassroots organizing.

If a student coalition can force a university president to resign over campus climate, imagine if they used that same leverage to force the university to make payments in lieu of taxes to underfunded city schools. Imagine if they demanded that the university use its land trust to build affordable housing for the displaced, or barred campus police from coordinating with local law enforcement.

Kelley is right that trauma is real, and the alienation Black students feel on campus is painful. But healing will not come from forcing a neoliberal institution to accommodate that pain. Healing comes through solidarity with the broader struggle. The true undercommons is not hiding in the margins of the university; it is out here on the blocks, in the neighborhoods, and in the streets. We are waiting for the students to join us.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The transition of activism from the streets to the campus shifted the focus from Black survival and state violence to insular academic concerns like syllabi and building names.
  2. 02Campus-centric demands for safe spaces and diversity initiatives do nothing to address the immediate crises of gentrification, evictions, and mass incarceration facing neighboring communities.
  3. 03The 'undercommons' model remains an exclusive, privileged space that traps radical intellectual energy within the academy rather than directing it toward grassroots organizing.
  4. 04Student activists should stop seeking institutional belonging and instead leverage university resources—such as funding and legal clinics—to support local community struggles.
  5. 05True healing and political efficacy for Black students come from solidarity with the broader struggle outside the university gates rather than trying to make neoliberal institutions accommodate their trauma.
