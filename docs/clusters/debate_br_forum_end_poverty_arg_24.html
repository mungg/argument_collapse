**Toyama Is Too Pessimistic About the Mobile Phone Revolution**

Kentaro Toyama writes as a chastened insider, and there is real value in his testimony from fifty telecenters across South Asia and Africa. But I think his essay commits a category error: it treats mobile phones as if they were just the next iteration of the failed telecenter model, when in fact mobile phones are doing something fundamentally different in poor communities, and the evidence on this point is already strong enough to justify the enthusiasm Toyama dismisses as a "cult."

Start with the numbers Toyama himself cites: 4.5 billion mobile accounts, 80 percent of the global population within range of a tower. These are not the numbers of a failed intervention. They are the numbers of one of the most rapid, voluntary technology adoptions in human history—an adoption driven by poor people themselves, paying their own money, often at considerable sacrifice. This is the opposite of the telecenter pattern, where outsiders pushed PCs into villages that had no organic demand for them. Mobile phones are pull, not push.

That distinction matters because Toyama's magnifier thesis is strongest when applied to push interventions. If outsiders impose a technology that no one was asking for, of course its effects will depend entirely on the local capacity to absorb it. But when poor people across continents and cultures spontaneously sacrifice food and water budgets for talk time—and yes, Diga's Uganda research does show this—the most natural interpretation is not that they are making bad choices but that the value of connectivity to them is enormous. We should be wary of paternalism here. The same people who decide that rural Ugandans are wasting money on phones rarely apply the same scrutiny to American teenagers' phone bills.

Jensen's Kerala fisheries study is the canonical example: mobile phones eliminated price dispersion across fish markets, raised fisher profits, lowered consumer prices, and reduced waste—all without any complementary capacity-building, training program, or institutional intervention. Aker's work on Niger grain markets shows similar effects. These are not magnifying-pre-existing-capacity stories. These are stories about how a simple, well-matched technology can directly improve material welfare for people with very limited formal education, no organizational backing, and no NGO holding their hand.

Toyama would say: but these are information-inefficiency gains, narrow in scope, and they don't add up to "ending poverty." Fair enough. No single intervention ends poverty. But Toyama's framing demands an impossibly high bar from technology that he doesn't apply to other interventions. Has microfinance ended poverty? Has primary education? Has democracy? These too have produced narrow, contingent, often disappointing results. Yet we rightly continue to invest in them. Why should mobile phones be held to a different standard?

There is also a generational issue Toyama underplays. Telecenters failed in part because adults in rural India had no prior relationship with digital interfaces and weren't going to develop one in middle age. But mobile phones are being adopted by everyone, including children who will spend their entire lives with these devices. The cumulative effect on rural literacy, on women's autonomy (phones provide privacy from family surveillance, a fact well-documented in South Asian fieldwork), on remittance flows from migrant workers, on weather information for smallholder farmers—these effects compound over decades. Judging them from a 2010 vantage point is like judging television from 1955.

Toyama's strongest move is the opportunity-cost argument: dollars spent on technology are dollars not spent on deworming or sanitation. But this assumes a zero-sum budget, which is rarely accurate for mobile phone infrastructure. Cellular networks are built by private telecom companies extracting profit from voluntary users, not by aid budgets crowding out public health. The Indian government did not divert primary-school funding to build cell towers; Bharti Airtel and its competitors did, because rural Indians were willing to pay. The opportunity-cost critique applies powerfully to OLPC and telecenters, which are donor-funded; it applies weakly to organic mobile phone adoption.

Where I do agree with Toyama: the "mHealth" and "mobile money" rhetoric coming from development agencies is often overheated and decoupled from evidence. M-Pesa in Kenya is genuinely impressive, but most mHealth pilots have not been rigorously evaluated, and the field is rife with the same scale-fetishism that infected telecenters. The discipline of randomized evaluation, championed by Banerjee, Duflo, and the J-PAL crowd, needs to be applied just as harshly to mobile interventions as to anything else.

But the underlying technology—voice and SMS communication, available cheaply to almost everyone—is doing real work that goes beyond what Toyama's magnifier thesis predicts. It is doing work even for people with limited capacity, because it serves a need (communication with distant family, employers, markets) that maps onto universal human capabilities. Not all technologies are PCs.

Toyama ends by asking, "if the spread of mobile phones is sufficient to help end global poverty, we will know soon enough. But if it doesn't, should we then pin our hopes on the next new shiny gadget?" This is a false dichotomy. The honest answer is that mobile phones, like vaccines and roads, will be one important contributor to a multi-front fight against poverty, neither a silver bullet nor a sideshow. The right posture is neither cult nor cynicism, but careful empirical assessment of which applications actually deliver and which don't. On that score, the verdict on basic mobile communications is already in, and it is positive.

  2. 02Empirical evidence from market studies shows that mobile phones can improve economic welfare by reducing information inefficiencies without requiring additional training or capacity-building.
  3. 03The opportunity-cost argument against technology is less applicable to mobile phones because their infrastructure is largely funded by private investment rather than diverted aid budgets.
  4. 04Mobile phones empower marginalized groups, such as women and migrant workers, through privacy and improved remittance flows in ways that compound over generations.
