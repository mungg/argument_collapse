"While David Grusky correctly identifies that market institutions must be reformed rather than just taxed, his proposal is insufficient because it fails to address the structural power imbalances and the inherently unequal nature of profit-driven capitalism."

David Grusky’s call to look beyond the tax code and repair the market itself is a welcome push against the shallow stopgap thinking that dominates the current inequality discussion. But his institutional critique, for all its analytical clarity, stops far too soon. Grusky identifies two key sources of rent—educational bottlenecks and cozy CEO pay-setting—and prescribes a liberal-egalitarian fix: equalize educational opportunity and clean up corporate governance, and we’ll get a fair competitive market that produces legitimate, lower inequality. The problem is not that his diagnosis is wrong, but that it is dangerously incomplete. The deeper forms of rent he ignores, and the very nature of a competitive market itself, ensure that inequality would remain explosive even if his reforms were fully realized. If we truly wish to take on the institutional critique OWS voices, we must push past the mirage of “cleaned-up” capitalism and transform the economic institutions that generate inequality from the ground up.

Begin with education. Grusky is right that the college wage premium is inflated by artificial supply constraints—poverty, underfunded schools, and elite university rationing. Equalizing school funding, expanding college slots, and making them affordable would indeed narrow that premium. But note what this does not change: it still leaves an economy in which a small number of highly credentialed workers command vastly disproportionate pay, while everyone else competes for a shrunken middle. The very existence of a “college premium” is a symptom of an economy that assigns a few people to the winner-take-all design and production of goods, while countless others are relegated to precarious service work. Even if everyone had a fair shot at those top slots, the hierarchy itself would remain. In fact, an intensified meritocratic scramble might only increase output and the absolute difference between the winners and the losers. A truly anti-inequality institutional critique must question the division of labor itself: who gets to do creative, autonomous work, and who is consigned to routinized, supervised drudgery? That is not a bottleneck problem; it’s a question of the ownership and governance of firms. Without worker representation on boards, sectoral wage-setting, and interventions that compress the entire wage structure—like a much higher minimum wage and strong unions—market repair will simply produce a slightly fairer pre-tax distribution that remains massively unequal.

The same limitation haunts Grusky’s CEO pay example. He deftly shows that boards packed with the CEO’s friends generate rent. But he then frames the solution narrowly: remove the opportunity for rent-seeking by changing pay-setting practices, perhaps through shareholder empowerment or independent compensation committees. Yet even if we could perfectly link CEO pay to contribution, in a globalized economy that contribution is measured by a stock price that already reflects enormous power imbalances. When a CEO closes a plant, extracts patent rents, or crushes a union, the resulting profit spike boosts her “contribution,” and thus her performance-based pay. Sanitizing the pay-setting process does nothing to challenge the underlying business strategies that produce extreme inequality between those at the top of the corporate pyramid and everyone else. If we want to reduce executive pay meaningfully, we must change who controls the corporation and what its goals are. That means, at a minimum, strong co-determination laws that give workers real power over strategic decisions, and a tax code that penalizes extreme pay ratios rather than simply skimming a bit off the top post hoc. Otherwise, institutional repair is just corporate governance tweaking that leaves the rent-generating machine intact.

Grusky’s core insight is that inequality results from rigged rules, not from abstract market forces. But he then pulls back and assumes that once the most egregious corruption is removed—eliminating sweetheart deals and rationing—the remaining inequality will be the benign outcome of genuine contribution. This is wishful thinking. Any profit-driven market economy that permits private ownership of productive assets will produce huge concentrations of income and wealth, because capital income itself is a form of rent extracted from the collective effort of workers and the public infrastructure that makes production possible. The boundaries Grusky draws between “licit” and “illicit” income are fuzzy: is a patent monopoly that charges exorbitant drug prices a market distortion or a legitimate incentive for innovation? Is a financial firm’s profit from high-frequency trading a product of skill or of a rigged system of co-location and preferential data access? In each case, the state actively creates the property rights that generate inequality. There is no natural, competition-only baseline to restore; we must choose what kind of inequality-producing rules we want.

If we follow the institutional critique to its logical conclusion, we arrive not at Grusky’s repaired competition but at a deeper democratization of economic life. That means public ownership or strong regulation of natural monopolies, a universal basic income that decommodifies labor and strengthens the bargaining position of the worst-off, and a global tax regime that prevents capital from playing nations off each other. It means recognizing that even “fair” competition in a market system will produce hierarchies that erode equality of dignity and political voice. Occupy Wall Street’s polyphony contained these strains, and they deserve a hearing now. Grusky’s essay is an important half-step. If we want to do something about inequality, we won’t just patch the market; we’ll change it.
