"Robert Pollin’s proposal to achieve full employment through redirected public spending is based on flawed economic assumptions that ignore the complexities of modern fiscal multipliers, monetary policy, and globalized markets."

Robert Pollin has written an ambitious essay that tries to salvage Keynesian economics from the wreckage of the Great Recession. While his diagnosis of unemployment and his critique of neoliberalism contain valuable insights, his policy prescriptions rest on shaky empirical and theoretical foundations.

The core of Pollin's argument rests on the claim that full employment can be achieved through fiscal stimulus and redirected spending. He offers the calculation that moving $330 billion from military and fossil fuel spending to education and clean energy would create 4.8 million jobs and reduce unemployment to 3.9 percent. This calculation, while not implausible, ignores several important feedback effects.

First, the employment effects of public spending depend critically on the fiscal multiplier, which varies significantly depending on the state of the economy, the composition of spending, and the response of monetary policy. Pollin's figures assume a uniform multiplier across sectors, but empirical evidence suggests substantial variation. Education spending may have high direct employment effects, but clean energy investments often involve capital-intensive technologies with lower employment multipliers.

Second, Pollin's analysis treats the Federal Reserve as a passive actor. In practice, any significant fiscal expansion would likely trigger a monetary response. If the Fed raises interest rates to combat inflation, the net employment effect could be much smaller than Pollin suggests. The Great Inflation of the 1970s, which Pollin dismisses as caused primarily by oil shocks, was in fact the product of an interaction between fiscal and monetary policy that policymakers failed to manage.

Third, the international dimension is largely absent from Pollin's analysis. The United States cannot pursue a full employment policy in isolation. If fiscal expansion pushes up wages and prices, the resulting deterioration in competitiveness could lead to a depreciation of the dollar or an increase in imports, offsetting the domestic employment gains. Pollin dismisses trade deficit concerns, but his argument that currency skirmishes would produce "little benefit" for workers assumes a level of international cooperation that current political conditions cannot support.

The Keynesian framework that Pollin advocates was designed for a closed economy with limited international capital mobility. The world of 2011 is radically different. Unless full employment policy is coordinated internationally, it faces inherent limitations.

This does not mean that fiscal stimulus and targeted public investment are useless. They can and should be used to mitigate unemployment and support recovery. But the goal of full employment as Pollin defines it—zero involuntary unemployment with rising wages—may be unachievable in a globalized economy without international coordination that appears politically infeasible.

A more pragmatic approach would focus on reducing unemployment to socially acceptable levels while maintaining macroeconomic stability. This might mean accepting unemployment rates of 5-6 percent as the best achievable outcome, rather than pursuing a target that history suggests is unattainable.

  1. 01Pollin’s job-creation calculations are unreliable because they assume uniform multipliers across sectors and fail to account for the capital-intensive nature of clean energy investments.

  3. 03A domestic full-employment policy is unsustainable in a globalized economy without international coordination, as rising domestic costs would undermine trade competitiveness.
  4. 04The goal of zero involuntary unemployment is an unrealistic target in the current political and economic climate, making a more modest unemployment rate of 5-6 percent a more pragmatic policy objective.
