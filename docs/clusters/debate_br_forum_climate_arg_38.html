"The proposed 'two-step' strategy of seizing state power to defeat fossil capital is fundamentally flawed because the state’s structural imperatives are inextricably linked to extraction, displacement, and systemic violence."

Olúfẹ́mi O. Táíwò’s “Climate, State, and Utopia” presents a compelling, if deeply flawed, blueprint for planetary survival. By advocating a “two-step” strategy—first seizing state power to dethrone fossil capital, then eventually transforming the world to achieve broader social justice—Táíwò offers a seductive pragmatism. He acknowledges the violent, colonial, and surveillance-heavy realities of the modern nation-state, yet insists that only the state possesses the scale and capacity to orchestrate a rapid energy transition. We must, in his view, swallow our anarchist or abolitionist pride, harness the Leviathan to crush ExxonMobil, and sort out our utopian visions later.

But in his rush to leverage the state’s “creative” capacities, Táíwò misapprehends the fundamental nature of state power and its inextricable historical entanglement with extraction. A strategy that relies on the state to manage the climate crisis does not simply risk failure; it guarantees the replication of the very systemic violences Táíwò hopes to eventually dismantle in his elusive “step two.” The state cannot be repurposed as an engine of ecological harmony because its structural imperatives—legibility, centralization, territorial control, and endless material throughput—are inherently hostile to both local self-determination and ecological limits.

Táíwò is right to point out that fossil capital operates with immense impunity, and he correctly identifies the danger of the state acting merely as a handmaiden to asset managers like BlackRock. But his alternative—a robust, public-led, state-managed renewable energy transition—fails to account for what that transition entails materially. A "green state," whether capitalist or nominally socialist, requires staggering amounts of critical minerals: lithium, cobalt, nickel, copper, and rare earth elements. To build the public renewables Táíwò champions, the state must secure supply chains for these materials. How does a powerful, centralized state secure resources? Historically and presently, it does so through displacement, expropriation, and the creation of sacrifice zones.

Already, we see the contours of this "green colonialism" taking shape. In the name of national energy security and the climate crisis, states are greenlighting lithium extraction in the salt flats of the Andean Triangle, threatening Indigenous livelihoods and fragile water ecosystems. In Nevada, the Thacker Pass lithium mine, fast-tracked by the very U.S. state machinery Táíwò wishes to capture, overrides the sovereignty and sacred sites of the Paiute and Shoshone peoples. A state empowered to aggressively build a renewable energy grid will inevitably invoke the "greater good" to steamroll frontline communities. The logic of the state is not altered simply by changing its energy source from carbon to lithium; the logic remains one of centralized accumulation at the expense of the periphery.

Furthermore, Táíwò casually dismisses the potency of movements that operate outside the state, such as the Zapatistas in Chiapas or the autonomous structures in Rojava. He asks how such abdication fits into a trajectory that ends the reign of fossil capital, arguing that the emissions of Exxon and the Pentagon poison the air in autonomous zones, too. This critique fundamentally misunderstands the theory of change embedded in these movements. The goal of autonomy is not simply to hide from the climate crisis, but to actively blockade and dismantle the infrastructural nodes of extractive capitalism while prefiguring a regenerative alternative.

The most effective climate victories of the past decade have not come from progressive state managers or public power mandates, but from mass direct action and indigenous-led blockades. The resistance at Standing Rock, the Wet'suwet'en blockades against the Coastal GasLink pipeline, and the decentralized sabotage of extractive infrastructure have delayed or cancelled billions of tons of greenhouse gas emissions. These movements do not seek to capture the state; they seek to render its extractive projects ungovernable. By creating zones of friction, they impose unbearable costs on fossil capital. Táíwò’s two-step model threatens to absorb these vital, disruptive movements into a bureaucratic, state-centric reformism that ultimately pacifies resistance.

We must also critically examine the temporal deferral built into the two-step strategy. Táíwò suggests we can postpone the abolition of borders, the eradication of racism, and the dismantling of the surveillance state until after we have secured a green energy transition. But the climate crisis is already being managed through the violent mechanisms of border imperialism and racialized policing. As climate-induced migration increases, the state’s primary response has not been solidarity, but the fortification of borders—the very razor wire Táíwò mentions. If we empower the state to manage the climate emergency without simultaneously dismantling its carceral and militarized apparatuses, we will inevitably arrive at "eco-fascism" or, at best, a green fortress world. The state will use its mandate to transition the economy as a pretext to further surveil, police, and manage populations deemed surplus or threatening.

Táíwò’s analogy to historical stagism—the idea that a bourgeois revolution must precede a socialist one—is telling, but not in the way he intends. The tragedy of twentieth-century state socialism was precisely its belief that the centralized power of the state could be used as a neutral tool for emancipation. Instead, the state became an end in itself, demanding the same alienation of labor and the same ecological destruction as its capitalist rivals in the name of catching up. If we repeat this error in the twenty-first century, sacrificing community autonomy and intersectional justice for the sake of an expeditious, state-led energy transition, we will find ourselves trapped in a Green Leviathan.

There is no “step two” if step one entrenches the very mechanisms of our domination. The alternative is not naive localism, but a globally networked movement of ungovernability and mutual aid—scaling up our capacities to block extractive flows while building decentralized, community-owned, and ecologically embedded ways of living. We must not save the state to save the climate; we must dismantle the state-capital nexus to save ourselves.

  5. 05The state and capital are so deeply intertwined that the state cannot be repurposed as a neutral tool for ecological harmony; it must be dismantled alongside the capitalist system.
