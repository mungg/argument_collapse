"The crisis of representative democracy is primarily caused by the expansion of the administrative state and the delegation of legislative power to the executive branch, rather than just external factors like money or a decline in civic virtue."

Ira Katznelson’s defense of representative democracy is welcome, especially at a moment when frustration with Congress encourages rule by executive order, court decree, and administrative improvisation. But his diagnosis understates one of the central causes of our democratic malaise: the enormous expansion of national administrative government. The legislature is weak not simply because money is strong or civic virtue has declined. It is weak because Congress has delegated vast authority to agencies and executives, then reorganized itself around blame avoidance rather than lawmaking.

This is not a new conservative complaint dressed up as democratic theory. It is a constitutional concern rooted in the same tradition Katznelson invokes. Locke warned that the legislative power cannot be transferred elsewhere. The American Constitution rests on a similar assumption: lawmaking should be difficult, public, and accountable. Yet much of modern governance takes place through rules written by agencies, guidance letters, prosecutorial discretion, waivers, consent decrees, and judicially supervised settlements. Congress often writes broad statutes, leaves decisive questions unresolved, and then condemns bureaucrats for answering them.

The Affordable Care Act illustrates the problem. Whatever one thinks of its goals, it is a sprawling statute whose operation depends on thousands of administrative judgments. Immigration policy offers another example. Congress has failed for years to legislate a durable settlement, while presidents of both parties have stretched discretion over enforcement. War powers provide still another. Since September 11, presidents have claimed broad authority to use force, conduct surveillance, and target enemies under authorizations that Congress has been reluctant to revisit. Legislative paralysis and executive expansion feed each other.

Katznelson rightly worries about citizens turning away from legislatures toward executives and courts. But he does not sufficiently ask why citizens do so. One reason is that national government promises more than representative institutions can responsibly deliver. As Washington assumes responsibility for health care, education standards, retirement security, environmental regulation, labor rules, financial supervision, criminal justice incentives, housing, transportation, and countless other matters, the stakes of national politics become existential. Every election feels like a struggle to control the whole social order. Polarization is intensified by centralization.

Federalism is therefore not an antiquarian remedy. It is a democratic technology. In a large, diverse republic, not every question should be nationalized. States and localities can deliberate, experiment, and reflect different judgments. Citizens can more easily monitor governments closer to them. Policy diversity allows losers in one jurisdiction to seek change in another without turning every disagreement into a national crisis. Of course federalism has an ugly history; states’ rights were invoked to defend slavery and segregation. But the abuse of a principle does not abolish its democratic value. Federalism disciplined by constitutional rights is one answer to the problem of scale.

The lead essay also treats money in politics as a principal distortion, especially after Citizens United. Campaign finance deserves scrutiny, but it is too simple to imagine that democracy would revive if only wealthy donors were less influential. Money flows to politics because politics controls so much. When government can confer subsidies, exemptions, mandates, contracts, licenses, and regulatory advantages, organized interests will invest in influencing it. Limiting contributions may reduce some corruption, but reducing the discretionary power available for purchase is equally important.

Nor should we romanticize “non-convertibility” between economic and political power. In a free society, citizens will use the resources they possess—time, money, expertise, reputation, association—to persuade others. Unions, environmental groups, churches, corporations, universities, and civil rights organizations all convert social resources into political influence. The democratic question is not whether influence can be equalized perfectly. It cannot. The question is whether government protects basic liberties, maintains fair electoral procedures, prevents bribery, and leaves enough space outside politics for citizens to live without asking permission from the state.

Katznelson’s admiration for the New Deal is understandable but partial. The New Deal demonstrated democratic capacity, but it also accelerated the transfer of power to the executive branch and administrative agencies. The postwar liberal state produced real achievements, yet it also habituated citizens to looking to Washington for comprehensive solutions. When those solutions disappoint, the response is not humility but demand for stronger executive action. The “imperial presidency” and the modern regulatory state are not deviations from the New Deal tradition; they are among its legacies.

A conservative reconstruction of democracy would begin by restoring Congress’s responsibility. Legislators should write clearer laws, reclaim war powers, subject major regulations to explicit approval, and stop using agencies as shields for controversial choices. The budget process should be made intelligible. Emergency legislating and omnibus bills should become rare. The Senate and House should debate more, not less, and members should be forced to vote on hard questions rather than posture for cable news.

But reconstruction should also mean constitutional modesty. The national legislature need not solve every social problem. A Congress that attempts everything will deliberate badly about everything. A Congress that confines itself to genuine national responsibilities may recover seriousness. Representative democracy requires more than participation; it requires limits. Without them, politics becomes a permanent struggle for command over an overloaded state.

Katznelson is right that democracy cannot do without representation. I would add that representation cannot flourish without constitutional boundaries. The scaffolding around the Capitol will come down when the dome is repaired. The scaffolding of self-government—separation of powers, federalism, enumerated authority, and legislative accountability—must remain.

"The crisis of representative democracy is primarily a crisis of political economy, where extreme capitalist inequality and the power of mobile capital have hollowed out formal citizenship and rendered the principle of political equality a fiction."

Ira Katznelson asks whether representative democracy can regain moral legitimacy and practical capacity. The answer depends on whether we are willing to confront the economic order that has hollowed it out. Democracy is not merely suffering from bad procedures, excessive polarization, or civic impatience. It is suffering because capitalism has become too unequal, too mobile, and too politically powerful for formal citizenship to control.

The essay’s most important concept is Claus Offe’s principle of non-convertibility: unequal economic assets should not become unequal political influence. But in the United States today that principle is less a norm under strain than a fiction. Wealth buys campaign access, lobbying power, expert knowledge, media reach, legal endurance, and agenda control. It shapes what is thinkable before voters are asked to choose. Citizens may possess equal votes, but they do not possess equal capacity to define alternatives.

This distortion long predates Citizens United, though the decision worsened it. The deeper transformation began with the weakening of organized labor, the liberalization of finance, the rise of shareholder capitalism, the globalization of production, and the growing dependence of both parties on affluent donors. The result is a democracy in which the preferences of the wealthy are heard continuously and those of working people episodically. Political scientists have recently shown what many citizens already sense: policy outcomes respond far more reliably to the affluent and organized interests than to average voters.

Katznelson celebrates the New Deal because it proved that democratic institutions could govern capitalism. That is the right lesson. But the New Deal was not just a legislative achievement. It was a class compromise made possible by mass unemployment, labor insurgency, left pressure, and fear among elites that capitalism itself might lose legitimacy. Social Security, labor rights, public works, banking regulation, and progressive taxation did not emerge from procedural reverence. They emerged because democratic politics briefly gained enough power to discipline capital.

The postwar democratic settlement rested on institutions that gave ordinary citizens countervailing power: unions, mass parties, locally rooted associations, public universities, regulated finance, and relatively high taxation of top incomes. Many of those institutions have been weakened or dismantled. Union density in the private sector has collapsed. Firms threaten exit when communities demand too much. Trade agreements protect investors more effectively than workers. The financial sector can wreck the economy and then emerge larger. Under these conditions, asking legislatures to deliberate better is necessary but insufficient. They deliberate in the shadow of capital strike.

A democracy that cannot shape investment cannot govern its future. Climate change makes this painfully clear. The scientific case for action is strong, and majorities often favor some response, yet fossil fuel interests can delay, dilute, and confuse. The same pattern appears in financial reform, drug pricing, taxation, and labor law. Legislatures are not simply failing to find common ground; they are operating in a political economy designed to make certain interests veto players.

This is why the anxiety of democracy is also an anxiety of distribution. Extreme inequality is not only unfair in outcome; it is anti-democratic in process. A billionaire’s preferences should not weigh thousands of times more than a nurse’s or warehouse worker’s. Yet the ordinary workings of our campaign, lobbying, and media systems produce something close to that imbalance. Even philanthropy, often admirable in motive, can become a private substitute for public priority-setting. When wealthy individuals decide which schools, diseases, museums, or reforms deserve resources, they exercise quasi-public power without democratic accountability.

What would reconstruction require? First, labor law must be rebuilt so workers can organize without intimidation. Democracy should not stop at the workplace door. If people spend much of their lives under private authority, then unions, works councils, and sectoral bargaining are not merely economic policies; they are democratic institutions. Second, campaign finance reform must be linked to public financing, small-donor matching, and limits on dark money. Third, taxation should reduce plutocratic power, not only raise revenue. Fourth, finance must be made boring again: smaller, more regulated, less able to blackmail the public with systemic risk.

Fifth, we need social rights that make citizenship meaningful: health care, education, housing security, retirement, and a decent income floor. A person who is terrified of losing a job, a home, or medical care is formally free but politically vulnerable. Economic insecurity narrows participation. It turns citizens into clients, supplicants, or spectators. Social democracy expands the material basis for political equality.

Katznelson warns against substituting populism for representation, and the warning is justified when populism becomes leader worship or hostility to pluralism. But a democratic left populism is not the enemy of representative democracy; it may be the condition for its renewal. Without organized pressure from below, legislatures will continue to respond to those already inside the room. The point is not to abolish representation but to make representatives fear ordinary citizens more than donors.

The old social-democratic compromise is under stress everywhere, especially in Europe under austerity and in the United States under polarization. Yet the alternative is not a neutral liberal democracy. It is oligarchic liberalism: rights for all in principle, influence for the few in practice, insecurity for many as a fact of life. Such a regime may hold elections indefinitely, but it will not command deep loyalty.

Democracy needs reconstruction, yes. But the scaffolding must reach beyond the Capitol dome. It must surround the corporation, the bank, the workplace, and the market itself. Political democracy cannot be secured while economic power remains autocratic.

"The crisis of modern democracy stems from a territorial mismatch where national legislatures are held accountable for global forces—such as capital flight, climate change, and migration—that exceed their jurisdictional reach."

Ira Katznelson’s essay is framed around national representative institutions, above all legislatures. That focus is understandable: democracy has historically been organized through the nation-state, and parliaments remain the chief sites of legitimate lawmaking. But one reason legislatures now appear weak is that many of the forces shaping citizens’ lives no longer operate primarily within national borders. Democracy’s anxiety is not only institutional. It is territorial.

The lead essay notes this concern but does not make it central enough. Citizens suspect that national borders no longer define a zone of security or control. They are right. Capital moves faster than tax authorities. Carbon emissions disregard jurisdiction. Migrants flee wars, poverty, and climate stress produced partly by global systems. Terror networks operate transnationally. Supply chains diffuse responsibility. European states negotiate domestic budgets under pressure from markets, Brussels, Frankfurt, and Berlin. Trade agreements constrain regulation. Even the most competent legislature faces problems whose causes and solutions exceed its reach.

This does not mean national democracy is obsolete. It means national democracy is overburdened. Voters hold representatives accountable for wage stagnation, deindustrialization, migration pressures, financial crises, and environmental risks that representatives can influence only partially. The resulting gap between responsibility and capacity breeds cynicism. Politicians promise control they cannot deliver; citizens conclude that politics is fraudulent.

The eurozone crisis is the clearest current example. Greece has held elections, changed governments, and voted in a referendum, yet its policy space remains sharply limited by creditors, European institutions, and the architecture of the euro. One may fault Greek governments for corruption, tax evasion, and fiscal irresponsibility, and still see the democratic problem. If voters cannot choose a meaningfully different economic path without risking financial collapse and expulsion from the currency union, then formal democracy becomes constrained by technocratic and market imperatives. The same tension, though less dramatic, affects Spain, Portugal, Ireland, and even France.

The European Union was created partly to tame nationalism and bind democracies together through law. It has achieved much. But its democratic deficit is not a slogan invented by extremists. The European Parliament has gained power, yet many crucial decisions are made through councils, commissions, central bankers, and negotiations remote from ordinary citizens. National politicians then blame “Europe” for constraints they helped create. This dynamic feeds parties of the radical right and left, from the National Front to Syriza to Podemos to UKIP. Their ideologies differ, but they draw energy from the perception that democracy has migrated away from the voter.

Globalization produces a related challenge in the United States. Congress can debate trade agreements, but once supply chains and investor expectations are entrenched, communities have limited recourse. A factory closure is experienced locally, justified globally, and addressed politically through promises of retraining that often ring hollow. Immigration debates become proxies for wider anxieties about sovereignty, labor competition, and cultural change. Climate policy is delayed because unilateral action seems costly unless others act too. National representatives are blamed for failing to master interdependence.

The answer cannot be simple withdrawal. Autarky is neither feasible nor desirable. Nationalist fantasies of sealed borders and restored economic independence are likely to produce poorer, meaner societies. But neither can democrats accept a world in which economic integration outruns political accountability. If markets are global and democracy remains national, markets will often win.

We need a multi-level democratic imagination. Some powers should be restored to national legislatures where international agreements have become too insulated from revision. Trade deals should be more transparent and more open to labor, environmental, and consumer interests, not merely corporate advisers. Investor-state dispute settlement deserves skepticism when it allows private tribunals to challenge public regulation. Central banks, while needing independence from short-term electoral manipulation, must be subject to clearer democratic mandates and oversight.

At the same time, some problems require stronger transnational democratic institutions. Climate change cannot be solved by national legislatures acting alone. Financial regulation requires coordination to prevent races to the bottom. Tax avoidance by multinational corporations demands international cooperation. Refugee policy cannot be humane if every state tries to shift burdens elsewhere. The democratic question is how to make such cooperation accountable rather than technocratic.

This is difficult because there is no global demos comparable to a national citizenry. Solidarity thins across borders; languages differ; media systems remain national; redistribution is politically hard even within states. But democracy has always been an institutional achievement, not a natural fact. National peoples were made through schools, parties, armies, newspapers, and social policy. Transnational publics may be weaker, but they can be strengthened through parliamentary networks, transparent negotiations, civil society participation, and cross-border party cooperation.

Katznelson’s defense of representation remains essential. The danger is that citizens, feeling dispossessed by global forces, will embrace illiberal leaders promising national rebirth. Viktor Orbán’s “illiberal state” is one answer to globalization: defend the nation by weakening liberal constraints. Similar temptations exist elsewhere. But liberal democrats need more than warnings. They need to show that democracy can regain control without retreating into xenophobia.

The Capitol dome can be repaired in Washington, but democratic capacity must also be repaired in Brussels, Athens, Beijing, Geneva, and the treaty rooms where rules are written. Representation must follow power. If consequential decisions are transnational, democratic accountability must become transnational too. Otherwise national elections will increasingly become rituals of frustration, and frustration will seek harsher remedies.

**The Forgotten International Dimension**

Katznelson briefly notes that "national borders no longer define a zone of security" and that anxiety about transnational institutions and global processes is part of what ails contemporary democracies. He then drops the thread. This is unfortunate, because the international dimension is arguably the most important variable he names—and it changes the diagnosis significantly.

Begin with the obvious. The advanced democracies became democratic in roughly their current form during a particular historical moment, roughly 1945 to 1975, in which several conditions held: capital was relatively immobile, currencies were pegged or managed, trade was substantial but bounded, immigration was modest, and the security framework was provided largely by American military power and bilateral alliances. Within this framework, national legislatures could in fact set economic policy: they could tax capital because capital could not easily flee; they could set labor standards because the alternative production sites were limited; they could regulate finance because financial institutions were largely domestic. The "embedded liberalism" of the postwar order, as John Ruggie called it, was the political-economic foundation on which postwar democratic legitimacy rested.

Almost every term in that description has changed. Capital is now mobile at the speed of light. Floating exchange rates and integrated capital markets mean that any national government attempting unilateral redistribution faces immediate capital flight and currency pressure. Production chains are global; labor standards become a competitiveness liability. Finance is transnational, regulated through international bodies and treaties that are themselves opaque to most citizens. Migration, both authorized and unauthorized, is at historic levels in many democracies. And the climate problem—perhaps the supreme test of contemporary governance—is irreducibly global.

In this context, the diagnosis of "legislative dysfunction" looks different. Many of the things citizens most want their legislatures to do—stabilize wages, control immigration, regulate finance, prevent climate catastrophe—are no longer fully within national legislative power. The legislatures are dysfunctional partly in the literal sense: they have lost function. Their nominal sovereignty has been hollowed by globalization, while no commensurate democratic structure has emerged at the supranational level. The European Union is the most ambitious experiment in democratic transnationalism, and its current legitimacy crisis—the Greek bailout, the refugee influx—suggests how hard the problem is.

This means that some of what Katznelson takes for populist temptation is in fact a quite rational response to a real deficit. When voters elect populist parties promising to "take back control," they are responding to the accurate perception that the legislatures they elect cannot, in current arrangements, deliver on the matters that matter most. Whether the populist promise of withdrawal can actually deliver is another question—the answer is usually no—but the underlying complaint is well-founded.

It also means that Katznelson's prescription of recommitting to representative legislatures is, on its own, insufficient. A more robust national legislature in a context of transnational economic flows can do more about distribution and regulation than a hollowed one, but it cannot do enough. The honest reformist agenda must include the construction of democratic accountability at transnational levels—a project of staggering difficulty, but one that the urgency of climate change alone makes unavoidable.

Here Katznelson's historical analogy with the New Deal becomes complicated. The New Deal succeeded partly because the United States, even in the 1930s, was a continental economy with a powerful federal state that could regulate the national market it largely contained. The analog today would be regulation at a level commensurate with the global market: something the world does not have and cannot easily build. The historical comparison can mislead us by suggesting that another vigorous burst of national lawmaking could restore democratic legitimacy. The structural situation is genuinely different.

I do not mean to counsel despair. There are openings. Climate diplomacy, the Paris meetings later this year, may yet produce something. International tax cooperation around base erosion and profit shifting is advancing in the OECD. Regional democratic structures may evolve. But any honest reckoning with democratic anxiety must place the international dimension at the center, not at the periphery, of the diagnosis.

Katznelson's essay is rich in the political theory of nation-state democracy from Locke to Linz. The anxiety he describes, however, is precisely the anxiety of a nation-state democratic order whose preconditions have eroded. The theoretical resources of the past are necessary but not sufficient. We need new theory, and new institutions, adequate to the scale of the problems our existing institutions can no longer reach.

"While political representation is indispensable to democracy, its survival depends on reforming legislative institutions and revitalizing the mediating civic organizations that prevent economic power from dominating the political process."

Ira Katznelson is right to warn against the fantasy that democracy can be rescued by bypassing representation. The impatience is understandable. In the United States, Congress has become a byword for futility: shutdowns, debt-ceiling brinkmanship, routine filibusters, gerrymandered House districts, and a campaign finance system in which billionaires can plausibly imagine themselves as parties of one. But the conclusion that legislatures are obsolete is exactly wrong. If legislatures fail, the answer is not to enthrone courts, executives, experts, or “the people” in some unmediated form. It is to rebuild the institutions that make popular conflict politically usable.

Still, Katznelson’s defense of representation risks sounding too much like a defense of legislatures as they are. The central problem is not simply that citizens have lost patience with the dignity of lawmaking. It is that many citizens have good reason to doubt that lawmaking is actually representative. When the Supreme Court disables key campaign finance restraints in Citizens United and McCutcheon, when Shelby County weakens the Voting Rights Act, when state governments experiment with voter ID laws and restrictions on early voting, and when legislative districts are drawn to protect incumbents from meaningful competition, skepticism is not anti-institutional romanticism. It is an empirical judgment.

The principle Katznelson borrows from Claus Offe—the non-convertibility of economic power into political power—is the crux. But it cannot be secured by reverence for legislatures alone. It requires countervailing organizations: unions, parties, civic associations, local newspapers, churches, neighborhood groups, and social movements capable of translating diffuse interests into public claims. Mid-century American democracy did not work merely because Congress was more decorous. It worked because organized labor, civil rights groups, farm organizations, party machines, and voluntary associations connected citizens to governing institutions. Those organizations were often exclusionary and corrupt; they also gave ordinary people leverage.
