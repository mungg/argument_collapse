"Surrogacy regulation should prioritize the interests and rights of the children produced through these arrangements rather than focusing solely on the desires of the adults involved."

Much of the surrogacy debate focuses on the adults involved—the intended parents who want children and the surrogates who carry them. But we should redirect attention to a group whose interests are often overlooked: the children themselves.

Children born through surrogacy have a stake in clear legal parentage from birth. Too often, the legal status of these children remains uncertain for weeks or months, creating anxiety for intended parents and potential vulnerability for the child. The right of a child to know their family situation should not depend on navigating a legal grey area.

Beyond parentage, there are identity concerns that merit serious attention. Surrogacy, like adoption, creates questions about origin and belonging that shape a child's development. Research on adopted children consistently shows that access to information about biological heritage matters for identity formation. Children conceived through donated gametes or gestated by surrogates deserve similar consideration. At minimum, this means ensuring that records are maintained in ways that allow individuals, once they reach adulthood, to understand their biological origins if they wish.

The international dimension compounds these concerns. When children are born in one country to a surrogate from another, they may carry citizenship or immigration complications that follow them for years. A child should not start life as a legal ambiguity because their parents wanted them badly enough to engage a surrogate abroad.

Regulation, from this perspective, should be child-centered: ensuring clear legal recognition, maintaining records for future reference, and minimizing the circumstances in which children begin their lives in legal limbo. The adults' desires matter, but the child's interests should come first.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Children born through surrogacy require immediate and clear legal parentage from birth to avoid vulnerability and legal uncertainty.
  2. 02Provisions must be made to ensure children have access to information regarding their biological heritage and origins to support healthy identity formation.
  3. 03International surrogacy regulations must address and resolve potential citizenship and immigration complications to prevent children from living in legal limbo.
  4. 04Record-keeping systems should be mandated to allow individuals to understand their genetic and gestational history once they reach adulthood.
