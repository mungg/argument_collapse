Diversified Claude

"The debate over Afghanistan strategy is fundamentally flawed because it treats the conflict as an internal Afghan issue rather than a regional problem centered on Pakistan's strategic interests."

Nir Rosen's essay is unusually honest about what we are seeing, but it leaves the most important word in the whole debate underdeveloped. The word is "Pakistan."

Almost every paragraph of Rosen's piece points across an unmentioned border. The young man arrested for the Taliban ringtone is on his way back from a madrassa in Peshawar. The "foreign fighters" the soldiers blame for accurate shooting are not Helmandis. The insurgency's leadership cadre, by every serious account, sits in Quetta and the tribal agencies, not in Afghanistan. Al Qaeda, as Rosen himself notes, is "already ensconced in Pakistan, where it is better protected from the United States than it would be in Afghanistan." The Haqqani network operates out of North Waziristan. The Pakistani state, or at least significant elements of it, has spent thirty years cultivating exactly the militant networks the United States is now spending lives and dollars to suppress on the wrong side of the Durand Line.

If you take this seriously, the debate between COIN and counterterrorism in Afghanistan starts to look like a debate over which leak to plug in a boat whose hull is rusted through somewhere else entirely.

I do not say this to mock either Rosen or McChrystal. Rosen is right that the McChrystal plan rests on an inflated and somewhat magical theory of what a centralized Afghan state can accomplish. McChrystal, to be fair, was tasked with making recommendations about Afghanistan, not Pakistan, and his report could not honestly address the regional problem without straying outside its remit. But the consequence is that the entire American policy is now built around a question — can we secure a functional Afghan state? — that is not the operationally decisive question. The operationally decisive question is: can we change the calculations of the Pakistani military and intelligence services, so that they stop hedging on the Afghan Taliban as a strategic asset against India?

This is a much harder problem than anything McChrystal has been asked to solve, and it is one that more troops in Helmand cannot touch. The Inter-Services Intelligence Directorate's relationship with the Quetta Shura was forged during the anti-Soviet jihad and reinforced by decades of strategic depth doctrine. The Pakistani army's vision of a friendly buffer state in Afghanistan is incompatible with anything resembling the Karzai government, which has cultivated India, signed trade agreements with New Delhi, and welcomed Indian consulates along the eastern frontier. So long as Rawalpindi calculates that the United States will eventually leave and India will remain, the rational play for the Pakistani security state is to keep the Taliban networks alive and intact, and to fight just enough of the Pakistani Taliban to keep Washington's aid flowing.

Rosen's reporting from Highway 601 inadvertently captures the logical endpoint of this dynamic. Team Prowler can clear the road. The marines can flood Helmand. The militants disperse, cross over, refit in Pakistani sanctuary, and return. The trickle of foreign fighters Dyer's men encounter is not a side note; it is the entire ballgame. We are not fighting an indigenous insurgency that draws its strength from Helmandi grievance. We are fighting an indigenous insurgency that draws its strength from Helmandi grievance and from a permissive and in places actively supportive cross-border ecosystem. Counterinsurgency theory, even in its best Petraeus-Galula form, has very little to say about the second half of that sentence.

This is why I think both Rosen and the McChrystal camp are arguing past each other. Rosen wants us to ask: can we build the Afghanistan we want? The honest answer, given the regional realities, is probably no. The McChrystal camp wants us to ask: what would it take to deny the Taliban victory inside Afghanistan? The answer, also given the regional realities, is: more than we are willing to commit, for longer than we can stomach, and even then only contingently.

The right question is the one neither side asks loudly enough: what mix of pressure and inducement, on which Pakistani institutions, might shift the strategic calculus in Rawalpindi enough to dry up the sanctuary? Drone strikes in the tribal agencies are one tool, and a brutal one. Military aid conditionality is another. Civilian aid like the Kerry-Lugar bill is a third. Quiet diplomatic work to lower the temperature between India and Pakistan over Kashmir would do more for Afghan stability than three more brigades in Kandahar. None of this is glamorous, none of it produces the kind of clean military narrative McChrystal's team prefers, and none of it offers the moral clarity of Rosen's call for withdrawal.

I share Rosen's suspicion of the COIN cult of personality. The hagiographies of Petraeus and McChrystal have crossed into the embarrassing, and the Washington celebrity-pundit ecosystem he skewers genuinely does substitute access for analysis. I share his impatience with the magical thinking about doubling the Afghan National Police while the existing force fails its drug tests. And I share his anger at the casual brutality his reporting documents — the kid kicked in the chest, the prisoner sent to Lashkar Gah on the basis of a ringtone.

But the conclusion Rosen reaches — that we should essentially walk away because al Qaeda was always a manageable threat and the Taliban are not really interested in global jihad — does not solve the Pakistan problem. It worsens it. A precipitous American withdrawal would be read in Rawalpindi as vindication of the hedge, and would deliver to the ISI's clients exactly the strategic depth they have sought for thirty years. The likely consequence is not a return to the pre-2001 status quo, which was bad enough; it is a strengthened jihadi infrastructure operating against an Indian neighbor that now possesses nuclear weapons, with all the escalation risks that implies.

The American policy mistake is not that we are doing counterinsurgency in Afghanistan rather than counterterrorism. The mistake is that we have framed the entire problem as an Afghan problem when it is, and always has been, an Af-Pak problem — and a regional South Asian problem beyond that. Rosen has written a fine essay about the wrong country. The most important debate is the one we have barely begun to have.
