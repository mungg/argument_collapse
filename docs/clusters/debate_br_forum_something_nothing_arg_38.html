Diversified Gemini

"The U.S. escalation in Afghanistan is a violent, neo-colonial occupation driven by imperial hubris that must be ended through immediate and unconditional withdrawal."

Nir Rosen’s “Something from Nothing” is a devastating, ground-level exposure of the imperial hubris driving the latest escalation in Afghanistan. By stripping away the sterile, academic jargon of Counterinsurgency (COIN) and showing us the reality of scared cops, arrogant American sergeants, and bewildered Afghan farmers, Rosen reveals the entire enterprise for what it is: a violent, neo-colonial occupation that manufactures the very enemies it claims to be fighting. Yet, even as Rosen dismantles the tactical failures of the military, he stops short of naming the true root of the disaster: the bipartisan consensus of American empire.

As of January 2010, the "good war" in Afghanistan has been unmasked. Barack Obama, elected on a wave of anti-war sentiment, has wholly adopted the military-industrial complex’s logic, committing tens of thousands of new troops to a country that has been bleeding for decades. The tragedy of Obama’s West Point speech is not just that he endorsed General McChrystal’s unworkable strategy; it is that he validated the imperial premise that the United States has the right, the wisdom, and the capability to violently re-engineer societies in the Global South.

Rosen is brilliant in his deconstruction of COIN. He rightly notes that COIN is treated by the Washington establishment as a magical incantation. The Petraeus-authored field manual has become a sacred text, allowing pseudo-intellectuals in think tanks to rebrand military occupation as armed social work. They talk of "protecting the population" while dropping bombs from unmanned drones that incinerate wedding parties. They speak of "capacity building" while empowering drug lords and death squads in the form of the Afghan National Police.

The vignette of Captain Westby negotiating with the local elders over a blocked water pipe is the perfect metaphor for the absurdity of U.S. intervention. The British military destroys the vital infrastructure of a farming community to protect its own convoys, and then the Americans expect gratitude when they promise to *maybe* fix it in a week. This is the reality of COIN: creating misery and then demanding that the occupied population thank the occupier for a band-aid.

Rosen accurately diagnoses that the U.S. military is fundamentally incapable of acting as a political party or a humanitarian NGO. Soldiers are trained to close with and destroy the enemy. When you place heavily armed, frightened young men from the American Midwest into rural Pashtun villages, the result will always be violence, alienation, and abuse. The cultural training the military touts is little more than learning not to show the soles of your feet; it cannot bridge the chasm of power dynamics inherent in an armed occupation.

However, where Rosen’s critique needs expansion is his treatment of the motives behind the war. He writes that "Afghanistan should have been easy" and laments that the U.S. "restored many brutal warlords." This assumes that the primary goal of the United States was actually democratic state-building. History suggests otherwise.

The U.S. state does not blunder into alliances with warlords out of innocent incompetence. From Vietnam to Central America to Iraq, the U.S. has consistently allied with the most reactionary, brutal elements of local societies to suppress popular, independent movements. In Afghanistan, the CIA empowered the warlords of the Northern Alliance to overthrow the Taliban cheaply in 2001. We installed Hamid Karzai not because he was a democratic visionary, but because he was a compliant client. The corruption that Rosen laments is not a failure of U.S. policy; it is the *product* of U.S. policy. Corruption is the mechanism by which imperial clients maintain control over a fractured society on behalf of their patrons.

Furthermore, Rosen touches briefly on al-Qaeda but needs to press harder on how the specter of terrorism is used to justify endless war. As he notes, the Taliban are not a global jihadist threat, and al-Qaeda is practically non-existent in Afghanistan. Yet, the Obama administration invokes 9/11 continuously to justify a massive troop surge. This is the "war on terror" operating as an ideological control mechanism. It provides a blank check for defense contractors, justifies the expansion of the surveillance state at home, and maintains a strategic footprint in Central Asia, sandwiched between Iran, China, and Russia.

The anti-imperialist left warned in 2001 that the invasion of Afghanistan would lead to a quagmire, that you cannot bring justice from the bomb bay of a B-52. We were told we were naive, that Afghanistan would be a quick, clean war of liberation. Eight years later, the architects of this disaster—many of whom advised McChrystal—are still treated as serious experts on Sunday morning talk shows.

Rosen’s essay is a vital document because it proves that there is no "smart" way to fight a colonial war. You cannot train an indigenous police force to violently suppress its own people on behalf of a foreign power and expect it to be clean and efficient. You cannot drop explosives on villages and expect the survivors to embrace your puppet government.

The solution is not a better COIN manual. The solution is not more troops, or different rules of engagement, or better PR. The solution is the immediate, unconditional withdrawal of all foreign military forces. Only when the boot of American empire is lifted from the neck of the Afghan people will they have the space to determine their own destiny. It will be a difficult, likely violent process, but it is the only path to genuine sovereignty. The U.S. cannot fix Afghanistan, because the U.S. presence is the primary driver of the war.
