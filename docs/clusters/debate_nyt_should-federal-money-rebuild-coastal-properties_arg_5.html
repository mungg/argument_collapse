"The federal government should not implement a blanket refusal to rebuild coastal properties but should instead adopt a selective national adaptation policy that funds reconstruction based on economic necessity and public benefit."

Before embracing a national retreat from the coast, we should ask whether the proposed cure is too broad. “Coastal property” includes Manhattan subway tunnels, New Jersey barrier towns, Gulf Coast refineries, Navy facilities, ports, public beaches, wastewater plants and modest year-round homes. A blanket refusal to rebuild would be economically reckless and politically impossible.

The coast is where much of the nation’s commerce occurs. Ports move goods, coastal cities generate enormous tax revenue, and shore communities support tourism and fishing. Some places are worth defending, even at high cost. The Dutch have understood for centuries that living with water requires engineering, maintenance and public investment. The United States cannot simply declare its Atlantic and Gulf shorelines disposable.

Moreover, federal disaster spending often repairs public goods: roads, bridges, schools, transit, hospitals and utilities. If Washington withdraws, poorer localities will be left with ruined infrastructure and declining tax bases, while wealthy enclaves self-insure and rebuild privately. That would not reduce coastal risk so much as redistribute it downward.

The better approach is a national coastal adaptation policy. We should identify which areas are critical and defensible, which can be made safer through elevation and natural buffers, and which should be gradually converted to open space. Federal money should support large-scale protection where the benefits justify the costs. In dense places, that may mean surge barriers, seawalls, pumping systems and hardened electrical networks. In less developed areas, it may mean dunes, wetlands and buyouts.

This requires planning, not post-storm improvisation. We should update flood maps, price insurance more honestly, enforce building codes and prevent new construction in the most exposed areas. But we should not confuse prudence with abandonment. The fact that storms are dangerous does not mean every coastal investment is foolish.

Federal money should rebuild coastal properties when rebuilding serves a broader public purpose and reduces future vulnerability. It should not rebuild everything everywhere. The challenge is discrimination, not withdrawal.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A total withdrawal of federal support would be economically reckless because coastal areas are vital hubs for national commerce, tax revenue, tourism, and fishing.
  2. 02Withdrawing federal disaster spending would disproportionately harm poorer localities that rely on public infrastructure, while wealthy enclaves would simply rebuild privately.
  3. 03Strategic engineering and public investment, modeled after successful international examples like the Dutch, can effectively protect critical coastal assets from water-related risks.
  4. 04Federal funding should be prioritized for areas where the benefits of protection justify the costs, utilizing a mix of hard infrastructure, natural buffers, and managed buyouts.
  5. 05Effective coastal management requires proactive planning, such as updating flood maps and enforcing building codes, rather than reactive post-storm improvisation.
