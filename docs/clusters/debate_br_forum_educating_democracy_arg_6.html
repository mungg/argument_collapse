"While Deborah Meier’s vision of small, democratic school communities is admirable, a truly just educational reform must balance local autonomy with external standards to ensure that poor and marginalized children are neither abandoned by local complacency nor crushed by rigid, high-stakes testing."

I read Deborah Meier’s essay with admiration and unease. Admiration, because she describes the kind of school every parent wants: small enough that adults know your child, serious enough that work matters, humane enough that discipline does not become disposal. Unease, because many parents in poor urban neighborhoods have heard beautiful language about community before, while their children came home unable to read.

The debate over standards often sounds different depending on where one sits. For governors, standards are a management tool. For educators like Meier, they are a threat to democratic judgment. For many parents, they are both a warning and a promise. The warning is that our children may be judged by tests that ignore the conditions of their lives. The promise is that someone, somewhere, will finally say publicly whether the school is teaching them what they need to know.

Parents do not need experts to tell them that high-stakes tests can be unfair. We know children who freeze on exams, children who are learning English, children who missed school because of asthma or family crisis, children whose schools spent months without a regular teacher. To deny such a child a diploma on the basis of one state test is not justice. It is bureaucracy wearing a stern face.

But parents also know the unfairness of false promotion. A child is told he is doing fine. He receives passing grades. Then suddenly, in high school or community college or a job application, he discovers that he cannot write a coherent paragraph or handle fractions. The system’s kindness was counterfeit. It spared adults discomfort and left the child with the bill.

That is why I cannot simply join the anti-standards side. The old local system did not work democratically for everyone. In many cities, school boards were political machines. Parents who complained were labeled troublemakers. Middle-class parents could move, organize, or call lawyers. Poor parents were asked to attend meetings where decisions had already been made. If “local control” means control by the same insiders, it is not democracy.

Meier’s version of local control is different. Her schools are small, transparent, and chosen by families. Teachers meet with parents. Student work is public. Adults argue about standards. That is attractive. But the question is how to make it available to children whose parents work nights, do not speak English fluently, distrust school officials, or lack time to attend monthly suppers. A democratic school must not depend on a heroic level of parent participation. It must welcome participation, but also protect the child whose parent cannot be present.

The best part of Meier’s argument is her insistence that children need strong relationships with adults. In large urban schools, anonymity is a daily injury. A child can be absent for days before anyone calls. A teacher can see so many students that grading becomes triage. A counselor can be responsible for hundreds. Under those conditions, “standards” arrive as one more demand from outside, not as a shared commitment. Smaller schools, advisory systems, looping, and stable teaching teams would do more for many children than another round of test preparation.

Her warning about exclusion is also crucial. When test scores become the public measure of worth, some children become problems to be managed rather than students to be taught. Suspensions rise. Special education categories become hiding places. Transfers are encouraged. Dropouts are quietly accepted. The school improves by losing the child. Any accountability system that does not track who disappears is an invitation to abuse.

Still, Meier’s essay sometimes speaks as if the main enemy is external authority. For poor families, external authority can also be protection. Federal courts, civil rights laws, state monitors, and outside advocates have often been necessary because local authorities failed. A parent whose child is denied services, pushed out, or warehoused in a weak program may need an appeal beyond the school community. Democracy is not only face-to-face deliberation; it is also enforceable rights.

So the issue is not whether authority should be local or central. It is how power can be arranged so that children are neither abandoned by local complacency nor crushed by distant rules.

A fair system would include several commitments.

First, no high-stakes decision about a child should rest on one test. Promotion and graduation should consider classroom work, teacher judgment, portfolios, attendance, and opportunities to learn. If a state exam is used, students should have multiple chances and alternative ways to demonstrate competence. The goal should be to find out what a student knows, not to catch him failing.

Second, public reporting should include more than scores. Parents need to know class sizes, teacher qualifications, staff turnover, course offerings, safety, suspensions, expulsions, dropout rates, college placement, and resources. A school with low scores and few resources should not be shamed in the same way as a school with every advantage and poor instruction. Information should illuminate responsibility, not obscure it.

Third, standards should be understandable to families. Thick state documents written for specialists do not empower parents. Parents need examples: this is what a strong fourth-grade essay looks like; this is the mathematics your child should be able to explain; this is how we judge a science project; this is what reading on grade level means. Meier’s portfolio approach can help because it makes quality visible. A test score says “below proficient.” A portfolio conference can show why.

Fourth, schools should be small enough that someone is responsible for every child. If a city cannot immediately replace large high schools, it can create houses, academies, advisories, or teams. Each student should have an adult advocate who knows the family and follows the child’s progress. Accountability begins with a name.

Fifth, resources must be part of the standards debate. It is offensive to demand equal performance from unequal schools while calling the result accountability. Children need libraries, nurses, counselors, safe buildings, arts, science materials, and experienced teachers. They also need health care, stable housing, and safe streets, though schools cannot provide all of that alone. If the state sets standards, the state must accept responsibility for the conditions necessary to meet them.

Sixth, discipline must be educational, not merely exclusionary. The Chicago story with which Meier begins should trouble everyone. Safe schools may be necessary for some students, and no parent wants classrooms made unteachable by violence. But expulsion cannot become the main reform strategy. If children sent away do not show up, the public should ask where they are. A child outside school is not a solved problem.

The standards movement speaks often of “high expectations.” Parents want high expectations. But high expectations are not the same as high cut scores. A high expectation is an adult saying, “I know you can do better, and I will help you get there.” It includes teaching, time, feedback, and persistence. A cut score without support is merely a gate.

Meier’s democratic schools offer a vision of high expectations rooted in relationship. Standards advocates offer a demand that public schools stop hiding failure. Both are needed. The danger is that each side will ignore the truth in the other. If we adopt Meier’s language without public evidence, some children will continue to be passed through weak schools in the name of local judgment. If we adopt the testing agenda without Meier’s humanity, some children will be labeled failures and pushed out in the name of rigor.

Parents should refuse that choice. We should demand schools where adults know our children and where the quality of learning is visible. We should demand common expectations and multiple ways to meet them. We should demand tests used as tools, not weapons. We should demand local voice and outside protection. We should demand that no reform be judged successful if it raises scores by losing children.

Democracy in education cannot mean trusting officials, whether local or state, to do the right thing without scrutiny. It must mean giving families real power, children real support, teachers real responsibility, and the public real evidence. Deborah Meier has reminded us that schools should be communities of judgment and care. The standards movement has reminded us that care without evidence can betray children. A just reform would hold both truths at once.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Standardized testing provides a necessary promise of public accountability and transparency for parents whose children have historically been failed by 'counterfeit kindness' and social promotion.
  2. 02Local control can be exclusionary or dominated by political machines, meaning external authority and enforceable rights are often necessary to protect the most vulnerable students.
  3. 03A democratic school model should not rely on heroic levels of parental participation, as this disadvantages families who lack the time or resources to be constantly present.
  4. 04High-stakes decisions about a child's future should never be based on a single test score but should instead incorporate teacher judgment, portfolios, and multiple opportunities to demonstrate competence.
  5. 05Accountability systems must track 'who disappears' from the system to prevent schools from artificially raising scores by pushing out struggling students through suspension or expulsion.
  6. 06True standards require a state commitment to equitable resources, as it is offensive to demand equal performance from schools with vastly unequal funding and facilities.
