"While local school autonomy is valuable, a healthy democracy requires a common intellectual foundation and a shared curriculum to ensure civic cohesion and true educational equity."

Deborah Meier has offered us a powerful vision of schools as democratic communities in which adults and young people together negotiate the meaning of a good education. I share her deep concern about the shrinking of local authority and the alienation of students from the adult world. But her remedy—that each school community should largely invent its own standards in dialogue with its immediate constituency—rests on a misunderstanding of what it means to be educated in a large, pluralistic democracy. Meier fails to recognize that a democratic citizenry cannot be sustained without a common intellectual foundation, and that ensuring such a foundation is the legitimate business of the broader public, not an affront to local wisdom.

Consider the kind of public discourse that is possible when citizens share no common reference points. If one high school graduate has spent four years studying the history of East Harlem and ancient Egypt while another has studied the Pacific Rim and the European Enlightenment, they may both have developed admirable habits of mind. But when they meet in a town hall or a jury room and the conversation turns to the constitutional balance of powers, the legacy of slavery, or the scientific basis of an environmental proposal, they will have no shared vocabulary or narrative to draw on. Democracy is not merely a set of procedural habits—openness, tolerance, a willingness to reconsider one’s beliefs. It also requires a substantive body of knowledge: the common cultural literacy without which citizens cannot deliberate together on equal terms. E.D. Hirsch has been making this point for over a decade, and it is a shame his work is so often caricatured by progressives as a mere list of facts. His *Cultural Literacy* and *The Schools We Need* demonstrate that the ability to read a serious newspaper, to understand political argument, and to participate in civic life depends on a broad, shared schema of knowledge that the schools have a responsibility to transmit.

Meier’s own school, by her account, does not avoid this transmission: her students study ancient Egypt, ancient China, and Boston history. But what about the vast universe of knowledge she does not cover? And what happens when the Mission Hill of one neighborhood decides that European history is irrelevant while another decides that African civilization is all that matters? Meier says it is desirable to acknowledge a plurality of definitions of “well-educated.” But if that plurality becomes so extreme that we literally cannot talk to one another, then the centrifugal forces that are already pulling this country apart racially and economically will only accelerate. A democracy requires a centripetal curriculum, a core of common learning that intentionally bridges our differences.

The fear that such a core must descend into the sort of trivial, overstuffed frameworks Massachusetts has produced is understandable but not inevitable. A national or state curriculum can be lean, coherent, and intellectually serious. It can specify, for example, that all students should be able to write a cogent argument, understand the basic structure of the federal government, read a variety of literary genres, and know the broad arc of American history and its central documents. The details of which texts and which projects remain the province of teachers and local schools. This is what the best international systems—in France, Japan, and Singapore—manage to achieve: a strong national framework that leaves ample room for professional judgment about pedagogy and adaptation to local conditions. The United States, with our extreme localism, has produced a de facto national curriculum anyway, but it is the accidental and often mediocre curriculum of the mass-market textbook, as Meier herself notes. Replacing that hidden curriculum with a deliberate, democratically debated common curriculum is a gain for local control, not a loss.

Meier’s six alternative assumptions, which place authority in the hands of citizens and make wide room for pluralism, sound appealing but underestimate the danger of cognitive provincialism. She quotes Mill on the value of heresy, and I agree. But the ability to be a heretic—to challenge the mainstream—presupposes knowledge of what the mainstream is. If every community invents its own mainstream, then the heretic has nothing to push against, and the wider society’s orthodoxies go utterly unchallenged in countless isolated schoolhouses. The student who has never encountered the Federalist Papers because her local committee thought them “elite” will be unequipped to critique or even understand the arguments of those who govern her.

Equity, the great goal Meier and I both share, also demands a common curriculum. The children of the wealthy will always acquire, through their families, travel, and private lessons, the cultural knowledge that unlocks academic and professional success. The only way to break that inherited advantage is to ensure that public schools deliberately teach that knowledge to everyone. Leaving the definition of “what’s important” to each local community, as Meier advocates, is a formula for ensuring that the children of the powerful get one curriculum and the children of the powerless get another. The history of decentralized education in this country is, as I noted, a history of radically unequal access to powerful knowledge. A common curriculum, by contrast, is a statement of collective faith that every child, regardless of zip code, deserves access to the same intellectual heritage. Meier says the primary national responsibility is to narrow the resource gap. I say one of the most valuable resources we can ensure is a shared, rigorous curriculum, and that cannot be accomplished by leaving each school to its own devices.

Finally, Meier’s insistence that external standards destroy relationships is a non sequitur. A national curriculum does not walk into a classroom and tell a teacher what to say on Tuesday morning. It is a map, and a good map allows a traveler to navigate the terrain with confidence while still choosing her own path. The teachers at Mission Hill who designed their curriculum around ancient Egypt would, I suspect, have welcomed a clear and thoughtful national framework that situated Egypt in the larger story of human civilization and connected it to later history. Such a framework would have deepened, not diminished, their work. The argument, at bottom, is not between standards and no standards, but between an explicit, public, and democratically accountable set of standards and the invisible, market-driven, arbitrary standards that prevail when we pretend that each school can and should invent the world anew.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A shared vocabulary and substantive body of knowledge are necessary for citizens to deliberate together on equal terms in a pluralistic society.
  2. 02Extreme curricular plurality risks accelerating social fragmentation by leaving citizens without common reference points for public discourse.
  3. 03A lean and intellectually serious national or state framework can provide necessary structure while still leaving room for local professional judgment regarding pedagogy.
  4. 04Meaningful dissent and heresy require students to first understand the mainstream cultural and political orthodoxies they might wish to challenge.
  5. 05Universal access to a rigorous common curriculum is essential for equity, as it prevents the children of the powerless from being denied the cultural capital that the wealthy acquire privately.
  6. 06External standards and a national curriculum act as a supportive map for educators rather than a force that inherently destroys the personal relationships within a school.
