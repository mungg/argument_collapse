"While citizen action is a vital component of national defense, a responsible aviation security policy must be a comprehensive, layered system of professional protocols rather than a model that relies primarily on untrained civilians making impossible choices."

The most valuable part of Elaine Scarry’s essay is her insistence that we examine what actually happened in the air on September 11. The least persuasive part is her conclusion that the contrast between Flight 77 and Flight 93 demonstrates the superiority of an “egalitarian” model of national defense. As a way of honoring courage, the argument is stirring. As a guide to aviation security, it is incomplete.

Aviation is a system. Its safety depends on routines, redundancies, training, equipment, communication, and assumptions. September 11 was devastating in part because the attackers understood the assumptions. For decades, the standard hijacking scenario was that hijackers wanted transportation, publicity, prisoners released, or political concessions. Crews were trained to keep passengers calm and get the aircraft on the ground. Passengers generally believed compliance increased the chance of survival. Cockpit doors were not built to withstand determined assault. Box cutters were not treated as strategic weapons. Transponders and radios were assumed to be tools of navigation, not signals whose disappearance might indicate an aircraft had become a missile.

The attackers turned those assumptions inside out. Once we recognize that fact, the policy question becomes: how do we redesign the system so that no single failure becomes catastrophic? Scarry answers: trust the citizens inside the plane. I answer: include them, but do not make them the plan.

The passengers and crew of Flight 93 acted with extraordinary intelligence. They collected information from calls, compared notes, understood that the old hijacking script no longer applied, and attacked. Their action almost certainly saved lives on the ground. The passengers and crew who subdued Richard Reid in December likewise showed the importance of alert people inside the cabin. In any future attempt, passengers will not be as passive as many were trained to be before September 11. That change in expectations is itself a major security improvement.

But passenger resistance is unpredictable. It depends on the number and physical capacity of passengers, the weapons and tactics of hijackers, the availability of information, the time before impact, the location of the aircraft, and the ability to coordinate under terror. It may succeed; it may fail; it may cause a crash that might otherwise have been avoided. No responsible security architecture should rely primarily on untrained civilians making impossible choices at altitude.

The first priority must be prevention before boarding. Screening failed on September 11 not because every screener was negligent, but because the prohibited-items regime and threat model were inadequate. We need better passenger and baggage screening, but also smarter screening. That does not mean harassing every traveler equally or treating airport inconvenience as proof of seriousness. It means identifying weapons, explosives, forged documents, suspicious travel patterns, and gaps in airport perimeter security. It also means federal standards, better training, better pay for screeners, and accountability. If the newly created Transportation Security Administration is to be more than a bureaucracy, it must professionalize a job that had too often been treated as low-wage contract work.

The second priority is cockpit security. The cockpit must be made extremely difficult to enter by force. Reinforced doors, revised access procedures, and pilot training change the geometry of hijacking. If terrorists cannot reach the controls, they cannot turn the plane into a guided weapon. This measure is more reliable than asking passengers to retake a cockpit after it has been seized. It is “internal” to the aircraft, as Scarry would say, but it is not a town meeting. It is engineering and procedure.

The third priority is communication. Here Scarry is especially right. Information saved lives on Flight 93. Passengers learned that other planes had been used as weapons, and that knowledge transformed their choices. Crews need secure ways to alert the ground quickly. Air traffic controllers need clear protocols for communicating anomalies. Airlines, FAA facilities, NORAD, and military command centers must share information without delay. Passengers may also need access to communication, and any proposal to commandeer cellular networks for officials must be examined carefully. Emergency communications cannot be designed on the assumption that citizens are merely clogging the lines. Sometimes they are the source of the decisive information.

The fourth priority is command clarity. Before September 11, there was no well-practiced domestic air defense system for suicide hijackings. Scrambling fighters is not like calling a taxi. Someone must identify the aircraft, locate it, determine intent, assign interceptors, communicate with pilots, and define rules of engagement. On that day, even if fighters had reached Flight 77, what then? Rock wings? Fire warning shots? Shoot down a civilian airliner? Scarry is right that the consent problem is terrible. But because it is terrible, it must be addressed before the moment comes. The country needs a publicly accountable legal framework for interception and, in the last extremity, shootdown authority.

That framework cannot require passenger consent. Imagine a cargo aircraft packed with explosives, or a passenger jet whose cabin has already been gassed, or a flight in which no one knows the destination. The government cannot wait for a vote from the doomed. But neither can it leave the decision to improvisation. Authority should be civilian, high-level, recorded when possible, and subject to later review. The evidentiary standard should be demanding: confirmed hijacking, failure to respond, hostile trajectory, exhaustion of alternatives, and imminent threat to a target whose destruction would likely kill many. There is no way to make such a decision clean. There is a way to make it lawful.

Scarry’s criticism of “speed” in defense policy is a needed rebuke to overconfidence. But in aviation, speed truly matters. A plane descending toward Washington or New York gives defenders minutes, not hours. The answer is not to abandon rapid response as authoritarian. The answer is to push detection and decision earlier in the chain. The sooner suspicious behavior is identified, the less likely anyone must decide whether to shoot down an aircraft. Prevention is humane because it avoids tragic choices.

I also worry that Scarry’s language of “the Pentagon could not defend the Pentagon” implies that the building’s vulnerability is a symbol of national failure. In one sense it is. In another, no open society can make every target invulnerable. The Pentagon is a massive office building near a major airport. The World Trade Center was a symbol and a workplace. Nuclear plants, bridges, tunnels, refineries, stadiums, and city centers cannot all be defended by fighters circling overhead. Security must be risk-based. It must reduce vulnerability without turning civil society into a fortress.

Aviation security after September 11 should therefore follow a layered model: intelligence to detect plots; visa and watch-list improvements to identify dangerous travelers; airport screening to keep weapons and explosives off planes; cockpit hardening to deny control; trained crews to manage cabin threats; alert passengers as a final reserve; air marshals on selected flights; and military interception protocols for the rare case in which all else fails. No layer is perfect. Together they make success harder for attackers.

Where Scarry’s larger political argument intersects with aviation is in the treatment of citizens. The public should not be lied to for its own good. Passengers should not be trained into helplessness. Crews should not be given obsolete scripts. Families on the ground should not be dismissed as irrelevant. Citizens are part of the system because they see, hear, call, report, decide, and sometimes resist. But they are one part of a system, not a substitute for it.

The passengers of Flight 93 did what citizens may be called to do when institutions fail or when danger penetrates every barrier. Our obligation is to learn from them without romanticizing the condition that made their action necessary. A good aviation-security policy would make room for courage while trying, relentlessly, to ensure that such courage is not required.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Passenger resistance is too unpredictable and dependent on specific variables to serve as the primary foundation for a national security architecture.
  2. 02The first priority of defense should be professionalized prevention, including improved federal screening standards and intelligence to stop threats before boarding.
  3. 03Hardening cockpits through engineering and revised procedures is a more reliable internal defense than asking passengers to retake a seized aircraft.
  4. 04Effective defense requires clear, high-level civilian command protocols and a legal framework for military interception that cannot wait for passenger consent in an emergency.
  5. 05Information sharing must be improved across all agencies so that detection and decisions occur earlier in the chain, reducing the need for tragic last-resort actions.
  6. 06Citizens should be treated as an informed part of the security system rather than being trained into helplessness, but they cannot be a substitute for institutional safeguards.
