"While the 'density tax' and constitutional biases against metropolitan coalitions are real, Democrats must move beyond viewing themselves as structural victims and focus on building long-term institutional power and local trust in non-metropolitan areas."

Hacker and Pierson’s essay is strongest as a corrective to a stale story about Democrats. The party has not simply become a boutique vehicle for affluent social liberals. Its recent economic agenda was broad, expensive, redistributive, and in several respects genuinely innovative. The authors are also right that America’s constitutional structure punishes the party’s metropolitan coalition. The Senate, Electoral College, single-member districts, and gerrymandering all make it possible for Republicans to wield power without majority support.

Yet there is a risk in making the “density tax” too central to the explanation. It can encourage Democrats to think of themselves as victims of structure rather than as political actors responsible for building power across the actual terrain of American institutions. The constitutional system is unfair. It is also the system in which any durable governing coalition must operate. Complaining about anti-metro bias is not enough.

The Democratic predicament is not only that its voters are geographically concentrated. It is that the party has allowed many non-metropolitan institutions to decay. State parties in much of the country have been hollowed out. Local newspapers have collapsed. Unions have weakened. Churches, civic associations, and fraternal organizations no longer connect the party to many small towns and rural areas. Candidate recruitment is thin in places deemed unwinnable. Campaign spending floods competitive suburbs every two years while long-term organizing in red regions remains episodic. A party cannot overcome geographic disadvantage if it treats large parts of the map as a messaging problem rather than an institutional problem.

The authors note that Democrats in Congress sought to redistribute resources to “left behind” regions whose representatives often opposed the spending. That is true and important. But voters in those regions do not experience federal policy as a spreadsheet showing net fiscal transfers. They experience politics through identity, local leadership, media, trust, and visible institutions. A broadband grant or factory tax credit may not help Democrats if it is announced by a Republican governor, filtered through conservative media, and built by private firms with little Democratic presence on the ground. Policy does not automatically build power. Organized interpretation builds power.

This is one reason the New Deal analogy is often misleading. The New Deal did not create Democratic loyalties simply by mailing checks. It operated in a world of powerful unions, urban machines, ethnic organizations, farm groups, local brokers, and mass media with different structures from today’s fragmented environment. Benefits were interpreted through institutions that connected voters to the party. Today, a family may receive an ACA subsidy, a child tax credit payment, and infrastructure-funded improvements while still hearing daily that Democrats are destroying America. Without trusted intermediaries, material benefits can be politically orphaned.

The density tax is real, but it should prompt a fifty-state organizing strategy, not fatalism. The Senate overrepresents smaller states; therefore Democrats need stronger organizations in smaller states. The Electoral College rewards competitiveness across states; therefore Democrats need to contest more terrain earlier and more consistently. State legislatures draw districts; therefore Democrats need to care about county commissions, secretary of state races, judicial elections, and state legislative benches even where presidential prospects are poor. This sounds obvious, but the party’s resource allocation has often been too short-term and too Washington-centered.

There are examples of what broader investment can accomplish. Democrats have become more competitive in states such as Georgia and Arizona not because demographics magically delivered victories, but because organizers, local leaders, and civic groups spent years registering voters, contesting offices, and building coalitions. Those states also show that “metro” politics can extend into suburbs and exurbs in ways that alter statewide outcomes. But there is no substitute for time. A party cannot parachute into a state in October and expect to overcome decades of distrust.

The democracy stakes make this even more urgent. Hacker and Pierson argue that resistance to authoritarian backsliding now rests almost entirely on Democratic electoral success. As a practical matter, they are largely right: the Republican Party has tolerated or encouraged election denial, attacks on voting rights, intimidation of officials, and an increasingly plebiscitary view of executive power. But it is unhealthy for democracy if one party becomes the sole vessel of constitutional defense while the other is structurally advantaged and radicalizing. Democrats must win elections, but they also need a strategy for rebuilding pro-democracy norms beyond their own coalition.

That requires care in how they describe the conflict. If democracy defense is framed only as the enlightened metro majority against reactionary rural America, it will deepen the very divide that empowers anti-democratic politics. Many voters in low-density areas are not authoritarians; they are embedded in social worlds where Democrats appear distant, contemptuous, or absent. They may dislike Trump’s conduct but distrust Democratic cultural and economic leadership. Winning some of them back, or at least reducing Republican margins, is not optional in a system that gives their states and districts disproportionate influence.

The party’s economic turn can help, but only if it is made tangible. Industrial policy is especially promising here. Clean energy manufacturing, semiconductor plants, infrastructure projects, broadband, abandoned mine land reclamation, and transportation investments can bring visible public action to places that have felt discarded. But Democrats must resist the temptation to talk about these investments only in technocratic or climate terms. In many communities, the relevant message is dignity, work, repair, and national competence. “We are rebuilding the country and your town is part of it” is stronger than “we optimized a tax credit to accelerate decarbonization.”

At the same time, Democrats should not romanticize rural or small-town voters by assuming that economics will dissolve cultural conflict. Guns, religion, immigration, race, energy, education, and national identity matter. A viable strategy must combine economic seriousness with cultural restraint and local credibility. Candidates rooted in their communities will often navigate these issues better than national consultants. A pro-labor Democrat in a small state may not sound like a Brooklyn progressive, and that is fine. A party serious about governing a continental republic needs internal diversity.

Institutional reform must also remain on the agenda. The density tax is not a law of nature. Independent redistricting commissions, voting rights protections, statehood for the District of Columbia and possibly Puerto Rico if its people choose it, Electoral Count Act reforms, court reform debates, campaign finance changes, and filibuster reform all matter. But reform requires power, and power requires winning under current rules. There is no escape hatch from politics.

The authors’ discussion of the 50–50 Senate illustrates the trap. Democrats represented far more people but had no margin for error. Manchin and Sinema could veto most of the agenda. That was structurally unfair, but it was also predictable. In a system with many veto points, a party needs redundancy. It needs 53 or 54 senators, not 50. It needs state legislative strength before redistricting, not lawsuits after maps are drawn. It needs local officials who can administer elections fairly. It needs governors who can veto suppression bills. The lesson of 2021 is not only that Democrats came close; it is that coming close is not good enough.

This may sound harsh, given the genuine obstacles Democrats face. But democratic politics is unforgiving. A party can be morally right about institutional bias and still lose power. It can propose popular policies and still fail to receive credit. It can represent more voters and still fail to govern. The response cannot be simply to document the unfairness. It must be to build organizations capable of surviving it.

Hacker and Pierson end with cautious optimism that a slightly stronger Democratic hand could revive the 2021 agenda. Perhaps. But a “slightly stronger hand” will not appear by demographic gravity. It must be constructed precinct by precinct, state by state, institution by institution. The new blue divide can be managed at the national level for a time, but the deeper challenge is territorial: can a metropolitan party govern a system designed before metropolitan America existed?

The answer depends on whether Democrats become more than the party of dense, productive, diverse regions. They must remain that, but they must also be present in the places where their brand has curdled into caricature. They do not need to win every rural county or small state. They do need to lose them less catastrophically, build local trust, and make public investment visible.

The Constitution may be anti-metro, but it is not anti-organizing. If Democrats want to save democracy and enact a durable economic agenda, they must stop treating geography as destiny. Geography is a constraint. Organization is the answer.
