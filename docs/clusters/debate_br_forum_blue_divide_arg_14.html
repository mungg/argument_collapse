"While the Democratic Party has successfully shifted toward a more ambitious economic agenda, its long-term success depends on moving beyond curated redistribution to confront the internal class privileges and administrative hurdles embedded within its own affluent metropolitan coalition."

Hacker and Pierson are right to insist on a fact too often obscured by both conservative caricature and left melancholy: the Democratic Party of 2021 was not the Democratic Party of 1993. Whatever one thinks of Joe Biden’s political instincts, the agenda his administration and congressional allies put forward marked a real break with the era of deficit fetishism, trade fatalism, welfare retrenchment, and deference to concentrated corporate power. The expanded child tax credit, care economy investments, green industrial policy, antitrust revival, pro-union appointments, and willingness to use public procurement and subsidies to shape markets were not rhetorical flourishes. They were evidence that a large part of the party had learned something from the failures of the Clinton and Obama periods.

But the story is less reassuring than the lead essay suggests. The “new blue divide” has not been overcome; it has been managed, deferred, and in some cases disguised. Democrats have found a form of economic activism that can coexist with affluent metropolitan liberalism, but that coexistence depends on avoiding the hardest questions: who pays, who builds, who gets access to high-opportunity places, and whose consumption patterns must change. The result is a party that has become more ambitious about public spending and industrial policy, but remains uncertain about whether it can confront the forms of private power most embedded in its own coalition.

The easiest part of the new Democratic economics is taxing people richer than most Democratic voters. Biden’s $400,000 pledge was politically shrewd, and Hacker and Pierson are right that American inequality makes it possible to raise serious money from corporations and the very wealthy. But this also reveals the limits of the coalition. The United States cannot build a durable social-democratic state solely on the promise that only someone else will pay for it. Universal social insurance systems in other rich democracies are not financed only by billionaires. They rest on broad tax bases, visible benefits, and a political bargain in which middle- and upper-middle-class citizens contribute because they also receive. Democrats have embraced the benefits side of that bargain but remain afraid of the contribution side.

That fear is understandable. Many of the voters who have moved toward Democrats in the Trump era are socially liberal, college-educated, and alarmed by authoritarianism, but they are not necessarily egalitarian in their day-to-day economic interests. They may support climate investment, abortion rights, gun regulation, and higher taxes on billionaires. They may even support unions in the abstract. Yet they also benefit from high home values, selective schools, exclusionary zoning, professional licensing, and metropolitan labor markets stratified by education. A politics that challenges those advantages will encounter resistance not from caricatured plutocrats alone, but from the upper-middle-class backbone of blue America.

Housing is the clearest case. Hacker and Pierson rightly identify “opportunity hoarding” as a dangerous unresolved conflict, but it deserves even more centrality. If Democrats cannot make expensive metropolitan regions build abundant housing, many of their other commitments will be undermined. Wage gains will be eaten by rents. Climate goals will be harder to reach as people are pushed into longer commutes. Racial and class segregation will persist behind progressive yard signs. Young families will leave high-productivity regions or delay having children. The party can pass subsidies for child care, transit, and clean energy, but if blue jurisdictions cannot permit apartments near jobs and trains, the governing model will look hypocritical and ineffective.

This is why the distinction between national and local politics cannot remain a convenient escape hatch. It is true that zoning is formally local. But national parties shape priorities, rhetoric, incentives, and intergovernmental bargaining. Democrats have become increasingly comfortable criticizing red-state attacks on democracy and civil rights; they should become equally comfortable criticizing blue-state scarcity politics. Governors and state legislatures in places such as California, Massachusetts, New York, Washington, and Colorado matter at least as much to the future of egalitarian metropolitan politics as senators from West Virginia or Arizona. A party of metro America has to govern metro America better.

The lead essay also underplays the geopolitical and macroeconomic forces behind Biden’s turn. The new industrial policy was not only the product of progressive pressure or lessons from the Great Recession. It also reflected a bipartisan reassessment of China, supply chains, and national resilience. COVID-19 exposed the fragility of global production networks; Russia’s invasion of Ukraine reinforced the strategic importance of energy and manufacturing capacity; China’s rise made laissez-faire assumptions about trade and technology look naïve across much of Washington. The CHIPS and Science Act, battery and semiconductor investments, domestic-content rules, and “foreign entity of concern” restrictions cannot be understood merely as redistribution. They are part of a national-development strategy shaped by security competition.

That matters politically because it helps explain why some forms of Democratic economic activism have advanced while others have failed. Industrial subsidies can be sold as jobs, climate policy, and national security at once. They create concentrated beneficiaries: firms, unions, regions, and local officials. Care work, paid leave, and the child tax credit are in some respects more directly redistributive and more transformative of family life, but they lack the same coalition of powerful producers. They also challenge gendered assumptions about private responsibility more directly. It is not an accident that the climate-and-manufacturing parts of Bidenism survived in the Inflation Reduction Act while much of the care agenda did not.

The inflation shock further complicates the “near miss” interpretation. Manchin used inflation as a rationale for shrinking Build Back Better, and one can argue he exaggerated the connection. Still, the politics of 2021–22 cannot be separated from rising prices. Voters experienced the economy less through legislative ambition than through groceries, gas, rent, and interest rates. A party that wants to restore faith in activist government must show not only that it can spend, but that it can deliver capacity, abundance, and stability. Permitting reform, grid expansion, administrative competence, and anti-bottleneck governance are not technocratic side issues. They are central to whether the new economic agenda can survive contact with public impatience.

The most promising element of Biden-era Democratic economics is its move from compensation to production: not just helping people after markets fail, but shaping markets so they generate better jobs, cleaner energy, and broader opportunity. Yet production politics has a discipline that Democrats have not fully accepted. It requires building things even when incumbents object; choosing among competing environmental, labor, speed, and cost goals; and admitting that process-heavy liberalism can empower veto points as surely as the Senate does. If every transmission line, apartment building, factory, rail project, and clean-energy installation must run a gauntlet of litigation and local obstruction, industrial policy will disappoint.

So the lead essay is right against the cynics: the Democratic Party has changed, and the change is consequential. But the next test is not whether Democrats can write a more ambitious platform. They have already done that. The test is whether they can turn a coalition united by anti-Trump alarm and broad support for taxing the very rich into a governing majority willing to confront scarcity, local exclusion, administrative sclerosis, and the privileges of its own winners.

A durable pro-democracy coalition cannot be built on negative polarization alone. Nor can it be built only on programs that almost pass. It will require visible improvements in ordinary life: cheaper housing, reliable child care, faster clean-energy deployment, better wages for non-college workers, and public institutions that work without heroic navigation. The new Democratic economics has opened that possibility. Whether the party can make good on it depends on moving from carefully curated redistribution to a more confrontational politics of democratic abundance.
