"To truly honor King’s legacy, we must move beyond viewing him as a radical icon or a civic ornament and instead engage with his sophisticated theory of democratic power, which emphasizes that moral truth must be organized, disciplined, and made institutionally consequential."

Brandon Terry’s central claim is right and urgently so: the King most Americans invoke is not the King who died in Memphis. The public King has been flattened into a civic ornament, a source of quotations suitable for school assemblies, corporate diversity brochures, and judicial opinions hostile to the very forms of racial redress King came to defend. Terry’s essay is valuable because it asks us to treat King neither as plaster saint nor as exhausted symbol, but as a political thinker whose late work still troubles our categories. That is the proper starting point for any commemoration worthy of the man.

Yet if we recover King’s radicalism, we should be careful not to recover it as mere atmosphere. It is now common, especially on the left, to say that King was more radical than his holiday image suggests. But “radical King” can become its own icon: the King of the Vietnam speech, the King of the Poor People’s Campaign, the King who named capitalism’s cruelty and America’s violence. This is a necessary correction, but not sufficient. King’s radicalism was not simply a set of more left-wing policy preferences attached to a more confrontational rhetoric. It was a theory of democratic power. He believed that moral truth had to be organized, dramatized, disciplined, and made institutionally consequential.

That is where Terry’s reconstruction of King’s thought is strongest. He emphasizes King’s insistence that racism was not reducible to individual hatred, nor poverty to bad luck, nor militarism to mistaken foreign policy. King saw these evils as mutually reinforcing features of American life. But he also refused the fatalism that sometimes follows from structural analysis. His language of “congenital deformity” did not mean permanent damnation. It meant that racial domination had shaped the American body politic from birth and that overcoming it would require more than sentiment, apology, or procedural liberalism. It would require power.

This matters especially in 2018 because the American left is rediscovering words—socialism, abolition, reparations, redistribution—that for decades were treated as disqualifying. The financial crisis, the long stagnation of wages, the wreckage of mass incarceration, and the Trump presidency have all made the old neoliberal common sense look morally bankrupt. Young activists and intellectuals are increasingly willing to say that inequality is not an unfortunate byproduct of freedom but a political choice. In this context, King should not be cited merely as a precursor to present slogans. He should discipline them.

Consider Terry’s discussion of economic justice. King supported a guaranteed income and full employment not as technocratic fixes but as movement demands. That distinction is crucial. In our own debates, universal basic income is often presented as an elegant response to automation, a way to preserve purchasing power while avoiding deeper conflicts over ownership, work, race, and political power. King’s interest was different. He wanted the poor themselves to become a disruptive democratic force. He did not imagine that policy experts, having discovered the correct formula, would deliver justice from above. He believed a multiracial army of the poor had to compel the nation to reorder its priorities.

That insight should unsettle both moderates and radicals. It unsettles moderates because it rejects the fantasy that American institutions, left to their ordinary rhythms, will correct gross injustice. Voting, litigation, and lobbying matter, but King came to understand their limits. Rights can be formally recognized while neighborhoods remain segregated, schools unequal, jobs unavailable, and police departments predatory. A politics that treats protest as legitimate only when polite, brief, and deferential has already surrendered to what King called negative peace.

But King also unsettles radicals who treat disruption itself as proof of seriousness. His nonviolence was not merely a branding strategy, nor a sentimental preference for niceness. It was an effort to build a form of coercive democratic power that did not reproduce the dehumanization it opposed. Terry is right to stress that King’s nonviolence was coercive. Boycotts, sit-ins, marches, jail-ins, and occupations were meant to create crises. They imposed costs. They forced negotiations. But King also believed that means shape ends. If the movement cultivated contempt, humiliation, and indifference to truth, it would damage the very democratic life it sought to create.

This is a hard teaching in the age of social media. Terry criticizes “mob mentality, virtue signaling, and scapegoating,” and the warning is warranted, though it must be handled carefully. Too often denunciations of “call-out culture” become excuses to protect the powerful from accountability. The problem is not that anger appears in public. Anger is often the first morally sane response to cruelty. The problem is that digital platforms reward compression, performance, and punishment. They make it easier to confuse the exposure of wrongdoing with the construction of power. A viral shaming can destroy a person’s reputation; it cannot, by itself, build a union, desegregate a school district, pass a housing program, or end cash bail.

King’s politics asks a question that much contemporary activism avoids: what forms of discipline make freedom possible? Discipline is an unfashionable word, associated with hierarchy, respectability, and repression. But movements cannot endure without it. Nonviolent discipline in Birmingham or Nashville did not mean emotional submission. It meant training people to withstand humiliation without surrendering strategic control to the oppressor. It meant preparing bodies, words, and organizations for confrontation. It meant accepting that courage is not the same as expression.

Terry’s distinction between respectability politics and a politics of character is therefore important. King certainly carried assumptions about gender, sexuality, and family that deserve criticism. He inhabited and benefited from patriarchal institutions. But it is a mistake to reduce all talk of virtue to respectability. The question is not whether the oppressed must prove themselves worthy of rights. They do not. The question is whether democratic struggle requires habits of courage, patience, honesty, generosity, and self-command. It does. A movement that cannot sustain trust among its members, tell the truth about its failures, or resist the intoxication of revenge will not remain liberatory for long.

Still, Terry’s essay might say more about institutions. King was a movement thinker, but he was also surrounded by organizations: churches, the Southern Christian Leadership Conference, unions, student groups, local associations, legal defense networks, newspapers, and colleges. His moral vocabulary was inseparable from institutional life. Today many of the institutions that once trained people for collective action have weakened. Unions are a fraction of their former strength. Churches, especially among younger progressives, no longer command the same authority. Local newspapers are shrinking. Political parties are simultaneously polarized and hollowed out. Online networks can mobilize rapidly but often cannot govern themselves.

If King is to matter now, the question is not only what he thought but what could play the role that his institutions played. The Poor People’s Campaign has been revived in our time by clergy and activists, and its moral language shows the continuing power of King’s idiom. Black Lives Matter and affiliated organizations have transformed public debate about policing and state violence, even without adopting King’s theological frame. Teacher strikes in West Virginia, Oklahoma, Arizona, and elsewhere have shown that mass economic action can reappear in unexpected places. The Fight for $15 has linked low-wage work, racial justice, and union power. These are not repetitions of King’s movement, but they are sites where his questions live.

The challenge is to connect them. King’s late genius was to see that civil rights, labor rights, peace, and poverty were not separate moral departments. They were fronts in a single struggle over the meaning of democracy. In 2018, that synthesis remains fragile. Racial justice campaigns sometimes lack a broad economic program; economic populism sometimes evades race; antiwar politics has become strangely marginal despite endless war; climate politics often struggles to speak to workers who fear displacement. King’s legacy presses toward a coalition capacious enough to confront all these realities without dissolving their differences.

Terry ends with King’s call to “stay awake.” Wakefulness, for King, did not mean permanent outrage. It meant moral clarity joined to strategic intelligence. It meant seeing the world as it is without granting injustice the final word. That is the King we need now: neither the harmless dreamer of civic myth nor the radical icon safely admired from a distance, but the democratic organizer who understood that love without power is sentimentality, and power without love is domination.

"Rather than treating Martin Luther King Jr. as a static moral icon, modern activists should engage with his legacy as a source of evolving strategy for nonviolent coercion and organizational discipline."

The most important line in Brandon Terry’s essay is not one of King’s famous phrases. It is Terry’s observation that “the morphology of protest should be treated as a perpetual question.” That is the King organizers need now: not a monument, not a meme, not a sanctified rebuke to impatient young people, but a strategist who kept asking what forms of collective action could make injustice impossible to ignore and costly to maintain.

Much of our public argument about King still turns on moral ownership. Was King a radical or a liberal? Was he colorblind or race-conscious? Was he closer to today’s Black Lives Matter activists or to their critics? These questions are not meaningless, but they can become sterile. Movements do not win because they possess the correct ancestor. They win because they organize people, choose targets, impose costs, shift common sense, divide opponents, and build institutions capable of sustaining gains. King matters because he thought about these tasks with unusual moral and strategic seriousness.

Terry helps by restoring the coercive King. Americans love to imagine nonviolence as gentle persuasion. In this telling, civil rights protesters suffered nobly, white Americans felt shame, and Congress responded. But King’s campaigns were not requests for empathy. They were confrontations. The Montgomery bus boycott attacked the revenue of a segregated transit system. Sit-ins disrupted commerce. Birmingham filled jails, provoked a crisis, and forced negotiation. The Selma campaign dramatized disenfranchisement in a way that made federal inaction untenable. Nonviolence was not passivity. It was organized pressure.

That lesson is badly needed in 2018 because much of what we call resistance is expressive rather than strategic. A march demonstrates numbers and emotion, but unless connected to demands, targets, and next steps, it dissipates. A hashtag spreads testimony, but unless linked to organization, it can become a graveyard of attention. A viral video of police violence may produce outrage, but without durable local power it rarely changes police contracts, prosecutors, budgets, or state law. King’s question was never simply “How do we show we are right?” It was “How do we create a crisis that forces negotiation and transformation?”

This does not mean every campaign should imitate Birmingham. Terry rightly notes that the media environment has changed. Images of black suffering do not shock as they once did; indeed, they may retraumatize the sympathetic while hardening the hostile. Police departments have learned protest management. The public is fragmented into partisan media worlds. A dramatic arrest may inspire one audience and confirm another’s belief that protesters are disorderly. The state surveils activists with technologies King’s generation could barely imagine. Terrorism fears alter the meaning of public assembly. In such conditions, repeating civil rights theater can become ritual rather than strategy.

So what would it mean to think with King rather than reenact him? First, it would mean rebuilding the organizational base of protest. King’s campaigns depended on churches, local leaders, student networks, labor allies, lawyers, bail funds, sympathetic journalists, and disciplined training. Today we often have mobilization without membership. People can be summoned quickly, but they are not necessarily formed politically or tied to one another in accountable structures. This makes movements nimble, but also fragile. It becomes easy for media personalities, foundations, or online factions to substitute for democratic decision-making.

The teacher strikes of 2018 offer one clue. In West Virginia, Oklahoma, Arizona, and elsewhere, educators used walkouts to challenge austerity, low pay, and the starving of public schools. These actions were disruptive, morally legible, and rooted in workplace relationships. They won public sympathy not by avoiding conflict but by connecting the interests of teachers to the interests of students and communities. They also showed that militancy need not be confined to urban progressive enclaves. King’s late turn toward labor and poverty should push racial justice organizers to study such struggles seriously.

Second, thinking with King means recovering the boycott and the strike as democratic weapons. Marches are visible, but economic disruption changes calculations. The Fight for $15 has understood this better than many campaigns. By linking low-wage workers’ dignity to wages, race, and corporate power, it has created pressure in cities and states even where federal action is blocked. Immigrant worker strikes, prison strikes, and consumer boycotts similarly point toward forms of nonviolent coercion that do not depend entirely on persuading cable news viewers. King’s Memphis campaign was about sanitation workers because the workplace is a site where dignity and power meet.

Third, King’s approach requires target clarity. “White supremacy,” “capitalism,” and “the system” may name real structures, but campaigns need decision-makers who can concede demands. One cannot negotiate with an abstraction. The genius of a lunch-counter sit-in was that it identified a practice, a place, a business interest, a legal regime, and a moral contradiction. Contemporary movements sometimes leap from personal testimony to maximal critique without building intermediate campaigns. Abolish ICE, end cash bail, restore voting rights, raise the minimum wage, cancel municipal fines, remove lead from water, renegotiate police union contracts—these demands vary in scale and wisdom, but they have the virtue of naming fights.

Fourth, King’s discipline about anger remains useful, though not because anger is illegitimate. Anger is fuel. The question is whether it powers an engine or starts a fire that consumes the garage. Online politics incentivizes the second. It turns internal disagreement into public spectacle, rewards the most accusatory reading, and makes apology nearly impossible. Organizations cannot survive if every conflict becomes a referendum on moral worth. King’s language of love may not persuade secular activists, but his organizational point should: movements need practices that keep people in relationship through conflict.

Here Terry’s distinction between humiliation and confrontation is crucial. A campaign must pressure opponents; it need not seek their degradation. This is not sentimental. Humiliation can produce short-term satisfaction and long-term resistance. It can also spread inward, as activists learn to relate to one another through suspicion and punishment. The point is not to avoid naming harm. The point is to build a culture in which accountability is connected to repair, learning, and strategy rather than endless expulsion.

Fifth, King’s broad coalition politics should be retrieved without erasing difference. Terry emphasizes King’s insight that automation and economic change threatened black workers and organized labor alike. Today the equivalent forces include automation, deindustrialization, precarious service work, mass incarceration, housing segregation, student debt, and ecological crisis. A serious movement must connect them. But connection is not the same as pretending everyone is injured in the same way. The white worker displaced by plant closure and the black mother whose son is harassed by police may share enemies in austerity and corporate power, but their experiences are not interchangeable. King’s best politics held together universal claims and particular injuries.

The risk, especially after Trump’s election, is that coalition becomes a euphemism for asking marginalized people to mute their claims in order to win back white voters. That would be a betrayal of King. He did not build unity by avoiding confrontation with racism; he forced confrontation in the name of a deeper unity. At the same time, he did not imagine that moral denunciation of whites would produce a governing majority. He sought ways to make shared interests visible without denying historical responsibility.

Finally, King’s internationalism needs a strategic update. Terry notes that Cold War shame no longer operates as it once did. American leaders seem increasingly indifferent to world opinion, and authoritarian nationalism is on the rise. Yet global capitalism creates targets King could not fully see: multinational corporations, supply chains, financial institutions, tax havens, fossil fuel companies, and digital platforms. Climate change especially demands a Kingian politics of nonviolent coercion at a scale we have barely begun to imagine. Divestment campaigns, pipeline resistance, shareholder pressure, municipal action, and transnational labor alliances are early experiments. The question is whether they can become mass politics rather than niche activism.

King’s greatness was not that he had a timeless formula. It was that he combined moral vision with tactical adaptation. He learned. He shifted from Montgomery to Birmingham to Selma to Chicago to Memphis, and each context exposed new limits. Chicago showed that Southern tactics did not automatically translate to Northern housing markets. The Poor People’s Campaign was unfinished and uncertain. King died in the middle of experimentation, not at the end of a completed doctrine.

That unfinished quality should free us. We do not need to ask whether King would have approved every tactic used by Black Lives Matter, immigrant rights activists, democratic socialists, prison abolitionists, climate campaigners, or striking teachers. We need to ask the questions he asked: Who is suffering? What structures produce that suffering? Who has the power to change them? What pressure can be applied? What discipline is required? What alliances are possible? What moral language can enlarge rather than shrink the movement? What institutions will remain when the march is over?

Terry has given us a King worth arguing with. Organizers should go further: treat King as a workshop, not a shrine. His legacy is not a set of quotations but a set of problems. The only faithful tribute is to keep solving them.
