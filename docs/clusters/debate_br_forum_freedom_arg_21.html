The Limits of the Settler Frame

Aziz Rana's argument rests on a single explanatory engine: American conceptions of freedom were shaped by settler expansion and then by imperial primacy, and the resulting culture of zero-sum, racialized, individualistic liberty is what the left must now overcome. The framework has real force, and it explains things that other frameworks miss. But it is also overextended, and the cost of relying on it as heavily as Rana does is that he ends up obscuring other sources of the American freedom tradition and other reasons for the right's current dominance of freedom-talk.

Consider first the comparative case. Rana implies that the United States is distinctively bad on these matters because of its settler past. But other settler societies — Canada, Australia, New Zealand — have moved in significantly different directions. Australia developed a powerful labor movement and a comprehensive welfare state in the early twentieth century, long before the US New Deal, and despite an arguably more violent and explicit settler ideology. Canadian political culture is famously more communitarian than American, with public health care, more redistributive taxation, and a Charter of Rights and Freedoms that explicitly balances individual and collective claims. New Zealand abolished its constitutional monarchy of property qualifications earlier than most peers and produced an indigenous-settler political settlement (imperfect, contested, but real) that has no American equivalent. If settler expansion produced the American account of freedom, why did parallel settler expansions produce different accounts elsewhere?

One answer is that settler expansion is not, by itself, sufficient. What distinguished the American case was a particular constellation of factors: the absence of an established church, the lateness of universal male suffrage being extended to non-property-holders (though earlier than in Europe), the constitutional structure of federalism that empowered subnational majorities to resist national redistribution, the specific religious culture of evangelical Protestantism with its emphasis on individual conversion, the inheritance of common-law property doctrines from England, and the catastrophic timing of slavery and its abolition. The settler-imperial frame captures some of this constellation but not all of it, and treating it as the master explanation distorts the rest.

Consider, second, the chronology Rana proposes. He argues that the early twentieth century was a moment when "settler expectations" had collapsed and a solidaristic freedom tradition gained ground, only to be overwhelmed again by the consolidation of US empire after World War II. This is suggestive but historically rough. The Progressive Era was not, in most accounts, a triumph of solidaristic freedom; it was a contested period in which technocratic reform, nativist immigration restriction (1924), Jim Crow consolidation, and labor repression coexisted with the genuine social democratic currents Rana highlights. The New Deal itself was a deeply ambivalent achievement, structured by compromises with Southern Democrats that excluded agricultural and domestic workers (largely Black) from Social Security and the Wagner Act. The story of mid-twentieth-century social democracy in America is not a clean tale of solidaristic freedom briefly ascendant.

Conversely, the post-1945 period was not uniformly the triumph of individualistic, imperial freedom Rana suggests. The civil rights movement, Great Society, environmental movement, second-wave feminism, gay liberation, disability rights — all of these arose during and after the consolidation of American global primacy, not in opposition to its decline. If empire's "rents" were so determinative of domestic conservatism, why did the period of maximum imperial extraction produce the period of maximum domestic egalitarian reform?

The answer is that the relationship between foreign policy and domestic ideology is more complicated than Rana's causal story allows. Sometimes empire abroad correlates with reaction at home (the McCarthy era); sometimes it correlates with reform (the Great Society, partly motivated by Cold War competition for the loyalty of decolonizing nations). To pick examples where the correlation supports the thesis and ignore those where it does not is to take the wind out of the argument's empirical sails.

Third, the settler frame can obscure as much as it reveals about contemporary American politics. Take the rural-urban divide, the religious-secular divide, the educational polarization that has restructured American partisan alignment over the past three decades. These do not map cleanly onto a settler/non-settler frame. Many of the most conservative voters today live in places — exurban Texas, the Florida panhandle, the Ohio Valley — whose settler histories are no more recent or vivid than those of liberal regions. Conversely, some of the most progressive coastal cities sit on land taken from Native peoples within living memory of certain settler dynasties. The geography of American conservatism is shaped by economic restructuring, religious affiliation, and media ecology at least as much as by inherited settler ideology.

Fourth, Rana's emphasis on settler conquest can crowd out attention to other deep sources of American individualism. The Protestant religious heritage matters. The peculiar absence of a feudal past matters (as Louis Hartz argued long ago). The constitutional design choices of 1787 — particularly the Senate, the Electoral College, federalism, and judicial review — matter. The specific structure of American immigration in waves matters. Each of these has shaped what "freedom" means in this country in ways that the settler frame, by itself, cannot fully explain.

None of this is to deny what Rana gets right. Settler expansion did shape the early American conception of freedom in ways that have lasting consequences. Race has always been at the center of American struggles over who counts as free. American imperial power abroad has corroded democratic culture at home. These are real points. The question is whether the settler-imperial frame is the best one for explaining contemporary American politics and for guiding contemporary left strategy.

I am not sure it is. A left strategy organized around the recognition that American freedom-talk is structurally settler-imperial may end up emphasizing the wrong reforms and missing potential coalition partners. It can become so focused on dismantling the inherited frame that it neglects the more proximate institutional drivers of inequality and unfreedom: the courts, the filibuster, the corporate consolidation of media, the structure of campaign finance, the absence of universal benefits. These are addressable through reforms that need not be framed in terms of anti-settler or anti-imperial politics, and that may attract broader coalitions when they are not.

There is also a risk that the settler frame becomes an alibi for inaction. If the problem is structural and centuries deep, the work of dismantling it is correspondingly enormous, and the temptation to talk about it more than act on it grows accordingly. The American left has, in my view, often suffered from a surplus of structural diagnosis and a deficit of strategic prioritization. Rana's essay, for all its sophistication, leans toward the former.

Reclaiming freedom may or may not be the right project. But if it is, the path to it runs through specific institutional fights — on labor law, voting rights, social provision, antitrust — that need not be filtered through the settler-imperial frame to be won. Sometimes the directest route is the best.
