"Mie Inouye’s essay provides a valuable pragmatic framework for solidarity by framing social endurance and internal conflict as essential tools for organizational transformation rather than obstacles to unity."

Mie Inouye's meditation on solidarity and social endurance is a genuine contribution to political theory, and I want to engage with it on its own terms before offering a more critical assessment. The essay is part of a tradition of American pragmatist thought that takes seriously the idea that political categories emerge from practical engagement rather than abstract theorizing. On this point, I think Inouye is on firm ground, and her essay enriches a tradition that too often neglects questions of race and gender.

The pragmatist tradition, from William James through John Dewey to Cornel West, has always insisted that ideas are tools for action. What matters is not whether a concept is "true" in some abstract sense but whether it enables effective engagement with the world. Inouye's essay exemplifies this orientation. Her theory of social endurance is not offered as a final word on solidarity but as a resource for organizers navigating complex political terrain. The essay is a tool, not a system.

This pragmatic orientation explains why the essay feels more like a working document than a finished theory. Inouye offers hypotheses—"social endurance might harbor a radical lesson"—rather than demonstrations. She tests her hypotheses against personal experience and historical example rather than deductive logic. She holds her conclusions open to revision in light of further experience. These features are not weaknesses; they are marks of a genuinely pragmatic approach to political theory.

What strikes me most about the essay is its treatment of conflict. Inouye draws on Nathan DuFord's work to argue that solidarity is "premised on disunity"—that internal conflict serves as a check on domination and exclusion. This is a genuinely challenging idea, and I want to develop it further.

The conventional wisdom about conflict in social movements is that it is destructive—that factions and disagreements fracture organizations and weaken movements. This view has some truth. Organizations that tear themselves apart through internal conflict often accomplish little. But the conventional wisdom also enables a dangerous silence about the power dynamics within organizations that are nominally committed to social justice. When organizations suppress internal conflict in the name of unity, they often reproduce the very hierarchies and exclusions they purport to contest.

Inouye's argument points toward a different approach. Instead of trying to eliminate conflict, we should cultivate the capacity to channel conflict productively. This means developing organizational cultures that can hold disagreement without rupture, that can transform conflict from a source of weakness into a source of learning. It also means distinguishing between realistic conflict—disagreements about strategy and purpose that can be resolved through collective deliberation—and unrealistic conflict—the petty quarrels and personality clashes that do undermine organizational capacity.

The practical challenge is that this distinction is often unclear in practice. What seems like a strategic disagreement may also involve personal antagonisms and status concerns. What seems like a petty squabble may reflect genuine differences in political perspective. Developing the capacity to navigate this ambiguity is one of the key skills that solidarity organizations must cultivate.

Inouye's essay points toward this cultivation, though it does not fully specify how it might work. I think this is appropriate given the pragmatic orientation of the essay. The practices that enable productive conflict will emerge from collective experience, not from theoretical prescription. What theory can do is provide orientation—suggesting that conflict is not merely a problem to be avoided but a resource to be cultivated.

I close with a note of appreciation. Essays like this one are rare: theoretically sophisticated without being sterile, politically engaged without being polemical, personal without being confessional. Whatever its limitations, the essay models the kind of thinking that social movements need. It takes ideas seriously while remaining accountable to practice. That is no small achievement.
