Diversified Claude

"The moral case for amnesty should be grounded in the structural global political economy that produces irregular migration rather than just individual stories or the passage of time."

Carens treats the question of amnesty as if it could be answered in isolation from the structural facts of contemporary migration, particularly the role of remittances, the demand for labor in receiving countries, and the legacies of imperial and economic intervention by rich states in poor ones. This is a mistake. The case for amnesty does not need to rest on individual stories alone, however moving. It can be made in a stronger form by reference to the global political economy that produces irregular migration in the first place.

Consider the case of Mexico, since most of the irregular migrants in the United States come from there. The migration flow accelerated dramatically after NAFTA took effect in 1994. Why? Because NAFTA opened Mexican agricultural markets to subsidized American corn, devastating the livelihoods of millions of small farmers. The Mexican economist Eduardo Zepeda and others have estimated that something like two million agricultural jobs were lost in Mexico in the decade after NAFTA. Where did those farmers go? Many of them came north. The U.S.-driven trade regime produced the very labor displacement that produced the irregular migrants whom American immigration enforcement now seeks to deport. The United States cannot disclaim responsibility for the consequences of its own trade policy.

Similar stories can be told about Central America, where decades of U.S. military and political intervention destabilized the region and produced refugee flows that have continued long after the formal wars ended. About Haiti, whose economic devastation has many fathers, several of them in Washington. About the Philippines and Korea, whose mass labor migrations to the United States have roots in U.S. military presence and political entanglement. The poor countries from which migrants come are not random sites of poverty unrelated to the rich countries to which they go. They are nodes in an economic and political system that the rich countries dominate.

This matters because it cuts the heart out of the standard restrictionist argument. The restrictionist position rests on the assumption that the receiving state confronts irregular migration as an external phenomenon — something that happens to it from outside, that it has every right to refuse. But this is not the structural reality. The receiving state has, in many cases, helped to produce the migration through its economic and foreign policy. It then benefits from the resulting labor flow, which keeps wages low in key sectors and provides workers willing to do jobs that citizens decline. And then, when the political mood shifts, it disclaims any responsibility and demands the right to deport the workers whose presence it engineered.

Carens touches on this with his "complicity" argument but treats it as a supplementary consideration, applicable in some cases but not others. I think the complicity argument is much more central than he allows. The very category of "irregular migration" obscures a structural fact: the migration is regular, in the sense of being a predictable and predictable consequence of the global economic order. It is only "irregular" as a matter of paperwork. The migrants are doing what the system invites them to do; the system then punishes them for doing it.

If we take this seriously, the case for amnesty becomes much stronger and more comprehensive. The migrants are not strangers asking for mercy. They are participants in a transnational economic system whose terms have been set largely by the rich countries to whose labor markets they have migrated. The political community of the United States is not just a "host" with the right to exclude. It is a beneficiary of, and contributor to, the conditions that produced the migration in the first place. The eleven million are not interlopers. They are creditors.

This framing also reshapes the response to Carens' objections. The "fairness to those waiting in line" worry is even weaker than Carens makes out, because the line itself reflects a global immigration regime designed to admit those whose labor the receiving country wants on its own terms (skilled workers, family-sponsored migrants in limited numbers) while excluding those whose labor it wants only under conditions of vulnerability (the undocumented). The line is rigged. The unfairness is not amnesty but the system of which amnesty is a partial correction.

The "rewards lawbreaking" worry, similarly, depends on a view of the law as a neutral framework that has been violated. But immigration law in its present form is not neutral. It is a structure designed, knowingly or not, to produce a steady supply of vulnerable labor. The law is part of the system that exploits the migrants. Calling them lawbreakers is like calling the workers in a sweatshop "wage thieves" for accepting illegally low wages — the violation is real, but the framing is upside down.

I would push Carens further than he goes. The amnesty he proposes is necessary but insufficient. A full response to the structural realities of contemporary migration would include: substantially expanded legal channels for the workers the rich economies actually demand; sharp constraints on the global trade and financial policies that displace agricultural workers in the poor world; serious investment in development in the sending countries (not the corporate-friendly version, but genuine development that produces jobs); and a recognition that border enforcement, as currently practiced, costs lives that the moral calculus cannot justify.

This is a more radical agenda than Carens' essay, but it is implicit in some of what he writes, particularly his treatment of the complicity argument and the human cost of border policy. The challenge for those of us who agree with him on amnesty is to articulate the broader vision of which amnesty is a part. Amnesty for the eleven million already here is morally necessary. But if we treat amnesty as the end of the conversation, we leave intact the system that will produce the next eleven million and demand the next amnesty fifteen years from now. We have been on this treadmill since the 1986 IRCA legalization. It is time to step off.

What Carens calls a "minimalist" argument is, in fact, the floor of decency. We owe the long-resident migrants legalization. We owe much more than that.
