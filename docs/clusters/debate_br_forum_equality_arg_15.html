"The capabilities approach developed by Amartya Sen and Martha Nussbaum provides a superior egalitarian framework to Sypnowich’s 'flourishing' model because it prioritizes substantive well-being while still respecting individual freedom and pluralism."

Sypnowich's critique of opportunity-focused egalitarianism and her advocacy of a flourishing approach are valuable contributions to an important debate. Yet her argument, while compelling on many points, does not adequately reckon with the capabilities approach developed by Amartya Sen and Martha Nussbaum—work she acknowledges but ultimately sidesteps. A capabilities-based response offers a more promising path than either traditional liberal egalitarianism or the flourishing approach she endorses.

Sen's central insight remains indispensable: the appropriate focus of egalitarian concern is not resources, not utility, but what people are actually able to do and be—their capabilities. A capability is the substantive freedom to achieve valuable functionings. Someone who is ill but has access to healthcare has the capability to be well; someone who is educated but never exercises that education still possesses the capability to be educated. This distinction between capabilities and functionings is crucial because it respects what Sen calls the "combination of freedom and responsibility." We care about what people can do, not what they actually do, because we respect their freedom to choose among different combinations of activities.

Sypnowich argues that capability theory is too similar to opportunity-based approaches because it measures capability to function rather than actual functioning. She is right that this is a limitation of the approach as standardly formulated. But this limitation is not inherent to the capabilities framework. Nussbaum's version, for instance, explicitly acknowledges that capabilities must eventually translate into functioning if they are to constitute genuine flourishing. Moreover, the framework is flexible enough to accommodate different specifications of the threshold at which capabilities become sufficient.

The key advantage of the capabilities approach is its combination of pluralism and specification. Nussbaum has proposed a list of ten central capabilities—including life, bodily health, bodily integrity, senses/imagination/thought, emotions, practical reason, affiliation, other species, play, and control over one's environment—that constitute the minimum requirements for human dignity. This list is the product of cross-cultural dialogue and is intended to be revisable, but it provides concrete guidance for policy without reducing flourishing to a single metric.

This specification addresses one of the deepest problems in egalitarian theory: how to compare different conceptions of the good life. The capabilities approach does not require us to rank comprehensive conceptions of flourishing against each other. Instead, it identifies the preconditions for any flourishing—health, education, bodily integrity, social affiliation, practical reason—and treats these as non-negotiable minimums. Beyond these minimums, individuals are free to pursue their own conceptions of the good.

Sypnowich's argument that we should not take choices at face value is important, but it can be accommodated within the capabilities framework. The approach is explicitly political in the sense that it involves public deliberation about what capabilities are essential and how they should be secured. Public discussion about which capabilities to prioritize, how to measure them, and how to design institutions to promote them is entirely consistent with Sen's and Nussbaum's approach. The difference is that such deliberation proceeds in terms of actual capabilities—the real freedoms people possess—rather than abstract flourishing.

The capabilities approach also avoids the implausibility of luck egalitarianism's choice-circumstance distinction without abandoning the important intuition that individuals should bear some responsibility for their choices. If we specify capabilities adequately, we can ask whether individuals have the genuine opportunity to function well before concluding that their actual functioning is their own responsibility. The question is not whether choices are "free" in some absolute sense but whether they are made under conditions of adequate capability.

Finally, the capabilities approach addresses Sypnowich's concern about solidarity without requiring the comprehensive theory of the good life that flourishing egalitarianism seems to demand. Capabilities are inherently relational: affiliation, for instance, requires relationships with others and social institutions that support meaningful participation in community life. The approach thus captures the importance of social connection without imposing a particular vision of the flourishing life.

I do not claim that the capabilities approach resolves all the difficulties Sypnowich raises. The specification of capabilities is contestable, the measurement of capabilities is difficult, and the relationship between capabilities and functionings remains problematic. But these are challenges to be addressed, not insuperable objections. The capabilities framework provides the most promising synthesis of the insights Sypnowich develops—attention to actual well-being, rejection of narrow proceduralism, concern with community—while avoiding the pitfalls of comprehensive flourishing egalitarianism.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The capabilities approach focuses on what people are actually able to do and be, providing a concrete metric for policy without reducing human life to a single, state-defined version of flourishing.
  2. 02By distinguishing between capabilities (the power to act) and functionings (the act itself), the framework respects individual agency and the responsibility to choose among different life paths.
  3. 03Nussbaum’s list of central capabilities identifies universal preconditions for dignity—such as health and affiliation—that allow for public deliberation on justice without requiring a comprehensive ranking of different 'good lives.'
  4. 04The framework can accommodate Sypnowich’s concerns about social influence by evaluating whether choices are made under conditions of adequate capability rather than relying on the flawed luck-egalitarian distinction between choice and circumstance.
  5. 05Capabilities are inherently relational and social, addressing the need for community and solidarity without imposing a singular, paternalistic vision of a flourishing life.
