Diversified MiniMax

"The suppression of campus protest is not merely a failure of principle but a predictable result of the structural transformation of universities into corporate, risk-averse entities that prioritize administrative control and external funding over academic values."

The lead essay diagnoses institutional symptoms with admirable precision but misses the deeper structural disease. The problem is not merely that universities have overreacted to recent protests or that administrators have accepted flawed legal theories about hostile environments. The problem is that universities have been fundamentally transformed over the past half-century into entities that are structurally incapable of defending the values they claim to uphold.

Gourevitch catalogs the structural changes correctly: the ballooning administrative class, the dependence on federal funding, the cultivation of wealthy donors, the decline of faculty governance. But the essay treats these as background conditions rather than the central problem. Universities did not suddenly become cowardly; they have been systematically restructured to prioritize responsiveness to external power over commitment to internal values.

Consider the administrative apparatus that now surrounds every major university. The Dean of Students office, the Office of Equity and Compliance, the Title IX office, the counseling center, the diversity offices—all of these units have institutional incentives to identify and respond to student distress. This creates what might be called the therapeutic-administrative complex: a network of professionals whose job is to make students feel safe, combined with bureaucratic procedures that convert any expression of discomfort into potential liability. When students say they feel unsafe, administrators are professionally and legally obligated to take that seriously. The result is not malevolent censorship but systemic overreaction driven by institutional incentives.

The essay mentions this dynamic in passing, noting that universities have "fashioned themselves as safety-guaranteeing institutions," but does not fully explore how this transformation has reshaped campus culture. The contemporary university student has been raised in an environment of unprecedented safety regulation—from helicopter parenting to trigger warnings to microaggression training. They genuinely believe that their feelings of discomfort require adult intervention. This is not merely a matter of individual fragility but a product of deliberate institutional cultivation.

The solution, then, is not merely to articulate better principles but to restructure universities so that better principles can actually govern. This means restoring meaningful faculty governance, which has been hollowed out by administrative expansion. It means reducing dependence on both federal funding and donor capital, which creates vulnerabilities to political pressure. It means reviving the culture of academic self-governance that characterized universities before the corporate transformation of the 1980s and 1990s.

None of this is easy. The structural changes Gourevitch describes reflect broader transformations in American capitalism—the financialization of higher education, the triumph of market ideology, the decline of public investment in collective goods. Universities did not choose to become what they are; they were remade by external forces. But the essay's focus on principles rather than structures leads it to propose solutions—vigorous defense of protest rights, viewpoint-neutral enforcement—that require the very institutional capacities that have been systematically destroyed.

The harder truth is that restoring robust protest rights requires rebuilding the institutional conditions that once made such rights possible. This means a longer and more difficult struggle than the essay suggests, one that challenges not merely bad policies but the entire corporate model of higher education that has emerged over the past generation.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The growth of a 'therapeutic-administrative complex' creates institutional incentives to pathologize student discomfort and convert subjective feelings into administrative liabilities.
  2. 02Contemporary campus culture is a product of deliberate institutional cultivation that encourages students to view personal unease as a justification for official intervention.
  3. 03Universities have been systematically restructured to prioritize responsiveness to donors and federal agencies over internal faculty governance and institutional integrity.
  4. 04Restoring the right to protest requires a fundamental dismantling of the corporate university model rather than a simple reassertion of democratic principles.
  5. 05The current crisis is a reflection of broader shifts in American capitalism, such as financialization and the decline of public investment, which have hollowed out the university's capacity for self-governance.
Diversified DeepSeek

"The liberal framework of proceduralism and tolerance advocated by Gourevitch is an inadequate response to campus crackdowns because it fails to recognize that the university is a counterinsurgent institution that must be disrupted rather than reformed."

It is hard to read Alex Gourevitch’s call for a tolerant, pluralist campus and not see it as a plea to the very authorities who have already proven they would rather defund departments and expel students than permit a whisper of dissent against the empire. Gourevitch is a lucid thinker, and his diagnosis of the neoliberal university is sharp. But his cure—a return to liberal proceduralism, a restoration of “modest and proportionate” discipline, a renewed commitment to the “right to be hostile”—misses the entire point. The protests were never about winning a spot in the campus marketplace of ideas. They were an act of solidarity with a people under siege, and an attack on the institutions that materially support that siege. No liberal framework can contain that.

Gourevitch frames the crackdowns as a deviation from a previously healthy norm, a sudden collapse into authoritarianism fueled by an unholy alliance of safetyist progressives and right-wing opportunists. But that norm was always a myth. The liberal university has always been a counterinsurgent institution, policing the boundaries of acceptable dissent. The difference is that for a brief moment, the protesters refused to accept those boundaries. They occupied space not to ask permission to speak but to disrupt the normal functioning of a war machine. Calling for the end of genocide is not an academic exercise; it is an imperative that cannot be calibrated to avoid offense. The very demand that it be so—that protesters “tolerate” their enemies, that they refrain from “incivility”—is itself a form of violence, a demand that the oppressed smile politely while their people are murdered.

Gourevitch is right to criticize the hostile environment standard, but for the wrong reasons. He worries that it will be weaponized against the left, and indeed it has been. But the deeper problem is that it locates the problem of violence in the feelings of the vulnerable rather than in the structures of power. Jewish students who felt unsafe because they saw keffiyehs or heard Arabic slogans were not the victims of antisemitic harassment in any meaningful sense; they were experiencing the discomfort of being morally implicated in a crime. The university’s rush to protect them was an act of political solidarity with the state of Israel, dressed up in the language of care. To accept the frame that this is a free speech issue is to accept that Palestinian solidarity is merely an opinion, rather than an obligation.

What should we do? Not call for a return to Lee Bollinger’s nostalgic vision of post-’60s tolerance. That tolerance was always contingent on the protests being manageable, on the demands being reformist, on the noise being at an acceptable decibel level. What is needed now is a refusal to accept the legitimacy of the institutions that crack down. Students should not be arguing for the right to protest under university rules; they should be arguing against the existence of those rules. They should escalate, not moderate. The goal is not to earn a hearing from the trustees or to win in court; it is to make the cost of doing business with genocide too high to sustain. If that means occupying buildings for longer, if it means making noise at all hours, if it means forcing the university to choose between its endowment and its reputation—so be it. The right to protest is not something you wait for the dean to grant you. It is something you take, and you hold onto it until the world changes.

Gourevitch’s essay, for all its radical sympathies, is ultimately a liberal concession. It tells us to be hostile, but politely. It asks us to tolerate our enemies, even as they bomb our families. It insists that the only protection we can rely on is a neutral authority that will, at best, punish everyone equally. That is a recipe for perpetual defeat. The alternative is not chaos but a politics that starts from the premise that liberation is not a speech act. It requires building power outside the structures of elite education and using the university not as a debating society but as a target. That is the protest we had, and it was the right one—crackdowns be damned.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The university's historical tolerance for protest was always a myth designed to manage dissent rather than protect genuine liberation movements.
  2. 02Framing the suppression of Palestinian solidarity as a free speech issue wrongly reduces a moral and material obligation to a mere difference of opinion.
  3. 03The 'hostile environment' standard is fundamentally flawed because it prioritizes the subjective discomfort of those morally implicated in state violence over the objective reality of structural power.
  4. 04Protesters should reject the legitimacy of university rules and escalate their tactics to make the institutional cost of supporting war and genocide unsustainable.
  5. 05True political power is built by treating the university as a target for disruption rather than a neutral forum for polite debate.
Diversified Gemini

"The lead essay's call for universities to voluntarily protect a right to hostile protest is a liberal fantasy that fundamentally misunderstands the university as a profit-driven institution of the state rather than a sanctuary for dissent."

Alex Gourevitch wants to have his cake and eat it too. In “The Right to Be Hostile,” he argues that universities must tolerate disruptive, hostile protests, effectively pleading with the neoliberal university to voluntarily suspend its own rules to accommodate radical dissent. It is a beautifully written liberal fantasy that fundamentally misunderstands the nature of power, protest, and the state.

Gourevitch’s central premise is that the university *should* be a sanctuary for disruptive speech, citing the idealized history of the 1960s. He acts as if the recent crackdowns by the Trump administration and university boards are a deviation from the university’s “nonprofit, educational mission.” This is a delusion. The modern American university is not a community of scholars; it is a multi-billion-dollar real estate hedge fund attached to a credentialing apparatus, heavily subsidized by federal defense and research grants. It does not “tolerate” disruption that threatens its bottom line, nor has it ever.

By demanding that administrators recognize an “absolutist defense of the right to protest,” Gourevitch reduces resistance to a permitted activity. But a protest that requires permission is just a parade. The encampments in solidarity with Gaza were powerful precisely *because* they broke the rules. They seized private property. They defied the dictates of capital. They made the normal functioning of the institution impossible.

Gourevitch tries to domesticate these actions, arguing they weren’t *that* disruptive, that people could still get to class, that it was merely "noise." Why is he apologizing for the disruption? The entire point of the encampments was to grind the machinery of the complicit university to a halt. If the protests didn't threaten the material interests of the university, the university wouldn't have called the riot police.

We should stop begging administrators to validate our right to be hostile. The state—and the university as its proxy—will always use the language of “safety” and “trespass” to crush threats to its hegemony. Expecting the very institutions funding and profiting from the war machine to protect the rights of those trying to dismantle that machine is historically illiterate. The goal is not to convince the oppressor to grant us a “public forum.” The goal is to build power outside their permission structures entirely.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The modern university is a multi-billion-dollar corporate entity that will never tolerate disruptions threatening its financial and political interests.
  2. 02A protest that seeks or requires institutional permission is merely a parade, whereas the power of the Gaza encampments derived specifically from their defiance of rules and property rights.
  3. 03The author's attempt to downplay the disruptiveness of the protests undermines the actual goal of radical action, which is to grind the machinery of complicit institutions to a halt.
  4. 04Instead of begging administrators to validate the right to protest, activists should focus on building power outside of the university's permission structures.
