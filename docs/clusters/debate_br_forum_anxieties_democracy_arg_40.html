"The crisis of democratic legitimacy should be addressed through concrete institutional engineering and electoral reform rather than a reliance on a revived civic spirit or background consensus."

Ira Katznelson has performed a valuable service by framing our democratic discontent as a problem of institutional design rather than merely a failure of political will. Yet his conclusion—that we need a revived background consensus and a spirit of self-abnegation—seems like a wish dressed as an argument. The question before us in 2015 is not whether to pray for civic virtue, but what specific alterations to the machinery of representation might restore its capacity and legitimacy. The most promising answer is a set of reforms that change the electoral incentives of legislators, breaking the cycles of partisan polarization and interest-group tyranny that Katznelson rightly identifies.

Start with the most straightforward diagnosis: the House of Representatives is a gerrymandered fortress. Thanks to sophisticated redistricting, the overwhelming majority of House seats are safe for one party, which means the only real election is the primary. Those primaries are dominated by the most ideological voters on each side, producing members with zero incentive to compromise. The result is not a deliberative chamber but two armed camps that view every issue as a battle for base purity. The solution, which is gaining momentum in states like California and Arizona, is independent redistricting commissions combined with nonpartisan, top-two primary systems. When candidates must appeal to the whole electorate, not just the partisan fringe, the physics of consent begins to shift. Bipartisan groups like No Labels are already working to expand this model, and early evidence suggests that it indeed produces more moderate, pragmatic legislators.

Campaign finance, too, demands a technical fix that goes beyond constitutional hand-wringing. Katznelson nods to Citizens United, but the real story is the explosion of dark money and the dominance of a tiny donor class. A promising path is small-donor public financing, modeled on New York City’s successful matching funds program. By multiplying the contributions of ordinary citizens with public dollars, such systems change the calculus: a politician can fund a competitive campaign without spending every waking hour dialing for wealthy donors. The Democracy for All amendment languishes in Congress, but cities and states are already experimenting with vouchers and tax credits that put political power back in the hands of the many. These are not utopian schemes; they are working now in places like Maine and Connecticut.

Beyond elections, the legislative process itself requires restoration. Katznelson mentions “arcane rules of procedure,” and he is correct that the filibuster, holds, and House procedural tricks have replaced committee deliberation with crisis governance. Reform does not require abolishing minority rights; it requires bringing back regular order. That means restoring the power of committees, requiring open amendments, and limiting the ability of leadership to jam through omnibus bills. When members actually have to debate, craft, and vote on individual provisions, the quality of legislation improves and the public can follow the process. Groups like the Congressional Reformers Caucus are trying to push such changes, though the institutional inertia is formidable.

Some will object that these reforms are merely technocratic and miss the deeper cultural malaise. But institutional design shapes culture. When legislators are freed from the permanent campaign of gerrymandered primaries and donor maintenance, they gain the space to rediscover what Katznelson values: the dignity of legislation, the ability to make provisional compromises that hold. Our democratic anxieties are not inborn; they are produced by machines we built, and we can build better ones. The scaffolding on the Capitol reminds us that physical structures can be repaired. So can our governing structures, if we approach them not as ineffable problems of spirit but as concrete challenges of engineering.
