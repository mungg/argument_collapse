"The failure of care feminism lies not in overvaluing care, but in its reliance on a heteronormative gender binary that must be dismantled to truly transform the division of labor."

Nancy Hirschmann’s essay is at its most incisive when it troubles the easy assumption that celebrating care will, by itself, lead to a more just world. She is correct that care can be narrow, self-serving, and used to perpetuate hierarchy. But her analysis remains trapped within a heteronormative, gender-binary frame that both limits her critique and leads her to a reactionary conclusion. The failure of care feminism is not that it values care too highly but that it has not yet fully queered the question of care itself. Until we detach care from the binary categories of “mother” and “father,” “woman” and “man,” the project of transforming the sexual division of labor will stall on the same gendered logic it seeks to overturn.

Hirschmann’s world is populated almost entirely by heterosexual, cisgender couples. The problem she identifies is that women do the caring and men do not, and her solution is that women should stop doing so much care so that men are forced to pick up the slack. But this framing presumes that care is a zero-sum negotiation between two gendered partners, each with a fixed identity and a predictable set of interests. That assumption is belied by the actual diversity of family forms. Same-sex couples, for instance, often negotiate care work with far greater equity, in part because they are not operating under the weight of a gendered script that says one partner is naturally suited to nurture. The division of labor in lesbian and gay families is not perfect, but the research suggests that it is more egalitarian, and the reasons for it are instructive: when the cultural assignment of care is not preordained by biological sex, people fall into patterns based on negotiation, preference, and external constraints rather than destiny. To point this out is not to suggest that everyone must live in a queer household; it is to suggest that the heterosexual family is not a natural inevitability but a particular arrangement that can be denaturalized.

The remedy, then, is not to tell women to care less—a demand that still presumes they are the ones responsible for care—but to dismantle the symbolic and institutional linkages between care and femininity. As long as caring labor is marked as women’s work, whether done in the home or in the paid care sector, women who do it will be seen as confirming their nature, and men who do it will be seen as noble exceptions or suspiciously soft. True transformation requires changing the meaning of care itself, so that feeding, wiping, holding, and listening are understood as fully human activities, not as expressions of a gendered essence. That can happen only if we refuse to frame the issue as “women caring too much” and instead frame it as “people not caring enough, because we have built a world in which care is both devalued and feminized.”

The institutional implications of this shift are significant. Policies like parental leave should not only be available to all parents regardless of gender or marital status; they should be designed to circumvent the assumption that there is a primary and a secondary caregiver. Leave can be structured as individual, non-transferable entitlements, not as a family pool that will, in practice, be taken by the mother. But more fundamentally, we should stop thinking of care as something that happens primarily within the nuclear family at all. Extended kinship networks, communal living arrangements, and non-family-based care collectives have long provided alternative models of shared care, and feminists should champion their expansion. Why not imagine a world in which state subsidies support not just “mothers” but any adult who takes on substantial care responsibilities, without reference to biological or legal parentage? Such a system would sever care from the marital dyad and distribute its costs and rewards across a wider social field.

Hirschmann’s worry that public compensation invites public oversight is real, but it becomes more manageable when care is no longer the province of a single idealized figure. When everyone has a stake in care, and when care is performed by a variety of people in a variety of settings, the state’s monitoring gaze is diffused and the definition of “good care” becomes a democratic question rather than a pretext for disciplining maternal deviance.

This is not to deny that women, as a class, have borne and still bear an unfair share of care work, or that the power imbalance Hirschmann identifies is urgent. But the solution lies not in her plea for women to become more like men in their market behavior, but in a politics that refuses the gender binary altogether. We need a feminism that makes men care not by strategic withdrawal but by making the refusal to care socially impossible, not just for fathers but for every adult. We need to build a culture that expects men to be nurturers from the earliest age, and that extends the circle of care beyond the privatized household. In such a world, care would not be a woman’s burden or a mother’s perversion; it would just be life.
