"While care is an essential human value, it must be integrated with power, justice, and economic independence to prevent it from becoming a tool for gender domination and private entitlement."

Nancy Hirschmann is right to name a problem that many feminists have preferred to circle around: care can become a velvet glove for domination. It can be an ethic of responsibility, attentiveness, and mutuality; it can also be a private ideology of exemption. “I am caring for my child” too easily becomes “my child is exempt from the rules that bind other people’s children.” The mother who lies to a professor, the parent who imagines Paris as an appropriate pretrial diversion after a fatal drunken crash, and the family that treats plagiarism as an inconvenience on the way to Wall Street are not aberrations from the world of care. They are part of it.

That does not mean care feminism caused such behavior. Hirschmann is careful on this point, and it matters. The entitled parents in her examples did not need Carol Gilligan to teach them how to confuse love with indulgence. But Hirschmann’s deeper claim is harder to dismiss: if a theory elevates care without attending sufficiently to power, it may lend feminist language to deeply anti-feminist choices. “Mothering is the most important job in the world” can be a radical statement when it means that all human beings are dependent and that the work of meeting dependency deserves recognition. It can be a reactionary statement when it means that educated women should exit public life while men continue to accumulate money, authority, and civic standing.

The central weakness of idealized care talk is that it often treats the family as if it were naturally opposed to the market and the state: intimate, generous, humane. But families are also institutions of hierarchy. They distribute labor, money, time, voice, and opportunity. They train children not only in affection but in entitlement, obedience, prejudice, and gender. A girl who watches her mother manage everyone’s needs while her father’s work is treated as the “real” contribution learns a political lesson before she can spell politics. A boy who is chauffeured, defended, excused, and praised for minimal domestic competence learns one too.

Hirschmann’s strongest point is that women’s economic power matters. It is fashionable in some circles to say that feminism means honoring every woman’s choice equally. But choices do not float free. They are made in tax codes, marriage markets, school schedules, workplaces, religious communities, and kitchens where somebody has to make dinner. A highly educated woman may sincerely want to stay home; sincerity does not make the decision politically innocent. If enough women with education and social capital leave paid work or avoid leadership, institutions remain male-dominated, men’s caregiving remains optional, and employers continue to treat serious caregiving as a female deviation from the norm.

This is not an argument for contempt toward mothers at home. Contempt is politically useless and morally cheap. Many women leave paid work because the workplace is punitive, childcare is expensive, school days are short, husbands are uncooperative, and the arithmetic of a second income can be discouraging. Some children have disabilities or illnesses requiring extraordinary care. Some jobs are so demeaning that “opting out” is less a retreat from public power than a refusal of exploitation. But none of this requires feminists to pretend that every withdrawal from public life is a triumph of autonomy.

A better feminism would distinguish between valuing care and sentimentalizing maternal self-erasure. It would say: care is indispensable, and therefore no class of people should be assigned to it by gender. It would say: children need love, but also limits, accountability, and encounters with equal others. It would say: mothers deserve support, but support should be designed to reduce dependency on husbands, not deepen it. It would ask whether a policy encourages men to care, whether it preserves women’s independent income and retirement security, and whether it protects children from being treated as private property.

Hirschmann worries that if care is defined as a public good, public oversight follows. That concern is real but not decisive. We already regulate parenting at the extremes: abuse, neglect, compulsory education, vaccination requirements in many contexts, child labor laws, child support. The question is not whether the public has any interest in children, but how that interest is expressed. Paying mothers directly because they are mothers may invite surveillance and moralism, especially against poor women. But universal supports—health care, childcare subsidies, paid leave tied to individual workers, Social Security credits for caregiving years, high-quality public education—need not rest on inspecting whether each mother has produced a sufficiently civic-minded child.

Still, Hirschmann’s warning is valuable because maternalist policy has so often been two-tiered: subsidies for middle-class wives, discipline for poor mothers. The stay-at-home wife benefits from tax preferences and a husband’s employer-provided health insurance and is called devoted. The welfare mother receives meager public assistance and is called dependent. A feminism worthy of the name cannot bless the first arrangement and scold the second.

The most promising route is not to care less in the sense of becoming colder, but to privatize care less. Care must be detached from femininity and from the mother-child dyad as the master image of moral life. Fathers should be expected to take leave, attend parent conferences, miss meetings for fevers, know the pediatrician’s name, and clean the bathroom without applause. Workplaces should be organized on the assumption that workers have bodies and relationships. Schools and communities should help raise citizens rather than merely credential competitors. But women cannot wait for men, employers, and legislatures to become enlightened. They need bargaining power now.

This is where Hirschmann’s “tough love” is most persuasive. Women should not be asked to martyr themselves for feminism, but neither should feminism provide a soft-focus vocabulary for decisions that leave women dependent and men unchanged. A woman who keeps a foothold in paid work, insists on a genuine division of domestic labor, and refuses to become the family’s shock absorber is not betraying care. She is taking care seriously enough to demand that it be shared.

The goal is not to make every mother into a corporate lawyer or professor. It is to make dependency compatible with equality. That requires money, time, institutions, and cultural change. But it also requires telling the truth: love without justice becomes favoritism; care without power becomes servitude; motherhood without citizenship becomes a gilded cage for some women and a disciplinary cell for others. Hirschmann has performed a feminist service by refusing to confuse reverence for mothers with respect for women.
