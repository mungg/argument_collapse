Diversified Gemini

"The Democratic Party's ambitious economic agenda is destined to fail unless the party leadership stops sidestepping the blue-state governance crisis and directly confronts the exclusionary zoning and regulatory obstructionism practiced by its affluent suburban base."

In their assessment of the Democratic Party's ambitious turn toward industrial policy and massive federal investment, Jacob S. Hacker and Paul Pierson identify a crucial tension within the party's new "U-shaped" coalition: the politics of local control and "opportunity hoarding" in affluent blue-state suburbs. The authors correctly point out that exclusionary zoning and opposition to housing development in these areas drive up costs for the working-class voters who make up the other end of the Democratic base. However, Hacker and Pierson are far too forgiving of the Democratic establishment's decision to "sidestep" these local issues. In reality, the failure to confront the blue-state governance crisis—specifically the inability to build housing and infrastructure efficiently—threatens to unravel the entire Biden economic agenda.

Hacker and Pierson celebrate the multi-trillion-dollar price tags of the American Jobs Plan and the American Families Plan, viewing these numbers as proof of a renewed commitment to working-class prosperity. But in the contemporary United States, allocating federal dollars is only a fraction of the battle; the true bottleneck is state capacity. The density divide that the authors describe has created a scenario where the areas that most need affordable housing and modernized infrastructure—the booming, Democratic-controlled metro areas—are exactly the places where it is most difficult and expensive to build anything.

The authors refer to exclusionary zoning as a "localized, low-profile character" that allows national party leaders to keep it off the core agenda. This is a profound political and economic mistake. The housing affordability crisis is no longer a peripheral issue; it is the central driver of inequality in America. When the Biden administration attempts to boost working-class incomes through tax credits or wage supports, those gains are immediately swallowed by exorbitant rent increases in supply-constrained metropolitan areas. By refusing to leverage federal power to break the back of local NIMBYism (Not In My Backyard), the national Democratic Party is essentially pouring water into a leaky bucket.

Furthermore, the very voters driving the Democratic Party's suburban gains are the architects of this dysfunction. The affluent, college-educated voters in places like the San Francisco Bay Area, the Boston suburbs, and the New York metropolitan region profess progressive values on a national level but ruthlessly weaponize local environmental regulations and zoning codes to block apartment buildings, transit projects, and renewable energy infrastructure in their own communities.

This hypocrisy exposes a fatal flaw in the new Democratic economic agenda. The Biden administration has committed hundreds of billions of dollars to the clean energy transition through the Inflation Reduction Act. But transitioning the grid requires building massive amounts of new physical infrastructure: high-voltage transmission lines, solar farms, wind turbines, and battery storage facilities. Currently, blue-state environmental laws (like CEQA in California) and the federal National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA) are frequently used by affluent suburbanites and local activist groups to stall these projects for years, if not decades.

Hacker and Pierson applaud the administration’s aggressive regulatory stance, but they fail to recognize that the accumulation of regulatory veto points has crippled the government's ability to execute the industrial policy they so admire. You cannot have a successful "Bigger, Bolder, Broader" agenda if it takes ten years and three times the cost of European equivalents to build a single subway line or transmission cable.

The authors argue that keeping "opportunity hoarding" off the national agenda is a clever way to avoid fracturing the U-shaped coalition. But this strategy of evasion is morally bankrupt and politically short-sighted. The working-class voters of color at the bottom of the "U" are being systematically priced out of the nation's most economically productive regions. They are forced into extreme super-commutes from distant exurbs or pushed out of blue states entirely, migrating to red states in the Sunbelt where housing is cheaper but the social safety net is weaker.

If the Democratic Party wants to be the party of "multiracial, cross-class, metro America," it must actually make metro America livable for the working class. This requires a head-on confrontation with the affluent suburbanites in its own coalition. The federal government must use its spending power as a cudgel, conditioning transportation and infrastructure grants on local governments abolishing exclusionary zoning and streamlining permitting for critical infrastructure.

The "near miss" of the 2021 agenda wasn't just about Joe Manchin shrinking the top-line number. It was a failure to pair massive spending with the structural reforms necessary to ensure that money translates into tangible, physical realities. A party that claims to champion the working class cannot turn a blind eye to the mechanisms by which its wealthiest supporters extract rent and hoard opportunity. Hacker and Pierson's essay provides a brilliant map of the Democratic Party's electoral geography, but it fundamentally misjudges the gravity of the rot within blue-state governance. Until Democrats figure out how to build, their ambitious economic proposals will remain expensive illusions.
