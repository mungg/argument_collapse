"The presidential primary calendar should be reformed into a rotating system of small, demographically representative states to ensure the opening contests reflect the diversity of the modern American electorate."

Iowa and New Hampshire have virtues worth preserving: they are small enough for unknown candidates to meet voters face to face, cheap enough that money is not the only gatekeeper, and politically engaged enough to test organization. But those virtues do not justify a permanent entitlement. Their electorates are far whiter, older and more rural than the country, and Iowa’s caucus system further narrows participation to citizens with the time, flexibility and confidence to spend an evening in a public meeting. That is a poor opening filter for a diverse mass democracy.

The answer is not to let California, Texas or Florida vote first. A giant-state opening would reward celebrity, fundraising and television advertising even more than the current system does. The better principle is: small first, but representative first. Early states should include racial and ethnic diversity, urban and rural communities, different regional economies, and high-access primaries rather than low-turnout caucuses.

On that standard, Nevada and South Carolina deserve at least equal status with Iowa and New Hampshire, and arguably should precede them in some cycles. Nevada brings a growing Latino electorate, a significant Asian-American population, organized labor, service-industry workers, immigrants and the housing-boom-and-bust experience that shaped much of the modern economy. South Carolina gives black voters, especially in the Democratic Party, a meaningful early voice, and it tests Republicans in a Southern conservative electorate that has long been central to their coalition.

But no four states should own the calendar forever. The fairest reform would be a rotating early window: one small or mid-sized state from each region, chosen for demographic breadth, administrative competence and voter access, with no state allowed to repeat as first in consecutive cycles. Iowa and New Hampshire could remain in the rotation, but not as hereditary gatekeepers. States such as Nevada, South Carolina, New Mexico, Colorado, North Carolina or Virginia could serve in different cycles, depending on party rules and population balance.

Presidential nominations should begin with conversation, not coronation. But the conversation should start with America as it is, not with two states that look too much like America as it used to be.

Iowa and New Hampshire's privileged position is indefensible on representational grounds. Iowa is roughly 91 percent white, New Hampshire 94 percent; together they contain almost no Latino voters, very few Black voters, and few of the dense, multiethnic metros where most Americans actually live. A better system would pair a small, diverse state (Nevada or New Mexico) with a small, working-class one (perhaps South Carolina or Michigan's Upper Peninsula region), then rotate regional primaries thereafter so no state holds permanent veto power. Iowa and New Hampshire have earned their organizational expertise, but expertise isn't entitlement. A nominating calendar that begins where America actually looks like itself would produce nominees better matched to the November electorate.

"The presidential nominating process should be restructured to replace Iowa and New Hampshire as the permanent first voters with states or rotating groups that better reflect the nation's demographic and geographic diversity."

The ritual is as familiar as it is undemocratic: every four years, presidential hopefuls spend a year trudging through Iowa’s county fairs and New Hampshire’s town halls, genuflecting before two of the nation’s least representative electorates. The question of who votes first is not merely procedural—it shapes policy priorities, winnows fields, and often anoints front-runners before most of the country has uttered a single preference. If the goal of a nominating process is to produce a candidate who reflects the party’s coalition and can govern a diverse republic, Iowa and New Hampshire should not be the permanent gatekeepers.

The demographic distortions are glaring. Both states are over 90 percent white, whereas the Democratic primary electorate in 2008 was roughly one-third nonwhite, and the nation itself is far more heterogeneous. Iowa’s caucuses, conducted in an overwhelmingly rural setting, overrepresent a sliver of the base: older, more ideological activists who reward candidates fluent in agricultural subsidies and retail politicking. New Hampshire’s primary, with its libertarian streak and lack of large urban centers, presents a similarly narrow lens. The issues that dominate early coverage—ethanol mandates, the “first in the nation” pieties—bear little resemblance to the concerns of metropolitan voters, African Americans, Latinos, or the working poor who form critical blocs in November.

A more defensible sequence would move states that more accurately mirror the party’s rank and file to the front of the calendar. For Democrats, Nevada and South Carolina already offer a corrective, but they arrive third and fourth, after momentum has hardened. Nevada, with its large Latino and union populations, urban dynamics in Las Vegas, and western libertarian-conservatism, is a microcosm of emerging electoral battlegrounds. South Carolina’s primary—where African Americans cast a majority of Democratic ballots—tests a candidate’s ability to speak to the party’s most loyal constituency. Front-loading these contests would force aspirants to build genuinely multiracial coalitions from the start, rather than relying on a head start among homogeneous electorates and hoping minority voters coalesce later.

Republicans, too, would benefit from a reshuffling. South Carolina’s socially conservative, military-heavy GOP electorate is far more representative of the national Republican base than Iowa’s caucus-goers, who have a history of elevating insurgents with niche appeal. A first contest that included Sun Belt states with growing diversification, like Arizona or Georgia, would pressure candidates to address immigration and economic mobility in ways that resonate beyond the Upper Midwest.

The deepest flaw is entrenchment itself. Any state that consistently goes first accumulates disproportionate power in perpetuity. A superior model would rotate the early window among several states eager for the responsibility—perhaps through a lottery system that groups states by region and demographic profile. This would prevent single-state industries from developing around the primary calendar and compel candidates to campaign broadly from day one. The current order is a historical accident, not a sacred design. It is time to retire it and let the full diversity of the country shape the choice of nominees.

Vanilla MiniMax

"The current primary system should be replaced with a structured regional rotation system to preserve the benefits of retail politics while ensuring candidates are tested by a more representative and diverse electorate."

The argument that Iowa and New Hampshire wield too much influence rests on a valid concern about demographic representation, but the critique oversimplifies what makes these states useful gatekeepers. The real question isn't whether these states mirror the nation's diversity—they clearly don't—but whether they test qualities that predict electoral success.

Retail politics rewards candidates who can actually connect with voters face-to-face. In small, retail-focused states, name recognition and money matter less than organizational depth and genuine persuasion skills. A candidate who can't build a coalition in Iowa, where voter turnout requires sustained personal commitment, likely lacks the organizational muscle to win in November. This isn't nothing; it's a meaningful filter.

That said, the current system needs reform. Giving four or five states permanent outsized influence creates distortions. Candidates tailor their messages to a narrow demographic, and voters in the vast majority of states participate in what amounts to coronation ceremonies after the race is effectively decided.

A regional rotation system would preserve the retail-politics benefit while improving representativeness. Dividing the country into regions—Northeast, Southeast, Midwest, West, Southwest—each holding early primaries on a rotating basis would expose candidates to diverse electoral environments. A candidate might face a union-heavy Midwestern primary, then a Sun Belt Hispanic voters primary, then a Northeastern suburban primary. This would better test a candidate's ability to build different coalitions.

South Carolina's recent addition demonstrated that the system can diversify. Adding Nevada, with its diverse union electorate, further helped. But incremental adjustments leave us with an awkward patchwork. A structured rotation would be more systematic and more fair.

The states voting first don't need to be perfectly representative. They need to test skills that translate to national victory. The current selection fails that test on representativeness without fully serving the practical function either. A regional rotation would better achieve both goals.

Tomorrow's New Hampshire primary, following last week's closely watched Iowa caucuses, once again highlights a recurring flaw in our presidential nominating process: the disproportionate influence of two largely white, rural states. The United States in 2016 is a highly diverse, predominantly urban and suburban nation. Allowing Iowa and New Hampshire to serve as the ultimate gatekeepers forces candidates to tailor their initial platforms to a demographic anomaly rather than the broader American electorate, skewing early momentum.

The traditional defense of this arrangement is that small, relatively inexpensive states force candidates into "retail politics." Supporters argue that if a large state like California or Texas went first, only heavily funded, established candidates could compete, relying on expensive television buys rather than intimate town halls. While there is undeniable value in testing a candidate’s ability to connect with everyday voters face-to-face, retail politics does not mandate that the exact same two states hold a monopoly every four years. We can preserve grassroots campaigning while introducing demographic realities.

A more equitable solution would be a rotating primary system featuring a shifting mixture of small to medium-sized states that better reflect the nation’s diverse electorate. It is true that Nevada and South Carolina currently follow Iowa and New Hampshire specifically to inject Latino and African American voices into the early process. However, voters in those states are still left reacting to the media narratives, fundraising booms, and candidate momentum established by the first two contests.

If a single state were to replace Iowa at the front of the pack, a midwestern microcosm like Illinois would be a far better bellwether. Illinois features a balanced mix of major urban centers, sprawling suburbs, and rural agricultural districts, closely mirroring the racial, economic, and industrial diversity of the contemporary United States. Alternatively, the national parties could group a rotating set of four smaller, diverse states—perhaps one from each major geographic region—to vote simultaneously on opening day. It is past time to design a primary calendar that actually looks like the nation it seeks to lead.

The framing of this debate misses the real problem. The question is not which two or four states should go first, but why we let any two states dominate the calendar at all. The American primary system is a Rube Goldberg machine, jury-rigged over decades by parties trying to balance grassroots input, elite control, and state pride. Small states would still go first—giving the retail-politics virtue some breathing room—but no single small state would always go first.

Or use a rotating regional primary, with four regions and the leadoff region changing every four years.

Any of these is better than what we have, because any of these distributes attention, vetting, and influence more evenly. The current calendar is defended by people who benefit from it: the Iowa and New Hampshire political classes, the consultants who specialize in those states, the journalists who have built sources there, and the ethanol lobby. The interests it serves are not democratic interests.

The real obstacle is collective action. Both parties have to move together, or one state defects and the cascade resumes. But the RNC and DNC have coordinated on calendar rules before (the "window" rule, penalties for jumping the queue). They could do it again if their leadership decided that demographic representativeness and democratic equality matter more than tradition. So far, they have not.

"The current primary calendar should be replaced with a system of rotating regional primaries to eliminate the permanent privilege of specific states while maintaining a sequential voting structure."

The quarrel over whether Iowa and New Hampshire “deserve” to vote first is a symptom of a deeper malady: a rigid, accidental calendar that no one designed. Instead of swapping out two small states for another, we should fundamentally reimagine the order through a system of rotating regional primaries. This would end the permanent privilege of any single state while preserving the sequential framework that allows for scrutiny and momentum.

Here’s how it could work. Divide the country into four large regions—Northeast, South, Midwest, and West. Every four years, one region would host the opening contests, with its states voting on staggered dates over a two-to-three-week window before the rest of the nation joins in. The next cycle, a different region leads off. To preserve the value of intimate campaigning, the parties could mandate that the very first contest within the lead-off region be held in its smallest state by population—say, Delaware in the Northeast, Mississippi in the South, Nebraska in the Midwest, or Wyoming in the West. This keeps the door open for underfunded insurgents while ensuring that no single state or its particular obsessions dominate cycle after cycle.

This approach has multiple virtues. First, it spreads the economic bonanza and political attention more fairly. Second, it forces candidates to confront distinct regional challenges: water rights in the West one cycle, deindustrialization in the Midwest the next, coastal infrastructure in the Northeast another. The result would be a nominee whose skills and message had been tested against the full spectrum of American life over time, rather than someone who merely mastered the quirks of the eastern Iowa and New Hampshire electorates. Rotating regions is a reform that both honors the tradition of sequential contests and corrects its profound geographic and demographic myopia.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A rotating system ensures that no single state or demographic group can dominate the national political conversation cycle after cycle.
  2. 02Mandating that the smallest state within a lead-off region votes first preserves the ability for underfunded insurgent candidates to compete through retail politics.
  3. 03Rotating the lead-off region forces candidates to engage with a broader and more diverse set of regional policy issues over time.
  4. 04This reform more equitably distributes the economic benefits and media attention associated with early primary contests across the entire country.
Diversified MiniMax

"The presidential primary calendar should be reformed to replace the disproportionate influence of Iowa and New Hampshire with a system that reflects the nation's demographic and geographic diversity."

The case for changing the primary calendar starts with basic math. Iowa and New Hampshire together represent roughly 1% of America's eligible voters. Their combined impact on candidate viability is instantaneous and outsized—a poor showing in either state can end a campaign before it truly begins.

This isn't rocket science. The first-primary states should reflect America's geographic and demographic diversity, not the accident of small-state privilege enshrined in a 1972 Democratic Party rule change. Why should two rural, overwhelmingly white states hold veto power over candidates who must eventually appeal to a nation that looks nothing like them?

The logical reform is a rotating regional primary system. Divide the country into regions—Northeast, South, Midwest, West—and rotate which region votes first each election cycle. Every four years, a different cross-section of voters gets meaningful first-influence. This alone would force candidates to build genuinely national coalitions from day one rather than gaming one quirks-heavy format.

Alternatively, let the megastates lead. Florida, Texas, and California represent tens of millions of voters across every demographic category. A strong finish in any of them carries information about electability that a 99%-white Iowa caucus simply cannot provide.

Critics say large states favor wealthy candidates and early favorites. True—but the current system favors candidates who know how to work Iowa's peculiar caucus math, which is hardly a transferable skill to general elections. At least big-state primaries force broad-based coalition building that mirrors November's actual contest.

The parties have every reason to resist reform: incumbents benefit from the current chaos that eliminates viable challengers. But for democracy's sake, we should want a system where voting power correlates roughly with population and diversity.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Iowa and New Hampshire represent a negligible fraction of the electorate and lack the diversity necessary to test a candidate's national appeal.
  2. 02A rotating regional primary system would ensure that different cross-sections of the American public have the opportunity to exert early influence every four years.
  3. 03Prioritizing large, diverse states like Florida, Texas, or California would provide more accurate data regarding a candidate's general election viability.
  4. 04The current system rewards niche campaigning skills that do not translate to the broad-based coalition building required for a general election.
  5. 05The existing primary structure persists primarily because it protects incumbents and established party interests rather than serving democratic principles.
Diversified Claude

"The current primary system should be replaced with a rotating four-state lineup that reflects the demographic and geographic diversity of the entire United States."

Replace them with the states that look like America.

The simplest version of the reform argument: if the point of a primary is to choose a nominee who can win a general election and govern a diverse country, the voters who choose that nominee should look something like the country. They don't have to mirror it perfectly, but they shouldn't be a caricature of one slice of it. Iowa and New Hampshire are a caricature. Together they have fewer Black residents than the city of Atlanta and fewer Latino residents than the San Fernando Valley.

What states would I put first? I'd choose four, rotating, with the following criteria: each cycle's early four should include at least one Southern state with a substantial Black population, at least one Western or Southwestern state with a substantial Latino population, at least one Midwestern industrial state, and at least one smaller state where retail politics is still feasible. Possible early-four lineups: South Carolina, Nevada, Michigan, and New Hampshire one cycle; Georgia, Arizona, Wisconsin, and Maine the next; North Carolina, New Mexico, Ohio, and Iowa the cycle after.

Notice that Iowa and New Hampshire are still in the rotation. The point is not to punish them; it is to end their monopoly. Notice also that I have included one mid-sized state (Michigan, Wisconsin, Ohio) in each lineup. The fetishization of pure retail politics has gone too far. Voters in a state of nine or ten million can still meet candidates at events; they just have to do it alongside television advertising, which is how general elections actually work.

The objection is always logistics: candidates can't afford to compete in Michigan the way they can in Iowa. But that is an artifact of campaign finance rules, not a law of nature. Pair calendar reform with public financing or free broadcast time for candidates who clear a threshold, and the logistical problem shrinks.

The case for the current system rests on inertia and on the romance of the New Hampshire town hall. The case against it rests on democratic equality and demographic representativeness. The second case is stronger. It is past time to act on it.

  1. 01Iowa and New Hampshire are demographically unrepresentative of the country and do not adequately test a candidate's ability to govern a diverse nation.
  2. 02A rotating system including Southern, Western, Midwestern, and small states would ensure that Black, Latino, and industrial voters have a decisive early voice.
  3. 03Ending the monopoly of Iowa and New Hampshire allows for a better balance between retail politics and the large-scale media campaigning required for general elections.
  4. 04Logistical and financial barriers to campaigning in larger states can be mitigated through campaign finance reform or public funding rather than maintaining an unrepresentative calendar.
  5. 05The principles of democratic equality and demographic representativeness outweigh the traditional arguments for maintaining the current primary order.
Diversified Gemini

"The current presidential nominating process should be replaced with a rotating regional primary system to ensure demographic and geographic fairness."

It is well past time to end the monopoly that Iowa and New Hampshire hold over the presidential nominating process. The argument that we must preserve these states to protect "retail politics" is a smokescreen for a system that fundamentally disenfranchises the diverse coalition of voters that makes up modern America. If we want a primary system that actually reflects the country, we need to move to a rotating regional primary.

Currently, the early states exert outsize influence through the power of "momentum." A candidate who wins the largely white, rural Iowa caucuses gains millions of dollars in earned media and fundraising momentum, while candidates who might appeal to a broader, more diverse electorate are starved of resources and forced to drop out before voters in the South or West even get a say. This dynamic forces candidates to pander to highly specific regional interests—like ethanol subsidies—while ignoring urban infrastructure or issues central to communities of color.

Under a rotating regional primary system, the country would be divided into four regions: Northeast, South, Midwest, and West. Each presidential election year, a different region would vote first, with the other three following at one-month intervals.

This system solves multiple problems at once. First, it ensures that over a sixteen-year cycle, every voter in the country gets the chance to participate in the crucial early stages of the election. Second, it balances demographic representation, as candidates would have to campaign across entire regions encompassing urban centers, suburbs, and rural areas, engaging with Black, Latino, and Asian-American voters from day one. Finally, it reduces the absurdly prolonged nature of the current primary calendar, which exhausts voters and candidates alike.

Our democracy shouldn't be held hostage by the historical quirks of two small states. A rotating regional primary brings fairness, diversity, and geographic equity to a deeply flawed process.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current system gives disproportionate influence to Iowa and New Hampshire, forcing candidates to drop out before diverse electorates can weigh in.
  2. 02A rotating regional primary ensures that every voter in the country eventually has the opportunity to participate in the influential early stages of the election.
  3. 03A regional approach forces candidates to engage with a more representative demographic mix of urban, suburban, and rural voters from the start of their campaigns.
  4. 04Moving to a regional primary schedule would shorten the election cycle and reduce the exhaustion caused by the current prolonged calendar.
