Diversified Claude

"Instead of attempting to differentiate between classes through complex tax preferences, the federal government should broaden the tax base and target the genuinely wealthy through direct structural reforms."

Stop Drawing Lines and Broaden the Base

The premise of the question is itself the problem. The American tax code is a thicket of carve-outs, credits, deductions, and phase-outs precisely because we have spent forty years trying to use it to differentiate among groups of taxpayers — rich, middle, poor, parents, students, homeowners, savers, retirees. The 529 episode is a perfect illustration of where this leads: a preference is created, a constituency forms around it, and any future attempt to repeal it is denounced as a tax hike on the middle class, regardless of who actually benefits.

A better approach would stop trying to micro-target. Broaden the base, lower the rates, eliminate most preferences, and use a small number of clearly progressive marginal brackets plus a refundable credit at the bottom to handle distributional concerns. The Tax Reform Act of 1986 — under a Republican president and a Democratic House — moved in this direction and was widely admired. Since then, we have undone most of its work by piling preferences back into the code.

If the President wants to address inequality, he has cleaner tools than tinkering with 529s. Raise the top marginal rate. Tax capital gains and dividends as ordinary income above some threshold. Close the carried-interest loophole. End the step-up in basis at death, which lets enormous fortunes pass untaxed across generations. Each of these touches the genuinely wealthy without affecting the dentist in Cincinnati saving for her daughter's tuition.

The reason these proposals are politically harder than killing 529s is instructive. The genuinely wealthy are well-represented in Washington; the upper-middle class is loud. The path of least resistance has been to nibble at upper-middle-class preferences while leaving the real fortunes alone. That is not progressive reform. It is tax policy theater, and the public is right to be skeptical.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current tax code is overly complex because it attempts to micro-target specific groups, leading to the creation of entrenched constituencies that resist reform.
  2. 02A more effective system would combine lower rates and fewer preferences with a small number of progressive marginal brackets and refundable credits for the poor.
  3. 03True income inequality should be addressed by targeting high-level wealth through measures like raising top marginal rates and ending the step-up in basis at death rather than tinkering with middle-class savings accounts.
  4. 04Current policy focuses on minor upper-middle-class preferences because it is politically easier than challenging the well-represented interests of the truly wealthy.
Diversified MiniMax

"Federal tax policy should move toward a simplified system with fewer brackets and deductions rather than attempting to define and distinguish between the rich and the middle class."

The Obama administration's 529 retreat revealed something important: the political class cannot clearly define who the rich are because the category is inherently political theater. What we witnessed was not careful policy analysis but class-warfare rhetoric colliding with electoral reality.

Consider the absurdity of the $200,000 threshold. In Manhattan, a family earning that amount may struggle with housing costs. In rural Kansas, the same income represents genuine prosperity. Washington cannot legislate geographic equity, yet it keeps trying to draw national lines that ignore local realities.

The deeper problem is that "middle class" has become a rhetorical shield. Any tax increase gets labeled an assault on ordinary Americans while any deduction gets defended as helping those same Americans. Politicians use these terms strategically, not analytically. The 529 debate demonstrated this perfectly: the accounts do primarily benefit upper earners, yet defenders immediately reframed them as middle-class savings vehicles.

Rather than continuing to draw increasingly arbitrary lines, we should simplify the tax code dramatically. Fewer brackets, fewer deductions, lower rates. This approach avoids the need to constantly adjudicate who counts as rich—it asks everyone to contribute at rates that reflect ability without pretending we can neutrally define economic categories.

The class-warfare approach to taxation may generate good headlines, but it produces incoherent policy. We should reject the premise that good tax policy requires distinguishing people by income category.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The definitions of 'rich' and 'middle class' are used as tools for political theater and strategic rhetoric rather than for objective economic analysis.
  2. 02National income thresholds fail to account for significant geographic variations in the cost of living and local economic realities.
  3. 03The term 'middle class' acts as a rhetorical shield that allows politicians to defend tax breaks for high earners by reframing them as benefits for ordinary Americans.
  4. 04A simplified tax code with lower rates and fewer deductions eliminates the need for the government to constantly adjudicate arbitrary economic categories.
