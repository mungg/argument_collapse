Diversified MiniMax

"Community-based, volunteer-driven security models are insufficient for the modern internet because users act as consumers in a market rather than as a cohesive civic community."

The essay's most poignant passage is its invocation of the "neighborhood watch" as a model for Internet security. I grew up in a neighborhood where the watch existed on paper and in newsletters but never actually deterred crime. The watch worked only when crime was visible, when neighbors knew each other, and when the police were responsive. None of these conditions hold on the Internet.

Zittrain wants us to believe that community-based solutions can substitute for structural reform. They cannot, and the reasons are social rather than technical. The early Internet worked because its users formed a community of sorts—small, self-selected, and united by shared expertise and shared values. This community produced norms that constrained bad behavior. It also produced the Morris worm, which suggests that the norms were never as robust as we remember.

The mass Internet has destroyed the conditions for communal governance. Users are not a community; they are a market. They are connected by commercial relationships rather than social ones. Their participation is mediated by platforms—Google, Facebook, Amazon—that have every incentive to extract value from their attention and no incentive to cultivate their civic capacities. The neighborhood watch required neighbors who knew each other. On the Internet, we are strangers passing in the dark.

Zittrain acknowledges this but offers hope: "tools that allow us to express and live our civic instincts online." The problem is that we do not have civic instincts online, or at least not the kind that Zittrain imagines. We have the instincts of consumers. We want things to work. We want them to be safe. We do not want to contribute our bandwidth and processing power to collective security experiments, because that would require us to understand what we are contributing and why, and most of us cannot.

The essay's vision of "netizens" is a fantasy projected onto users who have never identified as such. Wikipedia works because it offers contributors recognition, intellectual satisfaction, and the occasional ego boost. It does not work because contributors feel a civic duty to the encyclopedia. Zittrain's proposed security tools would offer no such satisfactions. They would ask users to contribute data about their own systems' vulnerabilities for the benefit of strangers. This is not civic participation. It is unpaid labor for an abstract collective good.

The real question is whether we can create institutions that make civic participation attractive rather than obligatory. The answer lies not in tools but in incentives. Zittrain's Herdict project might attract participants if it offered them something—reputation, discounts on security products, social connections. But he presents it as a pure public good, sustained by the generosity of strangers. This is not how public goods work.

We need security institutions that are funded, accountable, and effective. This means commercial security firms, regulated by consumer protection law, competing on the basis of actual protection rather than marketing. It means software vendors held liable for preventable vulnerabilities. It means ISPs that bear some responsibility for the traffic that flows through their networks. These are not glamorous solutions. They do not tap the power of community or express our civic instincts. But they might actually work.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The social conditions of the early internet that allowed for communal governance have been destroyed by the scale and commercial nature of the mass internet.
  2. 02Internet users lack the 'civic instincts' necessary to participate in collective security experiments, preferring safety and convenience over active technical contribution.
  3. 03Zittrain’s comparison to Wikipedia is flawed because security monitoring lacks the social rewards and intellectual satisfaction that motivate Wikipedia contributors.
  4. 04Proposed tools like Herdict fail to provide the necessary incentives to transform unpaid data-sharing into a sustainable public good.
  5. 05Effective internet security requires formal, accountable institutions—such as regulated commercial firms and liable software vendors—rather than a reliance on the generosity of strangers.
Diversified MiniMax

"Zittrain’s vision of a communal, generative Internet is a nostalgic fantasy that ignores the reality that market forces and commercial products are more effective at providing security and utility than collective civic action."

Zittrain's essay reads like a eulogy for a world that never quite existed. The generative Internet of his imagination—a space of open experimentation governed by shared norms and civic spirit—was always a minoritarian project. The mass Internet of the 1990s and 2000s was never governed by hacker ethics. It was a commercial space from the beginning, and it worked reasonably well precisely because commercial incentives aligned with user interests in ways that Zittrain's communal vision never could.

The PC revolution was not, primarily, a revolution in openness. It was a revolution in convenience. The Apple II was successful not because it was a blank slate but because it was a finished product that people could buy and use. The spreadsheet made the PC indispensable to businesses. The web browser made it indispensable to everyone else. Openness was a feature of the early PC, but it was never the reason most people bought one.

Zittrain correctly identifies that the three historical controls on malware have weakened. But his diagnosis is incomplete. The hacker ethos weakened not because the Internet community grew too large but because it was never as strong as he remembers. Morris was not a principled rebel—he was a careless graduate student who got lucky. The few genuinely malicious viruses of the 1990s were written by people who operated outside any community norm. The community that Zittrain mourns was always smaller and less influential than he implies.

The real lesson of the past twenty years is that market forces, not communal norms, have delivered the security improvements we have seen. Antivirus software, firewalls, and secure browsing features are products. Their development has been driven by consumer demand and competitive pressure. The malware problem is serious, but it is less serious than it would be in a world without commercial security products.

Zittrain's proposed solutions suffer from a common flaw: they require collective action that the market provides more efficiently. The Herdict project depends on users voluntarily reporting data about their systems' behavior. But users already have antivirus software that does this. They already have automatic updates that patch vulnerabilities. The marginal value of community reporting is small compared to the value of professional security research, which the market funds.

The essay's vision of "netizens" who "identify and belong" to the generative Internet is nostalgia dressed as prophecy. Real Internet users identify with their email providers, their social networks, and their streaming services. They do not identify with the protocol layer, and asking them to do so is not a realistic basis for security policy.

The end-to-end principle should be defended, not qualified. Zittrain suggests that "strict loyalty to end-to-end neutrality should give way to a new principle" that permits ISP filtering. But the reason we have end-to-end neutrality is that centralized control is more dangerous than distributed risk. An ISP that screens viruses also knows what you are doing. That knowledge can be abused by the ISP, by hackers who compromise it, or by governments that subpoena its records. The cure is worse than the disease.

The generative Internet does not need saving by netizens. It needs to be left alone.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The success of the PC revolution was driven by convenience and finished commercial products rather than a widespread desire for open experimentation.
  2. 02The 'hacker ethos' and communal norms Zittrain mourns were never strong or influential enough to serve as a reliable foundation for network security.
  3. 03Market-driven solutions like professional antivirus software and automatic updates are more efficient and effective than voluntary, community-based reporting tools like Herdict.
  4. 04Most Internet users identify with specific commercial services and platforms rather than the underlying protocols or a sense of 'netizen' identity.
  5. 05Qualifying the end-to-end principle to allow ISP filtering creates dangerous precedents for surveillance and centralized control that outweigh the risks of distributed malware.
  6. 06The generative Internet does not require a new social or legal strategy to survive; it simply needs to be left alone to function through existing market incentives.
