Let the Market Decide What "Generative" Is Worth

Jonathan Zittrain's essay is animated by an assumption that deserves to be challenged directly: that consumers who choose locked-down devices over generative PCs are making a choice they would not make if they understood what they were giving up. He acknowledges that "it is entirely reasonable for consumers to factor security and stability into their choice," but then adds, "it is an undesirable choice to have to make." The whole prescriptive program — appliance neutrality mandates, data portability rules, ISP responsibilities, statute-of-limitations tweaks for software copyright — flows from a conviction that the market, left to its own devices, will deliver a worse outcome than enlightened policy could deliver.

I think this is wrong as both economics and politics, and I want to defend a different position: that the rise of appliances alongside generative PCs is a healthy market response to genuine consumer preferences, that the regulatory remedies Zittrain proposes are likely to do more harm than good, and that the generative Internet's best protection lies not in clever legal architecture but in the continuing usefulness of generative platforms to the people who actually value them.

Begin with what the data tell us. PCs are not disappearing. Worldwide PC shipments grew roughly 13 percent in 2007. The Linux community is healthier than at any point in its history, with Ubuntu pushing toward genuine mass-market viability. Web development tools have proliferated; the cost of starting a Web service has collapsed; cloud computing services like Amazon EC2 (launched 2006) have made the resources needed to build generative platforms accessible to teenagers with credit cards. The thesis that the PC and the open Internet are being eclipsed by appliances has to confront an awkward fact: every category of generative activity Zittrain cares about is growing, not shrinking. Blog creation, open source contributions, indie game development, podcasting, Web 2.0 mashups — all up sharply since 2003.

What is also growing is the appliance market. iPods sold roughly 50 million units last year. The DVR has become standard equipment. Smartphones are reshaping mobile communication. But this is not a substitution story; it is a complementarity story. The same household that bought a TiVo also bought a laptop. The teenager with an iPod also runs Firefox. Appliances are filling niches that PCs filled poorly — they are not eating PCs alive. To treat the success of appliances as a threat to the PC ecosystem is to misread the market structure. Appliances and PCs occupy different parts of the consumer surplus curve, and consumers happily buy both.

Why might consumers reasonably prefer appliances for some purposes? Because the marginal generativity Zittrain wants them to value is worth less to most people than the convenience and reliability the appliance delivers. This is not false consciousness. It is the ordinary operation of revealed preference. The retiree who wants to read email and look at grandchildren photos derives almost no value from the ability to write and install a kernel module. The teenager who wants to play music does not need her iPod to also be a development environment. The appliance is a rational specialization, and the existence of specialized devices does not threaten the existence of general-purpose ones any more than the existence of toasters threatens the existence of ovens.

Now consider Zittrain's proposed remedies. "Appliance neutrality" — a legal regime obligating platforms that have accepted third-party code to continue doing so — sounds elegant but is administratively a nightmare. When has a platform "welcomed" third-party contribution sufficiently to trigger the obligation? How does a regulator decide that a security-motivated restriction on third-party code is really a bait-and-switch? The Microsoft antitrust case Zittrain analogizes to consumed a decade of litigation and produced ambiguous results; replicating that machinery across every consumer device category would impose enormous costs while delivering uncertain benefits. The result would be predictable: platforms would refuse to open at all, lest opening trigger ongoing legal obligations they cannot quantify in advance. The mandate that aims to preserve generativity would, by its own incentive structure, reduce it.

Mandatory data portability has similar problems. As a default rule it is sensible, and most major services have moved in this direction voluntarily — Google's Data Liberation Front, Yahoo's data export tools, and the various OpenID initiatives are evidence that competitive pressure is doing the work. A legal mandate, by contrast, would have to define what "data" means in contexts where the very concept is contested (Is your social graph yours? Is metadata derived from your behavior yours?). And it would freeze the answer at whatever point in the evolution of services regulators happen to codify.

The deeper objection is to the underlying premise that "we" — meaning policymakers — need to step in to preserve a particular architectural vision. The generative Internet was not built by policy. It was built by hobbyists, researchers, entrepreneurs, and standards bodies operating largely without government direction. When governments did intervene — antitrust against AT&T in 1982, the NSFNET acceptable-use policy, the limited-liability provisions of Section 230 — the interventions were structurally modest and aimed at removing barriers to private action rather than mandating particular outcomes. Zittrain's proposals tilt toward the more ambitious kind of intervention, the kind that purports to shape technological development in the public interest. The track record of such interventions in fast-moving industries is poor.

What should we do instead? Mostly, what we are already doing. Defend Section 230 and the DMCA's safe harbor provisions, which preserve room for new platforms to emerge without crippling intermediary liability. Resist regulatory capture by incumbent telcos who would prefer to ration network access. Reform software patents, which are a real and growing burden on small developers. Keep antitrust law alive as a backstop against monopolization. And, mostly, trust that the same conditions that produced the generative Internet — low barriers to entry, plural platforms, distributed expertise, and a competitive market — will continue to produce it, so long as we do not over-engineer the rules.

Zittrain's essay reads as if the generative Internet were a delicate plant that could die at any moment unless tended by skilled regulators. I think it is closer to a weed: hardy, opportunistic, and remarkably good at finding cracks in the pavement. We should be careful not to pull it out by trying too hard to help it grow.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Market data shows that generative activity and PC shipments are growing alongside the appliance market, suggesting the two categories are complementary rather than mutually exclusive.
  2. 02Consumers make a rational, informed choice when they prioritize the convenience and reliability of appliances over the marginal value of a device's generativity.
  3. 03Proposed 'appliance neutrality' mandates would create administrative nightmares and discourage platform owners from ever opening their devices to third-party code to avoid permanent legal obligations.
  4. 04Mandatory data portability rules risk freezing technological evolution and are unnecessary because competitive pressures are already driving major services toward voluntary data liberation.
  5. 05The generative Internet flourished through private action and modest barrier-removal rather than ambitious government shaping, and it remains hardy enough to survive without over-engineered legal protections.
  6. 06Policy efforts should focus on existing protections like intermediary safe harbors and patent reform rather than new, prescriptive regulations that attempt to dictate technological architecture.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Zittrain defines 'generative power' too narrowly, focusing on the whim of end-users to customize tools rather than the broader creative potential of computing.
  2. 02The shift toward less flexible, safer computing is a rational response to the real costs of digital mischief like viruses and spam.
  3. 03There is no empirical evidence or logical basis provided to support the prediction that society or the market will systematically overreact by prioritizing security over flexibility.
  4. 04Suppliers of hardware and services have natural market incentives to continue offering flexibility as long as users actually value it.
  5. 05The essay relies on a sentimental reverence for the early days of 'writing code' rather than a clear demonstration of the social value lost by moving toward more secure, specialized appliances.
  6. 06Without a specific analysis of the resources required and the benefits gained, Zittrain’s proposals cannot be judged as worthwhile compared to other social priorities.
