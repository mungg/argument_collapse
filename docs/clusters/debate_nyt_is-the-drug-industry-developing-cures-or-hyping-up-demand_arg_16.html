"Reducing the pharmaceutical industry's influence over medical treatments requires enforcing strict ethical boundaries and accountability within the medical profession, as doctors serve as the ultimate gatekeepers for prescriptions."

The debate over pharmaceutical influence frequently treats patients as helpless victims and drug companies as omnipotent puppet masters. But this framework entirely ignores the crucial gatekeepers in our medical system: the physicians. If A.D.H.D. drugs are being prescribed beyond what is medically sensible, the fault lies squarely with the doctors holding the prescription pads.

Pharmaceutical companies cannot sell a single pill of Adderall or Ritalin without a doctor’s signature. Therefore, reducing industry influence requires focusing on the medical profession's ethical boundaries. For decades, the pharmaceutical industry has woven itself into the fabric of medical practice through aggressive detailing, sponsoring Continuing Medical Education (CME) courses, and paying doctors lucrative consulting and speaking fees. When the doctor educating a pediatrician about A.D.H.D. is on the payroll of the company manufacturing the treatment, the conflict of interest is glaring.

Fortunately, we are standing at a critical juncture for transparency. The Physician Payments Sunshine Act, part of the Affordable Care Act, has just begun collecting data this year (2013) on the financial relationships between drug companies and doctors. By 2014, patients will be able to go online and see exactly how much money their doctor has received from the manufacturer of the A.D.H.D. drug they are recommending.

But transparency is only the first step. To truly reduce industry influence, medical schools and professional societies must build firewalls between themselves and drug money. Hospitals and private practices should ban pharmaceutical sales representatives from bringing lunches to staff, a psychological tactic proven to influence prescribing habits. Medical boards must require that CME credits be earned from independent, non-industry-funded sources.

Drug companies will always try to maximize their market share; it is their fiduciary duty to their shareholders. It is the ethical duty of the medical profession to act solely in the best interest of the patient. By enforcing strict conflict-of-interest rules and demanding physician accountability, we can ensure that medical science, not marketing, dictates who gets an A.D.H.D. diagnosis.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The primary responsibility for over-prescription lies with physicians because pharmaceutical companies cannot sell medication without a doctor's signature.
  2. 02The industry has compromised medical objectivity by embedding itself into physician education through sponsored courses and lucrative consulting fees.
  3. 03Increased transparency regarding financial relationships between doctors and drug companies, such as those mandated by the Physician Payments Sunshine Act, allows for greater public accountability.
  4. 04Medical institutions must establish formal firewalls, such as banning pharmaceutical sales representatives and requiring independent funding for continuing medical education, to eliminate conflicts of interest.
  5. 05While drug companies are driven by shareholder profit, the medical profession has a specific ethical duty to ensure that patient care is dictated by science rather than marketing.
