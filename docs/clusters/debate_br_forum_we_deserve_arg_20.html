"The democratic deficit in AI should be addressed not by abandoning instrumental reason in favor of 'ecological reason,' but by democratizing the process of setting the goals that AI systems pursue."

Morozov’s essay is a provocative attempt to diagnose AI’s democratic deficits by tracing them to a foundational attachment to instrumental reason. He wants us to abandon the teleological mindset of goal-oriented problem solving in favor of what he calls ecological reason, a mode of intelligence that is open-ended, context-sensitive, and curiously playful. It is a seductive vision, but it rests on a philosophical mistake. Instrumental reason is not the enemy Morozov imagines, and pretending we can transcend it leads not to liberation but to political confusion. The real challenge for democracy is not to escape goal-oriented thinking, but to democratize the setting of goals themselves.

All human action, when examined closely, is teleological. Even the idlest curiosity serves purposes—the satisfaction of wonder, the expansion of one’s repertoire for future challenges, the sheer pleasure of mental play. These are goals, just not narrowly utilitarian ones. Morozov’s flâneur, strolling the stonefield “thinking preferably about nothing at all,” is still engaged in purposeful activity: the very noticing of the stone is a directedness toward a possible use, a nascent project. The distinction between design and eolithism is therefore not one of presence or absence of telos, but of how fixed and predetermined the telos is. Design starts with a clearly specified end; eolithism lets the end emerge from the encounter with means. But both are forms of goal-directed intelligence, adapted to different circumstances. There is no pure idling that is not, in some broad sense, oriented toward something.

This matters because Morozov’s critique of AI’s origins risks throwing out the baby with the bathwater. He chastises the early pioneers for modeling intelligence on the bureaucratic organization man, and he is right that this was a narrowing. But the mistake was not that they pursued goal-oriented problem solving at all; it was that they pursued a particular, impoverished set of goals under the sway of the Efficiency Lobby. The democratic alternative is not to abandon problem solving but to expand the range of problems recognized as legitimate and to open the process of problem definition to public deliberation. In Deweyan pragmatism—which Morozov himself invokes approvingly—intelligence is precisely the capacity to transform a troubled situation into a resolved one, a process that involves both defining the problem and experimenting with solutions. Dewey never called for a flight from instrumental reason; he called for its socialization.

What would that look like in practice? It would mean building institutions that enable citizens to identify shared problems—from climate change to housing shortages to algorithmic discrimination—and to bring to bear the best available knowledge, including AI, to address them. It would treat AI not as a black-box problem solver but as a tool that can aid in modeling consequences, surfacing trade-offs, and informing deliberation. This is not so far from what Morozov himself admires in the Bariloche Foundation’s numerical experimentation. That project was thoroughly teleological: it aimed to chart alternative development paths in order to help societies choose among them. The goals were democratically contestable, but they were goals nonetheless. To call such a project “ecological reason” is to blur the fact that it was an exercise in collective means-ends reasoning.

Morozov’s allergic reaction to instrumental reason also leads him to undervalue the genuine progress that has been made in AI governance. The push for algorithmic impact assessments, participatory design processes, and public auditing of models is not about accepting the status quo; it is about subjecting AI’s goal-seeking behavior to democratic scrutiny. When a city deploys an AI system to allocate housing resources, the crucial question is not whether the system is teleological—it inevitably is—but whether the goals are just, whether the metrics are shaped by affected communities, and whether there are avenues for appeal and revision. These are questions that the realist tradition, in its best forms, takes seriously.

Moreover, there is a danger in Morozov’s aestheticized language of flânerie and eolithism: it can become a tool of depoliticization just as easily as the techno-utopianism he critiques. If the ideal is the wanderer who follows his idle curiosity without fixed ends, then any attempt to collectively set priorities or allocate resources according to a plan can appear coercive and anti-ecological. But democratic politics necessarily involves setting priorities and making binding decisions. A city council deciding to invest in public transit over road expansion is exercising a form of instrumental reason—it has a goal and chooses means to achieve it—and that is exactly what democratic self-government requires.

None of this absolves the Efficiency Lobby. Morozov is right to excoriate the narrow range of goals that have historically driven AI development, and he is right that much of the technology we have inherited is ill-suited to democratic purposes. But the remedy is not to dream of a technology without goals. It is to build a politics in which the people, not corporations or militaries, set the goals. That means fighting for public ownership, strong regulation, and participatory governance. The AI we deserve is not one that has escaped instrumental reason—an impossible dream—but one that puts instrumental reason in the service of democratically chosen ends.
