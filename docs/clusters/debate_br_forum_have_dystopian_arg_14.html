"While Daron Acemoglu correctly identifies the moral urgency of governing AI, his proposal to redirect the technology through a technocratic measurement framework distinguishing 'automation' from 'augmentation' is impractical and potentially harmful."

Daron Acemoglu’s essay is persuasive in its moral urgency, but I worry about one of its central policy ambitions: redirecting AI through a measurement framework that distinguishes socially beneficial human-complementing technologies from harmful automation. The aspiration is understandable. Policymakers need criteria. But the analogy to climate policy, where carbon emissions can be measured and taxed, does not transfer easily to AI. There is no AI equivalent of a ton of carbon.

Carbon has a relatively clear physical meaning. Emissions can be measured, compared, and linked to a global externality. The politics are hard, but the metric is not mysterious. AI’s social effects are multidimensional, context-dependent, and often ambiguous. The same system can help one group and hurt another. The same application can complement workers in one institutional setting and replace them in another. The same model can be used for accessibility or surveillance. Trying to reduce this complexity to an ex ante classification risks false precision and unintended consequences.

Acemoglu suggests labor share as one promising measure. Technologies that automate tasks tend to reduce labor’s share of value added, while technologies that create new tasks tend to increase it. As a broad empirical framework, this is illuminating. As a policy guide, it is dangerous. Labor share is affected by many factors: market power, outsourcing, accounting practices, global supply chains, bargaining institutions, depreciation, profit margins, and product cycles. A firm’s labor share may fall because it adopts harmful automation, but also because it becomes more capital-intensive in a way that benefits consumers, improves safety, or reduces environmental damage. Another firm’s labor share may rise because productivity stagnates or because it relies on low-paid labor.

Suppose an AI system automates dangerous inspection work in chemical plants, reducing the need for workers to enter hazardous environments. Labor share may fall. Should policy discourage it? Suppose AI improves logistics so that fewer truck miles are needed, reducing emissions and costs but displacing some dispatchers. Is that socially harmful? Suppose a hospital uses AI to reduce administrative billing staff but reallocates savings to nurses. The labor effects depend on organizational choices. Suppose an educational tool increases teacher productivity, but a cash-strapped district uses it to enlarge classes. The same technology changes category depending on governance.

The deeper problem is that “automation” is not inherently bad. Human history includes many emancipatory forms of automation: washing machines, agricultural machinery, elevators, water systems, industrial safety controls, medical devices, and software that eliminates tedious paperwork. A society that had resisted labor-saving technology in the name of preserving tasks would be poorer and, in many respects, less humane. The normative question is not whether a technology substitutes for human labor. It is whether the overall distribution of costs and benefits is just, whether people retain agency, and whether the technology serves legitimate human ends.

Acemoglu knows this, but his framework may still encourage policymakers to favor labor-intensive inefficiency over productivity. That would be especially harmful in sectors where costs are already crushing households and governments. Health care, housing, education, infrastructure, and clean energy all need productivity improvements. Some of those improvements will involve automating tasks. If AI can reduce administrative waste in health care, speed permitting for renewable energy, improve construction methods, or lower the cost of tutoring, we should be cautious about policies that penalize labor substitution.

This does not mean leaving AI to the market. Acemoglu’s critique of market fundamentalism is correct. Markets do not price dignity, privacy, democracy, or community stability. Firms can profit from systems that impose costs on workers and citizens. But the alternative should be domain-specific governance rather than a general anti-automation filter.

In the workplace, the right questions are concrete. Does the technology increase injury risk or reduce it? Does it intensify pace beyond humane limits? Are workers monitored continuously? Can they contest algorithmic evaluations? Are productivity gains shared? Are displaced workers given notice, severance, retraining, or transfer opportunities? Does adoption violate anti-discrimination law? These questions can be answered through labor standards, collective bargaining, audits, and enforcement. They do not require policymakers to decide whether a technology is metaphysically “automation” or “augmentation.”

In education, governance should focus on learning outcomes, equity, teacher autonomy, student privacy, and democratic accountability. In health care, it should focus on patient outcomes, clinical responsibility, bias, safety, privacy, and cost. In criminal justice, it should focus on due process, civil rights, transparency, and the presumption against coercive state power. In media, it should focus on platform accountability, advertising transparency, data use, and amplification. AI is too broad for one master metric.

The surveillance issue shows the need for rights-based rules. We do not need to know whether facial recognition raises or lowers labor share to judge its use at protests dangerous. We do not need a productivity analysis to regulate spyware. Some technologies threaten basic liberties and should be banned or tightly controlled. Others should be permitted only with warrants, audits, and public oversight. The relevant metric is not labor complementarity but power.

The climate analogy also underestimates disagreement about social goals. Most people agree, at least in principle, that catastrophic climate change is bad and carbon emissions contribute to it. AI policy involves more plural values. Should autonomous vehicles be encouraged if they reduce accidents but displace drivers? Should AI grading be used if it saves teachers time but changes pedagogy? Should predictive health tools be used if they improve population health but increase insurer power? Should recommendation algorithms personalize information or expose users to common public content? These are political questions without a single technical answer.

Democratic deliberation is therefore more important than technocratic classification. Acemoglu calls for democratic oversight, and that should be the centerpiece. Citizens, workers, consumers, patients, parents, and affected communities should help decide which AI uses are acceptable. Agencies should develop sectoral rules. Courts should enforce rights. Legislatures should set boundaries. Experts can inform these decisions, but they should not promise a simple measurement framework that resolves them.

There is another risk: large firms may capture any complex classification system. If government subsidies or penalties depend on proving that an AI application is “human-complementary,” companies will learn to describe their products that way. Every surveillance system will be “safety-enhancing.” Every layoff will be “upskilling.” Every chatbot will “free workers for higher-value tasks.” Firms with legal and lobbying resources will navigate the framework; smaller firms and public-interest projects may struggle. We have seen similar dynamics in environmental, financial, and privacy regulation. Metrics can discipline markets, but they can also become compliance theater.

A better approach would combine several blunt but enforceable policies. Strengthen labor rights so workers can bargain over technology. Reduce payroll taxes for low-wage work while taxing rents, monopoly profits, and externalities. Provide generous adjustment assistance and lifelong learning. Enforce antitrust law against dominant platforms. Ban or restrict the most dangerous surveillance uses. Require algorithmic impact assessments in high-stakes domains. Create public AI infrastructure for socially valuable applications. Expand the safety net so people are not terrified of technological change. These policies do not require us to predict every labor-market effect in advance.

We should also be humble about technological uncertainty. In 2021, AI has achieved impressive results in vision, speech, translation, game playing, and pattern recognition, but many systems remain brittle, data-hungry, and poorly understood. Some feared job losses may not materialize soon; some unexpected disruptions may appear quickly. Policymaking should be adaptive. Pilot programs, sunset clauses, independent evaluation, and public reporting can allow rules to evolve. A rigid measurement scheme may age badly.

Acemoglu’s most important insight is that technology is shaped by institutions. I would apply that insight less by steering research away from “automation” and more by building institutions that make any productivity gains socially beneficial. If workers have power, automation need not mean immiseration. If citizens have privacy rights, AI need not mean surveillance. If markets are competitive, innovation need not mean monopoly. If the welfare state is strong, displacement need not mean ruin.

AI’s future does not have to be dystopian. But neither should it be constrained by a nostalgic attachment to existing tasks. The aim is not to maximize labor’s share in every process or to ensure that machines never replace human effort. The aim is a society in which productivity, freedom, equality, and dignity advance together. That requires democratic judgment, not a single metric.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Unlike carbon emissions in climate policy, AI’s social and labor effects are too multidimensional and context-dependent to be reduced to a single, reliable metric like labor share.
  2. 02Automation is not inherently socially destructive, and penalizing it could stifle necessary productivity gains in critical sectors like healthcare, education, and green energy.
  3. 03A rigid classification system for AI applications is susceptible to corporate capture, where large firms use lobbying and creative labeling to bypass regulations.
  4. 04Effective AI governance should rely on domain-specific regulations and rights-based rules—such as labor standards and privacy laws—rather than a general anti-automation filter.
  5. 05The focus of policy should be on building robust democratic institutions and a stronger social safety net to ensure that productivity gains from any form of AI are distributed justly.
  6. 06Democratic deliberation and political judgment are superior to technocratic metrics for resolving the pluralistic value conflicts inherent in AI development.
