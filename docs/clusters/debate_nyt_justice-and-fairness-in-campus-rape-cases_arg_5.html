"The fundamental failure of campus rape investigations is a lack of professional expertise and standardized procedures, rather than an inherent bias toward one party over the other."

What is missing from most of these discussions is the experience of the women who actually go through campus reporting processes. I have spent the last several years talking with students at half a dozen universities about what happens when they tell someone they were assaulted, and the picture is far bleaker than the recent backlash suggests.

A student tells a resident adviser she was raped at a party. She is sent to a Title IX coordinator who, depending on the school, may or may not be trained, may or may not be neutral, and may or may not even tell her about her options. She is asked, sometimes in those exact words, what she was wearing and how much she had to drink. If she chooses to pursue a hearing, she may wait months. She will see her assailant in the dining hall, in class, in her dorm. She may be discouraged, subtly or overtly, from going to the police, because the school does not want a crime statistic. If a hearing happens, she may be cross-examined—directly or through a panel—by people who have no idea how trauma affects memory. If she "wins," her assailant may receive a one-semester suspension and graduate on time.

This is the system that critics now describe as stacked against the accused. It is worth asking who is actually being protected here.

That said, the recent attention to false accusations is not entirely unwelcome, because it forces clarity about what good process actually looks like. Belief and investigation are not opposed. A trauma-informed interviewer can take an account seriously, document it carefully, and still gather evidence that may corroborate or complicate it. A hearing panel can presume innocence as a legal matter without treating the complainant as a liar. These are professional skills, and the central failure of campus systems is that they are run by amateurs.

The reforms that would help everyone are unglamorous: trained investigators, written findings, consistent timelines, written procedures available to both parties, the right to see the evidence, the right to an adviser, and standards that distinguish coercion from regret. None of this requires choosing sides in the culture war that has formed around the Rolling Stone story.

The danger of the current moment is that one bad story will be used to justify rolling back protections that were hard won and remain inadequate. Survivors and the accused both deserve professionalism. They are not getting it now, and pretending the problem is bias in one direction or the other will not produce it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current reporting process often subjects survivors to untrained coordinators, lengthy delays, and inadequate disciplinary outcomes.
  2. 02Schools frequently prioritize protecting their own reputations and crime statistics over the well-being of the students involved.
  3. 03Trauma-informed interviewing and the presumption of innocence are compatible professional skills that can coexist in a fair investigation.
  4. 04Meaningful reform requires unglamorous procedural improvements, such as trained investigators and transparent access to evidence, rather than taking sides in a culture war.
  5. 05Using isolated instances of false accusations to roll back existing protections ignores the fact that current systems remain inadequate for all parties involved.
