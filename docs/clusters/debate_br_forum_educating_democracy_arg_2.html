Diversified GPT

"The current standards-based reform movement is a misguided political fantasy that replaces the formation of democratic judgment with a thin, centralized system of high-stakes testing and punitive accountability."

Deborah Meier’s essay is, among other things, a warning against a tempting political fantasy: that democracy can be rescued from disorder by finding the right experts, writing the right lists, and attaching the right penalties. The fantasy is especially tempting in education because the suffering is real. Too many children do leave school unable to read well, write clearly, reason mathematically, or imagine themselves as citizens with a claim on the public world. But the question is whether the cure now being offered—state frameworks, high-stakes tests, uniform sanctions, centrally prescribed pacing—addresses that suffering or merely gives public officials a clean-looking instrument with which to sort children more efficiently.

Meier’s strongest point is not that “standards” are bad. She is too serious an educator to say that. Her schools have standards, and demanding ones. The point is that standards are not self-enforcing abstractions. They live in institutions, relationships, and habits of judgment. A standard held by a teacher who knows a child, a family that understands the purposes of a school, and a community of adults who argue over the meaning of good work is different in kind from a standard embedded in a state test whose results arrive months later and whose consequences are imposed by people who will never meet the child. Both may use the same word—standard—but they embody very different conceptions of public life.

The current movement treats education as if its central problem were insufficient specification. If only we could say exactly what fourth graders should know about Egypt, fractions, or the main idea of a passage, then instruction would improve. If only teachers knew that failure carried consequences, they would teach better. If only students feared retention or diploma denial, they would work harder. This is a thin theory of learning and an even thinner theory of democracy.

The real work of education is not the transmission of discrete items from a list into a child’s head. It is the formation of judgment. Children learn what counts as evidence, how to revise an idea, how to listen to criticism, how to persist when something is difficult, how to distinguish authority from bullying, how to join a community without disappearing into it. These habits cannot be measured adequately by a single state instrument, and they cannot be cultivated by turning teachers into functionaries whose main public obligation is compliance.

This does not mean that schools should be left alone. Meier’s critics will surely say that “local control” has often meant local neglect, racial exclusion, low expectations, and cozy protection of adult interests. They will be right. American localism has an ugly history as well as a democratic one. School boards defended segregation. Districts tracked poor children into dead ends. Teachers and principals sometimes concealed failure behind warm language about self-esteem. A defense of local authority must not become a defense of local irresponsibility.

But that is not Meier’s argument. She is not asking us to trust schools because educators are saints. She is asking us to build institutions in which trust can be earned, examined, and renewed. Small schools, public exhibitions of student work, external reviews, parent participation, and school-level responsibility for curriculum and assessment are not evasions of accountability. They are a richer form of it. They make it harder, not easier, for adults to hide. A teacher who must sit with colleagues, parents, and outside reviewers while a student defends a portfolio is accountable in a way no multiple-choice score can capture. The evidence is visible. The reasoning is public. The standards can be debated.

The crucial contrast is between accountability as inspection and accountability as answerability. The standards movement prefers inspection. It imagines that distant authorities can create a fair system by measuring every school in the same way and publishing the results. This sounds democratic because it is uniform. But uniformity is not the same as fairness. A state test administered to children whose schools have libraries, laboratories, stable staffs, small classes, and college-educated parents is not the same event as that test administered to children in overcrowded buildings with substitute teachers and no quiet place to study. To report the scores without reporting the conditions is not candor; it is a form of concealment.

Indeed, one of the perverse attractions of high-stakes testing is that it makes inequality look like a defect of motivation. If a poor school fails, the explanation is suddenly that its teachers lacked urgency, its students lacked effort, or its parents lacked standards. The test shifts attention away from housing, health care, class size, teacher turnover, preschool, after-school safety, and the distribution of cultural opportunity. It offers a moral drama in place of a political economy.

This is why the rhetoric of “no excuses” should make democrats uneasy. Of course schools should not use poverty as an excuse for indifference. But neither should states use heroic exceptions as excuses for injustice. There are schools that do extraordinary work with very poor children; their existence should inspire us. It should not license us to pretend that resources do not matter. If we truly believed in common high standards, we would be as relentless about equalizing opportunity as we are about publishing test scores.

The civic implications of standardization are perhaps even more serious. Meier asks us to notice what children learn from the organization of school itself. If all important decisions come from elsewhere, students learn that public life is remote and that adults near them are powerless. If teachers say, in effect, “I know this assignment is foolish, but it is required,” students learn cynicism. If parents are invited to fundraisers but not to serious deliberation, they learn that “involvement” means service, not authority. These lessons may not appear on state assessments, but they shape citizens.

A democratic school should be one of the first places where young people see adults disagree honorably. They should watch teachers debate whether a piece of writing is good enough, whether a historical interpretation is supported by evidence, whether a student who has struggled deserves more time or a different task. They should see standards as human judgments disciplined by public reasons, not as numbers dropped from the sky. They should learn that authority is legitimate when it can explain itself.

The standards movement, at its worst, also narrows intellectual life. When the consequences attached to a test become severe, schools inevitably teach to the test. This is not because teachers are lazy; it is because they are rational. If a school’s reputation, a principal’s job, and a student’s diploma depend on a limited set of measures, those measures will dominate time, attention, and imagination. Subjects not tested will shrink. Questions too complex to score cheaply will disappear. Reading will become practice in extracting answers from short passages; writing will become formula; history will become coverage; science will become vocabulary. The official curriculum may be broad, but the lived curriculum will be what is rewarded.

Supporters of high-stakes testing often reply that without pressure nothing changes. There is truth here. Some schools do need disruption. Some adults do need to be made uncomfortable. But pressure is not a pedagogy. Fear may produce compliance, but it rarely produces intellectual courage. A child who is frightened of failure may memorize more facts in the short run, but the deeper purposes of education require confidence, curiosity, and attachment. Similarly, a teacher under threat may align lessons more tightly to a framework, but good teaching depends on perception—the ability to see what this student, in this moment, needs next. Systems that punish deviation tend to weaken that perception.

What then should states do? They should guarantee civil rights, adequate funding, safe buildings, transparent information, and access to qualified teachers. They should monitor exclusion, suspension, special education placement, racial disparities, and graduation. They should support multiple forms of assessment and require schools to make their standards public. They should create review systems in which teams of educators, parents, scholars, and community members examine schools deeply, not merely rank them. They should collect sample-based data to understand broad trends without distorting every classroom. They should intervene when schools violate rights or persistently fail children—but intervention should mean building capacity, not merely imposing punishments.

Most of all, states should make it easier to create the kinds of public schools Meier describes: small, diverse, self-governing, and accountable through exhibitions of real work. Such schools should not be boutique exceptions for charismatic founders. They should be one central strategy of public education. They answer both the bureaucratic failure of large systems and the privatizing temptation to abandon the public sphere altogether. They keep education public while making it personal.

Meier’s essay is vulnerable to one criticism: it may underestimate how difficult it is to sustain such schools at scale. Small democratic schools require time, skill, patience, and leadership. They cannot be mass-produced by memo. But that is not an argument for standardization. It is an argument for humility. If the work is difficult, we should not replace it with an easier thing and call that reform.

The great mistake of the current moment is to confuse public confidence with numerical control. Citizens are told that because schools will be ranked, sanctioned, and aligned, the system is finally accountable. But a democracy worthy of the name needs more than data. It needs institutions in which ordinary people practice judgment together. Schools should be among those institutions. If they become instead local branches of a testing bureaucracy, we may raise some scores and still diminish the very capacities democratic life requires.

Meier’s challenge is therefore not sentimental. It is stern. She asks whether we trust democracy enough to educate democratically. If we do not, then no framework, however rigorous, will save us.

"The conflict between standards-based reform and democratic education should be resolved by adopting a system of common public expectations that avoids rigid standardization and preserves local professional judgment."

The dispute between Deborah Meier and the champions of standards is often presented as a choice between rigor and softness, accountability and evasion, excellence and sentiment. That framing is false. Meier is not against standards, and many standards advocates are not against democratic schools. The real disagreement concerns where judgment should reside, how public expectations should be expressed, and what kinds of evidence should count when the future of a child or a school is at stake.

On these questions, both sides have something essential to say. Meier is right that high-stakes standardized testing is a dangerously crude mechanism for improving schools. Her examples from Chicago and Lynnfield show that when numerical performance becomes the master value, vulnerable children become liabilities. Schools learn to exclude, reclassify, retain, suspend, or discourage the students who threaten the institution’s standing. Any accountability system that makes children disappear has failed morally, whatever happens to average scores.

She is also right that the organization of schools teaches civic lessons. Students learn not only from civics textbooks but from the authority structures around them. If teachers are treated as clerks, if parents are treated as consumers, if principals are treated as compliance officers, then children absorb a lesson about public life: important decisions are made elsewhere. Such an education may produce test-takers, but it will not produce citizens confident in their ability to deliberate and act.

Yet the standards advocates are right about one thing Meier sometimes understates: public education cannot be a collection of entirely local definitions of success. Children move. Colleges and employers read transcripts. Citizens share institutions. Taxpayers support schools not only to satisfy local preferences but to secure public purposes. A child’s right to a good education should not depend on whether the adults in her school happen to define “good” ambitiously.

The question, then, is how to have common public expectations without standardizing the life out of schools. We need standards without standardization.

A workable approach would begin by drastically limiting what state standards attempt to do. The most destructive frameworks are encyclopedic. They try to specify too much content at too many grade levels, producing a curriculum that is “covered” but not understood. States should instead identify a small set of essential competencies: reading complex texts, writing clearly for different purposes, using mathematical reasoning, understanding scientific inquiry, interpreting historical evidence, and participating in civic argument. These expectations should be broad enough to permit different curricula and specific enough to prevent fraud.

Second, assessment should be plural. A state test may have a place, but it should be only one piece of evidence. Standardized tests can reveal patterns: large disparities among groups, schools that are persistently weak in mathematics, districts that graduate students who cannot read adequately. They are useful warning lights. But no one would drive a car using only warning lights; one also needs windows, mirrors, steering, and judgment. Decisions about promotion, graduation, and school quality should include course performance, portfolios, exhibitions, teacher evaluations, attendance, and external review.

Third, we should distinguish monitoring from high-stakes certification. The state needs information about how the system is doing. That can be obtained through sample-based assessments such as NAEP-style testing, periodic diagnostic exams, and public reporting. But using a single test to deny diplomas is a different matter. The closer an assessment comes to determining a child’s future, the more varied and individualized the evidence should be. A diploma should certify competence, but competence can be demonstrated in more than one way.

Fourth, local schools should be required to make their own standards public. Meier’s model is powerful because it does not hide behind vague progressivism. Students must demonstrate habits of mind, craftsmanship, and work habits. Their work is examined. Families can see what is expected. That kind of transparency should be universal. Every school should publish graduation expectations, examples of student work that meet them, and procedures for judging that work. Such publication allows comparison without forcing sameness.

Fifth, external review should become a serious alternative to test-driven accountability. Teams including educators from other schools, parents, scholars, and community members could visit schools, examine student work, observe classes, interview students and families, and evaluate whether the school’s claims are credible. This is labor-intensive, but so is education. We do not inspect hospitals solely by administering a multiple-choice test to patients; we examine practices, outcomes, records, and professional judgment. Schools deserve equally serious review.

Sixth, accountability must include resources. It is astonishing how often states demand equal outcomes while tolerating radically unequal conditions. If a school lacks qualified teachers, current books, safe facilities, and reasonable class sizes, then the public failure is not located only inside that school. Any report card that lists scores but not resources is incomplete. If we are serious about standards, we must be serious about the opportunity to meet them.

The virtue of this mixed system is that it treats democracy as a discipline, not a slogan. Local educators would have authority, but they would have to explain themselves. States would have oversight, but they would not dictate every lesson. Parents would receive information, but not only numbers. Students would face expectations, but not a single narrow gate. Experts would contribute knowledge, but not monopolize judgment.

This approach also acknowledges the diversity of legitimate educational aims. A school centered on science and technology, a bilingual community school, a classical academy, an arts-rich elementary school, and a Deweyan small school may all serve public purposes. They need not teach the same units in the same months. But they should all be able to show that students read, reason, write, calculate, investigate, and deliberate well. Pluralism requires common ground; common ground need not require uniformity.

Meier’s warning about democracy is especially timely because our public culture is increasingly impatient with deliberation. Governors and business leaders like standards because they promise clarity: set goals, measure results, reward success, punish failure. That language travels well from corporate management to politics. But schools are not factories, and children are not production units. The more complex the learning we value, the less adequate simple metrics become.

Still, educators should not respond by asking the public simply to trust them. Trust in public institutions is fragile, and sometimes for good reason. Too many families have been misled by schools that said everything was fine until it was too late. The answer is not to abolish accountability but to deepen it. A portfolio defense before knowledgeable adults is accountability. A public exhibition is accountability. A school review that examines whether students can explain their work is accountability. A transparent record of graduates’ success in college or work is accountability. We need to broaden the public meaning of that word.

The coming danger is that frustration with bad tests will be used to discredit any common expectations, while frustration with bad schools will be used to justify any test. Both reactions are mistakes. The country should refuse the false choice. We can insist that every child deserves a demanding education while also insisting that demanding education cannot be reduced to a state exam.

Meier has given us a vivid picture of schools as democratic communities. Standards advocates have given us a necessary reminder that public systems require public evidence. The task now is to join these insights without betraying either. Standards should be few, public, and important. Assessment should be multiple, transparent, and humane. Authority should be shared. Sanctions should be careful, and assistance should come first. Above all, reform should strengthen the capacity of adults near children to make wise judgments, not replace those judgments with remote commands.

If we can do that, standards may become not the enemy of democratic education but one of its tools. If we cannot, Meier’s fears will prove justified: we will have built a system that knows how to count answers but not how to cultivate minds.

"The standards-based reform movement is dangerous because it attempts to replace essential professional adult judgment with crude, high-stakes standardized testing that narrows curriculum and undermines the democratic purpose of schooling."

The most important word in Deborah Meier’s essay is not “standards,” “democracy,” or even “accountability.” It is “judgment.” Schools rise or fall on the quality of adult judgment: the teacher deciding whether a child’s error shows confusion or carelessness; the team deciding whether a project has become rich inquiry or pleasant drift; the principal deciding whether a disruptive student needs discipline, counseling, or both; the parent deciding whether to trust a school’s account of her child. The standards movement is dangerous because it pretends that judgment can be replaced by alignment.

As a matter of daily school life, that pretense is already reshaping classrooms. Teachers know what happens when a test becomes high-stakes. The bulletin boards change. The schedule changes. The conversation in faculty meetings changes. Children are grouped and regrouped according to predicted performance. Borderline students receive frantic attention while those far above or far below the cut score may be neglected. Writing becomes the five-paragraph form most likely to satisfy a rubric. Reading becomes practice in finding answers quickly in short passages. Mathematics becomes item types. History and science give way to tested literacy and numeracy, unless they too are tested, in which case they become lists.

This is not because teachers hate rigor. It is because systems tell teachers what they value by what they measure and punish. A principal may say, “Teach richly,” but if the school is publicly shamed for scores, test preparation becomes the real curriculum. The higher the stakes, the narrower the work.

Meier’s critics will say that without external pressure many schools will continue to fail. They are right to worry. Anyone who has worked in schools knows that sentimentality can masquerade as compassion, and that low expectations often wear humane clothing. A child may be passed along because retaining him seems cruel, because confronting his parents is hard, because the school lacks alternatives, or because everyone is tired. By ninth grade the accumulated evasions become catastrophe. The standards movement gained power because educators did not always tell the truth.

But truth-telling and test-based coercion are not the same thing. Indeed, a crude testing system may produce new evasions: excluding low scorers, retaining students before the tested grade, reclassifying children, drilling them in ways that raise scores without increasing understanding, or encouraging dropouts to leave before they damage the school’s statistics. When accountability is badly designed, it does not eliminate dishonesty. It changes its form.

A better path begins with a professional understanding of teaching. Teachers should not be treated either as autonomous artisans answerable to no one or as technicians implementing scripts. They should be members of a public profession with shared standards of practice, peer review, continuing study, and responsibility for evidence of student learning. Professions do not reject accountability; they locate it in informed judgment.

In medicine, we expect doctors to use evidence, follow established protocols where appropriate, consult colleagues, explain decisions, and submit to review. But we do not imagine that a single centralized test of patient outcomes can determine whether a doctor has acted well in every case. We know that cases differ, that judgment matters, and that good practice combines general knowledge with particular attention. Teaching deserves similar seriousness.

What would professional accountability in schools look like?

First, teachers would have time to examine student work together. This is not a luxury. It is the heart of improvement. If a faculty agrees that students should be able to make an argument from evidence, teachers must bring actual essays to the table and ask: What counts as evidence? What makes this argument strong? How do we help the student whose ideas are good but whose organization collapses? How do we judge English-language learners fairly while still teaching academic English? Such conversations create standards more powerful than documents in binders.

Second, schools would use portfolios and exhibitions not as decorative progressivism but as disciplined assessment. A portfolio should not be a scrapbook. It should contain work selected to demonstrate specific competencies, accompanied by student reflection and teacher commentary. Exhibitions should include outside adults who ask hard questions. The process should be public enough that families understand what quality looks like. Meier’s description of Central Park East’s portfolio defenses is important because it shows that alternative assessment can be more demanding than standardized testing, not less.

Third, external review should be routine. Schools can become self-protective. A faculty that loves its own culture may fail to see weaknesses. Visiting teams from other schools, universities, community organizations, and perhaps state agencies should examine curriculum, student work, classroom practice, and outcomes. Their reports should be public. The review should be qualitative as well as quantitative. A school is a human institution; it cannot be understood by numbers alone.

Fourth, standardized tests should be used sparingly and intelligently. They can provide useful information, especially about basic skills and broad patterns of inequality. If a school’s students consistently perform poorly in reading compared with similar students elsewhere, the school must investigate. But test results should trigger questions, not automatic punishments. They should be one source of evidence among many. No child should be denied promotion or a diploma solely on the basis of one exam.

Fifth, accountability must extend upward. Teachers are tired of being told that they alone are responsible for outcomes while decisions about class size, building conditions, special education services, curriculum materials, and staffing are made elsewhere. If a state mandates a science standard, does every school have laboratory materials? If a district demands early literacy, does every first-grade teacher have access to books, specialists, and manageable class size? If officials insist that all students pass algebra, are there enough qualified mathematics teachers? A serious accountability system asks these questions publicly.

Sixth, discipline and exclusion must be monitored as closely as test scores. Meier opens with Chicago’s expulsions because she understands the connection between standards and sorting. When schools are rewarded for scores and punished for disorder, they may define difficult children as contaminants. A child expelled to a “safe school” that he does not attend has not been educated; he has been removed from the denominator. Public systems must report suspensions, expulsions, transfers, dropouts, and special education placements by race, income, disability, and language. Otherwise we will mistake disappearance for improvement.

The democratic argument is not abstract. In a good school, students see adults doing serious work together. They see teachers disagree over books, standards, fairness, and evidence. They see that authority is not merely power but responsibility. They experience rules as part of a community, not commands from nowhere. This matters because schools are among the few public institutions where children spend sustained time with adults outside their families. If those adults are visibly powerless, the civic lesson is bleak.

Meier’s small-school model is compelling partly because it makes adult responsibility possible. In a huge high school, a teacher may see 150 students a day. The structure itself defeats knowledge. A student can fail quietly. A teacher can retreat into a classroom. A parent can be passed from office to office. Smallness does not guarantee wisdom, but it creates conditions in which wisdom can operate. The same is true of stable teams, advisory systems, common planning time, and schools-within-schools. These are not soft reforms; they are structural prerequisites for accountability that attaches to real persons.

Still, Meier’s essay should not lead educators to dismiss the public’s demand for evidence. Parents and taxpayers are entitled to know whether schools are working. Teachers should welcome that demand, provided the evidence is fair and meaningful. We should not defend vague report cards, inflated grades, or diplomas that conceal illiteracy. If we want the public to trust professional judgment, we must make that judgment visible.

One practical proposal is to require every public school to produce an annual “learning report” far richer than current test-score summaries. It would include samples of student work at different performance levels; explanations of curriculum; standardized test results interpreted cautiously; graduation, attendance, suspension, and college-going data; information about resources and staffing; summaries of external reviews; and school goals for the next year. Families could see not only how many students passed a test, but what students are actually asked to do. Such reports would be harder to compile than score tables, but far more honest.

Another proposal is to create regional networks of schools that moderate each other’s assessments. If one school says a student’s history exhibition meets standard, teachers from another school should occasionally review the evidence. Over time, shared expectations would develop without forcing identical curriculum. This is how professional communities build reliability: through repeated comparison, discussion, and revision.

The deepest flaw in high-stakes standardization is its impoverished view of motivation. It assumes that fear of failure will produce effort. Sometimes it does, briefly. But the learning that lasts is usually tied to purpose, relationship, curiosity, pride, and belonging. A child works hard for a teacher who sees him, for a project that matters, for a public presentation he wants to survive with dignity, for a future he can imagine. A faculty improves when it believes improvement is possible and when it has the authority to act. Shame may produce motion; it rarely produces growth.

This does not mean schools should be gentle places without demands. On the contrary, the best schools are demanding because they are personal. It is harder to disappear in them. It is harder to submit mediocre work when an adult who knows you asks you to explain it. It is harder for teachers to blame “the system” when they have real authority. The standards movement has borrowed the language of rigor while often weakening the relationships that make rigor possible.

We need a new bargain. Educators should accept public accountability, transparent evidence, peer review, and an end to hidden low expectations. Policymakers should abandon the fantasy that complex learning can be compelled by standardized sanctions. They should invest in small schools, teacher collaboration, rich assessment, and equitable resources. They should treat teachers not as obstacles to reform but as the people through whom reform either lives or dies.

Deborah Meier’s essay reminds us that education is a human practice before it is a policy system. That is not an argument against standards. It is an argument for standards worthy of human beings.

What My Daughter's School Taught Me

I am not an educator. I am a parent — a public-school parent in a small city in upstate New York — and I read Deborah Meier's essay with a strong sense of recognition. She has put into words what I have been trying to say at school-board meetings for three years.

My daughter, who is now eleven, started kindergarten at our neighborhood elementary school in 1993. Her kindergarten teacher, Mrs. K., was one of those people who simply knew children. She knew when my daughter was upset about something at home before I did. She had been teaching kindergarten in this building for twenty-six years, and she taught the children of her former students. She was, in Meier's phrase, an adult in my daughter's life who was "powerful enough to decide important things."

In the spring of 1998, our state introduced new fourth-grade tests in English and math. Mrs. K. is now retired. She told me, the last time I saw her, that she would have retired anyway, but that the new framework — which arrived in her mailbox shortly before she left — had made her feel like a stranger in her own classroom. "I don't know what they want anymore," she said. "I knew what five-year-olds needed. I don't know what the state needs."

I cite this not as a piece of policy analysis — one teacher's retirement proves nothing — but because Meier's essay is fundamentally about what it feels like to teach, to learn, and to raise a child inside an institution, and feeling is the right unit of analysis for that question. Meier is not just making an argument about test design or governance structures. She is making an argument about the human texture of schooling, and about what that texture does to children over time.

I want to add three observations from the parent side of the desk.

First, the standards movement, as it has arrived in our district, has made my daughter's school worse — measurably, in the ways that matter to me. There is less art. There is less music. There is no longer a fall field trip to the apple orchard, because the fourth-grade teachers cannot afford to lose a day. The library has been reorganized to "support the curriculum framework," which seems to mean removing books that aren't on the reading lists. The principal, who used to walk the halls knowing every child's name, now spends most of his time generating reports for the central office. Whatever else this is, it is not a richer education. It is a thinner one, defended on the grounds that thinness is rigor.

Second, the official rationale for all this — that we are preparing our children for the new economy — does not match what I, as a working adult, actually see in the workplaces I know. I am a mid-level manager at a regional manufacturer. The young people we hire — and we hire many, from the local high school and community college — do not fail us because they cannot factor polynomials. They fail us because they cannot show up on time, cannot work in a team, cannot follow a complicated set of written instructions, cannot write a clear email to a customer, cannot adapt when something goes wrong on the floor. These are not the skills that fourth-grade bubble tests measure. They are the skills that good schools teach by being good communities — by holding children to high but humane expectations day after day, by giving them real work to do, by letting them practice being responsible to other people. Meier's small schools, from everything I have read, do this. The big anonymous high school down the road from my office largely does not.

Third — and this is the point I most want to make — Meier is right that what is missing from American childhood is the consistent company of adults who matter. My daughter has more "enrichment activities" than I had at her age, more lessons, more leagues, more screens. But she has fewer adults in her life. Her teachers turn over faster. Her coaches are part-timers. Her neighborhood is full of houses whose grown-ups she does not know by name. School is the place where this could be repaired, and the standards movement, as we are living it, is making it worse — not because anyone intends to, but because it pulls teachers' attention upward, toward the state, and away from the children in front of them.

What is the standards reformer's reply to all this? I have heard it many times at our school-board meetings. It goes roughly: "We agree that what you describe is unfortunate, but the data show that too many children are failing to learn basic skills, and the only way to know whether they are learning is to test them, and the only way to make sure schools take the testing seriously is to attach consequences." This argument has a logic. But it assumes that the central problem of American education is that schools do not know what to teach or how hard to push. In my district, that is not the central problem. The central problem is that adults are exhausted, kids are anxious, families are stretched, and our public institutions feel further away every year. Adding a test does not fix any of this. It makes it worse, by signaling to children that the adults in their lives are no longer trusted to know what they need.

I do not have a complete policy alternative to propose. I am a parent, not a policymaker. But I want to register, as a citizen, my agreement with Meier's core instinct: that schools belong, in the first instance, to the families and communities they serve, and that experts and legislators owe those families a presumption of competence rather than a presumption of failure. The teacher who knew my daughter in kindergarten knew more about what she needed than any state framework will ever know. The school board members who voted last spring to add a third practice test in third grade did not know my daughter at all.

If the price of accountability is that the people closest to our children stop being trusted to teach them, the price is too high. Meier has written what amounts to a brief on behalf of the people who actually do the work of raising the next generation — teachers, parents, neighbors — against the idea that someone in a state capital knows better. I find the brief persuasive, and I hope more parents will read it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Standardization has measurably degraded the quality of education by narrowing the curriculum and diverting administrative focus away from students toward bureaucratic reporting.
  2. 02The skills measured by high-stakes testing do not align with the actual needs of the modern workplace, which requires the soft skills and responsibility fostered by strong school communities.
  3. 03The movement exacerbates the alienation of children by shifting teachers' attention toward state frameworks and away from the vital task of being present, influential adults in their students' lives.
  4. 04The rationale for high-stakes testing incorrectly assumes that the primary problem in education is a lack of rigor, ignoring the real crises of student anxiety and the exhaustion of families and educators.
  5. 05Educational authority should be returned to the families and local communities who possess the most intimate and relevant knowledge of their children's needs.
