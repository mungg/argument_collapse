"Social media are neither a passing fad nor a revolutionary substitute for traditional institutions, but rather a permanent political accelerant that integrates into the existing infrastructure of power and association."

The political question is where the debate becomes most exaggerated. Social media are neither a fad nor a revolution. They are an accelerant. They make certain kinds of association faster, cheaper and more visible, but they do not replace the slow work of building power.

Egypt is a good example precisely because it does not yield a simple answer. Facebook pages, Twitter feeds and mobile videos helped circulate images of police brutality, publicize demonstrations and connect Egyptian activists to journalists and sympathizers abroad. They also helped puncture the isolation on which authoritarian regimes often depend: a citizen who thinks “everyone is afraid” may act differently after seeing thousands of others express the same anger.

But Tahrir Square was not made of pixels. It was made of bodies, courage, labor networks, lawyers, students, religious groups, old opposition forces and ordinary people willing to risk injury or prison. Nor did Mubarak’s fall settle the harder questions: who controls the army, how elections are administered, how minority rights are protected, how parties bargain, how courts function. Thomas Friedman is right that Facebook and Twitter do not supply the brick and mortar of democratic life.

Yet it would be a mistake to conclude that because social media cannot complete revolutions, they are merely fashionable. The telephone could not create a union, but unions used telephones. Newspapers could not guarantee democracy, but democratic politics became unimaginable without print culture. Social media are becoming part of the political environment in the same way: useful to dissidents, useful to governments, useful to rumor-mongers, useful to organizers, useful to mobs.

Their most important effect may be on the tempo of politics. Scandals spread faster. Repression is documented faster. Coalitions form faster, but they may also dissolve faster. A movement can gain attention before it has developed leadership, which is both an opportunity and a danger. Occupy Wall Street showed a similar pattern in the United States: social media helped create visibility and replication, but not a clear program or durable organization.

So the right verdict is modest. Social networks are not a substitute for institutions, and they are not a passing toy. They are becoming part of the infrastructure through which political actors discover one another, narrate events and contest legitimacy. Whether that produces democracy depends on forces older and harder than software.

  4. 04The primary impact of social media is the acceleration of political tempo, allowing scandals, documentation, and coalitions to emerge much faster than in the past.
  5. 05Social media allow movements to gain rapid public attention before they have developed the leadership or durable organization necessary for long-term success.
