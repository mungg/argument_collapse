"The responding essay argues that Brandon Terry’s radicalized interpretation of Martin Luther King Jr. distorts King’s legacy by prioritizing his late-career political frustrations over his more authentic and effective identity as a Christian patriot committed to American founding ideals and colorblindness."

Brandon Terry’s “MLK Now” joins a long line of academic efforts to recruit Martin Luther King Jr. for the radical left, painting him as a proto-socialist, an anti-capitalist revolutionary, and a scourge of American hypocrisy. It is a meticulously constructed brief, but it rests on a narrow, selective reading that distorts King’s life and thought almost beyond recognition. The King who actually shaped American history and whose memory deserves to be honored is not the agitated prophet Terry describes, but the man who famously proclaimed his dream that his children would one day be judged by the content of their character, not the color of their skin. Terry’s King is a projection of contemporary identity politics and anti-American sentiment, not the Christian democratic patriot who appealed to the nation’s best self.

The essay’s central gambit is to elevate King’s late-1960s utterances—his strident criticisms of Vietnam, his call for a Poor People’s Campaign, and his talk of “coercive” civil disobedience—over the far more substantial body of work that defined the civil rights movement as a crusade for the fulfillment of American founding ideals. Terry dismisses the King who “called us to be true to who we already are” and “live out the true meaning of our founding creed” as a “mythic figure of consensus and conciliation.” But this is, in fact, the authentic and most politically effective King. The reason Americans across the political spectrum embrace King is not because of academic mystification but because his vision of a nation rising above racism through moral appeal and legal reform struck a chord that was both deeply Christian and entirely American.

Terry’s attempt to undermine the colorblind ideal at the heart of King’s dream is a profound misreading. He calls it “conservative gaslighting” to invoke King in support of colorblind jurisprudence, yet King’s own words in the 1963 March on Washington are unambiguous: he longed for a society where race would no longer determine one’s treatment or prospects. This is not a naïve denial of past and present discrimination; it is a moral aspiration that has driven racial progress from the abolition of slavery to the Civil Rights Act. The radical left’s insistence on treating racial categories as permanent, essential features of political life—and its embrace of group-based grievance and racial preferences—is precisely the sort of racial chauvinism King warned against. Terry notes King’s argument that racial domination crippled the nation from its inception, but he ignores King’s equally forceful insistence on the “democratic spirit” that has always confronted racism as a source of hope. That democratic spirit is what gave us the Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments, and it is what motivated the civil rights movement to frame its demands in terms of universal rights, not tribal spoils.

Nowhere does Terry adequately grapple with the reality that King’s radical pronouncements coincided with his growing isolation and political irrelevance. By 1967–68, King’s focus on poverty and his denunciations of the Vietnam War alienated much of the coalition that had secured the legislative victories of 1964 and 1965. His Poor People’s Campaign was marred by disorganization and ended in humiliation. These later ventures do not represent the flowering of King’s mature thought; they reflect a man grappling with a political cul-de-sac, reaching for a more confrontational posture after the moral clarity of the Jim Crow struggle had been resolved. To lift those fragments up as the “real” King is to canonize his least successful and most confused period.

Even on the economic front, King’s proposals were far more modest than Terry suggests. His support for a guaranteed income and full employment was couched in the language of Christian charity and the American social contract, not Marxist revolution. He called for helping the poor, not for dismantling capitalism. In Why We Can’t Wait, King wrote about the need to integrate African Americans into the economic mainstream, not about abolishing private property or establishing workers’ control. Terry’s inference that King’s economic thought remains the rallying cry for today’s demarxified left is tenuous at best; worker shortages and low unemployment, not a guaranteed income, have proven the most reliable path out of poverty.

The essay’s final obsession with King’s “politics of character” reveals the deeper confusion. Terry defends King’s moral rigor but tries to distance him from “respectability politics,” a term now used by the left to denigrate any call for personal responsibility. Yet King unambiguously preached the importance of moral virtue, thrift, sexual restraint, and family stability—values that are indispensable for human flourishing regardless of race. To sever those virtues from King and assign them to a discredited “respectability” is to hollow out the ethical core of his message. The social ills that plague many black communities today—father absence, educational underachievement, and a culture of violence—would have been obvious concerns to King, not marginalia to be explained away by structural oppression alone.

The most honest reading of King’s legacy is that he was a great American who challenged his country to live up to its principles. The multicultural left’s effort to remake him in its own image is a betrayal of the actual man and the remarkable movement he led. King sought to unite, not divide; he appealed to conscience, not to revolutionary destruction; he believed that the American promise was redeemable, not permanently tainted. Fifty years after his death, the best tribute we can offer is to take him at his word when he dreamed of a nation where character, not color, mattered—and to resist the siren songs of those who would turn him into a partisan ideologue.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01King’s most significant and successful contributions were rooted in his appeal to universal American principles and moral suasion rather than the 'coercive' radicalism of his final years.
  2. 02The attempt to dismiss King’s vision of a colorblind society as 'conservative gaslighting' ignores his explicit moral aspiration for a nation where race does not determine a person's prospects.
  3. 03King’s late-career radicalization and focus on the Vietnam War and the Poor People’s Campaign marked a period of political isolation and failure rather than the pinnacle of his intellectual maturity.
  4. 04King’s economic proposals were grounded in Christian charity and the existing social contract rather than a revolutionary desire to dismantle the capitalist system.
  5. 05Severing King’s emphasis on personal character and moral virtue from his political message hollows out the ethical core of his leadership and ignores his genuine commitment to personal responsibility.
