"The lead essay overstates the role of behavioral quirks like procrastination, failing to recognize that the low adoption of health and education products is often a rational response to extreme poverty, risk, and market constraints."

Glennerster and Kremer’s essay is an important contribution, but it overstates its case. By attributing low adoption of health and education products to procrastination and psychological quirks, it sells short the rationality of the poor. A closer look at the very evidence they marshal suggests that many of the “behavioral” effects they document are better understood as rational responses to extreme poverty, risk, and imperfect markets. The distinction is not semantic: if the poor are fundamentally rational, the solution is to fix the constraints, not to nudge the people.

It is undeniable that small costs deter uptake. But what is “small” to the authors—six dollars, 30 cents—can be impossibly large in a cash-strapped household where income is irregular and every cent is already mentally allocated. Standard economic theory predicts that when liquidity constraints bind, even highly beneficial investments will not be made. A farmer may know that fertilizer yields a high return, but if she cannot borrow to buy it, the knowledge is useless. The essay acknowledges cash constraints in a sentence, then rushes to frame the problem as procrastination. Yet many of the most celebrated findings are exactly what you would expect if people face credit constraints and have volatile incomes. For instance, the finding that households pay for bed nets more when given time to raise the funds is straight out of a liquidity-constraint model, not a behavioral one. The fact that school attendance jumps when fees are due at the right time relative to cash availability is a response to the timing of liquidity, not present bias.

Risk also looms large. In poor communities, the future is uncertain; a child might die, a crop fail, a job disappear. In such environments, putting off investments in preventative health or schooling can be a perfectly sensible strategy of waiting to see if the need is genuine, or if a crisis saps resources. The low willingness to pay for chlorine or bed nets at a given moment may reflect option value: wait and see if there is a worse illness later, or if a cheaper source appears. This is not procrastination in the sense of weakness of will; it is prudent management under radical uncertainty. The finding that free distribution of nets increased future willingness to pay is actually evidence that experience reduces uncertainty, confirming a Bayesian-learning story, not that people “won’t value what they don’t pay for”—a straw man the authors rightly knock down but then don’t replace with a fully rational alternative.

The essay invokes peer effects and social norms, but these too can emerge from rational calculation. When conditional cash transfers induce the non-poor to attend school more, it may be not because “it’s not much fun being out of school if all your playmates are there,” as the essay suggests, but because the program signals a new social norm around education, or because the value of schooling rises when more peers are educated and can help one another. Peer effects are not inherently irrational; they can be driven by strategic complementarities.

The danger of the behavioral framing is that it directs policy toward fixing the mind rather than the circumstances. If we diagnose the poor as procrastinators, the answer is default enrollment, incentives, and reminders. But if the core problem is that incomes are too low, too uncertain, and capital markets are absent, then the answer is unconditional cash transfers, insurance products, and savings facilitation—all of which can also produce the “big results” while respecting agency. Indeed, a growing body of research shows that simply giving poor people money, with no strings attached, leads to increased investment in education and health, sometimes with larger effects than conditional programs. The authors’ own data from Malawi, where the smallest cash transfer achieved the average effect, hints at this: a small amount of unconditional cash might have done the trick. The fact that a kilogram of lentils boosts immunization suggests not that mothers need to be tricked into valuing their children’s health, but that they need a small economic buffer to absorb the costs (transport, time, risk of side effects) that come with accessing health services.

I am not denying that psychological factors matter. They clearly do. But the balance of evidence in the lead essay itself is more consistent with a model of rational, constrained decision-making than with a model of endemic present bias. The essay’s conclusion that “people are not good at making even small upfront investments in order to obtain a steady stream of modest future benefits” is too sweeping. Many of the same people exhibit striking patience: they save for weddings, religious festivals, and funerals; they manage complex farming strategies across seasons. The fact that they don’t buy chlorine may say more about its perceived value and the competing demands on their purse than about their discount curves.

If we take the rational-constraints view seriously, the policy implications shift. Instead of devising ever more clever nudges, we should concentrate on raising and stabilizing incomes, expanding access to credit and savings, and providing direct cash support. Nudges like the chlorine dispenser may still be useful—but because they lower costs, not because they outsmart human psychology. Recognizing the rationality of the poor would yield development policy that is both more respectful and more attuned to the real, structural barriers they face.
