Can Agape Survive Without God? On the Theological Stakes of Terry's King

Toward the end of his rich essay, Brandon Terry pauses over what he calls the "knotty metaphysics of natural law and Christian ethics" that grounds King's case for nonviolent direct action. He concedes that, "in our more secular and pluralist era," these roots "may invite suspicion," and he wonders whether King's vision can be "suitably rethought in other institutions and traditions." The wondering is brief. Terry then proceeds, in the body of his essay, to translate King's ethical vocabulary—agape, redemptive suffering, the beloved community, the virtues of magnanimity and humility—into a register that is largely secular, philosophical, and Rawlsian. I want to argue that this translation, however well-intentioned, cannot succeed; that the most important things King has to teach us cannot be detached from the theology that produced them; and that any serious recovery of King for our moment has to confront, rather than finesse, what the loss of his religious world does to his politics.

Begin with agape. Terry follows King in distinguishing agape from erotic and romantic love and in defining it as the disposition that treats even political enemies as moral equals whose welfare we must consider. So described, agape can sound like a philosophical doctrine—a kind of universalist Kantianism, a maximally generous reading of the moral law. But that is not what agape is in King's actual usage. Agape, for King, is a participation in the love of God for human beings. It is grounded in the conviction that every person bears the image of God, that this image is not extinguishable by any sin or oppression, and that the appropriate human response to the image is a love that mirrors God's. Strip the theology away and you have a sentiment without a foundation. You can say, with the secular philosopher, that we ought to treat each person as an end in herself. You cannot say, in the same way, that we are commanded to love our enemies, that this love is possible, and that practicing it draws us into a redemptive process whose outcome is guaranteed by something beyond our political failures. The latter set of claims is what gives King's nonviolence its psychological power and its strategic patience. The former set, by itself, is consistent with despair.

The same point applies to redemptive suffering. Kenneth Clark's complaint to King—that it is "too much" to expect victims of cruelty to love their tormentors—reads, in a secular frame, as devastating. And in fact within secular ethics it is devastating. There is no philosophical resource that can convert the suffering of the oppressed into a meaningful contribution to the moral education of the oppressor. The suffering is just suffering. The torment is just torment. To volunteer for more of it, in the hope that it will work some change in the hearts of one's enemies, is, in a purely secular frame, masochism. Terry is honest enough to acknowledge this: "given the ample historical record of unrequited love, unredeemed sacrifice, and failed integration, it is easily argued that such sacrifices are not just beyond the duties of the disadvantaged but indeed masochistic." But he gestures past the worry rather than answering it. The answer King would give—that suffering accepted in love participates in the suffering of Christ and is taken up into the divine work of reconciliation—is not available to most contemporary readers, and Terry knows it. What he does not quite admit is that without that answer, the case for accepting suffering loses much of its force, and what remains is a tactical argument about how unearned suffering affects the consciences of bystanders. That argument was already weakening in King's lifetime, as Malcolm noted. In an age of saturation imagery and political polarization, it has weakened further. Terry says as much in his closing pages. He does not draw the implication: that without the theology, the practice of redemptive nonviolence is on borrowed time.

The point is not, of course, that we should all become Christians or that nonviolence cannot be defended at all on non-theological grounds. There are serious secular cases for nonviolent direct action—Gene Sharp's, for example—that rest on careful empirical analyses of when nonviolent campaigns succeed and why. Those cases are valuable. But they are not King's case. They cannot generate the demand to love one's enemy. They cannot vindicate the practice of suffering as such. They cannot ground the unconditional commitment to the dignity of the oppressor that distinguishes King's politics from a purely instrumental nonviolence. A secularized King is a King reduced to strategy, and a King reduced to strategy is a King whose strategy is in trouble.

Terry's deepest move in the essay is to offer, in place of King's metaphysics, an ethics of virtue: humility, compassion, generosity, courage, thrift, magnanimity. He is right that this language has been unjustly dismissed as "respectability politics" by readers who do not know Higginbotham. He is right that the cultivation of these virtues is essential to the flourishing of the activist and to the moral seriousness of any movement. But he is wrong, I think, to suppose that virtue ethics can be detached from a thicker account of human flourishing without losing its grip. Aristotle's virtues are intelligible only within the polis whose ends they serve. King's virtues are intelligible only within the beloved community whose realization they prepare us for. Without the eschatology—without the conviction that the arc of the moral universe is long but bends toward justice, because God is bending it—the practice of these virtues becomes a personal discipline, not a politics. It can sustain individuals. It cannot organize movements.

There is also a related institutional point, which Terry raises and then declines to develop. The black church was, for King and his generation, not merely a source of moral language. It was the organizational infrastructure of the movement. It provided meeting space, money, networks, training, song, and—decisively—a public sphere insulated from the white gaze, where black political life could be planned. The church is now, as Terry concedes, "a profoundly weakened institution." No comparable institution has emerged to replace it. The Movement for Black Lives is largely organized through social media and through nonprofits. These are powerful coordinating tools, but they cannot do what the church did, because they are not communities that meet weekly, hold one another accountable across decades, and educate children into a shared moral world. The depth of King's commitments was sustained by depth of community, and the depth of his community was sustained by the church. Take the church away and you do not get a secular movement of equivalent strength. You get something thinner and more brittle, however much it can mobilize in the short term.

What follows from all this? Not, I think, that we should abandon King. The contrary. It is that we should be honest, more honest than Terry quite manages to be, about what we are losing when we try to make him available to a post-Christian readership. We are losing the metaphysical guarantee that nonviolence is meaningful even when it fails. We are losing the theological resource that converted suffering from waste into participation. We are losing the institutional ground that made his rhetoric land. The choice before secular admirers of King is therefore harder than Terry suggests. Either we find new sources—and I do not know what they would be—for the depth that his Christianity provided, or we adopt a more honest, more limited, more instrumental nonviolence that does not pretend to be agape. Terry's essay is a serious attempt to keep King's full vision alive in a register stripped of its foundations. I admire the attempt. I am not yet persuaded it can be done.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Agape loses its psychological power and strategic patience when stripped of the theological conviction that it is a participation in God’s love for all humanity.
  2. 02Without the belief that suffering participates in a divine work of reconciliation, the call for activists to accept unearned suffering becomes a form of masochism rather than a meaningful moral contribution.
  3. 03King’s emphasis on personal virtues like humility and magnanimity becomes a mere personal discipline rather than a political force when detached from the eschatological hope of the 'beloved community.'
  4. 04The decline of the black church as a robust organizational infrastructure cannot be compensated for by social media or nonprofits, which lack the same capacity for long-term moral education and community accountability.
  5. 05A secularized version of King reduces his vision to a purely instrumental strategy that is increasingly vulnerable to failure in a polarized and media-saturated age.
