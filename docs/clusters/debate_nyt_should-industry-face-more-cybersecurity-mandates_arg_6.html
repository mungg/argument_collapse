"The federal government should implement mandatory cybersecurity standards for critical infrastructure while simultaneously providing financial and technical assistance to help smaller operators meet those requirements."

The national debate about cybersecurity often imagines giant banks, multinational oil companies and sophisticated hackers in foreign capitals. But a large part of the country’s vulnerability lies in less glamorous places: municipal water systems, rural electric cooperatives, small pipeline operators, regional hospitals and vendors that serve bigger firms. These organizations may not have the budgets or personnel to match the threat. That is why some mandates are necessary — but mandates alone will not be enough.

Large companies often complain that federal standards would be burdensome. For the biggest firms, that argument is not always persuasive. Many have the resources to build security operations centers, hire outside auditors and run exercises. If a company operates assets whose failure could endanger millions, cybersecurity is part of the cost of doing business.

For smaller operators, however, the burden is real. A small utility cannot simply outbid Wall Street or the National Security Agency for top cyber talent. It may run specialized industrial equipment from vendors that no longer provide strong support. It may know it has problems but lack the money to replace systems quickly. If Washington responds only with fines and checklists, it could punish the very organizations that most need help.

A serious policy would combine minimum standards with assistance. Critical infrastructure operators should be required to meet baseline practices: inventory essential systems, separate business networks from control systems where feasible, use strong authentication for remote access, maintain backups, patch known vulnerabilities, train personnel and report significant incidents. These are not exotic demands. They are the cyber equivalent of locking doors and conducting fire drills.

But Congress should also provide grants, technical assistance and shared services for smaller critical providers. State and local governments should be involved because many infrastructure systems are regulated or owned locally. Sector-specific agencies can organize exercises and help operators understand threats in plain language. The federal government should encourage secure procurement so that vendors of industrial control systems have incentives to build safer products from the start.

The threat is serious because interdependence is serious. A small component can create a large failure. A weak vendor can become an entry point into a larger network. A regional disruption can have national economic consequences. Cybersecurity is not only a problem for elite corporations.

The Senate debate has been too binary: mandates versus no mandates. The better distinction is between unfunded, inflexible mandates and a national resilience program. We should require critical providers to meet basic standards, but we should also help them get there. Security is a public good, and public goods require shared investment.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Large corporations have the resources to treat cybersecurity as a standard cost of doing business and should be held to federal standards.
  2. 02Smaller infrastructure operators, such as municipal water systems and rural cooperatives, lack the budget and personnel to compete for cyber talent or replace legacy systems without assistance.
  3. 03Mandates should focus on baseline practices like system inventories, strong authentication, and incident reporting rather than overly complex or exotic demands.
  4. 04The interconnected nature of national infrastructure means that a security failure in a small component or vendor can lead to large-scale national disruptions.
  5. 05Cybersecurity should be treated as a public good that requires shared investment through grants, technical assistance, and secure procurement incentives.
