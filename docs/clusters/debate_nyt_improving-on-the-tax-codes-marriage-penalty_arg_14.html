"The federal government should reform the tax code to eliminate the marriage penalty, ensuring that couples are not financially punished for entering into a legally and socially beneficial institution."

The federal government should not punish people for getting married. That is the simple starting point. Marriage creates legal responsibility: spouses assume duties of support, form a recognized household and, when children are involved, provide the most stable framework government knows how to recognize. Yet the tax code often tells two working adults, especially modest-income parents, that they may be better off remaining unmarried. That is perverse.

The marriage penalty is not merely a problem for affluent professionals comparing brackets. It can be especially painful for lower-income couples whose combined earnings reduce or eliminate benefits such as the earned-income tax credit. For a couple trying to move from insecurity to stability, a few thousand dollars can matter more than a lecture about family values. If public officials say marriage is good for children and communities, they should not design transfer and tax systems that make marriage a financial gamble.

Some critics object that removing the penalty creates a “marriage bonus” for one-earner couples. But that phrase can be misleading. A spouse who stays home with young children, cares for an elderly parent or supports a partner’s work is not a nullity. Joint filing recognizes that marriage is a shared economic enterprise. A single earner supporting two people does not have the same ability to pay as a single earner supporting only himself.

The most practical reform would be to allow married couples to pay no more than they would have paid as two single filers. Congress could also make married brackets and standard deductions fully reflect two adults, and expand marriage relief in the earned-income tax credit. If the cost is serious, it should be financed by simplifying deductions and broadening the base, not by preserving a penalty on family formation.

This spring’s marriage debate has focused on who may enter the institution. That question is important. But for all couples, opposite-sex or same-sex where states recognize them, the logic should be the same: government should not celebrate marriage in speeches and then penalize it in ledgers.

The tax code cannot make people marry, and it should not try to coerce them. But it can at least stop making the responsible choice more expensive. Neutrality is the minimum; a modest preference for marriage is defensible. A penalty is not.
