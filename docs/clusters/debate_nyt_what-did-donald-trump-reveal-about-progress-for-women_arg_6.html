"The tendency of male politicians to condemn Donald Trump’s comments by invoking their roles as husbands and fathers reveals that society still views women’s dignity as dependent on their relationships to men rather than as an inherent right."

As the political world reels from the release of Donald Trump’s 2005 hot-mic comments, an interesting pattern has emerged in the condemnations raining down upon him. Politician after politician, particularly men in the Republican Party, have stepped forward to denounce the Republican nominee. But pay close attention to *how* they are doing it.

"As the father of three daughters..." begins one statement. "As a husband and a father..." echoes another. "I have a wife, I have daughters..."

While the repudiation of Trump’s boasts about sexual assault is certainly welcome, the rhetorical framing of this outrage reveals a deeply frustrating limitation in our societal progress regarding women. The instinct to invoke a familial relationship to a woman in order to validate outrage over her mistreatment is rooted in a fundamentally patriarchal worldview. It implies that a woman’s dignity is not inherent, but rather derived from her proximity to a man.

This "wives and daughters" trope suggests that men cannot simply empathize with women as equal human beings. Instead, they must filter the offense through their own male experience: *How would I feel if another man did this to MY property?* It is an old, paternalistic framework dressed up as modern moral indignation. It frames sexual harassment and assault not as violations of a woman’s basic human rights and bodily autonomy, but as an insult to the men who protect them.

We do not see this in other realms of civil rights. When politicians condemn racism, they do not generally start by saying, "As someone who has a Black friend..." When they condemn religious bigotry, they do not require a personal connection to a marginalized faith to recognize that the bigotry is wrong. Yet when it comes to women, the male political class still struggles to view them as autonomous individuals who deserve respect simply by virtue of existing.

True progress for women will not be achieved when men only care about sexual assault because they happen to be fathers of daughters. True progress will be marked by a society that recognizes women as complete, sovereign individuals. The reaction to the Trump tape shows that we are still stuck in a paradigm of benevolent sexism—one where women are to be protected by good men from bad men, rather than respected as equals by all men. Until we can condemn misogyny without needing to check our family trees first, our progress remains painfully incomplete.
