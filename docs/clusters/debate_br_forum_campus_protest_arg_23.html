Diversified Gemini

"Robin D. G. Kelley’s critique of contemporary student activism fails to recognize that the focus on trauma and mental health is a radical extension of Black feminist politics rather than a depoliticizing neoliberal trap."

In his essay "Black Study, Black Struggle," Robin D. G. Kelley provides a sweeping and insightful critique of the neoliberal university and the limitations of liberal multiculturalism. Yet, in his critique of the contemporary student movement, Kelley reveals a profound blind spot regarding the politics of care, mental health, and emotional labor—elements that have long been central to Black feminist thought. By dismissing students’ reliance on the language of trauma as a depoliticizing "trick" of neoliberalism, Kelley inadvertently marginalizes the lived realities of Black women and queer students who are leading these movements, and he upholds a deeply masculinized vision of what constitutes legitimate political "struggle."

Kelley observes that students frequently frame their grievances around personal trauma, demanding safe spaces and better mental health services. He argues that this focus "shifts race from the public sphere to the psyche," elevating personal pain over the creation of social justice. To counter this, he points to the political study groups of his youth, citing heavyweights like Marx, Fanon, Rodney, and Lenin, and calls for a "ruthless criticism of everything existing."

But Kelley misses a crucial lesson of the Black feminist tradition: the personal *is* political. When Audre Lorde wrote, "Caring for myself is not self-indulgence, it is self-preservation, and that is an act of political warfare," she was articulating a radical truth that today's campus activists deeply understand. In a society—and a university system—that extracts the intellectual and emotional labor of Black women while treating their bodies as disposable, demanding the resources to heal is not a retreat from politics. It is a direct confrontation with a system designed to work us to death.

The "trauma" that Kelley warns against centering is not an abstraction or a mere buzzword adopted from human resources manuals. It is the physical and psychological toll of navigating white supremacy. When Black students demand mental health services, they are recognizing that structural oppression exacts a biological cost. High blood pressure, severe anxiety, and depression are rampant among marginalized students at elite institutions. You cannot engage in the rigorous "Black study" or the "relentless struggle" that Kelley demands if you are having a panic attack in your dorm room. Dismissing the demand for mental health support as a distraction from "true" radicalism ignores the fact that emotional well-being is a prerequisite for sustained organizing.

Furthermore, Kelley’s framing of struggle relies heavily on a traditional, outward-facing militancy. He champions Harney and Moten’s "guerrillas," the Mississippi Freedom Schools, and the militant dismantling of capitalism. While he does beautifully highlight the Mt. Vernon collective of Black feminists (Patricia Robinson, Patricia Haden, Donna Middleton), he uses their work to argue that activism "cannot stop" at trauma, emphasizing their call to overthrow capitalism. But he underplays *how* they worked: focusing on the internal lives, the psychology, and the immediate community needs of poor Black women and children.

The current student movements—groups like Black Lives Matter and the campus coalitions Kelley mentions—are overwhelmingly led by Black women and queer people. Their insistence on centering trauma, vulnerability, and safety is a deliberate rejection of the patriarchal civil rights and Black Power models that demanded Black people (specifically men) present as stoic, unbreakable soldiers for the cause. The modern movement insists that we do not have to be invincible to be worthy of liberation. We are allowed to be broken, and we are allowed to demand the time and space to heal.

Kelley’s worry that trauma-talk leads to seeing ourselves as "victims and objects rather than agents" is a false dichotomy. Naming oneself as a victim of systemic violence is often the first, necessary step toward claiming agency. When students demand trigger warnings or safe spaces, they are not asking to be coddled; they are asserting their right to curate their educational environment in a way that allows them to engage with the material on their own terms, without being re-traumatized by professors who treat anti-Black violence as a mere intellectual exercise.

Ultimately, Kelley’s call to "love, study, struggle" is a powerful one, but his definition of these terms needs expansion. Love must include a radical grace for our own battered psyches. Study must include an interrogation of how trauma lives in the body. And struggle must include the fight for institutional resources that allow us to survive the university intact. The students are not adopting the language of their oppressors; they are forcing the university to confront the human cost of its complicity. That is not neoliberalism. That is survival.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The demand for mental health resources is a direct confrontation with a university system that extracts labor from Black bodies while treating them as disposable.
  2. 02Emotional well-being and healing are necessary prerequisites for the sustained intellectual study and political struggle that Kelley advocates.
  3. 03Kelley’s dismissal of trauma-centered activism upholds a masculinized, stoic vision of struggle that marginalizes the leadership and lived experiences of Black women and queer students.
  4. 04Naming systemic trauma is a step toward claiming agency rather than an acceptance of victimhood, as it allows students to engage with the university on their own terms.
  5. 05The focus on internal life and community care in modern movements is a deliberate and valid rejection of patriarchal civil rights models that demanded invincibility from activists.
