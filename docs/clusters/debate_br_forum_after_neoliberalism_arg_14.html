"While the authors of the lead essay correctly argue that economics can be rescued from neoliberalism to serve inclusive prosperity, their project must go beyond technocratic design to confront the political power dynamics and professional biases that have historically sustained the status quo."

Dani Rodrik, Suresh Naidu, and Gabriel Zucman are right about the central fact of our political economy: the old consensus has broken down, and it will not be put back together by repeating the formulas that helped break it. The combination of stagnant wages, extreme concentration of wealth, precarious work, regional decline, unaffordable health care, and ecological danger has made “inclusive prosperity” not a slogan but a necessity. A society that delivers growth without broadly shared security will eventually lose both social peace and democratic legitimacy.

Their most important contribution is to separate economics from neoliberalism. This distinction matters. In public debate, “economics” too often means a handful of policy reflexes: cut taxes, deregulate, liberalize trade, weaken unions, privatize public goods, and assume that whatever happens in markets is efficient unless government has interfered. But this is not economics as a discipline. It is a political program that selectively borrows from economics, strips away the qualifications, and presents contestable assumptions as scientific law.

The authors are also right that contemporary economics contains the resources for a different program. Labor markets are not the frictionless spot markets of textbook diagrams. Employers often have monopsony power. Workers lack information, mobility, savings, and bargaining leverage. Minimum wage research over the last quarter century has shown that modest increases do not necessarily destroy jobs, especially where employers have wage-setting power. Finance is not a neutral plumbing system; it generates leverage, instability, and crises. Trade creates aggregate gains but can devastate particular workers and places, and the political system has repeatedly failed to provide meaningful adjustment. Climate change is perhaps the most obvious market failure in history. Health care and education are riddled with externalities, information asymmetries, and moral commitments that make simple market provision inadequate.

In this sense, the authors’ project is less a revolt against economics than a rescue of economics from caricature. It asks economists to do what the best of them have always done: examine institutions, incentives, power, constraints, and evidence rather than repeat slogans. That is a welcome intervention.

Still, the project will succeed only if it avoids two temptations. The first is the temptation to present inclusive prosperity as a technocratic correction to neoliberalism. The second is the temptation to treat the profession’s past political role as mostly a misunderstanding.

On the first point, one can applaud wage boards, stronger unions, corporate tax reform, financial regulation, social insurance, and pro-worker technology policy while still asking: who will fight for these things? The authors emphasize evidence and institutional design, but inclusive prosperity is not merely a design challenge. It is a conflict over power. The winners of the existing order—multinational corporations, wealthy households, dominant technology firms, financial institutions, and their political allies—will not step aside because better models have been written. They will resist. They already do.

Consider Zucman’s proposal to allocate multinational profits according to sales. It is elegant and promising. It directly addresses the absurdity of a world in which companies book profits in tax havens where little real activity occurs. But the obstacle is not intellectual. Many policymakers already know the current system is indefensible. The obstacle is political: the firms that benefit from tax avoidance have resources, lawyers, lobbyists, and the ability to threaten relocation. Similarly, economists have accumulated evidence that unions reduce inequality and that labor’s decline has contributed to wage stagnation. Yet rebuilding unions requires more than recognizing a collective action problem. It requires changing labor law against intense business opposition.

The authors know this. Their discussion of power is one of the essay’s strengths. But the policy program sometimes sounds as if better economics can make a more equal economy appear through persuasion. In reality, better economics can clarify the stakes and puncture false objections; it cannot substitute for social movements, electoral coalitions, administrative capacity, and political confrontation.

The second temptation concerns the discipline itself. Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman acknowledge that economists have bad habits: excessive faith in first-best reasoning, disdain for other disciplines, overconfidence in markets, and narrowness in policy analysis. But the indictment should go deeper. For decades, many economists did not merely fail to prevent neoliberalism from appropriating their language; they helped legitimate it. When trade liberalization was sold to the public, economists emphasized aggregate gains and treated losses as side payments to be handled later. Later often never came. When financial deregulation advanced, too many economists trusted models that treated markets as self-correcting and risk as efficiently priced. When austerity policies were justified after crises, economists were frequently found on both sides, but the aura of economic expertise helped elevate deficit panic over employment and public investment.

This does not mean economics is inherently reactionary. It means the sociology of the profession matters more than the authors admit. What questions are rewarded? Which assumptions are considered elegant? Which journals define excellence? Which policy institutions hire economists? Which kinds of evidence count? A discipline can contain excellent tools and still have a professional culture that nudges its members toward deference to capital, suspicion of popular demands, and impatience with democratic messiness.

The authors’ own list of proposals is encouraging precisely because it challenges those tendencies. Wage boards would treat labor standards as a matter of institutional bargaining, not merely individual contracting. Stronger union rights would recognize that workers need countervailing power. Profit apportionment would confront tax competition rather than accommodate it. Social safeguards in trade agreements would subordinate certain forms of global integration to domestic social contracts. These are not minor tweaks; they imply a different hierarchy of values.

But that hierarchy should be made explicit. “Inclusive prosperity” should not mean “maximize GDP, then distribute better.” It should mean that the economy is judged by whether people have secure livelihoods, democratic voice, equal citizenship, ecological sustainability, and freedom from domination. Growth matters, but it is not the master value. A coal miner whose community has collapsed, a care worker earning poverty wages, a renter displaced by speculation, or a diabetic rationing insulin cannot be answered with an aggregate welfare theorem.

The next economics should therefore be institutional, empirical, and democratic. Institutional, because markets are made by law, norms, public investment, and social power. Empirical, because we have had enough ideology masquerading as necessity. Democratic, because economics cannot tell us how to weigh liberty, equality, security, dignity, and sustainability. Those are political judgments.

The lead essay opens a door. It tells non-economists that they need not reject economics to reject market fundamentalism. That is true and important. But economists should also hear a message from non-economists: trust will not be restored by saying “you misunderstood us.” It will be restored when economists show, repeatedly and publicly, that they are willing to challenge concentrated power, learn from other disciplines, take social movements seriously, and treat democratic values as more than constraints on efficient policy.

After neoliberalism, economics can help. But it must be an economics that knows its place: indispensable, not sovereign.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The lead essay correctly distinguishes between the scientific discipline of economics and the political program of neoliberalism that has selectively co-opted economic language.
  2. 02Inclusive prosperity is not merely a technical design challenge but a political conflict that requires social movements and electoral coalitions to overcome the resistance of powerful interest groups.
  3. 03The economics profession must acknowledge its own historical role in legitimizing neoliberal policies rather than treating the association as a simple misunderstanding by the public.
  4. 04The sociology of the economics profession, including its internal incentives and professional culture, often nudges practitioners toward deference to capital and suspicion of democratic demands.
  5. 05A truly inclusive economic framework must explicitly subordinate GDP growth to values such as ecological sustainability, democratic voice, and freedom from domination.
  6. 06To restore public trust, economics must function as an indispensable tool for democratic debate rather than a sovereign authority that dictates policy outcomes.
