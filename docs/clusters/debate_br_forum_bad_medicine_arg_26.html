"Marcia Angell’s critique of the pharmaceutical industry is undermined by a fundamental misunderstanding of how 'me-too' drugs and market incentives drive medical progress and patient care."

In "Big Pharma, Bad Medicine," Marcia Angell delivers a sweeping critique of the pharmaceutical industry, arguing that its profit-driven nature is fundamentally at odds with the goals of medical innovation. However, her economically illiterate dismissal of how the pharmaceutical market actually functions undermines her argument. Specifically, her casual derision of "me-too" drugs—which she paints as the ultimate proof of industry laziness and greed—reveals a deep misunderstanding of pharmacology, patient care, and market economics.

Dr. Angell asserts that drug companies are "supposed to develop profitable drugs, not necessarily important or innovative ones, and paradoxically enough, the most profitable drugs are the least innovative." She points to the proliferation of "me-too" drugs—compounds that are structurally similar to existing first-in-class drugs—as evidence that the industry has abandoned real research. This narrative is popular among critics of capitalism, but it is scientifically and economically backwards. Incremental innovation is the bedrock of medical progress, and "me-too" drugs are essential to public health for two critical reasons: biological diversity and price competition.

First, from a clinical perspective, humans are not identical biological machines. A drug that works miraculously well for one patient may cause intolerable side effects in another. Consider the statin class of cholesterol-lowering drugs, or SSRI antidepressants, or ACE inhibitors for blood pressure. The first drug in a class is rarely the best one. It is often the subsequent "me-too" drugs that refine the mechanism, improve the safety profile, or reduce harmful drug-drug interactions.

If we adopted Angell’s implicitly preferred model—where only "truly innovative" first-in-class drugs are approved and rewarded—a patient who suffers severe muscle pain from the first statin brought to market would simply be out of luck. The availability of multiple drugs within the same therapeutic class allows physicians to tailor treatments to individual patients. To dismiss this massive clinical benefit as mere corporate profiteering is to ignore the daily reality of patient care.

Second, from an economic perspective, "me-too" drugs are the primary mechanism for controlling drug costs before patents expire. If a company invents a novel drug and holds a monopoly on that entire therapeutic class, they can charge whatever they want. The only force that restrains their pricing power is the introduction of a competitor's "me-too" drug. When a second, third, or fourth drug enters the same therapeutic space, pharmaceutical companies must compete for market share. They negotiate with pharmacy benefit managers (PBMs) and insurance companies, offering significant rebates and discounts to secure a place on the formulary. This invisible price competition saves patients and the healthcare system billions of dollars.

Angell’s contempt for the profit motive blinds her to the fact that it is precisely the pursuit of profit that drives companies to develop these necessary alternative treatments. She decries the fact that pharmaceutical companies make more in profits than they spend on R&D. But this is the nature of a high-risk, high-reward industry. For every blockbuster drug that makes it to market, there are dozens of failed compounds that cost hundreds of millions of dollars, representing total losses for the investors. The high profits on successful drugs are the necessary risk premium that attracts capital to the sector in the first place.

Instead of trying to dismantle the profit motive or sever the vital ties between academic research and industry development, we should harness market forces more effectively. If we want more research into novel antibiotics, we shouldn't ask companies to act against their fiduciary duties; we should alter the market incentives through government prizes, tax credits, or extended patent exclusivity for priority diseases. Denouncing the pharmaceutical industry for acting like a business is a futile exercise. We must work with the realities of market economics to drive innovation, not retreat into an academic fantasy where progress is fueled solely by altruism.
