Diversified MiniMax

"While the Shuanghui-Smithfield deal is unlikely to cause a national security catastrophe, American regulators should use this acquisition as an opportunity to strengthen and refine their review protocols for agricultural deals."

The debate over Shuanghui's bid for Smithfield has generated more heat than light, with both sides oversimplifying a genuinely complex issue.

Critics who warn of imminent national security catastrophe are overstating the case. America will not become nutritionally dependent on China because one meat company changes owners. The food supply chain involves thousands of independent producers, distributors, and retailers—concentration in one segment doesn't equate to control of the whole system. CFIUS has reviewed foreign acquisitions of American food companies before without identifying the catastrophic risks some now predict.

Similarly, advocates who dismiss all concern as protectionism miss the genuine strategic questions involved. Food systems matter to national security in ways that became clearer during the 2008 price crisis and subsequent volatility. Ceding control of major production infrastructure to companies from a country that is, at minimum, a strategic competitor deserves thoughtful analysis rather than breezy free-trade platitudes.

The right framework involves asking specific questions: What specific facilities does Smithfield operate near military bases or other sensitive sites? What research and development assets would transfer to Chinese ownership? How would ownership affect food safety practices and supply chain transparency? These are empirical questions, and the answers should drive regulatory decisions.

My inclination is that this deal will proceed and that the sky will not fall. But "we probably won't regret this" is not the same as "we thoughtfully evaluated the risks." CFIUS should use this occasion to strengthen its review protocols for agricultural acquisitions, to engage more deliberately with food security experts, and to communicate its reasoning more transparently to the public. Whether the deal is approved or not, the review process can be improved—and that improvement serves American interests regardless of the outcome.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Concerns that the acquisition will lead to national nutritional dependence on China are overstated due to the decentralized nature of the American food supply chain.
  2. 02Dismissing all opposition to the deal as protectionism ignores legitimate strategic concerns regarding the control of major production infrastructure by a strategic competitor.
  3. 03Regulatory decisions should be based on empirical questions regarding proximity to sensitive sites, the transfer of R&D assets, and impacts on food safety transparency.
  4. 04The Committee on Foreign Investment in the United States (CFIUS) needs to improve its transparency and engagement with food security experts to better serve American interests.
