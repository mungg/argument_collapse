"To effectively counter a hostile Supreme Court, progressives must move beyond recovering constitutional traditions and adopt an operational governing strategy centered on legislative redundancy, institutional diversification, and state capacity."

The lead essay is right to warn that the next great obstacle to progressive reform may not be winning public support or even passing legislation, but surviving judicial review. On voting rights, labor law, campaign finance, climate regulation, health care, racial justice, taxation, antitrust, and corporate governance, any ambitious Democratic Congress would face a Supreme Court more than willing to entertain constitutional theories that would have seemed implausible a generation ago. The lesson is not that progressives should ignore courts. It is that they should plan for constitutional conflict before it arrives.

But planning for conflict requires more than recovering a tradition. The lead essay’s account of the democracy-of-opportunity tradition is valuable, even inspiring. It reminds progressives that they are not constitutional trespassers when they seek to disperse power and secure equal citizenship. Yet the immediate challenge is operational. How should legislators, administrators, organizers, and lawyers design reforms in a world where a hostile Court may strike some of them down? What does constitutional politics look like as a governing strategy?

The first rule should be redundancy. Progressive legislation should be built with fallback provisions, severability clauses, alternative authorities, and multiple implementation pathways. The lead essay briefly suggests this in discussing wealth taxation: if Congress enacts a wealth tax, it should also prepare alternative taxes that take effect if the Court invalidates the first design. That principle should be generalized. A Congress serious about governing under judicial uncertainty should legislate the way engineers build bridges: assuming stress, anticipating failure points, and preventing collapse.

For example, if Congress wants to reduce concentrated wealth, it should not rely on a single constitutionally vulnerable instrument. It can combine estate tax reform, mark-to-market taxation where feasible, corporate minimum taxes, surtaxes on high incomes, stronger enforcement against avoidance, excise taxes on stock buybacks, and reforms to capital gains treatment. Some tools may be less elegant than a direct wealth tax. But a package with overlapping mechanisms is harder for courts to disable in one blow.

The same applies to voting rights. A new Voting Rights Act should be supported by extensive findings, updated coverage formulas, nationwide rules, private rights of action, federal enforcement authority, election administration funding, and constitutional grounding in multiple amendments. If preclearance is narrowed, other rules should remain. If one coverage formula falls, another should activate. If private enforcement is restricted, federal enforcement capacity should be strengthened. The goal is not only to win the ideal constitutional argument but to ensure that voters are protected even if judges reject part of it.

The second rule should be institutional diversification. The lead essay focuses heavily on Congress and the Supreme Court, understandably. But progressive constitutional politics must be federal, state, local, administrative, and organizational. State constitutions often contain affirmative education clauses, labor protections, environmental provisions, privacy guarantees, and democratic commitments that have no federal equivalent. State courts can be reactionary, but they can also be sites of innovation. City governments can experiment with public financing, labor standards, tenant protections, procurement rules, and public options. Agencies can enforce existing statutes with renewed attention to power disparities.

This matters because national reform is bottlenecked by the Senate, filibuster, and Court. A democracy-of-opportunity politics that waits for unified federal power will remain mostly aspirational. The right understood this long ago. It built power through state legislatures, school boards, attorneys general, legal networks, and administrative appointments. Progressives need a similarly distributed strategy. Constitutional argument should be heard not only in Supreme Court briefs but in state legislative hearings, agency rulemakings, union campaigns, ballot initiatives, city charters, and public utility commissions.

The third rule should be record-building. Courts often pretend to decide legal questions in abstraction, but factual records matter. Congress’s failure to assemble the right record can make legislation vulnerable; a strong record can make judicial invalidation look more openly political. Before passing major reforms, committees should document how concentrated economic power distorts democracy, how voter suppression operates, how employer coercion defeats worker choice, how racial inequality is reproduced through facially neutral systems, how monopoly power harms workers and communities, and how climate risk threatens national welfare. These records should be public-facing, not only lawyer-facing.

The original Voting Rights Act was powerful partly because Congress built an undeniable record of disenfranchisement. Modern legislation requires the same seriousness. If Congress seeks to regulate corporate political spending, it should document channels of influence, dependence, corruption risks, and the inadequacy of disclosure alone. If it seeks labor reform, it should document captive-audience meetings, retaliation, fissured employment, misclassification, and the decline of worker voice. If it seeks antitrust reform, it should document harms beyond consumer prices: wages, suppliers, innovation, privacy, local communities, and political influence.

The fourth rule should be administrative competence. Constitutional politics is not only about winning arguments; it is about making government worthy of trust. The pandemic showed both the capacity and the fragility of the American state. Direct relief payments, expanded unemployment benefits, the child tax credit, vaccine development, and emergency public health measures demonstrated that government can act at scale. But administrative failures, confusing eligibility rules, state-level unemployment breakdowns, and uneven public health messaging damaged confidence.

If progressives argue that the Constitution permits and even requires democratic government to secure material foundations, they must show that democratic government can deliver. A poorly administered benefit becomes an argument for austerity. A confusing regulatory scheme becomes an argument for deregulation. A public program that is hard to access empowers those who say government cannot solve problems. Social Security and Medicare endure not because they are constitutionally theorized but because people experience them as reliable. Any revived constitutional political economy must be paired with a revival of state capacity.

The fifth rule should be movement partnership. The lead essay rightly emphasizes labor, racial justice movements, antimonopoly advocates, and campaigns for democratic reform. But constitutional arguments cannot remain the property of professors and appellate lawyers. Workers organizing a warehouse, tenants resisting eviction, formerly incarcerated people fighting disenfranchisement, debtors challenging predatory lending, and communities opposing environmental racism all have constitutional stories to tell. They know what domination feels like before it is named in doctrine.

Yet partnership requires humility from legal elites. Movements often need lawyers, but they do not need every demand translated into the safest possible constitutional language at the outset. Sometimes disruption, moral clarity, and ambitious demands shift the terrain more effectively than carefully tailored arguments. The role of constitutional lawyers should be to expand options, not narrow imagination. They can help identify powers Congress already has, draft durable statutes, defend local experiments, and expose judicial overreach. They should not become gatekeepers of political possibility.

The sixth rule should be democratic reform as foundation. The lead essay discusses campaign finance and voting rights, but these should be understood not as separate issue areas but as prerequisites for everything else. A polity distorted by gerrymandering, voter suppression, malapportioned representation, dark money, partisan election administration, and the filibuster cannot reliably enact or sustain egalitarian reform. The Constitution’s republican aspiration is hollow if legislative majorities cannot govern.

Progressives should therefore prioritize a democracy agenda: voting rights protections, independent redistricting or national anti-gerrymandering standards, statehood for disenfranchised citizens where appropriate, campaign finance public matching, election administration funding, protection against election subversion, and reform of Senate rules. Some of these measures face constitutional challenge. All face political difficulty. But without them, the democracy-of-opportunity tradition becomes a sermon delivered to institutions designed to ignore majorities.

The seventh rule should be a realistic approach to the Supreme Court. The lead essay is right that progressives should not treat the Court as the sole interpreter of the Constitution. But they should be clear about what follows. If the Court continues to invalidate major legislation on aggressive and contestable grounds, political actors must consider structural responses: term limits, ethics rules, transparency requirements, jurisdictional regulation, expansion of the lower courts, and perhaps expansion of the Supreme Court itself. These proposals differ in feasibility and wisdom, but they belong on the table.

At the same time, court reform cannot be a substitute for popular legitimacy. Many Americans do not follow doctrine closely, but they do have intuitions about fairness, democracy, rights, and institutional abuse. If progressives want to challenge judicial supremacy, they must explain not only that the Court is wrong but why its decisions harm people’s ability to govern themselves. “The Court sided with corporations against workers.” “The Court made it easier for states to suppress votes.” “The Court blocked Congress from providing health care.” “The Court invented a rule that protects money as power.” These claims should be precise, repeated, and connected to everyday consequences.

The eighth rule should be constitutional pluralism. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition is an important resource, but it should not become a single master narrative. Different communities draw on different constitutional memories: Reconstruction, abolitionism, Indigenous sovereignty, labor republicanism, feminist equality, disability justice, immigrant rights, religious dissent, civil libertarianism, environmental stewardship. A successful progressive constitutional politics will not flatten these into one story. It will create alliances among them.

This matters because there are real tensions inside the progressive coalition. Labor rights and environmental regulation can collide in fossil fuel regions unless joined by a credible just transition. Religious liberty and LGBTQ equality require careful institutional design. Race-conscious remedies and universal programs must be balanced strategically. Antitrust enforcement against technology platforms may intersect uneasily with concerns about speech moderation and disinformation. Public health authority must be defended without indifference to civil liberties. A constitutional politics that denies these tensions will sound propagandistic. One that acknowledges them can build trust.

Finally, progressives should avoid promising that constitutional argument alone can overcome political weakness. The conservative legal movement did not triumph simply because it had a better story. It built institutions for decades: law school networks, judicial pipelines, think tanks, donor alliances, litigation shops, media ecosystems, and party discipline. Progressives have begun to build counter-institutions, but they remain uneven. A democracy-of-opportunity constitutionalism will need candidate recruitment, judicial nominations, clerkship networks, state solicitor general offices, public-interest litigation, popular education, labor education, and sustained funding. Ideas matter when they are carried by institutions.

The lead essay’s core insight remains indispensable: progressive reformers should stop approaching the Constitution as hostile terrain. The Constitution has been invoked to defend slavery and property, but also to destroy slavery, reconstruct citizenship, protect labor, expand voting, and authorize national action against private domination. It does not belong to judges alone, and certainly not to the current Court majority. Citizens and legislators have constitutional responsibilities too.

But the next step is to turn that insight into craft. Draft laws with fallbacks. Build records. Use state and local power. Strengthen administration. Partner with movements. Reform democracy first. Prepare for judicial attack. Explain the stakes in ordinary language. Develop institutions capable of sustaining conflict over years, not news cycles.

The question is not merely whether progressive politics should be constitutional again. It must also become strategic again. A hostile Court can defeat isolated reforms. It will have a harder time defeating a movement that legislates redundantly, governs competently, organizes broadly, and speaks constitutionally in the name of democratic power.
