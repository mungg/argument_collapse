Diversified Gemini

"University administrators have a legal and moral obligation to dismantle campus encampments because they represent an unsafe physical occupation of shared resources rather than a protected form of free speech."

As a former university administrator who spent the better part of the last two years managing the fallout from campus encampments, I read Alex Gourevitch’s “The Right to Be Hostile” with a mixture of profound frustration and weary familiarity. Gourevitch writes from the insulated vantage point of the faculty lounge, where the consequences of "disruption" are entirely theoretical. In his framing, the university is a villainous monolith, bending the knee to right-wing politicians and wealthy donors, eagerly crushing the idealistic expressions of its students.

The reality of running a modern university—a physical municipality of tens of thousands of people, operating 24 hours a day, 365 days a year—is vastly more complex than his binary of "free speech" versus "McCarthyism." Gourevitch’s essay represents a fundamental misunderstanding of the Time, Place, and Manner (TPM) framework, the legal and moral obligations of institutional liability, and the sheer logistical nightmare of indefinite physical occupations of shared space.

Let us begin with the concept of the “commons.” Gourevitch argues that the campus quad should be treated as a First Amendment public forum, an “ungoverned” or “less governed” space. But a university quad is not a city park. A city park is funded by municipal taxes and maintained by city workers for general public use. A university quad is maintained by the institution specifically to serve its educational and residential mission.

When a group of students (and, as was routinely the case last year, unaffiliated outside activists) decides to permanently occupy the central artery of a campus, they are not merely engaging in speech. They are physically commandeering a shared resource. Gourevitch minimizes this, stating that “students could still access their dorms and classes.” In many cases, this was simply false. But even when alternate routes were available, why should thousands of tuition-paying students be forced to alter their daily lives to accommodate the physical occupation of a specific political faction?

The logistics of these encampments are entirely absent from Gourevitch’s analysis. When 200 people pitch tents on a lawn for four weeks, who handles the sanitation? Who ensures fire lanes are kept clear? At my institution, we had instances of extension cords running across wet grass to power space heaters inside highly flammable nylon tents. We had human waste issues. We had severe ADA compliance failures because wheelchair ramps were blocked by barricades erected by protesters to "defend" themselves.

Are university administrators supposed to ignore the fire marshal? Are we supposed to tell disabled students that their civil right to access buildings is temporarily suspended because another group of students has a “right to be hostile”?

Gourevitch waves away the concept of trespass, noting that universities have tolerated encampments in the past. It is true that universities have often exercised immense forbearance. But forbearance is a managerial choice, not a legal right granted to the protesters. When an administration decides that an encampment has crossed the line from a temporary demonstration into an indefinite, unsafe occupation, it has every right to enforce its property laws. Gourevitch asserts that enforcing these laws is a "betrayal of the educational mission." I argue the exact opposite: allowing a loud, unpermitted, unsanitary, and physically exclusionary camp to dominate the campus center for weeks on end is an abdication of our duty to the 95% of students who are simply trying to study, sleep, and go to class.

Furthermore, Gourevitch severely underplays the physical security challenges involved. He casually notes that “universities have fashioned themselves as safety-guaranteeing institutions” as if this is a negative development driven by “anxious parents.” But universities *are* legally liable for the physical safety of the people on their property. During the Gaza protests, the sheer volume of violent rhetoric—both from the encampments and from the counter-protesters they attracted—created an incredibly volatile environment.

We saw outside groups, armed with pepper spray and makeshift weapons, descending on our campus to clash with protesters. Our campus public safety officers, who are trained primarily to handle noise complaints and petty theft, were suddenly expected to manage hundreds of screaming, hostile demonstrators. When Gourevitch criticizes universities for bringing in local police, he fails to explain what the alternative is when a crowd of 500 people refuses a lawful order to disperse and physical altercations begin breaking out. Are administrators supposed to just cross their fingers and hope nobody gets killed? (As he himself notes, UCLA’s failure to intervene quickly led to disastrous mob violence—yet he uses this to blame the administration for not "defending the right to protest," an astonishing rhetorical contortion).

Then there is the issue of Title VI and the hostile environment standard. Gourevitch accuses universities of bowing to the “subjective feelings” of Jewish students. He writes that “feelings of unease would never suffice to meet the legal standard of a ‘hostile environment.’” But he completely ignores the cumulative effect of the behavior we witnessed.

It was not just "speech." It was the physical surrounding of Jewish students. It was the vandalization of Hillel buildings. It was the chanting of slogans demanding the eradication of the state where half the world's Jews live, piped through megaphones directly outside freshman dormitories at 2:00 AM.

Gourevitch demands "viewpoint-neutral enforcement." I strongly agree. If a group of anti-immigrant students set up a permanent encampment on the quad, plastered the area with xenophobic slogans, banged drums outside the dorms of Latino students, and blocked pathways while demanding the university divest from any companies employing undocumented labor, how long would Gourevitch demand we "tolerate" it? Would he write 5,000 words defending their “right to be hostile”? Of course not. The progressive faculty who championed the Gaza encampments would be pounding on the doors of the administration building within an hour, demanding police intervention to protect marginalized students from "literal violence."

This is the impossible position of the university administrator today. We are squeezed between a faculty that views the university as a sandbox for radical political theater, a federal government threatening our funding if we do not strictly enforce Title VI, and a student body that has been told that their emotional comfort is paramount.

Gourevitch yearns for the days of 1968. But the protests of 1968 caused massive, lasting damage to the institutions they targeted. They resulted in decades of severed relationships, delayed academic careers, and severe community backlash. Today’s administrators are trying to prevent our institutions from tearing themselves apart.

Enforcing Time, Place, and Manner restrictions is not "McCarthyism." Clearing unpermitted overnight encampments is not the death of free speech. Punishing students who repeatedly violate the terms of their enrollment is not "collective punishment." It is the bare minimum required to maintain a functioning academic community. Universities must be places of fierce, uncomfortable debate. But they cannot function if we surrender our physical and operational infrastructure to whichever group is willing to be the most hostile.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Campus quads are not public parks but private property maintained specifically to serve the university's educational and residential mission.
  2. 02Indefinite encampments create severe logistical, sanitation, and safety hazards that administrators cannot legally or ethically ignore.
  3. 03The physical occupation of campus arteries infringes upon the civil rights and daily lives of the majority of students who are not participating in the protest.
  4. 04Universities are legally liable for the physical safety of everyone on campus and must intervene when demonstrations attract outside agitators and volatile conflict.
  5. 05The behavior of protesters often exceeds 'subjective unease' and constitutes a genuine hostile environment through physical intimidation and targeted harassment.
  6. 06The demand for absolute tolerance of hostility is hypocritical, as the same faculty members would likely demand intervention if the protests targeted different marginalized groups.
  7. 07Enforcing reasonable time, place, and manner restrictions is a necessary administrative function to maintain a functioning academic community, not a form of political repression.
