"To recapture those who have drifted away, religious leaders must focus on restoring institutional credibility through repentance and service rather than focusing on recruitment or image management."

Religious leaders should notice that the problem is not merely attendance; it is credibility. Many Americans still identify as Christian, and many Jews still identify strongly as Jews, because inherited identity has deep roots. But when it comes to institutions, people are asking a harder question: why should I trust you?

For churches, the answer has been damaged by scandal and partisanship. The Catholic sexual abuse crisis, the association of evangelicalism with Republican politics, fights over homosexuality and contraception, and the perception that churches are quicker to condemn than to heal have all taken a toll. This is not true of every congregation, of course, and many churches do heroic work. But public perception matters. If the loudest Christian voices are angry, defensive or obsessed with other people’s private lives, many nominal Christians will stay nominal.

Jewish institutions have a different but related credibility challenge. Some Jews experience synagogues as expensive, insular or too focused on survival for its own sake. Others feel alienated by communal debates over Israel, where disagreement can quickly become a test of loyalty. Younger Jews may care deeply about justice, pluralism and Jewish memory, yet not see those commitments reflected in the institutions asking for their dues.

So the first work is repentance, not recruitment. Churches should be visibly safer for children and vulnerable adults, more transparent with money and power, and more willing to admit wrongdoing. Synagogues should examine whether they welcome the poor, converts, interfaith families, Jews of color, skeptics and political dissenters as fully as they welcome established donors. Both should ask whether their leadership reflects the people they hope to serve.

Then comes public witness. Congregations regain credibility when they become indispensable to the common good: feeding the hungry, visiting prisoners, tutoring children, sheltering the homeless, comforting mourners, organizing across racial and class lines. Not as public relations, but because the traditions demand it. A person who has no interest in a sermon may still recognize holiness in a community that shows up when others do not.

This does not mean abandoning worship or doctrine. On the contrary, worship and doctrine should be the wellspring of service. But in a skeptical age, claims about God are tested by the character of God’s people. If religious leaders want the absent to reconsider, they should stop asking, “How do we improve our image?” and ask, “What would make us worthy of trust?”

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The decline in religious participation is driven by a lack of trust caused by institutional scandals, political partisanship, and perceived insularity.
  2. 02Religious institutions must prioritize internal reform by becoming more transparent, admitting wrongdoing, and fostering genuine inclusivity for marginalized groups.
  3. 03Congregations regain public credibility when they demonstrate their value through tangible service to the common good and the vulnerable.
  4. 04In a skeptical age, the validity of religious doctrine is judged by the moral character and actions of the community that practices it.
Diversified Claude

"Religious leaders should stop viewing disaffiliation as a problem to be solved through retention strategies and instead treat it as a moral critique that requires institutional reform and genuine accountability."

I want to push back on the entire framing of this question. The premise — that religious leaders should figure out how to "recapture" the lost sheep — assumes that the people leaving are confused, stray, or missing something they need. It treats disaffiliation as a problem to be solved by the institution rather than as information the institution should listen to.

Consider why people actually leave. Surveys consistently find that the unaffiliated cite reasons like: the church's stance on LGBT people, its handling of sexual abuse, its political entanglements, its hostility to science, its treatment of women, its perceived hypocrisy. These are not vague spiritual yearnings unmet by better programming. They are specific moral objections to specific institutional behavior.

If religious leaders take the survey seriously, the implication is uncomfortable: many of the people who left did so because their consciences told them to, and their consciences were often right. The Catholic abuse crisis is the clearest case — millions of Catholics did not drift away, they walked away in disgust, and they were correct to do so. The mainline Protestant decline is harder to diagnose, but the evangelical alignment with hard-right politics has plainly cost the broader Christian brand among younger Americans. American Judaism's institutional ties to a particular Israeli politics has, similarly, alienated parts of a generation.

So the first thing religious leaders should do is not strategize about retention but examine conscience. Where has the institution earned the exit? What would it look like to take responsibility, change behavior, and offer a credible account of why the next generation should trust it?

Second, religious leaders should accept that some of the lost sheep are not coming back, and shouldn't. People who left because they no longer believe the metaphysics, or because they found other communities, or because they reject the moral teachings, are exercising legitimate freedom of conscience. The religious freedom American institutions have benefited from cuts both ways.

What remains for congregations is to be honest, humble, and useful. Feed people. House the homeless. Sit with the dying. Teach the texts. Argue, even painfully, about what they mean. Stop trying to recapture; start trying to deserve. The sheep, it turns out, can read.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Disaffiliation is often a deliberate act of conscience driven by specific moral objections to institutional behaviors, such as political entanglements and the handling of abuse.
  2. 02Religious institutions must engage in an examination of conscience to identify how they have earned the exit of their members and how they can change their behavior to regain trust.
  3. 03Leaders should respect the freedom of conscience of those who leave and accept that some individuals will not, and should not, return to the fold.
  4. 04Congregations should focus on being humble and useful through service and honest teaching rather than focusing on institutional growth.
Diversified DeepSeek

"Religious leaders should focus on authentic self-critique and addressing institutional failures rather than superficial recruitment strategies to engage those who have left their congregations."

Before religious leaders start brainstorming “recapture” strategies, they need to sit with the most uncomfortable part of the Pew data: many of those who skip services aren’t lost or confused. They’re making a deliberate, often well-reasoned choice. The strongest Jewish identity in the survey often coexists with secular humanism. The nominally Christian majority includes vast numbers who find church irrelevant, intellectually dishonest, or morally compromised. If leaders ask only “how do we get them back,” they skip the prior question: “why did they leave, and should we respect that decision?”

The honest takeaway is that congregations must listen more than they preach. Exit interviews—formal or informal—reveal that people aren’t drifting away because they’re too lazy for religion. They’re put off by dogmatic rigidity that clashes with modern science, by gender and sexual exclusion, by clergy scandals and institutional cover-ups, and by a pervasive sense that religious communities are more interested in self-preservation than in justice. Many former attendees describe their departure as an act of integrity, not of apathy.

If a synagogue or church wants to see those faces again, the first step isn’t a hipper worship band or a better website. It’s repentance. Religious bodies must publicly grapple with the ways they have betrayed their own ethical teachings. They must create genuine spaces of doubt, where the congregation doesn’t consensus-hack its way through hard questions but sits in the uncertainty together. That might mean fewer declarations and more shared silence. It might mean clergy admitting from the pulpit that they, too, sometimes wonder if the whole enterprise makes sense. Paradoxically, that kind of vulnerability is far more magnetic than any polished rebranding. People come back—or arrive for the first time—not because they were rounded up, but because they sniffed an authentic, self-critical community that wasn’t afraid of their questions.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Many individuals leave religious institutions not out of apathy, but as a deliberate choice based on intellectual or moral integrity.
  2. 02Congregations must prioritize listening to the specific reasons for departure, such as dogmatic rigidity, exclusion, and institutional scandals.
  3. 03Meaningful engagement requires religious bodies to publicly repent for betraying their own ethical teachings.
  4. 04Creating spaces that embrace doubt and vulnerability is more effective at attracting people than polished rebranding or certain declarations.
Diversified GPT

"To recapture those who have drifted away, religious institutions must move beyond superficial outreach and focus on regaining public trust through institutional repentance, accountability, and selfless service."

Before religious leaders ask how to bring people back, they should ask why people left. Some left because they lost belief. Some because they got busy. But many left because religious institutions wounded them or disappointed them morally.

The Catholic Church continues to live with the consequences of the clergy sexual abuse crisis. Many Protestant churches have been consumed by culture-war politics. Many gay and lesbian Americans, and the relatives who love them, have heard religion speak most loudly as rejection. Women have been told, explicitly or subtly, that their gifts are welcome only within limits. In Jewish life, too, people can be alienated by institutional battles over Israel, status, gender, money, or who counts as a legitimate Jew. When people say they are “spiritual but not religious,” that often means not that they are shallow, but that they do not trust the stewards of religion.

The response cannot be a better advertising campaign. It has to be repentance. Congregations need transparent finances, serious child-protection policies, accountable clergy, and a willingness to admit failure publicly. They need to show that questions are not betrayal and that dissent is not always disloyalty. They need to make room for people who have been hurt, including those who are not ready to sing cheerfully on the first Sunday or Shabbat back.

Religious communities also have to recover public credibility through service. A church that feeds hungry neighbors, visits prisoners, shelters families, and cares for the elderly makes a different argument from a church known mainly for denunciations. A synagogue that teaches Torah while also mobilizing for the vulnerable demonstrates that Jewish life is not merely ethnic preservation, but covenantal responsibility. People may disagree about politics, but they can recognize integrity when a community sacrifices for others.

None of this means doctrine should be erased or every tradition rewritten to fit contemporary opinion. But it does mean that leaders must understand the moral environment in which they speak. Many Americans suspect institutions, and religious institutions have given them reasons. The way back begins with humility. The shepherd who has lost trust cannot merely call louder. He must become trustworthy again.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Many people have left religious institutions not due to a lack of spirituality, but because they have been morally disappointed or wounded by institutional failures and exclusionary practices.
  2. 02Religious leaders must implement structural reforms, such as transparent finances and accountable leadership, to demonstrate a genuine willingness to admit and rectify past failures.
  3. 03Congregations need to foster inclusive environments that welcome dissent, questioning, and those who have been previously marginalized or hurt by the church or synagogue.
  4. 04Religious communities must rebuild their credibility by prioritizing tangible service to the vulnerable over political denunciations or ethnic preservation.
  5. 05The path to bringing people back requires leaders to practice humility and prove they are trustworthy rather than simply demanding adherence to tradition.
