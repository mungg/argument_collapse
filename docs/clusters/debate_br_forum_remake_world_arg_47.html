"To truly dismantle racial capitalism, we must center Black feminist frameworks of reproductive justice and bodily autonomy, as the legal commodification of Black maternal life was the foundational engine of capitalist expansion."

Walter Johnson’s essay offers a profound recalibration of how we understand the origins of the modern world, centering the plantation, the expropriation of indigenous land, and the reproductive labor of Black women as the bedrock of global capitalism. Particularly compelling is his fourth point regarding a new account of justice: the insistence that we recognize the "subordination of the social reproduction of one group of people to the purposes of another." By integrating Black feminist critiques of slavery into the macro-economic history of capitalism, Johnson highlights a fatal flaw in traditional economic histories.

However, Johnson’s framework can be pushed even further. While he draws on Deborah Gray White to note that enslaved people were both capital and labor, and that racial capitalism “swung on a reproductive hinge,” we must explicitly name the mechanism at work: the commodification of Black maternal life. Under the legal doctrine of *partus sequitur ventrem* (1662), which dictated that the legal status of a child followed that of the mother, the womb of the enslaved Black woman became the literal engine of capitalist expansion in the Americas.

This was not merely an “elementary aspect” of slavery; it was the foundational legal fiction that allowed capitalism to achieve its dream of infinite growth. Traditional capitalism relies on the external reproduction of the labor force—the unpaid domestic labor of women to produce the next generation of wage workers. Racial capitalism, through chattel slavery, internalized this reproduction into the market itself. The Black child was born as capital.

Understanding this shifts our contemporary demands for justice. If the expropriation of Black reproductive labor was the engine of capital accumulation, then contemporary struggles for reproductive justice—against maternal mortality disparities, the criminalization of Black pregnancy, and the dismantling of the social safety net—cannot be viewed as peripheral "social issues." They are central anti-capitalist struggles. Johnson’s call to rethink justice from the standpoint of slavery requires us to place Black feminist frameworks of bodily autonomy and reproductive freedom at the very center of any movement to dismantle racial capitalism today.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The legal doctrine of partus sequitur ventrem transformed the wombs of enslaved women into the literal site of capital production and infinite economic growth.
  2. 02Racial capitalism differs from traditional capitalism by internalizing the reproduction of the labor force directly into the market through chattel status.
  3. 03Contemporary issues such as maternal mortality disparities and the criminalization of Black pregnancy are central anti-capitalist struggles rather than peripheral social concerns.
  4. 04A justice framework rooted in the history of slavery must prioritize the redress of reproductive labor expropriation to be effective.
