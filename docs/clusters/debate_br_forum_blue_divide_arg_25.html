"The Democratic Party’s recent leftward shift on economic policy is not a result of elite agenda-setting or strategic manipulation, but rather a reflection of a base that has organically moved left due to shared material insecurities across class lines."

Hacker and Pierson have written a characteristically smart and data-rich account of the Democratic Party’s recent transformation, one that usefully complicates the dominant narrative of a party captured by affluent social liberals. Their central claim—that economic issues, not cultural ones, are driving the party’s agenda—is a necessary corrective to a public discourse that often mistakes the provocations of the online left for the actual substance of Democratic governance. And their description of the “density divide” vividly captures the structural headwinds Democrats face. But for all its sophistication, the essay shares a critical weakness with the very “Brahmin left” thesis it seeks to rebut: it treats the voters who make up the Democratic coalition as an afterthought, a mass of preferences to be managed by savvy elites rather than the actual engine of the party’s ideological direction. The story of the Democratic U-turn is not primarily a story about elite agenda-setting. It is a story about a base that has moved left on economics faster than its leaders, dragging the party along with it.

Hacker and Pierson do nod toward the base, acknowledging the growing strength of the progressive wing. They mention the Sanders and Warren campaigns and the threat of primary challenges. But these forces appear in their narrative largely as external pressures on elites, or as sources of organized “policy demanders” staffing the administration. This framing vastly understates the depth and breadth of the leftward economic shift among rank-and-file Democratic voters, a shift driven not by factional maneuvering but by material conditions. The authors are correct that Democrats now draw heavily from both the affluent and the struggling. But they are too quick to assume that this heterogeneity must generate a tension that elites must finesse. In fact, the lived experience of life in the metro economy has created a shared set of economic grievances that cut across the U-shaped coalition.

Consider the affluent suburban voters Hacker and Pierson see as a potential constraint. Yes, many of them are thriving in the knowledge economy, but they are also acutely aware of the fragility of that prosperity. They carry enormous student debt, they face eye-popping housing costs, their children face even worse, and they are one medical crisis away from financial ruin in a system where even good insurance leaves families exposed. The cost of childcare in major metro areas already rivals college tuition. These are not the concerns of the poor alone; they are the primary economic anxieties of the upper-middle class, a class that is asset-rich but often cash-poor and deeply insecure. When Democrats propose universal pre-K, expanded health care subsidies, and a permanent child tax credit, they are not just offering redistribution to the poor that the affluent must nobly tolerate. They are directly addressing the central economic strain in the lives of their own suburban base. This is not a coalition held together by elite manipulation; it is a coalition united—imperfectly but powerfully—by the shared experience of living in an economy where the essentials of a middle-class life feel increasingly out of reach for everyone except the very richest.

The same dynamic holds for the working-class voters of color who form the other pole of the U-shaped coalition. Their economic precarity is more acute, but the underlying diagnosis is widely shared: the market, left to its own devices, has failed to deliver security and opportunity. The popularity of the expanded child tax credit, which Hacker and Pierson note cut child poverty dramatically, was not just a policy success but a political revelation. It demonstrated in real time that direct government investment in families is immensely popular across racial and class lines. The fact that its expiration has not yet yielded a massive electoral backlash does not mean it has had no political impact; it means the lesson has been internalized by voters as an aspiration, a possibility that now defines the party’s horizon.

This base-driven view better explains the “puzzle” Hacker and Pierson identify. Why did the party embrace such a bold agenda with such slim majorities? Not just because elites shrewdly curated a pocketbook message, but because the party’s voters had already concluded that half-measures and Rubinomics were insufficient for the scale of the problems they faced. The 2020 primary, which Biden won, was nevertheless a decisive ideological contest in which every serious candidate, including Biden himself, was pulled toward a more ambitious economic platform. The Biden-Sanders unity task forces were not a concession wrung from a reluctant moderate; they were a ratification of a new consensus that had already formed among the electorate.

This is not to say that racial and cultural divisions are imaginary. They are real and dangerous. But their management is not a story of elites organizing issues “out” of politics against the wishes of their base. The multiracial, cross-class Democratic coalition has consistently shown that it prefers a broad economic agenda that lifts everyone, disproportionately benefiting communities of color by design, over a race-specific messaging strategy that many voters across racial lines believe is politically counterproductive and divisive. The shift toward an economic framing is not an elite imposition. It is a reflection of the organic, often unspoken, strategic wisdom of a coalition that wants to win and govern in a multiracial democracy. The new Democratic economic agenda didn’t emerge from a think-tank conspiracy or a presidential whim. It emerged because the party’s voters, in all their diversity, demanded it.
