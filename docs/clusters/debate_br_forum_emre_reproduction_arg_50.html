Diversified Gemini

"Feminism must move beyond the grueling and invasive stopgap of assisted reproductive technologies like IVF and aggressively advocate for the development of artificial wombs to achieve true bodily liberation and gender equality."

In “All Reproduction Is Assisted,” Merve Emre brilliantly excavates the physical and psychic toll of assisted reproductive technology (ART), arguing that we must discard the exclusionary fantasy of the “natural” to build a truly inclusive feminist politics. By centering the experiences of queer and trans individuals, Emre aligns herself with a progressive, intersectional vision of reproductive justice. Yet, in reading Emre’s vivid accounts of the pain, expense, and exhaustion inherent in IVF and egg freezing, one cannot help but feel that her proposed solution—a narrative embrace of ART to build solidarity—is woefully inadequate. Emre points us toward the cliff of radical possibility, but refuses to jump. If we take the feminist mandate of bodily liberation seriously, we must move beyond the grueling stopgap of IVF and aggressively advocate for the very technology Emre consigns to the realm of dystopian fiction: the artificial womb.

Early in the essay, Emre briefly outlines the history of ectogenesis (gestation outside the human body), noting how Shulamith Firestone’s 1970 call for artificial wombs was met with "incredulity, scorn, and outrage" by feminists who clung to the "natural." Emre agrees with Firestone’s critique of the natural, yet the rest of her essay remains entirely bound to the biological realities of gestation. She details S’s hormone injections, B’s hyperstimulated ovaries, and K’s miscarriages. These narratives are meant to generate empathy, but what they actually generate is a profound sense of horror at the primitive, brutal nature of contemporary reproductive technology.

IVF is not liberation. It is, as Emre’s reporting clearly shows, an agonizing biological hack. It requires women to subject themselves to massive doses of synthetic hormones, invasive surgeries, and the constant surveillance of the medical gaze, all to force an often-reluctant biology to bend to the timelines of modern capitalism. Emre writes that "there is an unforgiving choreography to freezing your eggs," detailing the bruising, weight gain, exhaustion, and threat of ovarian torsion. Why should this be the horizon of feminist ambition?

If "all reproduction is assisted," as Emre claims, why are we settling for assistance that requires so much suffering? The xenofeminist collective Laboria Cuboniks, whom Emre briefly name-checks, argues that "If nature is unjust, change nature." The true injustice of reproduction is not just that society stigmatizes ART or fails to provide paid maternity leave; the true injustice is the biological reality of gestation itself, which disproportionately burdens women, trans men, and non-binary people with physical risk, pain, and economic disadvantage.

Firestone was right. As long as the burden of gestation falls solely on human bodies, true equality will remain impossible. The artificial womb is the only technology that can permanently sever the link between reproduction and biology, truly democratizing the creation of life.

Consider how ectogenesis would instantly resolve the crises Emre describes. B would not have to risk her life with ovarian hyperstimulation or endure the heartbreak of miscarriage inside her own body; her embryos could be gestated safely in a controlled environment. N, who was told that carrying a pregnancy was too dangerous due to her chronic medical issues, would not have to rely on K’s body; they could gestate their child externally. The trans women Emre mentions, Spataro and cárdenas, would not be constrained by the lack of a uterus. The biological inequality that structures so much of human society would be rendered obsolete.

Critics, of course, will argue that artificial wombs are dystopian, pointing to *Brave New World* or the horrors of capitalist commodification. Emre herself notes that the artificial womb’s promise of "creating life without any woman’s consent began to look increasingly dystopian" in the 1960s. But this fear is misplaced. The danger is not the technology; it is the economic system that controls it. Just as the printing press was not inherently oppressive, though it could be used to print propaganda, ectogenesis is a neutral tool. If it is developed and controlled by a patriarchal, capitalist system, it will indeed be a nightmare. But if it is developed as a public good, as a fundamental pillar of universal healthcare, it would be the greatest leap forward for gender equality in human history.

Emre writes that "people’s bodies are unruly sites for politics," and that they "do not cling to universal or identitarian positions." This is true, but it is exactly why we must demand technologies that free us from the tyranny of the unruly body. Narrative solidarity and empathy are beautiful things, but they do not stop a hemorrhage, they do not cure endometriosis, and they do not prevent miscarriage. Instead of romanticizing the suffering inherent in our current, crude forms of assisted reproduction, feminism must reclaim its techno-utopian roots. We must stop apologizing for wanting to transcend our biology, and start demanding the research, funding, and ethical frameworks necessary to make the artificial womb a reality.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The narratives of IVF and egg freezing described by Emre reveal that current reproductive technologies are brutal biological hacks that cause immense physical and psychic suffering.
  2. 02True equality is impossible as long as the physical and economic burdens of gestation fall disproportionately on human bodies.
  3. 03Ectogenesis would resolve reproductive crises by allowing safe, external gestation for those with chronic medical issues, trans individuals, and those at risk of pregnancy complications.
  4. 04The perceived dystopian nature of artificial wombs is a product of the capitalist systems that control them rather than the technology itself, which should be developed as a public good.
  5. 05Feminism should reclaim its techno-utopian roots by demanding the transcendence of biological limitations rather than merely seeking narrative solidarity within them.
