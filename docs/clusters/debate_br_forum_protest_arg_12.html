"While universities must protect a robust right to hostile and disruptive protest, they must also maintain viewpoint-neutral rules that distinguish between offensive expression and conduct that objectively denies others equal access to the campus commons."

Alex Gourevitch is right about the central danger: universities have treated protest against the war in Gaza less as a normal, if unruly, feature of campus life than as a security emergency. They have summoned police too quickly, punished too harshly, and allowed donors, politicians, and litigation fears to set the terms of academic government. The resulting regime is not one in which Jewish students are safer, Palestinian and Muslim students are protected, or universities are more faithful to their educational mission. It is one in which everyone learns that rights exist only until a sufficiently powerful constituency finds them intolerable.

But the case for a “right to be hostile” needs a more careful account of institutional obligation than Gourevitch gives it. The hard question is not whether students may express fierce, offensive, even morally repugnant political judgments. They must be able to. Nor is it whether subjective discomfort should be enough to shut down protest. It should not. The hard question is how a university can protect robust protest while also preserving the equal membership of students who live, study, teach, and work in the same spaces. A campus is a commons, but it is also a workplace, a residence, a classroom, and, for many students, the center of daily life. That complexity does not justify repression. It does require rules.

Gourevitch’s strongest point is his critique of the slide from safety to feeling safe. If the claim “I feel unsafe” automatically triggers discipline, then the most censorious or politically connected group wins. This is especially dangerous because all serious political protest is meant to unsettle. Antiwar protest tells supporters of war that they are complicit in killing. Labor protest tells administrators and trustees that they are exploiting workers. Anti-abortion protest tells women seeking abortions and doctors providing them that they are participating in murder. Anti-racist protest tells institutions that their ordinary procedures reproduce domination. None of these claims can be made forcefully without causing anger, shame, fear, or alienation.

It is therefore essential to distinguish offense from harassment, and fear from threat. A keffiyeh is not a threat. A chant denouncing Zionism, Israel, Hamas, the United States, or a university’s investments is not, without more, harassment. A poster that uses the word “genocide,” however bitterly contested, cannot be treated as an act of violence. If universities collapse these distinctions, they will not produce inclusion; they will produce a politics of complaint routed through administrators and lawyers.

Still, the distinction between subjective fear and objective exclusion cannot do all the work. A hostile environment is not created only by explicit individualized threats. It can also be created by repeated conduct that predictably denies people access to education on equal terms. The legal standard is demanding, as it should be: harassment must be severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive. But that standard recognizes something real. A student need not wait for a punch, a death threat, or a blocked doorway before a university has any responsibility to act.

This matters because some campus conduct during the last year and a half has not fit neatly into Gourevitch’s description of noisy but essentially nonobstructive protest. Some protesters did block entrances, surround students, interrupt classes, occupy buildings, vandalize property, or refuse identification when enforcing self-created access rules. Some counterprotesters, most notoriously at UCLA, attacked encampments while authorities failed to intervene. Some slogans and signs, depending on context, moved from denunciation of a state or ideology into celebration of slaughter or intimidation of a people. These incidents did not justify mass arrests, blanket suspensions, or the political weaponization of Title VI. But they did require universities to do what many conspicuously failed to do: investigate facts, identify individuals, and apply narrow, viewpoint-neutral sanctions.

The danger in Gourevitch’s formulation is that “protest” can become too capacious a category. Protesters often break rules for conscientious reasons, and institutions should treat such rule-breaking differently from ordinary misconduct. But civil disobedience has never meant immunity from proportionate consequences. The moral seriousness of a protest may mitigate punishment; it does not erase the interests of others in accessing libraries, classrooms, dormitories, hospitals, laboratories, or graduation ceremonies. A university that cannot maintain those functions has not protected a commons. It has ceded it.

That does not mean encampments are inherently illegitimate. They have a long and honorable history, and the mere fact that they are continuous, visible, and inconvenient is not a reason to destroy them. Universities should be far more tolerant of sustained protest than they have been. But duration and control matter. A daytime rally on a quad, a weeklong vigil, or even a tent encampment that leaves pathways open and does not monopolize essential space is one thing. An indefinite occupation that claims authority to decide who may enter, what symbols may be displayed, or which university activities may continue is another. The first tests whether public space exists. The second may begin to replace a public space with factional control.

Nor should we ignore the special responsibilities universities have toward students who are subject to discrimination. Gourevitch is right that “listen to the oppressed” cannot mean “delegate coercive authority to whoever claims injury.” Groups are internally diverse; elites often speak in the name of vulnerable people; and concepts such as antisemitism, racism, Islamophobia, and anti-Palestinian racism are themselves contested. Yet universities should listen seriously to students who report that they are being targeted because they are Jewish, Israeli, Palestinian, Muslim, Arab, Black, Asian, queer, or anything else. The answer to weaponized vulnerability is not indifference to vulnerability. It is fair process, public standards, and equal concern.

Such standards are not mysterious. Universities should presumptively protect political expression, including expression that is hostile, uncivil, accusatory, and offensive. They should regulate time, place, and manner only when rules are clear, content-neutral, and necessary to preserve access to core functions. They should punish violence, true threats, vandalism, targeted harassment, and material obstruction, but only after individualized findings. They should not outsource discipline to police except in genuine emergencies. They should not treat masks, tents, chants, or controversial slogans as presumptive evidence of danger. And they should disclose enough about their reasoning that students and faculty can tell whether rules are being applied evenly.

The deepest issue is governance. Universities are asking students to trust administrators who have given them little reason to do so. A president who calls police on peaceful protesters, rewrites rules under congressional pressure, or disciplines students to appease donors cannot credibly claim to be neutrally balancing rights. If campuses are to sustain both protest and equal membership, faculty, students, and staff need a real role in making and reviewing protest rules. Otherwise “safety” will remain a language through which trustees, donors, politicians, and risk managers govern from above.

So yes: universities need a stronger right to protest, including a right to be harsh, accusatory, and disruptive. But they also need a clearer account of the commons as shared rather than captured space. The alternative to repression is not administrative paralysis. It is disciplined toleration: broad protection for expression, narrow punishment for misconduct, and a refusal to confuse either discomfort with danger or protest with unlimited entitlement. That is the only settlement likely to protect Jews, Palestinians, Muslims, Israelis, dissidents, conservatives, radicals, and everyone else when the authorities are not on their side.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The shift from protecting objective safety to prioritizing subjective 'feelings of safety' allows politically powerful groups to censor any protest that causes them discomfort or anger.
  2. 02A 'hostile environment' should be defined by severe, pervasive, and objective exclusion rather than by the mere presence of controversial slogans or symbols.
  3. 03Protest does not grant an unlimited entitlement to monopolize essential spaces or indefinitely obstruct the core functions of the university, such as access to libraries and classrooms.
  4. 04Universities must replace mass repression and collective punishment with individualized investigations and proportionate, viewpoint-neutral sanctions for specific acts of misconduct.
  5. 05To restore institutional credibility, the governance of protest rules must be shared among faculty, students, and staff rather than dictated by donors, politicians, and administrators.
