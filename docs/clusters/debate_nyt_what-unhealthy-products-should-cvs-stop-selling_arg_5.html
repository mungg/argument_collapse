Diversified DeepSeek

"To remain consistent with its mission as a health care provider, CVS should stop selling alcohol because of its significant health risks and social harms."

If CVS is serious about shedding its identity as a vendor of disease-causing products, the most logical next step is to clear the shelves of alcohol. For decades, public health experts have documented alcohol’s role in liver disease, several cancers, neurological damage, and a staggering toll of injuries and deaths from drunk driving. In terms of overall social harm—including violence and family disruption—alcohol’s impact arguably surpasses tobacco’s. A chain that has declared it will no longer sell cigarettes because they are incompatible with a health care mission must reckon with the beer and wine sitting in its coolers.

The inconsistency is glaring. MinuteClinic practitioners inside the same store counsel patients on medication interactions with alcohol, and pharmacists warn that mixing alcohol with many prescriptions is dangerous. Yet the store casually sells the substance that creates those risks. If CVS truly wants to be a health company, not just a retailer with a health logo, it cannot draw a principled line at tobacco and then continue to profit from a product that the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention links to approximately 88,000 deaths annually in the United States. While alcohol, unlike tobacco, can be consumed moderately with low risk for some, the store has no way to ensure its customers drink responsibly. It simply hands over the bottle.

Skeptics will argue that alcohol sales generate significant revenue and that pulling them would be financially irresponsible. But the tobacco decision already demonstrated a willingness to sacrifice short-term profit for long-term brand coherence. If the company truly believes its own rhetoric about becoming a health care provider, it must apply that same calculus to a product whose health costs are similarly devastating. Half-measures will only fuel the perception that the cigarette ban was a public-relations stunt rather than a genuine ethical pivot. A health care company cannot sell sickness by the six-pack.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Alcohol contributes to a wide range of severe medical conditions, including liver disease, cancer, and neurological damage.
  2. 02Selling alcohol is inconsistent with the medical advice provided by CVS pharmacists and MinuteClinic practitioners regarding dangerous drug interactions.
  3. 03The high annual death toll and social harm associated with alcohol consumption mirror the negative impacts of tobacco that CVS has already rejected.
  4. 04Continuing to sell alcohol undermines the company's credibility and suggests that its transition to a health care provider is a public relations stunt rather than a genuine ethical commitment.
  5. 05CVS has already demonstrated a willingness to prioritize brand coherence over short-term profits by removing tobacco products.
Diversified Gemini

"To remain consistent with its mission as a health care provider, CVS should stop selling alcohol because of its significant contribution to preventable disease and death."

The applause for CVS’s decision to stop selling tobacco is well-deserved, but it exposes a glaring inconsistency in the company's retail strategy. If a product’s contribution to preventable disease and death is the metric by which CVS determines its inventory, then the pharmacy chain must take the next logical step and cease the sale of alcohol.

In many states, CVS functions as a prominent retailer of beer, wine, and even hard liquor. Yet, alcohol is a massive public health burden, implicated in liver disease, cardiovascular issues, various cancers, and fatal traffic collisions. The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention estimates that excessive alcohol use leads to nearly 90,000 deaths in the United States each year. Beyond the physical toll, alcohol addiction ravages families and communities in ways that parallel, and sometimes exceed, the immediate societal impact of smoking.

How can a company credibly claim to be a sanctuary for healing and wellness while stacking cases of beer near the pharmacy waiting area? When a patient picks up a prescription for an antidepressant or a blood thinner—medications that carry strict warnings against mixing with alcohol—walking past a prominent display of discounted wine sends a deeply contradictory message.

The argument for removing alcohol is even stronger than the argument against junk food, because alcohol, like tobacco, is an intoxicating, addictive substance with a profound societal cost. Some will argue that alcohol can be consumed safely in moderation, unlike cigarettes. But as a self-identified health care provider, CVS does not have to be the one supplying it. Let the liquor stores and supermarkets handle alcohol sales. If CVS wants to prove that this week's announcement was a fundamental shift in its corporate ethos rather than a one-off public relations stunt, the alcohol aisles must be the next to go.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Alcohol consumption is a major public health burden linked to numerous chronic diseases and tens of thousands of annual deaths in the United States.
  2. 02Selling alcohol in a pharmacy setting creates a dangerous environment for patients picking up medications that have harmful interactions with alcohol.
  3. 03The addictive nature and high societal cost of alcohol make it more comparable to tobacco than to other unhealthy products like junk food.
  4. 04Continuing to sell alcohol undermines the credibility of CVS's claim that its corporate ethos is centered on healing and wellness.
