Diversified Claude

"Presidential debates should incorporate real-time, on-screen fact-checking to improve the accuracy and substance of candidate claims."

Real-time fact-checking, displayed on screen, would transform debates overnight.

The technology exists. PolitiFact, FactCheck.org, and the Washington Post's Fact Checker already produce post-debate analyses within hours. With a modest team of researchers working live during the broadcast, claims could be flagged on a chyron beneath the speaker — "Misleading," "Unsupported," "False" — with a citation that scrolls past for the curious. Candidates would know, in advance, that their assertions about Planned Parenthood videos, vaccine schedules, or the size of the federal workforce would be checked in real time, in front of millions.

The objection is that fact-checking is itself contestable. Sometimes it is. But most political falsehoods are not borderline; they are simply wrong, and the people watching deserve to know it. The current arrangement — where a candidate can say something demonstrably untrue, the moderator moves on, and a fact-check published the next morning is read by a few thousand wonks — rewards confident lying. Reverse the incentive, and the lies will diminish.

This single reform would do more to elevate substance than any reshuffling of speaking times.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The technological and research infrastructure already exists to provide live verification of candidate assertions during a broadcast.
  2. 02Real-time fact-checking would act as a deterrent against candidates making demonstrably false claims to a mass audience.
  3. 03The current system of post-debate analysis is ineffective because it reaches far fewer people than the original broadcast and fails to hold candidates accountable in the moment.
  4. 04While some facts are contestable, the majority of political falsehoods are clear-cut and require immediate correction for the benefit of the voters.
Diversified Gemini

"To improve the substance of presidential debates, networks should integrate real-time fact-checking by non-partisan experts to hold candidates accountable for their claims."

The primary flaw in our current debate system is the impunity with which candidates can distort the truth. In the frantic pace of a multi-candidate debate, politicians routinely toss out exaggerated statistics, misrepresent their opponents’ voting records, and make sweeping claims that go entirely unchallenged. Moderators, constrained by time and the pressure to keep the show moving, rarely have the ability to push back effectively.

To make debates truly substantive, networks must integrate real-time fact-checking into the broadcast. We have the technology and the journalistic resources to do this right now. Networks should employ a dedicated panel of non-partisan researchers and policy experts operating backstage. When a candidate makes a factual claim about job growth, immigration numbers, or foreign policy, this team would immediately verify it.

If a candidate tells a blatant falsehood, the moderators should be alerted and empowered to pause the debate and correct the record on the spot. Alternatively, networks could use on-screen graphics—a subtle ticker or an overlay—to provide voters with context and corrections as the candidates are speaking.

Critics will argue that fact-checking is inherently biased and that this would give media corporations too much power. But we are not talking about ideological disputes; we are talking about basic empirical realities. Did a candidate vote for a specific bill or not? Has a particular economic metric gone up or down?

Knowing that their statements will be immediately verified and broadcast to millions of viewers would force candidates to be far more careful and precise with their rhetoric. It would elevate the discourse by ensuring that the debate is grounded in a shared reality, rather than a web of politically convenient fictions. Voters deserve to judge candidates on their actual records and policies, not on who can spin the most convincing lie on live television.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current debate format allows candidates to distort the truth and misrepresent facts without facing immediate consequences or challenges from moderators.
  2. 02Networks possess the technological and journalistic resources to verify factual claims about policy and records as they are being made.
  3. 03Moderators should be empowered to pause proceedings to correct the record or use on-screen graphics to provide viewers with immediate context.
  4. 04Real-time verification focuses on empirical realities rather than ideological disputes, mitigating concerns about media bias.
  5. 05The threat of immediate public correction would compel candidates to be more precise with their rhetoric and ground the discussion in shared facts.
Diversified DeepSeek

"To improve the substance of presidential debates, moderators must transition from passive timekeepers to active referees who perform real-time fact-checking based on nonpartisan sources."

If we are serious about substance, moderators must stop being passive timekeepers and start acting as referees armed with facts. The most corrosive habit in modern debates is the unopposed lie or misleading claim — a candidate says something demonstrably false about their own record or an opponent's position, and the moderator simply thanks them and moves on to the next person. Viewers are left with no way to know what's true, and the candidate paid no price for the deception.

The solution is real-time fact-checking by moderators. When a candidate claims that their tax plan is revenue-neutral or that they never supported a particular policy, the moderator should be permitted — indeed, required — to state the factual record, citing agreed-upon nonpartisan sources like the Congressional Budget Office or the Tax Policy Center. This is not bias; it's exactly the service journalists provide in every other setting. If a candidate says something that is contested, the moderator can explain both sides. But when an assertion is plainly contradicted by the public record, silence from the moderator is complicity in misinformation.

Skeptics will argue that this makes moderators arbiters of truth and invites accusations of partisanship. But we already trust moderators to choose questions and enforce time limits, which also requires judgment. The alternative is a format where the candidate with the boldest disregard for accuracy wins. A fact-checking moderator doesn't silence the candidate — it forces them to engage with reality, and that is the first step toward a substantive debate.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Allowing candidates to make demonstrably false claims without immediate correction prevents viewers from discerning the truth and removes the penalty for deception.
  2. 02Real-time fact-checking is a standard journalistic service that ensures candidates are held accountable to the public record rather than being allowed to spread misinformation.
  3. 03Empowering moderators to correct factual errors forces candidates to engage with reality rather than rewarding those who show the greatest disregard for accuracy.
  4. 04The responsibility of fact-checking is a natural extension of the editorial judgment moderators already exercise when selecting questions and enforcing rules.
Diversified GPT

"Presidential debates should incorporate real-time, nonpartisan fact-checking to ensure candidates are held accountable for the accuracy of their claims during the event."

One modest but useful reform would be to treat facts as part of the debate rather than as an after-the-fact clean-up operation. Candidates routinely cite budget numbers, crime statistics, immigration figures, unemployment rates and foreign policy claims that most viewers cannot evaluate in real time. By the next morning, fact-checkers may have sorted some of it out, but the impression has already been made.

Debates should include a nonpartisan fact desk operating under rules announced in advance. The goal should not be to interrupt every exaggeration or settle every ideological dispute. Many political claims involve predictions or values, not simple facts. But when a candidate says a bill did something it plainly did not do, or attributes a number to an official source that says the opposite, moderators should be able to ask an immediate follow-up: “Our fact desk notes that the Congressional Budget Office estimated a different figure. Can you explain your number?”

This would improve the incentives before the debate even begins. Campaigns would know that unsupported statistics might be challenged in front of millions of viewers. They would prepare more carefully. Viewers would learn not only who has the best line but who can defend the basis for a claim.

There should also be a fully annotated transcript released quickly after the debate, with links or citations to the major claims. Campaigns could be invited to submit supporting evidence for assertions made onstage. If they cannot support them, that too is useful information.

The danger is turning fact-checkers into unelected referees of politics. That is why the system must be limited to verifiable claims and rely on agreed public sources where possible: government data, official votes, enacted laws, budget offices, court decisions. A debate should still be about judgment, priorities and philosophy. But judgment improves when candidates cannot build their answers on sand.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A nonpartisan fact desk would allow moderators to challenge verifiable inaccuracies with immediate follow-up questions.
  2. 02The presence of real-time fact-checking would incentivize campaigns to prepare more carefully and avoid using unsupported statistics.
  3. 03Providing a fully annotated transcript with supporting evidence after the debate would help viewers evaluate the basis of candidates' assertions.
  4. 04Limiting fact-checking to verifiable public sources prevents the process from becoming an ideological or subjective political referee.
