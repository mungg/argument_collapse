"The current discourse on campus activism must expand beyond elite, predominantly white institutions to address the material realities and institutional histories of the broader landscape of Black higher education."

The debate over campus protest has been dominated by the most selective universities in the country. Missouri matters, and so do Yale, Princeton, Amherst, Brown, Harvard, UCLA, and the University of North Carolina. But the focus on elite and flagship campuses risks distorting the question of black study and black struggle. Most black students are not at Ivy League schools or wealthy liberal arts colleges. Many are at historically black colleges and universities, community colleges, regional public institutions, commuter campuses, and for-profit schools. Any account of black study must begin there too.

Robin D. G. Kelley knows this history, and his essay gestures toward institutions and study groups beyond elite spaces. Yet the current national conversation often treats black student protest as if it were mainly the story of black students seeking comfort at predominantly white universities. That story is too narrow. For millions of students, the central issues are not whether a residential campus feels like home, but whether they can afford tuition, transportation, books, childcare, food, and time away from paid work. Their “safe space” may be a bus ride between a job and class. Their trauma may be debt collection, eviction, family obligation, or the exhaustion of being one emergency away from stopping out.

This broader view complicates the meaning of university reform. Kelley asks why activists remain wedded to standard achievement measures rather than open admissions. That question deserves far more attention. The most radical educational demand in recent history may not be a new diversity office at an elite college, but the old promise of open, affordable, public higher education. City University of New York’s experiment with open admissions, the community college movement, land-grant institutions, and HBCUs all represent different, contested attempts to make higher learning accessible to people excluded by wealth and race. Those institutions have always been underfunded compared to elite universities, yet they have educated enormous numbers of black teachers, nurses, engineers, ministers, organizers, and public servants.

HBCUs in particular challenge the assumption that black students seek love from white institutions. They were founded under conditions of segregation and racial terror, often with limited resources and paternalistic oversight, but they also became spaces of black intellectual life, professional formation, political debate, and cultural production. They have never been utopias. They have had their own hierarchies of class, gender, sexuality, respectability, and governance. But any discussion of black study that centers only the predominantly white university misses a long tradition of black institution-building.

At the same time, it would be a mistake to romanticize HBCUs or community colleges as pure alternatives. They too operate in a neoliberal landscape of austerity, accreditation pressures, managerial growth, philanthropic influence, and student debt. Many public HBCUs have been starved by state governments. Community colleges are asked to serve the students with the greatest needs while receiving fewer resources per student than selective institutions. For-profit colleges have preyed on black aspiration with devastating consequences. The question, then, is not simply whether black study should happen inside or outside the university. It is how to build durable educational institutions accountable to black communities under hostile economic conditions.

Kelley’s call for fugitive study is useful precisely because it can travel. A study group in a dorm at UCLA, a reading circle at a community college, a Saturday school in a church basement, a prison education program, a union hall seminar, and a political education committee in a Black Lives Matter chapter can all be forms of black study. They need not wait for departmental approval. They also need not reject institutional resources when those can be won. The tradition of freedom schools teaches us that education can be both improvised and rigorous, both local and world-historical.

Still, we should ask who has access to fugitive study. Graduate students at elite universities may be precarious relative to tenured professors, but they often have libraries, time, networks, and cultural capital unavailable to working students elsewhere. A student taking twelve credits while working thirty hours a week may not be able to attend a three-hour evening reading group. A mother at a community college may need childcare before she needs Moten. This is not an argument against theory. It is an argument for organizing the material conditions that make theory shareable.

The demand for free tuition for black and indigenous students, which Kelley cites, points in this direction. One can debate its political feasibility or whether it should be framed universally with targeted repair, but it correctly identifies the university as part of a history of dispossession. Student debt is not merely a personal financial choice; it is a mechanism that disciplines imagination. A student who must borrow heavily to attend college is pressured to treat education as credential acquisition. Political study becomes a luxury. Risk becomes expensive.

If campus movements want to speak for black study broadly, they should connect symbolic demands at elite institutions to material demands across higher education: free or debt-free public college, full funding for HBCUs, support for community colleges, childcare, transportation, food security, prison education, and pathways for adults returning to school. They should challenge admissions systems that hoard prestige and then congratulate themselves for admitting a few more black students. They should ask why the institutions serving the most vulnerable students receive the least.

Kelley is right: the university is not a loving family. But black people have made learning under conditions far harsher than administrative indifference. The future of black study will not be decided only in quads where national reporters gather. It will be decided in night classes, church basements, union meetings, HBCU classrooms, online forums, prisons, public libraries, and community colleges. The task is to link these sites, redistribute resources among them, and refuse the idea that intellectual life belongs where endowments are largest.

  3. 03Radical educational reform should prioritize the revival of open admissions and affordable public higher education over the creation of diversity offices at selective colleges.
  4. 04The concept of 'fugitive study' must account for the unequal distribution of time and cultural capital, ensuring that the material conditions for intellectual life are accessible to working-class students and parents.
  5. 05Campus movements should link symbolic demands at prestigious schools to systemic demands for the redistribution of resources toward underfunded institutions that serve the most vulnerable students.
