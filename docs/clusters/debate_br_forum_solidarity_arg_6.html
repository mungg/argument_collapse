Diversified DeepSeek

"While social endurance is a necessary foundation for solidarity, it must be coupled with a coherent political ideology and a project of political education to transform individual grievances into a collective, world-changing movement."

Mie Inouye’s “Solidarity Now” manages to do something rare in contemporary left writing: it holds together a rigorous critical analysis of deference politics and standpoint theory with a deeply personal, even tender, account of her own formation within the Mormon Church. I find her central proposal—that “social endurance” is a crucial basis for solidarity, perhaps more crucial than either moral conversion or shared material interest—to be not only compelling but necessary. However, I want to draw out a dimension she only gestures at: the role of ideology and shared vision, and the need to connect the practice of endurance to a larger project of political education that she calls “ideology.” Without that connection, endurance can become a mere tool for coping, not for transforming the world.

Inouye rightly critiques Jared Clemons’s pessimism about the white PMC’s capacity for genuine antiracist action. She asks, if the PMC is the most accessible base for movements, shouldn’t we find ways to work with their contradictions? Her answer is yes, and she proposes that people need to have “something at stake that they feel viscerally.” But what can PMC people have at stake? Their material interests, in the narrow sense, are often served by maintaining the system that provides them status. Their visceral stakes, however, can be found in the very real suffering that capitalism inflicts on them too—precarity, meaninglessness, loneliness, ecological dread, and the daily moral injuries of working in institutions that harm others. Inouye names these: she was in the streets in 2020 because she was “dying of being alone” just as much as because she opposed police murder. That is not a confession of bad faith; it is an honest assessment of why a person with relative privilege might be sustained in struggle.

The missing piece is a coherent political ideology that can bind these disparate visceral grievances together and point them toward a transformative demand. I use “ideology” not in the pejorative sense of false consciousness, but in the Gramscian sense of a coherent worldview that organizes masses of people around a hegemonic project. The Rainbow Coalition had such an ideology: a combination of Black liberation, anti-capitalism, and anti-imperialism articulated by the Panthers and their allies. Bob Lee was not just facilitating self-expression; he was conducting a process of political education that inserted white grievances into that ideological framework. The young woman whose brother was stabbed by a cop needed to see that incident not as an isolated evil but as a symptom of a state that protects property over human life, regardless of race. That education is what made the coalition durable.

Inouye’s own Mormon background offers a model of ideological formation, albeit a reactionary one. Mormonism gave her family a narrative of belonging and progress—the American Dream sanctified by religion—that helped them survive internment’s after-effects. But it also disciplined them into “Caucasian democracy,” aligning their interests with white supremacy and capitalism. The left, Inouye suggests, failed to offer an alternative solidaristic ideology. Her Uncle Gerald died of COVID-19 partly because his libertarian, anti-government ideology prevented him from seeking timely care. That is a tragedy of inadequate political education, not just individual stubbornness. If the left wants to reach people like Gerald, it must do more than patiently endure their reactionary views; it must offer a compelling, emotionally resonant story about why the system is killing them and what can be done.

Inouye’s proposal of “ideology” as a basis for solidarity is briefly mentioned via Jodi Dean’s concept of comradeship: “a shared vision for the future.” I wish Inouye had developed this more. What would a left ideology for the current moment look like? It would need to connect the dots between police violence, climate change, healthcare collapse, student debt, and workplace exploitation in a way that is both intellectually rigorous and viscerally gripping. It would need to give people a sense of historical agency, a way of understanding themselves as part of a long lineage of resistance. And it would need to be propagated through disciplined organizations that can do the slow work of political education, not just through reading groups but through the experience of collective struggle.

The practice of social endurance is essential, but it is the soil, not the plant. Without the seed of a shared political project, the soil remains barren. Inouye’s insistence on keeping meetings, on showing up, on not fleeing conflict, is the necessary preparation. But we must also be clear about what we are building toward. Otherwise, we risk creating inward-looking communities of the like-minded but alienated, performing solidarity as a lifestyle rather than as a weapon. The left needs more than endurance; it needs hope, and hope is something that must be cultivated through a vision that is worth enduring for.

I want to close by noting that Inouye’s own essay is itself an act of ideological labor. It weaves together her family history, political theory, and organizing experience into a narrative that models the kind of integrative thinking that a new left ideology requires. If we can produce more such narratives—stories that help people from different locations see their own struggles as part of a larger whole—then perhaps the endurance will be not an end in itself but a vehicle for building the new world in the shell of the old.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The visceral stakes for the professional-managerial class, such as loneliness and moral injury, must be integrated into a broader ideological framework to sustain their participation in struggle.
  2. 02Political education is required to help individuals understand their personal suffering not as isolated incidents, but as symptoms of systemic failures like capitalism and state violence.
  3. 03The failure of the left to provide a compelling, solidaristic ideology leaves people vulnerable to reactionary worldviews that can lead to self-destructive outcomes.
  4. 04A shared vision for the future is what prevents social endurance from becoming a mere coping mechanism and instead turns it into a vehicle for historical agency.
  5. 05Solidarity organizations must function as sites for disciplined political education that connect disparate issues like climate change and healthcare into a viscerally gripping narrative.
Diversified DeepSeek

"While social endurance and epistemic humility are valuable, the left must prioritize the development of a clear, compelling ideological vision to provide a necessary foundation for long-term solidarity and political power."

I want to respond to Mie Inouye’s “Solidarity Now” by lifting up something she mentions but does not fully develop: the importance of ideology as a shared vision. Inouye writes, “Maybe a better basis for solidarity than either morality or material interest… is ideology: a shared vision of a just society that aligns with but exceeds our material interests, that motivates us both because it would make our lives tangibly better and because we find it inspiring and attractive.” She cites Jodi Dean’s concept of comradeship that “collectivizes and directs action in light of a shared vision for the future.” This, I believe, is the heart of the matter, and it deserves a more central role in her account than the emphasis on social endurance allows.

The left has too often ceded the terrain of vision, of utopian thinking, to the right. The conservative movement offers a powerful, if grotesque, vision of restored national greatness, traditional families, and order. The left, by contrast, has tended to operate in a defensive mode: against police brutality, against climate catastrophe, against austerity. These are urgent, but they do not on their own constitute a vision of what we want. The result is that many people, especially those who are not already politicized, experience left movements as a cacophony of grievances rather than a coherent alternative. Inouye’s Mormon family, shaped by a church that offered a totalizing narrative of belonging and eternal progression, illustrates how powerful such a vision can be. The tragedy is that the vision they embraced tied them to racial capitalism. But the need for a vision remains.

Inouye’s concept of social endurance is meant to create the conditions in which a shared vision might emerge through conflict and proximity. But this puts the cart before the horse. Historically, shared visions have often been articulated first, through political education and agitation, and then organizational endurance followed. The Abolitionist movement was built on a clear moral and political vision of a world without slavery. The labor movement was propelled by the dream of a cooperative commonwealth. The Black Panther Party offered not just a critique of police violence but a socialist vision of community control. Today, we have fragments of such visions—the Green New Deal, abolition feminism, democratic socialism—but they are not yet synthesized into a compelling popular narrative that can reach a broad working class across racial lines.

Endurance without vision is mere persistence, and it is vulnerable to burnout and co-optation. Many of the 2020 uprisings’ participants drifted away not because they lacked the capacity to endure, but because they lacked a clear answer to the question “What are we fighting for beyond this moment?” The answer “dismantle systemic racism” is abstract and, for many, fails to connect to their daily lives. Inouye herself admits that the connections between the various grievances in 2020 were not made explicit; they remained a “somehow.” That “somehow” needs to be filled with ideology.

What would a new, compelling left ideology look like? It would need to address the deep desires for security, meaning, and community that capitalism systematically thwarts. It would need to combine a critique of concentrated wealth with a positive program for decommodifying housing, healthcare, education, and care work. It would need to link racial justice to universal programs in a way that avoids both colorblindness and narrow identitarianism. And it would need to be articulated by a cadre of organizers who are themselves deeply rooted in multiracial working-class communities. Inouye’s essay, with its autobiographical grounding, models the kind of storytelling that can help people see the connections between their own lives and systemic forces. But storytelling alone is not enough. We need disciplined organizations that can contest for hegemony in the public sphere.

I worry that Inouye’s focus on interpersonal endurance and epistemic humility, while valuable, could distract from the harder work of building ideological unity. Being open to challenge is good, but at some point, an organization must take a stand: “This is what we believe, this is what we are fighting for, and we invite you to join us.” That stand may be provisional, subject to revision through democratic process, but it cannot be endlessly deferred in the name of humility. The deferral of ideological commitment is itself a privilege of those who are not in immediate crisis. Henry, the first-generation organizer, knows what he wants: material changes for his family’s survival. That clarity is not a flaw; it is a strength. A movement that cannot say what it wants will never attract Henrys, or keep them.

Inouye’s essay is a profound meditation on the difficulty of being together. But I think we must be bolder. We need not just to endure but to declare: to say “this is the world we want” with such conviction and specificity that it becomes a magnetic force, drawing in even those who initially resist. Then, and only then, will the endurance have a reason to continue through the heartbreak.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A shared vision of a just society is the primary driver of organizational endurance, rather than a byproduct of it.
  2. 02Without a coherent ideological alternative to the right's narratives, left movements remain a defensive collection of grievances that fail to engage the unpoliticized.
  3. 03The decline of the 2020 uprisings demonstrates that persistence alone is insufficient if participants lack a clear, concrete answer to what they are fighting for beyond the immediate moment.
  4. 04An overemphasis on interpersonal humility and the deferral of ideological commitment can become a distraction from the disciplined work of contesting for public hegemony.
  5. 05To attract and retain those with urgent material needs, movements must be able to make specific, bold declarations about the world they intend to build.
