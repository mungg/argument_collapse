**The Precautionary Principle Is Not Anti-Science**

Pamela Ronald is a serious scientist, and her essay deserves a serious reply. Let me give one. I write as an organic farmer who has spent thirty years working with seed, soil, and the people who eat what I grow. I am not a scientific illiterate, and I resent being lumped with Jenny McCarthy.

Ronald's central rhetorical move is to assimilate the GMO debate to the climate-change and vaccine debates, casting opponents as the left-wing mirror image of right-wing science deniers. This is a clever and corrosive analogy. It positions her on the side of reason and her critics on the side of superstition before any actual argument has been made. But the analogy fails on inspection. Climate science rests on a century of physics and a vast body of observational data. Vaccine science rests on more than two centuries of epidemiology and immunology. The safety record of commercial GE crops, by contrast, rests on seventeen years of cultivation and a much smaller, more contested body of feeding studies, many of them ninety days long and conducted by, or in collaboration with, the firms whose products are under review.

That is not nothing. It is genuine evidence, and I do not dismiss it. But it is not the kind of overwhelming convergent evidence that warrants Ronald's confidence, and it is certainly not enough to license the dismissive tone she takes toward those who counsel caution. The precautionary principle — the idea that, in the face of novel technologies with potentially irreversible consequences, the burden of proof should rest on the proponents — is not anti-science. It is a perfectly defensible epistemic stance, the same one that gave us the FDA's drug approval regime, environmental impact statements, and the moratorium that followed Asilomar in 1975. The scientists who drafted the Asilomar principles understood that the absence of demonstrated harm is not equivalent to the demonstration of safety.

Ronald notes, correctly, that no instance of adverse health effects from GE crops has been documented in seventeen years. She does not note that the United States does not label these foods, does not track their consumption, and has not commissioned the long-term epidemiological studies that would be necessary to detect chronic effects. We could not document harm even if it existed. This is not a paranoid observation. It is a methodological one.

I want to take up two more of her arguments. First, the claim that conventional plant breeding produces more genetic disruption than transgenesis and is therefore equivalent in risk. The first half is true; the second does not follow. Mutagenesis and wide crosses shuffle existing genomes within roughly compatible material. Transgenesis can introduce genes from bacteria into plants, or from animals into fungi, in combinations that no breeding system could produce. Whether this matters for safety is precisely the empirical question, and waving at the celery example does not settle it. Genuine novelty deserves genuine scrutiny.

Second, the labeling argument. Ronald says labels imply danger where there is none, and that the GMO category is too heterogeneous for meaningful labeling. Yet we label kosher food, halal food, organic food, and country of origin, none of which speaks to safety. We do so because consumers have asked to know, and because in a market democracy the burden falls on those who would withhold information, not on those who would provide it. Whole Foods is in the business of selling food its customers want; that customers want labeled food is data, not propaganda.

I would close with what Ronald and I share. We both want farms that build soil rather than mining it, that support pollinators rather than poisoning them, that pay farmers a living and feed eaters well. I believe that goal is more readily reached through agroecological practices — diverse rotations, cover cropping, integrated livestock, on-farm seed selection — than through genetic engineering of individual traits, because the problems of industrial agriculture are systemic and engineering responses tend to address symptoms one at a time. Where genetic engineering can serve agroecology — a disease-resistant banana that lets a Ugandan farmer keep her perennial crop, for instance — I will not stand in the way. Where it serves only to entrench the monoculture-and-chemical system, I will.

That is not anti-science. That is the kind of judgment that good science alone cannot make, and that scientists who pretend otherwise mistake their authority for their domain. Ronald is right that we share goals. She would be more persuasive if she acknowledged that we might reasonably disagree about means.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The scientific evidence for GMO safety is not as extensive or settled as the evidence for climate change or vaccines, making the author's analogy between these issues flawed.
  2. 02The absence of documented health harms is a result of a lack of labeling and long-term epidemiological tracking rather than a definitive proof of safety.
  3. 03The precautionary principle is a valid epistemic stance that places the burden of proof on proponents of novel technologies with potentially irreversible consequences.
  4. 04Transgenesis creates biological combinations that are fundamentally different from conventional breeding and therefore require unique scrutiny.
  5. 05Labeling is a matter of consumer right-to-know and market democracy rather than a scientific claim about inherent danger.
  6. 06The systemic problems of industrial agriculture are better addressed through agroecological practices than through the engineering of individual genetic traits.
