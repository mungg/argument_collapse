"The purpose of Lent is to provide a communal and spiritual rhythm that allows individuals to examine their desires, face their mortality, and open up space for healing and renewal."

Lent’s purpose is to place human longing inside a story and a calendar. That may sound abstract, but it is precisely what many people lack. We live by deadlines, school terms, market cycles and election seasons; these calendars train us to value productivity, consumption and competition. Lent offers a different rhythm: preparation, examination, restraint, sorrow, hope.

For Christians, that rhythm leads toward Easter. It is not merely a memorial of Jesus’ 40 days in the wilderness, but participation in a drama of temptation, suffering and renewal. The point is not to pretend to be miserable. It is to become more truthful. What do I love? What do I fear? What am I avoiding? What must die in me if I am to live more fully?

The practice of giving something up is one answer to those questions, but it is not the only one. Some people should not fast from food, particularly those with medical conditions or histories of disordered eating. Some already live with scarcity and need not romanticize it. For them, Lent may mean taking something on: daily prayer, Sabbath rest, therapy, acts of mercy, honest conversation, political solidarity, gratitude. Discipline should serve healing, not pride.

At its best, Lent is communal rather than heroic. It tells the individual, “You are not inventing the spiritual life from scratch.” The ashes of Ash Wednesday, the simpler meals, the subdued music, the anticipation of Holy Week — these shared signs help people face mortality without despair. “Remember that you are dust” is not an insult. It is a liberation from the fantasy that we are self-made and permanent.

For nonbelievers, the season can still have meaning as an inherited cultural form of attention. One need not accept every doctrine to see the wisdom of a yearly audit of desire. But the Christian center should not be erased: Lent is finally about learning to receive life, not mastering it.

The point of giving something up, then, is not the thing surrendered. It is the space opened. If that space fills with humility, compassion and hope, Lent has done its work.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Lent offers an alternative to the productivity-driven cycles of modern life by prioritizing preparation, restraint, and truthfulness.
  2. 02The season facilitates a participation in a spiritual drama that encourages individuals to confront their fears and identify what must change for them to live more fully.
  3. 03Lenten discipline, whether through fasting or taking on new practices, should be tailored to the individual's needs to serve healing rather than pride.
  4. 04The communal nature of Lent liberates individuals from the burden of self-sufficiency by reminding them of their shared mortality and dependence.
  5. 05Even for nonbelievers, Lent serves as a valuable cultural tool for the annual audit of one's desires and the practice of focused attention.
  6. 06The ultimate goal of Lenten sacrifice is not the act of surrender itself, but the creation of space for humility, compassion, and hope to grow.
