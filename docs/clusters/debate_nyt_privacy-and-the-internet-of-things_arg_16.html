**The Promise Is Real, But So Is the Peril**

The Internet of Things will be transformative, and it's worth naming what's actually exciting about it. A pill bottle that reminds an elderly parent to take her medication—and quietly alerts a caregiver when she doesn't—could keep millions of people in their homes longer. A municipal water system that detects leaks in real time could save billions of gallons. Running shoes that track gait could help injured athletes recover. Thermostats that learn your patterns can cut household energy use by double digits. These aren't science-fiction gadgets; many already exist.

But the baby-camera horror story from earlier this year, when a Houston family discovered a stranger shouting at their toddler through a hacked Foscam, illustrates the structural problem. The companies racing to network everything are not, by and large, security companies. They are appliance makers, toy makers, sneaker companies. Many ship products with default passwords, no encryption, and no mechanism for pushing security updates. Once the device is in your home, it's frozen in time—vulnerabilities and all.

Minimizing the risk requires three things. First, the FTC should treat insecure connected devices as an unfair trade practice, as it began doing in the TRENDnet settling earlier this year. That sends a market signal. Second, we need basic baseline standards: forced password changes on setup, encrypted communications, and a commitment to patching for a defined product lifetime. Third, consumers need labeling—something like an Energy Star rating for security—so that the market can reward responsible manufacturers rather than the cheapest.

The deeper privacy question, beyond hackers, is what the manufacturer itself does with the data. A connected dishwasher knows when you're home; a connected car knows where you drive. Without enforceable data-minimization rules, every object becomes a surveillance node operated by someone whose business model you didn't sign up for. That's the conversation we haven't really started having yet.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Connected devices provide tangible societal benefits, such as improving elderly care, conserving water, and reducing household energy consumption.
  2. 02The current market is plagued by structural security flaws because manufacturers often prioritize speed to market over essential protections like encryption and update mechanisms.
  3. 03The Federal Trade Commission should classify the sale of insecure connected devices as an unfair trade practice to incentivize better corporate behavior.
  4. 04Industry-wide baseline standards must be established, including mandatory password changes upon setup and guaranteed security patching for the product's lifetime.
  5. 05A security labeling system, similar to Energy Star ratings, is necessary to help consumers identify and reward manufacturers that prioritize data protection.
  6. 06Enforceable data-minimization rules are required to prevent manufacturers from using connected objects as unauthorized surveillance tools for data harvesting.
