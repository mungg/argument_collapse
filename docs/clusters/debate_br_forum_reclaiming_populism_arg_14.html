"The left's failure to achieve lasting political change stems not from a lack of populist theory, but from its inability to construct a narrative framework capable of displacing the deeply embedded American story of individual striving and meritocracy."

Reading Adom Getachew's essay and the forum it introduces, I am struck by the tension between the historical optimism the authors clearly feel about the possibilities for progressive change and the sobering evidence that surrounds us. The Chicago Teachers Union's struggles and victories are genuinely inspiring. The Sanders campaign mobilized millions and shifted political discourse in ways that will outlast any particular election. The emergence of a Socialist Caucus in Chicago's city council represents a genuine advance for left politics. And yet: Evo Morales is gone, Jeremy Corbyn has been defeated, Bernie Sanders has suspended his campaign, and the COVID-19 pandemic is revealing the limits of our collective capacity to respond to crisis even as it demonstrates—devastatingly—the need for precisely the kind of politics Getachew and her collaborators envision. What are we to make of this paradox?

In my view, the answer lies in part in the relationship between populism and what we might call, following Robert Reich, the "story" that politics tells about who we are and what we owe each other. Every political formation requires a narrative framework that makes sense of the world, identifies villains and heroes, and offers a vision of collective transformation. Getachew is right that populism has been unfairly stigmatized; the capacity to articulate a compelling story about "the people" is not inherently reactionary. But the left's efforts to reclaim populism have been complicated by a failure to reckon seriously with the stories that are already dominant in American political culture—stories that populism, however creatively reimagined, struggles to displace.

The most powerful of these stories is the one that frames American history as a narrative of individual striving, meritocratic reward, and the eventual triumph of the deserving over the undeserving. This story has been so thoroughly naturalized that it structures the imagination of large portions of the electorate, including many who would benefit from progressive policies but vote against their material interests. The populist framework that Getachew and her collaborators favor—a framework that emphasizes collective identity, common vulnerability, and structural explanation—runs up against this deeply embedded individualist narrative in ways that are not easily resolved through better messaging or more sophisticated theorizing.

The Sanders campaign illustrates the problem. Bernie Sanders told a powerful story about the billionaire class and the rigged economy, about the 99 percent and the 1 percent, about the democratic struggle for dignity and against oligarchy. This story resonated with millions, especially young people who had experienced the 2008 crisis as a betrayal of everything they had been promised. But it also alienated voters—particularly working-class voters of color—who did not recognize themselves in a narrative that seemed to foreground class over race, that sometimes slid into an abstraction ("the people") that obscured the particular experiences of specific communities, and that was filtered through the personality of a seventy-eight-year-old white senator from Vermont who could not credibly claim the experiences of the workers he was trying to mobilize.

The question is not whether populism can be reclaimed as a term but whether the left can construct a narrative framework compelling enough to compete with the stories already on offer. And here I must part ways with my colleagues in this forum, who seem to assume that a more sophisticated understanding of populism, one that centers race and multiracial coalition-building, will solve the narrative problem. It will not—or at least, it will not solve it automatically. The multiracial dimension is essential, as the essays rightly insist. But the articulation of a genuinely multiracial populist narrative that can overcome the deep racialized individualism embedded in American political culture remains the left's unsolved challenge.

The COVID-19 pandemic has not, in my observation, brought us closer to a solution. The crisis has certainly revealed the hollowness of individualist narratives about personal responsibility and self-reliance when millions are ordered to stay home and are then abandoned by the political system that ordered them there. It has demonstrated, viscerally, the ways that structural inequalities—in healthcare access, in housing density, in occupational exposure—determine who survives and who dies. And it has prompted a rhetoric of collective sacrifice and mutual obligation that sounds, superficially, like the language of populism. But the dominant political response has been to double down on individualist and nationalist narratives rather than to build on the moment's potential for a more fundamental rethinking. The CARES Act's checks to individuals were framed not as a recognition of collective responsibility but as a one-time gesture, a bridge back to normal rather than a foundation for a different normal.

What would it take for the left to build the narrative capacity it needs? First, a much more serious engagement with the cultural dimensions of politics—not the superficial focus on "messaging" that characterizes much of progressive strategy but a genuine effort to understand how ordinary people make sense of their lives and their political options. Second, a willingness to learn from the successes and failures of other movements and traditions, including the ways that conservative movements have constructed and transmitted their own narratives. Third, an acceptance that narrative-building is a long-term project that cannot be reduced to electoral cycles or campaign seasons. The stories that dominate American politics did not achieve their dominance overnight, and they will not be displaced quickly or easily.

The essays in this forum gesture toward some of these requirements without fully engaging them. The emphasis on internationalism and the comparative perspective on Bolivia and the UK are valuable, but they tend to focus on the structural conditions for progressive politics rather than the cultural and narrative conditions. The invocation of the CTU's successes is inspiring but does not adequately explain how a multiracial coalition was actually built in Chicago—or whether the conditions that made it possible there exist elsewhere. And the engagement with COVID-19 is necessarily speculative, given the moment at which these essays were written, but it underestimates the ways that crisis can consolidate narratives as well as disrupt them.

I write this not to despair but to insist on clarity about the challenges ahead. The left will not succeed simply by reclaiming a word or by constructing a more sophisticated theory of populism. It will succeed only by building the narrative capacity to tell a story about who we are and what we can become—a story compelling enough to compete with the individualist narratives that have dominated American political culture for generations. This is a task that will require not only policy expertise and electoral strategy but also imagination, cultural engagement, and patience. The alternative is to continue doing what we have been doing, expecting different results, and wondering why the left remains weak.
