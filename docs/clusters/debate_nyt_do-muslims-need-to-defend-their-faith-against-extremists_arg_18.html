"To effectively defend their faith against extremism, Muslims must move beyond condemning violence and fundamentally reject political Islam in favor of secularism and individual human rights."

The debate over whether to "defend the faith" often dances around the elephant in the room: Political Islam. We cannot effectively counter violent extremists like ISIS while simultaneously tolerating or making excuses for the non-violent, yet deeply illiberal, Islamist movements that share their foundational goals.

ISIS did not emerge from a vacuum. It is the extreme manifestation of a broader political project that seeks to merge mosque and state, enforce religious law, and resurrect medieval concepts of governance. While many moderate Muslims eagerly condemn the beheadings and massacres committed by ISIS, there is often a troubling reluctance to condemn the underlying premise: that Islam is a political system meant to govern all aspects of public life.

When mainstream Islamic organizations push for blasphemy laws, seek to enforce gender segregation, or promote the idea of a global political ummah at the expense of national citizenship, they are tilling the soil from which violent extremism grows. They legitimize the very grievances and utopian dreams that ISIS weaponizes.

If Muslims truly want to defend their faith from being hijacked by violent radicals, they must forcefully divorce their religion from political ideology. The defense cannot just be, "ISIS is misinterpreting the rules of the caliphate." The defense must be, "We reject the concept of a caliphate entirely in the modern world."

To defeat extremism, Muslims must champion secularism, freedom of speech, and human rights without religious caveats. They must loudly declare that Islam is a personal spiritual path, not a blueprint for statecraft. Until the Muslim world confronts and dismantles the creeping influence of political Islam, the conveyor belt of extremism will continue to run, and the violent fringe will continually hijack the faith.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Violent extremist groups like ISIS are the extreme manifestation of a broader, illiberal political project that seeks to merge religion with state governance.
  2. 02Non-violent Islamist movements legitimize the grievances and utopian goals that radicals weaponize by promoting concepts like blasphemy laws and religious segregation.
  3. 03The defense of Islam must involve a total rejection of the caliphate concept rather than merely arguing over its correct interpretation.
  4. 04Muslims must champion the idea that Islam is a personal spiritual path rather than a blueprint for statecraft to stop the ideological conveyor belt of extremism.
