"The value of care should be understood as an essential human relationship rather than an economic service or a production process for creating citizens."

Nancy Hirschmann's essay raises questions that go beyond feminism to the deeper issue of how we understand human relationships and their place in a good life. At stake is not just the political question of how to achieve gender equality but the philosophical question of what equality requires and whether relationships of care can survive the demand that they serve collective goals.

Hirschmann correctly notes that care feminism has often ignored the goods that parents receive from their children. Love, companionship, the experience of nurturing another human being—these are not just burdens that society should compensate; they are benefits that parents receive and that childless people do not. If the state compensates mothers for the labor of raising children, should it not also tax mothers for the love they receive?

This argument is meant to expose a contradiction in care feminism, but I think it reveals something deeper about the inadequacy of treating human relationships in economic terms. The attempt to value care as a contribution to the public good, to measure it in hours and tax it accordingly, misunderstands what care is. Care is not a service rendered to a client; it is a relationship between persons, and its value cannot be captured in economic categories.

This does not mean that care work should not be supported or that caregivers should not be compensated. It means that the purpose of such support is not to create a market for care but to enable relationships that would otherwise be impossible or unsustainable. A society that values care should make it possible for people to care for those they love without sacrificing their own well-being or independence.

Hirschmann's focus on what children become—citizens or criminals, contributors or drains—reflects a consequentialist logic that treats parenting as a kind of production process. Children are outputs; parents are inputs; the question is whether the outputs justify the inputs. But this way of thinking about family is dehumanizing. We do not have children to produce good citizens; we have children because we want to love and be loved, to experience the particular joys and challenges of raising another human being.

The best parents I know are not focused on whether their children will contribute to the common good. They are focused on whether their children are happy, healthy, and loved. And paradoxically, children who are loved in this unconditional way often do become good citizens, not because their parents trained them to be but because they grew up in an environment where love was abundant and therefore had the security to develop into people who could love others.

Hirschmann is right that not all parents succeed, and that the existence of parental failures is evidence against the naive view that good parenting is simply a matter of sufficient resources and loving attention. Some parents do a terrible job regardless of their circumstances, and their children pay the price. But this is an argument for better support for parents, not for judging parents by their outputs.

I want to suggest a different framework, one that takes seriously both the value of care and the goal of equality. This framework would start from the recognition that care is a fundamental human activity, one that all people should have the opportunity to engage in and that all people should share. It would aim not to make care optional or to assign it to those who are most economically dispensable, but to make it possible for everyone—men and women, rich and poor—to care for those they love without sacrificing their independence or their equality.

Such a framework would require political changes: affordable childcare, parental leave, flexible work, wage equity. But it would also require a cultural shift in how we understand care. Care is not a feminine virtue or a private family matter; it is a fundamental human activity that should be valued and shared. When men share care work, they become more fully human, and so do women who are relieved of the assumption that care is their special responsibility.

Hirschmann's conclusion—that women need to care a little less—misunderstands this goal. The aim is not for women to care less but for everyone to care more, and differently. Men need to care more about the people they live with; women need to be supported in caring without being defined by it. This is a transformation not just of families but of society, and it will require both individual change and collective action.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Treating care as a public good with measurable economic outputs dehumanizes the parent-child relationship and ignores its intrinsic emotional value.
  2. 02The goal of social policy should be to enable sustainable caregiving relationships for everyone rather than creating a market for care or taxing its emotional benefits.
  3. 03Judging parents based on whether their children become 'good citizens' applies a flawed consequentialist logic to the unconditional nature of parenting.
  4. 04True gender equality requires a cultural shift where care is recognized as a fundamental human activity shared by all, rather than a burden women should simply do less of.
  5. 05Political reforms like affordable childcare and flexible work should aim to protect the independence of all caregivers rather than merely compensating them for labor.
