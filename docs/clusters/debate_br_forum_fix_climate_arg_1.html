"While experimentalist governance is a necessary shift from failed global diplomacy, it must be integrated with a focus on power dynamics, social justice, and state capacity to overcome the entrenched interests of the fossil fuel economy."

Victor and Sabel are right to puncture the comforting myth that a single global bargain will “solve” climate change. The climate regime has too often been judged by the elegance of its summit declarations rather than by tons of carbon dioxide kept out of the atmosphere. Their central insight—that decarbonization will advance through iterative, sectoral problem solving rather than one grand act of diplomatic will—is indispensable. But experimentalist governance, if it is to be more than a sophisticated name for muddling through, must be joined to a more explicit account of power, justice, and political economy. The climate crisis is not only a problem of uncertainty. It is also a problem of entrenched wealth, unequal vulnerability, and organized obstruction.

The Montreal Protocol is indeed the best example we have of a successful global environmental regime. Its virtues are exactly as Victor and Sabel describe: sectoral knowledge, repeated revision, financial assistance for poorer countries, and a credible threat that laggards would lose access to markets. It also shows that policy can create facts. Once governments signaled that ozone-depleting substances would be phased out, firms searched harder for substitutes; once substitutes existed, regulation could tighten. The apparent “cost” of environmental protection changed as innovation unfolded.

Climate policy needs this dynamic view of costs. For decades, opponents of climate action treated the energy system as if it were fixed: any reduction in emissions must therefore mean a reduction in prosperity. That assumption has already been shattered in parts of the economy. The cost of solar power, wind power, and batteries has fallen dramatically over the last decade, not by magic but through deployment, learning, supply chains, standards, and public support. California’s zero-emission vehicle rules, Germany’s feed-in tariffs, Chinese industrial policy, and U.S. federal tax credits have all helped create markets in which firms learned how to produce clean technologies at scale. The lesson is not that markets alone will save us. It is that markets respond to rules, subsidies, procurement, and expectations.

Still, the Montreal analogy has limits that should make us cautious. Ozone-depleting chemicals were important, but they were not the foundation of modern political economy. Fossil fuels are. Coal, oil, and gas are embedded in electricity, transport, agriculture, buildings, trade, geopolitics, and public finance. They generate not only emissions but rents: revenues for states, profits for companies, wages for workers, and cheap energy for consumers. The world does not merely need substitutes for a class of chemicals; it needs to transform the material basis of development.

That difference matters because obstruction in climate politics is not simply a temporary information problem. It is often a rational strategy by incumbents whose assets lose value in a decarbonizing world. Oil and gas companies may invest in low-carbon technologies, but many have also funded doubt, delayed regulation, and lobbied to preserve demand for their products. Coal interests have fought air pollution rules as well as carbon rules. Automakers have alternately cooperated with and resisted tighter standards. Utilities have embraced renewables where regulation rewards them and resisted them where business models are threatened. Experimental governance must therefore include conflict, not just learning.

The phrase “penalty default” is useful here, but it should be sharpened. Penalties do not appear automatically when innovators demonstrate feasibility. They are made by politics. Trade measures, procurement standards, clean electricity mandates, phaseout dates for internal combustion engines, methane rules, financial disclosure requirements, and public litigation are not merely technical devices. They are exercises of public authority that change the bargaining position of incumbents. Without them, experimentation can become a refuge for delay: pilot projects without phaseouts, voluntary pledges without accountability, innovation funds without emissions cuts.

The Paris Agreement is weaker than many of us would like, but its weakness should not be overstated. Paris did not create a world government; it created a framework for expectation, comparison, mobilization, and ratcheting. That is not enough, but it is not nothing. The very fact that governments, cities, investors, and companies now argue in the language of “net zero” reflects a change in the political field. The European Union has moved toward a Green Deal. China has announced an aim of carbon neutrality before 2060. Japan and South Korea have announced mid-century net-zero goals. These pledges are not self-executing and may prove hollow. But they create openings for domestic actors to demand plans, budgets, standards, and investments.

This is where Victor and Sabel’s sectoral approach can strengthen Paris rather than bypass it. The problem with nationally determined contributions is not simply that they are national. It is that they are too aggregated. A national pledge can conceal whether a country has a credible plan for steel, cement, transport, buildings, methane, land use, and electricity. Sectoral review would make the Paris process more real. Instead of asking only whether a country has promised a 30 or 50 percent reduction by a given date, reviewers should ask: How fast is coal power retiring? Are new buildings being required to meet high efficiency standards? Is the grid being prepared for variable renewables? Are industrial plants testing hydrogen, electrification, or carbon capture where appropriate? Are methane leaks measured and regulated? Are public funds supporting workers and communities through transition?

Such questions would also expose a deeper issue: the capacity of states. Experimentalism requires capable public institutions. It needs agencies able to gather information, convene firms and workers, set standards, monitor results, revise rules, and resist capture. CARB is not powerful because it is experimental in the abstract; it is powerful because California built a durable regulatory apparatus with legal authority, technical expertise, and political backing. Many governments lack that capacity. Some are dependent on fossil revenues. Some face debt burdens, weak grids, informal economies, or basic development needs that make decarbonization harder. If climate cooperation is to work, it must finance not only technologies but institutions.

That is why the Multilateral Fund in the Montreal regime is so important—and why climate finance remains a moral and practical test. Wealthy countries promised to mobilize $100 billion annually for climate action in developing countries by 2020. Even if the accounting is disputed, the scale is plainly inadequate relative to the need. A serious climate regime must help countries build clean power systems, adapt to unavoidable warming, protect forests without impoverishing local communities, and avoid locking in high-carbon infrastructure. Loans alone will not do. Nor will a model in which poor countries are asked to bear the risks of experimentation while rich countries capture the patents and supply chains.

Justice is not an ornament to climate policy; it is a condition of durability. In the United States, the politics of decarbonization will fail if it is perceived as a project by professionals for professionals, with costs dumped on workers and benefits captured by investors. Coal miners, refinery workers, oilfield communities, and manufacturing towns have reason to distrust promises of “transition” when previous waves of economic change left many places abandoned. A Green New Deal, whatever one thinks of the slogan, names a real political requirement: climate policy must visibly improve people’s lives. Cleaner air, cheaper energy bills, good jobs, reliable transit, healthier housing, and investment in neglected communities are not side benefits. They are the social foundation of ambitious decarbonization.

Here experimental governance has something valuable to offer progressives as well as technocrats. Grand spending bills can fail if they do not learn. Weatherization programs, transit investments, grid upgrades, and industrial policy all require feedback from the people who use and implement them. A national clean energy standard or large public investment program should not be designed as a rigid blueprint from Washington. It should set goals, fund local and regional experimentation, compare performance, spread successes, and correct failures. But it must also have deadlines and consequences. Learning without obligation becomes drift; obligation without learning becomes backlash.

The same is true internationally. Sectoral clubs may be essential, especially in steel, cement, aviation, shipping, methane, and vehicles. Countries and firms willing to move first should coordinate standards, share early costs, and protect cleaner producers from being undercut by dirtier competitors. Border carbon adjustments, if carefully designed, may become part of that strategy. But clubs must not become green protectionism in disguise. If wealthy countries impose carbon standards on imports while refusing finance and technology cooperation, they will reproduce the inequities that have long poisoned climate diplomacy. The point should be to raise the floor, not pull up the ladder.

The immediate task, in late 2020, is to turn the new political opening into institutions that can survive polarization. A Biden administration can rejoin Paris quickly, but executive action alone is fragile. Standards for vehicles, methane, appliances, federal procurement, public lands, and power plant pollution matter, but they can be litigated, delayed, or reversed. Durable progress will require congressional investment if possible, state action where necessary, and alliances among labor, environmental justice groups, firms, farmers, engineers, and local officials. The federal government should use its purchasing power to create markets for low-carbon steel, cement, vehicles, and electricity. It should fund demonstration projects while requiring transparent evaluation. It should support communities dependent on fossil fuels before collapse arrives, not after.

Victor and Sabel’s most important contribution is to redirect attention from climate diplomacy as theater to climate governance as practice. They remind us that uncertainty is not an excuse for paralysis; it is a reason to build institutions that learn. But the climate problem is urgent enough that learning must occur under pressure. We need experimentation, yes—but experimentation disciplined by emissions targets, public investment, enforceable standards, and justice. We need sectoral problem solving, but also mass politics capable of overcoming those who profit from delay. We need Paris, not as a savior, but as a platform on which more concrete regimes can be built.

The world will not fix the climate in one agreement. It will fix it, if it does, by making fossil fuel dependence less profitable, clean alternatives more available, and transition more just. Experimentalist governance can help us discover how. Politics will decide whether we actually do it.

"While experimentalist governance is a useful framework for technical implementation, it is insufficient for solving the climate crisis because it fails to address the fundamental political challenges of building mass coalitions, confronting entrenched fossil fuel interests, and meeting urgent scientific timelines."

There is an admirable patience in Sabel and Victor's essay, and a kind of intellectual honesty too rare in climate writing: they admit that we do not know how to decarbonize, that the best paths will be discovered through trial and error, and that the institutions for that discovery do not yet exist. They are right that Kyoto-style top-down targets failed, right that Paris is a thin reed, and right that Montreal worked through a sectoral, iterative, peer-reviewed process rather than because a single comprehensive treaty waved a magic wand. As a description of how technical problems get solved inside industries, "experimentalist governance" is a useful name for something that good regulators and good firms have always done.

And yet, reading the essay carefully, one comes away with an unsettling realization: the model of politics it implies has almost no place in it for the people who actually have to live in the world being remade. The "ground-level actors" are industrial enterprises, local regulators, technical committees, peer reviewers. The "stakeholders" are pharmaceutical companies and device manufacturers. John Dewey is invoked to bless this arrangement, but Dewey's "public" was not the assembled R&D departments of DuPont and Maersk. The public was the people whose lives are being rearranged by industrial transformations they did not choose. In the Montreal case, the rearrangement was minor — switching propellants in an inhaler is not a political event. In the case of deep decarbonization, the rearrangement is total: how people heat their homes, how they get to work, what jobs exist in their region, what their food costs, what their landscape looks like. To call this an experimentalist governance problem is to misname it. It is a political problem about who pays, who gains, and who decides.

This is why the Green New Deal frame, which Sabel and Victor mention only briefly and somewhat dismissively, is more than just a "vision strong on mandates and silent on governance." It is an attempt to do something the experimentalist model conspicuously avoids: to name an explicit political coalition that will benefit from decarbonization and to bind that coalition into the project. Workers in retrofitting trades. Tenants in newly weatherized public housing. Communities promised the cleanup of toxic sites and the closure of coal plants on their borders. Bus riders in cities with newly electrified fleets. The "governance" of the Green New Deal is not silent; it is the governance of a democratic majority assembled around tangible material gains and willing to defend them against the incumbents who lose.

Sabel and Victor's model, by contrast, treats incumbents as participants in the experimentalist committees. DuPont, in their telling, broke ranks because the alternatives "might prove more profitable." This is the model: enlightened firms see the writing on the wall and innovate their way to advantage. But the fossil fuel sector is not the CFC business. ExxonMobil's reserves are valued at trillions of dollars; the entire enterprise of the company depends on those reserves being burned. There is no benign alternative product that allows Saudi Aramco to continue earning monopoly rents. The political economy of carbon is not the political economy of refrigerants, and the strategy of nudging incumbents toward profitable alternatives confuses a problem of capital destruction with a problem of technical substitution.

Naomi Klein has put this point in a way that Sabel and Victor's essay does not engage: the climate crisis is the consequence of an economic order, and it will not be solved by tweaking the procedures of that order's regulators. Andreas Malm has put it more bluntly: fossil capital must be defeated, not invited onto sectoral committees. One does not have to accept the entirety of these arguments to feel the force of their objection to the essay's framing. When Sabel and Victor describe Montreal's success at threatening Russia with trade sanctions, the threat was wielded by states whose home industries had already begun the transition and stood to benefit from forcing competitors to follow. When the question is fossil energy, the equivalent leverage does not yet exist. The Saudis are not going to be sanctioned by anyone for selling oil. The "penalty default" that destabilizes the status quo has to be built, politically, before it can be wielded technocratically. That building is the work of mass movements, electoral coalitions, and confrontations with concentrated wealth — work that the experimentalist vocabulary cannot quite see.

Consider the actual sites of climate progress in the United States over the past decade. The fact that coal has collapsed in the American electricity sector is the result of three forces: cheap gas from fracking (a mixed environmental blessing), cheap renewables driven by Chinese manufacturing scale and German feed-in tariffs (a deliberate industrial policy choice), and Sierra Club's Beyond Coal campaign, which fought plant by plant in state public utility commissions to deny permits and force closures. The third of these is the one most like Sabel and Victor's vision — local, iterative, learning by doing — but it succeeded because it was animated by a confrontational politics that named coal companies as adversaries rather than partners in a sectoral committee. The Sierra Club did not invite Peabody Energy to help design the phase-out.

Sabel and Victor will respond, I think, that nothing in their model precludes confrontational politics; that "penalty defaults" can be wielded by protesters as much as by regulators; and that their model is meant to describe how change consolidates after political opening, not how political opening occurs. Fair enough. But the essay's center of gravity is in the consolidation phase, not the opening phase, and it gives almost no guidance about how the opening is to be achieved. The closing paragraphs on the Biden administration are revealing: they speak of "administrative action" and "place-based solutions" as if these were the natural extensions of the Montreal-style sectoral approach. They are not. They are the constrained substitutes available because Congress is captured and the political coalition for transformation has not been assembled. Working around political deadlock is not the same as overcoming it.

There is also a question of timeline that the essay touches on but does not really sit with. Montreal took, charitably, a decade from first treaty to substantial reductions, and longer to reach the developing world. The IMO has been working on shipping decarbonization for fifteen years and is still arguing about whether to set a binding emissions target. If sectoral experimentalism on the climate problem proceeds at this pace, across the dozens of sectors that need to be transformed, we will warm well past 2°C while the committees deliberate. The IPCC's 1.5°C report from 2018 gave us roughly a decade to halve global emissions. Experimentalist governance, as Sabel and Victor describe it, is a beautiful long-run institutional ecology. We do not have a long run.

This is where the Green New Deal vocabulary, for all its sloppiness, has the advantage of being honest about timescale. It says: we are going to spend money, on a massive scale, immediately, to build out clean infrastructure, because we have no time for incremental sectoral learning. It says: we are going to use public power to commission, deploy, and standardize, because the market is too slow and the incumbents too entrenched. It says: we are going to assemble a political majority that will defend this program against rollback, because without that majority the program will be reversed. These are claims about politics, not about governance procedures, and they are the claims that the essay's experimentalist frame cannot accommodate.

I do not want to oppose Sabel and Victor's model entirely. The work of sectoral standard-setting, peer review, and iterative adjustment is real and necessary. Once a political coalition has authorized transformation, the experimentalist machinery they describe is exactly what is needed to translate authorization into accomplishment. But that machinery does not authorize itself. It does not generate the political will that funds it, the public legitimacy that defends it, or the confrontation with incumbents that clears the field for it. Dewey understood this; his pragmatism was always in service of a more democratic politics, not a substitute for one. To borrow his metaphor: it is the citizen who knows where the shoe pinches. On climate, the shoe pinches in places — communities sacrificed to extraction, workers stranded by transition, futures foreclosed by inaction — that no sectoral committee, however expertly composed, is positioned to feel.

"While experimentalist governance provides a useful framework for institutional learning, it is insufficient for solving the climate crisis unless it is integrated with a political strategy that builds democratic power to defeat entrenched fossil fuel interests."

David Victor and Charles Sabel offer a sophisticated answer to a question that has haunted climate politics for decades: why have so many declarations produced so little decarbonization? Their answer is institutional. Global climate diplomacy has tried to secure universal agreement before discovering workable pathways. It has relied too much on top-down pledges and too little on iterative, sectoral problem solving. We should instead build experimental institutions that learn from practice, tighten standards as solutions emerge, and make obstruction increasingly costly.

There is much to admire in this account. But it leaves too little room for conflict. Climate action is not only a puzzle to be solved. It is a political struggle to be won.

The distinction matters. If climate change were mainly a problem of uncertainty, then experimentalist governance would be the natural response. Bring together regulators, firms, experts, and stakeholders; set provisional goals; try multiple approaches; monitor results; revise; scale successes. In many areas this is exactly right. No one can know in advance the best way to retrofit every building, integrate renewables into every grid, or decarbonize every industrial process. Institutions that can learn are indispensable.

But climate inaction has never been caused only by lack of knowledge. For at least three decades, the world has known enough to act far more aggressively than it has. Scientists have warned of warming. Economists have described externalities. Engineers have improved alternatives. Public health experts have documented the co-benefits of reducing fossil fuel combustion. The problem is not that governments and firms have been waiting innocently for better information. It is that powerful interests benefit from the carbon economy and have fought to preserve it.

That fight has taken many forms: lobbying against regulation, funding doubt about climate science, attacking renewable energy mandates, defending fossil fuel subsidies, shaping permitting rules, influencing trade policy, and warning workers that climate action threatens their livelihoods. In the United States, the fossil fuel industry and its allies helped turn climate change into a partisan identity marker. Elsewhere, state-owned oil, gas, and coal interests are deeply entangled with public revenue and national development strategies. These are not mere “laggards.” They are organized opponents.

Victor and Sabel know this, of course. They write about penalties, defaults, and destabilizing the status quo. Yet the tone of experimentalism can make the transition sound more consensual than it will be. Some incumbents can be induced to innovate. Others must be defeated. A politics that cannot say this plainly will not be adequate to the task.

This is where popular protest, which the lead essay treats ambivalently, becomes central. It is true that protest can complicate global bargaining. But protest has also done more than almost any diplomatic process to change the climate agenda. Youth climate strikes, Indigenous pipeline resistance, fossil fuel divestment campaigns, anti-fracking movements, environmental justice organizing, and groups willing to use civil disobedience have shifted the boundaries of the possible. They have made climate delay morally disreputable. They have forced institutions to declare emergencies, universities and pension funds to reconsider investments, banks to defend fossil lending, and politicians to support policies they once dismissed as unrealistic.

Without movements, there is no “penalty default.” Firms do not fear reputational damage in the abstract; they fear organized publics. Governments do not raise ambition because policy learning gently reveals better options; they do so because citizens demand action, courts intervene, opposition parties mobilize, and social legitimacy changes. Even the Paris Agreement’s temperature goals owe something to the pressure of vulnerable states and civil society movements insisting that 2 degrees was not enough and that 1.5 degrees mattered.

The Green New Deal should be understood in this light. Victor and Sabel are right that Green New Deal proposals often say more about vision than governance. They identify a real weakness. Any serious program must grapple with implementation: which agencies, which standards, which funds, which timelines, which accountability mechanisms? A slogan cannot build transmission lines or train workers. But the Green New Deal’s contribution is not primarily administrative. It is political. It reframes climate action from sacrifice to reconstruction, from technocratic carbon management to jobs, justice, housing, transit, health, and public investment. That reframing is not ornamental. It is how majorities are built.

Experimentalist governance needs that kind of democratic energy. Otherwise it risks becoming a conversation among regulators, experts, and firms, with citizens invited only as sources of local information. Dewey’s image of the shoe and the wearer is helpful, but democracy is not just telling the shoemaker where it pinches. It is deciding what kind of shoes society should make, who makes them, under what conditions, who can afford them, and whether some industries should stop making harmful products altogether.

The climate transition will reach deeply into daily life. It will change cars, homes, farms, diets, landscapes, electricity systems, workplaces, and public budgets. These changes cannot be legitimated only through expert review. They require democratic contestation. People will disagree about nuclear power, carbon capture, transmission siting, biofuels, mining for battery minerals, public ownership, carbon pricing, and the role of markets. Some disagreements will reflect misinformation or incumbent manipulation. Others will reflect real tradeoffs. Experimentalism can help test claims, but it cannot substitute for collective choice.

Take the example of California. The California Air Resources Board has indeed shown that regulation can push technology forward. But California’s climate politics has also revealed tensions that are not simply technical. Cap-and-trade has been criticized by environmental justice advocates who argue that market mechanisms can allow local pollution burdens to persist in disadvantaged communities. Housing policy and transportation emissions are bound up with land use, exclusionary zoning, and inequality. Wildfires and utility failures raise questions about infrastructure, public safety, and corporate accountability. California is a laboratory, but laboratories are political.

The same is true of Europe’s water governance, another example in the lead essay. Watershed management may involve expert monitoring and local adaptation, but it also implicates farmers’ livelihoods, food prices, land ownership, and rural power. If agricultural runoff is reduced, someone pays. If animal waste is regulated, some production models become less viable. Experimental institutions can make these conflicts more informed, but they do not make them disappear.

This is why climate governance should be designed not merely for learning but for countervailing power. Labor unions must be able to bargain over transition plans. Environmental justice communities must have enforceable rights, not just consultation opportunities. Indigenous peoples must have sovereignty and consent, not stakeholder status. Public agencies must have capacity to challenge corporate claims about what is feasible. Antitrust law may be needed to prevent dominant firms from shaping sectoral standards in their own interest. Transparency rules must expose lobbying, emissions data, and financial risk. Public ownership should be on the table where private utilities or infrastructure firms obstruct decarbonization.

The lead essay’s emphasis on sectoral action is useful, but sectors are not neutral spaces. A steel decarbonization forum dominated by large producers may protect incumbents against new entrants. An aviation process may focus on future fuels while avoiding demand management or frequent-flyer inequities. An oil and gas methane initiative may reduce leaks while extending the life of gas infrastructure. A shipping coalition may privilege the largest firms. Experimentalist governance must therefore be adversarial enough to ask whether a sector should shrink, not only how it should innovate.

This is especially important for fossil fuels. Much climate policy focuses on demand: cleaner cars, cleaner power, efficiency, renewables. But a serious transition also requires supply-side policy: stopping new exploration, restricting leases, ending public finance for fossil infrastructure, planning managed decline, and supporting workers and regions through the transition. Oil and gas companies may participate in carbon capture or hydrogen projects, but they cannot be allowed to define climate action as anything compatible with continued expansion. There is no experimental mystery about whether burning more fossil carbon increases warming.

The urgency of the climate crisis also requires democratic institutions capable of acting quickly. Deliberation is essential, but endless process can be weaponized. Fossil interests often demand more study, more consultation, more gradualism, and more “balance” as strategies of delay. Experimentalism must distinguish between learning and stalling. One way to do this is to set non-negotiable boundaries: emissions must fall, environmental justice must improve, workers must be protected, and new infrastructure must be consistent with net zero. Within those boundaries, experimentation can flourish.

The Biden administration, assuming the transition proceeds, will face exactly this dilemma. Rejoining Paris will be easy. Passing ambitious legislation through a polarized Congress may be hard, especially if the Senate remains closely divided. Administrative action will matter, as will state and local policy. But none of it will survive without organized constituencies. Regulations can be reversed. Standards can be litigated. Investments can be captured. Climate policy needs people who will defend it because it has improved their lives and because they see themselves in it.

That is the deepest weakness of climate diplomacy as we have known it: not only that it has been too global or too rigid, but that it has often been too distant from democratic life. Experimentalist governance can help close that distance if it is built around participation and accountability. But if it becomes another language of expert management, it will fail politically even when it succeeds technically.

The climate movement’s task is therefore twofold. It must demand institutions that learn, because rigid plans will break against reality. And it must build power, because learning alone will not overcome entrenched interests. The world needs standards, demonstration projects, sectoral clubs, finance, and review. It also needs strikes, elections, litigation, organizing, public ownership campaigns, and moral confrontation.

Victor and Sabel have given us an important institutional vocabulary. But the climate will not be fixed by governance design alone. It will be fixed, if at all, by democratic power using institutions to transform the economy faster than incumbents can stop it.

"While the experimentalist governance framework correctly identifies that climate progress depends on sectoral learning and technical feasibility rather than abstract global treaties, it must be supplemented by active movement-building and the symbolic legitimacy of the Paris Agreement to create the political space necessary for ambition."

Victor and Sabel have produced an essay of genuine intellectual distinction. Their experimentalist governance framework offers something increasingly rare in climate policy discourse: a pragmatic, empirically grounded approach that actually engages with how governance works in practice rather than how idealists imagine it should. I find myself largely persuaded by their analysis and want to extend it in a direction they only hint at.

The core insight—that climate diplomacy has been paralyzed by the assumption that solutions must be global from the outset—is exactly right. The Paris Agreement represents the logical endpoint of this assumption: a framework so accommodating that it allows every nation to do essentially nothing while maintaining the appearance of serious commitment. The NDC system is a masterpiece of diplomatic theater but a failure as policy architecture. When the measure of success is whether emissions peak, not whether they fall, you've already conceded the argument to the obstructionists.

The Montreal Protocol's success, by contrast, demonstrates what becomes possible when governance is organized around learning rather than predetermined outcomes. The sectoral approach is crucial here. Organizing problem-solving by industry—solvents, foams, refrigerants—allows regulators to engage directly with the technical realities that global frameworks obscure. What strikes me as most important, and underemphasized in Victor and Sabel's account, is how experimentalism creates political space for ambition. The "penalty default" mechanism they describe—where early innovators make it risky for laggards to resist—isn't just an enforcement tool. It's a political strategy. When California proved that vehicle emissions standards could work, it shifted the terrain of debate. When EU demonstrated that renewable integration was feasible, it became harder for other nations to claim impossibility. Each successful experiment doesn't just solve a piece of the problem; it changes what seems politically possible.

If shipping emissions become the site of a Maersk-led demonstration program that proves zero-carbon fuels viable, that changes the politics of maritime regulation everywhere. The experimentalist framework provides the governance architecture, but movement-building remains essential to populate that architecture with ambitious experiments.

The distinction Victor and Sabel draw between globalized, concentrated industries and place-based sectors is useful, but I think they understate the importance of the former. Deep decarbonization requires transforming the sectors they identify as "globalized and concentrated"—steel, cement, shipping, aviation. These industries are responsible for a substantial fraction of remaining emissions, and their global reach means that standards set in one jurisdiction ripple outward. The International Maritime Organization's slow progress on shipping emissions reflects precisely the dysfunction that experimentalism aims to address, but it also shows how high the stakes are. If Maersk and similar firms can be brought into sectoral demonstration programs with credible penalty defaults for nonparticipation, the effects could be transformative.

I want to push back, however, on the essay's treatment of the Paris Agreement. Victor and Sabel argue that Paris "has little to contribute" to the actual work of decarbonization. This seems to me to underestimate its political utility. Paris provides the legitimacy framework within which activist pressure operates. When Extinction Rebellion disrupts airline operations or when shareholders file climate resolutions, they invoke Paris. When journalists cover climate policy, they frame it against Paris commitments. This symbolic architecture matters politically, even if the agreement itself has no enforcement teeth. Paris is, as the essay acknowledges, the most legitimate venue for global climate discussion—a role that cannot easily be replicated by sectoral institutions no matter how technically sophisticated.

The essay's invocation of John Dewey is apt. Dewey's pragmatism emphasized learning through experience, adapting institutions to new circumstances, and integrating expert knowledge with democratic participation. Victor and Sabel's synthesis of these ideas with Montreal's institutional innovations points toward a genuinely post-ideological approach to climate governance—one that abandons both the fantasy of binding global commitments and the despair of "every little bit helps" incrementalism. Instead, it offers a pathway for ambitious action that remains responsive to technical reality and political constraint.

What remains underspecified is how to scale this approach. Montreal took over a decade to demonstrate success and required favorable geopolitical conditions—the Cold War's waning, American leadership, manageable industry structure. Climate change demands faster transformation across more diverse and politically powerful sectors. The experimentalist framework needs supplementation with strategies for accelerating the pace of institutional development. Perhaps that means more aggressive use of penalty defaults—immediate trade sanctions for noncompliance rather than graduated responses. Perhaps it means larger multilateral funds to make alternatives economically compelling for developing countries. Perhaps it means building experimentalist institutions in parallel with rather than after securing political commitments.

Regardless, Victor and Sabel have identified the right problem and pointed toward promising solutions. The world does not lack for ambitious climate targets; it lacks for governance mechanisms capable of achieving them. Experimentalist governance, properly understood and aggressively pursued, offers a way forward that neither corporate voluntarism nor global treaty-making has provided. The essay deserves the serious engagement it has already received and should inform how policymakers think about institutional design for the decades of difficult work ahead.

David G. Victor and Charles Sabel offer a deeply pragmatic, structurally sound diagnosis of the failures of global climate diplomacy. By correctly identifying that the top-down, consensus-driven model of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) is ill-equipped to handle the immense complexities of deep decarbonization, they clear the air of a pervasive diplomatic delusion. Their proposed alternative—experimentalist governance—provides a compelling operational blueprint. Shifting our focus from monolithic, legally binding global treaties to sector-by-sector innovation, driven by frontline actors and enforced by "penalty defaults," is precisely the strategic pivot the climate crisis demands.

However, while the architecture of the Montreal Protocol serves as an instructional model for this kind of governance, its political economy is dangerously misleading when applied to the fossil fuel economy. Victor and Sabel accurately extract the governance mechanisms that made Montreal work, but they underestimate the sheer gravitational pull of the carbon economy. To make experimentalist governance succeed in the 2020s, we must recognize that the "penalty defaults" required to break the fossil fuel status quo will need to be vastly more muscular, confrontational, and economically disruptive than anything attempted in the 1980s.

The core limitation of the Montreal analogy lies in the nature of the pollutant and the industries that produce it. Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) and other ozone-depleting substances were discrete industrial chemicals utilized in specific, largely peripheral sectors: refrigerants, aerosols, and solvents. When the ozone hole was discovered, the chemical manufacturers—most notably DuPont—initially resisted regulation. But once the science became undeniable and the regulatory writing was on the wall, these firms realized they could pivot. In fact, DuPont championed the phase-out of CFCs precisely because they held the patents for the hydrofluorocarbon (HFC) alternatives. The transition was a manageable, and ultimately profitable, technological substitution for a concentrated sector.

Greenhouse gas emissions, by contrast, are not a byproduct of a specific chemical process; they are the fundamental energy backbone of modern industrial civilization. Decarbonizing the global economy does not mean asking a few chemical companies to switch their product lines; it means rendering trillions of dollars of fossil fuel reserves permanently stranded. It presents an existential threat to multinational oil majors, massive utility companies, and entirely petroleum-dependent sovereign states.

Consequently, Victor and Sabel’s vision of sectoral working groups, where industry regulators and producers collaborate to find alternatives, risks profound regulatory capture if applied naively to fossil fuels. The fossil fuel industry has spent the last three decades systematically funding disinformation, capturing legislative bodies, and delaying action. Unlike the makers of metered-dose inhalers or refrigerators, the incumbent giants of the carbon economy will not sit at the table in good faith to engineer their own demise. If they are invited to co-design the transition, they will use the language of experimentalism—pilot projects, feasibility studies, and transition schedules—to institutionalize delay.

This means that for experimentalist governance to work, the "penalty defaults"—the threats that make inaction riskier than innovation—must be external and incredibly severe. Victor and Sabel note that penalty defaults destabilize the status quo, transforming obstruction into a risky bet. But where do these defaults come from today? The Paris Agreement, as they note, provides a moral and political authorization for action, but it lacks teeth. In November 2020, as we look to rebuild the post-pandemic global economy, the necessary penalty defaults must be forged in the realms of hard economic policy: international trade, financial regulation, and aggressive industrial policy.

At the international level, the most potent penalty default currently under development is the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM). As the European Union moves forward with its European Green Deal, the implementation of a CBAM would effectively impose a tariff on carbon-intensive imports from countries that fail to price or regulate emissions. This perfectly aligns with Victor and Sabel’s framework: it does not demand global consensus at the WTO or the UNFCCC, but rather uses the leverage of a massive economic bloc to force a sectoral penalty on laggards. If the EU penalizes imported steel or cement made with high-carbon energy, producers in China, India, and the United States will be forced to innovate simply to maintain market access. This is experimentalist governance with real geopolitical leverage. The incoming Biden administration should immediately look to coordinate with the EU on such border adjustments to create a transatlantic penalty default for high-emitting sectors.

A second vital domain for penalty defaults lies in the global financial system. Central banks, financial regulators, and massive asset managers are slowly waking up to the systemic risks of climate change. Efforts to mandate climate-risk disclosures and perform climate stress-tests on major financial institutions operate precisely on the logic of experimentalist governance. By requiring banks to transparently model how their portfolios would fare under rapid decarbonization scenarios, regulators force the market to price in the risk of stranded assets. If the cost of capital for coal plants and oil exploration skyrockets because investors fear future regulation, the market itself becomes a penalty default. Frontline actors—in this case, investors and corporate boards—are forced to shift capital toward clean energy, not out of altruism, but out of financial self-preservation.

Furthermore, applying experimentalist governance to the global climate crisis requires a massive escalation of the funding mechanisms Victor and Sabel praise. The authors rightly highlight the Montreal Protocol’s Multilateral Fund, which subsidized the transition for developing nations and helped build local administrative capacity. It was an essential tool for preventing diplomatic deadlock. However, replacing CFCs globally cost roughly a few billion dollars over a couple of decades. Montreal succeeded in significant part because DuPont saw profit in alternatives to CFCs. The pharmaceutical industry cooperated on meter-dose inhalers because substitutes were commercially viable. These were not expressions of environmental virtue; they were responses to economic opportunity. Victor and Sabel recognize this but seem to believe that similar conditions can be created through institutional design. The penalty default, they argue, will make laggards face market exclusion once leaders demonstrate alternatives. But this mechanism requires that alternatives actually become available and economically competitive—a process that may take decades and require public investment at scales no experimentalist framework can generate.

Consider what their approach would require for steel, a sector they identify as critical. The primary route to green steel—hydrogen direct reduction—remains more expensive than conventional blast furnace production. No amount of institutional clever- ness will make hydrogen-based steel cost-competitive without massive public subsidy for research, demonstration, and deployment. The same holds for cement, aviation, shipping, and most industrial processes. Victor and Sabel are right that sectoral approaches can accelerate learning and identify feasible pathways, but they understate how much fundamental research and development remains necessary before experimentalist governance can operate as they envision.

The essay also underestimates the political power of fossil fuel interests. Victor and Sabel correctly document how "well-organized high-emitting industries" have obstructed climate policy for decades. But their governance recommendations do not address the underlying political economy. Experimentalism assumes that actors will engage constructively once institutional incentives align. History suggests otherwise. The same forces that captured climate diplomacy will seek to capture experimentalist institutions. Sectoral committees and assessment panels can be staffed with industryfriendly experts, populated with favorable case studies, and structured to favor gradualism over ambition. Without explicit attention to preventing regulatory capture, experimentalist governance could become a more technocratic version of the same dysfunction.

The essay's treatment of the Global South compounds this problem. Victor and Sabel note that Montreal's Multilateral Fund was "probably the best managed funding mechanism in the history of international environmental governance." They do not ask whether it was sufficient. For a relatively contained problem—eliminating specific chemicals used by specific industries—this level of support was adequate. Climate change is categorically different. Deep decarbonization in developing countries requires not just technology transfer but comprehensive restructuring of energy systems, industrial processes, and transportation infrastructure. The investment needs are measured in trillions, not billions. Unless wealthy nations commit to providing financing at scales comparable to these needs, experimentalist governance in developing countries will remain aspirational.

I raise these objections not to defend the status quo of failed global diplomacy but to argue that experimentalism, as currently formulated, is necessary but not sufficient. What is missing is a theory of political change—how to build constituencies capable of demanding ambitious action and sustaining it over the decades required.

Here the essay is oddly silent. Victor and Sabel write as if institutional design determines political possibility rather than the reverse. But history suggests that governance innovations follow political breakthroughs, not precede them. The environmental regulations of the 1970s emerged from social movements that built pressure for change. The Clean Air Act succeeded not because it embodied clever institutional design but because activists created political space for ambitious regulation. If the Green New Deal has any merit, it is as a political project for building constituencies—regardless of whether its specific provisions become law.

This suggests a different priority for climate advocates. Rather than focusing primarily on governance architecture, we should ask how to build political power. This means supporting candidates who will pursue aggressive climate action, funding organizations that can sustain pressure on recalcitrant politicians, and building alliances across the environmental, labor, and justice movements. It means making the economic case for decarbonization in terms that resonate with working-class voters who have legitimate concerns about jobs and costs. And it means accepting that some battles will be lost before they are won—because political change requires learning from failure as much as success.

Victor and Sabel might respond that experimentalism is precisely this kind of learning process—that by trying many approaches and scaling what works, we build political knowledge alongside technical knowledge. There is something to this. The essay's account of how Montreal's sectoral committees identified and spread successful alternatives suggests a model for political learning as well. But the parallel is incomplete. Political experimentation cannot be monitored through peer review in the same way as technical demonstrations. When experiments in economic policy or regulatory approach fail, the costs fall on real people whose suffering cannot be waved away as "necessary learning."

I do not want to be unfair to an essay that has much to offer. The Montreal analysis is the best I have read. The distinction between globalized and place-based sectors genuinely illuminates the different governance challenges they pose. And the call to "show that we can learn from our unconventional successes" is timely and important. The Paris Agreement has consumed enormous diplomatic energy for modest results; it makes sense to look elsewhere.

But the elsewhere we look should include not just governance innovation but political organizing. Experimentalism can contribute to the latter—it provides a framework for trying different political strategies and learning from results. What it cannot do is substitute for the harder, slower, more uncertain work of building movements capable of demanding and sustaining ambitious climate action. Victor and Sabel have given us useful tools for that project. They have not given us a theory of how to use them.

Victor and Sabel have written an essay that deserves credit for intellectual honesty. Rather than calling for more ambitious global targets or more binding commitments—the standard prescription for failed climate diplomacy—they ask what actually works and why. This is the right approach, and the essay deserves serious engagement from anyone concerned with climate policy.

Victor and Sabel document decades of failure in global climate diplomacy and correctly identify governance pathologies as central to that failure. But governance pathologies are themselves products of political dynamics—the power of fossil fuel interests, the short-termism of electoral politics, the divergence between global costs and local benefits. Fixing governance without fixing these underlying politics leaves the fundamental problem unaddressed.

Consider the Montreal Protocol, which the essay rightly celebrates. What made Montreal possible was not clever institutional design but a specific political configuration: American leadership under a Republican administration that was willing and able to impose costs on noncompliant actors, an industry structure in which major players saw profit in alternatives, and a manageable set of chemicals whose sources and substitutes were well understood. These conditions do not currently exist for climate change. The United States is polarized on climate policy; the fossil fuel industry remains politically powerful; and the technologies for deep decarbonization are often not yet competitive. Institutional innovation cannot substitute for the political work of creating conditions analogous to those that enabled Montreal.

This suggests that experimentalist governance, as Victor and Sabel describe it, is better understood as a complement to political mobilization than a substitute for it. The framework provides tools for organizing sectoral efforts, creating learning mechanisms, and applying pressure to laggards. Victor and Sabel note that "we must build institutions for learning under uncertainty as rapidly as possible" and that "global agreements will not be the engine for change." This is not a fatal flaw—the essay's primary contribution is analytical rather than prescriptive—but it leaves a gap between diagnosis and program.

I also want to note what the essay gets exactly right: the critique of the top-down/bottom-up dichotomy that has paralyzed climate diplomacy. Paris was adopted in reaction to Kyoto's failure, which was understood as a failure of top-down binding commitments. Paris's voluntarism was thus presented as the opposite of Kyoto's approach. But the opposite of a failed approach is not necessarily a successful one; it may simply be a different failure mode. In practice, Montreal's framework allowed wealthy countries to maintain their technological advantages while requiring developing countries to adopt standards set by others. Despite these concerns, I endorse the essay's core argument. Global climate diplomacy has failed because it was designed around assumptions—universal binding commitments, formal consensus, top-down implementation—that do not match the nature of the problem. Experimentalist governance offers an alternative: thin consensus on goals, devolved problem-solving, peer review, and penalty defaults that make obstruction costly. But "might" is the operative word. The essay provides a vision of what successful climate governance could look like. Building the political conditions for that vision is the work that remains. It is the harder work, and the essay's silence on how to accomplish it is its most significant limitation.

When diplomats negotiate sectoral agreements, they reference Paris goals. This legitimizing function is not trivial; it shapes what is politically possible.

The NDC system, whatever its substantive weakness, provides a framework for tracking national commitments. The periodic review process creates opportunities for pressure and learning. "The proposed "experimentalist governance" model is a dangerously inadequate solution to the climate crisis because it fundamentally misinterprets the fossil fuel industry's entrenched power and the existential threat decarbonization poses to its business model."

In "How to Fix the Climate," David G. Victor and Charles Sabel diagnose a real disease—the paralysis of global climate diplomacy—but prescribe a dangerously inadequate cure. Their argument for "experimentalist governance" hinges on a fundamental category error: they equate the challenge of phasing out ozone-depleting substances with the challenge of dismantling the fossil fuel economy. In doing so, they drastically misunderstand the political economy of the climate crisis, specifically the nature of the entrenched corporate power that profits from planetary destruction.

Victor and Sabel’s blueprint relies heavily on the success of the Montreal Protocol. They argue that by breaking the problem down into sectors and bringing industry, academia, and regulators together into committees, we can spur innovation, find substitutes, and gradually ratchet up standards. They point to DuPont, the dominant manufacturer of CFCs, noting that the company "broke ranks with the rest of industry to demand a phaseout" because the alternatives proved profitable. The authors suggest we can replicate this dynamic with greenhouse gas emissions.

This analogy collapses upon contact with reality. DuPont was a diversified chemical company for whom CFCs represented a fraction of overall revenue. When science revealed that CFCs were destroying the ozone layer, DuPont could transition to producing hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs) and continue its business model largely uninterrupted. The company was in the business of selling chemical refrigerants; the specific atomic makeup of the chemicals was incidental.

The fossil fuel industry is entirely different. ExxonMobil, Chevron, Saudi Aramco, and Peabody Energy are not diversified energy companies that happen to sell carbon; their entire capitalization, business model, and asset base are tied inextricably to the extraction, refining, and combustion of fossil fuels. There is no drop-in, highly profitable substitute that allows an oil major to simply swap out its core product while maintaining its current market dominance and asset valuation. To decarbonize the global economy is not to invite the fossil fuel industry to "innovate"—it is to demand its managed extinction.

By failing to recognize this distinction, Victor and Sabel propose a governance model that is ripe for regulatory capture. They advocate for sectoral committees where "ground-level initiative" is encouraged by "actors with detailed knowledge of mitigation problems." In the energy, automotive, and heavy industry sectors, the actors with the most "detailed knowledge" are the very corporations that have spent the last four decades funding climate denial, lobbying against emissions standards, and deliberately obfuscating the science.

Inviting these actors to co-design the pace of decarbonization through experimentalist committees is akin to inviting the tobacco industry to design lung cancer prevention protocols. We do not have to guess how this turns out. When the fossil fuel industry is given a seat at the table to determine "feasible" timelines for emission reductions, they invariably produce timelines that align with the total depletion of their proven reserves. They promote false solutions like carbon capture and storage (CCS) to justify the continued extraction of coal and gas, turning "experimentalism" into an endless pilot-project purgatory designed to delay the implementation of existing, viable renewable technologies.

Victor and Sabel also place great faith in "penalty defaults"—the threat of exclusion from markets or draconian regulatory actions—to force laggard firms into compliance. But penalty defaults only work if the governing body has the political power to enforce them. The authors casually mention that we must "unseat powerful interests," but they offer no theory of power for how to achieve this. How do experimentalist sectoral committees impose penalty defaults on the fossil fuel industry when that industry effectively owns the political systems of the United States, Russia, Saudi Arabia, and Australia?

The authors express a mild disdain for calls for "massive public investments such as a Green New Deal," categorizing such visions as finding "only tentative success." But the Green New Deal framework recognizes what Victor and Sabel ignore: climate change is a problem of power, not just a problem of technological optimization. You cannot nudge or experiment your way out of a crisis driven by the most profitable enterprise in human history. Decarbonization requires the mobilization of state power to actively dismantle fossil fuel infrastructure, forcefully retire stranded assets, and protect workers and communities during the transition.

We are writing in late 2020. The impending Biden administration faces a polarized political landscape, as the authors note. But the solution is not to retreat into technocratic, industry-led sectoral committees. The solution is to recognize that climate policy is industrial policy, and industrial policy must be directed by the public interest, not by the very corporations whose fiduciary duty is to dig up more carbon. Experimentalist governance might be an excellent way to figure out the best battery chemistry for electric vehicles or the optimal feed additive to reduce methane in cattle. But as a master framework for global climate survival, it is a dangerous illusion that underestimates the ruthlessness of the enemy.
