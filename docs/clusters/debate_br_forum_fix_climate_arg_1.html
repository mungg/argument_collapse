Diversified Claude

"While experimentalist governance is a useful framework for technical implementation, it is insufficient for solving the climate crisis because it fails to address the fundamental political challenges of building mass coalitions, confronting entrenched fossil fuel interests, and meeting urgent scientific timelines."

There is an admirable patience in Sabel and Victor's essay, and a kind of intellectual honesty too rare in climate writing: they admit that we do not know how to decarbonize, that the best paths will be discovered through trial and error, and that the institutions for that discovery do not yet exist. They are right that Kyoto-style top-down targets failed, right that Paris is a thin reed, and right that Montreal worked through a sectoral, iterative, peer-reviewed process rather than because a single comprehensive treaty waved a magic wand. As a description of how technical problems get solved inside industries, "experimentalist governance" is a useful name for something that good regulators and good firms have always done.

And yet, reading the essay carefully, one comes away with an unsettling realization: the model of politics it implies has almost no place in it for the people who actually have to live in the world being remade. The "ground-level actors" are industrial enterprises, local regulators, technical committees, peer reviewers. The "stakeholders" are pharmaceutical companies and device manufacturers. John Dewey is invoked to bless this arrangement, but Dewey's "public" was not the assembled R&D departments of DuPont and Maersk. The public was the people whose lives are being rearranged by industrial transformations they did not choose. In the Montreal case, the rearrangement was minor — switching propellants in an inhaler is not a political event. In the case of deep decarbonization, the rearrangement is total: how people heat their homes, how they get to work, what jobs exist in their region, what their food costs, what their landscape looks like. To call this an experimentalist governance problem is to misname it. It is a political problem about who pays, who gains, and who decides.

This is why the Green New Deal frame, which Sabel and Victor mention only briefly and somewhat dismissively, is more than just a "vision strong on mandates and silent on governance." It is an attempt to do something the experimentalist model conspicuously avoids: to name an explicit political coalition that will benefit from decarbonization and to bind that coalition into the project. Workers in retrofitting trades. Tenants in newly weatherized public housing. Communities promised the cleanup of toxic sites and the closure of coal plants on their borders. Bus riders in cities with newly electrified fleets. The "governance" of the Green New Deal is not silent; it is the governance of a democratic majority assembled around tangible material gains and willing to defend them against the incumbents who lose.

Sabel and Victor's model, by contrast, treats incumbents as participants in the experimentalist committees. DuPont, in their telling, broke ranks because the alternatives "might prove more profitable." This is the model: enlightened firms see the writing on the wall and innovate their way to advantage. But the fossil fuel sector is not the CFC business. ExxonMobil's reserves are valued at trillions of dollars; the entire enterprise of the company depends on those reserves being burned. There is no benign alternative product that allows Saudi Aramco to continue earning monopoly rents. The political economy of carbon is not the political economy of refrigerants, and the strategy of nudging incumbents toward profitable alternatives confuses a problem of capital destruction with a problem of technical substitution.

Naomi Klein has put this point in a way that Sabel and Victor's essay does not engage: the climate crisis is the consequence of an economic order, and it will not be solved by tweaking the procedures of that order's regulators. Andreas Malm has put it more bluntly: fossil capital must be defeated, not invited onto sectoral committees. One does not have to accept the entirety of these arguments to feel the force of their objection to the essay's framing. When Sabel and Victor describe Montreal's success at threatening Russia with trade sanctions, the threat was wielded by states whose home industries had already begun the transition and stood to benefit from forcing competitors to follow. When the question is fossil energy, the equivalent leverage does not yet exist. The Saudis are not going to be sanctioned by anyone for selling oil. The "penalty default" that destabilizes the status quo has to be built, politically, before it can be wielded technocratically. That building is the work of mass movements, electoral coalitions, and confrontations with concentrated wealth — work that the experimentalist vocabulary cannot quite see.

Consider the actual sites of climate progress in the United States over the past decade. The fact that coal has collapsed in the American electricity sector is the result of three forces: cheap gas from fracking (a mixed environmental blessing), cheap renewables driven by Chinese manufacturing scale and German feed-in tariffs (a deliberate industrial policy choice), and Sierra Club's Beyond Coal campaign, which fought plant by plant in state public utility commissions to deny permits and force closures. The third of these is the one most like Sabel and Victor's vision — local, iterative, learning by doing — but it succeeded because it was animated by a confrontational politics that named coal companies as adversaries rather than partners in a sectoral committee. The Sierra Club did not invite Peabody Energy to help design the phase-out.

Sabel and Victor will respond, I think, that nothing in their model precludes confrontational politics; that "penalty defaults" can be wielded by protesters as much as by regulators; and that their model is meant to describe how change consolidates after political opening, not how political opening occurs. Fair enough. But the essay's center of gravity is in the consolidation phase, not the opening phase, and it gives almost no guidance about how the opening is to be achieved. The closing paragraphs on the Biden administration are revealing: they speak of "administrative action" and "place-based solutions" as if these were the natural extensions of the Montreal-style sectoral approach. They are not. They are the constrained substitutes available because Congress is captured and the political coalition for transformation has not been assembled. Working around political deadlock is not the same as overcoming it.

There is also a question of timeline that the essay touches on but does not really sit with. Montreal took, charitably, a decade from first treaty to substantial reductions, and longer to reach the developing world. The IMO has been working on shipping decarbonization for fifteen years and is still arguing about whether to set a binding emissions target. If sectoral experimentalism on the climate problem proceeds at this pace, across the dozens of sectors that need to be transformed, we will warm well past 2°C while the committees deliberate. The IPCC's 1.5°C report from 2018 gave us roughly a decade to halve global emissions. Experimentalist governance, as Sabel and Victor describe it, is a beautiful long-run institutional ecology. We do not have a long run.

This is where the Green New Deal vocabulary, for all its sloppiness, has the advantage of being honest about timescale. It says: we are going to spend money, on a massive scale, immediately, to build out clean infrastructure, because we have no time for incremental sectoral learning. It says: we are going to use public power to commission, deploy, and standardize, because the market is too slow and the incumbents too entrenched. It says: we are going to assemble a political majority that will defend this program against rollback, because without that majority the program will be reversed. These are claims about politics, not about governance procedures, and they are the claims that the essay's experimentalist frame cannot accommodate.

I do not want to oppose Sabel and Victor's model entirely. The work of sectoral standard-setting, peer review, and iterative adjustment is real and necessary. Once a political coalition has authorized transformation, the experimentalist machinery they describe is exactly what is needed to translate authorization into accomplishment. But that machinery does not authorize itself. It does not generate the political will that funds it, the public legitimacy that defends it, or the confrontation with incumbents that clears the field for it. Dewey understood this; his pragmatism was always in service of a more democratic politics, not a substitute for one. To borrow his metaphor: it is the citizen who knows where the shoe pinches. On climate, the shoe pinches in places — communities sacrificed to extraction, workers stranded by transition, futures foreclosed by inaction — that no sectoral committee, however expertly composed, is positioned to feel.

Diversified GPT

"While experimentalist governance provides a useful framework for institutional learning, it is insufficient for solving the climate crisis unless it is integrated with a political strategy that builds democratic power to defeat entrenched fossil fuel interests."

David Victor and Charles Sabel offer a sophisticated answer to a question that has haunted climate politics for decades: why have so many declarations produced so little decarbonization? Their answer is institutional. Global climate diplomacy has tried to secure universal agreement before discovering workable pathways. It has relied too much on top-down pledges and too little on iterative, sectoral problem solving. We should instead build experimental institutions that learn from practice, tighten standards as solutions emerge, and make obstruction increasingly costly.

There is much to admire in this account. But it leaves too little room for conflict. Climate action is not only a puzzle to be solved. It is a political struggle to be won.

The distinction matters. If climate change were mainly a problem of uncertainty, then experimentalist governance would be the natural response. Bring together regulators, firms, experts, and stakeholders; set provisional goals; try multiple approaches; monitor results; revise; scale successes. In many areas this is exactly right. No one can know in advance the best way to retrofit every building, integrate renewables into every grid, or decarbonize every industrial process. Institutions that can learn are indispensable.

But climate inaction has never been caused only by lack of knowledge. For at least three decades, the world has known enough to act far more aggressively than it has. Scientists have warned of warming. Economists have described externalities. Engineers have improved alternatives. Public health experts have documented the co-benefits of reducing fossil fuel combustion. The problem is not that governments and firms have been waiting innocently for better information. It is that powerful interests benefit from the carbon economy and have fought to preserve it.

That fight has taken many forms: lobbying against regulation, funding doubt about climate science, attacking renewable energy mandates, defending fossil fuel subsidies, shaping permitting rules, influencing trade policy, and warning workers that climate action threatens their livelihoods. In the United States, the fossil fuel industry and its allies helped turn climate change into a partisan identity marker. Elsewhere, state-owned oil, gas, and coal interests are deeply entangled with public revenue and national development strategies. These are not mere “laggards.” They are organized opponents.

Victor and Sabel know this, of course. They write about penalties, defaults, and destabilizing the status quo. Yet the tone of experimentalism can make the transition sound more consensual than it will be. Some incumbents can be induced to innovate. Others must be defeated. A politics that cannot say this plainly will not be adequate to the task.

This is where popular protest, which the lead essay treats ambivalently, becomes central. It is true that protest can complicate global bargaining. But protest has also done more than almost any diplomatic process to change the climate agenda. Youth climate strikes, Indigenous pipeline resistance, fossil fuel divestment campaigns, anti-fracking movements, environmental justice organizing, and groups willing to use civil disobedience have shifted the boundaries of the possible. They have made climate delay morally disreputable. They have forced institutions to declare emergencies, universities and pension funds to reconsider investments, banks to defend fossil lending, and politicians to support policies they once dismissed as unrealistic.

Without movements, there is no “penalty default.” Firms do not fear reputational damage in the abstract; they fear organized publics. Governments do not raise ambition because policy learning gently reveals better options; they do so because citizens demand action, courts intervene, opposition parties mobilize, and social legitimacy changes. Even the Paris Agreement’s temperature goals owe something to the pressure of vulnerable states and civil society movements insisting that 2 degrees was not enough and that 1.5 degrees mattered.

The Green New Deal should be understood in this light. Victor and Sabel are right that Green New Deal proposals often say more about vision than governance. They identify a real weakness. Any serious program must grapple with implementation: which agencies, which standards, which funds, which timelines, which accountability mechanisms? A slogan cannot build transmission lines or train workers. But the Green New Deal’s contribution is not primarily administrative. It is political. It reframes climate action from sacrifice to reconstruction, from technocratic carbon management to jobs, justice, housing, transit, health, and public investment. That reframing is not ornamental. It is how majorities are built.

Experimentalist governance needs that kind of democratic energy. Otherwise it risks becoming a conversation among regulators, experts, and firms, with citizens invited only as sources of local information. Dewey’s image of the shoe and the wearer is helpful, but democracy is not just telling the shoemaker where it pinches. It is deciding what kind of shoes society should make, who makes them, under what conditions, who can afford them, and whether some industries should stop making harmful products altogether.

The climate transition will reach deeply into daily life. It will change cars, homes, farms, diets, landscapes, electricity systems, workplaces, and public budgets. These changes cannot be legitimated only through expert review. They require democratic contestation. People will disagree about nuclear power, carbon capture, transmission siting, biofuels, mining for battery minerals, public ownership, carbon pricing, and the role of markets. Some disagreements will reflect misinformation or incumbent manipulation. Others will reflect real tradeoffs. Experimentalism can help test claims, but it cannot substitute for collective choice.

Take the example of California. The California Air Resources Board has indeed shown that regulation can push technology forward. But California’s climate politics has also revealed tensions that are not simply technical. Cap-and-trade has been criticized by environmental justice advocates who argue that market mechanisms can allow local pollution burdens to persist in disadvantaged communities. Housing policy and transportation emissions are bound up with land use, exclusionary zoning, and inequality. Wildfires and utility failures raise questions about infrastructure, public safety, and corporate accountability. California is a laboratory, but laboratories are political.

The same is true of Europe’s water governance, another example in the lead essay. Watershed management may involve expert monitoring and local adaptation, but it also implicates farmers’ livelihoods, food prices, land ownership, and rural power. If agricultural runoff is reduced, someone pays. If animal waste is regulated, some production models become less viable. Experimental institutions can make these conflicts more informed, but they do not make them disappear.

This is why climate governance should be designed not merely for learning but for countervailing power. Labor unions must be able to bargain over transition plans. Environmental justice communities must have enforceable rights, not just consultation opportunities. Indigenous peoples must have sovereignty and consent, not stakeholder status. Public agencies must have capacity to challenge corporate claims about what is feasible. Antitrust law may be needed to prevent dominant firms from shaping sectoral standards in their own interest. Transparency rules must expose lobbying, emissions data, and financial risk. Public ownership should be on the table where private utilities or infrastructure firms obstruct decarbonization.

The lead essay’s emphasis on sectoral action is useful, but sectors are not neutral spaces. A steel decarbonization forum dominated by large producers may protect incumbents against new entrants. An aviation process may focus on future fuels while avoiding demand management or frequent-flyer inequities. An oil and gas methane initiative may reduce leaks while extending the life of gas infrastructure. A shipping coalition may privilege the largest firms. Experimentalist governance must therefore be adversarial enough to ask whether a sector should shrink, not only how it should innovate.

This is especially important for fossil fuels. Much climate policy focuses on demand: cleaner cars, cleaner power, efficiency, renewables. But a serious transition also requires supply-side policy: stopping new exploration, restricting leases, ending public finance for fossil infrastructure, planning managed decline, and supporting workers and regions through the transition. Oil and gas companies may participate in carbon capture or hydrogen projects, but they cannot be allowed to define climate action as anything compatible with continued expansion. There is no experimental mystery about whether burning more fossil carbon increases warming.

The urgency of the climate crisis also requires democratic institutions capable of acting quickly. Deliberation is essential, but endless process can be weaponized. Fossil interests often demand more study, more consultation, more gradualism, and more “balance” as strategies of delay. Experimentalism must distinguish between learning and stalling. One way to do this is to set non-negotiable boundaries: emissions must fall, environmental justice must improve, workers must be protected, and new infrastructure must be consistent with net zero. Within those boundaries, experimentation can flourish.

The Biden administration, assuming the transition proceeds, will face exactly this dilemma. Rejoining Paris will be easy. Passing ambitious legislation through a polarized Congress may be hard, especially if the Senate remains closely divided. Administrative action will matter, as will state and local policy. But none of it will survive without organized constituencies. Regulations can be reversed. Standards can be litigated. Investments can be captured. Climate policy needs people who will defend it because it has improved their lives and because they see themselves in it.

That is the deepest weakness of climate diplomacy as we have known it: not only that it has been too global or too rigid, but that it has often been too distant from democratic life. Experimentalist governance can help close that distance if it is built around participation and accountability. But if it becomes another language of expert management, it will fail politically even when it succeeds technically.

The climate movement’s task is therefore twofold. It must demand institutions that learn, because rigid plans will break against reality. And it must build power, because learning alone will not overcome entrenched interests. The world needs standards, demonstration projects, sectoral clubs, finance, and review. It also needs strikes, elections, litigation, organizing, public ownership campaigns, and moral confrontation.

Victor and Sabel have given us an important institutional vocabulary. But the climate will not be fixed by governance design alone. It will be fixed, if at all, by democratic power using institutions to transform the economy faster than incumbents can stop it.

Vanilla DeepSeek

"While experimentalist governance offers a pragmatic alternative to failed top-down climate diplomacy, it underestimates the political power of the fossil fuel industry and the necessity of massive public investment and democratic mobilization."

The essay by Victor and Sabel offers a bracing diagnosis of three decades of diplomatic failure on climate change and advances a tempting alternative—one rooted in a pragmatic, piecemeal experimentalism that learns from the unexpected success of the Montreal Protocol. Their core insight is that the search for a binding global treaty from the start has been a recipe for paralysis, and that building consensus through on-the-ground problem solving, sector by sector, is more likely to disrupt the status quo. Yet while their critique of the orthodox approach is largely persuasive, the solution they champion underestimates the sheer political and economic power of entrenched fossil fuel interests and glosses over the gulf between the ozone crisis and the far deeper transformation that decarbonization demands. Experimentalist governance, for all its appeal, risks becoming a technocratic retreat from the kind of muscular public action and global solidarity that climate change still requires.

The Montreal Protocol is an irresistible model, but its very success rests on features that climate change does not share. The ozone crisis implicated a handful of industrial chemicals for which substitutes could be developed at manageable cost and, crucially, produced at a profit by the same firms that made the offending substances. DuPont broke ranks because it recognized that a phase-out could yield commercial advantage, and because the reputational liability of destroying the ozone layer threatened its broader chemical business. In the climate arena, by contrast, the core business of the world’s most profitable and politically potent corporations—fossil fuel extraction, refining, and distribution—is incompatible with decarbonization. There is no equivalent of DuPont: a major oil company has no pathway to profitability by simply swapping crude oil for a benign substitute while retaining its corporate identity. The penalty default that Victor and Sabel describe requires that once one innovator moves, laggards face market exclusion. But in climate, the laggards are not niche chemical manufacturers; they are nation-states whose entire rentier economies depend on oil and gas revenues, and whose refusal to cooperate cannot be disciplined by trade sanctions without fracturing the global trading system on which experimentalist governance itself depends.

This brings us to a deeper tension in the essay’s argument. Victor and Sabel note the paralysis of the World Trade Organization and the fraying of global order. Yet the penalty defaults that make the Montreal model work rely on powerful states—mainly the United States—threatening trade sanctions against violators. In a world of rising nationalism, great-power rivalry, and a hollowed-out WTO, the credibility of such threats is vanishingly low. The United States may threaten China or India with trade penalties for climate obstruction, but in practice such threats would ignite retaliatory spirals that no administration, least of all one facing domestic polarization, is likely to sustain. The experimentalist cycle assumes that once local innovations succeed, they can be codified as standards and enforced through market access, but without a hegemonic enforcer willing to punish laggards, the penalty default collapses into a voluntary scheme that the least ambitious can ignore. The very geopolitical fragmentation the authors lament thus erodes the foundations of their proposed solution.

Equally troubling is the democratic deficit that experimentalist governance can conceal. The essay invokes John Dewey’s vision of collaboration between expert and citizen, but the actual institutions it celebrates—Montreal’s sectoral committees, California’s Air Resources Board, the EU’s Water Framework Directive—are overwhelmingly technocratic bodies in which corporate and regulatory elites negotiate standards, often with minimal public participation. The Maersk example in the shipping sector is instructive: the largest container firm coordinates demonstration programs and helps write the rules inside the International Maritime Organization. That may accelerate innovation, but it also cements the influence of incumbent players who can steer standards to their own advantage, protecting themselves from nimbler disruptors while doing just enough to appear climate-friendly. Without robust mechanisms of democratic accountability, experimentalist governance can degenerate into the kind of elite consensus that populist movements rightly distrust—and that has already inflamed climate politics in many countries. The Green New Deal is mentioned as a vision strong on mandates but silent on governance, yet the opposite danger is equally acute: an elaborate governance architecture that lacks the democratic mandate for the trillions in public investment and the industrial transformation that deep decarbonization requires.

Indeed, the essay’s silence on the scale of public finance and the politics of a just transition is a conspicuous gap. The Multilateral Fund that smoothed developing-country participation in the Montreal Protocol was tiny relative to the costs of climate mitigation and adaptation. The funding required to phase out coal in India, to build renewable grids across Africa, or to compensate fossil fuel workers in West Virginia and Alberta is orders of magnitude larger. Victor and Sabel hint that a Biden administration might dramatically increase resources, but they offer no governance framework for mobilizing that capital or for ensuring that it reaches the communities that fear—with good reason—that decarbonization means economic devastation. Experimentalist governance excels at iterative learning, but it does not, by itself, generate the massive fiscal commitments that will determine whether climate action is perceived as fair or as yet another elite imposition. Without a narrative of shared sacrifice and shared benefit, and without the institutional muscle to redistribute costs and gains, the thin consensus that gets experimentalism started is likely to shatter when the losers begin to organize.

Finally, the essay’s dismissal of the Paris Agreement as little more than a norm-setting forum underestimates the potential of that framework to evolve. Paris’s reliance on nationally determined contributions and periodic review is indeed weak, but it also creates a transparency mechanism and a five-year ratchet cycle that, combined with growing civil society pressure and the logic of peer accountability, can tighten over time. The “rulebook” that Victor and Sabel deride as obstructive is precisely the kind of information infrastructure that experimentalist governance needs to compare efforts and identify laggards. Rather than jettisoning Paris in favor of a fragmented sectoral approach, the wiser course would be to embed experimentalist initiatives within the Paris framework—allowing sectoral clubs and transnational networks to generate credible pathways, but anchoring them in a universal process that preserves the norm of common but differentiated responsibility and maintains political pressure on all nations. The symbolic power of global goals, however unenforceable in the short term, creates the penalty default writ large: it empowers protesters, shareholders, and litigants to demand that firms and governments act in line with declared ambitions, and it provides a baseline against which experimentalist achievements can be measured.

Victor and Sabel are right that the top-down, consensus-or-bust model of climate diplomacy has failed and that learning from local and sectoral successes is indispensable. But the climate crisis is not merely a coordination problem under uncertainty; it is a confrontation with the most powerful industrial complex in history. Overcoming it demands not only the ingenuity of experimentalist governance but also the raw political power to break the stranglehold of incumbents and the fiscal capacity to make the transition just. The Montreal Protocol worked because the affected industries could be redirected with relatively modest pressure and because rich countries paid the tab. Climate change will require a far more formidable combination of public investment, democratic mobilization, and global burden-sharing. For all the elegance of their framework, Victor and Sabel offer only half the story, and the missing half is the one that will determine whether their quiet revolution gets started at all.

"The experimentalist governance model proposed by Victor and Sabel is a technocratic management tool that fails to address the fundamental political challenge of defeating the powerful fossil fuel interests that profit from the status quo."

David Victor and Charles Sabel have given us a diagnosis that is as bracing as it is incomplete. They are right to catalogue the failures of the top-down, consensus-bound model of global climate diplomacy that gave us the Kyoto Protocol, and they are right to point to the Montreal Protocol as an example of something that worked rather better. But they are wrong about why Montreal worked and what that means for how we should fight climate change now. Their call to embrace “experimentalist governance” as the alternative to Paris is a category error. It mistakes a technocratic management tool for a political strategy. The reason climate action has been so hard to sustain is not that we lack the right institutional design for learning; it is that we lack a sufficiently powerful political coalition to defeat the interests that profit from the status quo. The experimentalist model, for all its sophistication, dodges the central question of power.

Victor and Sabel argue that climate diplomacy failed because it demanded binding consensus from the start, assuming that solutions had to be global and uniform. Their alternative, drawn from the Montreal Protocol, involves starting with a thin consensus among a motivated group, devolving problem-solving to front-line actors in specific sectors, and then using peer review and penalty defaults to ratchet up ambition and spread innovation. This is an elegant description of how certain regulatory regimes have managed technological transitions. But it fundamentally misreads the political economy of the problem. The ozone crisis was, in comparative terms, a policy pleasure cruise. The chemicals in question—chlorofluorocarbons—were produced by a tiny handful of firms in a concentrated industry, the most important of which, DuPont, had already broken ranks and backed regulation because it saw a profitable path forward in alternatives. The costs of the transition were modest and could be offset by a well-managed Multilateral Fund. There was no massive, globally distributed, rent-seeking industry whose entire business model depended on blocking the transition.

Climate change is different in kind, not just degree. Fossil fuels are not an optional industrial input used in a few specific products; they are the lifeblood of the global economy, generating trillions of dollars in revenue and sustaining the political power of some of the world’s most influential corporations and petrostates. An effective response to climate change must, by necessity, impose massive losses on these interests—not just marginally inconvenience them with new compliance costs, but render vast reserves of coal, oil, and gas economically stranded. The political fight is not about fine-tuning innovation under conditions of uncertainty; it is a bare-knuckle battle over who will bear those trillions in losses and who will capture the trillions in gains from building the new energy economy. The Montreal Protocol was not a successful case of bypassing entrenched power; it was a case where the most powerful industrial actor switched sides early, driven by a unique combination of public relations liability and profit incentive in the chemicals sector. In the oil and gas sector, no equivalent actor has defected to lead the charge. The Exxons and Saudi Aramcos of the world remain firmly committed to a business-as-usual trajectory, and they have spent decades funding disinformation, lobbying against policy, and capturing political parties to ensure that the penalty defaults Victor and Sabel describe are never credibly applied.

This matters because the entire experimentalist enterprise depends on the existence of a forward-leaning group that is willing and able to impose costs on laggards. Where, in the climate case, is that credible enforcement coalition? Victor and Sabel gesture at trade sanctions and the legitimacy of the Paris Agreement as a source of authority for protesters and governments to “punish” foot-draggers. But the Paris Agreement is not a framework that authorizes enforcement; its nationally determined contributions are, by design, voluntary. The European Union’s recent talk of a carbon border adjustment mechanism is a tentative step in the direction of penalty defaults, but it has met ferocious resistance from trading partners who see it as green protectionism, and its legality under World Trade Organization rules is highly uncertain. The United States, far from being a reliable leader ready to penalize the villainous, has oscillated wildly on climate policy with each change of administration. The idea that we can piece together a global enforcement regime from a patchwork of trade measures, NGO shaming campaigns, and sectoral standards without a prior political settlement that breaks the power of the fossil fuel lobby is wishful thinking dressed up as institutional design.

Furthermore, the focus on sectoral experimentation, while sensible, addresses only the supply side of the innovation puzzle and neglects the raw politics of demand. It matters enormously that fossil fuels are, in many applications, still cheaper than their cleaner competitors, and their price does not reflect their social cost. The single most powerful policy for driving decarbonisation across every sector—from shipping to steel to power grids—is a robust, economy-wide price on carbon. A carbon price creates exactly the penalty default Victor and Sabel celebrate: it makes the status quo of high emissions permanently risky and unprofitable, while rewarding every act of innovation, whether in a ship engine or a cement kiln, with an immediate competitive advantage. Yet the global history of carbon pricing is a testament to the sheer force of political obstruction by fossil fuel interests. Australia’s carbon tax was repealed after a furious industry campaign. France’s attempt to raise fuel taxes triggered the Yellow Vest uprising. In the United States, cap-and-trade died in the Senate in 2010, and even the more modest Biden climate plan, released in 2020, carefully avoids the language of taxing carbon, focusing instead on subsidies and clean energy standards.

The Montreal Protocol’s model of industry committees evaluating alternative chemicals worked not just because the committees were smart, but because the political path had been cleared by DuPont’s defection and by a concentrated cost structure. In climate, the technology assessments are already well underway; the committees exist in the form of the International Energy Agency, the International Renewable Energy Agency, and countless industry consortia. The logjam is not a lack of knowledge about what is technically feasible. The logjam is that the political systems of major emitters are incapable of imposing a binding, escalating cost on carbon that would force the entire global economy to reorganize itself around low-carbon technologies. Without that political precondition, “experimentalist governance” risks becoming a justification for permanent delay, an endless process of pilot projects and sectoral roundtables while the Keeling Curve keeps climbing.

The real lesson of the climate deadlock is that we need to build a political movement capable of breaking the power of the fossil fuel industry, not just a more clever committee structure. That means organizing labor unions, environmental justice groups, and community organizations into a durable coalition that can demand and defend a managed transition. It means confronting, not circumventing, the fact that decarbonization will require massive redistribution—from polluters to workers, from rich countries to poor ones—on a scale that makes the Multilateral Fund look like a rounding error in a national budget. Victor and Sabel are right that we must learn from our successes. But the success we should learn from is not the technocratic smoothness of the ozone negotiations. It is the history of every major social transformation—the abolition of slavery, the civil rights movement, the building of the welfare state—where entrenched power was not cleverly reorganized from above but defeated from below. The climate fight is not a design problem awaiting its principles. It is a contest of power, and we are losing it not for lack of pilots, but for lack of politics.

"While experimentalist governance offers a useful framework for decentralized problem-solving, it is insufficient for the climate crisis because it prioritizes technical institutional design over the necessary direct challenge to political power and social transformation."

Reading Victor and Sabel's essay, I found myself nodding in agreement with the diagnosis but growing uneasy as they move toward prescription. The experimentalist governance framework they describe has genuine merit, but I worry it obscures as much as it illuminates about the nature of the climate challenge.

The core claim—that top-down global agreements have failed and that solutions must be built from partial successes at multiple scales—is well-supported by the evidence. The Paris Agreement's architecture of voluntary NDCs without enforcement mechanisms is indeed a diplomatic fig leaf. The Kyoto Protocol's binding targets for developed countries while excluding the developing world was a recipe for failure. The emissions trajectory since 1990—rising by two-thirds, with temporary COVID-driven drops being the only respite—confirms that current approaches are not working.

But the alternative Victor and Sabel propose raises questions they do not adequately address. Experimentalist governance, as they describe it, is neither fully hierarchical nor fully decentralized. It combines "thin consensus" on goals with devolved problem-solving, peer review, and penalty defaults for laggards. This is an attractive model that sounds good in theory. But how does it actually function in practice, particularly for climate change?

Montreal succeeded, but Montreal was a much simpler problem. The science was clear: CFCs were destroying the ozone layer. The sources were identifiable: a limited set of chemicals used in specific industrial applications. The alternatives were technically feasible: HFCs and other compounds were available or quickly developed. And the industry structure was manageable: a handful of major producers could be brought into alignment.

Climate change shares none of these characteristics. The science is complex and contested in the public mind even where expert consensus is clear. The sources are ubiquitous: virtually every human economic activity emits greenhouse gases. The alternatives are often not yet competitive without subsidy. And the industry structure is fragmented across millions of actors, from fossil fuel giants to individual car owners. Experimentalism works well when you can organize around sectors and workgroups of experts. It works less well when the problem permeates every aspect of modern life.

More fundamentally, I question the essay's account of how change happens. Victor and Sabel suggest that once innovative actors demonstrate alternatives, penalty defaults make obstruction the riskiest strategy. The historical record is less benign. The fossil fuel industry has spent decades and billions of dollars demonstrating that this logic does not automatically prevail. Companies have known for decades that their products cause climate change; they have invested in delay, denial, and obstruction regardless of the economic logic of transition. The lesson is not that institutional design can overcome political power but that political power must be challenged directly.

The essay also elides important questions about who benefits from experimentalist governance and who bears its costs. Victor and Sabel are correct that the Montreal framework incorporated developing countries through the Multilateral Fund. But they do not ask whether the specific terms of incorporation served developing country interests. The Montreal Fund paid for technology transfer and administrative capacity, but it did not challenge the underlying economic structures that keep developing countries dependent on technologies developed elsewhere. Experimentalist climate governance could have similar limitations: facilitating technically successful transitions while leaving in place the economic arrangements that concentrate benefits in wealthy nations and among wealthy actors within all nations.

The Green New Deal, which the essay mentions only to dismiss as achieving "tentative success in only a small fraction of the global economy," deserves more serious treatment. The Green New Deal is not primarily a governance blueprint but a political project—one that explicitly links climate action to economic justice, explicitly acknowledges that decarbonization must benefit working people, and explicitly refuses to separate environmental from social concerns. Whatever its specific policy proposals, its significance lies in framing climate change as a project of social transformation rather than technical adjustment.

Victor and Sabel might respond that social transformation requires the kind of institutional innovation they propose—that experimentalism provides the governance infrastructure for a more ambitious political agenda. Perhaps. But their essay reads as if governance innovation can substitute for political mobilization rather than complement it. The Montreal Protocol did not emerge from clever institutional design; it emerged from sustained public pressure, scientific advocacy, and a geopolitical context that made global cooperation possible. The governance structures followed the political breakthrough; they did not cause it.

This suggests that the most important work is not designing better institutions but building the political conditions for ambitious climate action. This work includes supporting climate movements, electing officials committed to action, and creating accountability structures that make obstruction costly. Experimentalist governance can contribute to this project—by demonstrating what is possible, by providing frameworks for implementing demonstrated solutions, and by building constituencies for further action. But it cannot substitute for the harder, slower work of political transformation.

I want to conclude with a different worry. Victor and Sabel write as if climate change were primarily a governance problem. Perhaps. But it may also be a civilizational challenge—one that requires not just new institutions but new ways of understanding humanity's relationship to the natural world. The experimentalist framework assumes that the goal is to maintain current levels of economic activity with lower emissions. This is a reasonable assumption for policy purposes, but it may not capture what climate action ultimately requires. A truly transformed relationship to the environment might involve not just green technologies but reduced consumption, different values, and a willingness to accept limits. Victor and Sabel's framework is admirably pragmatic, but pragmatism can become its own limitation when the problem requires not just better means but different ends.

"While experimentalist governance offers a sophisticated framework for climate action, it is insufficient because it mistakes institutional design for political strategy and fails to address the underlying power dynamics and massive funding requirements necessary for deep decarbonization."

Victor and Sabel have written an impressive essay, but I fear it falls into a common intellectual trap: mistaking governance design for political strategy. Their experimentalist framework is sophisticated and empirically grounded, but it presupposes conditions that do not currently exist and cannot be wished into being by clever institutional architecture.

The core problem is that experimentalism works—when it works—because powerful actors have incentives to participate. Montreal succeeded in significant part because DuPont saw profit in alternatives to CFCs. The pharmaceutical industry cooperated on meter-dose inhalers because substitutes were commercially viable. These were not expressions of environmental virtue; they were responses to economic opportunity. Victor and Sabel recognize this but seem to believe that similar conditions can be created through institutional design. The penalty default, they argue, will make laggards face market exclusion once leaders demonstrate alternatives. But this mechanism requires that alternatives actually become available and economically competitive—a process that may take decades and require public investment at scales no experimentalist framework can generate.

Consider what their approach would require for steel, a sector they identify as critical. The primary route to green steel—hydrogen direct reduction—remains more expensive than conventional blast furnace production. No amount of institutional clever- ness will make hydrogen-based steel cost-competitive without massive public subsidy for research, demonstration, and deployment. The same holds for cement, aviation, shipping, and most industrial processes. Victor and Sabel are right that sectoral approaches can accelerate learning and identify feasible pathways, but they understate how much fundamental research and development remains necessary before experimentalist governance can operate as they envision.

The essay also underestimates the political power of fossil fuel interests. Victor and Sabel correctly document how "well-organized high-emitting industries" have obstructed climate policy for decades. But their governance recommendations do not address the underlying political economy. Experimentalism assumes that actors will engage constructively once institutional incentives align. History suggests otherwise. The same forces that captured climate diplomacy will seek to capture experimentalist institutions. Sectoral committees and assessment panels can be staffed with industryfriendly experts, populated with favorable case studies, and structured to favor gradualism over ambition. Without explicit attention to preventing regulatory capture, experimentalist governance could become a more technocratic version of the same dysfunction.

The essay's treatment of the Global South compounds this problem. Victor and Sabel note that Montreal's Multilateral Fund was "probably the best managed funding mechanism in the history of international environmental governance." They do not ask whether it was sufficient. For a relatively contained problem—eliminating specific chemicals used by specific industries—this level of support was adequate. Climate change is categorically different. Deep decarbonization in developing countries requires not just technology transfer but comprehensive restructuring of energy systems, industrial processes, and transportation infrastructure. The investment needs are measured in trillions, not billions. Unless wealthy nations commit to providing financing at scales comparable to these needs, experimentalist governance in developing countries will remain aspirational.

I raise these objections not to defend the status quo of failed global diplomacy but to argue that experimentalism, as currently formulated, is necessary but not sufficient. What is missing is a theory of political change—how to build constituencies capable of demanding ambitious action and sustaining it over the decades required.

Here the essay is oddly silent. Victor and Sabel write as if institutional design determines political possibility rather than the reverse. But history suggests that governance innovations follow political breakthroughs, not precede them. The environmental regulations of the 1970s emerged from social movements that built pressure for change. The Clean Air Act succeeded not because it embodied clever institutional design but because activists created political space for ambitious regulation. If the Green New Deal has any merit, it is as a political project for building constituencies—regardless of whether its specific provisions become law.

This suggests a different priority for climate advocates. Rather than focusing primarily on governance architecture, we should ask how to build political power. This means supporting candidates who will pursue aggressive climate action, funding organizations that can sustain pressure on recalcitrant politicians, and building alliances across the environmental, labor, and justice movements. It means making the economic case for decarbonization in terms that resonate with working-class voters who have legitimate concerns about jobs and costs. And it means accepting that some battles will be lost before they are won—because political change requires learning from failure as much as success.

Victor and Sabel might respond that experimentalism is precisely this kind of learning process—that by trying many approaches and scaling what works, we build political knowledge alongside technical knowledge. There is something to this. The essay's account of how Montreal's sectoral committees identified and spread successful alternatives suggests a model for political learning as well. But the parallel is incomplete. Political experimentation cannot be monitored through peer review in the same way as technical demonstrations. When experiments in economic policy or regulatory approach fail, the costs fall on real people whose suffering cannot be waved away as "necessary learning."

I do not want to be unfair to an essay that has much to offer. The Montreal analysis is the best I have read. The distinction between globalized and place-based sectors genuinely illuminates the different governance challenges they pose. And the call to "show that we can learn from our unconventional successes" is timely and important. The Paris Agreement has consumed enormous diplomatic energy for modest results; it makes sense to look elsewhere.

But the elsewhere we look should include not just governance innovation but political organizing. Experimentalism can contribute to the latter—it provides a framework for trying different political strategies and learning from results. What it cannot do is substitute for the harder, slower, more uncertain work of building movements capable of demanding and sustaining ambitious climate action. Victor and Sabel have given us useful tools for that project. They have not given us a theory of how to use them.
