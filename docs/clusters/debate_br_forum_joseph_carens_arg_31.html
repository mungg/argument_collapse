Diversified Claude

"The moral case for amnesty should be grounded in the principle of proportionality—weighing the harm of removal against the wrong of unauthorized entry—rather than the problematic and conceptually wobbly doctrine of 'social membership.'"

Carens has produced an essay that is impossible to read without sympathy, and his sympathies are mine. But I want to put pressure on the philosophical core of his argument — the doctrine of "social membership" — because I think it does less work than he supposes and more work than is healthy.

The membership doctrine holds, in Carens' formulation, that people become members of a society by living in it, regardless of legal status, and that their membership generates moral claims against the political community that grow stronger with time. This is intuitive. It is also problematic in several ways that the essay does not address.

First, the doctrine is curiously asymmetric. It accumulates claims against the receiving society but does not subtract claims from the sending society. Miguel Sanchez has become, on Carens' view, a member of American society after nine years. Has he ceased to be a member of Mexican society? Presumably not entirely; he sends money home, speaks to his family, perhaps intends to return someday. So what is membership, exactly? It is not exclusive, like citizenship. It is not officially recognized, like residency. It is some kind of moral fact, generated by the activities of daily life, that exists alongside whatever other memberships the person holds. But if membership can be plural and informal in this way, why does it have the power to override the official, exclusive, formal membership rules of the receiving society? Carens does not say.

Second, the doctrine has trouble distinguishing between the migrant and the long-term visitor. Suppose a Japanese businessman is posted to New York for fifteen years on a renewable work visa. He raises his children in American schools, builds friendships, becomes part of the neighborhood. At year fifteen, his company recalls him to Tokyo and his visa expires. Has he become a "member of American society" in Carens' sense? He has done all the things that Miguel Sanchez has done. Is he being wronged by being required to leave? The intuition is unclear. Most people would say no, he understood the terms of his stay, he can leave with dignity. But then membership cannot be doing all the work Carens wants it to do, because the same facts of daily life that constitute membership in the irregular migrant's case fail to constitute it in the visa-holder's case. The difference, on closer inspection, is not membership but the absence of an alternative — Miguel Sanchez has no realistic option to return to Mexico without grievous loss, while the visa-holder does. If this is so, then what is doing the moral work is not membership but hardship, and the membership vocabulary is a misleading way of describing it.

Third, the doctrine cannot accommodate the fact that some people who have lived in a place for a long time do not become members of it in the usual sense. Consider an irregular migrant who lives in an ethnic enclave, works for co-ethnic employers, never learns the local language, never participates in civic life, never forms relationships outside her immediate community. By the standard markers, she is not a member of the wider society. Does she nevertheless have the same claim as Miguel Sanchez? On Carens' "time-only" criterion, yes. But this suggests that what generates the claim is not membership but residence, and the conceptual framework should be adjusted accordingly.

I do not raise these objections to defeat the conclusion. I raise them because the conclusion deserves better grounding than the membership doctrine provides. What is really doing the moral work in Carens' essay, I think, is something simpler: a recognition that human beings establish lives in places, that displacement from those lives is a serious harm, and that the harm is grossly disproportionate to the wrong of unauthorized entry. This argument can be made without invoking the technical concept of "membership." It is, at its heart, a proportionality argument.

If we ground the case for amnesty in proportionality rather than membership, several things become clearer. First, the question of "how much time?" stops being about when membership crystallizes and becomes a question about when the harm of removal becomes disproportionate to the wrong of unauthorized entry. This is, I think, an easier question to answer. The harm of removal is greatest when the person has built a life that cannot be reconstituted elsewhere — a family, a livelihood, deep social ties. The wrong of unauthorized entry is essentially fixed at the moment of entry and does not grow over time. So the calculus of proportionality tilts toward amnesty as time passes, just as Carens describes, but for clearer reasons.

Second, the proportionality framing helps us see why some kinds of cases generate especially strong claims. The migrant who arrived as a young child has built a life that could not even in principle be reconstituted in the country of origin, because the country of origin is for her an unfamiliar place. The migrant who has married a citizen has built ties that cannot be transferred. These are not facts about a deeper kind of membership; they are facts about the magnitude of the harm of removal.

Third, proportionality clarifies why the prospective signaling worry is overdrawn. Carens responds to the "magnet" objection by saying that migrants are short-term thinkers. The proportionality framing offers a better response: even if migrants are long-term thinkers, the proportionality calculus depends on the actual life they build, not on the prospect of legalization. A migrant who comes intending to legalize will still have to build a life in order to claim that the harm of removal is disproportionate; and if she does build such a life, she has the same claim that Miguel Sanchez has. There is no double-counting and no perverse incentive.

So I think Carens' conclusion is right but his framework is wobbly. The "social membership" vocabulary suggests a kind of political-philosophical anthropology — that human beings are essentially members, that membership is morally fundamental — that is more than the argument needs and that opens it to objections about plural memberships, ethnic enclaves, and the like. A simpler proportionality framework gets us where we need to go with less metaphysical baggage. Strip away the membership talk and the case for amnesty does not weaken. It clarifies.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'social membership' doctrine is logically inconsistent because it fails to explain how informal, non-exclusive social ties can override formal, exclusive legal citizenship rules.
  2. 02The membership framework fails to distinguish between irregular migrants and long-term visa holders, suggesting that the true moral factor is the hardship of displacement rather than social integration.
  3. 03The membership doctrine cannot account for migrants who live in isolated enclaves without participating in wider society, whereas a residence-based proportionality argument covers all long-term inhabitants.
  4. 04A proportionality framework more clearly explains why the passage of time strengthens a migrant's claim, as the harm of uprooting a settled life eventually outweighs the static offense of illegal entry.
  5. 05Focusing on the magnitude of harm caused by removal provides a more robust defense against the 'magnet' objection than the psychological claim that migrants are short-term thinkers.
