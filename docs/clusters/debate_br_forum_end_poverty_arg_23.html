**Yes, and: Sharpening Toyama's Thesis**

Toyama has it right. I'll add three observations that sharpen the picture and one practical recommendation.

The first observation: the magnifier thesis explains why so much development practice that doesn't involve information technology also fails. Microcredit was supposed to magnify entrepreneurial capacity among the poor and end poverty; rigorous evaluations now show modest effects at best. Charter schools were supposed to magnify educational outcomes; effects are mixed and depend overwhelmingly on the quality of the teachers and administrators in each school. Smallholder farmer cooperatives were supposed to magnify bargaining power; many collapse without strong organizational leadership. The pattern isn't unique to ICT4D. The pattern is that interventions that work in well-functioning institutional contexts often fail in dysfunctional ones, and the intervention itself rarely fixes the dysfunction. Toyama's insight applies broadly to development, not just to its technological subfield.

This generalization has uncomfortable implications. If most development interventions face the same magnifier problem, then the field's overall track record—decades of effort, trillions in spending, persistent poverty—starts to make depressing sense. Development is hard not because we haven't found the right intervention, but because human and institutional capacity is the real constraint, and capacity is slow and expensive to build. There are no shortcuts. The interventions that work most reliably are the ones that build capacity directly: cash transfers (which let recipients build their own capacity), deworming (which removes a chronic drag on capacity development), primary education (which builds capacity from scratch). Notice that none of these involve fancy technology.

The second observation: Toyama is too gentle on the incentive structures that produce ICT4D failure. He locates the problem in misguided enthusiasm—technologists who don't understand context, development workers who reach for shiny gadgets. But the deeper issue is that ICT4D is a market, and the customers of that market are not poor people but donors, governments, and reputation-seeking corporations. Those customers buy what ICT4D providers sell: novel, photogenic, scalable-sounding interventions. The poor people who are nominally the beneficiaries are not the customers; they have no voice in product design or evaluation.

In any market where the buyer is not the user, you get systematic mismatches between what's offered and what's actually useful. ICT4D is no exception. The Gates Foundation buys mobile health pilots; consultants supply them; villagers receive them; outcomes are weakly measured and rarely follow up; the cycle repeats. The "myth of scale" Toyama identifies is not a cognitive error; it is a market response. Donors prefer projects that promise scale because scale lets them claim impact without doing the slow work of capacity-building. Suppliers supply what donors demand. The system is functioning as designed; the problem is the design.

Reforming ICT4D therefore requires reforming the buyer-supplier-user relationship. This means more rigorous evaluation that closes the feedback loop between user outcomes and supplier rewards (J-PAL-style randomized trials are a step in this direction). It means giving intended beneficiaries more voice in project design and assessment, perhaps through cash-based or voucher-based models where users select the services they want. It means donors imposing humility on themselves about scalability and accepting that valuable interventions are often non-scalable.

The third observation: Toyama's essay implicitly assumes that "ending poverty" is the right framing for what technology should do. I'd push back on the framing. Most technologies in human history have not "ended" any social problem; they have changed the texture of life, opened some possibilities, closed others, redistributed welfare in complicated ways. We don't ask whether the bicycle or the sewing machine "ended poverty"; we ask whether they made certain lives better at the margin, and the answer is usually yes. Mobile phones are doing that in many poor communities right now. They are not ending poverty. They are making it marginally easier to find work, stay in touch with family, send money home, get a weather forecast. That's a real contribution. It's also not transformative in the sense the techno-utopians promise.

The trap of the "end poverty" framing is that it sets up a binary in which either technology delivers transformative change (justifying massive investment) or it doesn't (justifying skepticism). The reality is in between. Most technologies in poor communities will produce modest, contextual benefits that are real but unromantic. We should fund them on a modest, contextual basis. We should stop expecting them to be silver bullets.

Now the practical recommendation. If Toyama is right that the binding constraint on ICT4D effectiveness is human and institutional capacity, then the field should reorganize itself around capacity-building, not technology deployment. Concretely, this means:

ICT4D programs should be structured as partnerships with strong local organizations that already do "real development" work. The technology should support those organizations' missions, not replace them. Digital Green is Toyama's example; there should be a hundred more like it.

Funders should require, before approving any technology deployment, evidence of the local organizational capacity to absorb it. If that capacity doesn't exist, the project should be redirected to building it first.

Evaluation should focus on whether the technology actually changed outcomes that matter to users, not on whether it was successfully deployed at scale. Most ICT4D evaluation currently measures the wrong things.

Designers should accept that the most valuable technologies for poor communities are usually the boring ones—voice, SMS, video—not the cutting-edge ones. Glamour is the enemy of effectiveness.

Toyama's essay should be required reading for everyone funding or implementing ICT4D programs. It won't end the cycle of hype and disappointment—the market incentives are too strong—but it will at least give thoughtful practitioners a vocabulary for pushing back. That's worth something.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'magnifier' thesis applies to all development interventions, explaining why programs like microcredit or charter schools fail when they lack strong institutional foundations.
  2. 02ICT4D failures are not just cognitive errors but the result of a market where donors and governments prioritize photogenic, scalable projects over the slow work of building local capacity.
  3. 03The goal of technology should be reframed from 'ending poverty' to providing modest, marginal improvements to the quality of life for the poor.
  4. 04ICT4D programs should be reorganized to prioritize partnerships with established local organizations and focus on 'boring' but effective technologies like SMS and voice.
  5. 05Reforming the field requires shifting power to beneficiaries through cash-based models and more rigorous, user-centered outcome evaluations.
