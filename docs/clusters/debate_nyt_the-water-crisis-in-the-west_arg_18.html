**Federalism, Not Federal Fixes**

The temptation in any crisis is to call for a comprehensive national plan. Resist it. Western water has been shaped by a century of federal intervention — the Bureau of Reclamation's dams, the Army Corps' levees, the heavily subsidized Central Valley Project, the federally negotiated Colorado River Compact — and many of the dysfunctions we now face are downstream consequences of those interventions. The federal hand has been heavy, and not always wise.

What the West actually needs is sharper, more accountable governance at the state and local level, supported by clearer rules at the interstate level. Consider how different the Western states already are. Arizona, after the disasters of unregulated pumping in the mid-20th century, passed the 1980 Groundwater Management Act and now requires developers in urban areas to demonstrate a 100-year assured water supply. Colorado has a sophisticated water court system that adjudicates rights case by case. New Mexico has active acequia traditions that distribute water communally through centuries-old institutions. California, by contrast, has barely regulated groundwater at all, and its surface-water rights system is so opaque that the state itself doesn't fully know who is entitled to what.

The right response is not to overwrite this variety with a national template. It is to push each state to fix its own glaring failures. California must regulate groundwater — full stop. Texas must address the "rule of capture" that lets landowners pump without limit while their neighbors' wells go dry. Nevada must reckon with the fact that Las Vegas, however impressive its conservation gains, sits in one of the most water-stressed basins in the country and cannot grow indefinitely.

Interstate compacts, meanwhile, are properly the realm of negotiation, not federal fiat. The 1922 Colorado River Compact divided water based on flows that we now know were anomalously high. The seven basin states must renegotiate, with federal facilitation, and they have shown some ability to do so — the 2007 Interim Guidelines for shortage sharing were a useful start. Mexico's inclusion through Minute 319 last year, allowing pulse flows to restore the delta, is another encouraging step. These hard-won negotiated agreements work better than anything Washington could impose.

There is a legitimate federal role: funding research on drought-resistant crops and efficient irrigation, supporting tribal water-rights settlements that have languished for decades, and using its leverage over reclamation contracts to encourage reform. But the day-to-day work of allocation belongs to those who live with the consequences.

The West is too varied — coastal California and high-desert Wyoming face genuinely different problems — for one-size-fits-all solutions. Subsidiarity, applied seriously, would force each state to confront its own dysfunctions rather than waiting for a federal rescue that will not, and should not, come.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Historical federal interventions in Western water management have often created the very dysfunctions and inefficiencies the region faces today.
  2. 02The diverse legal and cultural water management traditions across Western states require localized solutions rather than a national template.
  3. 03States must take individual responsibility for fixing their specific regulatory failures, such as California's lack of groundwater oversight or Texas's 'rule of capture.'
  4. 04Interstate water sharing is best managed through negotiated compacts between the affected states rather than through federal fiat.
  5. 05The federal government should limit its role to supporting research, settling tribal water rights, and providing financial leverage for reform rather than managing day-to-day allocation.
