"While Callard correctly identifies that the historical fact of wrongdoing never changes, her defense of grudges and revenge fails because it conflates the persistence of a logical fact with the rationality of a permanent emotional response."

Callard's core move — the Argument for Grudges and the Argument for Revenge — deserves to be taken seriously, and I will do so. But I want to argue that her case, while compelling in isolation, rests on a premisedistinction that her own essay eventually undermines.

The Argument for Grudges holds that once you have a reason to be angry, you have a reason to be angry forever, because apologies and restitution cannot alter the fact that wrongdoing occurred. Callard is right about this. If I steal from you and then return what I took, the return does not un-steal. The event happened, and it happened wrongly, and no subsequent action can change that temporal fact. So your reason to be angry — that I did something wrong — persists in some logical sense. QED.

But here is the problem: Callard's argument conflates the persistence of a logical fact with the rationality of a persistent emotional response. Just because the proposition "You wronged me" remains true forever does not mean that the psychological state of being angry at that fact is rationally sustained forever. Rationality, even in its most permissive formulations, is not purely logical. It is also concerned with the functioning of the agent — with what allows a person to act, decide, flourish, and maintain meaningful relationships.

Consider an analogy. Suppose I make a promise to you on January 1. The fact that I made the promise is true forever; it is an immutable historical fact. But if I have since honored the promise in every possible way — kept it meticulously, gone above and beyond its terms, apologized for any incidental inconvenience — the rational response to the question "Did you make a promise?" is not "Yes, and therefore you are eternally bound in the way you were on January 1." The force of the promise has been discharged. The fact remains; its normative grip weakens.

Something similar applies to anger. Callard is right that apologies do not un-happen the wrong. But the moral demand generated by a wrong is not exhausted by its initial occurrence. Restitution, genuine apology, and demonstrated behavioral change speak not only to the original wrong but to the moral orientation of the wrongdoer. And orientation matters. A person who genuinely repents and reforms is not the same moral subject as the person who wronged you. Treating them identically — remaining just as angry, holding the grudge without concession — is not, as Callard implies, the height of rational moral responsiveness. It is a failure to track morally relevant changes.

I want to be precise about what I am conceding. Callard is right that there is something bogus about the common rhetorical move in which an apology is treated as a full moral reset — the "I said sorry, so move on" maneuver. That is morally evasive. But the opposite move — the insistence that no amount of amends can alter the original fact of wrongdoing — is equally evasive in the other direction. It refuses to recognize moral change in the wrongdoer, which is itself a failure of moral perception.

The Argument for Revenge fairs somewhat better in Callard's hands, but I think it still overreaches. Her claim is that revenge holds the wrongdoer accountable by refusing to let them "live it down" — by making the one-off opposition between your interests and mine into a permanent principle of interaction. This is a genuinely insightful point. There is something to the idea that allowing someone to forget their wrongdoing means the accountability relationship simply dissolves, and that this dissolution is morally significant.

But accountability and vengeance are not the same thing, and Callard's essay tends to slide between them. Holding someone accountable can take forms that are not vengeful — sustained scrutiny, conditional relationships, public accounting — and these forms need not involve "making your evil my good." The intimate, colonizing anger Callard describes, in which the wrongdoer "infiltrates the patterns of your thought," is not the only way to maintain moral accountability. It may not even be the best way. It is, as Callard acknowledges, psychologically costly and relationally destructive. A more measured accountability — one that recognizes the wrong, tracks the wrongdoer's moral progress, and adjusts accordingly — is both less corrupt and more rational.

None of this rescues the Stoic fantasy of purified anger. Callard is right that we cannot extract the moral sensing from the vengeful tendency. But the inability to purify does not entail that the two are identical in every context. It means we need a more nuanced ethics of anger than either the Stoics or the sentimentalists have given us — one that holds the tension Callard identifies without dissolving into either Stoic denial or vengeful permanence.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The rationality of an emotion should be judged by its contribution to an agent's flourishing and ability to maintain relationships, not just by the immutable truth of a past event.
  2. 02Remaining eternally angry ignores morally relevant changes in a wrongdoer’s orientation, such as genuine repentance and restitution, which effectively discharge the normative grip of the original wrong.
  3. 03Callard’s argument for revenge mistakenly equates moral accountability with vengeance, overlooking more measured forms of accountability that do not require the psychological corruption of 'making the wrongdoer's evil one's good.'
  4. 04The inability to fully purify anger of its dark impulses does not mean we must accept limitless grudges; rather, it necessitates a nuanced ethics that tracks moral progress while acknowledging the original offense.
Diversified MiniMax

"The Argument for Grudges is flawed because it incorrectly assumes that the permanent truth of a past wrongdoing constitutes a permanent normative reason to remain angry."

I want to focus on a single claim in Callard's essay, because I believe it is the hinge on which the entire argument turns: "Once you have a reason to be angry, you have a reason to be angry forever."

This is the Argument for Grudges, and it is stated with confident brevity. But confidence is not clarity, and I want to press on what it actually means. "Have a reason to be angry" is doing a lot of work in this formulation, and I am not sure it can bear the weight Callard puts on it.

On one reading, to "have a reason to be angry" means that a certain proposition is true: that you wronged me, that the wrong was serious, that you were responsible. If this is what it means, then Callard is right that the fact persists — it will always be true that you wronged me. But the question is whether the proposition's persisting truth is a persistent reason for anger. A reason, in the normative sense, is not just a fact. It is a fact in light of which a certain response is appropriate. And appropriateness is sensitive to context in ways that raw factuality is not.

To use an analogy: "He made a promise" is a fact that is true forever. But it is not always a reason to hold someone to a promise. If the promise was made under duress, if the circumstances have changed so radically that keeping it would produce disproportionate harm, if the person to whom the promise was made has explicitly released the promiser from the obligation — in all these cases, the fact persists but the reason dissolves. The fact that you wronged me is the reason I got angry. Whether it remains a reason to stay angry depends on whether the other normative facts have changed: whether you have repented, whether the relationship has been reconstituted, whether holding the grudge serves any purpose that the original anger was meant to serve.

This is not the sentimentalist's move of "letting go" through an act of will. It is a recognition that the normative situation has shifted. When I wrong you and then genuinely make amends, the moral situation is not identical to the one in which I wronged you and did nothing. The wrong occurred, yes. But a relationship of accountability has been established, and that relationship has a structure — it has terms, expectations, and trajectory. The person who remains permanently angry is not tracking a static fact; they are refusing to acknowledge the morally relevant developments that have occurred since the fact was established.

I am aware that Callard anticipates this objection. She says that restitution and apology "do nothing to cancel or alter the fact that I stole." True. But the question is not whether they cancel the fact; it is whether they alter the reason. And in most cases, they do. A thief who returns what was stolen and demonstrates genuine remorse has changed the moral landscape in ways that are not merely psychological but normatively significant. The reason to be angry has not disappeared, but it has been joined by other reasons — reasons of proportion, reasons of relation, reasons of forward-looking justice — that pull in the other direction.

Callard wants to tell a story in which the only morally relevant fact is the original wrong and its logical consequences. But morality is not a logical system; it is a practice, and practices are sensitive to change. The Argument for Grudges mistakes the foundation of a practice for the whole of it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A normative reason for an emotion is not merely a static fact but a consideration of appropriateness that is sensitive to changing contexts.
  2. 02Just as the fact of a promise does not always provide a reason to enforce it, the fact of a past wrong does not provide a perpetual reason for anger if the normative situation has shifted.
  3. 03Genuine amends, repentance, and the reconstitution of a relationship are morally relevant developments that can dissolve the original reason for anger.
  4. 04The person who maintains a permanent grudge is failing to track the evolving moral landscape of accountability in favor of a static, historical fact.
  5. 05Morality is a dynamic practice sensitive to change rather than a rigid logical system where an initial wrong dictates all future responses.
