Diversified Gemini

"The proposal to revive a 'democracy-of-opportunity' tradition is fundamentally flawed because it relies on historical progressive eras that were structurally built upon white supremacy and racial exclusion."

In their lead essay, Fishkin, Forbath, Katz, Rana, and Tushnet issue a clarion call to progressives: stop ceding the Constitution to the political right. They advocate reviving a “democracy-of-opportunity” tradition, arguing that a true reading of the Constitution compels the state to dismantle oligarchy and ensure broad, egalitarian distribution of power and wealth. Crucially, they assert that "racial justice is bound up with political economy," pointing out how the conservative Supreme Court’s jurisprudence—from gutting the Voting Rights Act to severely limiting Medicaid expansion—functions to maintain a racialized hierarchy.

While the authors are absolutely correct to identify the inextricable link between race, class, and constitutional interpretation, their proposed solution—resuscitating a nostalgic “democracy-of-opportunity” tradition—is fundamentally flawed. By romanticizing past progressive eras, the authors fail to reckon with the reality that American constitutionalism, even in its most populist iterations, has always been built on a foundation of white supremacy and racial exclusion. You cannot simply dust off the Jacksonian or New Deal constitutional traditions without importing the racial caste systems that were integral to their political economies.

The authors cite the Jacksonian Democrats as early champions of the anti-oligarchy tradition. It is true that Andrew Jackson railed against the Bank of the United States and the entrenched eastern establishment. But Jacksonian “democracy of opportunity” was explicitly formulated for white men, and it was paid for with the blood and land of Indigenous people and the enslaved. The expansion of the agrarian, white republic was achieved through the Indian Removal Act of 1830—a massive exercise of federal power to ethnically cleanse the Southeast. The Jacksonian political economy was not incidentally racist; its anti-monopolistic populism was structurally dependent on the expropriation of Native land and the violent expansion of the slavocracy. To claim this as a foundational tradition for modern progressives is to erase the victims of that tradition.

Similarly, the authors look to the New Deal as a triumph of progressive constitutionalism. They rightly praise the Wagner Act and the establishment of labor rights as essential for creating countervailing power against capital. But they gloss over the fact that the New Deal political economy was intentionally designed to exclude Black Americans. To secure the votes of Southern Dixiecrats, Franklin Roosevelt and his congressional allies excluded agricultural and domestic workers from the protections of the National Labor Relations Act and the Fair Labor Standards Act. Social Security originally excluded these same overwhelmingly Black professions. Furthermore, the federal government’s housing policies during this era invented redlining, systematically denying capital to Black neighborhoods while subsidizing white flight to the suburbs. The New Deal did not just fail to address racial wealth inequality; it actively engineered the modern racial wealth gap.

When the authors argue that we must show Americans that "certain progressive ideas are deeply rooted in American traditions of constitutional argument," they are asking us to engage in historical whitewashing. If we ground our demands for racial and economic justice in the traditions of the 19th and early 20th centuries, we tether ourselves to frameworks that were designed to accommodate, if not advance, racial apartheid.

The authors astutely analyze how Chief Justice Roberts’s decisions in *NFIB v. Sebelius* (Medicaid expansion) and *Shelby County v. Holder* (Voting Rights Act) utilized invented federalism doctrines to harm Black populations disproportionately. They are right that these are battles over constitutional political economy. However, their proposed response—to pass new universal statutory schemes and argue that they are constitutionally required—underestimates the ideological flexibility of the conservative courts and the limitations of colorblind universalism.

In a society structured by centuries of targeted racial plunder, universal programs often fail to close racial gaps, and sometimes widen them. Furthermore, the conservative legal movement is currently weaponizing the Equal Protection Clause to strike down any race-conscious policies, arguing that the Constitution demands absolute colorblindness. We see this in the relentless attacks on affirmative action, minority business contracting, and even disparate impact theory.

If progressives try to fight this by merely arguing for a colorblind "democracy of opportunity," they leave the specific mechanisms of racial subordination intact. The Constitution’s text, even the Reconstruction Amendments, has been successfully co-opted by conservatives to protect the status quo. The 14th Amendment, drafted to protect formerly enslaved Black people, was used for a century primarily to protect corporations from regulation, and is now used to strike down policies designed to integrate schools or workplaces.

What is required is not a retrieval of past traditions, but a radical rupture. If we are to "Make Progressive Politics Constitutional Again," we need a new constitutionalism—what some scholars and activists have called a Third Reconstruction. This vision must explicitly reject the compromises of the Founding, the Jacksonian era, and the New Deal. It must start from the premise that the United States was founded as a racial capitalist state, and that the Constitution was an instrument of that founding.

A truly anti-racist constitutional political economy would not merely argue that a wealth tax is permissible or necessary to prevent oligarchy; it would argue that wealth redistribution is a constitutional necessity as a form of reparations for centuries of unpaid enslaved labor, Jim Crow exploitation, and redlining. It would argue that the abolition of the carceral state is not just a policy preference, but a constitutional requirement to fulfill the promise of the 13th Amendment, which left a glaring loophole allowing slavery as punishment for a crime.

The Movement for Black Lives and the Poor People's Campaign, which the authors cite, do not frame their demands as a return to the roots of American constitutionalism. They frame them as demands for human rights and liberation in the face of a state that has historically denied them both. Their power comes from their moral clarity and their willingness to disrupt the system, not their adherence to traditional constitutional hermeneutics.

The authors fear that ignoring the Constitution cedes the terrain to conservatives. But we must ask whether the terrain itself is fatally compromised. By constraining our political imagination to what can be justified by a document written by enslavers, or interpreted through the traditions of settler-colonialism and Jim Crow, we limit our capacity to envision true justice.

Progressives and radicals should certainly fight in the courts to protect the vulnerable, using whatever legal tools are available. But our primary political rhetoric should not be constitutional fidelity. It should be democratic reconstruction. We must tell the American people the truth: that our constitutional history is largely a history of racialized exploitation, and that building a true, multiracial democracy requires not returning to an imagined golden age, but creating something entirely unprecedented on this continent. We do not need to make progressive politics constitutional; we need to make the Constitution just.
