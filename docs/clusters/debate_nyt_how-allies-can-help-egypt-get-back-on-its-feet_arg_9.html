"To foster long-term stability and economic recovery, Egypt’s allies must shift from supporting military-led order to financing accountable, pro-civilian institutions and the rule of law."

The phrase “maintain stability” should make Egyptians nervous. For three decades, outside powers used stability as the justification for supporting Hosni Mubarak’s police state. That bargain produced neither real stability nor real prosperity. It produced corruption, emergency law, a narrow business elite and a generation of young people with education but few prospects. If allies now define stability as restoring order under the Supreme Council of the Armed Forces, they will repeat the error that helped create the uprising.

Egypt’s economy is in serious trouble: tourism has fallen, reserves are being depleted, investors are waiting, and the public sector remains bloated and inefficient. But these are not merely technocratic problems. They are symptoms of a political transition in which authority remains opaque and accountability weak. No amount of aid can compensate for a system in which generals control major economic interests, civilians do not know who is making decisions, and activists face prosecution while old-regime networks survive.

Allies should therefore make their assistance openly pro-civilian and pro-rule-of-law. This does not mean dictating election results or excluding Islamists. It means supporting procedures: an end to military trials for civilians, protection for independent unions and associations, transparent public accounts, and a constitution-writing process that cannot be monopolized by either the army or a temporary parliamentary majority. Economic help should flow through accountable civilian institutions wherever possible, not through military channels.

The United States has special responsibility because its annual military aid helped sustain the old order. Washington should review that assistance and make clear that repression will carry costs. The recent pressure on nongovernmental organizations and democracy groups is not a side issue; it is a test of whether the new Egypt will permit citizens to organize independently. Europe should take a similar approach, emphasizing legal protections, judicial independence and anti-corruption mechanisms.

At the same time, allies should help ordinary Egyptians weather the transition. Food security, employment programs, microcredit, support for schools and hospitals, and aid to small farmers can reduce hardship without empowering authoritarian structures. Debt relief should be linked to transparency so that new funds do not disappear into the same patronage networks that flourished under Mubarak.

The road to recovery runs through legitimacy. A government trusted by its citizens can ask for sacrifice, attract investment and negotiate with international lenders. A government feared by its citizens can only postpone crisis. Egypt’s allies must choose which future they are financing.
