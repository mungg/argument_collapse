"The strategic focus on defeating fossil capital through state power is insufficient and physically impossible unless it is coupled with a commitment to degrowth and the reduction of aggregate material demand."

Olúfẹ́mi O. Táíwò’s essay, “Climate, State, and Utopia,” correctly identifies fossil capital as the immediate existential threat to human flourishing and rightfully rehabilitates the state as the only institutional apparatus capable of coordinating a rapid energy transition. His "two-step" framework—first defeat fossil capital, then remake the world—is a powerful rhetorical maneuver meant to cut through left-wing paralysis. Yet, in his effort to streamline our strategic focus, Táíwò makes a fatal concession: he treats the debate over economic growth as a secondary squabble, a mere disagreement over the "precise manner of delinking" from fossil fuels. From the perspective of ecological economics, this is not a tactical detail; it is the entire ballgame. By ignoring the imperative of degrowth, Táíwò’s step one risks building a "green" state that ultimately devours the biosphere anyway.

Táíwò dismisses the debates between "circular," "steady state," "post-growth," and "green growth" economies as subordinate to the primary goal of politically defeating fossil capital. This implies that as long as the state takes control and phases out oil, coal, and gas, the specific metabolic profile of the ensuing economy can be sorted out later. This assumption relies on the illusion of absolute decoupling—the idea that a state-managed economy can continue to infinitely expand its GDP and material throughput using solely renewable energy.

The empirical reality, however, is that replacing our current energy system entirely with wind, solar, and batteries while maintaining the compounding growth rates demanded by both private capital and contemporary state planning is ecologically impossible. The material footprint of renewable infrastructure is massive. To maintain a 2-3% annual global growth rate, the extraction of critical minerals, land use changes, and industrial processing would have to scale exponentially. Even if this growth is powered by the sun and wind, the sheer volume of material throughput would inevitably breach other planetary boundaries identified by the Stockholm Resilience Centre, such as biodiversity loss, land system change, and novel entity pollution (chemical and plastic waste).

Fossil fuels are not the only problem; they are the most acute symptom of a deeper pathology: an economic system structurally reliant on infinite expansion on a finite planet. If Táíwò’s empowered state successfully dethrones fossil capital but remains committed to the logic of productivism and endless growth—perhaps to justify the massive public expenditures required, or to maintain geopolitical competitiveness—it will simply shift the ecological crisis from atmospheric carbon to terrestrial devastation.

Furthermore, the Jevons paradox teaches us that increases in energy efficiency or the introduction of cheaper energy sources (like mass state-subsidized solar) often lead to an absolute increase in energy consumption unless explicitly constrained. If a progressive state builds out massive public renewable capacity but allows the economy to continue expanding its aggregate energy demand to power increasingly frivolous and resource-intensive production (from AI data centers to planned obsolescence in consumer goods), the transition will never catch up to the growth in demand. We will find ourselves in a "green addition" scenario rather than an energy transition, where renewables are built alongside, rather than replacing, baseline fossil fuel extraction.

Therefore, defeating fossil capital (Táíwò’s Step One) is materially impossible without simultaneously reducing aggregate energy and material demand in the Global North. Degrowth is not a utopian "Step Two" to be pursued after we have stabilized the climate; it is the physical prerequisite for making the energy transition mathematically feasible.

If we attempt to build out enough renewable capacity to replace the entirety of our current, hyper-inflated energy usage, the political and economic friction will be insurmountable. The cost, both in capital and in the violence of extraction, will be too high. A serious state-led climate strategy must begin by rationing and reducing energy use in the wealthiest sectors of society: banning private jets, phasing out fast fashion, legally mandating the durability and repairability of goods, and shifting the economy away from the production of exchange-value toward the provisioning of use-value (healthcare, education, care work).

Táíwò argues that any system where fossil fuels retain their centrality is incompatible with broader justice. We must add: any system that relies on compounding material growth, even if powered by renewables, is incompatible with a habitable biosphere. If the left seizes the state only to manage a "green growth" empire, we will have defeated ExxonMobil only to surrender the earth to the mining conglomerates and the logic of endless extraction.

The state power we must build is not one of simple technological substitution, but of planned, democratic economic contraction in the Global North. We cannot wait for "step two" to rethink our relationship to the material world. We must scale down to survive.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The material footprint required to replace current energy systems while maintaining economic growth would breach planetary boundaries like biodiversity and land use, even if powered by renewables.
  2. 02The illusion of absolute decoupling—expanding GDP while reducing ecological impact—is empirically unsupported and ignores the massive extraction of minerals needed for green infrastructure.
  3. 03Without explicit constraints on growth, the Jevons paradox suggests that increased energy efficiency and cheaper renewables will lead to higher total energy consumption rather than a total phase-out of fossil fuels.
  4. 04Degrowth is a physical prerequisite for a feasible energy transition because reducing energy demand in the Global North lowers the insurmountable capital and extractive costs of a total technological substitution.
  5. 05A state-led climate strategy must prioritize the democratic contraction of resource-intensive industries and shift from exchange-value production to the provisioning of essential use-values.
