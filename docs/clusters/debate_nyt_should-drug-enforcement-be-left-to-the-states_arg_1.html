"The federal government should refrain from prosecuting local drug cases and instead reserve its resources for national-level issues while allowing states to experiment with their own enforcement and treatment policies."

The federal government should not be in the business of routinely prosecuting local drug cases. It has legitimate responsibilities in drug enforcement, but those responsibilities are not the same as treating every small possession case, street sale or state-regulated marijuana transaction as a federal matter.

For decades, federal drug policy has leaned heavily on prosecution, mandatory minimums and broad conspiracy laws. That approach has helped fill federal prisons without eliminating drug markets. It has also produced severe racial and class disparities, especially in crack cocaine and low-level trafficking cases. Attorney General Eric Holder’s recent “Smart on Crime” initiative is an overdue admission that the federal government has overused its harshest tools.

States are better positioned to decide how to handle ordinary drug use within their borders. Vermont’s turn toward treatment for opiate addiction reflects a public-health judgment. Colorado and Washington’s marijuana laws reflect a different judgment: that regulation may work better than prohibition. These experiments should be allowed to proceed, with careful measurement of health, crime, youth use and impaired driving.

That does not mean Washington should disappear. Federal prosecutors should focus on interstate trafficking networks, cartels, gun violence, money laundering, corruption, sales to minors, and diversion of legal drugs into illegal markets. The Justice Department’s marijuana guidance already points in this direction by identifying federal priorities rather than promising blanket nonenforcement.

A sensible division of labor would reserve federal power for genuinely national problems and leave local enforcement largely to states and cities. The Constitution permits broad federal drug laws, but wisdom counsels restraint. If states are willing to replace reflexive punishment with treatment, regulation and accountability, federal prosecutors should not stand in the way merely to preserve a failed uniformity.

Vanilla Claude

"The federal government should codify a policy of deference to the states by limiting federal drug prosecution to interstate or international cases while leaving local possession and distribution to state law."

Federalism is not just a slogan; it is the constitutional design that lets states act as laboratories on contested social questions. On drug policy, that experimentation is overdue. Vermont's pivot toward treatment for opiate addiction, Colorado and Washington's marijuana legalization, and the quieter shift in dozens of states toward drug courts and diversion all reflect different judgments about what works in different communities. The federal government's role should shrink to match.

That doesn't mean Washington should withdraw entirely. Interstate drug trafficking, cartels, large-scale manufacturing, and money laundering are precisely the problems federal law enforcement was designed to handle. The DEA and FBI have comparative advantages in cross-border investigations that no state agency can match. But routine possession cases, street-level sales, and even mid-level distribution within a state are already prosecuted ably in state courts, which handle the vast majority of drug cases anyway. Federal prosecution there mostly duplicates effort, fills federal prisons—now roughly half of whose inmates are drug offenders—and imposes mandatory minimums that state legislatures have increasingly rejected.

Attorney General Holder's recent guidance scaling back mandatory minimums and the Cole memo deferring to Colorado and Washington point in the right direction, but they are reversible by the next administration. Congress should codify the deference: leave possession and intrastate distribution to state law, and limit federal prosecution to genuinely interstate or international cases.

Critics worry about a patchwork. But a patchwork is what federalism produces, and on a question where the national consensus has visibly fractured, that is a feature, not a bug.

Vanilla DeepSeek

"The federal government should shift its focus away from local drug possession and low-level offenses to allow for state-led innovation in public health, while maintaining its role in prosecuting large-scale interstate and international trafficking networks."

The states are right to treat drug abuse as a public health challenge rather than solely a criminal one, and the federal government should respect those choices—but that does not mean Washington should withdraw entirely from drug enforcement. A sweeping retreat would be as reckless as the one-size-fits-all crackdowns of the past. The proper role for federal prosecutors lies not in local possession cases but in thwarting the interstate and international trafficking networks that overwhelm any single state’s capacity to respond.

When Colorado and Washington legalized recreational marijuana, the Justice Department’s August 2013 memorandum—which declined to challenge those laws provided they met strict regulatory safeguards—showed that the federal government can accommodate state innovation without abdicating its constitutional authority. Similarly, Attorney General Holder’s “Smart on Crime” initiative, which directs U.S. attorneys to avoid mandatory minimums for low-level, nonviolent drug offenders, acknowledges that over-prosecution of street-level users and small-time sellers has filled federal prisons without making communities safer. These calibrated steps suggest a practical path: the federal government should generally leave local drug cases to state courts, which are better positioned to blend punishment, treatment, and social services. Vermont’s emphasis on rehabilitation over incarceration, for example, reflects local priorities that Washington should not second-guess.

Yet a complete federal withdrawal would be a dangerous overcorrection. Drug cartels do not respect state lines, and no single state has the investigative reach to disrupt the trafficking organizations that move heroin, methamphetamine, or cocaine across borders and through national distribution chains. Federal prosecutors remain essential to target these large-scale networks, and the Controlled Substances Act retains a valid role in fighting interstate commerce in the most harmful substances. Handing all drug enforcement to the states would also invite a regulatory race to the bottom, where traffickers exploit the weakest jurisdiction.

The federal government should not prosecute a student caught with a joint or a low-level addict peddling heroin to support a habit. Those cases belong in state systems, where therapeutic courts and diversion programs can offer a smarter response. But when organized crime moves drugs across state boundaries, Washington must remain the backstop. The goal is not monolithic enforcement but a sensible division of labor—one that honors federalism while recognizing that the drug trade is a national, not merely local, problem.

"The federal government should adopt a model of functional federalism that grants states autonomy over local drug policy while reserving federal enforcement for major trafficking and interstate concerns."

The shift toward letting states experiment with drug policy represents a welcome recognition that one-size-fits-all enforcement has failed. Colorado and Washington's marijuana experiments, Vermont's treatment emphasis, and the Justice Department's recent restraint all suggest that Americans are increasingly skeptical of the war on drugs' promise. Yet wholesale abandonment of federal involvement would be premature.

The strongest case for state flexibility rests on practical results. Treatment-focused approaches in Vermont and similar states have shown better outcomes than purely punitive models, and states appear better positioned to tailor responses to local conditions. The laboratory-of-democracy argument has merit: when 50 jurisdictions try different approaches, we learn what works. TheCole Memorandum signaled that the Obama administration understood this dynamic.

But complete devolution creates genuine problems. The Controlled Substances Act remains federal law, and drug trafficking networks operate across state boundaries with ease. A state that legalizes or deprioritizes certain substances effectively exports its policy to neighboring jurisdictions, complicating enforcement elsewhere. Additionally, federal prosecution has historically served as a backstop against the most egregious local abuses—discriminatory enforcement, for instance—that state systems sometimes produce.

The better approach is functional federalism: federal enforcement should focus on genuinely national concerns—major trafficking organizations, interstate commerce, and cases involving clear civil liberties violations—while allowing states substantial autonomy over possession, small-scale distribution, and rehabilitation-first responses. This isn't abdication; it's a realistic acknowledgment that Washington cannot micromanage every drug case effectively.

States have demonstrated both the capacity and the will to develop smarter approaches. The federal government's role should be to encourage that innovation while reserving its resources and authority for problems that genuinely require national attention.

The recent shifts in state drug policies—from Colorado and Washington’s legalization of marijuana to Vermont Governor Peter Shumlin dedicating his entire January 2014 State of the State address to the heroin crisis—highlight a critical reality: the consequences of the War on Drugs are uniquely local. As states pivot from punitive models to regulatory and public health approaches, the federal government must cede jurisdiction over local drug enforcement, reserving its resources exclusively for interstate and international trafficking.

States have long been recognized as the "laboratories of democracy," and nowhere is this experimentation more necessary than in drug policy. For decades, blanket federal prohibition has driven mass incarceration without effectively curbing addiction rates or street-level crime. Vermont’s new emphasis on treatment and rehabilitation acknowledges that substance abuse is primarily a public health crisis, not a criminal one. Meanwhile, the legal frameworks currently rolling out in Washington and Colorado aim to dismantle the black market through taxation and regulation. When federal prosecutors pursue local, low-level drug cases, they undermine these democratically enacted, community-specific solutions and waste limited resources.

This does not mean the federal government has no role in drug enforcement, but rather that its role must be strictly redefined. The Justice Department, guided by recent directives like the Cole Memorandum, is beginning to recognize the need for restraint. Federal agencies like the DEA and the FBI should focus their immense capabilities on what state and local police cannot manage: transnational cartels, violent organized crime, interstate smuggling networks, and large-scale money laundering. Prosecuting a street-level dealer or a non-violent user under federal statutes—often triggering draconian mandatory minimums—does nothing to dismantle the structural supply chains of illicit narcotics.

Attorney General Eric Holder’s recent pledge to scale back federal mandatory minimums for non-violent drug offenses is a promising first step. However, lasting reform requires a formal devolution of local drug enforcement to the states. By allowing states the autonomy to treat addiction as a medical issue or to regulate marijuana as a commercial commodity, the federal government can finally realign its focus toward true national security and interstate threats. Local drug enforcement is inherently a community issue, and it is time the federal government allows communities the freedom to solve it.

The Constitution does not give Congress a general police power. That power belongs primarily to the states. For that reason, the federal government should not be in the business of prosecuting ordinary local drug cases. It should police borders, regulate interstate commerce and honor treaty obligations. It should not behave as if every plant grown, sold or consumed within a state is automatically a national matter.

Modern constitutional doctrine allows Congress to reach very far under the Commerce Clause. The Supreme Court’s decision in Gonzales v. Raich upheld federal power even over locally grown medical marijuana permitted by California law. As a statement of current law, that may settle what Washington can do. It does not settle what Washington should do. A system in which Congress may criminalize nearly any local conduct by invoking a national market leaves little meaningful space for state self-government.

Drug policy is exactly the kind of subject on which state authority matters. The people of Colorado and Washington have chosen regulated marijuana legalization. Vermont is emphasizing treatment and rehabilitation in response to rising drug abuse. Other states may choose stricter prohibition. Citizens can compare results, revise laws and hold state officials accountable. That process is healthier than one national policy imposed from Washington and enforced selectively depending on the priorities of whichever administration happens to be in office.

Selective enforcement is one of the great dangers of the present arrangement. The federal government says marijuana remains illegal, but it will not generally interfere in Colorado and Washington if certain conditions are met. That may be politically prudent, but it is also unstable. Citizens and businesses should not have to live under a law that is officially absolute but practically optional. If federal officials are not prepared to prosecute state-compliant marijuana activity, Congress should change federal law.

A principled federalist approach would draw a firm line. Federal authorities should target cross-border smuggling, international cartels, drug trafficking on federal property, money laundering, and conspiracies that genuinely operate across state lines. They should not prosecute possession, intrastate cultivation, or state-regulated sales that remain within a state’s borders. States that legalize would then bear responsibility for preventing leakage into other states; if they fail, federal jurisdiction would be triggered by the interstate conduct.

This position is not libertarian indifference to drug abuse. States can and should address addiction, impaired driving, sales to minors and public disorder. Indeed, they may do so more effectively because they are closer to the problem. The federal government’s proper role is limited but important. Respecting that limit would make drug policy more accountable, more constitutional and less dependent on prosecutorial grace.

"The federal government must maintain a central role in drug enforcement because drug trafficking is an interstate and international problem that states lack the jurisdiction and resources to handle alone."

Federal Power Exists for a Reason

It has become fashionable to argue that drug policy should be handed over to the states. The argument has a certain appeal, especially as voters in Colorado and Washington have grown impatient with marijuana prohibition. But before we dismantle a federal role that has existed in some form since the Harrison Act of 1914, we should remember why that role was built in the first place.

Drugs do not respect state lines. The heroin killing Vermonters does not come from a Vermont poppy field; it travels up I-91 from cities further south, and before that, across an international border. Methamphetamine in the Midwest is moved by transnational networks. Pills diverted from Florida pain clinics end up in Kentucky and West Virginia. State police and county sheriffs cannot subpoena bank records in Mexico, run wiretaps on a multistate conspiracy, or coordinate with foreign governments. The DEA and federal prosecutors can. Removing them from local cases would leave the supply side untouched while states scramble to clean up the demand side.

There is also a constitutional point worth making. The Supreme Court settled in Gonzales v. Raich (2005) that Congress's Commerce Clause power extends to local marijuana cultivation because of its aggregate effect on the interstate market. One can disagree with that ruling, but it is the law. A federal government that selectively declines to enforce duly enacted statutes — depending on which state's voters most recently passed a referendum — corrodes the rule of law. If the Controlled Substances Act is bad policy, Congress should change it. Until then, executive nonenforcement is a troubling precedent that future administrations may apply to laws progressives like.

The states-rights frame also tends to assume that state policy is uniformly more humane than federal policy. It isn't. Some states impose harsher penalties than federal law, particularly for crack and methamphetamine. Some have done virtually nothing to address the opioid crisis. A purely state-driven system would produce not just Colorado and Vermont, but also jurisdictions where addicted people get longer sentences and less treatment than they do today.

The right answer is not abdication but recalibration. The Justice Department should continue to prioritize traffickers over users, support state treatment initiatives, and use its prosecutorial discretion sensibly. But "leaving it to the states" as a slogan papers over the genuine national interests — public health, organized crime, border security, equal protection of the laws — that a federal role is meant to serve. We should be careful what we wish away.

  2. 02Selective federal non-enforcement of the Controlled Substances Act based on state referendums undermines the rule of law and sets a dangerous precedent for executive overreach.
  3. 03A purely state-driven system would result in a lack of uniform protection, as some states may impose harsher penalties and offer less treatment than the current federal standard.
  4. 04The federal government has a constitutional mandate and a national interest in regulating drug markets due to their aggregate effect on interstate commerce.

"The federal government should leave the enforcement of intrastate drug matters to the states because the current federal prohibition of local drug activity lacks a sound constitutional basis."

The Constitutional Question We Keep Avoiding

Most of the debate over drug enforcement is about policy: does prohibition work, are sentences too long, is marijuana more or less harmful than alcohol? These are important questions, but they obscure a prior one. Under what constitutional authority does the federal government criminalize the possession of a plant grown in someone's backyard for personal use?

The historic answer was: none. When the country wanted to ban alcohol in 1919, it understood that a constitutional amendment was required. The Eighteenth Amendment did the work that mere statute could not. When Prohibition was repealed in 1933, the Twenty-First Amendment explicitly recognized state primacy over alcohol regulation. Drugs got no such amendment. The Controlled Substances Act of 1970 rests on the Commerce Clause, stretched in Gonzales v. Raich (2005) to cover homegrown marijuana on the theory that even non-commercial cultivation affects interstate markets.

That reasoning, as Justices Thomas and O'Connor pointed out in dissent, would justify almost anything. If growing six plants in your basement is "interstate commerce," the limiting principle of enumerated powers has effectively dissolved. One need not be a Tea Party constitutionalist to be uncomfortable with that. A federal government that can prosecute a Colorado dispensary operating in full compliance with Colorado law has powers the Framers would not have recognized.

Leaving drug enforcement to the states is therefore not just good policy; it is closer to the original constitutional design. States have always exercised the general police power — public health, safety, and morals — that the federal government does not possess. Murder, theft, assault, and the regulation of doctors and pharmacies are state matters. Drugs, historically and constitutionally, belong in the same category.

This does not mean the federal government has no role. Interstate trafficking is genuinely interstate; international smuggling implicates the federal foreign affairs power; controlled substances moving through federal facilities or the federal mails are properly federal concerns. These are real interstate commerce questions, not legal fictions. The DEA can and should police them.

But the everyday prosecution of growers, sellers, and users operating wholly within a single state goes beyond what the Constitution, fairly read, permits. The current détente — federal nonenforcement of federal law where state law differs — is unsatisfying because it makes federal policy turn on prosecutorial whim rather than legal principle. The cleaner answer is for Congress to repeal or substantially narrow the CSA, returning to the states the authority over intrastate drug matters that they never properly surrendered. Federalism, in this area, is not a slogan. It is the law.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02The use of the Commerce Clause to justify federal drug enforcement over non-commercial, local cultivation stretches the principle of enumerated powers beyond its intended limits.
  3. 03States possess the general police power to regulate public health, safety, and morals, a power the federal government was never granted by the Constitution.
  4. 04While the federal government has a legitimate role in policing interstate trafficking and international smuggling, it lacks the authority to prosecute drug activities occurring wholly within a single state.
  5. 05Relying on federal prosecutorial discretion to ignore state-legal drug activity is an unstable solution that should be replaced by formal legislative repeal or narrowing of the Controlled Substances Act.

Asking whether the federal government should "even prosecute local drug cases" frames the issue too starkly. In practice, federal and state prosecutors already divide work along reasonably sensible lines, and the question is whether to refine that division — not whether to scrap one side of it.

State systems have the local intelligence, the patrol officers, and the courts with the bandwidth to handle high volume.

That is not a thrilling answer. It is, I think, the right one.
