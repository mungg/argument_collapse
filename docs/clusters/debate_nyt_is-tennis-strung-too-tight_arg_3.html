"Tennis etiquette should prioritize reforming player conduct and on-court decorum over addressing the behavior of the crowd."

The hand-wringing over rowdy fans is a diversion. The aspect of tennis etiquette most urgently in need of an update isn’t the volume of the crowd, but the conduct of the players themselves. For every fan silenced for a sneeze, there’s a player shattering the auditory peace with a grunt that registers as loud as a lawnmower on the decibel meter. The current tolerance for prolonged, high-decibel shrieking—a feature of the modern power game, not an accident—borders on farcical. It’s a form of gamesmanship that distracts an opponent and drowns out the sound of the ball off the racket, which is both a safety issue and a fundamental deprivation of the auditory cues tennis is built upon.

After grunting, the next target for reform is coaching. The pretense that a player is a lone gladiator receiving zero tactical help from their box is a charade. We see the surreptitious signals, the coded claps, and occasionally outright verbal instructions from the stands. Updating the etiquette to legalize limited on-court coaching, as the WTA has experimented with, would bring an honest, teachable dynamic to the game without the farce. Finally, the liberal use of injury timeouts for strategic momentum-shifting needs a clear, enforceable criteria that prioritizes integrity over drama. Cleaning up the players’ own on-court decorum would restore more dignity to the sport than any redefinition of the audience's role. Before we let the crowd shout, the sport itself needs to stop yelling.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The sport needs to regulate excessive grunting and shrieking, which serves as a form of gamesmanship that distracts opponents and obscures vital auditory cues.
  2. 02Tennis should modernize its rules to legalize limited on-court coaching to eliminate the current charade of surreptitious and illegal communication from the stands.
  3. 03The criteria for injury timeouts must be tightened and strictly enforced to prevent players from using them as strategic tools to shift match momentum.
Diversified GPT

"Modernizing tennis etiquette should focus on reforming player conduct and on-court routines rather than targeting spectator behavior."

If tennis etiquette is to be modernized, the most important changes should concern the players, not the spectators. Fans are an easy target because a loud shout is obvious. But much of what slows and sours the professional game happens on court under the cover of “routine.”

The first reform should be a stricter culture around time. The rules already set limits between points, yet enforcement is inconsistent and often depends on the chair umpire’s courage and the player’s stature. Viewers can admire Rafael Nadal’s intensity or Novak Djokovic’s ball-bouncing without wanting every service game to become a ritual drama. When one player routinely exceeds the limit, the opponent is forced to live inside that rhythm. A visible shot clock, or at least more automatic warnings, would not be a gimmick. It would make the etiquette of pace transparent.

Second, tennis should rethink the towel ritual. Ball kids are not personal valets. Somewhere along the line it became normal for players to demand a towel after nearly every point, even when they have barely perspired. Put towel boxes at the back of the court and make players retrieve them. It would be more sanitary, more dignified for the children, and quicker once players adjusted.

Third, the sport needs a clearer attitude toward gamesmanship. Tactical medical timeouts, bathroom breaks taken to halt momentum, exaggerated challenges to catch a breath, and complaints designed to pressure officials all erode the compact between opponents. Tennis prides itself on honor because, historically, players called their own lines. The professional version should still expect more than “do whatever the umpire permits.”

On coaching, however, etiquette may be too rigid. The ban on coaching in most major competition is increasingly artificial when players communicate with boxes through glances, gestures and coded encouragement. Either enforce the rule seriously or allow limited, transparent coaching at set breaks. What looks dignified on paper can look hypocritical in practice.

As for crowds, preserve quiet during points and allow enthusiasm around them. That balance is not difficult. The harder task is asking stars to accept that etiquette is not merely something imposed on paying customers. It is the shared discipline that keeps an individual sport from becoming a collection of private indulgences.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Stricter and more consistent enforcement of time limits between points is necessary to prevent players from dictating an excessively slow pace.
  2. 02The towel ritual should be reformed by requiring players to retrieve their own towels from boxes to improve efficiency and dignity for ball kids.
  3. 03The sport needs to address gamesmanship by curbing the use of tactical medical timeouts and bathroom breaks intended to disrupt an opponent's momentum.
  4. 04The current ban on coaching should be replaced with transparent, regulated coaching to eliminate the hypocrisy of clandestine communication.
  5. 05Etiquette should be viewed as a shared discipline for players to uphold rather than a set of rules primarily imposed on the audience.
