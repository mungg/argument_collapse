"The state pension industry must undergo a radical overhaul that specifically targets and eliminates the corrupting influence of placement agents and pay-to-play political patronage."

The outrage over the shift of public pension money into alternative investments is justified, but much of it is misdirected at the investments themselves rather than the corrupt intermediaries who broker them. The multibillion-dollar state pension industry absolutely needs a radical overhaul, but that reform must be wielded like a scalpel, surgically targeting the "pay-to-play" culture and the use of placement agents that have turned public retirement systems into political slush funds.

When critics attack the secrecy of hedge funds, they often focus on the complexity of the trading models. But the real danger to the public isn't a secret algorithm; it's a secret handshake. Over the past few years, we have seen scandals erupt in states like New York, California, and New Mexico, where politically connected middlemen—known as placement agents—have extracted millions of dollars in fees simply for making introductions between Wall Street firms and public pension officials.

These middlemen do not manage money. They do not generate returns. Their only asset is their Rolodex and their proximity to the politicians who appoint the pension board trustees. In many cases, these agents channel campaign contributions to the very officials who hold the purse strings of the pension fund. When a hedge fund or private equity firm forces a strict confidentiality agreement on a public pension, that secrecy often serves to obscure these kickbacks and finder’s fees from public and journalistic scrutiny.

We do not necessarily need to ban alternative investments, which can play a legitimate role in a diversified portfolio. But we must absolutely ban the corrupting influence of placement agents in public finance.

Transparency mandates must be strictly focused on the flow of money outside the actual investment vehicle. Any firm seeking to manage public pension money should be required, under penalty of perjury and forfeiture of fees, to publicly disclose every dollar paid to third-party marketers, lobbyists, or consultants. Furthermore, we need strict federal enforcement of SEC pay-to-play rules that prohibit investment advisers from managing public money if they have made political contributions to the officials overseeing those funds. Eliminate the middlemen, and you eliminate the worst of the corruption, leaving pensions free to invest purely on the basis of merit rather than political patronage.

  3. 03Confidentiality agreements in alternative investments are often used to hide the flow of money to lobbyists and campaign contributors from public scrutiny.
  4. 04Reform should focus on mandatory disclosure of all third-party payments and strict enforcement of SEC rules prohibiting investment advisers from making political contributions to pension officials.
