"To address the institutional corruption exacerbated by Citizens United, the United States must implement practical reforms that realign candidate incentives toward ordinary voters and build a broad-based political coalition to overcome incumbent resistance."

The practical question after Citizens United is what can be done to address the legitimate concerns about money in politics. Lessig offers several proposals, and they deserve serious consideration.

The Fair Elections Now Act, which would provide public matching funds for small-dollar contributions, is the most promising near-term option. It addresses the dependency problem by creating incentives for candidates to build small-dollar donor bases rather than cultivating relationships with wealthy contributors. Early versions of this proposal have attracted bipartisan support, suggesting it could pass a divided Congress.

The democracy voucher system that Lessig discusses is more ambitious. It would provide every citizen with public funds to donate to candidates, creating a structure in which representatives are literally dependent on voters rather than wealthy donors. This proposal deserves serious pilot testing, perhaps at the state level before national implementation.

Disclosure requirements can be strengthened within the framework Citizens United permits. Full disclosure of independent expenditures, including the identity of contributors to organizations that fund such expenditures, would give voters information they need to evaluate political messages. The Court has generally upheld disclosure requirements, and they impose less burden on speech than outright prohibitions.

Coordination rules should be clarified and strengthened. The Court's assumption that independent expenditures are truly independent may be unrealistic given the informal coordination that Lessig rightly identifies. Tighter rules about communication between campaigns and outside groups would help.

Constitutional amendment remains an option of last resort. The difficulty of the amendment process is a feature, not a bug—it ensures that only broadly supported reforms become law. But the very difficulty of amendment means it should not be the first resort.

The deeper challenge is political. Campaign finance reform has failed repeatedly because the people who would benefit most from reform—ordinary citizens—have the least capacity to organize and demand it. The people who would lose from reform—incumbents who benefit from the current system—have strong incentives to block change.

Breaking this deadlock will require building coalitions across partisan lines. The concerns Lessig identifies are not partisan concerns. Democrats and Republicans alike are dependent on wealthy contributors. Both parties should be interested in a system that reduces their collective dependency on money.

Lessig's essay provides a valuable service by framing the issue in terms that transcend partisan politics. The problem is not that Republicans or Democrats are corrupt; it is that our political system is corrupted by a structure of incentives that draws representatives away from the interests of ordinary voters. Addressing this problem will require more than technical fixes. It will require a democratic revival that makes citizens active participants in political life rather than passive observers of a system dominated by money.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Fair Elections Now Act is a promising near-term solution because it uses public matching funds to incentivize candidates to prioritize small-dollar donors over wealthy contributors.
  2. 02A democracy voucher system represents a more ambitious and effective long-term structure for ensuring representatives are financially dependent on the electorate rather than special interests.
  3. 03Strengthening disclosure requirements and tightening coordination rules are necessary and legally viable methods to mitigate the influence of independent expenditures within the current judicial framework.
  4. 04A constitutional amendment should be viewed as a last resort due to the difficulty of the process, prioritizing legislative and regulatory fixes first.
  5. 05Overcoming the political deadlock of campaign finance reform requires building a non-partisan movement of citizens to challenge incumbents who benefit from the existing system of dependency.
