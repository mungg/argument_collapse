"While genetic engineering is a valuable scientific tool for food security, Ronald’s focus on technological fixes overlooks the systemic socio-economic barriers and alternative interventions that are often more practical for developing nations."

Pamela Ronald's essay takes on one of the most urgent questions facing developing countries: how to feed growing populations while preserving ecosystems and empowering smallholder farmers. As someone who has worked in agricultural development for two decades, I find much to admire in her scientific rigor and her commitment to food security. But I also see significant gaps in how her framework translates to the contexts where I work.

Ronald's examples are carefully chosen: Bt cotton in India, GE papaya in Hawaii, disease-resistant bananas in development. These are success stories—but they are also stories of externally developed technologies deployed in relatively favorable contexts. The reality of agricultural development is messier. Extension systems are underfunded. Infrastructure is lacking. Markets are thin. Political economies reward large-scale commercial agriculture over subsistence farmers. In these conditions, technology transfer is necessary but insufficient.

The example I find most revealing is golden rice. Ronald correctly notes that vitamin A deficiency kills thousands of children annually and blinds hundreds of thousands more. She is right that golden rice could help. But golden rice has been "almost ready" for deployment for over a decade, facing regulatory and public resistance. Why? Partly because of the anti-GMO activism Ronald decries. But partly because of legitimate questions about whether golden rice is the best use of scarce research and regulatory resources.

Vitamin A deficiency can be addressed through supplementation, fortification, and diet diversification—approaches that have demonstrated impact at scale and do not require controversial technology. The question is not whether golden rice could work but whether it is the optimal intervention when we have other tools available. Ronald frames alternatives as more expensive and less effective, but the comparison is more nuanced than she suggests.

More broadly, I worry about the dependency trap that technological fixes can create. When we develop GE seeds that address specific problems, we invest intellectual and financial capital in seeds rather than in the diverse, resilient farming systems that have sustained communities for generations. We create farmer demand for external inputs rather than cultivating local knowledge and innovation. We strengthen the research institutions of wealthy countries while neglecting the capacity of national agricultural systems in developing nations.

Genetic engineering should be part of the development toolkit. But it should be one tool among many, deployed alongside agroecological approaches, market development, and institutional reform. Ronald's essay does not give us this balanced view.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The success of GE crops is limited by real-world constraints in developing countries, such as underfunded extension systems, poor infrastructure, and political economies that favor large-scale commerce over subsistence farmers.
  2. 02The debate over biofortified crops like golden rice should consider whether they are the optimal use of scarce resources compared to proven, less controversial methods like diet diversification and supplementation.
  3. 03An over-reliance on externally developed GE seeds risks creating a dependency trap that prioritizes expensive external inputs over local knowledge and the development of national agricultural research systems.
  4. 04Genetic engineering should be treated as just one component of a broader strategy that includes agroecological approaches, market development, and institutional reform rather than a singular solution.
