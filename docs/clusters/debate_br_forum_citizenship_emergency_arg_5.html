Diversified Gemini

"The events of Flight 93 demonstrate that national defense should be a fundamental responsibility of an engaged citizenry rather than a centralized service outsourced to a professional military elite."

Elaine Scarry’s profound essay, “Citizenship in Emergency,” reaches deep into the American political tradition to unearth a vital concept that has been nearly forgotten in our modern era of professionalized warfare: civic republicanism. By analyzing the harrowing and heroic events of United Airlines Flight 93, Scarry reminds us that national defense is not merely a service provided by the government to its consumers; it is a fundamental responsibility of citizenship. In doing so, she offers a compelling critique not just of our military structure, but of the passive, spectator democracy we have allowed ourselves to become.

Since the end of the Cold War, and accelerating dramatically after September 11, Americans have been treated by their leaders largely as vulnerable children who must be shielded by a secretive, highly centralized security state. We are told to go shopping, to report suspicious packages, and to trust that the experts in the Pentagon, the CIA, and the newly proposed Department of Homeland Security will handle the rest. This paradigm completely strips the citizen of agency.

Scarry’s analysis of Flight 93 shatters this paradigm. She describes the plane as a “small piece of American territory” that was restored to the country by “civilian passengers who became citizen-soldiers.” Her detailing of their actions is striking in its democratic mechanics: they gathered intelligence from distributed sources, deliberated, took a vote, and executed a collective action. This was not anarchy, nor was it the blind following of a supreme leader. It was the purest manifestation of self-governance.

This model of the citizen-soldier is deeply rooted in the founding of the republic. The framers of the Constitution harbored a deep distrust of standing armies, which they associated with tyranny and imperial overreach. The Second Amendment—often misunderstood today as merely a right to individual self-defense or sport—was originally conceived to guarantee a distributed militia. The founders believed that a free state could only be defended by the people themselves, because a people who outsource their defense to a professional military class will inevitably lose control over their own government.

Scarry’s argument that we must return to an “egalitarian, distributed” model of defense challenges the moral atrophy of modern American citizenship. For decades, we have insulated ourselves from the costs of our foreign policies. We rely on an all-volunteer military, drawn heavily from rural areas and lower socio-economic classes, to fight our wars, while the vast majority of the population sacrifices nothing. As we contemplate a massive preemptive invasion of Iraq this fall, the general public faces no draft, no war taxes, and no personal risk. It is easy to cheer for war when someone else is doing the bleeding.

If we take Scarry’s thesis seriously, returning to a democratic form of defense means we must share the burden of violence. She bravely notes that this would “almost certainly... mean the return of a draft.” This is a radical proposal in 2002, but it is entirely consistent with civic republicanism. A draft ensures that the military is truly representative of the citizenry. If every member of Congress knew that their own children would be sent to the front lines of Baghdad, the threshold for military intervention would be rightfully insurmountable without overwhelming national consensus.

Furthermore, Scarry’s vision demands that we resist the current push to expand the secretive powers of the executive branch. The proposed total-information-awareness programs and the suspension of habeas corpus for alleged combatants are all justified by the "argument from speed" and the supposed need for supreme centralized authority. But as Flight 93 proved, citizens are highly capable of understanding threats and acting decisively without waiting for top-down orders.

We cannot expect to maintain a constitutional democracy if we treat national defense as a black box operated by elites. True security is not found in submitting to authoritarianism out of fear; it is found in the collective resilience and moral courage of a free people. Scarry’s essay is a brilliant reminder that the ultimate defense of the United States lies not in the supersonic speed of our missiles, but in the engaged, deliberative, and courageous actions of its citizens.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The actions of the passengers on Flight 93 represent the purest manifestation of democratic self-governance and intelligence-gathering.
  2. 02The modern security state infantilizes Americans by stripping them of agency and demanding passive trust in secretive government experts.
  3. 03A return to the 'citizen-soldier' model is necessary to honor the constitutional intent of a distributed militia and to prevent the tyranny associated with standing armies.
  4. 04Restoring a democratic form of defense, including the return of a military draft, would ensure that the burdens and risks of war are shared equally across all social classes.
  5. 05A representative military would create a higher, more appropriate threshold for military intervention by forcing leaders and citizens to personally weigh the costs of conflict.
  6. 06The 'argument from speed' used to justify expanded executive powers and secrecy is undermined by the fact that citizens are capable of decisive, decentralized action during emergencies.
Diversified Gemini

"The American public must reclaim its constitutional responsibility for national defense by rejecting the centralized, executive-led model of war-making that has bypassed democratic consent for decades."

In “Citizenship in Emergency,” Elaine Scarry captures a profound and unsettling truth about the American political condition in the aftermath of September 11: we have systematically ceded our constitutional responsibilities for national defense to an unaccountable executive branch. While military tacticians may quibble with her timeline of air defense failures on that terrible morning, Scarry’s deeper argument regarding the erosion of the Constitution’s war powers is both historically accurate and urgently necessary. Writing today, in October 2002, as the Bush administration demands a blank check for a preemptive war against Iraq, Scarry’s call to restore the “civil frame” of national defense has never been more vital.

The framers of the United States Constitution were deeply suspicious of standing armies and unchecked executive power. They deliberately divided the war powers, making the President the Commander in Chief of the armed forces but granting Congress alone the power to declare war. This was not a mere procedural hurdle; it was a profound moral and political safeguard. As Scarry points out, the requirement for a congressional declaration of war necessitates public debate. It forces the military ambitions of the state to be tested against the will, the conscience, and the blood of the citizenry who will be asked to fight, pay, and die for those ambitions.

Yet, for the past fifty years, this constitutional design has been treated as a quaint anachronism. Scarry correctly identifies the advent of the nuclear age and the doctrine of “speed” as the primary rationalizations for this unconstitutional drift. The argument has always been that the pace of modern warfare—specifically, the threat of incoming supersonic nuclear missiles—leaves no time for legislative deliberation. If the President must have the unilateral authority to end the world in twenty minutes, the logic goes, surely he does not need to consult Congress for lesser military interventions. This reasoning has licensed decades of executive war-making, from Korea and Vietnam to Panama, Grenada, and now, potentially, Baghdad.

The genius of Scarry’s essay is how she uses the events of September 11 to puncture the myth of centralized speed. The vast, multi-billion-dollar military apparatus, justified for decades by its ability to react in “a matter of minutes,” was completely paralyzed when faced with domestic hijacked airliners. Meanwhile, the passengers on Flight 93—acting as a microcosm of democratic deliberation—gathered information, debated their options, voted, and acted within a mere twenty-three minutes. Scarry does not suggest that citizens should literally fight all wars hand-to-hand; rather, she uses this juxtaposition as a powerful metaphor for the efficiency and moral necessity of democratic consent.

Currently, the nation is being hurried into a war with Iraq based on shifting rationales and classified intelligence that the public is not permitted to see. The Bush administration has articulated a new doctrine of preemptive war, claiming the right to strike first against potential threats before they materialize. This doctrine is the ultimate culmination of the top-down, authoritarian model Scarry critiques. It asks the American people to trust the instincts of a small cadre of executive branch officials and to surrender their right to democratic deliberation in the name of security.

But as Scarry reminds us, “what exactly do you ‘self-govern’ if you have ceded the governing of your own body and life to someone else?” A nation that fights wars without the informed consent of its people is no longer a functioning democracy; it is an empire managed by a national security elite. The costs of this arrangement are becoming increasingly obvious, not just abroad, but at home, where civil liberties are being quietly dismantled in the name of Homeland Security.

We must reject the false choice between safety and democracy. The Constitution does not require us to be defenseless; it requires us to be responsible. Returning to a constitutional model of war-making means that if the President wishes to invade Iraq, he must lay out the evidence, face rigorous questioning, and secure a formal declaration of war from the people’s representatives. It means reviving the concept of the citizen-soldier, ensuring that the burdens of defense are shared equitably across society rather than outsourced to a professional warrior caste drawn disproportionately from the working class.

Scarry’s essay is a wake-up call. The failure to defend the Pentagon on September 11 was a tragedy, but the continued abdication of our constitutional duties will be a catastrophe. We cannot allow the trauma of that day to be used as an excuse to finalize the centralization of war-making power in the Oval Office. If we wish to honor the democratic spirit demonstrated by the passengers of Flight 93, we must reclaim our role as active participants in the defense of our own republic. We must demand that the laws of our nation be followed, that our representatives debate openly, and that the awesome power of war be returned to the civil frame where it belongs.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The constitutional requirement for a congressional declaration of war is a vital moral safeguard that ensures military actions are tested against the conscience and will of the citizenry.
  2. 02The 'argument from speed' used to justify unilateral executive power is a myth, as evidenced by the failure of the centralized military apparatus on September 11 compared to the swift, deliberative action of the passengers on Flight 93.
  3. 03The Bush administration's doctrine of preemptive war represents the dangerous culmination of an authoritarian defense model that demands blind trust in a small elite rather than informed public debate.
  4. 04True self-governance is impossible if citizens cede the authority over their own lives and the decision to go to war to an unaccountable executive branch.
  5. 05Restoring the 'civil frame' of defense requires reviving the concept of the citizen-soldier and ensuring that the burdens of national security are shared equitably across all of society.
