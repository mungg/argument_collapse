"The United States should stop treating Russia as a central foreign policy priority and instead adopt a transactional approach that recognizes Russia as a declining power."

The United States should worry less about Russia than it usually does. Not ignore it, not humiliate it, and not pretend its nuclear arsenal is irrelevant — but stop treating Moscow as the central organizing problem of American foreign policy.

Russia today is a declining petro-state with a great-power memory. Its nominal economy is smaller than those of several American allies. Its budget is tied to oil prices. Capital flight, corruption and weak rule of law limit its ability to become a magnet for talent and investment. Its conventional military is being modernized, but it is not the Red Army. It can defeat Georgia; it cannot project power globally the way the United States can. Its alliances are thin, and its soft power is thinner.

The election itself demonstrates weakness as much as strength. A genuinely confident government would not need such a skewed political field, state television dominance, administrative pressure and disputed vote counts. Putin won, but the protests in Moscow and other cities show that the old bargain — stability in exchange for political passivity — is fraying among Russia’s urban middle class.

Russia’s Security Council vote is important, but a veto is not a strategy. It can block action on Syria, slow pressure on Iran, and make diplomatic life difficult. It cannot build a durable international order or attract broad voluntary followership. Even the gas weapon is constrained. Gazprom needs European revenue at least as much as Europe needs Russian gas, and repeated coercion only encourages diversification.

America’s larger strategic challenge lies with rising powers, especially China, whose economic weight and military modernization are reshaping Asia. India, Brazil and Turkey also represent a world in which influence is increasingly dispersed. Russia is part of that world, but it is not the future of it.

The right Russia policy is transactional: cooperate on arms control and nonproliferation, push back against bullying of neighbors, speak honestly about fraud and rights abuses, and avoid grand ideological crusades. Russia can irritate the United States. It cannot replace the Soviet Union, and Washington should not help Putin pretend otherwise.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Russia's economy is overly dependent on oil prices and suffers from systemic issues like corruption and capital flight that limit its global influence.
  2. 02The Russian military lacks the capacity for global power projection and its soft power is significantly diminished compared to the Soviet era.
  3. 03The recent election and subsequent protests indicate that the Russian government's domestic stability is fraying and relies on administrative pressure rather than genuine confidence.
  4. 04Russia's diplomatic influence is primarily obstructive rather than constructive, as it can veto international actions but cannot build a durable global order.
  5. 05The 'gas weapon' is limited by Russia's own economic dependence on European energy revenue and the resulting push for energy diversification.
  6. 06The United States faces more significant long-term strategic challenges from rising powers like China, India, and Brazil than from a declining Russia.
