"An effective domestic intelligence system must be built on a foundation of federalism that treats local police and community trust as essential infrastructure rather than obstacles to security."

Daniel Richman is right to insist that the argument about domestic intelligence cannot be divided into two neat piles: effectiveness over here, liberties over there. That division has governed too much debate since September 11. Civil libertarians ask how to restrain government; security officials ask how to find the next plot. Each side suspects the other of missing the point. Richman’s great virtue is to show that the two questions are institutionally connected. A domestic intelligence system that alienates the communities from which it needs information will be less effective, not merely less just. And a system that incorporates local police departments, local elected officials, and local community relationships may be not only more legitimate but also smarter.

That proposition is especially important because the most common metaphor of the last three years—“connecting the dots”—is dangerously incomplete. It suggests that the dots already exist, lying in files somewhere, waiting for an analyst with the right database access. Sometimes that is true. The 9/11 Commission showed that fragments of information were present in the system before the attacks and were not properly shared or interpreted. But future prevention will not depend only on better plumbing between federal agencies. Many of the most important dots will never appear unless someone is willing to speak to a police officer, report suspicious conduct, explain a local dispute, or distinguish between ordinary religious conservatism and something more ominous. Intelligence is not only collected; it is elicited. And it is elicited from human beings who make judgments about whether government can be trusted.

That is why the use of immigration enforcement as a counterterrorism dragnet is so damaging. It may produce numbers: arrests, interviews, absconder warrants, detentions. But numbers are not the same as intelligence. If a local officer in Dearborn, Queens, Paterson, or Falls Church becomes, in the eyes of immigrant residents, an adjunct of deportation, then he loses access to precisely the relationships that might matter most in a crisis. The International Association of Chiefs of Police understood this point not as an abstract civil-liberties slogan but as a matter of police craft. People who fear that any contact with law enforcement may destroy their family will not call when they are victims, will not serve as witnesses, and will not volunteer delicate information about acquaintances.

Richman’s most useful move is to treat local reluctance not as obstructionism but as knowledge. Local police know things federal agents often do not know: which community leaders are broadly respected and which are self-appointed; which tensions are political, sectarian, generational, or criminal; which rumors are significant and which are merely the noise of neighborhood life. They also know that the same community may be simultaneously a source of vulnerability and a source of protection. Arab-American and Muslim communities are not “problems” to be surveilled; they are American communities whose members may be among the first to notice a real danger. Treating them as suspect classes is both unjust and foolish.

Still, Richman’s argument should not be read as a hymn to local virtue. American police departments have their own history of abuse: Red Squads, infiltration of lawful protest, racial profiling, brutality, and intelligence files built on ideology rather than crime. Community policing has improved some departments, but it has not transformed all of them. In many cities, “community policing” remains more rhetoric than reality, and even where it is real, communities are internally unequal. A police chief who consults a few prominent clergy or business leaders may miss youth voices, women’s organizations, political dissidents, or recent immigrants. Local accountability can correct federal excess; it can also reproduce local prejudice.

The solution, then, is not to romanticize local police but to use federalism as a system of friction. Richman’s strongest insight is that friction can be valuable. A domestic intelligence apparatus should not be frictionless. The federal government needs local knowledge; local governments need federal information and resources. Each level should be able to say no, ask why, demand justification, and insist on procedures. If every federal initiative flows downward as a command, we will get resentment and evasion. If every local department can opt out entirely, we will get gaps that terrorists may exploit. The point is negotiated cooperation.

What would negotiated cooperation require? First, the federal government must stop treating immigration enforcement as the default instrument of counterterrorism. Immigration violations may sometimes be relevant. No serious person would argue that local police should ignore a dangerous person merely because the initial legal hook is immigration-related. But broad mandates requiring local enforcement of civil immigration law are likely to produce a net intelligence loss. The question should not be, “Do local officers have legal authority to make these arrests?” It should be, “Will making local officers responsible for these arrests improve security?” In many places the answer will be no.

Second, information sharing must be genuinely two-way. Local officers have complained since 2001 that they are asked to provide information but receive little useful intelligence in return. Federal secrecy is sometimes necessary, but secrecy can also become bureaucratic habit. If local departments are expected to help identify threats, they need timely, usable, and appropriately filtered information about what to look for. Vague alerts that raise overtime costs without operational guidance do little except exhaust city budgets and public patience.

Third, homeland-security funding should reflect risk and responsibility. Richman rightly notes the absurdity of per-capita distributions that leave high-risk urban areas undercompensated while low-risk states receive windfalls. Of course every state has vulnerabilities: chemical plants, reservoirs, ports, symbolic sites, transportation systems. But the distribution of federal money has been distorted by congressional politics. If localities are to bear the costs of prevention—police overtime, target hardening, community outreach, emergency preparation—then funding should be tied more closely to threat, consequence, and demonstrated need.

Fourth, local oversight should be incorporated rather than evaded. The clashes in Portland and Boise over federal restrictions and civilian review point to a fundamental issue: participation in national intelligence work cannot become a device for escaping local democratic controls. If a city has adopted rules limiting political surveillance, those rules deserve respect. If they are too rigid to permit necessary counterterrorism work, local officials should debate and revise them openly. The answer cannot be secret federal deputation that places local officers beyond local accountability.

Finally, both federal and local officials must distinguish intelligence from suspicion. A national intelligence network will fail if it encourages officers to treat religious observance, ethnicity, foreign travel, or dissent as proxies for danger. The great difficulty of counterterrorism is that genuine warning signs may be subtle, ambiguous, and embedded in lawful conduct. But that difficulty makes discipline more important, not less. Training, supervision, auditing, and clear standards for retaining and disseminating information are indispensable.

The domestic intelligence problem is not going away. The Madrid train bombings this year were a reminder that open societies remain vulnerable; the 9/11 Commission was right to say that the threat is real and continuing. But fear is a poor architect. If we build a system that bypasses local knowledge, treats immigrant communities as targets, and measures success by arrests rather than trusted information, we will have created a machine that is both heavy-handed and dull-witted.

Richman’s federalism is not a cure-all. It cannot by itself prevent abuse, guarantee competence, or resolve the tension between secrecy and democracy. But it identifies the right fight: not liberty against security, but intelligent security against self-defeating coercion. The federal government needs local police because they know the terrain. Local police need the federal government because terrorism is not merely local crime. And the country needs both to remember that trust is not a sentimental luxury. It is infrastructure.

"To build an effective and legitimate American counterterrorism network, the U.S. must institutionalize a 'golden thread' of cooperation where local police provide social knowledge and community mediation while federal agencies provide national analysis and legal boundaries."

Daniel Richman invokes the British “golden thread” linking local police Special Branches with national security agencies. The comparison is suggestive, but it should be used carefully. Britain’s experience with Irish republican terrorism, and more recently with Islamist threats, shows both the necessity of local intelligence and the dangers of heavy-handed security policy. The lesson is not simply that local police should mediate between national agencies and communities. It is that legitimacy is an operational asset that can be squandered for years.

The United States is not Britain. We have a written Constitution, a federal system, thousands of law-enforcement agencies, a stronger tradition of local policing, and a different history of immigration and race. We also lack a single national police structure. But the underlying counterterrorism problem is similar: violent clandestine networks hide among much larger communities that are not themselves the enemy. The state must find the few without alienating the many. That is the central dilemma Richman addresses.

In Northern Ireland, British authorities learned repeatedly that coercive measures could produce short-term gains and long-term damage. Internment without trial in the early 1970s, aggressive military tactics, and abusive interrogation practices deepened nationalist hostility and aided recruitment by the Irish Republican Army. Intelligence eventually became more refined, and local knowledge was indispensable. But legitimacy deficits haunted the security effort. The lesson for the United States is obvious enough: if Arab and Muslim communities come to see counterterrorism as collective punishment or political surveillance, the government will lose cooperation and may strengthen the narratives it seeks to defeat.

At the same time, the British experience also cautions against an overly romantic view of community relations. Local knowledge can be parochial, biased, or compromised. Community leaders may not know what younger radicals are doing; they may be afraid to say; or they may have their own political reasons for shaping what police hear. Informants may be valuable but morally and legally complicated. Intelligence work is full of ambiguity. A friendly relationship with respected community figures is not a substitute for disciplined analysis, lawful surveillance, and national coordination.

Richman’s essay is strongest when it identifies the misalignment between federal and local incentives. Terrorism prevention is a national good, while many costs are local: damaged trust, overtime, protests, lawsuits, and strained immigrant relations. In Britain, a more centralized system can impose national priorities more directly. In the United States, federalism creates bargaining. Richman sees promise in that bargaining because local police may force federal agencies to moderate. I agree, but only if the bargaining is institutionalized. Ad hoc resistance will not suffice.

An American “golden thread” should have four strands.

The first is federal analytic responsibility. Terrorist threats often cross borders, both national and state. Only federal agencies can integrate foreign intelligence, visa data, financial information, watchlists, and multi-state investigations. Local police cannot be expected to determine whether a local observation fits an international pattern. The FBI and the Department of Homeland Security must therefore provide analysis that is timely and usable. They should not simply collect from localities; they should interpret and return value.

The second strand is local relational responsibility. Local police, city officials, school administrators, and community organizations understand the social terrain. They know when a federal request will be perceived as a fishing expedition. They know which mosques have been threatened, which immigrant businesses are afraid, which rumors are spreading, and which youth are alienated from both parents and public institutions. This information is not “soft” in the pejorative sense. It is often the difference between a lead that can be pursued quietly and a blunder that makes the evening news.

The third strand is legal constraint. Britain’s unwritten constitutional tradition and emergency legislation have sometimes allowed troubling elasticity. The United States should not imitate that. Our domestic counterterrorism system must have clear rules on political and religious surveillance, data retention, immigration cooperation, undercover activity, and task-force accountability. Richman emphasizes institutional structure because courts and legislatures cannot supervise every discretionary decision. True, but rules shape discretion before it is exercised. They also create a standard for after-the-fact review.

The fourth strand is civic reciprocity. Communities asked to cooperate must see that government protects them as well as investigates them. After September 11, Muslim, Arab, and Sikh Americans faced harassment and violence. Visible protection of mosques, prosecution of hate crimes, and respectful engagement are not public-relations extras. They are part of the legitimacy bargain. A community that sees police only when federal agents want interviews will not believe it is an equal partner.

What would this look like in practice? Joint Terrorism Task Forces should remain, but they should not be the only model of cooperation. JTTFs are useful for investigations and operations; they are less suited to broad community trust. Local departments need separate community liaison structures whose purpose is not simply intelligence collection. These officers should be trained in civil rights, cultural competence, and threat recognition. They should be able to bring community concerns upward, not merely bring federal requests downward.

There should also be regional intelligence centers with participation from federal, state, and local agencies, but governed by privacy policies. Their job should be to assess credible threat information, protect infrastructure, and coordinate preparedness—not to accumulate files on lawful political activity. Reports should include source reliability, factual basis, and retention rules. Analysts should be trained to distinguish ideology from operational indicators. A fiery sermon, a political argument about Iraq or Palestine, or conservative religious practice is not evidence of terrorism.

Immigration enforcement should be handled with particular care. Britain has faced its own tensions over immigrant communities and national identity, and European governments more broadly have often struggled with integration. The United States has an advantage: a civic national identity that can incorporate immigrants more readily, at least in principle. We should not throw that advantage away by making local police into immigration agents. Federal immigration authorities have their role, especially in cases involving fraud, smuggling, or security threats. But routine civil immigration enforcement by local police risks converting potential partners into fugitives.

Funding must support the model. The British system’s “golden thread” depends on trained personnel and institutional continuity. In the United States, many local departments cannot sustain specialized counterterrorism work without federal support. Grants should be risk-based and should support personnel, training, translation, community liaison, and interoperable communications. Equipment alone will not create intelligence capacity. Nor will statewide plans that neglect the needs of major cities.

The American system should also learn from mistakes in threat communication. Vague national alerts can exhaust local agencies and frighten the public without improving preparedness. Threat information should be specific when possible: sectors, tactics, indicators, protective measures. If specificity is impossible, federal officials should be honest about uncertainty. Credibility is finite. Repeated alerts without explanation may lead local officials and citizens to tune out.

Richman’s optimism about local accountability is warranted only if we remember that local police are not naturally trusted by all communities. In some neighborhoods, the federal government may actually be viewed as less biased than local authorities. In others, both are distrusted. Building legitimacy will require more than assigning officers to liaison posts. It will require complaint mechanisms, transparency about rules, consequences for misconduct, and a willingness to hear criticism of foreign and domestic policy without treating criticism itself as suspicious.

The comparative lesson, then, is balance. Centralized systems can become insulated and coercive. Decentralized systems can become fragmented and uneven. Community engagement can generate trust, but it can also become tokenism or intelligence cover. Legal rules can protect liberty, but they can also be evaded through secrecy unless oversight is real. No single institutional design will solve the dilemma.

Richman is right that the United States must not build a federally dominated domestic intelligence system that ignores local realities. He is also right that local governments should participate constructively rather than merely condemn from the sidelines. But the participation must be principled. The “golden thread” should not be a pipeline through which information flows upward and commands flow downward. It should be a two-way constitutional relationship: federal agencies provide national perspective; local agencies provide social knowledge; law provides boundaries; communities provide consent and criticism.

The threat of terrorism is serious and will likely remain so for many years. A frightened democracy may be tempted by shortcuts: mass interviews, immigration sweeps, secret files, broad surveillance, and symbolic toughness. Other countries’ experiences suggest that such shortcuts often lengthen the road. The patient work of legitimate intelligence is slower, more disciplined, and more accountable. It is also more likely to succeed.

"To move beyond the theoretical benefits of federalism in counterterrorism, the United States must transition from improvised cooperation to a professionalized, operationally disciplined partnership between federal and local law enforcement."

As a practical matter, the debate over domestic intelligence often sounds too grand. We speak of federalism, liberty, secrecy, accountability, and national strategy. All of that matters. But the success or failure of counterterrorism cooperation will often turn on mundane questions: Who gets the call at 2 a.m.? Does the patrol officer know what information is worth reporting? Does the FBI give feedback? Can the city’s radio system talk to the county’s? Does the local detective assigned to a task force know whether city rules still apply? Are tips recorded in a way that can be evaluated later? Does anyone tell community leaders what happened after a disruptive interview campaign?

Daniel Richman’s essay is valuable because it connects these practicalities to democratic structure. He argues that local police are not merely extra manpower for federal agencies; they are holders of relationships, local knowledge, and political accountability. That is true. But to make the idea work, we need less abstraction and more operational discipline. Partnership cannot be improvised case by case. It requires rules, training, and habits.

The first operational problem is information quality. Since September 11, “information sharing” has become a mantra. But sharing bad information widely can be worse than not sharing at all. Local police may send in vague tips based on stereotypes, personal grudges, or misunderstandings. Federal agencies may send down warnings so general that they cannot guide action. Analysts may be overwhelmed by noise. The lesson of September 11 was not simply that agencies failed to share; it was that they failed to understand what mattered. A domestic network must therefore emphasize standards: what should be reported, what facts must accompany a report, how reliability is assessed, how long information is retained, and when it is purged.

The second problem is feedback. Local officers are more likely to participate intelligently if they learn what happens to the information they provide. If a patrol officer reports suspicious photography near a bridge and never hears anything again, he may either stop reporting or report everything. Neither is useful. Federal agencies cannot disclose every investigation, but they can provide aggregated feedback, training examples, and case studies. They can tell local partners which patterns have mattered and which have not. Intelligence is a craft; it improves through learning.

The third problem is the boundary between community contact and intelligence collection. Beat officers and community liaison officers should not be turned into amateur spies. Their value lies in ordinary relationships. If every conversation becomes a report, those relationships will collapse. Departments need clear guidance: a resident’s political anger, religious conservatism, or criticism of U.S. foreign policy is not intelligence. A specific threat, procurement of suspicious materials, document fraud tied to a network, or an expressed desire to commit violence may be. The line will not always be easy, but pretending there is no line is disastrous.

The fourth problem is immigration. Richman is right that routine local enforcement of civil immigration law can damage policing. From an operational standpoint, it also consumes time. Street officers are not immigration lawyers. Determining status can be complicated, and detaining people for civil violations can pull officers away from urgent local duties. Worse, it may dry up witnesses and informants. Cooperation with immigration authorities should be targeted and supervised. If immigration information is relevant to a terrorism or serious criminal investigation, there should be a process for involving federal specialists. But turning every traffic stop into a status inquiry is not counterterrorism; it is a recipe for alienation and inefficiency.

The fifth problem is task-force governance. Joint Terrorism Task Forces are indispensable in many cities. They allow federal agents and local detectives to sit together, share leads, and coordinate operations. But they also create accountability confusion. A local officer assigned to a JTTF may have access to federal information and may operate under federal direction. What happens if city policy prohibits certain intelligence practices? What happens if a civilian review board asks about a complaint? What happens when federal secrecy rules conflict with local transparency? These questions should be answered in written agreements before controversies arise. The agreements should specify command structure, applicable rules, reporting obligations, complaint procedures, and the circumstances under which local executives are briefed.

The sixth problem is training. Counterterrorism training must be more than a list of suspicious behaviors. Poor training can encourage officers to equate Islam with extremism, photography with surveillance, or foreignness with danger. Good training should include legal standards, cultural competence, behavioral indicators, intelligence reporting, civil liberties, and case-based exercises. It should also be honest about uncertainty. Officers should be encouraged to ask: What exactly is suspicious here? What facts support it? Is there an innocent explanation? What additional information would clarify the situation? Suspicion should be disciplined, not theatrical.

The seventh problem is community reassurance after enforcement action. Sometimes aggressive action will be necessary. A raid, arrest, or urgent interview may disrupt a community and produce fear. When operationally possible, local officials should be prepared to explain what they can, correct rumors, protect against backlash, and distinguish individual allegations from collective suspicion. Silence may be legally convenient, but it leaves a vacuum. In that vacuum, fear grows.

The eighth problem is measurement. We measure ordinary policing poorly enough; measuring prevention is harder. If nothing happens, was an attack prevented or was there no plot? Arrest numbers may reward low-level immigration cases rather than genuine security gains. Tip volume may reward over-reporting. Equipment purchases may reward spending rather than preparedness. We need better measures: quality of threat reporting, response times, interoperability, exercise performance, community willingness to report crimes, hate-crime response, and after-action reviews. Some measures will be imperfect, but without them agencies will chase appearances.

Richman’s federalism can help with all of this because local police bring ground truth. A federal plan may look good in Washington and fail in Dearborn, Portland, Las Vegas, or Queens. Local officials can say, “This interview list is too broad,” “This alert is unusable,” “This mosque needs protection,” “This community leader does not speak for the young people,” or “This raid will compromise a local investigation.” But local officials also need to accept responsibility. They cannot simply complain that the FBI does not share. They must build their own capacity to receive, evaluate, and protect information. They must train officers. They must adopt privacy policies. They must engage communities before a crisis.

The best domestic intelligence system would have several layers. At the federal level, agencies would integrate national and international information, set priorities, and provide classified threat assessments. At the regional level, multi-agency centers would analyze patterns, coordinate infrastructure protection, and connect neighboring jurisdictions. At the local level, police would maintain community relationships, report specific concerns, protect targets, and respond to incidents. Oversight would exist at each level: inspectors general and congressional committees federally, governors and legislatures at the state level, mayors and councils locally.

But no chart will solve the cultural problem. Federal agents must stop treating local officers as either unsophisticated tip collectors or obstacles. Local officers must stop assuming that federal secrecy is always arrogance. Community leaders must be treated as partners, not props. And civil liberties lawyers must be included early enough to shape rules, not merely sue after damage is done.

The attacks of September 11 created pressure to act quickly. Some improvisation was inevitable. Three years later, improvisation is no longer an excuse. We know the basic tensions: secrecy and oversight, immigration and trust, national risk and local cost, information sharing and information quality. The task now is to professionalize the partnership.

Richman is right that local police can make counterterrorism more accountable. But they will do so only if their participation is structured. Otherwise, we will get the worst of both worlds: federal secrecy combined with local reach, broad collection combined with weak analysis, and community outreach combined with quiet surveillance. The right fight is to build a system that is disciplined enough to be useful and restrained enough to be trusted. In policing, those two qualities are not enemies. They are the same craft practiced well.

"The federal government should embrace a collaborative federalism model that empowers local police as genuine partners in counterterrorism, leveraging their unique community trust and granular knowledge to create a more effective and accountable intelligence network."

Daniel Richman has given us a rare thing in the post-9/11 debate: a genuinely constructive proposal that refuses to treat security and liberty as a zero-sum game. His central insight—that the very structure of federalism, properly harnessed, can make domestic intelligence both more effective and more accountable—deserves far more attention than it has received. Too often, the conversation has been dominated by absolutists on both sides: those who see any local resistance as sabotage, and those who see any federal coordination as a police state in embryo. Richman charts a middle path that is at once pragmatic and principled. I wish to build on his argument by exploring more deeply why local police are uniquely positioned to reconcile the competing demands of counterterrorism and democratic accountability, and by suggesting some practical measures that could bring his “optimistic story” closer to reality.

The past two decades have witnessed a transformation in urban policing so profound that it is almost unrecognizable from the era of COINTELPRO. The old model—reactive, incident-driven, often brutal—has been slowly supplanted, though not entirely replaced, by what its practitioners call community- or problem-oriented policing. This shift, associated with the work of Herman Goldstein, James Q. Wilson, and George Kelling, among others, rests on the recognition that effective crime control depends on the active cooperation of the communities being policed. Officers on the beat are no longer merely responders to 911 calls; they are expected to build sustained relationships with neighborhood leaders, to attend community meetings, to solve the “little” problems—the abandoned cars, the disorderly bars—that erode public trust. This approach has been driven not primarily by altruism, but by the hard-nosed realization that without community trust, the flow of information that solves crimes dries up.

Richman is right to identify this as the source of local police’s comparative advantage. Federal agents, no matter how well trained or intentioned, are transients. They swoop into a neighborhood for a specific investigation, and then they leave. They do not walk the streets at night, respond to domestic disturbances, or help find lost children. The beat cop, by contrast, is a permanent presence, often a known face. It is the beat cop who knows which storekeeper is worried about his son’s friends, which imam has credibility, which community leader is respected and which is a blowhard. This kind of granular, contextual knowledge cannot be acquired through “outreach meetings” organized by the FBI’s public affairs office; it is the accumulated capital of years on the same beat.

This is why, when the Justice Department’s post-9/11 interview drive came to Ann Arbor, Michigan, and Dearborn, local Arab-American leaders specifically requested that local officers be present. They trusted the local police—imperfectly, perhaps, but far more than they trusted the federal agents who appeared at their doors asking questions about political and religious activities. As Chief Daniel Oates of Ann Arbor later recounted, the local officers were able to serve as a familiar buffer, reducing the intimidation inherent in the encounter. That is a small but telling example of the “mediation” Richman describes.

But this role is not merely a passive one. Richman suggests that local departments should “exact a toll” from federal authorities as the price of their cooperation. I would go further and argue that the most significant toll they can exact is the demand to be genuine partners in defining the intelligence mission itself. At present, the flow of tasks is overwhelmingly top-down: Washington decides what threats to watch for, and local officers are asked to be eyes and ears. But local police are far better placed to know what kinds of inquiries will damage community relations and what alternative approaches might yield better information with less harm. A truly collaborative network would involve local chiefs in setting priorities and crafting operational guidelines. The nascent National Criminal Intelligence Sharing Plan, spearheaded by the International Association of Chiefs of Police, gestures in this direction, but it remains a consultative document, not a binding framework.

Why should the federal government agree to such shared governance? Because the alternative is a dried-up well. Intelligence is not like a mineral that can be extracted by force; it is a human product that depends on willing cooperation. As Richman notes, every felony arrest is an intelligence opportunity. But if that arrest is perceived as a pretext for immigration enforcement or as part of a broader system of suspicion directed at entire communities, the opportunity vanishes. The officer who is seen as a spy loses the ability to be a peacekeeper, and thereby loses the intelligence that comes from peacekeeping. The “chilling effect” the IACP warns about is not a liberal abstraction; it translates directly into lost information about the local criminals—including, potentially, the terrorists embedded among them.

I do not wish to be naive. The community policing revolution is still incomplete and has had its share of failures and public-relations exercises. The persistence of racial profiling, brutality scandals, and “blue walls of silence” in many departments is undeniable. But the direction of change is real, and it has created both a new professional ethos and, for the first time, a widespread institutional recognition that legitimacy is an operational necessity. This is what separates our moment from 1968. The police intelligence units of that era were detached from the regular patrol and community functions; they could be recruited into COINTELPRO precisely because their work had no bearing on the daily task of keeping neighborhoods safe. Today, the same officers who would be asked to gather intelligence are the ones who depend on the goodwill of the community to solve burglaries and quell gang violence. Their institutional self-interest is not to alienate that community. The tension Richman describes is not a problem to be solved; it is a productive friction to be sustained.

To move toward the happier ending, three concrete steps are needed. First, Congress should pass legislation explicitly authorizing local-federal intelligence partnerships but conditioning substantial federal funding on the existence of local civilian oversight mechanisms that have access—if only through cleared designees—to the broad outlines of local intelligence activities. This would resolve the conflict Richman notes between federal secrecy rules and local accountability, turning it into a catalyst for stronger oversight. Second, the Justice Department should issue binding regulations requiring federal agents to consult with local chiefs before undertaking any broad intelligence-gathering initiative in their jurisdictions, and to give those chiefs a meaningful opportunity to suggest modifications. Third, we need a major investment in joint training: not just teaching locals how to write intelligence reports, but teaching federal agents how community policing works and why it matters.

None of this is a guarantee against abuse. But perfect is the enemy of good, and the only available alternatives to Richman’s cooperative framework are far worse: either a federalized domestic intelligence agency that lacks local roots entirely, or a chaotic patchwork in which some cities refuse to cooperate and others cooperate too eagerly, with no democratic constraint either way. Better to embrace the messiness of federalism, and to trust that the daily negotiations between a beat cop and the people on his beat can do more to temper state power than any number of blue-ribbon commissions. The right fight, as Richman shows, is not between security and liberty, but between a centralized surveillance state and a distributed, accountable, and therefore more effective intelligence network. We should take up that fight.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Local police possess a comparative advantage over federal agents because their permanent presence and community-policing relationships allow them to gather granular, contextual intelligence that transients cannot access.
  2. 02The institutional self-interest of modern local police departments acts as a safeguard against federal overreach, as officers must maintain community legitimacy to effectively solve local crimes and maintain order.
  3. 03A truly effective intelligence network requires a bottom-up approach where local chiefs help set priorities and craft operational guidelines rather than merely executing top-down federal mandates.
  4. 04To formalize this partnership, federal funding should be conditioned on local civilian oversight, and federal agents should be legally required to consult with local authorities before launching broad intelligence initiatives.
  5. 05The 'productive friction' between federal security needs and local community sensitivities is a structural benefit of federalism that prevents the creation of a centralized surveillance state.
