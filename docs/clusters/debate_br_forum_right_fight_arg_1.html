"Effective counterterrorism intelligence depends on local police maintaining trust with immigrant communities by acting as mediators who resist federal tactics that prioritize mass surveillance and immigration enforcement over community safety."

The most important sentence in Daniel Richman’s essay may be his warning that turning the beat cop into “a potential source of personal ruin” will produce a net intelligence loss. For Arab, Muslim, South Asian, and immigrant communities in the United States, this is not an abstraction. Since September 11, many families have learned that a knock on the door from government may mean an interview, a visa inquiry, a detention, a frozen bank account, a workplace raid, or a humiliating public suspicion that never quite disappears. In that environment, “information sharing” can sound less like public safety than like surveillance.

Richman deserves credit for recognizing that immigrant communities are not merely objects of counterterrorism policy. They are participants in the safety of the country. Their members work in airports, drive taxis, run shops, teach students, serve in the military, worship in mosques, and raise children who are as American as anyone else’s. They are likely to be among the first to notice if a troubled young man is being drawn into violence, if a charity has been captured by extremists, or if a newcomer’s story does not add up. But people share such concerns only when they believe government will distinguish between a genuine threat and a community’s ordinary life.

The federal government has too often failed that test. The post-9/11 interviews of thousands of Middle Eastern men, the special registration program, the detention of immigration violators under a cloud of terrorism suspicion, and public rhetoric that blurs Islam, immigration, and security have all sent the same message: your status is conditional, your associations suspect, your mistakes dangerous. Even when officials insist that they are merely enforcing neutral laws, the pattern is obvious to those on the receiving end. When enforcement concentrates on particular nationalities and religions, neutrality is hard to credit.

Richman’s argument is that local police can mediate this relationship. In some places, they already have. A local officer who has helped a mosque with traffic during Friday prayers, responded to vandalism, protected a business, or attended neighborhood meetings may be trusted in ways the FBI is not. Local departments have practical reasons to preserve that trust. They need witnesses to robberies, domestic violence reports, cooperation in gang investigations, and calm during international crises. They cannot afford to have entire neighborhoods go silent.

But mediation is not automatic. It depends on the character of the local department and the rules under which it operates. An officer cannot build trust on Monday and participate in an immigration sweep on Tuesday. A police chief cannot ask community leaders for cooperation while refusing to tell them whether local officers are collecting political or religious information for federal files. A mayor cannot boast of inclusion while allowing residents to be questioned because of their ethnicity rather than individualized suspicion. Trust is not created by outreach meetings alone. It is created by restraint when restraint is costly.

Immigrant communities are often told that if they have nothing to hide, they have nothing to fear. That slogan misunderstands both immigration law and human experience. Many families include people of mixed status. Some have pending applications, old paperwork errors, overstayed visas, or relatives who are undocumented. Others are citizens but fear being drawn into investigations they do not understand. Some come from countries where police and intelligence services are instruments of repression. Some have experienced discrimination in the United States. Fear is not proof of guilt. It is often the rational response of people who know that bureaucracies make mistakes and that the costs of those mistakes fall on the weak.

The challenge is to design counterterrorism cooperation that treats communities as citizens and neighbors, not as mines from which information is extracted. Several principles follow.

First, local police should not enforce civil immigration law except in narrow circumstances tied to serious crime or specific security threats. This is not “sanctuary” for terrorism; it is sound policing. If a domestic violence victim fears deportation, she will not call. If a store owner believes reporting extortion may expose his cousin, he may stay quiet. If a mosque leader thinks a vague concern about a visitor will bring immigration agents down on half the congregation, he may handle the matter privately. That is bad for everyone.

Second, interviews should be based on facts, not categories. After September 11, federal authorities sought interviews with thousands of men largely because of age, gender, nationality, and immigration status. Such programs may look productive from Washington, but on the ground they announce that identity itself is suspicious. A targeted interview based on a lead is one thing; a census of fear is another.

Third, community outreach must be separated from intelligence recruitment. Of course relationships sometimes generate information, and no one should pretend otherwise. But if every town-hall meeting is perceived as an intelligence operation, the meetings will fail. Communities need spaces where they can raise civil rights complaints, hate crimes, school bullying, airport profiling, and immigration concerns without being treated as sources to be cultivated. Federal and local officials should listen even when the criticism is uncomfortable.

Fourth, local oversight matters. Richman is right that cities and counties can speak for local concerns more legitimately than distant federal agencies. But “community” must be understood broadly. Government should not rely only on a handful of male religious leaders or business elites. Muslim and Arab communities are internally diverse: black Muslims, immigrants from many countries, converts, secular Arabs, women’s organizations, students, professionals, recent refugees, and second-generation Americans. Some may distrust one another. If law enforcement hears only from the most convenient intermediaries, it will misunderstand the community and miss important information.

Fifth, anti-discrimination enforcement should be part of counterterrorism strategy. When mosques are vandalized, women wearing hijab are harassed, or students are bullied, public officials should respond visibly. Protecting communities from backlash is not a favor; it is a statement that they belong. A resident who sees police guarding a mosque during a tense period may be more willing to believe that the same police department is concerned with safety rather than suspicion.

Richman’s essay perhaps underplays the emotional reality of the last three years. For many Muslim Americans, the threat is twofold: fear of terrorism and fear of being blamed for terrorism. They watched the towers fall with horror like other Americans. Then many watched their neighbors look at them differently. Children were taunted. Charities were scrutinized. Travelers were pulled aside. Men disappeared into detention. Public officials sometimes spoke carefully, but the machinery of enforcement spoke more loudly.

That experience matters because counterterrorism depends on legitimacy. A government that wants help must show that it can be fair. It must admit mistakes. It must punish abuse. It must avoid public announcements that imply guilt before evidence is tested. It must recognize that the Constitution is not a technical obstacle but the common ground on which diverse communities can cooperate.

Local police can help, but only if they are willing to say no to federal practices that would destroy their relationships. The most valuable local contribution may not be more names in a database. It may be a warning to Washington: this tactic will make us less safe; this raid will silence witnesses; this interview program will stigmatize children; this demand will turn partners into suspects. If federal agencies listen, they may gain something more valuable than compliance. They may gain credibility.

The fight against terrorism is real. The Madrid bombings this year reminded everyone that civilians remain targets. No responsible community leader can ignore the danger of political violence committed in the name of Islam or any other cause. But a strategy that pressures Muslim and immigrant communities while claiming to protect them will fail morally and operationally. Security built on humiliation is brittle. Security built on equal citizenship is stronger.

Richman is right to seek a structure in which effectiveness and accountability reinforce each other. From the perspective of immigrant communities, the test is simple: when local police join the national counterterrorism effort, do residents feel safer calling them? If the answer is yes, the partnership may work. If the answer is no, the network will collect fear and call it intelligence.

"The effectiveness of the U.S. domestic-intelligence network depends on reforming the fiscal architecture of homeland security to align federal funding with local operational realities and risks."

Daniel Richman’s essay is framed as an argument about intelligence and accountability, but its most concrete lesson concerns money. The emerging domestic-security system is being built through incentives, grants, mandates, overtime rules, equipment purchases, and jurisdictional bargaining. If those incentives are badly designed, no amount of rhetoric about partnership will produce a coherent national strategy. The federal government cannot ask local governments to serve as the front line of homeland security while funding them as an afterthought.

Before September 11, federal-local law-enforcement relations were lubricated by money. The COPS program, Byrne grants, drug task forces, and federal violent-crime initiatives gave local police and prosecutors resources they could use for problems visible to their constituents. The federal government gained political credit; local governments gained officers, overtime, equipment, and federal prosecutorial leverage. The arrangement had flaws, including arbitrary federalization of local crime and the excesses of the drug war. But it worked politically because the costs and benefits were aligned. Local officials could see what they were getting.

Counterterrorism disrupted that alignment. The federal government now needs local police to gather information, protect targets, respond to alerts, and reassure communities. Yet the benefits of prevention are national and often invisible. If a suspicious lead in Detroit helps prevent an attack in Los Angeles, Detroit bears the political and fiscal costs while the nation receives the benefit. If New York spends heavily to protect subways and landmarks, the whole country benefits from the protection of national symbols and economic infrastructure, but New York taxpayers foot much of the bill. This is a classic intergovernmental mismatch.

The mismatch is worsened by the structure of homeland-security grants. Richman notes the striking disparities: Wyoming and Alaska receiving far more per capita than New York or California. No one should pretend that rural states have no security needs. Agriculture, energy facilities, dams, and transportation corridors matter. But the existing distribution reflects political geography more than risk. In the Senate, every state has two votes; in terrorism, every target does not have equal probability or consequence. A grant formula that ignores threat, vulnerability, and consequence is not a security policy. It is revenue sharing with sirens attached.

The problem is not only how much money goes where, but through whom it flows. Governors understandably favor statewide plans. Interoperable communications, mutual aid, public health coordination, and regional preparedness require state involvement. But mayors and police chiefs are right to object when funds are delayed, filtered, or restricted in ways that do not meet operational needs. A 911 call does not get a state trooper, as one mayor put it. Nor does a vague orange alert pay the overtime bill for city officers guarding bridges, synagogues, mosques, train stations, and office towers.

The current model risks producing cynicism. Federal officials raise alert levels; local departments cancel days off and deploy officers; city budgets absorb the costs; residents see more police but receive little explanation; and then Washington announces another grant program for equipment that cannot be used to hire personnel. Preparedness becomes a stack of applications rather than a functioning system.

A better fiscal architecture would begin with five principles.

First, homeland-security funding should be risk-based. Risk is not simply population. It includes threat intelligence, symbolic value, economic consequence, critical infrastructure, population density, transportation nodes, and vulnerability. A small jurisdiction with a major chemical plant may deserve substantial support. A large city with transit systems and iconic targets certainly does. Congress should resist the temptation to guarantee every state a politically satisfying share before risk is considered. Baseline preparedness matters, but the marginal dollar should go where it reduces the most danger.

Second, funds should reach operational agencies quickly and predictably. Statewide planning is valuable, but it should not become a bottleneck or a political tollbooth. Major urban areas need direct grants for police, fire, emergency medical services, public health, and transportation security. These grants should be coordinated with state plans, not subordinated entirely to them. Regional compacts may often make more sense than state boundaries, especially in metropolitan areas that cross state lines.

Third, federal grants must pay for people, not just things. Equipment is attractive because it is visible and easier to audit: radios, protective gear, sensors, command vehicles. But homeland security is labor-intensive. Intelligence liaison, community outreach, target patrols, training, exercises, language capacity, and overtime all require personnel. If grants cannot be used for staffing, they will not address the actual burden imposed on local agencies. Washington should not tell cities to be vigilant and then forbid them from using money to pay the officers who must do the watching.

Fourth, funding should reward trust-building, not just hardware acquisition. Richman’s central point is that effective intelligence depends on community relationships. Yet grant programs often favor equipment and incident response over prevention through legitimacy. Local departments should be able to fund community liaison officers, language services, civil rights training, hate-crime response, and mechanisms for receiving and evaluating tips without stigmatizing communities. These activities may not look like homeland security to appropriators, but they are part of the intelligence infrastructure.

Fifth, mandates should be funded or abandoned. Proposals such as the CLEAR Act, which would pressure local police to enforce federal immigration law on pain of losing funds, are perverse. They impose new duties, threaten local trust, and shift federal responsibilities onto city budgets. If the federal government believes immigration enforcement is essential to counterterrorism, it should supply federal officers and assume political responsibility. It should not conscript local police and then call resistance a failure of patriotism.

Richman is right that local governments should do more than pass symbolic resolutions. But symbolism often fills the space left by exclusion. When cities lack a meaningful role in designing policy, they protest. When they are asked to implement federal decisions without resources, they resent. When their concerns about immigrant cooperation are dismissed, they resist. A smarter funding system would give local officials both resources and responsibility.

There must also be accountability for how money is spent. Risk-based funding should not become a blank check for every gadget marketed under the homeland-security label. The post-9/11 environment has created a booming industry of vendors, consultants, and technologies promising security. Local agencies may be tempted to buy what is available rather than what is needed. Federal grants should require planning, measurable objectives, interoperability, maintenance budgets, and privacy safeguards. A city should not receive money for databases without rules governing data quality and access. A state should not receive communications funds without demonstrating that local responders can actually talk to one another.

Nor should prevention crowd out ordinary public safety. One of Richman’s subtler observations is that local officials perceive homeland-security money as replacing violent-crime assistance. That perception matters. If cities lose officers or drug-treatment support while gaining hazmat suits, public safety may decline. Terrorism is a grave threat, but so are homicide, domestic violence, robbery, and disorder. The same communities asked to cooperate in counterterrorism also want protection from everyday crime. A national strategy that drains local policing capacity in the name of homeland security will undermine the relationships on which intelligence depends.

This is not an argument for returning to the pre-9/11 grant politics of crime control. Some of those programs were poorly evaluated and politically distributed. It is an argument for recognizing that local capacity is indivisible. The officer who attends a mosque meeting may also solve burglaries. The detective who learns about a suspicious document ring may be assigned to auto theft. The trust that produces a terrorism tip may have been built during years of ordinary service. Funding streams that separate “homeland security” from “community policing” too rigidly misunderstand how local policing works.

Federalism can improve counterterrorism, but only if it is financed honestly. Washington cannot centralize credit, decentralize costs, and expect loyalty. It cannot distribute money by political formula and expect risk reduction. It cannot demand local intelligence while undermining the local relationships that produce it. The right fight is not only over legal authority; it is over institutional incentives.

If we want local police to be partners in national security, we must treat them as partners in budgets as well as speeches. That means risk-based grants, direct urban funding, support for personnel, respect for local trust, and funded mandates. Otherwise the domestic intelligence network will be built on resentment, improvisation, and unfunded overtime. That is no way to protect a country.

"Integrating local police into a national domestic-intelligence network is more likely to decrease accountability and increase surveillance abuses than to provide the democratic restraint suggested by Richman."

Daniel Richman has written a careful and in many ways persuasive essay, but I want to register a more skeptical response to its central conceit: that drawing local police more deeply into the federal counterterrorism intelligence apparatus will, on balance, make that apparatus more accountable. I think the opposite is at least as likely. The history Richman gestures at — COINTELPRO, the American Protective League, the Church Committee findings on how the FBI laundered domestic spying through municipal "red squads" — should not be quickly dispatched with a wave at the supposed maturation of community policing since 1968. It should be the starting point.

Consider what Richman is actually proposing. Roughly 700,000 state and local police officers, with informal access to neighborhoods, schools, mosques, social-service agencies, and informants of every description, are to become the capillaries of a national domestic-intelligence network whose arteries run to the FBI, DHS, and the rest of the alphabet. The promise is that, because mayors are elected and chiefs respond to mayors, this network will be answerable to local political values. But the actual mechanism of accountability requires that local officials know what their officers are doing on behalf of federal agencies. Everything we know about classified intelligence work suggests they will not. JTTF detectives are routinely sworn to secrecy that supersedes their reporting obligations to their own chains of command. The Portland and Boise experiences Richman cites in passing are precisely cases in which local civilian oversight boards were told that federal classification rules prevented them from seeing what their own cops were up to. That is not an inconvenient wrinkle in the model; it is the model failing on its own terms.

Richman's reassurance that policing has changed since 1968 is also overstated. Community policing as a doctrine has indeed spread, but its translation into operational reality is wildly uneven. The New York Street Crime Unit that killed Amadou Diallo, the Los Angeles Rampart scandal, the Cincinnati riots of 2001 — these are not artifacts of a vanished policing culture. The same NYPD that Richman lauds for its international counterterrorism reach has, since 2002, been quietly building an intelligence division under former CIA officer David Cohen whose internal practices are largely unknown to the City Council, let alone to the communities surveilled. The consent decree in New York that Richman mentions exists precisely because the older NYPD intelligence unit ran amok; the relevant question is whether the new arrangements will be any different, and we have very little evidence either way.

There is a deeper problem with Richman's framework. He treats "the local" as if it is structurally inclined toward restraint and toward immigrant communities' interests, because police "need" their cooperation to fight ordinary crime. But this cuts both ways. The same dependence that makes police protective of immigrants for crime-clearance reasons also makes them prone to recruit informants in those communities through coercion — threatening minor immigration consequences to extract cooperation on terrorism matters. We are already hearing reports of this from defense lawyers in Detroit and Brooklyn. The "balanced portfolio" that Richman praises is, viewed from inside an immigrant community, indistinguishable from the "everything is leverage" mentality that fuels the worst kinds of informant abuse.

I would also question Richman's casual claim that "the need for a national domestic-intelligence network focused on threats of terrorist attack ought not be in dispute." It ought to be in considerable dispute. The premise that another 9/11 is around the corner unless we build a massive domestic intelligence apparatus is an assumption, not a finding. There are serious analysts — including some who served on the staff of the 9/11 Commission — who think that the actual threat from al-Qaeda inside the United States is far smaller than the political class needs it to be, and that the principal danger of the post-9/11 mobilization is the construction of a surveillance infrastructure that will outlive any actual threat and be turned, eventually, on other targets. Richman barely engages this possibility.

What would a more cautious response to the same problem look like? It would begin by drawing sharp legal lines around what local police can and cannot collect, share, and retain on people not suspected of crimes, and would give those lines real teeth through private rights of action and independent inspectors general at the municipal level. It would resist, rather than embrace, the dissolution of the wall between criminal investigation and intelligence collection. It would treat the JTTF model as a narrow exception to be tightly controlled rather than a template to be expanded. And it would be honest that the principal accountability mechanism for domestic intelligence in a free society is not federalism — it is law.

Richman ends by acknowledging that much of what he is worried about is not amenable to judicial or legislative oversight, and that "institutional structure" must do the work. That concession is more telling than he allows. If the only thing standing between us and a recrudescence of COINTELPRO is the hope that police chiefs, responding to mayors, responding to voters, will exercise restraint about practices the voters cannot see, then we are not building accountability. We are building a story about accountability. The right fight, in my view, is not to deepen the federal–local intelligence partnership in exchange for a vague promise of local voice. It is to insist that domestic intelligence remain narrow, statutorily bounded, and legally contestable — even at some cost to the project of "connecting dots."

"The responding essay argues that relying on federalism and local police as accountability mechanisms is insufficient because the post-9/11 shift from a criminal-justice paradigm to an intelligence paradigm removes the visibility and legal testing necessary for democratic oversight."

Daniel Richman has produced a careful map of post-9/11 federalism in law enforcement, and I do not want to dispute the geography. I want to dispute the legend. Richman tells a story in which the principal axis of conflict and accommodation runs between levels of government — federal, state, local. I think the more revealing axis runs through a different distinction: between the criminal-justice paradigm and the intelligence paradigm. Once you center that distinction, much of Richman's optimism about local police as accountability mechanisms looks less secure.

The criminal-justice paradigm is what Richman implicitly assumes when he writes about local police. It involves crimes, suspects, evidence, charges, public proceedings, defense counsel, juries, appellate review. It is constituted by legal categories and disciplined by legal process. Police officers operating within it know that what they do will eventually be tested in open court, by adversarial cross-examination, before a judge. That knowledge structures their behavior — sometimes well, sometimes badly, but always in ways that are eventually contestable.

The intelligence paradigm is fundamentally different. It involves targets, not suspects; collection, not evidence; reports, not charges; secret proceedings, not public ones; and oversight by inspectors and committees rather than judges and juries. Its categories are not legal but operational: "of interest," "subject of investigation," "of concern." Information moves through it under classification rules that exclude defense lawyers, journalists, and most legislators. The disciplines that constitute the criminal-justice paradigm — the eventual obligation to prove your case to a stranger — are absent.

What 9/11 has done is to push the FBI and now local police departments out of the first paradigm and into the second, without giving them the institutional culture, oversight regime, or legal architecture that intelligence agencies in other democracies have built up over decades to compensate for the absence of judicial accountability. The "intelligence" that local police are now being asked to collect for federal counterterrorism purposes is not destined for any courtroom. It will sit in databases, populate watchlists, inform investigative leads, and — increasingly — be shared back and forth between agencies whose accountability structures were built for very different functions.

Richman's accountability story assumes the criminal-justice paradigm. Local cops, in his account, are accountable because their work eventually surfaces — through arrests, prosecutions, complaints, lawsuits, press coverage — into the public sphere where it can be evaluated. This is true of ordinary policing. It is not true of intelligence work. Intelligence work, by design, does not surface. The accountability levers Richman invokes — mayoral elections, prosecutorial scrutiny, community pushback — depend on visibility. Without visibility, they do not operate.

This problem cuts across all of Richman's specific recommendations. Take his proposal that local police negotiate the terms of immigration cooperation with the federal government. In the criminal-justice paradigm, this is sensible: the boundary between civil immigration enforcement and criminal investigation should be policy-driven and contestable. In the intelligence paradigm, the question is different — it is about which deportable individuals get added to which intelligence files, and how those files are used downstream. That question is not really negotiable between police chiefs and federal agents in the way Richman imagines, because it operates inside classification rules that local officials are not cleared to see.

Or take his celebration of JTTFs as forums for "operational coordination." In a criminal-justice frame, joint task forces are familiar and reasonably well-understood (DEA task forces, fugitive task forces, etc.). In an intelligence frame, they are something else: they are vehicles for fusing local information into a national intelligence picture whose contours are unavailable to the local participants. The JTTF detective from the NYPD does not know what is being done with the lead she passed up the chain. Neither does Commissioner Kelly, not really, despite his impressive new counterterrorism bureau.

What follows from this reframing? Several things. First, the central institutional reform we need is not better federalism but better intelligence law. We need clear statutory authorities for domestic intelligence collection, with minimization rules, retention limits, purpose restrictions, and audit requirements. We need to decide whether the FBI is doing intelligence or law enforcement and structure each function accordingly. We need oversight bodies — congressional, executive, independent — that operate inside classification with real powers. Richman acknowledges that judicial and legislative tools are limited; the answer is to build the tools we need, not to give up and rely on federalism instead.

Second, the use of local police as intelligence collectors should be tightly constrained. If we are going to do it at all, we should do it through formally trained, separately funded, audited intelligence units within local departments, governed by clear federal-state-local memoranda of understanding that specify what can be collected, retained, and shared. The informal, capillary intelligence-gathering that Richman seems to imagine — every beat cop's interaction a potential intelligence opportunity — is precisely what produced COINTELPRO. The fact that beat cops now have better community-policing instincts than they did in 1968 does not change the structural problem. It just gives us cleaner inputs into an unclean system.

Third, we should be more honest that there will be a tradeoff. A constitutionally serious domestic intelligence system, with real legal limits and real oversight, will be less efficient at "connecting dots" than one that is unbounded. That is the price of operating in a free society. Richman wants to suggest that there is no tradeoff — that the federalism solution gives us both effectiveness and accountability. I do not think this is true, and I do not think it is helpful to pretend it is. The right conversation to have is which dots we are prepared not to connect, and which kinds of intrusions we are prepared not to authorize, in exchange for living under a government whose intelligence functions are legally bounded. Richman's essay does not really have that conversation. It should.

The detectives we assign to JTTFs disappear from our own intelligence picture. Local chiefs are placed in the position of having to defend operations in their cities that they were not consulted on and cannot describe. The same two decades that produced Herman Goldstein and George Kelling produced aggressive order-maintenance, broken-windows enforcement, and the stop-and-frisk patterns that have already strained police relationships with the very immigrant and minority neighborhoods we now want them to liaise with. The Dearborn corporal whom Richman quotes is real, but so is the experience of young men in Brooklyn or East L.A. who already regard local cops as a source of personal ruin. The argument that the beat cop is a peacekeeper rather than a deportation threat works only where the local department has decided, often against political headwinds, to be one.

The populations on whom the costs of counterterrorism investigation fall most heavily—non-citizens, recent immigrants, asylum seekers, foreign students—are precisely the residents who cannot vote. What Richman calls "informational accountability"—the cops' need to keep tip lines open—is a real check, but it is narrower than democratic accountability and is itself contingent on whether the police actually want tips from that community. A department that has written off Arab-American Dearborn or Muslim Queens as politically irrelevant feels none of the structural pressure Richman describes.

There is also a problem internal to his optimistic scenario. The principal mechanism by which local police currently participate in federal counterterrorism is the Joint Terrorism Task Force, and JTTF participation comes wrapped in federal security clearances, non-disclosure obligations, and rules that make it difficult or impossible for participating officers to report up to city councils, civilian review boards, or even their own commissioners. Daniel Richman concludes his essay by observing that "the primary sources of accountability and due moderation will have to come from institutional structure" because judicial and legislative oversight of executive discretion in this domain is so limited. I want to focus on that concession, because I think it is the most important sentence in the essay and also the most troubling.

What Richman is acknowledging — though he glides past it — is that the post-9/11 executive has constructed a domestic security regime that operates substantially outside the legal architecture the United States built up over the course of the twentieth century to constrain government investigation of its own citizens. Material witness warrants used as preventive detention. National Security Letters used to bypass grand jury process. The Attorney General's revised investigative guidelines, which authorize the FBI to attend public meetings and surveil religious and political gatherings without predication. Administrative subpoenas of records once protected by judicial process. The "enemy combatant" designation applied to U.S. citizens. The whole infrastructure of secret detentions, secret hearings, and secret evidence that the Inspector General's reports have begun to document.

Most of these moves have been judicially or congressionally untested, or tested only at the margins. The Supreme Court's decisions this past June in Hamdi and Rasul — which Richman briefly invokes — were narrow, hedged, and procedural; they will discipline a small subset of cases involving formal military detention but leave the broader administrative and intelligence apparatus essentially untouched. Congress, for its part, passed the PATRIOT Act in October 2001 with virtually no deliberation and has, three years on, largely declined to revisit its provisions, even as the four-year sunsets approach.

This is the institutional context for Richman's federalism argument. The federalism argument says: since law cannot constrain the executive in this domain, structure must. Local police, through their political accountability and community embeddedness, will provide a check that statute and court cannot. This is a creative move and I respect its ingenuity. But I think it is a mistake — perhaps a generous mistake, but a mistake nonetheless — to accept the premise that legal constraint is unavailable here.

Legal constraint is available. It is just not being constructed, because the political class has not insisted on it. The framework legislation for domestic intelligence that has long existed in the United Kingdom, in Germany, in Canada, in Australia could be constructed here. A statutory definition of "domestic intelligence collection," with predication requirements, minimization rules, retention limits, and audit obligations, is well within Congress's power. A FISA-like court with jurisdiction over a broader range of domestic intelligence activities — not just electronic surveillance — could be created. An inspector general for the intelligence community with cross-agency jurisdiction and broader public reporting authority is also doable. A statutory framework for watchlisting, including notice and challenge procedures for U.S. persons, has been proposed by serious commentators and is being ignored.

Richman's response to all this might be that none of it is going to happen, given current political realities. He may be right. But that is a forecast about politics, not a finding about the structure of the problem. The Church Committee reforms of the late 1970s were also unlikely to happen until they did. Political windows open. The work of advocates and scholars in the meantime is to articulate what should fill those windows when they open — and to insist that the right institutional response to a domestic intelligence emergency is law, not workarounds.

I worry that Richman's essay, by treating legal underdevelopment as a given and federalism as a substitute, reinforces the political pattern that produced the underdevelopment in the first place. If the message to the executive branch is "we will work around the absence of statutory constraint with creative federalism," the executive has even less reason to support the construction of statutory constraint. The federalism argument becomes, against its author's intent, a legitimation strategy for the unbounded administrative state in domestic security.

This is, I should say, a common pattern in post-9/11 commentary. Many thoughtful scholars have offered second-best solutions — better internal review, more transparency, sunsets, ombudsmen — that operate inside the basic frame of expansive executive authority. Each is reasonable on its own terms. Cumulatively, they normalize a constitutional posture that should not be normalized. The premise of the American security state used to be that domestic spying was sharply limited by the criminal-procedure framework and the First Amendment. That premise is now widely treated as quaint. It should not be.

What does the alternative look like, concretely? A 21st-century equivalent of the Church Committee reforms, embedded in statute, that would: (1) codify the line between criminal investigation and intelligence collection, including the predication required for each; (2) establish a statutory framework for domestic intelligence comparable to FISA but broader, with judicial oversight of non-content collection programs; (3) restructure watchlisting under clear legal standards with redress procedures; (4) require congressional reporting on domestic intelligence activities comparable to what is required of the foreign intelligence agencies; (5) provide remedies — including damages and exclusion — for unlawful collection that, unlike current doctrine, are actually available in practice.

These are not pipe dreams. They are the kinds of structural reforms that other democracies have implemented and that the United States itself implemented after the abuses of the 1960s and 1970s. They are also, I would argue, more durable than Richman's federalism solution, which depends on a continuing alignment of political interest between cities and immigrant communities that may not always hold.

I do not think Richman is wrong that local police should have more voice in the construction of the post-9/11 security apparatus. I think he is wrong that this voice can substitute for what is missing — namely, a serious legal architecture for domestic intelligence in a constitutional democracy. The right fight, in my view, is for that architecture. Federalism is at best a complement to it, not a replacement.

In his thoughtful and nuanced essay, Daniel Richman attempts something that might seem impossible after the last three years: to imagine a domestic intelligence network that is both effective and rights-respecting, and to find the key to that balance in the very structure of American federalism. He argues that state and local governments, if brought in as genuine partners, can act as a mediating layer between the federal government’s national security imperatives and the civil-liberties concerns of the communities they serve. Richman’s error is not in his institutional analysis, but in his willingness to accept the fundamental premise of the “War on Terror” at home.

The heart of the problem is the blurring of the line between policing and spying. Traditional criminal law enforcement is reactive: it investigates crimes that have occurred or are imminent, based on individualized suspicion, and operates within a framework of probable cause, warrants, and the adversarial trial process. Intelligence-gathering, by contrast, is proactive and speculative. It aims to collect vast amounts of information about people, associations, and ideas in the hope of “connecting dots” that will prevent future attacks. This model has always been the norm for foreign intelligence—we expect the CIA to spy on other countries—but when turned inward, it represents a fundamental shift in the relationship between the citizen and the state. Richman’s proposal to involve local police simply extends this model to every precinct house in the country, making the beat cop an intelligence node. The friendly, community-oriented officer who once helped old ladies across the street now also files reports on their acquaintances, their reading habits, and their political discussions.

This transformation has already begun. Section 215 of the USA PATRIOT Act allows the FBI to obtain “any tangible thing” (including library and bookstore records) upon a minimal certification to the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court, with no showing of individualized suspicion. Local police, acting as agents of the Joint Terrorism Task Forces, routinely share with federal counterparts the information they gather during routine encounters—traffic stops, domestic calls, building inspections. The “felony arrest as intelligence opportunity” that Richman celebrates is precisely what should alarm us. It means that every arrest, for any offense, becomes a chance to question the suspect about his community, his travels, his opinions, and to enter that information into a permanent, unaccountable database. This is not community policing; it is the domestic equivalent of the informant networks that sustained the East German Stasi.

Richman acknowledges the COINTELPRO analogy and attempts to distinguish our era from the late 1960s by pointing to the changes in police culture and the broader consensus about the threat. But the similarities are more striking than the differences. Then, as now, the federal government argued that an existential threat—communist subversion, terrorism—required broad surveillance of political and religious groups. Then, as now, local police were recruited to do much of the dirty work, bypassing the restrictions that applied more directly to federal agencies. The Church Committee found that local intelligence units “provided a convenient manner for the FBI to acquire information it wanted while avoiding criticism for using covert techniques.” Substitute the names of a few agencies, and the statement could apply today. The Portland example Richman cites—the city attorney’s refusal to allow local officers to ask certain questions—is the exception that proves the rule: most departments complied, and the ones that resisted did so only because they had preexisting state laws barring such inquiries, laws that are now under attack in Congress.

Moreover, Richman’s reliance on “accountability” misunderstands the nature of accountability in intelligence work. Accountability presupposes transparency: we can hold officials responsible only if we know what they are doing. But domestic intelligence operations are inherently secret. Richman notes that even if the most criticized provisions of the Patriot Act were repealed, the FBI could still obtain the same materials through grand-jury subpoenas issued in secret. That is precisely the point: the problem is not this or that statute, but the underlying architecture of surveillance and the lack of public access to decision-making. The negotiations Richman envisions between local police chiefs and federal agents would take place behind closed doors, excluded from the scrutiny of even the most dedicated city council. What leverage would a mayor have over a counterterrorism operation she cannot see? The very concept of “local voice” is reduced to a private plea.

I do not propose that we simply do nothing to prevent terrorism. But the alternative to Richman’s model is not passivity; it is a return to the principles of criminal investigation. Terrorism is a crime, and suspects can be investigated, prosecuted, and punished using the ordinary tools of law enforcement—search warrants, wiretaps with probable cause, grand juries—with all the protections that the Bill of Rights provides. To the extent that current law falls short, we can strengthen it without abandoning the adversary system. The argument that such tools are insufficient is the argument for police powers, and it is an argument that has been made many times in our history, always to be regretted later.

The “right fight” is not to give local governments a say in how their residents are spied upon; it is to insist that, in a free society, the state has no business building a permanent system of domestic espionage, whether run from Washington or from city hall. If Richman’s federalist intelligence network comes to pass, we will discover, too late, that the velvet glove of community policing does not alter the iron fist beneath.

Local departments have little leverage when the alternative is losing federal funding. The National League of Cities' opposition to unfunded mandates is correct but toothless. The deeper problem is accountability. Richman argues that local police involvement will make the intelligence network more accountable because police chiefs report to elected mayors, who face voters. Mayors have strong incentives to be seen as tough on crime and terrorism; they have weaker incentives to protect civil liberties in immigrant communities that may not vote in large numbers. The history of local police abuse—from surveillance of political groups to discriminatory enforcement against minorities—shows that local accountability is no guarantee of rights protection.

Richman is right that we need to engage local agencies rather than dismiss them. But engagement requires not just cooperation but also genuine constraints on federal authority—and the history of federal-state relations in criminal law suggests that those constraints are unlikely to emerge from goodwill alone. We need institutional reforms that make accountability structural rather than aspirational.

"The federal government should embrace a collaborative federalism model that empowers local police as genuine partners in counterterrorism, leveraging their unique community trust and granular knowledge to create a more effective and accountable intelligence network."

Daniel Richman has given us a rare thing in the post-9/11 debate: a genuinely constructive proposal that refuses to treat security and liberty as a zero-sum game. His central insight—that the very structure of federalism, properly harnessed, can make domestic intelligence both more effective and more accountable—deserves far more attention than it has received. Too often, the conversation has been dominated by absolutists on both sides: those who see any local resistance as sabotage, and those who see any federal coordination as a police state in embryo. Richman charts a middle path that is at once pragmatic and principled. I wish to build on his argument by exploring more deeply why local police are uniquely positioned to reconcile the competing demands of counterterrorism and democratic accountability, and by suggesting some practical measures that could bring his “optimistic story” closer to reality.

The past two decades have witnessed a transformation in urban policing so profound that it is almost unrecognizable from the era of COINTELPRO. The old model—reactive, incident-driven, often brutal—has been slowly supplanted, though not entirely replaced, by what its practitioners call community- or problem-oriented policing. This shift, associated with the work of Herman Goldstein, James Q. Wilson, and George Kelling, among others, rests on the recognition that effective crime control depends on the active cooperation of the communities being policed. Officers on the beat are no longer merely responders to 911 calls; they are expected to build sustained relationships with neighborhood leaders, to attend community meetings, to solve the “little” problems—the abandoned cars, the disorderly bars—that erode public trust. This approach has been driven not primarily by altruism, but by the hard-nosed realization that without community trust, the flow of information that solves crimes dries up.

Richman is right to identify this as the source of local police’s comparative advantage. Federal agents, no matter how well trained or intentioned, are transients. They swoop into a neighborhood for a specific investigation, and then they leave. They do not walk the streets at night, respond to domestic disturbances, or help find lost children. The beat cop, by contrast, is a permanent presence, often a known face. It is the beat cop who knows which storekeeper is worried about his son’s friends, which imam has credibility, which community leader is respected and which is a blowhard. This kind of granular, contextual knowledge cannot be acquired through “outreach meetings” organized by the FBI’s public affairs office; it is the accumulated capital of years on the same beat.

This is why, when the Justice Department’s post-9/11 interview drive came to Ann Arbor, Michigan, and Dearborn, local Arab-American leaders specifically requested that local officers be present. They trusted the local police—imperfectly, perhaps, but far more than they trusted the federal agents who appeared at their doors asking questions about political and religious activities. As Chief Daniel Oates of Ann Arbor later recounted, the local officers were able to serve as a familiar buffer, reducing the intimidation inherent in the encounter. That is a small but telling example of the “mediation” Richman describes.

But this role is not merely a passive one. Richman suggests that local departments should “exact a toll” from federal authorities as the price of their cooperation. I would go further and argue that the most significant toll they can exact is the demand to be genuine partners in defining the intelligence mission itself. At present, the flow of tasks is overwhelmingly top-down: Washington decides what threats to watch for, and local officers are asked to be eyes and ears. But local police are far better placed to know what kinds of inquiries will damage community relations and what alternative approaches might yield better information with less harm. A truly collaborative network would involve local chiefs in setting priorities and crafting operational guidelines. The nascent National Criminal Intelligence Sharing Plan, spearheaded by the International Association of Chiefs of Police, gestures in this direction, but it remains a consultative document, not a binding framework.

Why should the federal government agree to such shared governance? Because the alternative is a dried-up well. Intelligence is not like a mineral that can be extracted by force; it is a human product that depends on willing cooperation. As Richman notes, every felony arrest is an intelligence opportunity. But if that arrest is perceived as a pretext for immigration enforcement or as part of a broader system of suspicion directed at entire communities, the opportunity vanishes. The officer who is seen as a spy loses the ability to be a peacekeeper, and thereby loses the intelligence that comes from peacekeeping. The “chilling effect” the IACP warns about is not a liberal abstraction; it translates directly into lost information about the local criminals—including, potentially, the terrorists embedded among them.

I do not wish to be naive. The community policing revolution is still incomplete and has had its share of failures and public-relations exercises. The persistence of racial profiling, brutality scandals, and “blue walls of silence” in many departments is undeniable. But the direction of change is real, and it has created both a new professional ethos and, for the first time, a widespread institutional recognition that legitimacy is an operational necessity. This is what separates our moment from 1968. The police intelligence units of that era were detached from the regular patrol and community functions; they could be recruited into COINTELPRO precisely because their work had no bearing on the daily task of keeping neighborhoods safe. Today, the same officers who would be asked to gather intelligence are the ones who depend on the goodwill of the community to solve burglaries and quell gang violence. Their institutional self-interest is not to alienate that community. The tension Richman describes is not a problem to be solved; it is a productive friction to be sustained.

To move toward the happier ending, three concrete steps are needed. First, Congress should pass legislation explicitly authorizing local-federal intelligence partnerships but conditioning substantial federal funding on the existence of local civilian oversight mechanisms that have access—if only through cleared designees—to the broad outlines of local intelligence activities. This would resolve the conflict Richman notes between federal secrecy rules and local accountability, turning it into a catalyst for stronger oversight. Second, the Justice Department should issue binding regulations requiring federal agents to consult with local chiefs before undertaking any broad intelligence-gathering initiative in their jurisdictions, and to give those chiefs a meaningful opportunity to suggest modifications. Third, we need a major investment in joint training: not just teaching locals how to write intelligence reports, but teaching federal agents how community policing works and why it matters.

None of this is a guarantee against abuse. But perfect is the enemy of good, and the only available alternatives to Richman’s cooperative framework are far worse: either a federalized domestic intelligence agency that lacks local roots entirely, or a chaotic patchwork in which some cities refuse to cooperate and others cooperate too eagerly, with no democratic constraint either way. Better to embrace the messiness of federalism, and to trust that the daily negotiations between a beat cop and the people on his beat can do more to temper state power than any number of blue-ribbon commissions. The right fight, as Richman shows, is not between security and liberty, but between a centralized surveillance state and a distributed, accountable, and therefore more effective intelligence network. We should take up that fight.
