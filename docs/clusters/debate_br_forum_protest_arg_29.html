"An absolutist defense of the right to protest is incompatible with the university's core function as a community of learning, which requires clear and enforceable regulations to maintain academic life."

It is painful to read Alex Gourevitch’s essay as someone who has spent decades in university leadership and who deeply believes in the value of protest. I agree with much of his diagnosis of the structural vulnerabilities that made universities so susceptible to the Trump administration’s bullying: the overreliance on federal research dollars, the donor-driven model of prestige, the administrative bloat, and the erosion of shared governance. These are all real, and they have produced a leadership class that is often more committed to institutional preservation than to institutional integrity. And I agree fully that the administration’s demand to gut departments and blacklist certain speech in exchange for federal funds is an outrageous assault on academic autonomy that must be fought with every tool available.

But Gourevitch’s solution—an absolutist defense of the right to protest, with only the most minimal restrictions allowed—is simply not realistic for any university that hopes to function as a community of learning. His narrative of the past year elides the genuine governance crisis that large-scale, open-ended encampments create. He writes that “in the vast majority of cases, everyone could find their way to where they needed to be,” and that disruptions were “inconvenient, but only that.” That is not the testimony I heard on the ground, from faculty whose courses were impossible to conduct in buildings near the encampments, from staff whose workplaces became inaccessible, and from students who did not feel unsafe because of the content of chants but because of the sheer physical congestion and the presence of large, often aggressive crowds. A university is not a city park; it is a residential community with thousands of inhabitants who have legitimate expectations of quiet study, predictable access, and a secure environment. To label all those expectations as mere signs of a “therapeutic consumerism” is to dismiss the ordinary conditions necessary for academic life.

Moreover, the idea that universities should adopt a viewpoint-neutral posture is, in principle, correct. The problem is that neutrality in these circumstances is nearly impossible to achieve. When a large, ideologically committed group occupies the symbolic center of campus for weeks, it changes the political climate for everyone. It may not explicitly bar anyone from entering, but it creates an atmosphere of implicit pressure: students who disagree with the protesters may avoid the area, self-censor in class, or feel that the university has tacitly endorsed the protest. That, too, is a form of exclusion, and it is one that administrators cannot simply ignore. The difficult task is to balance the right of protesters to make their case with the right of others not to have their educational environment dominated by a single, all-consuming political message. Gourevitch’s essay offers no guidance on how to strike that balance. He seems to think that any regulation beyond the most minimal is per se illegitimate.

This is not an abstract problem. During the height of the protests, many of my colleagues in administration spent their days in triage—trying to ensure that exams could proceed, that commencement could be planned, that students on both sides of the divide did not come to blows. We failed, sometimes spectacularly, as at UCLA. But the lesson of those failures is not that we should stop trying to manage protest; it is that management requires clear, consistently enforced rules that are decided through legitimate governance processes, not imposed under the threat of a federal funding cut. Those rules must include reasonable limits on amplified sound, on the erection of structures, on overnight camping, and on blocking access to buildings. They must also ensure that disciplinary responses are proportionate and not vindictive. Expelling students for nonviolent occupation of a building is extreme; a one-semester suspension may be appropriate.

The deepest difficulty is that we are living through a moment in which the very concept of institutional neutrality has been weaponized. The right demands neutrality when it comes to climate change or racial justice but insists on active protection of a particular definition of antisemitism. The left demands institutional statements on every social issue but cries censorship when the statements turn against it. The result is a chaotic, politically charged environment where every decision about protest is read as taking sides. Gourevitch wants universities to be forums for unbridled hostility; I suspect he would not like the result if that norm were applied consistently and the Genocide Awareness Project erected its display every month or Turning Point USA brought in a speaker who denied the Holocaust. The campus would become a perpetual warzone, and the intellectual life he claims to defend would evaporate.

What we need is not absolute rights but a recommitment to a set of institutional practices that allow multiple, competing claims to coexist. That means restoring faculty governance, so that decisions about protest policies are made by those closest to the educational mission, not by administrators responding to trustees and donors. It means rebuilding a culture of civil disagreement in which protest is one mode among many, not a permanent occupation. And it means resisting, with every legal and political means, the federal government’s attempt to dictate the content of campus speech. But it also means accepting that a university has a right to protect its core functions and that the exercise of this right is not, in itself, a betrayal of its principles. Gourevitch’s absolutism, I fear, would make that impossible.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Large-scale, open-ended encampments create a genuine governance crisis by physically obstructing the ordinary work of faculty, staff, and students.
  2. 02A viewpoint-neutral posture is insufficient because a permanent, all-consuming political occupation of campus space creates an atmosphere of implicit pressure and exclusion for those who disagree.
  3. 03Universities must have the authority to enforce reasonable time, place, and manner restrictions—such as limits on amplified sound and overnight camping—to protect the educational environment.
  4. 04The solution to current campus tensions lies in restoring shared faculty governance and proportionate discipline rather than adopting an absolute right to unbridled hostility.
  5. 05If universities allowed unrestricted protest and hostility, the resulting perpetual conflict would cause the intellectual life of the institution to evaporate.
