"To achieve a durable politics of immigrant freedom, the left must move beyond moral appeals and embrace a policy of institutional seriousness that manages borders humanely while integrating immigrants as full legal and political participants."

Aziz Rana’s essay is strongest when it shows how American freedom has been built through a boundary: freedom for those inside, force for those outside. Few issues reveal that boundary more starkly than immigration. The border is where the United States tells a story about who may seek opportunity, who may work, who may belong, and who may be governed without representation. Rana is right that a left freedom politics must reject the right’s racialized border theater. But if the left wants to win a durable politics of immigrant freedom, it must combine moral clarity with institutional seriousness.

Start with the moral clarity. The United States depends on immigrant labor while denying many immigrant workers full legal and political personhood. Undocumented workers harvest food, clean homes, build houses, staff restaurants, care for children and elders, process meat, deliver goods, and sustain cities. Yet their vulnerability is not incidental. It is useful to employers. The threat of deportation disciplines not only undocumented workers but entire workplaces. It makes wage theft easier, injuries easier to hide, organizing riskier, and sexual harassment harder to report. Deportability is a labor regime.

For that reason, immigrant rights are labor rights. A worker who can be removed from the country for calling attention to abuse is not free. A citizen worker competing in a labor market where employers can exploit undocumented status is also less free. The right says immigrants lower standards. Often employers lower standards by using immigration status as a weapon. The solution is not mass deportation, which would be cruel, economically disastrous, and authoritarian. The solution is legalization, strong labor enforcement, firewalls between workplace complaints and immigration enforcement, and sector-wide standards that prevent any group of workers from being turned into a disposable caste.

Rana also rightly insists that immigration does not begin at the border. People move because of family ties, jobs, violence, climate stress, state failure, aspiration, and historical connection. U.S. policy has helped shape many of these conditions: interventions in Latin America, support for anti-communist violence during the Cold War, trade policies that disrupted rural livelihoods, drug war militarization, greenhouse gas emissions, and demand for low-wage labor. To speak of migration as if it were an invasion detached from history is dishonest.

Yet here is where I part company with Rana’s implied prescription of presumptive admission and basic decriminalization as the central left answer. Decriminalizing unlawful entry may be defensible as a way to reduce the carceral cruelty of immigration enforcement; civil processing is usually enough. But “presumptive admission for all” is too vague to sustain democratic consent. A political community has legitimate interests in managing entry, planning infrastructure, adjudicating asylum, integrating newcomers, and maintaining public support for welfare and labor standards. If progressives appear indifferent to these tasks, they leave the field to restrictionists.

The left should not be afraid to say that borders can be administered humanely and democratically. The question is not borders or no borders. The question is what borders are for. Are they caste lines that preserve a racialized national identity? Are they militarized zones where asylum seekers are treated as criminals? Are they labor filters that admit workers without rights? Or can they be legal institutions that recognize mobility, protect refugees, facilitate family unity, match labor needs without indenture, and uphold universal dignity?

A freedom-centered immigration policy would begin with legalization for the long-settled undocumented population. People who have built lives here, raised children here, paid taxes here, and become part of communities should not live indefinitely as rightless subjects. Legalization would be one of the most significant expansions of freedom available in American politics: freedom to work openly, travel, report crimes, change jobs, join unions, and participate civically.

Second, the asylum system needs capacity and fairness. The right’s answer is deterrence through suffering: make conditions so miserable that people stop coming. That violates the spirit of refugee protection and brutalizes border communities. But the current system is also failing. Years-long backlogs serve no one. The United States should invest in asylum officers, immigration judges, counsel for children and vulnerable applicants, humane reception, and regional processing that expands lawful routes without trapping people in danger. Fast decisions need not mean unfair decisions if the system is properly resourced.

Third, legal migration channels must be expanded and redesigned. Temporary worker programs have too often created employer-dependent labor, where losing a job means losing legal status. That is not freedom; it is a guestworker caste. If the economy demands immigrant labor, workers should have portable status, labor protections, family unity, and paths to permanence. Circular migration should be possible without coercion. People should be able to come and go without being forced into illegality by unrealistic visa caps.

Fourth, border enforcement should be demilitarized. The buildup of walls, surveillance towers, drones, detention centers, and armed agents has not eliminated migration. It has made crossings more deadly and enriched contractors. It has also transformed the borderlands into a constitutional gray zone. Residents of border communities, many of them citizens and many Latino or Indigenous, live with checkpoints, stops, and surveillance that would be intolerable elsewhere. Their freedom matters too.

Fifth, immigrant integration requires public investment. Schools need resources for English learners. Cities receiving newcomers need federal support. Work authorization should be swift so people can support themselves. Housing policy must address scarcity that fuels resentment. Health care access protects everyone. If newcomers are placed in communities already struggling with underfunded services, conflict is predictable. Solidarity cannot be built on austerity.

This is the point Rana’s institution-building argument helps clarify. Immigrant freedom will not be secured by moral appeals alone. It requires unions that organize across status, schools that welcome children without treating them as burdens, local governments that provide services, legal aid networks, churches and civic groups that build trust, and political parties willing to defend immigrants as members of the community rather than as symbols.

It also requires a better language for the anxious citizen. Many Americans who worry about immigration are not white nationalists, though white nationalists exploit the issue relentlessly. Some fear wage competition, cultural change, overcrowded schools, or loss of control. The left should answer without contempt: your wages are threatened by employers, not by migrants; your schools are strained by underfunding, not by children; your hospitals need investment; your community can change and still be yours; democracy means newcomers should become participants rather than permanent outsiders. But reassurance must be paired with policy competence.

Rana’s history of settler freedom reminds us that the old American bargain offered freedom to insiders by creating outsiders. Immigration is the living test of whether we can refuse that bargain. The alternative is not a borderless utopia conjured by slogan. It is a republic confident enough to extend rights, honest enough to confront its history, organized enough to manage migration humanely, and democratic enough to let those who live and work here belong. Immigrant freedom and national self-government need not be enemies. Properly understood, each requires the other.
