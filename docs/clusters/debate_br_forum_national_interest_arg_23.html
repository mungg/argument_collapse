Diversified Claude

"Stephen Walt’s proposal to coerce Israel into a total withdrawal to pre-1967 borders is based on a flawed understanding of Middle Eastern geopolitics and would ultimately undermine American credibility without ending the threat of terrorism."

I have read Stephen Walt's essay with the care that any contribution from a scholar of his stature deserves, and with growing dismay. The portion devoted to Israel is not merely wrong on the merits; it traffics in tropes and assumptions that deserve more sustained scrutiny than they typically receive in this kind of forum.

Walt argues that "unconditional support for Israel has done great harm to America's position in the Arab and Islamic world," that this support is the product of "the Israel lobby," that bin Laden and his sympathizers are driven primarily by Israeli policy in the territories, and that the United States should threaten to cut off all economic and military aid unless Israel withdraws to its pre-1967 lines. Each of these claims fails on inspection.

Start with the empirical question of what drives anti-Americanism in the Arab and Islamic world. Walt cites the Pew Global Attitudes data, which is real and concerning. But the diagnosis he draws from it is selective. The same surveys show enormous variation across Muslim-majority countries that cannot be explained by attitudes toward Israel. Anti-Americanism is high in Pakistan, where Israel-Palestine is a distant abstraction, and lower in some Gulf states more proximate to the conflict. The 9/11 hijackers were largely Saudi, motivated by bin Laden's ideology of a global caliphate, by his rage at American troops on the Arabian Peninsula, and by a long catalogue of grievances in which Palestine appeared but was not paramount. Read bin Laden's actual declarations: the "Zionist-Crusader alliance" is denounced in the same breath as the apostate Saudi monarchy, the partition of the Levant after World War I, the loss of al-Andalus, and the broader humiliation of the umma. The Palestinian cause is one item on a long list, and not historically the leading one. To make it the master variable in our Middle East policy is to misread our adversaries.

Now consider Walt's claim that the "Israel lobby" has captured American policymaking. This formulation, increasingly common in academic circles, conflates ordinary interest-group politics with something more sinister. AIPAC is an effective lobby; so is the NAACP, the AARP, the NRA, and the National Association of Manufacturers. American support for Israel is not the artifact of clever lobbying against the better judgment of the American public; it is the reflection of a broad and durable popular consensus, sustained across Republican and Democratic administrations, that Israel is a fellow democracy in a region of dictatorships and that its security is a legitimate American interest. Polling has shown this consistently for decades. To suggest that elected officials are following the lobby rather than their constituents is to invert the actual dynamic of representative government — and to flirt with older insinuations about whose interests are being secretly served.

The strategic critique of unconditional aid is more interesting and deserves a real reply. Walt is correct that no American interest is served by automatically endorsing every Israeli policy, and that the expansion of settlements in particular has been strategically harmful — to Israel as well as to us. American presidents from Carter through Clinton through the elder Bush have said exactly this and applied real pressure. The current administration's deference to Sharon on the settlement question is, I agree, a mistake. But "conditional support" is not the same thing as the threat Walt proposes — withdrawal of all aid unless Israel withdraws to the 1967 lines. The Clinton parameters Walt invokes did not envision a literal return to the 1967 borders. They envisioned land swaps, security arrangements, and a negotiated end to the conflict in which Israel retained the major settlement blocs and got something in return.

Walt also elides the actual posture of the Palestinian leadership during the Oslo process and its collapse. He notes the "myth" that Arafat rejected Clinton's terms but does not engage with the contemporary testimony of Dennis Ross, Aaron Miller, or for that matter Clinton himself, who has written that Arafat's failure to engage seriously with the parameters was a personal and historical tragedy. The Second Intifada, which has now killed over a thousand Israelis and thousands more Palestinians, was not a spontaneous protest against the failure of Camp David; it was, by the testimony of senior Palestinian officials themselves, a strategy. To recount this history as if Israeli intransigence were the sole obstacle is to miss the central reason a settlement has been so difficult.

There is also the new situation we find ourselves in after Arafat's death. Mahmoud Abbas has been elected. He has condemned the militarization of the intifada. The Sharon government is preparing to withdraw from Gaza. Israel and the Palestinian Authority are talking again. This is a moment in which active American engagement could yield real progress — not by issuing ultimatums to one side, but by helping both sides take risks. Walt's preferred posture — threatening Israel with abandonment — would not have produced this opening, and would not consolidate it.

On the broader question of how to discourage terrorism, Walt's argument essentially endorses the bin Laden narrative: that American policy in the region creates the terrorists, and that adjusting our policy will reduce their numbers. This is a partial truth dressed as a complete one. Yes, specific American policies generate grievance. But the deeper sources of jihadist mobilization lie within Muslim societies themselves: failed states, frustrated demographics, religious-political ideologies that predate the State of Israel by centuries, the slow-motion crisis of legitimacy of the Arab regimes. Pretending that we can defuse these by adjusting our Israel policy is, in its own way, as self-flattering as the neoconservative belief that we can defuse them by installing democracy at gunpoint. Both fantasies put America at the center of a story in which we are not, in fact, the protagonist.

I share Walt's discomfort with the Bush administration's posture on the peace process. I share his sense that American leverage has not been fully used. But the framework he proposes — that Israel is a strategic liability sustained only by a domestic lobby and that we would be safer if we treated it like Norway — is wrong as analysis and wrong as policy. It would not buy us peace with our enemies. It would teach our friends, here and elsewhere, that American commitments evaporate when fashionable opinion turns. That is a lesson we should not be in the business of teaching.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Anti-Americanism in the Arab and Islamic world is driven by deep-seated internal societal crises and diverse ideological grievances rather than being primarily a reaction to U.S. support for Israel.
  2. 02U.S. support for Israel is the result of a broad, durable democratic consensus among the American public rather than the undue influence of a singular 'Israel lobby.'
  3. 03The failure of the peace process is largely attributable to Palestinian leadership's strategic choices and rejection of past compromises rather than unilateral Israeli intransigence.
  4. 04Issuing ultimatums and threatening to abandon a democratic ally would signal to the world that American commitments are unreliable, damaging the nation's global standing.
  5. 05The current political opening following Arafat's death requires active, balanced engagement and risk-taking from both sides rather than the one-sided coercion Walt suggests.
