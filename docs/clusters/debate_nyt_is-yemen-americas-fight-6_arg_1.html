The United States should avoid becoming a party, even indirectly, to the Saudi-led war against the Houthis. A better policy would press for a cease-fire, U.N.-led negotiations, humanitarian access and an inclusive transitional arrangement, while keeping any counterterrorism action against AQAP narrow, lawful and accountable. "The United States should keep its support for the Saudi-led campaign in Yemen minimal and conditional because the conflict is a regional civil war that contradicts broader U.S. strategic interests."

The Yemen crisis exposes the incoherence at the heart of America's posture in the region, and that is precisely why Washington should keep its indirect support to the Saudi-led campaign minimal and conditional, rather than embrace it as our fight.

Consider the contradiction. In Iraq, U.S. air power is effectively flying cover for Iranian-backed Shia militias battling the Islamic State. In Yemen, we are providing targeting intelligence and logistics to a Sunni coalition bombing Iranian-backed Shia militias. Whatever else this is, it is not a strategy — it is a series of reflexes shaped by which capital is asking. The Houthis are not Hezbollah; their grievances are largely domestic, their relationship with Tehran is more opportunistic than command-and-control, and they have spent years fighting AQAP, the Al Qaeda franchise that genuinely does threaten Americans. Helping degrade the Houthis militarily risks handing AQAP — and possibly the nascent Islamic State affiliate — exactly the ungoverned space they need. The collapse of our counterterrorism partnership in Sana'a and the evacuation of U.S. personnel from al-Anad should already be sobering lessons.

The humanitarian dimension compounds the strategic one. Yemen imports roughly 90 percent of its food. A sustained air campaign and naval blockade against the poorest country in the Arab world will produce a famine the Saudis cannot fix and Americans will be blamed for enabling. The early reports of strikes on refugee camps and residential neighborhoods in Sana'a are a preview.

None of this means abandoning Riyadh. The Saudis have legitimate anxieties about a hostile militia on their southern border firing across it, and the alliance matters — especially as nuclear talks with Iran enter their decisive phase. But alliance management is not the same as endorsement. Washington should press hard for a negotiated settlement that restores a power-sharing government, condition intelligence-sharing on compliance with the laws of war, and keep its own counterterrorism operations against AQAP narrowly focused.

Yemen is a Saudi war of choice fought in an Arabian civil war we do not understand well. We should help our ally avoid losing it badly — not help them wage it.

The United States should engage where its direct interests are at stake—counterterrorism operations against AQAP leadership, for instance—but should avoid becoming a party to an intra-Yemeni conflict whose outcome remains uncertain and whose costs could extend far beyond what any administration has acknowledged.

The question “Is Yemen America’s fight?” is too narrow if it means “Should America pick the Saudi side and help fight the Houthis?” The answer to that should be no. But the United States cannot wash its hands of Yemen either. A country of roughly 25 million people is sliding toward state collapse, and collapse will strengthen exactly the forces Washington fears: Al Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula, smugglers, warlords and regional patrons who profit from fragmentation.

A better American role would be diplomatic and humanitarian rather than military. The Houthis are not liberal democrats, and their takeover of Sana was coercive and dangerous. But they are also a real Yemeni political and military force with roots in the country’s north. They cannot be bombed out of Yemen. Nor can President Hadi simply be restored by foreign airstrikes and treated as the answer to Yemen’s legitimacy crisis. The transition after the 2011 uprising left many Yemenis dissatisfied, including southerners, Houthis, youth activists and those angry at corruption and elite bargaining. Airstrikes do not resolve those grievances.

The Saudi coalition’s campaign may even harden the conflict along sectarian lines. Yemen’s Zaydi tradition is not the same as Iranian Twelver Shiism, and Yemen’s politics have historically not mapped neatly onto the Sunni-Shiite wars now tearing apart parts of the region. If Washington accepts the Saudi framing of the Houthis as an Iranian spearhead, it will help import a regional sectarian logic into a conflict that already has enough combustible material. Iran should not get a free pass for meddling, but exaggerating its control over the Houthis could become a self-fulfilling prophecy, driving the Houthis to seek more support from Tehran.

The United States has leverage with Saudi Arabia and should use it to shorten, not facilitate, the war. It should support a U.N.-led process that brings the Houthis, Hadi loyalists, southern representatives, tribal leaders and parties such as Islah into negotiations. It should push for an arms embargo on all sides that violate agreements, targeted sanctions on spoilers, and immediate humanitarian access. It should also separate its narrow counterterrorism operations from the broader Yemeni civil war as much as possible, recognizing that drone strikes cannot substitute for a functioning state.

There are moments when allies need reassurance. But reassurance can take the form of missile defense cooperation, maritime security, intelligence on genuine cross-border threats and diplomatic backing against Iranian escalation. It need not include assistance to a bombing campaign whose strategic endpoint is unclear.

Yemen is America’s responsibility in the sense that U.S. choices matter and past policies helped shape the current landscape. It is not America’s fight in the sense of joining one camp in a civil war. The United States should be a broker, relief provider and counterterrorism watchdog — not an accessory to regional escalation.

Walk Away From This One

The United States has no compelling national interest in helping Saudi Arabia bomb Yemen, and every reason to expect that involvement — even "indirect" involvement of the kind currently underway — will end badly. Washington should pull back its logistical and intelligence support and tell Riyadh that this is its war to fight or, better, to end.

Start with the basic question of who the Houthis are. They are a Zaydi Shia movement from northern Yemen with a long list of local grievances against the Sanaa government. They are not an Iranian proxy in any meaningful sense, however convenient that label is for the Saudis. Tehran provides some money and rhetoric; it does not direct Houthi operations, and the Houthis advanced into Sanaa last September on the back of an alliance with forces loyal to former president Ali Abdullah Saleh — the same Saleh the United States once worked with against Al Qaeda. The notion that we are blunting an Iranian march to the Red Sea is a Saudi talking point, not a fact.

Meanwhile, the Houthis are at war with Al Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula, the single most dangerous Al Qaeda franchise to the American homeland. AQAP has tried repeatedly to blow up U.S.-bound airliners. By degrading the Houthis, the Saudi coalition is directly relieving pressure on the group we have been hunting from drones for a decade. That is not an alliance of convenience; it is a strategic contradiction.

Add the humanitarian dimension. Yemen is the poorest country in the Arab world, dependent on imports for roughly 90 percent of its food. A Saudi air and naval campaign that closes ports and destroys infrastructure will produce famine, displacement and exactly the kind of state collapse in which jihadist groups thrive. The civilian toll will be attributed, fairly, to American bombs and American targeting cells.

Finally, consider the precedent. We are being drawn in not because anyone has made a case to Congress or the public, but because the Saudis asked and the White House did not want to say no while nuclear talks with Iran are at a delicate stage. That is a terrible reason to enter another Middle Eastern war. If the lesson of Iraq, Libya and Syria is anything, it is that American firepower in Arab civil conflicts tends to deepen the chaos it was meant to resolve.

The Houthis are not our enemy. AQAP is. Helping the Saudis fight the wrong war, in the wrong country, against the wrong adversary is a mistake we should refuse to make.

Framing the debate as a choice between “getting involved” or “avoiding involvement” misses the point entirely. It should work with Oman, which has kept channels to the Houthis open, and with Iran itself to broker a power-sharing arrangement that halts the fighting. The real fight for America should be for a peace deal — one that stops the bloodshed, saves lives, and prevents the country from becoming a permanent terrorist base. That requires turning off the logistical spigot, not arguing over how tightly it is calibrated.

A War Sold on a Slogan

Before American officials sign off on another Middle Eastern campaign, they might consider how thin the public case for this one actually is. We are told that Iran is taking over Yemen through the Houthis, that Saudi Arabia is defending the regional order, and that backing Riyadh is therefore a natural extension of longstanding U.S. policy. Almost every part of that story is exaggerated or false, and the consequences of acting as if it were true will fall, as usual, on Yemeni civilians.

The Houthis are not Hezbollah. They are a homegrown movement rooted in the Zaydi communities of Saada, with deep grievances against decades of marginalization. Iran's support has grown, but U.S. intelligence officials themselves have repeatedly said that Tehran did not order the Houthi advance on Sanaa and does not control their decision-making. To recast this as Iranian aggression is to grant the Saudis a sectarian framing that suits their domestic politics far more than it describes reality.

Saudi Arabia, meanwhile, is not a status-quo power being forced into reluctant action. It is an absolute monarchy that has spent decades exporting an intolerant strand of Sunni Islam, that crushed a peaceful uprising in Bahrain in 2011, that is currently flogging a blogger for the crime of free expression, and whose air force has, in its first week of operations in Yemen, already hit a refugee camp.
