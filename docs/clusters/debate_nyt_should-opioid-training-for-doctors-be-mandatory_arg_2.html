"Mandatory federal opioid training for all doctors is an ineffective, symbolic gesture that fails to address the systemic causes of the opioid crisis and may negatively impact patient care."

Mandatory opioid training sounds sensible, but it risks becoming another symbolic gesture that lets policymakers claim action while avoiding harder work. Doctors do need better education about pain, addiction and prescribing. The question is whether federal coercion attached to prescribing authority is the best way to get it. I am not convinced.

The opioid crisis was not caused simply by doctors lacking a course. It grew from aggressive pharmaceutical marketing, patient-satisfaction pressures, the elevation of pain as a “fifth vital sign,” fragmented medical records, limited access to addiction treatment, economic despair and a health system that often pays for pills more readily than physical therapy, counseling or multidisciplinary pain care. A mandatory module may check a box while leaving those incentives intact. Worse, it may push some clinicians to abandon pain patients wholesale, not because opioids are always wrong, but because the regulatory climate makes them feel too risky.

The American Medical Association is right to worry about one-size-fits-all mandates. A family physician in rural America, an oncologist, an emergency doctor and a dermatologist do not prescribe opioids in the same way. Their educational needs differ. Doctors also already face state licensing requirements, hospital credentialing rules, board certification demands and continuing education obligations. Piling on a federal requirement can produce resentment and compliance behavior rather than genuine learning.

A better approach would combine targeted education with accountability. Require high-volume or high-risk prescribers to receive intensive training. Use prescription drug monitoring programs to identify dangerous patterns, such as overlapping opioid and benzodiazepine prescriptions or unusually high doses. Make medical schools and residency programs teach addiction medicine and pain management properly, so the next generation is not retrofitted after the fact. Expand access to buprenorphine and methadone treatment. Hold drug manufacturers and pill mills accountable. Reform insurance coverage so non-opioid pain treatments are practical options.

Voluntary professional education can be strengthened without making every prescriber jump through the same hoop. States and specialty boards can tailor requirements more intelligently than a broad federal mandate. The danger is not that education is unimportant; it is that mandatory training will be mistaken for a solution. The country needs a serious opioid strategy, not merely another certificate in a doctor’s file.

"Mandatory opioid training for doctors should not be implemented because there is no evidence it effectively reduces overdose deaths and it distracts from more impactful policy interventions."

Before reflexively endorsing mandatory training, we should ask a harder question: is there good evidence that it will actually reduce overdose deaths? The honest answer is that we don't know. The FDA's voluntary REMS program for extended-release opioids has been running since 2012, and rigorous evaluations of its impact on prescribing behavior and patient outcomes are thin. Layering on a federal mandate without that evidence base risks substituting the appearance of action for action itself.

Consider what mandatory training typically looks like in practice. A physician logs into an online module, clicks through slides, answers a multiple-choice quiz, and prints a certificate. Whether that experience changes prescribing decisions a year later at 4 p.m. on a Friday, with a demanding patient in the room, is an empirical question—and the literature on continuing medical education generally finds that passive, didactic formats produce modest behavior change at best.

There are also better-targeted tools available. Prescription drug monitoring programs (PDMPs), which let physicians see a patient's controlled-substance history at the point of care, have produced measurable reductions in doctor-shopping and high-risk prescribing in states like New York and Kentucky. Expanding access to naloxone, broadening medication-assisted treatment with buprenorphine, and reforming pain-management metrics in hospital reimbursement schemes (the infamous HCAHPS pain questions) all have stronger evidence behind them than mandatory CME.

None of this means training is worthless. The CDC's March 2016 guidelines are genuinely useful, and many physicians would benefit from working through them carefully. But "useful" and "should be federally mandated for every prescriber in the country" are different propositions. A national mandate creates uniform compliance burdens while doing little to distinguish the overprescribing pain clinic from the conscientious internist.

If policymakers want to require something, they should require participation in PDMPs, not seat time in a webinar. They should fund residency-level curriculum reform, where habits are actually formed. And they should be honest that the opioid crisis is now being driven increasingly by illicit fentanyl and heroin—problems no amount of physician education will solve. Mandatory training is the easy answer. The harder work lies elsewhere.

  3. 03Alternative tools, such as Prescription Drug Monitoring Programs and expanded access to naloxone, have a much stronger evidence base for reducing opioid-related harm.
  4. 04A federal mandate imposes a uniform administrative burden on all physicians without addressing the specific sources of overprescribing.
  5. 05Physician education cannot solve the current opioid crisis because the epidemic is increasingly driven by illicit substances like fentanyl and heroin rather than prescription practices.
Diversified Claude

"The federal government should not mandate opioid training for doctors because it creates unintended consequences that harm patients and the medical profession without effectively targeting the actual sources of the crisis."

I'm a primary care physician, and I want to push back on the rush to make opioid training a federal mandate. Not because training is bad—it isn't—but because mandates of this kind tend to produce predictable unintended consequences that the policy conversation is ignoring.

The first consequence is overcorrection. Already, in the wake of CDC guidelines and DEA enforcement actions, colleagues of mine are simply refusing to prescribe opioids at all. Chronic pain patients who have been stable for years on moderate doses are being abruptly tapered or discharged. Cancer survivors, sickle-cell patients, and people with severe neuropathic pain are reporting that they cannot find physicians willing to treat them. A national mandate signals, in effect, that opioid prescribing is presumptively suspect. Many busy clinicians will respond rationally by exiting the practice altogether, leaving the most vulnerable patients to suffer or to seek relief on the street.

The second consequence is regulatory creep. Once the FDA establishes a precedent of mandating training for a drug class, the model will be extended. Will we soon require mandatory training for benzodiazepines? Stimulants? Anticoagulants, which kill more people than opioids in some age groups? Each addition is defensible in isolation; collectively, they transform the practice of medicine into an exercise in compliance.

The third issue is targeting. The overprescribers driving the crisis are not, by and large, thoughtful internists who would benefit from a refresher course. They are a relatively small number of high-volume prescribers operating in identifiable patterns that state PDMPs and DEA data can flag. A precision approach—auditing outliers, revoking licenses, prosecuting pill mills—would do far more good than blanketing 700,000 DEA registrants with mandatory CME.

I support better education on pain and addiction. I support it being built into residency training, into board recertification, and into the routine work of professional societies. What I do not support is a federal mandate that treats every prescriber as a potential abuser, that further deters appropriate pain treatment, and that substitutes symbolic policy for the harder work of going after the actual bad actors. The road to a worse problem is paved with good mandates.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Mandatory training signals that opioid prescribing is inherently suspect, causing many physicians to stop treating chronic pain patients entirely to avoid regulatory scrutiny.
  2. 02Establishing a federal mandate for one drug class sets a precedent for regulatory creep that could eventually burden medical practice with endless compliance requirements for various medications.
  3. 03The policy unfairly targets all clinicians rather than focusing on the small number of high-volume overprescribers and 'pill mills' that are the primary drivers of the opioid epidemic.
  4. 04Education on pain and addiction should be integrated into existing professional structures like residency and board certification rather than being imposed as a symbolic federal mandate.

As a physician who has practiced for over two decades, I find myself caught between two concerns that pull in different directions. The opioid crisis is real and devastating—I've seen patients struggle with addiction and families shattered by overdose. No one in good conscience can ignore this tragedy. Yet I am also deeply skeptical of mandatory training as the solution.

Here's what concerns me most: mandatory training implicitly assumes that the problem is physician ignorance. But most of us know the risks. What we often lack are viable alternatives. When a patient with chronic back pain returns for the third visit, having tried physical therapy and anti-inflammatories without adequate relief, what options do we realistically offer? Insurance may not cover acupuncture. Pain management specialists have months-long wait lists. The patient wants relief. The path of least resistance is another prescription.

Training might teach me to say "no" more firmly, but does that help the patient sitting across from me in pain?

Furthermore, mandatory requirements tend to be blunt instruments. They treat all prescribers as equally culpable, when in fact most physicians practice responsibly. The exceptions—pill mills and reckless prescribers—should face disciplinary action, not collective punishment of an entire profession.

If training is implemented, it should be paired with systemic changes: better insurance coverage for alternative treatments, prescription monitoring programs that actually work, and support systems for patients struggling with chronic pain without effective medications. Education without these supports is incomplete.

I support any initiative that helps patients—I'm just not convinced this particular approach will achieve that goal.

"Mandatory federal opioid training for all doctors should be rejected because it is an inefficient bureaucratic burden that fails to address the root causes of the overdose epidemic."

The impulse to mandate opioid training for physicians is understandable. The rise in overdose deaths is a national tragedy, and policymakers are desperate for solutions. However, forcing all doctors to undergo a federal training program is a blunt, bureaucratic instrument that will do little to stop the epidemic while placing an unnecessary burden on an already strained healthcare system. The American Medical Association is right to oppose it.

First, a blanket mandate ignores the diversity of medical practice. A blanket requirement would force oncologists, palliative care specialists, and hospice doctors—professionals who already possess deep expertise in managing severe pain—to sit through generic training modules designed for general practitioners. It treats all physicians as if they are equally responsible for the prescription drug crisis, when the reality is that a disproportionate amount of dangerous prescribing stems from a small minority of doctors, some of whom operate illicit "pill mills" that no amount of education will fix. Those bad actors require law enforcement intervention, not a multiple-choice F.D.A. quiz.

Furthermore, medicine is already drowning in administrative mandates. Physicians spend hours each day clicking through electronic health records, fighting with insurance companies over prior authorizations, and completing endless compliance modules. Adding another federal mandate subtracts from the very limited time doctors have to actually see and speak with their patients.

If the F.D.A. and policymakers want to improve prescribing practices, they should empower state medical boards and specialty societies to tailor continuing medical education (CME) requirements to the specific needs of their practitioners. The recently released CDC guidelines for prescribing opioids for chronic pain offer a much better model: providing clear, evidence-based recommendations that doctors can integrate into their clinical judgment. True medical reform comes from fostering better clinical practices at the peer level, not from heavy-handed federal mandates that treat physicians as part of the problem rather than partners in the solution.

"Mandatory opioid training for doctors is an ineffective policy that misidentifies the root causes of the addiction crisis and unfairly scapegoats physicians for a complex social and structural epidemic."

Mandating opioid training for doctors is a convenient scapegoat that misdiagnoses a fundamentally social disease. The rising tide of opioid addiction and overdose is not a narrow failure of physician knowledge; it is a symptom of a much larger crisis of despair, driven by decades of deindustrialization, eroding community ties, and a health system that treats pain as a chemical imbalance rather than a human experience. To single out doctors for mandatory reeducation is to ignore the structural forces that made OxyContin a household name and to punish the profession in a way that will not halt the dying.

Consider the timeline. The explosion in opioid prescribing between 1999 and 2010 was fueled by a well-documented campaign of misinformation from pharmaceutical companies, aggressively promoted through key opinion leaders and pain advocacy groups that insisted addiction risk was minimal. Many physicians were acting on what they were told was the standard of care. Today, prescription rates are already declining, yet the death rate keeps climbing because people have shifted to heroin and illicit fentanyl. The crisis has moved beyond the prescription pad. A mandatory training course retroactively implies that front-line doctors were the architects of the epidemic, when in reality they were as much its targets as its unwitting vectors.

More deeply, the opioid crisis maps onto what economists Anne Case and Angus Deaton have called “deaths of despair.” Regions with the highest overdose rates are often those with the most job loss, the weakest social bonds, and the greatest sense of hopelessness. Training doctors about the mu-opioid receptor will not give a laid-off factory worker a reason to live or provide accessible, affordable mental health care. If we are serious about saving lives, the policy response must include a massive investment in addiction treatment, mental health services, and economic revitalization—not another checkbox on a physician’s license renewal. Doctors do need education, but making it mandatory by federal decree turns a complex tragedy into a blame game that lets the far more powerful drug companies and the wider society off the hook.

"Mandatory opioid training for doctors is an insufficient solution that fails to address the systemic healthcare failures driving the addiction crisis."

Debating whether opioid training for doctors should be mandatory is largely a distraction from the systemic failures driving the addiction crisis. While requiring physicians to learn more about the risks of opioids sounds like a common-sense victory, it is merely a band-aid on a deeply broken healthcare system. If we truly want to stem the tide of overdose deaths, we need structural changes, not just another continuing education requirement.

Consider the reality of primary care in America today. A doctor often has just fifteen minutes to see a patient complaining of chronic lower back pain. In that brief window, prescribing a generic opioid is cheap, fast, and fully covered by insurance. What happens if that doctor—newly educated by mandatory F.D.A. training—decides to prescribe physical therapy, cognitive behavioral therapy, or acupuncture instead? The patient will likely face massive out-of-pocket costs, strict limits on visits, or outright denial of coverage from their insurance provider. We cannot train our way out of a crisis when the financial incentives of the healthcare system actively push patients and providers toward cheap pills.

Moreover, if we are going to mandate anything, we should mandate universal, interoperable Prescription Drug Monitoring Programs (PDMPs). Currently, doctors in many states are not required to check a database to see if their patient has recently received opioid prescriptions from other providers. A doctor can be perfectly trained in addiction science but still unwittingly prescribe to a patient who is doctor-shopping across state lines because the data is siloed or voluntary.

Finally, we must address the glaring lack of access to addiction treatment. Only a fraction of doctors are waivered to prescribe buprenorphine, a crucial medication for treating opioid use disorder, due to absurdly strict federal regulations. It is incredibly hypocritical for the government to mandate training for prescribing drugs that cause addiction, while making it prohibitively difficult for doctors to prescribe the drugs that treat it. Mandatory education is fine in theory, but without fixing insurance disincentives, modernizing data systems, and expanding treatment access, it will achieve very little.

Mandatory opioid training sounds sensible, but it risks becoming the kind of symbolic regulation that makes officials look serious while doing little to save lives. Doctors should know how to prescribe opioids safely. But compulsion is not automatically effectiveness. A mandate that substitutes a lecture for a health system will let too many policymakers congratulate themselves while the crisis continues.
