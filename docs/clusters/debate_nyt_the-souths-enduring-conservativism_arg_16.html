"The Deep South remains more conservative than the rest of the country primarily because of the Republican Party's long-term strategic organizational investment and the Democratic Party's subsequent abandonment of the region."

Let me offer a more cynical answer than the others on offer: the Deep South is conservative because the Republican Party has invested enormous resources, over four decades, in making it so, and Democrats have largely conceded the ground.

Political behavior isn't just a reflection of underlying culture. It's a product of organization, recruitment, messaging, and money. The Republican Party in the 1970s and 1980s made a deliberate strategic decision — call it the Southern Strategy, call it whatever you like — to invest in evangelical outreach, talk-radio infrastructure, state legislative races, and judicial appointments across the South. That investment compounded. Today, a young white Southerner grows up in a media environment dominated by conservative talk radio and Fox News, attends a church whose denominational leadership is openly aligned with the GOP, and is represented at every level of government by Republicans who deliver concrete benefits to their constituents.

Meanwhile, the Democratic Party essentially abandoned the white South after 1994. With a handful of exceptions, the party stopped recruiting credible candidates for rural Southern districts, stopped investing in state parties, and stopped trying to construct an economic message that might compete with cultural conservatism. The result is a region where, in many counties, no Democrat has appeared on a ballot for years. Voters cannot choose what is not offered to them.

This is not the whole story — race, religion, and history all play their roles — but it is a part of the story that gets systematically underweighted because it implicates the present rather than the past. The PRRI poll showing Romney up 40 points among Southern white working-class voters reflects forty years of one party showing up and the other not bothering.

It also suggests, contrary to most of the fatalistic commentary, that the situation is not permanent. Political conditions made by deliberate organizational choices can be unmade by deliberate organizational choices. The South was solidly Democratic for a century, then it flipped. To assume it cannot flip again, or at least become genuinely competitive, is to mistake a recent equilibrium for a natural law.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Republican Party built a powerful conservative infrastructure through decades of investment in evangelical outreach, media dominance, and local political recruitment.
  2. 02The Democratic Party effectively conceded the region by failing to recruit credible candidates or maintain an economic message that could compete with cultural conservatism.
  3. 03Political behavior in the South is a product of deliberate organizational choices and resource allocation rather than just an unchangeable reflection of underlying culture.
  4. 04The current conservative dominance is not a permanent natural law, as political alignments can be shifted again through renewed organizational effort.
