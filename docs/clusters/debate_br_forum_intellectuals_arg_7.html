"While Robin D. G. Kelley correctly identifies the historical role of organic intellectuals in fighting fascism, his response is insufficient because it fails to advocate for the disciplined, institutional political organization necessary to seize power and defeat the fascist bloc."

Robin D. G. Kelley’s sweeping reconsideration of intellectuals’ responsibilities is a vital intervention, rightly centering the Black antifascist tradition and exposing the complicity of university administrations in today’s reactionary offensive. Yet for all its historical richness, the essay stops short at the threshold of the most urgent question: how are organic intellectuals to be organized into a coherent political force capable of defeating the fascist bloc? Kelley gestures toward unionization, movement spaces, and a nebulous “new university” but never confronts the hard institutional logic required to transform dispersed insurgent energies into a counter-hegemonic bloc that can seize and hold power. His call to “cast our lot with the insurgent class” is stirring, but without the infrastructure of strategic coordination, organic intellectuals risk becoming little more than the radical wing of the university’s crisis-management machinery—useful for channeling dissent into symbolic concessions, but incapable of breaking the stranglehold of finance capital and the national security state over intellectual life.

The essay’s historical narrative itself demands a sharper political conclusion. Kelley powerfully demonstrates that the Black antifascist organic intellectuals of the 1930s and 1940s—from St. Clair Drake to Claudia Jones—were not merely critics but disciplined militants embedded in organizations that aimed at state transformation. Jones was a leader of the Communist Party USA; Drake worked through the pacifist movement and later, in his Chicago years, engaged with the Congress of Industrial Organizations; the Black Panther Party, which Kelley invokes in passing, fused organic intellectual production with the building of a vanguard party. These formations were not identical, but they shared a premise that today’s movementism largely abandons: the necessity of a political instrument that can coordinate local struggles, impose programmatic discipline, and contest for sovereign power. In contrast, the contemporary left’s organizational landscape is dominated by advocacy networks, non-profit projects, and ad-hoc coalitions—what the late Marxist scholar Daniel Bensaïd termed the “movement of movements”—which remain structurally incapable of confronting the centralized, authoritarian state that MAGA and its organic intellectuals are constructing.

To be sure, Kelley’s own examples of resistance—UCLA’s Institute on Inequality and Democracy, Scholars for Social Justice, the Working Families Party—are commendable experiments in carving out space for radical thought. But they largely operate within the limits of what the system permits: foundation funding, non-profit tax status, university appointments. The Institute on Inequality and Democracy, for all its activism, is still embedded in the same institutional structure that Chancellor Howard Gilman weaponized against the Palestine solidarity encampment. Its existence does not fundamentally challenge the power of trustees and donors to fire scholars like Maura Finkelstein or deploy police against students; it merely demonstrates that, in the right circumstances, some radicals can find temporary shelter. That is not a strategy for the destruction of fascism—it is a survival tactic for a besieged left intelligentsia.

The essay’s treatment of unionization, while welcome, suffers from a similar limitation. Unionizing faculty and graduate workers is important, but the contemporary American labor movement is itself integrated into the Democratic Party’s electoral machine, its leadership overwhelmingly tied to a liberal agenda that has, for decades, accommodated military Keynesianism, mass incarceration, and imperial war. The major academic unions—the American Association of University Professors, the American Federation of Teachers, and even many campus-based units of the United Auto Workers—have been reluctant to take strong positions on Palestine, and in some cases actively collaborated with university administrations to suppress encampments under the guise of health and safety concerns. To expect that expanding such unions will deliver the “academic freedom, free speech, and the right to protest” Kelley invokes is to ignore the class-collaborationist history of American trade unionism. Real academic freedom in this conjuncture would require unions willing to strike not merely for better contracts but for the dismantling of university police forces, the divestment from weapons manufacturers, and the complete severance of ties with Israeli academic institutions complicit in scholasticide. That kind of militancy would demand a political break with the Democratic Party and the formation of a genuinely independent working-class pole—nothing less than a new communist or socialist party.

This is where the legacy of organic intellectuals must be pushed to its logical conclusion. Gramsci’s concept was not a romantic celebration of subaltern knowledge; it was a strategic insight into how class rule is organized and contested through the production of consent. The fascist right has been building its own organic intellectual apparatus for decades—through the Claremont Institute, Hillsdale College, the Federalist Society, and now Project 2025’s shadow civil service. These are not mere “movements”; they are state-building projects with a clear theory of executive power and a coordinated plan to capture every lever of administrative and judicial authority. The left, by contrast, has invested its collective energy into a constellation of critical journals, non-profit research centers, and social media influencers that, dazzling as they sometimes are, operate primarily at the level of critique and cultural intervention. The result is a profound asymmetry: one side has an ideological infrastructure geared toward taking the state, while the other has a discourse infrastructure geared toward making the university slightly more bearable.

The consequences of this asymmetry were already visible by early 2025. The Trump administration’s Project Esther, which Kelley cites, was not just a propaganda campaign; it was an operational program that used legal harassment, visa revocation, and financial audits to physically remove pro-Palestine international students and scholars from the country. Universities, as Kelley notes, largely capitulated. But what was the strategic response? More petitions, more statements of solidarity, more op-eds, more encampments. All of these had immense moral value, but they could not stop the deportations or the funding cuts because they were not linked to an organizational form capable of exerting real material leverage—such as a general strike of academic workers across the country, or a coordinated campaign of international solidarity that would threaten the economic interests of the donor class.

Kelley’s essay inadvertently reveals this strategic deficit when he celebrates Tiffany Willoughby-Herard’s brave statement while being arrested: “What job do I have if the students don’t have a future?” It is a profound question, but one that the essay does not fully answer. What job, indeed? If the university as currently constituted is an engine of fascist reproduction, then defending one’s place within it cannot be the horizon of political struggle. The responsibility of the organic intellectual is not only to speak truth in the face of power but to help build the counter-power that can break the existing state and erect a new one. That means, among other things, organizing to withdraw our labor and our intellectual production from institutions of complicity, and to redirect them toward the construction of alternative institutions—people’s universities, worker-controlled research centers, revolutionary publishing houses—that are explicitly tied to a mass political party capable of contesting elections and, when necessary, confronting the repressive apparatus in the streets.

Of course, the historical obstacles are immense. The destruction of the Communist Party during the McCarthy era, the demobilization of the New Left after the 1970s, and the decades-long neoliberal offensive have left the US working class without any independent political vehicle. Kelley rightly reminds us that the current repression echoes the Red Scare, but he does not draw the necessary lesson: the left survived those years not because of the ingenuity of its organic intellectuals alone, but because it had, however battered, organizational continuities—trade unions with radical leadership, underground networks, and above all a political party that, for all its deformations, provided a central point of reference. Today we have none of that. The task facing organic intellectuals is therefore nothing less than party-building. Without it, every victory won within the university will be temporary, and every sacrifice—like Willoughby-Herard’s—will be merely symbolic, an entry in the archive of noble defeats.

This is not to dismiss Kelley’s call to “create a new university.” A new university is indeed necessary, but it cannot be created from within the shell of the old one. It must be built as part of a dual power strategy, parallel to the state and eventually supplanting it. That requires organic intellectuals to step outside the comfort of their disciplinary silos and engage in the slow, unglamorous work of political organization: neighborhood-level study circles, worker education programs, tenant union legal clinics, and above all the construction of a democratic party structure that can link these disparate efforts into a national political offensive. The Black Panther Party understood this when it expanded from armed patrols into community survival programs, but it also learned, at a terrible cost, that without a mass base and a clear revolutionary line, armed propaganda and service provision alone invite state destruction.

Kelley’s essay asks “What is the responsibility of intellectuals in the age of fascism and genocide?” The answer is: to become more than intellectuals. To become revolutionaries. To recognize that the “privilege” of which Chomsky wrote is a diminishing asset, and that the only way to protect the values of justice and freedom is to politically organize for the overthrow of the system that breeds fascism. That is the lesson of St. Clair Drake, of Claudia Jones, of the Black antifascist tradition. They did not just document genocide and resist within existing institutions; they joined and built organizations that sought to dismantle empire itself. If today’s organic intellectuals fail to do the same, the fascist bloc will continue its march through the institutions, and the next Tiffany Willoughby-Herard dragged from an encampment may find that there is no longer even a camera to record her question.
