The Pandemic Was a Polarization Event, Not an Elite Failure

The most interesting fact in Macedo and Lee's essay is one they note but, I think, do not fully metabolize. Through mid-March 2020, COVID-19 policy in the United States was bipartisan. Forty-three governors issued shelter-in-place orders within three weeks of March 19. Republican and Democratic states alike closed schools, restricted gatherings, and shut nonessential businesses. The initial response was a national consensus, supported by Trump's own (inconsistent) statements and implemented with public approval that crossed partisan lines.

The divergence Macedo and Lee document—Democratic states maintaining stringent measures more than twice as long as Republican states, school closures stretching into 2021 in blue jurisdictions, the partisan sorting of attitudes toward masks and later vaccines—did not begin in February 2020. It began in late spring and accelerated through the summer. By the time the Great Barrington Declaration appeared in October, COVID-19 was no longer a public health emergency in the public mind. It was a political identity marker, structured around the upcoming election and the Trump–anti-Trump axis that dominated everything else.

This is the analytical frame I think the lead essay underweights. Macedo and Lee describe the pandemic response as a failure of expert judgment, distorted by elite class interests and a moralized intolerance of dissent. There is truth in this. But "elite failure" misses what was actually happening, which is that the pandemic response became a domain in a much broader, prior pattern of affective polarization. The substantive policy disagreements that emerged—about lockdowns, masks, schools, vaccines, lab leak—did not produce the polarization. The polarization absorbed them.

Consider the timing. Mask wearing did not become a partisan signal until Trump made it one in spring 2020, refusing to wear masks publicly and mocking opponents who did. Once that signal was set, blue Americans wore masks as identity expression and red Americans refused them as identity expression, more or less independent of evolving evidence about mask efficacy. The Great Barrington Declaration entered this environment, and the response to it—both the dismissal by Collins and Fauci and the embrace by anti-lockdown activists—was structured by the same identity logic. The declaration's authors became partisan symbols whether they wanted to be or not. Bhattacharya's eventual elevation to direct the NIH under Trump in 2025 completes the arc: the dissenters were not merely vindicated; they were absorbed into the opposing political coalition.

What does this mean for Macedo and Lee's diagnosis? Mainly that the prescription—a more honest, balanced expert discourse that acknowledges uncertainty and weighs tradeoffs—was, by mid-2020, no longer available. The expert institutions the authors criticize were not operating in a neutral public sphere where careful argument might have shifted opinion. They were operating in a political environment in which any acknowledgment of uncertainty about NPIs would have been weaponized by the Trump administration to justify abandoning the response entirely. Francis Collins's "devastating takedown" email looks ugly out of context; in context, it reflects the (probably correct) judgment that any opening for skepticism would be exploited not for reasoned policy adjustment but for total abandonment of mitigation.

This is not a justification of the suppression of dissent. It is an explanation, and the distinction matters because the prescriptive implications differ. If the problem were narrowly elite failure, the solution would be better elites: more humble, more honest, more attuned to tradeoffs. If the problem is structural—polarized publics making nuanced expert communication impossible, partisan media transforming every empirical question into an identity claim, social platforms accelerating both—then the solution requires structural change in the information environment, not just better personnel at the CDC.

Macedo and Lee gesture at this when they discuss "moral and epistemic tribalism." But they then revert to treating the failure as one of elite judgment: scientists, journalists, and academics should have been more honest, more balanced, more willing to engage dissent. I agree that they should have. I am skeptical that doing so would have changed outcomes much. The cognitive scientists who study polarization—Dan Kahan's work on cultural cognition, Lilliana Mason's on social identity, the abundant work on motivated reasoning—suggest that in a high-salience political environment, more information and more nuance from experts tend to amplify polarization, not reduce it, because partisans selectively absorb evidence consistent with their priors. The climate change case, which Macedo and Lee briefly mention, is precisely such a case: decades of careful, increasingly confident expert communication have not produced consensus because the issue is not, for most citizens, a knowledge question but an identity question.

The pandemic ran on the same track, only faster. Within months, attitudes about masks, lockdowns, and schools had crystallized into stable partisan positions that proved largely impervious to subsequent evidence. The state-level data Macedo and Lee present—Democratic states maintaining high stringency through summer 2021 even as Republican states reopened with no obvious mortality penalty—are not, in my reading, mainly a story about cautious experts insulated from costs. They are a story about partisan governance: governors responding to the preferences of their political coalitions, with Democratic voters demanding caution and Republican voters demanding normalcy. The "experts" the authors target were operating within, and partly responsive to, this partisan structure.

This reframing has implications for what a postmortem should look like.

First, it implies that institutional reform of public health agencies, while needed, will not solve the underlying problem. The CDC could become more transparent, more humble, more willing to acknowledge uncertainty—and still find its messaging absorbed into partisan identity battles in the next emergency. Reform is necessary but insufficient.

Second, it implies that the focus of the postmortem should include the political economy of polarized media and platforms, not just expert institutions. The role of cable news, partisan podcasters, and social media algorithms in transforming COVID policy into identity politics deserves as much attention as the role of the WHO and CDC. Macedo and Lee largely confine themselves to the latter.

Third, it suggests that the most important pandemic preparedness investment for the next crisis is not better NPI evidence (though we should fund that), but mechanisms for de-escalating partisan capture of public health communication. This is genuinely hard. It might involve depoliticizing the structure of expert advisory bodies, pre-committing to evidence-review protocols before crises strike, or building cross-partisan public health communication infrastructure. I do not have a clean recipe. I am confident that "more honest experts" is not, by itself, the answer.

Fourth, and finally, the polarization frame should make us cautious about the postmortem itself. The lead essay is appearing in a political moment in which a particular reading of COVID—the lockdowns were a catastrophic mistake, experts cannot be trusted, the dissenters were heroes—is ascendant in the new administration and aligned media. Macedo and Lee write carefully and do not endorse the more extreme versions of this narrative. But the public reception of their essay will be shaped by the polarized environment, with their measured critique enlisted on one side and dismissed by the other. We risk replacing one polarized misreading of COVID with another, with no greater institutional learning at the end.

The deeper challenge Macedo and Lee identify—how can liberal democracy maintain expert institutions that are both authoritative and accountable in a polarized environment?—is not specific to pandemics. It is the central institutional question of our political moment. Climate, AI, elections, foreign policy, monetary policy: each domain faces some version of the same problem, in which credentialed expertise must operate amid public distrust without becoming either unaccountable technocracy or pure partisan instrument. The pandemic was a particularly intense expression of this challenge, not an aberration from it.

So I agree with the authors that we need a reckoning. I just think the reckoning has to look harder at ourselves—at the polarized publics that, in 2020, neither expert humility nor expert confidence could have made into a deliberative democracy. The pandemic did not reveal a failure of expertise. It revealed the limits of expertise in a polity that has lost the capacity to deliberate. Recovering that capacity, not improving the experts, is the project ahead.
