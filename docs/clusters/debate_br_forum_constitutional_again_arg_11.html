"The proposal to redefine the Constitution as a mandate for progressive economic policy is a historically and legally unsound attempt to bypass the democratic process by imposing partisan preferences as constitutional requirements."

In "Make Progressive Politics Constitutional Again," Joseph Fishkin, William E. Forbath, and their co-authors offer a revealing glimpse into the contemporary progressive mind. Frustrated by their inability to enact sweeping, transformative economic policies through the democratic process, and stymied by a judiciary committed to the actual text of the Constitution, they propose a radical reinvention of American constitutional law. They argue that the Constitution is not merely a framework for limited government, but a mandate for a left-wing political economy—a "democracy-of-opportunity" that compels the redistribution of wealth, the entrenchment of labor unions, and the breaking up of corporations. This argument is historically dubious, legally unsound, and ultimately corrosive to the rule of law.

The authors’ foundational premise is that the Constitution contains a substantive vision of political economy that requires the state to prevent "oligarchy." This is a profound misreading of the American founding. The Constitution was deliberately designed to be agnostic on most specific questions of economic policy, precisely because the Framers believed such matters should be left to the democratic process within a framework of enumerated powers. The Constitution protects individual liberty, private property, and the freedom of contract. It does not mandate any particular distribution of wealth.

What the authors are proposing is essentially a left-wing mirror image of the *Lochner* era. For decades, progressives have rightly criticized the early 20th-century Supreme Court for reading laissez-faire capitalism into the Due Process Clause. Justice Oliver Wendell Holmes famously dissented in *Lochner*, writing that "a constitution is not intended to embody a particular economic theory." Yet Fishkin and his colleagues now wish to do exactly what they condemn the *Lochner* Court for doing: hijacking the Constitution to impose their preferred economic theories—this time, those of Louis Brandeis and modern progressivism—upon the nation.

This attempt to constitutionalize progressive policy preferences requires mangling the text and history of the Constitution, nowhere more obviously than in their treatment of the First Amendment. The authors lament that the current Supreme Court has "weaponized" the First Amendment by applying it to campaign finance in *Citizens United* and to compulsory union dues in *Janus*. They long for an era where the First Amendment was used to "diminish the inequalities of power between capital and labor."

But the First Amendment does not say, "Congress shall make no law abridging the freedom of speech, unless that speech creates inequalities of power." It is a categorical prohibition on government censorship. In *Citizens United*, the Court correctly recognized that preventing groups of citizens (organized as corporations) from publishing political speech during an election is a blatant violation of the First Amendment. The authors view this as protecting "oligarchy," but what they actually seek is the power of the federal government to silence political opponents to "level the playing field." As Chief Justice John Roberts has noted, the idea that the government may restrict the speech of some to enhance the relative voice of others is wholly foreign to the First Amendment.

Similarly, in *Janus*, the Court ruled that public employees cannot be forced to pay dues to a union whose political advocacy they oppose. The authors see this as a conservative attack on the "countervailing power" of labor. But forced subsidization of political speech is a classic First Amendment violation. The authors’ vision of "constitutional political economy" demands that we strip dissenting workers of their individual constitutional rights in order to artificially inflate the political power of labor unions. This is collectivism, not constitutionalism.

The essay’s treatment of federalism and the Affordable Care Act is equally unmoored from constitutional principles. The authors decry Chief Justice Roberts’ decision in *NFIB v. Sebelius*, which held that the federal government could not force states to expand Medicaid by threatening to withdraw all their existing Medicaid funding. The authors claim this decision was built on "new constitutional doctrine" and strongly imply it was motivated by a desire to preserve racial hierarchy in the South.

This is a smear that ignores the basic architecture of American federalism. The anti-coercion doctrine is not an invention of the Roberts Court; it is a necessary corollary to the principle of dual sovereignty. If the federal government can put a "gun to the head" of the states, forcing them to administer federal programs or face financial ruin, then the states cease to be independent sovereigns and become mere administrative appendages of Washington. The authors’ complaint is not that the Court misread the Constitution, but that the Constitution’s federalist structure obstructs their goal of "universal social insurance."

The authors repeatedly assert that conservative judges have strayed "too far from the views of the elected branches and the American people." But the entire purpose of a written Constitution and an independent judiciary is to enforce constitutional limits even—and especially—when those limits frustrate the momentary desires of the elected branches. If the Constitution merely reflected whatever the current Congress deemed necessary for a "democracy of opportunity," it would not be a Constitution at all; it would be a blank check for majoritarian overreach.

This becomes glaringly apparent in the authors' discussion of antitrust and corporate law. They argue that antitrust law should be weaponized to break up monopolies not to protect consumers from higher prices—the traditional, economically sound consumer-welfare standard—but to prevent the concentration of political power. They even suggest that forcing corporations to have workers on their boards (codetermination) is a constitutional imperative. This is pure policy preference masked as constitutional imperative. There is simply no plausible reading of the Sherman Act, let alone the Constitution, that mandates such radical interventions in private corporate governance.

Ultimately, "Make Progressive Politics Constitutional Again" is less a legal argument than a political strategy. Recognizing that their agenda—wealth taxes, universal basic income, the abolition of right-to-work laws, and the federal takeover of corporate chartering—often lacks sufficient democratic support, and knowing it pushes the boundaries of federal power, the authors want progressives to preemptively declare these policies constitutionally required.

This strategy is deeply cynical. It seeks to bypass the hard work of democratic persuasion by elevating partisan policy goals to the status of constitutional mandates. If progressives want a European-style social democracy, they are free to argue for it. They can propose constitutional amendments to eliminate the Electoral College, rewrite the First Amendment to allow for political censorship, and explicitly grant the federal government the power to equalize wealth. But they should not pretend that the Constitution we actually have—the one drafted in 1787 and amended over the last two centuries—secretly mandates their socialist utopia.

The Constitution is a framework for a free people to govern themselves, secure in their individual rights and protected from arbitrary government power. It is not a weapon for progressives to reshape the American economy. Conservatives have succeeded in the courts not because they have politicized the law, but because they have faithfully applied the Constitution’s original public meaning. If progressives truly wish to engage in constitutional debate, they must start with the text the Framers wrote, not the political economy they wish the Framers had mandated.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Constitution was designed to be agnostic on specific economic theories, focusing instead on limited government, private property, and individual liberty.
  2. 02The authors' vision of 'constitutional political economy' mirrors the discredited Lochner era by attempting to read a specific economic ideology into the nation's foundational law.
  3. 03The progressive critique of First Amendment jurisprudence ignores that the amendment is a categorical prohibition on government censorship rather than a tool for leveling political playing fields.
  4. 04Protecting public employees from forced union dues and preventing federal coercion of state Medicaid programs are necessary applications of individual rights and federalism, not attacks on social justice.
  5. 05The purpose of an independent judiciary is to uphold written constitutional limits even when they frustrate the policy goals of the current majority.
  6. 06Redefining antitrust and corporate law as constitutional imperatives lacks any basis in the actual text of the Constitution or the Sherman Act.
