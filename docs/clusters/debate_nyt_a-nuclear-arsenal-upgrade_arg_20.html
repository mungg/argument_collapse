"The United States must modernize all three legs of its nuclear triad, including the development of flexible and targeted weapons, to ensure a credible deterrent against evolving global threats."

A nuclear arsenal that cannot adapt to real-world threats is not a deterrent; it is a museum exhibit. The United States should modernize all three legs of the nuclear triad and retain flexible options, including limited, accurate nuclear weapons, not because nuclear war is desirable but because deterrence depends on convincing adversaries that every form of nuclear coercion will fail.

The United States faces a much different environment than it did when President Obama spoke in Prague in 2009. Russia has seized Crimea, threatened neighbors, invested heavily in its own nuclear forces and brandished nuclear rhetoric. It is accused of violating the Intermediate-Range Nuclear Forces Treaty. China is expanding and diversifying its forces. North Korea has conducted nuclear and missile tests. In such a world, unilateral restraint does not automatically produce stability.

The gravest weakness in a purely “big retaliation” posture is that it may not be credible. If Russia used a small nuclear weapon against a NATO military target, would an American president really respond by destroying Russian cities? If the answer is no, then Moscow may conclude that it can use limited nuclear escalation to freeze a conflict on its terms. Deterrence requires denying that option. A smaller, more targeted weapon may be morally uncomfortable, but it can be strategically stabilizing if it convinces an adversary that limited nuclear use will not create a sanctuary.

Modernization should include the Columbia-class ballistic missile submarine, a new bomber, replacement of aging Minuteman III missiles, updated warheads, the B61 life-extension program and a long-range standoff missile. It should also include hardened, redundant command-and-control systems. Much of the current arsenal was designed decades ago. Keeping it indefinitely without replacement is not prudence; it is decay.

Critics emphasize the trillion-dollar price tag, but spread over thirty years it represents a limited share of defense spending. The cost of failed deterrence would be immeasurably higher. Moreover, nuclear forces allow the United States to deter existential threats without matching every adversary tank for tank or missile for missile.

This does not mean abandoning arms control. Verification, transparency and negotiated limits remain valuable, especially with Russia. But arms control works when it is backed by strength and compliance, not wishful thinking. The United States should not build nuclear weapons casually, nor should it design them for battlefield enthusiasm. Yet it must maintain a force that adversaries believe can respond proportionately and decisively. Credibility is not warmongering; it is what keeps nuclear weapons from being used.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The deteriorating global security environment, characterized by nuclear expansion in Russia, China, and North Korea, makes unilateral restraint a risk to stability.
  2. 02A posture relying solely on massive retaliation lacks credibility against limited nuclear use, whereas smaller, targeted weapons can deter adversaries from low-level nuclear escalation.
  3. 03The current nuclear arsenal is aging into obsolescence and requires comprehensive replacement of delivery systems, warheads, and command-and-control infrastructure to remain functional.
  4. 04The long-term financial cost of modernization is a manageable portion of the defense budget and is far lower than the potential cost of a failure in deterrence.
  5. 05Maintaining a strong and modern nuclear force provides the necessary leverage to pursue effective arms control and ensures that adversaries view U.S. responses as proportionate and decisive.
