Vanilla Gemini

"While experimentalist governance is the correct strategic framework for addressing climate change, it will only succeed if it is backed by aggressive, economically disruptive 'penalty defaults' capable of overcoming the existential resistance of the fossil fuel industry."

David G. Victor and Charles Sabel offer a deeply pragmatic, structurally sound diagnosis of the failures of global climate diplomacy. By correctly identifying that the top-down, consensus-driven model of the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) is ill-equipped to handle the immense complexities of deep decarbonization, they clear the air of a pervasive diplomatic delusion. Their proposed alternative—experimentalist governance—provides a compelling operational blueprint. Shifting our focus from monolithic, legally binding global treaties to sector-by-sector innovation, driven by frontline actors and enforced by "penalty defaults," is precisely the strategic pivot the climate crisis demands.

However, while the architecture of the Montreal Protocol serves as an instructional model for this kind of governance, its political economy is dangerously misleading when applied to the fossil fuel economy. Victor and Sabel accurately extract the governance mechanisms that made Montreal work, but they underestimate the sheer gravitational pull of the carbon economy. To make experimentalist governance succeed in the 2020s, we must recognize that the "penalty defaults" required to break the fossil fuel status quo will need to be vastly more muscular, confrontational, and economically disruptive than anything attempted in the 1980s.

The core limitation of the Montreal analogy lies in the nature of the pollutant and the industries that produce it. Chlorofluorocarbons (CFCs) and other ozone-depleting substances were discrete industrial chemicals utilized in specific, largely peripheral sectors: refrigerants, aerosols, and solvents. When the ozone hole was discovered, the chemical manufacturers—most notably DuPont—initially resisted regulation. But once the science became undeniable and the regulatory writing was on the wall, these firms realized they could pivot. In fact, DuPont championed the phase-out of CFCs precisely because they held the patents for the hydrofluorocarbon (HFC) alternatives. The transition was a manageable, and ultimately profitable, technological substitution for a concentrated sector.

Greenhouse gas emissions, by contrast, are not a byproduct of a specific chemical process; they are the fundamental energy backbone of modern industrial civilization. Decarbonizing the global economy does not mean asking a few chemical companies to switch their product lines; it means rendering trillions of dollars of fossil fuel reserves permanently stranded. It presents an existential threat to multinational oil majors, massive utility companies, and entirely petroleum-dependent sovereign states.

Consequently, Victor and Sabel’s vision of sectoral working groups, where industry regulators and producers collaborate to find alternatives, risks profound regulatory capture if applied naively to fossil fuels. The fossil fuel industry has spent the last three decades systematically funding disinformation, capturing legislative bodies, and delaying action. Unlike the makers of metered-dose inhalers or refrigerators, the incumbent giants of the carbon economy will not sit at the table in good faith to engineer their own demise. If they are invited to co-design the transition, they will use the language of experimentalism—pilot projects, feasibility studies, and transition schedules—to institutionalize delay.

This means that for experimentalist governance to work, the "penalty defaults"—the threats that make inaction riskier than innovation—must be external and incredibly severe. Victor and Sabel note that penalty defaults destabilize the status quo, transforming obstruction into a risky bet. But where do these defaults come from today? The Paris Agreement, as they note, provides a moral and political authorization for action, but it lacks teeth. In November 2020, as we look to rebuild the post-pandemic global economy, the necessary penalty defaults must be forged in the realms of hard economic policy: international trade, financial regulation, and aggressive industrial policy.

At the international level, the most potent penalty default currently under development is the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM). As the European Union moves forward with its European Green Deal, the implementation of a CBAM would effectively impose a tariff on carbon-intensive imports from countries that fail to price or regulate emissions. This perfectly aligns with Victor and Sabel’s framework: it does not demand global consensus at the WTO or the UNFCCC, but rather uses the leverage of a massive economic bloc to force a sectoral penalty on laggards. If the EU penalizes imported steel or cement made with high-carbon energy, producers in China, India, and the United States will be forced to innovate simply to maintain market access. This is experimentalist governance with real geopolitical leverage. The incoming Biden administration should immediately look to coordinate with the EU on such border adjustments to create a transatlantic penalty default for high-emitting sectors.

A second vital domain for penalty defaults lies in the global financial system. Central banks, financial regulators, and massive asset managers are slowly waking up to the systemic risks of climate change. Efforts to mandate climate-risk disclosures and perform climate stress-tests on major financial institutions operate precisely on the logic of experimentalist governance. By requiring banks to transparently model how their portfolios would fare under rapid decarbonization scenarios, regulators force the market to price in the risk of stranded assets. If the cost of capital for coal plants and oil exploration skyrockets because investors fear future regulation, the market itself becomes a penalty default. Frontline actors—in this case, investors and corporate boards—are forced to shift capital toward clean energy, not out of altruism, but out of financial self-preservation.

Furthermore, applying experimentalist governance to the global climate crisis requires a massive escalation of the funding mechanisms Victor and Sabel praise. The authors rightly highlight the Montreal Protocol’s Multilateral Fund, which subsidized the transition for developing nations and helped build local administrative capacity. It was an essential tool for preventing diplomatic deadlock. However, replacing CFCs globally cost roughly a few billion dollars over a couple of decades. Transitioning the Global South away from fossil fuels, adapting to the locked-in impacts of a warming planet, and building out zero-carbon grids will require trillions of dollars in new capital.

If we are to piece together partial successes across diverse nations, the Bretton Woods institutions—the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank—must be radically repurposed. Developing nations cannot be expected to act as laboratories for green innovation if they are simultaneously suffocated by sovereign debt and starved of capital. A true climate experimentalism requires debt forgiveness tied to climate action, massive green technology transfers, and the issuance of Special Drawing Rights to finance clean infrastructure. Without a financial engine scaled to the problem, the Global South will simply be locked out of the sectoral innovations occurring in Europe, California, or China.

Domestically, the political dynamics of experimentalist governance also require a more forceful approach than Victor and Sabel imply. They note that progressive calls for massive public investments, like the Green New Deal, are "often silent on governance." While this may be true of the broadest rhetorical frameworks, the underlying political economy of these movements is vital for providing the political pressure that makes experimentalism possible. The youth climate movement and progressive insurgents have done more to threaten the social license of the fossil fuel industry than decades of technocratic tinkering.

As the Biden administration takes office, it will face a divided, polarized Congress. Victor and Sabel are correct that progress will likely depend on aggressive administrative action and place-based solutions at the state level. But these executive actions—whether tightening fuel efficiency standards, utilizing the EPA to regulate power plant emissions, or using federal procurement to guarantee markets for clean steel and electric vehicles—must be explicitly designed to fracture the political coalition of the fossil fuel industry. By directing federal subsidies and research grants toward clean energy sectors, the government can incubate new, powerful green constituencies that will fight to maintain and expand those regulations over time.

Ultimately, Victor and Sabel are right to abandon the illusion that a single, legally binding global treaty will save us from climate catastrophe. The top-down versus bottom-up dichotomy has indeed paralyzed us for too long. Sector-by-sector problem solving, driven by a thin initial consensus andratcheted up through continuous review, is the only historically proven way to manage complex, technological transitions under deep uncertainty.

But as we embrace experimentalist governance, we must do so with our eyes wide open to the brute realities of power. The fossil fuel economy will not be phased out through polite committee work alone. The successes of the California Air Resources Board or the European Water Framework Directive were achieved because regulators were willing to bring the hammer down on entrenched interests. To fix the climate in the decades ahead, we must pair the agile, iterative structures of experimentalist governance with the raw political and economic power necessary to make the continuation of the fossil fuel era impossible. We have the governance model; now we must forge the penalty defaults that will force the world to use it.
