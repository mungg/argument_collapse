"To save the generative internet from the dual threats of cybercrime and restrictive 'appliance' devices, we must move beyond Zittrain’s voluntary community-based solutions and implement affirmative government regulations that mandate security baselines for intermediaries."

Jonathan Zittrain’s compelling portrait of a generative internet under siege is a vital wake-up call, but it stops frustratingly short of what the emergency demands. He correctly identifies the malignancy—botnets of millions, organized crime treating code as a business, a user base too bewildered to protect itself—and he brilliantly warns that the cure of sterile appliances will be worse than the disease. His proposed remedies, however, lean almost entirely on voluntary community action and technological voluntarism. That approach is noble, but it is the intellectual equivalent of treating a cholera epidemic with responsible hand-washing campaigns while refusing to build a public sewer system. We need an affirmative role for government regulation—not to lock down the internet, but to mandate the baseline of safety that community efforts are structurally incapable of providing.

Zittrain rightly celebrates Wikipedia’s self-governance, but he understates how much of that success hinges on a specific, highly motivated community and a low-stakes environment. No one dies if a Wikipedia article is defaced for an hour. But when a botnet of thirty million machines can launch extortion attacks or shut down emergency services, the harm is existential, and the community of amateur code reviewers cannot possibly respond with the speed and scale required. We do not leave automobile safety to a volunteer “driver’s watch” that shares tips about faulty brakes. We mandate seat belts, airbags, and regular inspections through law. The internet’s status as a critical global infrastructure now justifies a similar regulatory floor.

Start with the Internet Service Providers, whom Zittrain conspicuously lets off the hook with little more than a suggestion that they might “reasonably be asked or required to help.” For years, ISPs have hidden behind the end-to-end principle to wash their hands of the freight of malicious traffic coursing through their pipes. It is time to require them to act. ISPs are in the best position to detect and block traffic patterns characteristic of botnets—sudden spikes in port scanning, encrypted command-and-control channels, anomalous email volumes. A federal mandate that all ISPs serving residential customers deploy basic, transparent network-wide intrusion detection and share threat intelligence with a public clearinghouse would be a far more effective brake on malware than hoping that a few thousand users will download Herdict. This is not deep packet inspection aimed at user behavior; it is the digital equivalent of requiring restaurants to have grease traps. The regulations must be written to forbid any linkage between the security monitoring apparatus and commercial profiling, enforced by independent auditors. The alternative—doing nothing at the network level—endorses a world in which millions of grandmothers’ machines are quietly enslaved by criminals while ISPs shrug and point to the “dumb network” design.

Operating system vendors, too, have been the beneficiaries of a market that has externalized the costs of insecurity. Microsoft’s Windows has been the great generative platform of our time, but its security model was built on an implicit assumption that users would be knowledgeable administrators. That assumption is now laughable. Zittrain’s virtual “Red-Green” PC proposal is a clever hack, but it places the burden of safe configuration once again on the user. Instead, the law should demand that consumer operating systems ship with a secure-by-default posture: a “Green” mode that prohibits any software not digitally signed by a known, accountable party from accessing sensitive areas of the file system or network. This does not kill generativity—power users can toggle to an “advanced” mode, much as a car enthusiast can disable traction control—but it protects the vast majority who should not be expected to make security decisions they do not understand.

Zittrain frets about “chilling effects” of regulation on small, unaccredited developers. But a well-drafted statute would distinguish between the amateur sharing her code on SourceForge and a predatory toolbar that infiltrates Internet Explorer. The law could establish a safe harbor: code made available in source form under an approved open-source license, subject to peer scrutiny, faces no liability for incidental malice unless the author intended harm; closed-source executables distributed through mass-market channels, by contrast, carry strict product liability for any damage they cause. This mirrors the liability regimes that make food and toy manufacturers responsible for their products’ safety without shutting down community bake sales.

None of this is to counsel the heavy-handed, Internet-wrecking legislation that Zittrain fears. Banning “deceptive code” by fiat would indeed be as effective as banning rain. But his leap from the impossibility of criminalizing individual bad actors to a near-total reliance on social norms is a false binary. The most effective regulatory interventions target the intermediaries—the ISPs and platform vendors—that possess the technical capacity to filter out the worst threats at scale. Properly calibrated, such mandates can be transparent, privacy-respecting, and subject to judicial oversight.

We are, as Zittrain notes, facing a “death of a thousand cuts.” Each new botnet, each quarter-million-dollar extortion attack, each person who loses a decade of family photos to ransomware, chips away at the public’s willingness to tolerate the open PC. In that environment, waiting for the slow emergence of a Wikipedia-like security culture is a luxury we cannot afford. Zittrain’s generative ideal is too precious to leave to well-meaning volunteer fire departments alone. It needs a public fire code, enforced by a professional inspector, to keep the whole neighborhood from burning down.

  2. 02Operating system vendors should be required by law to ship consumer devices with a 'secure-by-default' posture that protects non-expert users from unauthorized software execution.
  3. 03The law should impose strict product liability on closed-source commercial software while providing a safe harbor for open-source developers to balance accountability with innovation.
  4. 04Regulatory interventions targeting technical intermediaries are more effective and scalable than relying on the slow emergence of social norms or amateur code review.
  5. 05Establishing a 'public fire code' for the internet through regulation is the only way to prevent the existential harms of cybercrime from driving the public toward locked-down, non-generative appliances.
