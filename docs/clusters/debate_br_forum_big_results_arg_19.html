"While the experimental findings of behavioral economics are rigorous and valuable, they focus on technocratic workarounds that ignore the underlying political failures and state-capacity issues that necessitate such interventions in the first place."

**Missing the Politics**

There is much to admire in Glennerster and Kremer's careful synthesis of a decade's worth of experimental development research. The findings are real, the methodology is rigorous, and the policy implications — particularly on user fees — are humane. And yet, reading the essay, I could not shake the feeling that something enormous was missing from the picture. That something is politics.

Notice what the essay does not contain. It does not mention the state, except as a vague provider of services or remover of fees. It does not discuss why some governments deliver public goods competently and others do not. It does not ask why, after sixty years of independence, parts of sub-Saharan Africa still lack functional primary health systems while others have built them. It does not consider that the chlorine dispenser at the Kenyan spring exists in a country whose chronic underinvestment in rural water infrastructure is itself a political fact, the product of decisions about who counts and who does not. The dispenser is a clever workaround for a political failure, and the authors describe it as if politics had nothing to do with it.

This silence is not accidental. It is a feature of the experimental method. RCTs work best when the intervention is bounded, the implementer is reliable, and the political context can be held constant by design. NGOs make ideal partners precisely because they are flexible, project-shaped, and uninterested in confronting the larger structures around them. The randomista program has, in effect, carved out the depoliticized portion of development and studied it intensively. What it has learned in that bounded space is valuable. But the bounded space is not where the action is.

Consider the conditional cash transfer literature, which the authors rightly celebrate. PROGRESA in Mexico worked. So have its imitators in Brazil, Colombia, and elsewhere. But none of these programs is primarily an "intervention." They are pieces of a broader political project — typically associated with center-left governments responding to mobilized constituencies, building electoral coalitions among the poor, and constructing the administrative capacity to deliver benefits at scale. The cash transfer is the visible tip; the iceberg is a state capable of identifying poor families, transferring money to them reliably, monitoring conditions, and absorbing the political backlash from those who object. When other countries try to copy the cash transfer without the underlying political infrastructure, they often fail. The "treatment effect" is not portable because what is being transferred is not just a policy but a state capacity.

Or consider user fees. The authors present the evidence against fees as a triumph of behavioral economics over a flawed economic model. But the user-fee regime was never the product of an honest economic argument. It was imposed on dozens of African and Latin American countries during the 1980s and 1990s by the World Bank and the IMF as part of structural adjustment, against the wishes of national health ministries and despite warnings from many economists at the time. The reason fees persisted for decades was not that economists believed they worked. It was that they were embedded in a global political structure that constrained what indebted governments could do. Showing in a trial that fees reduce uptake is useful, but it is responding to a political imposition with a technocratic finding. The political imposition is the deeper story.

The same pattern shows up in education. Glennerster and Kremer report that providing information about returns to schooling, or recruiters visiting villages, raises enrollment. Excellent. But why do families underestimate returns in the first place? Often because the labor markets they observe really do have low returns to education — because the formal sector is small, because credentialing is rationed by ethnicity or politics, because the schools available to the rural poor are so bad that the returns to attending them are genuinely low. These are political facts about how labor markets and school systems are organized. An information campaign can shift beliefs, but if it shifts beliefs that were tracking reality, the effect will fade.

I do not mean to suggest that any of this invalidates the experimental findings. It does not. But the framing in which "small changes" produce "big results" can become misleading when readers conclude that the small changes alone are the explanation. The chlorine dispenser produces big results because it is solving a real problem of water contamination — a problem created by the political failure to provide safe piped water. The conditional cash transfer produces big results because it is delivering resources to households that have been politically excluded from the gains of growth. The deworming pill produces big results because the parasites have been allowed to flourish in the absence of adequate public health systems. Each "small change" is, in a sense, an admission of a large failure.

A more candid framing would acknowledge this. The experimental literature has identified a class of cheap, effective workarounds for the failures of weak states and ill-functioning markets. These workarounds are worth pursuing. They save lives. But pursued in isolation, they risk substituting for the harder political project of building states that can provide clean water, decent schools, and functional clinics without requiring an NGO with a randomized trial to figure out the optimal incentive structure.

Albert Hirschman warned long ago about the seduction of small, technical projects in development, and the way they could displace attention from the larger institutional questions. Glennerster and Kremer's program is not guilty of Hirschman's worst fears — the rigor is real and the humility is genuine. But the displacement effect is worth worrying about. We are producing a generation of development economists exquisitely trained to evaluate interventions and barely trained to think about politics, the state, or power. The "small changes" agenda needs to be paired with a serious account of where the big changes come from. Otherwise we will keep learning, with ever greater precision, how to cope with structures we have given up trying to change.

  1. 01The experimental method inherently depoliticizes development by focusing on bounded interventions that can be studied in isolation from the state.
  2. 02Successful programs like conditional cash transfers are not just policy interventions but are products of broader political projects and state administrative capacity.
  3. 03The history of user fees demonstrates that they were a political imposition from global financial institutions rather than a simple mistake in economic modeling.
  4. 04Small-scale interventions like information campaigns often address symptoms of political failure, such as dysfunctional labor markets, rather than the root causes.
  5. 05An over-reliance on the 'small changes' agenda risks displacing the essential, harder work of building functional states and addressing power structures.
