"While the Yasusada hoax effectively exposes the literary establishment's superficial appetite for 'authentic' otherness, it is unlikely to catalyze a genuine engagement with actual Japanese poetry because it has been absorbed into a cycle of literary gossip rather than institutional change."

Marjorie Perloff ends her essay by arguing that Kent Johnson's hoax, whatever its intentions, has performed a valuable service by prompting reconsideration of actual Hiroshima testimony and Japanese poetry. "What we need," she writes, "are not more 'authentic' and 'sensitive' witnesses to what we take to be exotic cultural and ethnic practices, but a willingness, on the part of poet as well as reader, to look searchingly and critically at what is always already there."

This conclusion is sound, but it raises a question the essay does not answer: Why should we believe that Johnson's hoax will have this effect? The history of hoaxes offers little grounds for optimism. McPherson's Ossian was exposed within a few decades, but Scottish poetry did not thereby flourish with renewed energy. The exposure did not lead to serious engagement with actual Gaelic traditions; it led to embarrassment and a search for the next sensation. The hoax became a footnote rather than a catalyst.

The Yasusada affair is already becoming a footnote. Lingua Franca covered it as a story about literary politics. The journals that published the poems have moved on to other matters. The poetry reading circuit continues its usual business. There is no evidence that American editors have suddenly become interested in contemporary Japanese poetry, or that Japanese poets are receiving more attention in American journals. The hoax has been absorbed into the entertainment economy of literary gossip.

This outcome should not surprise us. Perloff's essay argues that the literary establishment's appetite for authentic Otherness was so strong that it swallowed an obvious fabrication. That same appetite is strong enough to absorb the exposure of the fabrication without changing any habits. The editors who praised Yasusada were not converted by its exposure; they were inconvenienced. Their embarrassment will fade, their positions will remain secure, their editorial preferences will continue to favor familiar forms over unfamiliar traditions.

Johnson may have intended his hoax as a contribution to multicultural critique, but intentions do not determine effects. The effect of the hoax, in the current historical moment, is to provide ammunition for those who wish to dismiss multicultural concerns as sentimental or hypocritical. "See how the multiculturalists were taken in by a fake," the argument will go. "Their concern for the Other is so much posturing." This argument is unfair — the hoax exploited universal human desires, not specifically multicultural ones — but it will be made nonetheless.

I do not wish to end on a note of despair. Perloff's essay is a serious contribution to understanding the dynamics that made the hoax possible. If her analysis is widely read and seriously discussed, it might contribute to the kind of change she calls for. But we should be clear about the obstacles. The literary establishment's failures are structural, not simply matters of individual ignorance or bad faith. Changing them will require more than exposure of a hoax; it will require restructuring the institutions and incentives that shape what gets published and read.

Perhaps the best response to the Yasusada affair is not moral outrage but practical engagement. Who are the actual contemporary Japanese poets? What are their concerns? How might American readers access their work? These are questions that the hoax has not prompted anyone to ask. They remain unanswered, and until they are answered, the literary relationship between America and Japan will continue to operate in the register of fantasy and appropriation that Johnson has so effectively, if unintentionally, exposed.
