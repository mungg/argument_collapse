"To be an evangelical today is to stand at a crossroads between a political identity defined by cultural grievance and a religious identity defined by historic theological convictions."

To be an evangelical today is to belong to a movement whose theological meaning is clearer than its public reputation. Historically, the word has not meant “Republican,” “white,” “Southern,” or “angry voter.” It has meant a form of Protestant Christianity marked by conversion, the authority of the Bible, the centrality of Christ’s atoning work on the cross and an obligation to spread the faith. That familiar definition, often associated with historian David Bebbington, still describes millions of believers in black churches, immigrant churches, Pentecostal congregations, Baptist churches, campus ministries and nondenominational megachurches.

But in American public life, “evangelical” has become a political demographic before it is heard as a religious identity. Pollsters ask whether voters are “born again or evangelical,” and the answer is often treated as a proxy for cultural conservatism. That shorthand has some basis in reality: white evangelicals have been a central part of the Republican coalition since the late 1970s, mobilized around abortion, religious liberty, school prayer, sexuality and distrust of secular elites. Yet the shorthand distorts. It flattens theology into partisanship and turns a multiracial, global Christian tradition into a voting bloc.

The Trump campaign has exposed this confusion rather than created it. His appeal to many self-identified evangelicals is not obviously rooted in evangelical doctrine. His language of strength, winning, resentment and national restoration sounds less like the Sermon on the Mount than like a politics of grievance. Many evangelicals who support him may be expressing fear about cultural loss, anger at political leaders, concern about immigration or a desire for protection in a changing country. Those are political instincts, not necessarily evangelical convictions. When church leaders recoil from the label, they are not merely being fastidious about branding. They are asking whether a word tied to “good news” can survive being identified with contempt, cruelty or celebrity.

Still, abandoning the term may concede too much. If “evangelical” is surrendered whenever it is abused, then its meaning will be left entirely to campaign strategists and exit polls. The better response is retrieval and repentance. Retrieval means insisting that evangelical faith is first about Jesus Christ, not access to power. Repentance means admitting that many evangelicals have too often confused moral witness with partisan loyalty, and have sometimes shown more alarm about losing influence than about poverty, racism, addiction, refugees or the character of their own leaders.

There is also no single evangelical future. Younger evangelicals often remain theologically orthodox while sounding less automatic in their political loyalties. Latino, Asian-American and African-American evangelicals complicate the idea that the movement belongs to white conservatives. Globally, evangelical Christianity is growing in Africa, Latin America and Asia, where American culture-war categories do not map neatly onto church life.

So to be evangelical today is to stand at a crossroads. It can mean a voting identity shaped by fear and nostalgia. Or it can mean what the word originally promised: a people formed by Scripture, conversion, the cross and mission, whose public life bears witness not only to what they oppose, but to the humility, mercy and truth they claim to preach.

To be evangelical, in the public mind, increasingly meant less about one’s prayer life and more about opposing abortion, defending religious liberty against a secularizing state, and voting for candidates who promised to fight those battles. Church attendance and doctrinal fidelity grew less predictive of the label than partisan allegiance.

There are those, like Moore, trying to reclaim the term for a gospel-centered, multiethnic, countercultural Christianity. There are political operatives who deploy the bloc for policy victories. And there is a vast, mostly white, middle-class constituency for whom being evangelical means defending a traditional way of life—family, flag, and free market—with Jesus as a supporting chaplain rather than a commanding Lord. The Trump phenomenon hasn’t created this crisis; it has simply illuminated it, forcing a long-overdue question: can a word that now denotes a voting pattern more than a creed still shepherd souls, or has the salt truly lost its savor?
