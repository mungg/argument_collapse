"A truly just migration agenda must address the systemic inequalities at both ends of the journey, moving beyond the Western-centric debate to protect both the right to move and the right to stay."

Lea Ypi asks readers in wealthy democracies to see migration as a symptom of a broken global order rather than as a civilizational threat. From the vantage point of the countries people leave, this is both welcome and incomplete. Welcome, because the dominant debate in Europe and North America still begins at the wrong end of the journey: the boat, the desert crossing, the airport interview, the asylum hotel, the border fence. Incomplete, because even a sympathetic account can turn migrants into evidence for arguments about the moral condition of receiving states. The drama remains centered on the West: its hypocrisy, its Enlightenment inheritance, its welfare state, its far right, its guilty conscience.

But the right to move must be held together with another right Ypi names near the end: the right not to be forced to move. For many sending societies, this second right is the more urgent one. Migration is not only an escape from poverty or violence; it can also be a form of extraction. The global economy does not merely receive people displaced by crisis. It often recruits away the very capacities poorer societies need in order to survive crisis.

Consider health care. Rich countries with aging populations and understaffed hospitals increasingly rely on nurses, doctors, care workers, and technicians trained elsewhere. For the individual worker, migration may be transformative. Remittances support families, educate siblings, build homes, and create savings no local wage could provide. But for the sending country, the loss can be severe. A public education system subsidizes training; a richer country harvests the skill. The migrant is praised as “essential” abroad while the hospital at home remains understaffed. This is not simply freedom of movement. It is a global labor chain structured by inequality.

The same dynamic appears in education, engineering, construction, agriculture, and domestic work. Poorer countries export unemployment, but they also export care. Grandmothers raise children whose mothers care for elderly people in Italy, Britain, Canada, or the Gulf. Teachers leave classrooms to become nannies. Fathers build cities they cannot afford to live in while their own villages are emptied of working-age adults. Remittances arrive, but so do absences. Money crosses borders more easily than childhoods can be repaired.

This is why romantic accounts of remittances should be treated carefully. Ypi notes, correctly, that remittances exceed foreign aid. But remittances are not a development strategy in any democratic sense. They are private transfers extracted from sacrifice. They can reduce poverty and stabilize households, but they can also inflate land prices, deepen dependency, encourage states to neglect job creation, and shift the burden of social policy onto migrants’ bodies. A government that survives on remittances may become less accountable for building an economy in which citizens can remain.

Nor should we assume that “development” will quickly reduce migration. In the short and medium term, rising incomes can increase mobility by giving people the resources to leave. Education expands aspirations faster than local labor markets absorb them. Climate adaptation may require seasonal or circular movement. The relationship between development and migration is not a simple switch: make countries richer and migration stops. The deeper goal is not immobility. It is genuine choice.

Ypi’s class analysis helps here. The poor do not enjoy the same mobility as the rich. But in many sending societies class is intertwined with region, ethnicity, caste, religion, gender, and household position in ways that cannot be reduced to worker versus capitalist. Who leaves? Who stays? Who pays the recruiter? Who bears the debt? Who is expected to remit? Who is shamed for failing abroad? Who cares for the children and elderly left behind? These are class questions, but also gendered and communal ones.

Recruitment debt is especially important. Many migrants do not simply move; they are financially bound before they depart. Fees to brokers, travel costs, document charges, bribes, training programs, and loans can turn mobility into indenture. A worker arriving in a rich country may owe thousands of dollars at high interest. Deportation would not merely end a job; it would ruin a family. Employers know this. So do recruiters. Border vulnerability begins long before the border.

If liberal democracies are serious about justice, they should regulate recruitment chains with the same energy they devote to border enforcement. No worker should pay illegal recruitment fees. Employers who benefit from debt-bonded labor abroad should face liability at home. Bilateral labor agreements should include enforceable wage standards, portability of visas, housing rules, complaint mechanisms, and the right to organize. Consulates should not function merely as document offices; they should be labor rights institutions. Sending states, too, must stop treating migrants as export commodities and start protecting them as citizens.

Ypi is right to attack golden visas and the commodification of citizenship. But there is a parallel commodification on the poor side of the ledger: the commodification of laboring bodies through temporary migration schemes. The rich buy membership; the poor rent themselves under constraint. In both cases, citizenship is hollowed out. One person gets rights without social obligation. Another provides labor without political standing.

The Gulf states show the extreme version of this model, though it exists in milder forms elsewhere. Temporary workers build, clean, drive, cook, and care while remaining outside the political community, often under sponsorship systems that give employers enormous power. Western democracies criticize such systems while adopting their own versions: seasonal agricultural visas, tied care-worker permits, temporary foreign worker programs, offshore processing, and deportability as labor discipline. The difference is often degree, not kind.

Yet sending states are not innocent. Many governments have institutionalized emigration as policy. They celebrate the diaspora, court remittances, create ministries for overseas workers, and praise migrants as national heroes while failing to create decent work at home. Some use migration as a safety valve for unemployment and dissent. Others neglect citizens abroad until scandal erupts. Corruption in passport offices, licensing agencies, and recruitment systems is part of the migration infrastructure. A just analysis must criticize both global hierarchy and domestic elites.

Climate change sharpens all of this. For island states, drought-prone regions, river deltas, and agricultural zones exposed to heat and flood, mobility will become part of adaptation. But climate migration must not become another excuse for rich countries to select the useful and abandon the rest. If emissions have been unequal, responsibility must be unequal too. Climate finance should be grants rather than debt. Adaptation should support people who want to stay. Planned relocation, where necessary, should preserve communities rather than scatter them as disposable labor. Legal pathways should be designed before disaster, not improvised afterward.

The phrase “nobody is forced to leave their home” is powerful, but it should not be interpreted as a world without migration. For many communities, mobility is historical, seasonal, familial, and creative. People have always moved for marriage, trade, study, pilgrimage, work, and curiosity. The injustice is not movement. The injustice is when staying becomes impossible and moving becomes humiliating.

What would a migrant justice agenda look like if designed from both ends of the route?

First, it would protect the right to stay: peacebuilding, climate adaptation, debt relief, tax justice, food security, public health, and industrial policies that create decent work. This requires changing global rules that allow capital flight, abusive debt, land grabs, and environmental destruction. It also requires accountable governments in sending countries.

Second, it would protect the right to move: affordable visas, regional mobility, recognition of qualifications, safe travel, family unity, and asylum for those in danger. The current system often makes movement most dangerous for those with the strongest need.

Third, it would protect rights while away: equal labor standards, union rights, access to courts, consular protection, freedom from employer-tied status, and paths to permanence for those who settle.

Fourth, it would protect transnational families: portable benefits, affordable remittance systems, child support across borders, pension portability, and social services that recognize the costs of separation.

Fifth, it would democratize diaspora politics. Migrants should not be treated only as sources of money or patriotic nostalgia. They should have voice in both home and host societies, especially when their remittances sustain public life.

Ypi’s essay is a necessary rebuke to wealthy democracies that blame migrants for crises created by austerity, war, inequality, and capital. But the next step is to refuse a debate in which migrants appear only when they knock on Europe’s or America’s door. By then, much has already happened: a farm failed, a loan was taken, a recruiter was paid, a child was left with relatives, a credential was discounted, a risk was accepted, a border became a business.

The real border crisis is not only at the border. It is in the village where departure becomes the only plan, in the hospital that loses its nurses, in the family that survives on remittances but not presence, in the recruitment office that sells hope on credit, in the ministry that praises emigrants while abandoning citizens, and in the rich country that calls a worker essential but not equal.

Nathan the Wise asks us to look to the future rather than worship inherited identities. A just future must be one in which movement is neither a privilege of wealth nor a sentence of poverty. It must be one in which people can leave without being exiled, stay without being trapped, and return without having failed. That is a larger demand than open borders or closed borders. It is the demand that mobility itself be democratized.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01International migration should ideally be viewed as a beneficial activity similar to interstate travel within the U.S., rather than as an inherent problem.
  2. 02Not all migration is a symptom of desperation; much of it is driven by the same positive desires for education, family unification, and better opportunities that motivate domestic movement.
  3. 03The human preference to remain in one's place of habitual residence suggests the existence of a 'right to remain' that could be used to legally challenge the global systems causing forced displacement.
  4. 04Addressing the root causes of involuntary migration requires strategies beyond class solidarity, such as seeking reparations from states and corporations responsible for climate-driven displacement.
  5. 05Remedying global injustices is the only way to ensure that the movement of people across borders becomes a matter of human agency rather than a forced response to unlivable conditions.
