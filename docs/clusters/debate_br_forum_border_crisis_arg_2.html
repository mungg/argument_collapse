**In Defense of the Bounded Demos**

Lea Ypi has written a brilliant and frustrating essay. Brilliant because her diagnosis of how citizenship has been commodified at the top and weaponized at the bottom is unanswerable: the Trump Platinum Card and the chained deportation flights are indeed two faces of the same political economy, and any honest politics must reckon with that. Frustrating because she uses this real and outrageous asymmetry as a wedge to dislodge something quite different — the idea that democratic political community has, or could have, legitimate boundaries at all. The first argument is correct. The second does not follow, and adopting it would, I think, undermine the very emancipatory tradition Ypi claims to be defending.

Let me start where Ypi is strongest. The contrast between the investor visa and the deportation flight is not incidental. It tells us that the contemporary border is not really a wall around a community; it is a sorting mechanism for capital. The same state that cannot find a lawful pathway for a Salvadoran agricultural worker who has been picking American tomatoes for twelve years can find a very quick pathway for a Russian fertilizer magnate willing to write a check. Ypi is right that this is corrupting, that it exposes the "rule of law" rhetoric as ideology, and that no progressive politics can accept it. If her essay were simply a sustained polemic against the sale of citizenship and the criminalization of poverty at the border, I would sign it without revision.

But Ypi wants more than this. She wants to argue that the very distinction between members and non-members of a polity is "parasitic" on capital's prior interests — that to speak of the obligations a state owes its own citizens before those it owes outsiders is already to have surrendered to the class enemy. Here she goes too far, and her argument depends on an idealization of the alternative — transnational, class-based solidarity — that is at least as utopian as the liberal nationalism she critiques.

The bounded democratic community has a real and defensible point. It is the only political form we have ever found that can do three things at once: hold rulers accountable to the ruled, redistribute resources from the better-off to the worse-off, and sustain those redistributions across time through cycles of reciprocity that voters can verify. None of these functions operates well — perhaps none operates at all — once the demos becomes coextensive with humanity. Accountability requires identifiable rulers and identifiable ruled. Redistribution requires a fiscal community whose members trust that their contributions will be matched by others'. Reciprocity requires a horizon of expectation that today's contributor will be tomorrow's beneficiary, and vice versa. The historical record is fairly clear here: every robust welfare state on earth was built within the boundaries of a nation, and most were built on quite thick assumptions about shared fate.

Ypi knows this. Her response is to characterize such arguments as "an idealized account of the liberal democratic state." She points out, correctly, that real existing states have entrenched property hierarchies, excluded racial minorities, and recognized rights only formally. But the same could be said — and Ypi has elsewhere said it powerfully, in her writing on Albania — about real existing socialism, real existing internationalism, real existing anti-imperialism. The fact that an ideal has been imperfectly realized is not an argument against the ideal; it is, if anything, an argument for working out the conditions under which it might be better realized. And those conditions, historically, have involved binding particular peoples to particular institutions through which they could exercise collective authorship of their conditions of life.

Consider Ypi's own example: the great expansions of the franchise. Suffragists, trade unionists, civil-rights campaigners, anticolonial nationalists — she lists them as the heroes of an expansive citizenship. But the striking thing about all four movements is that they sought *inclusion into* a bounded political community, not its dissolution. The Indian National Congress wanted Indian self-government, not the abolition of the political. The British suffragettes wanted votes in British elections. The American civil-rights movement wanted full membership in the American republic. None of them imagined that the answer to their exclusion was to deny that bounded membership had any moral weight. They imagined that membership had to be made real — and they were, of course, right.

Ypi will reply that I am missing the asymmetry: yes, citizenship has been the prize of struggle, but the struggle has always been from below by the excluded, and treating today's excluded migrants as the equivalent of yesterday's disenfranchised workers requires us to dismantle, not defend, the citizen/non-citizen line. This is a serious argument and I want to take it seriously. But I think it equivocates between two very different categories of "excluded": those who live and work permanently inside a polity but are denied its rights (the appropriate analogue to the disenfranchised worker), and those who happen to live elsewhere and would like to come (a different category altogether). The first group has an overwhelmingly strong claim — they are subject to the state's coercion, they are part of its productive life, they should be inside the demos. The second group's claim is real but different: it concerns the global distribution of opportunity, which is a question about international justice, development, and the structure of world institutions, not about whether national democracies should exist.

Run them together — as Ypi tends to — and you produce a politics that cannot speak to either constituency well. To the long-resident undocumented worker, you say: your problem is the existence of borders, not your particular exclusion from rights inside one. To the would-be migrant in Lagos or Tegucigalpa, you say: the answer to global inequality is that you should be able to move to Europe or the United States, rather than that your country should be able to develop on terms that do not require you to leave. The first answer is too radical for the problem; the second is too modest. And politically, neither answer can sustain the cross-class coalitions on which any actually-existing left depends.

There is also a question Ypi underweights: what democracy requires from those who participate in it. Ypi treats integration tests, language requirements, and civic education with a fairly broad brush as obstacles imposed on the poor. Some of them are. But the underlying principle — that membership in a self-governing community involves some shared linguistic and civic infrastructure — is not a Trumpian invention. It is closer to a precondition for being able to argue at all. A society in which no common language can be assumed, in which civic traditions are not transmitted, in which the demos is in continual demographic flux that outpaces institutional adaptation, is not a society more conducive to working-class power. It is a society more conducive to the very oligarchy Ypi rightly fears, because oligarchy thrives wherever the demos cannot constitute itself as a collective interlocutor.

What Ypi's essay actually demonstrates, against its own framing, is that we need *more* democratic nationalism, not less — but a democratic nationalism cleansed of its ethnocultural pathologies. Pay full citizens fully; integrate long-resident migrants quickly and unconditionally; make asylum work; ban the sale of passports; tax capital where it accumulates; redistribute aggressively; cooperate internationally on the global structures that generate displacement. This is a recognizably social-democratic program. It is not the cosmopolitan deconstruction of citizenship. And it is, I would suggest, the only program that has ever delivered any portion of the freedom, equality, and solidarity Ypi correctly identifies as the unfinished business of the Enlightenment.

  3. 03Conflating the rights of long-term undocumented residents with the claims of potential migrants elsewhere confuses domestic political inclusion with broader issues of international justice.

**Solidarity Is Thicker Than Ypi Thinks**

Lea Ypi's essay is animated by a generous moral vision and a sharp class analysis, but it depends on a sociological premise that I think is mistaken. The premise is that the obstacles to migrant inclusion in rich democracies are essentially ideological — products of "narrative," "hegemony," and a "lack of courage to think critically." Strip away the bad ideas, in this telling, and the conflicts between working-class natives and working-class migrants would be exposed as a confidence trick perpetrated by capital. I want to argue that this gets the causality wrong, and that the social-democratic concerns Ypi dismisses — the welfare-state-and-solidarity argument associated with figures like Wolfgang Streeck, David Goodhart, and yes, Sahra Wagenknecht — deserve a more serious hearing than she gives them.

Start with what is actually known about how welfare states function. A welfare state is not merely a redistributive mechanism; it is a long-running cooperative scheme in which citizens make sacrifices today (taxes, deferred consumption, compliance with regulations) in expectation of receiving benefits over the life cycle and across generations. Such schemes depend on what political scientists awkwardly call "fellow-feeling" or "diffuse reciprocity": the willingness of net contributors to keep contributing when they cannot directly observe whether the net beneficiaries are using the resources well, and the willingness of beneficiaries to acknowledge contributors' efforts. Decades of research — including by scholars sympathetic to immigration like Alberto Alesina, and by left-wing political economists like Wolfgang Streeck — suggest that the heterogeneity of the demos matters here. Not because there is anything wrong with diverse societies in principle, but because trust in shared institutions is built slowly and can be eroded quickly when people cease to recognize one another as participants in a common project.

This is not a "cultural" argument in the racist sense Ypi rightly excoriates. It is a structural argument about what makes generous welfare states politically sustainable. The Nordic states — the high-water mark of social-democratic achievement — were built on extraordinarily dense civic infrastructure, near-universal labor-market participation, strong unions, shared linguistic and educational backgrounds, and the resulting willingness of high earners to pay marginal tax rates that would be politically unthinkable in more heterogeneous polities. The point is not that diversity is bad; it is that the *speed* and *scale* of demographic change in many European societies in the 2010s and 2020s has run ahead of the institutional capacity to integrate new arrivals into the cooperative scheme — and that this lag has material consequences for the legitimacy of redistributive politics.

Ypi will say: but the right exploits these anxieties dishonestly, and the empirical record on whether migrants actually burden welfare states is mixed at best. Both true. But the question is not only what migrants empirically cost; it is whether voters perceive the system as fair. And fairness perceptions are sensitive to visible asymmetries — for example, between someone who has paid into a system for thirty years and someone who arrived last year, regardless of either party's underlying merit. Mocking these perceptions as false consciousness has been the social-democratic strategy for two decades. It has not worked. The Labour Party under Starmer, which Ypi correctly notes has adopted a depressing anti-migration register, did so precisely because its predecessors lost five elections in a row trying to make the cosmopolitan case Ypi prefers.

Here is where I think Ypi's analogy to past struggles for inclusion breaks down. The suffragettes, the labor movements, the civil-rights campaigners were demanding inclusion in a polity to which they already belonged in every meaningful sense: they were born there, raised there, worked there, paid taxes there, fought in its wars, were subject to its laws. The injustice of their exclusion was that the formal demos denied what the substantive demos already was. The case for migrant inclusion of long-resident workers — undocumented people who have lived in the U.S. or U.K. for decades, raised children, paid into the system — is exactly analogous, and progressives should fight for it tooth and nail. But the case for treating *prospective* migrants as if they were already members of the demos is a categorically different argument, and conflating the two is a rhetorical move, not a logical one.

What Ypi calls "the reduction of democracy to membership" is in fact one of democracy's foundational features. A demos that does not constitute itself — that does not say "we, these particular people, are going to govern ourselves together" — cannot democratically deliberate at all, because it has no stable referent for the "we" that does the deliberating. Migration policy, far from being a question on which the demos has no legitimate authority, is one of the questions on which a self-governing community most clearly does have authority, because it concerns the very composition of the community over time. To say that the demos cannot have meaningful views about who joins it is to say the demos is not really a self-governing community.

This is not a counsel for closed borders. There are many strong reasons — moral, economic, demographic — for relatively open immigration policies. But these reasons must be made within the framework of democratic self-government, not against it. A defensible center-left position would say: yes to large-scale legal migration, properly resourced; yes to a fast and fair asylum system; yes to a clear pathway to citizenship for long-resident undocumented people; yes to aggressive labor enforcement against employers who exploit migrant workers to undercut wages; no to the criminalization of poverty at the border; no to the commodification of citizenship at the top. But also: yes to integration policies that take language, civics, and shared institutions seriously, because these are the substrate of any solidaristic politics, and the demos has a legitimate interest in maintaining them.

The strongest section of Ypi's essay is her insistence that the rich and the poor face radically different border regimes. She is right that golden visas are a scandal and that they expose the hypocrisy of "rule of law" rhetoric. But the appropriate inference is not that all borders are illegitimate; it is that borders should not be a market. Citizenship should not be for sale, full stop. And the reason it should not be for sale is precisely because it is supposed to be a relationship of mutual obligation among co-citizens, not a commodity transaction. The argument *against* selling citizenship is parasitic on the very thick conception of bounded political community that Ypi elsewhere wants to dissolve.

Finally, a word about Lessing. Nathan the Wise is a beautiful text, but it is also, in its own way, an idealization. Nathan is a figure who can be cosmopolitan because he stands above the actual conflicts of medieval Jerusalem — wealthy, learned, indifferent to revenge. Most actual humans, including most actual migrants and most actual citizens, are not Nathans. They are embedded in particular communities, attached to particular places, dependent on particular institutions. A politics that treats this embeddedness as a problem to be overcome by sufficient critical reflection has misunderstood what politics is for. The Enlightenment Ypi rightly cherishes is not the dissolution of the particular into the universal; it is the patient, fallible construction of universalisms *through* particular institutions. Citizenship in a bounded, self-governing community is not the enemy of that project. It is, on the available evidence, its indispensable vehicle.
