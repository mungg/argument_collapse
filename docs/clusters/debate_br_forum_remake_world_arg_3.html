"While Walter Johnson’s racial capitalism framework provides a vital historical and structural analysis for the Movement for Black Lives, it must be bridged with the practical realities of political power-building and the immediate material needs of marginalized communities."

Theory from the Movement Floor

I write as someone who has spent most of my working life in organizing rather than in scholarship, and I want to offer a response to Walter Johnson's essay from that vantage. Johnson is a scholar I respect, and the framework he develops — racial capitalism rooted in the history of slavery, oriented toward reparative justice — resonates strongly with how movement people I know talk about what we are trying to do. But there are also things the essay gestures at without quite capturing, and I want to put them on the table.

The first thing to say is that the essay matters because it takes seriously what the Movement for Black Lives platform actually argues for. The platform, released in August 2016, was an enormous collective achievement — months of work by dozens of organizations to articulate a vision that goes well beyond the immediate police-violence concerns that brought the movement to public attention. The reparations plank is the most ambitious part of the platform, and it has been the most controversial. Liberal commentators have treated it as a maximalist demand that distracts from achievable reforms. Scholars in the Beckert/Baptist tradition have engaged with the history but rarely with the political demand. Johnson's essay is one of the few academic interventions that takes the demand seriously as a serious demand, and that engagement matters.

What movement people I know are trying to do, in our better moments, is hold together three things at once: an analysis that names the deep structural causes of the harm we are responding to, a politics that can build the power needed to address those causes, and a practice that responds to the immediate suffering of our communities while we build that power. Johnson's essay is strongest on the first of these, and it is the second and third that I want to talk about.

On the politics of power-building: Johnson's framework, as articulated, places the standpoint of justice in the Global South and the "dark and dispossessed." This is true to the historical reality his essay describes. But it raises a question that organizers face every day: what is the relationship between the standpoint of justice and the building of political majorities? In a country where black Americans are roughly 13 percent of the population, the political math of racial-justice organizing requires alliances. The question is what kind of alliances, on what terms, with whom.

Johnson's framework tilts in a direction that some movement people I work with find generative and others find restricting. The generative reading is that it provides a clear analytical basis for treating racial justice as central rather than peripheral to the broader work of building a more just society — that it resists the chronic temptation of multiracial movements to relegate racial issues to the margins in pursuit of "broader" coalitions that turn out to be predominantly white. The restricting reading is that it can be used to justify forms of movement-building that treat potential allies (Latino communities, Asian-American communities, white working-class communities, working-class people generally) as either subordinate constituencies or as not really part of the project at all.

The most successful movement work I have seen does not resolve this tension cleanly. It holds it. It centers the experience and analysis of black communities in a way that takes the racial-capitalism framework seriously, while also building real alliances across racial lines on the basis of overlapping material interests. The Fight for $15 has been a useful example — a campaign that has black workers at its core, that takes seriously the racial dimension of low-wage work, but that builds across racial lines because the material interest in higher wages is widely shared. The Movement for Black Lives platform itself is more open to coalitional politics than the racial-capitalism framework, narrowly construed, would suggest — it explicitly engages with environmental justice, immigrant rights, queer and trans liberation, and other movements that are not principally about racial justice but that are bound up with it.

On the practice that responds to immediate suffering: Johnson's framework points toward long-term structural transformation, and that is appropriate to the moral analysis he is offering. But movement work cannot wait for structural transformation. We have to feed people now, defend people from deportation now, get people out of cages now, respond to police violence now. The question is whether the framework Johnson articulates is compatible with this immediate work, or whether it implicitly demands a kind of revolutionary patience that defers immediate response in favor of long-term analysis.

I think the framework is compatible — Johnson's own writing on Ferguson and similar moments suggests he sees the immediate and the structural as connected — but the essay does not develop this connection. What movement people I know need from scholars is not just analysis of the deep structures but help thinking about how immediate work can be informed by and contribute to the longer-term project. The bail funds, the mutual-aid networks, the rapid-response teams, the participatory-defense projects — these are the daily work, and they are also the building blocks of something larger. Connecting the daily work to the structural analysis is intellectual labor that the academy could be doing more of.

There is a related question about the role of the academy in movement work. Johnson cites a number of scholars whose work is central to the racial-capitalism tradition — Gilmore, Kelley, Lipsitz, Hartman, others. Many of these scholars are also movement-engaged in significant ways, and their scholarship is shaped by movement work even as it shapes it. This is good. What is harder, and what I think the essay does not fully address, is the gap between the scholarly conversation and the conversation in organizing spaces. The vocabulary of the academic conversation — "racial capitalism," "the dark proletariat," "natal alienation," "the carceral state" — has migrated into organizing spaces, sometimes productively and sometimes in ways that produce more confusion than clarity. Building real connection between scholarly and movement work requires more than the migration of vocabulary; it requires sustained relationships, mutual accountability, and a willingness on the scholarly side to take direction from movements rather than only providing analysis to them.

A final point. Johnson invokes, in his closing paragraph, Du Bois's argument about moments propitious for change — moments when the world might be remade in a better image. The political moment we are entering does not look propitious in any obvious sense. The new administration is hostile to virtually everything the racial-capitalism analysis would prescribe. Federal institutions that could be levers for racial-justice work are being staffed by people committed to the opposite. The political coalitions that delivered marginal but real gains under the previous administration are demoralized and disorganized.

But Du Bois's point about propitious moments was not just about how to recognize them; it was about the cost of failing to act when they arrive. The next four years will be a defensive period for movement work in important ways. The work of preserving what can be preserved, of protecting people who are immediately threatened, of building infrastructure that can survive the period — this is the work that the moment demands. But it is also possible to do more than defense in a defensive period. The conditions under which a future propitious moment will arrive are being shaped now, and the analysis and organizing we do now will determine whether we are ready when that moment comes.

Johnson's essay contributes to that readiness. It gives us a framework that goes beyond the inherited liberal vocabularies, that takes seriously the history of the harm we are responding to, and that orients us toward redress that is commensurate with that harm. What movement people I know need to do with the framework is what we always do with intellectual resources — take them, test them against our work, adapt them as needed, and put them in service of the projects we are building. The essay is useful to that work. We should say so, even as we keep our own counsel about how to use it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The racial capitalism framework creates a tension between centering the Black experience and the practical necessity of building broad, multiracial political majorities.
  2. 02Scholarly analysis of deep structures must be better integrated with the 'daily work' of movements, such as mutual aid and rapid response, to address immediate suffering during the long-term struggle for transformation.
  3. 03Effective collaboration requires more than the migration of academic vocabulary into organizing spaces; it requires sustained relationships and mutual accountability between scholars and activists.
  4. 04In hostile political climates, the framework serves as a tool for readiness, ensuring that defensive organizing today builds the necessary infrastructure for future moments of radical change.

The Reparations Question: A Friendly Skeptic Responds

Walter Johnson's essay is, among other things, an argument for reparations — though it does not put the argument in those words, and it does not engage in detail with how a reparations program might be designed or implemented. He cites the Movement for Black Lives platform, whose call for reparations is unambiguous, and he frames his "six principal virtues" of a justice rooted in racial capitalism in ways that lead unmistakably in a reparative direction. I want to engage Johnson as a friendly skeptic on the reparations question — friendly because I think the moral case for reparations is essentially correct, skeptic because I worry that the political case has not been adequately made, and that Johnson's framework, while powerful as historical analysis, generates fewer answers than it appears to about the form reparations should take.

Let me begin by stipulating what I take Johnson's historical analysis to establish. The wealth of the United States, and of the broader North Atlantic world, was built in significant part on the enslaved labor of Africans and their descendants and on the dispossessed land of Indigenous peoples. The continuing disparities in wealth, health, education, incarceration, and life expectancy between white Americans and African Americans are not accidents but the predictable consequences of centuries of accumulated extraction and exclusion. The case for some form of compensatory redress is, on these premises, very strong. I am not interested in litigating the moral case against people like David Horowitz, whose objections seem to me to dissolve on contact with the historical record. The moral case is, in my view, settled.

What remains contested are questions of design and implementation, and it is here that I want to press Johnson. Reparations programs face a series of practical and political challenges that have to be confronted on their own terms.

First, who pays? Johnson's framework points toward "the dark and dispossessed" as the standpoint of justice and toward something like the metropolitan beneficiaries as the bearers of the obligation. But the actual distribution of contemporary wealth in the United States and globally is more complex than this binary suggests. The white working class that Johnson, following Du Bois, treats as having received "wages of whiteness" is also, by other measures, increasingly economically immiserated. The current political moment, with its rising white nationalism, is being read by many as a response to that immiseration. A reparations program funded by a broad tax base will fall on people who are themselves struggling, and the political result is predictable. A reparations program funded narrowly by the very wealthy faces a different problem: the very wealthy are politically powerful, the financial system protects them from taxation, and even small steps in this direction (the Obama estate tax adjustments) have proven politically costly.

Second, who receives? The category of those owed reparations is harder to define than it might first appear. Are reparations owed to all African Americans, including recent immigrants from Africa or the Caribbean whose ancestors were not enslaved in the United States? Are they owed only to descendants of those enslaved in the United States, and how is such descent to be established when the historical record was so deliberately incomplete? Are they owed to Indigenous peoples on a separate track, or together with African Americans, and how are the different forms of redress (land back, monetary compensation, structural reform) to be weighted? Johnson's framework gestures at the unity of these claims but does not work out their differences.

Third, what form? Reparations can take many forms — direct payments to individuals, payments to communities, structural investments in housing, education, and health, the cancellation of debt, the return of land, or some combination. Each form has different distributive consequences, different political implications, and different vulnerabilities. Direct payments have the virtue of treating the recipients as autonomous moral agents but face the criticism that they may be quickly absorbed back into structures of disadvantage. Structural investments may produce more durable change but raise questions about who controls the funds and how priorities are set. Land returns to Indigenous communities raise sovereignty questions that direct payment programs do not. Johnson's framework is suggestive but does not resolve these design questions.

Fourth, how much? The numbers in serious reparations proposals range widely — from the relatively modest sums implied by truth-and-reconciliation models to the figures generated by full accounting of unpaid labor over centuries (William Darity's work has been important here). The choice of figure has enormous practical consequences. A small program can be implemented within existing fiscal frameworks but may be inadequate to the harm. A large program is potentially transformative but raises questions about economic stability, inflation, and political backlash that have to be faced.

Fifth, and most importantly, what is the political path? Reparations have been on the table as a serious proposal in U.S. political discourse since at least the 1969 Black Manifesto. They have not advanced significantly in fifty years, and the recent revival of the discussion (Ta-Nehisi Coates's 2014 essay was a milestone) has not so far translated into legislative momentum. H.R. 40, the bill to study reparations introduced by John Conyers every Congress since 1989, has yet to pass. The political moment, with a new administration that ran on a platform explicitly antagonistic to the racial-justice frame, looks worse rather than better for the project.

I raise these questions not to dismiss reparations but to suggest that the gap between Johnson's historical and theoretical analysis and the political work of building a reparations coalition is considerable. The analysis tells us why reparations are morally owed. It does not tell us how to build the political force capable of delivering them.

There is an alternative framework worth considering, and I want to lay it out not as a substitute for reparations but as a possible complement. The framework is what we might call universal social democracy with reparative weighting. Rather than building a politics around a category of recipients defined by descent, we might build a politics around universal entitlements — to health care, housing, education, employment, a basic income — that are sized and structured in ways that close the gaps Johnson rightly identifies. A robust public-housing program that builds where the need is greatest will disproportionately benefit black and Indigenous communities. A jobs guarantee will disproportionately benefit communities most affected by deindustrialization and incarceration. A wealth tax of sufficient scale will disproportionately fall on those whose wealth derives most directly from the histories of extraction Johnson describes.

The advantages of this framework are political. It builds a broader coalition by attaching the redress of historical injury to programs that also benefit the white working class, whose interests are objectively more aligned with anti-racist politics than with white nationalism. It avoids the legal and definitional challenges that haunt reparations design. It can be implemented incrementally without waiting for a transformative political moment. And it operates within institutional channels (Congress, federal agencies, state governments) that exist and have track records.

The disadvantages are moral. Universal programs do not name the specific historical wrong they are responding to. They do not provide the symbolic vindication that explicit reparations offer. They allow beneficiaries who are not victims of the historical wrong to participate equally in the redress, which some will find offensive. And they may be insufficient: the wealth gap between white and black Americans is large enough that universal programs may not close it.

I am genuinely uncertain about how to weigh these considerations, and I suspect the most defensible position is some combination — a universal social-democratic program plus a targeted reparations program, with the targeting designed to address the residual gap that universalism does not close. But this combination requires a politics that can hold both elements together, and I do not yet see what that politics looks like.

Johnson's essay does not engage with these questions, and I do not fault him for that — they are not the questions his essay is asking. But I think the essay would be more useful if it were more candid about what its framework does and does not deliver. The framework establishes that reparations are owed. It does not, by itself, build the political coalition capable of paying them. The work of building that coalition is, in my view, the central political task for those who agree with Johnson's analysis. And that work will require engagement with constituencies — including the white working class — that the racial capitalism framework, in some of its formulations, tends to treat as obstacles rather than as potential allies.

The propitious moment Du Bois invoked is not, I suspect, our moment. The political winds are running the other way. The task for those committed to Johnson's analysis is to prepare the ground for a moment that may come — to build the institutions, the analyses, and the coalitions that can move when an opening appears. This is patient work, and it is harder than the writing of essays. But it is the work the moment requires.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02A reparations program funded by a broad tax base risks a severe political backlash from an increasingly immiserated white working class.
  3. 03The historical record is often too incomplete to clearly define eligible recipients or distinguish between the varying claims of different marginalized groups.
  4. 04Universal social democracy with reparative weighting may be a more politically viable complement to reparations because it builds a broader coalition by addressing shared economic needs.
  5. 05The current political climate is antagonistic to explicit racial-justice frames, necessitating patient institutional work rather than immediate legislative expectations.
  6. 06To be successful, the movement must engage the white working class as potential allies rather than treating them solely as obstacles within the racial capitalism framework.
