The Trees and the Forest: On Capitalism's Scale

Walter Johnson's essay performs a series of intellectual gestures that deserve to be examined separately, because they do not all travel together as easily as he suggests. He links a critique of the language of "dehumanization" to a critique of the liberal subject of human rights, links that critique to an embrace of Cedric Robinson's racial capitalism, and links that embrace to a program for reparative justice rooted in the history of slavery. Each step is bracing. But I want to dwell on the middle step — the claim that the older debate over whether slavery was "capitalist" should be settled, definitively and analytically, in the affirmative — because I think Johnson is more right than his critics in James Oakes's camp, and yet less rigorous than the moment demands.

The case for what is now being called the New History of Capitalism is strongest when stated at the level Johnson states it: as a historiographic refusal to pretend that Manchester happened without Mississippi. The empirical evidence Sven Beckert assembled in *Empire of Cotton* and Ed Baptist developed in *The Half Has Never Been Told* makes the older view — in which slavery is a precapitalist holdover destined to be swept away by free wage labor — look less like a defensible interpretation than a moral preference dressed up as periodization. Johnson's own *River of Dark Dreams* belongs on this shelf. The cotton-credit nexus he describes, in which enslaved people were collateral for the loans that capitalized the very land and tools that would extract their labor, is not metaphorical capitalism. It is capitalism in the technical sense Marx would recognize: an integrated circuit of money, commodities, and labor power organized for accumulation.

Where I think Johnson moves too quickly is in his suggestion that there is no real intellectual loss in collapsing distinctions Oakes wants to preserve. Oakes's worry — that calling Mississippi and Manchester equally "capitalism" obscures fundamental differences between economies based on enslaved and on free labor — is not a quibble. Whether labor is alienated by sale of labor-power or by sale of the laborer is not a minor technical difference. It changes the dynamics of class formation, the politics of resistance, the temporal structure of exploitation, even the rate at which capital can respond to price signals. Johnson is right that nothing in the affirmative claim ("both belong to a single integrated system") requires denying these differences. But there is a tendency in the literature he is championing to do exactly that — to wave away difference because emphasizing similarity does more progressive work.

Here Johnson's own analogy actually points toward a more careful position than the one he defends. When he writes that arguing for a single system "does not necessarily pose any greater threat to historical and analytical precision than arguing that both Harriet Tubman and John C. Calhoun were human beings," he is saying something important: the category of "the human" is large enough to contain enormous difference without dissolving into uselessness. Capitalism, if we are to deploy it the way Du Bois deploys it, has to be a similarly capacious category — one that names a global system of accumulation organized around the differential valuation of human life, while still permitting us to describe with specificity how that system worked in the cotton South, the Lancashire mill, the Brazilian coffee plantation, the Belgian Congo. The unity is at the level of the world-system; the differentiation operates at the level of regional regimes of labor.

What this means in practice is that the most useful version of "racial capitalism" is not the one that declares the debate over and walks off the field. It is the one that insists the question of how, exactly, the parts fit together is the central historical question, and that the parts cannot be understood in isolation. Robinson's actual argument in *Black Marxism* — which is more nuanced than the slogans extracted from it often suggest — is that European capitalism was racialized at its origin, in the differential treatment of Irish, Slavs, Roma, and others, long before the encounter with Africa. The racial character of capitalism is not a contamination of an otherwise pristine economic form; it is a property of the form itself. That is a strong claim, and it deserves more interrogation than Johnson's essay has space to give.

There is a second worry I want to raise about Johnson's essay, which concerns the relationship between his theoretical claims and his programmatic conclusions. The "six principal virtues" he lists at the end constitute a substantive political program — a standpoint epistemology rooted in the Global South, a focus on extraction and distribution, restorative justice and reparations, an emphasis on reproductive politics, the integration of race and class, and the linking of human and ecological domination. I support most of this. But the list is doing a lot of work that the historical argument has not really done. Why does the demonstration that slavery and capitalism were entangled imply that reparations are the only adequate form of redress? It might; the argument is plausible. But it requires its own warrants. One can accept Johnson's history and still believe that, say, universal social-democratic transfers do more for the descendants of the enslaved than targeted reparations, or that the relevant political subject is the multiracial working class rather than "the dark and dispossessed." These are arguments to be had, not entailments of the historical thesis.

This matters because the strongest move in Johnson's essay — the move that strikes me as genuinely original — is the one about historical time. Du Bois, he reminds us, believed that there are moments propitious for remaking the world, and that the cost of missing them is paid in the blood of the next generation. The essay is published in February 2017. A new administration in Washington has just begun an explicitly nativist program, the Movement for Black Lives has put reparations back on the table as a serious political demand, and the climate clock is ticking. Whether this is one of Du Bois's moments is an open question, but it is the right question to be asking. What Johnson's essay does not quite acknowledge is that propitious moments are not won by analytical clarity alone; they are won by the construction of political coalitions broad enough to be effective. Insisting that the standpoint of justice must be that of the Global South rather than the metropolitan working class is, whatever its theoretical virtues, a way of foreclosing certain coalitional possibilities. Du Bois's own most expansive work — *Black Reconstruction* in particular — was at pains to argue that the white worker who refused identification with the black worker was misrecognizing his own interests. The interest-convergence argument is not the same as the standpoint argument, and the relationship between them is something Johnson's essay leaves to the reader.

None of this is to retreat to the comfortable position that everyone was right and we should split the difference. Johnson's essay is, on balance, more right than the school it criticizes. The orthodox separation of slavery and capitalism cannot survive the empirical record. The liberal language of "dehumanization" really does carry the paternalist freight he identifies. The history of the Atlantic world really does collapse into incoherence if you try to abstract any of its component regions from the others. What I am suggesting is only that the affirmative argument is best made in its most demanding form — one that takes the worries of an Oakes seriously rather than dismissing them, one that distinguishes the claim that slavery and capitalism were entangled from the claim that they were identical, and one that does not allow the rhetorical pleasures of denouncing liberalism to substitute for the harder work of saying what should replace it.

The deepest service Johnson's essay performs is to reframe the question. For too long the slavery-capitalism debate has been conducted as if capitalism were the dignifying category and slavery the degraded one, so that admitting slavery to capitalism's tent was a kind of upgrade. Johnson, following Du Bois and Robinson, suggests we should run the comparison the other way: the question is not whether slavery measures up to capitalism, but whether capitalism, properly understood, has ever been anything other than a racial project of accumulation by dispossession. That is a question worth a generation of work, and Johnson has helped start it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The historical evidence confirms that slavery was not a pre-capitalist holdover but a technical circuit of capital accumulation essential to the global economy.
  2. 02Collapsing the distinction between enslaved labor and free wage labor risks obscuring the specific class dynamics and political resistances unique to different regional regimes.
  3. 03The concept of 'racial capitalism' should be treated as a capacious world-system category that accounts for global unity while permitting regional differentiation.
  4. 04The programmatic call for reparative justice and a Global South standpoint is a distinct political choice that does not necessarily follow from the historical fact of slavery's entanglement with capitalism.
  5. 05Prioritizing a 'standpoint of the dispossessed' over a broad multiracial working-class coalition may foreclose political possibilities necessary for the 'propitious moments' of change Johnson identifies.
  6. 06The debate should shift from whether slavery 'qualifies' as capitalism to whether capitalism itself has ever functioned as anything other than a racial project of accumulation by dispossession.

A Marxist Worries About Racial Capitalism

I write as someone who has spent a career working in a broadly Marxist tradition and who has watched, with a mixture of admiration and concern, the rise of "racial capitalism" as a master category in the new history of American slavery. Walter Johnson's essay is one of the most ambitious recent statements of this position, and it deserves a serious response from those of us who are not persuaded that the orthodox tools have been exhausted. My disagreements are not, I want to stress, with Johnson's politics, which I largely share. They are with the analytical move that subordinates the category of capital to the category of race, and with what I think is lost when we do so.

Let me start with what is right in Johnson's account. The claim that slavery and capitalism were historically entangled is correct and overdue. The empirical work of Beckert, Baptist, Bonnie Martin on slave-mortgaged credit, and Johnson's own *River of Dark Dreams* has made it impossible to maintain the older view in which slavery is a precapitalist or noncapitalist formation. Du Bois saw this in 1935 and we are still catching up. The integration of plantation, factory, and counting house into a single account of nineteenth-century accumulation is a real historiographic achievement.

What I am skeptical about is the further step that Johnson, following Robinson, takes: the claim that capitalism was, from the start, "racial capitalism," and that race is not a contingent feature of historical capitalism but a constitutive one. This is a strong metaphysical claim, and it has political implications I think need to be examined.

Consider, first, the actual argument in Robinson's *Black Marxism*. Robinson's claim is that European feudalism was already structured by what he calls "racialism" — hierarchical distinctions among Europeans (Slavs, Irish, Roma, Jews) that were articulated in proto-racial terms before the encounter with Africa. Capitalism, on this view, inherited racialism from feudalism and projected it outward in the age of conquest and slave trade. This is an interesting historical claim, but it has the peculiar effect of detaching "race" from the specific history of anti-black racism that the framework was developed to illuminate. If everything from medieval anti-Irish prejudice to nineteenth-century anti-black ideology counts as "racialism," then the category becomes too broad to do the work of differentiation that historical analysis requires. We end up with a thesis — capitalism is racialism all the way down — that can absorb any counterexample by reclassifying it as a variant of racialism.

The orthodox Marxist approach, by contrast, treats race as a historically specific ideological formation that emerged in particular conditions to do particular work, and which can be analyzed in terms of its functions, limits, and contradictions. This is, I would argue, what Du Bois actually does in *Black Reconstruction*, despite Robinson's appropriation of him. Du Bois's famous account of the "wages of whiteness" is an analysis of how the racialization of the white working class served specific functions in the consolidation of post-Reconstruction capitalism — functions that can be understood, criticized, and potentially undone. To turn this into a thesis that capitalism is permanently and constitutively racial is to remove the analytical leverage that Du Bois's account provided. It also tends to obscure the fact that the racialization of capitalism takes different forms in different conjunctures: the racialization of Irish labor in the nineteenth-century United States is not the racialization of African labor under apartheid, and the differences matter for the politics of solidarity.

There is a related worry about the political consequences of the racial capitalism thesis. If race and class are, as Johnson puts it, "functionally the same," then the prospect of class-based solidarity across racial lines becomes theoretically suspect. The white worker is, on this account, not simply a member of a class with objective interests that align (in important ways) with those of black workers; he is a beneficiary of racial capitalism whose interests are structured against those of the dispossessed. This is the thesis of the "psychological wage" extended into a permanent feature of the system. The trouble is that it makes multiracial working-class politics a more difficult project than the historical record suggests it has to be. Du Bois's *Black Reconstruction* contains, alongside the analysis of the wages of whiteness, a sustained meditation on the moments when interracial labor solidarity was within reach and was sabotaged by political and economic forces. The lesson is not that white workers are permanently disqualified from solidarity; it is that solidarity is a political achievement that has to be built against the forces that work to prevent it.

Johnson's essay does not deny this, exactly, but its framing tilts in the direction I am worried about. The fifth of his "principal virtues" — that the framework "correlates both the entitlement and vulnerability of the white working class with the subjection of the 'dark proletariat'" — treats the white working class principally as beneficiary rather than as both beneficiary and victim of the racial structure. The vulnerabilities of the white working class are real (declining life expectancy, opioid epidemic, deindustrialization, the very phenomena that are being weaponized into white nationalism in our current political moment). Treating these as continuous with rather than opposed to the subjection of the dark proletariat is precisely the analytical task — and it is a task that requires the orthodox Marxist tools of class analysis, even as it requires the additional analytical work that race demands.

A second worry concerns the relation between capitalism and slavery as productive systems. Johnson is right that slavery in the nineteenth-century South was integrated into the global capitalist economy and that the financial structures that made cotton production possible were continuous with those that organized industrial capitalism. But the social relations of production on the plantation were not the same as those in the Lancashire mill, and the differences mattered for the trajectory of the system. The plantation, despite its integration with global markets, was a fundamentally different productive form from the factory: it relied on direct coercion rather than the market in labor power, it organized labor through gang work and the lash rather than through clock discipline, and it tended toward forms of social organization (paternalist, hierarchical, anti-democratic) quite different from those of industrial capitalism. These differences are why Marx, despite his evident moral condemnation of slavery, treated it as a distinct mode of production rather than as a variant of capitalism.

To say this is not to retreat to the position that slavery was "precapitalist." It is to insist that the relationship between modes of production within a world-system is more interesting than a simple identification. Eugene Genovese, despite the limitations of his analysis, was onto something when he treated the slave South as a society that contained capitalist elements but was not reducible to them. The seigneurial culture of the planter class, the resistance to commercial agriculture in the modern sense, the politics of honor — these were not epiphenomena of capitalism but features of a specifically slaveholding social formation that had its own logic, even as it was articulated with the broader capitalist world. James Oakes's worry that calling everything "capitalism" obscures these distinctions is not pedantic conservatism; it is a defense of the analytical specificity that historical materialism requires.

Where does this leave us? I want to be clear that I am not advocating a return to the older periodization in which slavery is the antechamber to capitalism proper. The empirical case for integration is settled. What I am advocating is a more disciplined use of the integrative framework — one that recognizes the integration of slavery and capitalism without collapsing them into a single undifferentiated category, and one that retains the analytical tools of class analysis even as it incorporates the analytical demands of race.

There is also a methodological observation. The strongest version of the racial capitalism thesis tends to work by accumulation rather than by argument: by piling up examples of racial violence within capitalist accumulation until the cumulative effect produces conviction. This is not nothing — the cumulative effect can be epistemically powerful — but it is not the same as having demonstrated the constitutive thesis. The constitutive thesis would require showing that capitalism could not have arisen, or could not function, without racial differentiation. The orthodox view, which I still find more persuasive, is that capitalism has typically operated through some form of social differentiation (race, gender, nationality, religion, caste) and that the specific form it takes in any conjuncture is historically variable. Race has been, in the modern North Atlantic, the dominant axis of differentiation. It is not therefore the only one capitalism could have used.

The political stakes here are real. If racial capitalism is constitutive, then anti-racism and anti-capitalism become a single struggle, and the question of which strategies advance both becomes correspondingly easier (or seems to). If racial capitalism is conjunctural, then the relations between anti-racist and anti-capitalist struggle are matters of political construction, and the question of strategy becomes correspondingly harder — but also more open to the possibility of cross-racial coalition built on shared material interest. I think the second view is both more analytically defensible and more politically generative. Johnson's essay leans toward the first, and I want to register that lean as a concern.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Defining capitalism as inherently racial makes the category of 'race' too broad to distinguish between specific historical ideologies, such as medieval prejudice versus anti-black racism.
  2. 02Collapsing race and class into a single functional category undermines the possibility of multiracial working-class solidarity by framing white workers primarily as beneficiaries of the system rather than potential allies with shared material interests.
  3. 03Labeling the slave plantation and the industrial factory as a single undifferentiated system obscures the fundamental differences in their social relations of production and their distinct historical logics.
  4. 04The 'racial capitalism' thesis relies on an accumulation of examples of violence rather than proving the theoretical necessity that capitalism requires racial differentiation to function.
  5. 05Treating anti-racism and anti-capitalism as a single constitutive struggle limits political strategy compared to a Marxist approach that views their relationship as a matter of active political construction.
