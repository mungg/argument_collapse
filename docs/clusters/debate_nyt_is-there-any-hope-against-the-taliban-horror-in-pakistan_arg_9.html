"The Peshawar school massacre has created a unique moment of national unity and political resolve that provides a genuine opportunity to finally defeat the Taliban through sustained action."

The Peshawar school massacre has finally produced what years of half-hearted military campaigns could not: genuine national horror and unified political resolve. The answer to whether there is hope against the Taliban lies not in questioning our capacity but in demanding the political will that now, mercifully, seems finally achievable.

Pakistan's military has proven itself more than capable of confronting the TTP and affiliated groups. What has been lacking is sustained commitment. The army has preferred to hedge its bets, maintaining relationships with some militant groups while targeting others—the infamous "good Taliban, bad Taliban" calculus that has repeatedly undermined our efforts. But the slaughter of 132 children in a school tears away that moral ambiguity. Prime Minister Sharif's announcement of the death penalty for terrorism convicts and the end of the moratorium on executions signals a turning point.

The path forward requires three elements. First, military operations in North Waziristan and adjoining areas must be escalated into a comprehensive, sustained campaign—not another limited offensive that allows militants to regroup. Second, intelligence coordination between federal and provincial agencies must be dramatically improved; too often, militants have exploited jurisdictional boundaries. Third, the civilian government must resist pressure to negotiate from a position of weakness. Past attempts to accommodate the TTP only legitimized their demands and strengthened their recruiting.

The international community, particularly the United States and China, should be pressed to cut off all funding and sanctuary support for Pakistani militants. For too long, geopolitical calculations have protected terrorists from consequences.

The horror of December 16 can become the catalyst for finally confronting a menace we have coddled for too long. Pakistanis have demonstrated their capacity for outrage. That outrage must now be channeled into sustained action.

Sub-arguments
