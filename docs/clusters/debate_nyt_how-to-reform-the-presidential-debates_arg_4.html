Diversified DeepSeek

"To make presidential debates more substantive, the role of questioning should be shifted from journalists to a representative panel of ordinary, undecided citizens."

To make debates more substantive, we must break the grip of insider journalists and hand the questioning directly to ordinary citizens. Too often, the press corps asks questions that reflect their own preoccupations — process stories, campaign horse-race narratives, hypothetical scandals — rather than the practical concerns that keep Americans up at night. No wonder candidates skate by with talking points.

What if, before each debate, a transparent, randomized process selected a panel of undecided voters from across the country, representing a cross-section of geography, income, and outlook? Those citizens would submit questions in advance, and on debate night, they would ask them directly — on video screen if logistics demand it. The moderator's role would shrink to facilitating the conversation, ensuring each candidate has a chance to respond. Crucially, the citizen questioner would be allowed a brief rebuttal: “That didn’t answer my concern about the cost of prescription drugs.”

This idea borrows from the British “Question Time” model but goes further. It forces candidates to speak to real people, not to television panels. When a steelworker in Ohio asks about the trade deal that shuttered her plant, the candidate cannot hide behind pundit-speak. The authenticity of the exchange can’t be faked. And because the voters are uncommitted, their tolerance for evasion is low. That direct accountability is the surest path to substance.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Journalists often focus on horse-race narratives and process stories rather than the practical policy concerns of the American public.
  2. 02Allowing citizens to ask questions directly forces candidates to abandon scripted talking points and address real-world issues with authenticity.
  3. 03Granting citizen questioners the power of rebuttal ensures candidates are held accountable for evasive or non-responsive answers.
  4. 04A transparent, randomized selection of uncommitted voters provides a diverse range of perspectives that professional moderators lack.
Diversified Gemini

"To improve the substance of presidential debates, the current media-driven format should be replaced with unscripted town halls where citizens, rather than journalists, lead the questioning."

The primary reason our debates lack policy depth is that they are designed by media executives to maximize ratings, not to educate voters. Journalists often ask process questions—"Why are you dropping in the polls?", "How do you respond to what Candidate X said about you yesterday?"—because it generates conflict.

To bring substance back to the forefront, we need to replace journalists with the people who actually have a stake in the policies being discussed: the voters. The town hall format should be the standard, not the exception.

But these shouldn't be the heavily screened, sanitized town halls we are used to, where voters are handed pre-approved questions by network producers. We need authentic, unscripted questions from average citizens. Let a factory worker ask directly about trade policy. Let a public school teacher ask about education funding. Let a veteran ask about VA reform.

When a politician is face-to-face with a citizen whose life is directly impacted by their policies, the dynamic shifts. It is easy to brush off a journalist's question with a slick pivot; it is much harder to look a struggling parent in the eye and deliver a meaningless platitude.

Citizen moderators force candidates to explain their policies in practical, real-world terms. It grounds the abstract policy debates in lived reality. Removing the media middlemen and allowing the American people to drive the questioning would strip away the insider political games and refocus the campaigns on the actual needs of the country.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Media executives and journalists prioritize ratings and political conflict over substantive policy education.
  2. 02Direct interaction with citizens makes it more difficult for candidates to use evasive political pivots and platitudes.
  3. 03Citizen-led questioning forces candidates to explain their platforms in practical, real-world terms rather than abstract political jargon.
  4. 04Removing media moderators eliminates insider political games and refocuses the discussion on the actual needs of the electorate.
Diversified GPT

"To make presidential debates more substantive, the format should shift from journalist-led questioning to a system centered on citizen panels that directly engage with candidates."

To make debates more substantive, stop making journalists the only gatekeepers of what counts as a good question. Political reporters are skilled at exposing contradictions and chasing news, but they often ask questions from inside the campaign conversation: Who is up? Who attacked whom? How will you respond to this quote? Ordinary voters tend to ask questions from life: Can I afford medicine? Will my son be sent to war? Why are my wages flat? What happens if my factory closes?

A better debate would be built around citizens’ panels. Not the usual town hall in which campaigns plant supporters or networks select the most telegenic undecided voter, but a carefully assembled group reflecting the electorate of the state or region hosting the debate. Before the debate, these citizens could meet with nonpartisan policy experts who help them sharpen questions without scripting their views. Then they would question the candidates directly.

The key is follow-up. A voter asking about college debt should be allowed to say, “That does not answer how you would pay for it.” A veteran asking about the Department of Veterans Affairs should be able to ask whether a candidate’s proposal requires new spending, privatization, or administrative reform. Moderators would still keep time and prevent chaos, but they would not monopolize the conversation.

This would change the emotional chemistry of debates. It is easy to dodge a television anchor with a slogan about “American leadership” or “middle-class values.” It is harder to dodge a parent, nurse, small-business owner, student or police officer who asks for practical consequences.

Citizens’ panels would also broaden the agenda. Cable debates gravitate toward the controversies already dominating cable news. Voters might ask about child care, addiction, public transportation, rural hospitals, veterans’ benefits, mental health, crop insurance, or local water systems. These issues are not always glamorous, but they are the stuff of government.

The objection is that ordinary citizens may ask long, emotional or poorly framed questions. So do journalists. The answer is preparation, not exclusion. Democracy is not improved when the public is reduced to applause and instant polling. A presidential debate should let voters do more than watch professionals talk about them. It should let them demand answers.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Ordinary voters prioritize practical life concerns over the political strategy and campaign optics that journalists typically focus on.
  2. 02Providing citizens with access to nonpartisan policy experts before the debate would allow them to sharpen their questions without losing their authentic perspectives.
  3. 03Allowing citizens to ask direct follow-up questions prevents candidates from using vague slogans to dodge accountability.
  4. 04Citizen-led questioning would broaden the debate agenda to include essential governance issues that are often ignored by cable news cycles.
  5. 05Shifting the role of the public from passive observers to active participants strengthens the democratic process by forcing candidates to answer to the electorate directly.
