"China’s engagement in Africa is an exploitative resource grab that reproduces colonial patterns of dependency and actively undermines the continent's long-term development and democratic progress."

Edward Miguel’s essay delivers a measured assessment of China’s growing footprint in Africa, acknowledging both its economic stimulus and its troubling labor and human rights record. But measured is not the same as adequate. China’s engagement with the continent, as of 2008, is not a mixed blessing; it is an unfolding disaster disguised as partnership. The economic boom that Miguel and others celebrate is, in important respects, a Chinese-fuelled resource grab that reproduces the very patterns of dependency and elite enrichment that characterized the colonial and early post-colonial periods. To cast China as a potential engine of Africa’s “turn” is to ignore the exploitative logic at its core.

Start with trade. The dramatic rise in Asia-Africa trade that Miguel cites is overwhelmingly about African raw materials flowing east and Chinese manufactured goods flowing west. This is not economic transformation; it is the classic extractive pattern, with Africa occupying the losing end of the value chain. Chinese demand has indeed raised world prices for oil, copper, and coffee, and some of that revenue trickles down to producers and border towns like Busia. But the benefits are fleeting and fail to create the manufacturing base that historically propelled sustained development in East Asia. Worse, the flood of cheap Chinese textiles, electronics, and machinery is decimating Africa’s own nascent manufacturing sectors. Miguel notes that AGOA’s initial gains have eroded as China’s low-cost factories now compete directly with African textile producers. By 2008, many factories in Kenya, Lesotho, and South Africa have closed or slashed employment, unable to match China’s scale and subsidies. China’s export machine is not just competing with Africa; it is actively de-industrializing it.

Then there is investment. Chinese foreign direct investment is widely touted, but a closer look reveals a deeply problematic picture. Much of it is tied aid: credits from China’s Exim Bank that require African governments to hire Chinese firms and use Chinese inputs. The roads and stadiums those firms build are often staffed largely by imported Chinese workers, so local employment gains are minimal. The reopened Chambishi copper mine in Zambia is now notorious for substandard wages, unsafe conditions, and a fatal 2005 accident — not a deviation from the norm but an expression of the Chinese state-owned enterprise model, in which profit maximization and political loyalty trump worker safety and community relations. Far from being a more “developing-country-friendly” investor, as some Chinese officials claim, Chinese firms are out-competing Western companies precisely by lowering standards Western firms would be shamed into respecting.

Politically, China’s strategy of unconditional support for sovereign governments is a direct endorsement of repression. Sudanese oil, which Miguel discusses, is the clearest case. By providing arms, diplomatic cover, and investment, Beijing enables the Khartoum regime to prosecute its war in Darfur with impunity. The argument that Western sanctions created a vacuum that China merely filled is a poor defense; China could have chosen, as many African civil-society groups demand, to use its leverage to press for a peace settlement. Instead, it has become Khartoum’s chief protector. Across the continent, China’s approach strengthens the hand of presidents who seek to bypass parliaments, stifle the press, and crush dissent — precisely the authoritarian practices that Miguel’s democratic narrative deplores.

Africans themselves are increasingly vocal about these dangers. Zambian mineworkers, Nigerian civil-society activists, and Congolese human rights groups have all protested Chinese practices. Their voices, not the enthusiasm of western economists for positive GDP numbers, should guide our assessment. The “Afro-Chinese community” of small entrepreneurs that Miguel notes is a real phenomenon, but it is dwarfed by the state-capital complex that drives the relationship. China is not helping Africa to develop; it is helping itself to Africa’s resources, leaving behind environmental damage, labor strife, and entrenched authoritarianism. If Africa is going to have its turn, it will need to break free from this new form of subordination, not embrace it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The trade relationship with China reinforces an extractive economic model that de-industrializes Africa by flooding its markets with cheap manufactured goods.
  2. 02Chinese investment projects rely on tied aid and imported labor, providing minimal employment benefits to local populations while maintaining substandard safety and wage conditions.
  3. 03Beijing’s policy of unconditional support for sovereign governments provides diplomatic and military cover for repressive regimes, directly enabling human rights abuses.
  4. 04China’s state-capital complex strengthens the power of autocratic leaders, allowing them to bypass democratic institutions and stifle civil society.
  5. 05The environmental damage and labor strife caused by Chinese state-owned enterprises create a new form of subordination rather than a genuine developmental partnership.
