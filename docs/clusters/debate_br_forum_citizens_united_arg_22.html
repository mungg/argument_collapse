**Lessig's Reformism Is Too Small for the Crisis He Describes**

Lawrence Lessig is one of the most thoughtful liberal critics of the American campaign-finance system, and his concept of dependency corruption is a genuine contribution. But the essay he has written for *Boston Review* exemplifies a deeper limitation of the legal-academic reform tradition: it cannot bring itself to name the structural forces that actually produce the corruption it identifies. The result is a diagnosis far more radical than the cure Lessig proposes, and a politics that drifts steadily toward technocratic Band-Aids while the underlying disorder metastasizes.

What is the real problem? It is not, in the first instance, that the Supreme Court got *Citizens United* wrong. It is that an enormously powerful concentration of corporate wealth has, over the last four decades, captured the political institutions of the United States—legislative, executive, judicial, and regulatory—and oriented them around the interests of capital rather than labor, the planet, or the public. Campaign finance is one channel of this capture. It is far from the only one. Lobbying, regulatory revolving doors, the think-tank ecosystem, corporate media ownership, the intellectual hegemony of neoclassical economics in universities and policymaking, the disciplining force of capital flight on small open economies—all of these operate together as a system. *Citizens United* did not create this system. It removed one of the few legal restraints on its most visible manifestation.

Lessig's framework cannot quite see this because it insists on treating corruption as an institutional drift from "intended" purposes, as if there were a pristine original design from which Congress has unfortunately deviated. But the framers' Congress was itself a tool of merchant capital, slaveholders, and propertied elites. The "dependence on the people" Lessig invokes was a dependence on a sharply restricted electorate of white male property owners. The history of American democracy is not a history of decline from founding purity; it is a history of struggle by excluded groups—workers, women, Black Americans, immigrants—to wrest substantive representation from a system designed to deny it to them. *Citizens United* is one episode in the long counterrevolution against that struggle, not a unique departure from a previously sound design.

This matters because it determines what kind of reform might actually work. Lessig's preferred remedies—Ackerman-Ayres democracy vouchers, Fair Elections Now matching funds—are clever, well-designed, and almost certainly inadequate. The voucher system would inject several billion dollars of small-donor money into campaigns. Independent expenditures by corporations and wealthy individuals would continue to dwarf this in the high-stakes races where money matters most. Even if voucher money funded primary challenges and grassroots candidates, those candidates would still face hostile coverage in corporate-owned media, opposition from a well-funded think-tank ecosystem, and the structural pressures of governing in an economy that punishes deviation from elite policy preferences. The voucher system addresses the symptom (uneven distribution of contributions) without touching the underlying inequality of resources that gives some Americans far more political power than others.

A more honest reform agenda would have to grapple with the fact that political equality is impossible without substantial economic equality. This was understood by an earlier generation of American progressives—Brandeis, La Follette, even some Republicans of the trust-busting era—who recognized that concentrated wealth and democratic self-government cannot coexist. Their response was structural: antitrust, the income tax, labor rights, securities regulation, public utilities. These were not, primarily, campaign-finance reforms. They were attempts to break the economic power that translates into political power. The campaign-finance reform tradition, by contrast, has too often functioned as a substitute for structural economic reform, allowing reformers to talk about corruption without talking about capitalism.

Lessig's own framing illustrates the limitation. He writes that the institutional corruption he describes has "likely done more damage to the United States in the last decade than all of the quid pro quo corruption in American history combined." Strong words. He cites the Great Recession and global warming. But the Great Recession was not caused by campaign contributions. It was caused by financial deregulation pursued by Democrats and Republicans alike, embedded in a global financial architecture, justified by an economic ideology that has dominated American policymaking since the late 1970s, and made possible by the destruction of countervailing power—organized labor, in particular—over the same period. Climate inaction has similar structural roots: the political weakness of environmental constituencies relative to fossil-fuel-producing regions, the dependency of the American economy on cheap energy, the global collective-action problem. Campaign contributions are downstream of these factors, not upstream. Reforming campaign finance without reforming the underlying political economy is like reforming the symptoms while leaving the disease in place.

There is also a question about the political theory underlying Lessig's argument. He cites Federalist 57 with apparent approval, and he treats the framers' design as authoritative. But the framers' design produced the very system Lessig now decries. Frequent elections, single-member districts, an underfunded legislative branch dependent on outside expertise, weak parties, separation of powers that requires extensive coalition-building—all of these structural features create the entry points through which money operates. A serious reform of American democracy would have to consider proportional representation, public financing of media, sectoral collective bargaining, antitrust enforcement to break up corporate giants, and probably constitutional amendment to limit private wealth in politics directly. None of these are in Lessig's essay.

I do not mean to be ungenerous. Lessig is right that *Citizens United* is bad, that dependency corruption is a useful concept, and that some reform of campaign finance is better than none. The matching-funds and voucher proposals are worth pursuing. But we should not pretend that they are sufficient to the crisis he himself describes. A democracy in which fossil-fuel interests can block climate action, financial interests can capture regulation, and the median voter's preferences are systematically ignored on issue after issue is not a democracy with a campaign-finance problem. It is a democracy with a political-economic-power problem. The campaign-finance frame, by localizing the diagnosis, ends up minimizing the remedy.

If Lessig's essay marks the limit of what a major American legal academic is willing to say in 2010, that itself is a measure of how constrained our political imagination has become. The corruption is deeper than he is willing to name. The reform required is correspondingly bigger.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Lessig’s reliance on the 'original intent' of the framers is flawed because the U.S. constitutional design was originally built to favor propertied elites and exclude the broader public.
  2. 02Campaign-finance reform alone cannot achieve political equality as long as extreme economic inequality allows capital to dominate through lobbying, media ownership, and think tanks.
  3. 03The crises Lessig cites, such as the Great Recession and climate change, are rooted in structural political-economic shifts and the decline of countervailing powers like labor unions, not just campaign contributions.
  4. 04Technocratic solutions like democracy vouchers fail to address the underlying power of private wealth to discipline the political system and punish policy deviations.
  5. 05A sufficient response to the current crisis requires radical structural changes—such as antitrust enforcement, labor rights, and constitutional amendments—rather than minor adjustments to election funding.
