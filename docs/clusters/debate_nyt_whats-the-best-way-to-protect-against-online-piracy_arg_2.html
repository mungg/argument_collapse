Diversified DeepSeek

"The best way to protect against online piracy is to enact a narrowly tailored law that surgically targets the financial resources of foreign pirate sites through judicial oversight rather than broad technical censorship."

The collective sigh of relief following the derailment of SOPA and PIPA is understandable, but it obscures a sobering reality: American creators are hemorrhaging jobs and revenue to foreign pirate sites, and the current legal toolkit is an antique in a digital age. The protests rightly highlighted serious flaws in the legislative language, particularly the potential for overbroad domain-name filtering, but the core impulse—to give law enforcement and rights holders meaningful tools against offshore theft—remains entirely valid. The best path forward is not to abandon legislation, but to craft a sharply focused bill that surgically targets the worst actors while embracing robust due process.

SOPA’s fatal error was making the site operator, rather than a court, the initial arbiter of infringement. Any new bill must flip that entirely. A targeted statute should allow the Department of Justice, after a full adversarial hearing before a federal judge, to seek orders against foreign websites whose primary purpose is to traffic in infringing goods. The orders could compel domestic intermediaries—payment processors and advertising networks, not internet service providers—to cease doing business with the adjudicated pirate site. This approach avoids the messy, encryption-defeating DNS meddling that so alarmed the technical community and preserves the secure functioning of the domain name system.

Crucially, such a law must also protect legitimate intermediaries. Defining a “site dedicated to infringement” needs to be as narrow as possible, explicitly excluding sites with substantial non-infringing uses and platforms that merely host user-generated content. Wikipedia and YouTube would have nothing to fear from a well-written statute; the target is the criminally run, foreign-hosted cyberlocker or streaming hub that makes a mockery of U.S. law. By confining the remedy to cutting off the money supply, we can starve these parasites without damaging the host. The protests weren't the end of the debate; they were the necessary, painful correction that can now lead to a law both effective and just.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01New legislation must ensure robust due process by requiring a full adversarial hearing before a federal judge to determine if a site's primary purpose is infringement.
  2. 02Anti-piracy efforts should focus on cutting off the money supply via payment processors and advertising networks rather than using disruptive domain-name filtering.
  3. 03The legal definition of infringing sites must be strictly narrowed to exclude legitimate platforms that host user-generated content or have substantial non-infringing uses.
  4. 04Targeting offshore criminal entities is necessary to protect American jobs and revenue from the limitations of current, outdated legal tools.

"Congress should replace the overly broad SOPA and PIPA with narrower legislation that targets foreign rogue sites through judicial oversight and financial sanctions rather than abandoning anti-piracy efforts entirely."

The revolt against SOPA and PIPA has been healthy in one respect: it has forced Congress to notice that the Internet is not merely another distribution channel controlled by Hollywood studios and record labels. But the celebration should not obscure the real injury being done to American creators and companies by rogue foreign sites. If lawmakers simply retreat, they will have rewarded the most extreme rhetoric of the protests and left the underlying theft untouched.

The United States cannot maintain a serious creative economy if digital piracy is treated as a nuisance rather than a business model. Some sites exist for no meaningful purpose except to distribute stolen films, television programs, music, games and software. They sell advertising, collect subscription fees and hide behind foreign registrars or lax jurisdictions. Suing individual downloaders is both unpopular and ineffective. Asking creators to “adapt” is not an answer when their work is being commercially exploited without permission.

Still, SOPA and PIPA were drafted too broadly. The remedy is not surrender but revision. Congress should write a narrower bill aimed only at foreign sites that are overwhelmingly dedicated to infringement. Before any sanction, a rights holder or the government should have to go to federal court, identify the site, show substantial evidence, and give notice where possible. The accused site should have a prompt opportunity to appear. Orders should be limited to specific remedies: barring payment processors and advertising networks from doing business with the site, and requiring search engines to remove links to adjudicated rogue domains.

DNS blocking is the most troubling proposal and should be dropped unless technical experts can establish that it will not undermine security or fragment the network. But it is hard to see why Visa, MasterCard, PayPal or American ad networks should be compelled to keep servicing a site a court has found to be a piracy operation. That is not censorship. It is refusing to finance unlawful commerce.

The technology companies also need to accept responsibility. Safe harbors were designed to protect good-faith intermediaries, not to enable willful blindness. If Silicon Valley wants to defeat bad legislation, it should help craft better legislation and cooperate in enforcement against genuinely bad actors. Protecting the Internet and protecting intellectual property are not mutually exclusive. A functioning digital marketplace requires both freedom to innovate and confidence that creative work will not be stolen with impunity.

  3. 03Enforcement should focus on cutting off the financial lifeblood of infringing sites by barring payment processors and advertising networks from doing business with them.
  4. 04DNS blocking should be removed from legislative proposals unless technical experts can prove it does not compromise internet security or stability.
  5. 05Technology companies must move beyond willful blindness and cooperate in crafting and enforcing regulations against genuinely bad actors to ensure a functional digital marketplace.

"The best way to combat online piracy is to target the financial and commercial infrastructure of infringing sites rather than interfering with the Internet's technical addressing and navigation layers."

SOPA and PIPA are bad Internet engineering masquerading as copyright policy. That is why the opposition has been so intense and why lawmakers are wise to pause. The proposed use of DNS blocking, search manipulation and broad intermediary liability misunderstands how the network works and threatens collateral damage far beyond the entertainment industry.

The domain name system is not a copyright switchboard. It is a core layer of Internet navigation and security. For years, technologists have been trying to improve DNS security so users can trust that when they type a domain name, they reach the intended destination. A law that encourages or requires deliberate interference with DNS responses cuts against that effort. It also invites circumvention: users can switch to foreign DNS servers, use direct IP addresses, or rely on tools that may themselves be insecure. The result could be less respect for law and a less secure network.

Search blocking is also less clean than it sounds. Search engines index a messy universe of lawful and unlawful material. Orders that are too broad can erase legitimate speech, commentary, reviews, forums and user-generated content. Once the mechanism exists, other industries and governments will want to use it. Today the target is foreign piracy; tomorrow it may be defamation, leaks, gambling, political extremism or anything else someone with influence wants made less visible.

The better path is to target the business layer, not the addressing layer. Commercial piracy at scale depends on money. It needs ad networks, payment processors, hosting providers and sometimes shipping or fulfillment services. Courts can issue narrow orders cutting off those services after an evidentiary showing that a site is primarily dedicated to infringement. Such orders should be public, appealable and limited in time. A mistaken cutoff of payment is serious, but it is less architecturally dangerous than corrupting Internet naming or imposing broad monitoring duties on every platform.

We should also improve authentication of rights claims. One reason platforms struggle is that ownership information is fragmented and sometimes disputed. Better registries, standardized licensing and machine-readable rights data would help lawful services flourish and make bad actors easier to identify.

Piracy is a real problem, but the Internet is not a machine Congress can safely rewire under pressure from lobbyists. Sound policy should be technically humble. Enforce the law against commercial infringers, follow the money, and preserve the neutral infrastructure on which both free expression and digital commerce depend.

  1. 01Manipulating the Domain Name System (DNS) for copyright enforcement undermines global network security and encourages users to adopt insecure circumvention tools.
  2. 02Mandating search engine blocking risks the accidental erasure of legitimate speech and creates a dangerous precedent for broader government censorship.
  3. 03Targeting ad networks and payment processors is a more effective and less architecturally dangerous method of stopping commercial piracy than technical network interference.
  4. 04Improving the authentication of rights claims through standardized registries and machine-readable data would help lawful services flourish and simplify the identification of bad actors.
  5. 05Anti-piracy policy should remain technically humble to preserve the neutral infrastructure necessary for both free expression and digital commerce.

"The best way to protect against online piracy is to replace the flawed SOPA and PIPA bills with a narrower, court-supervised legislative framework that targets the financial intermediaries of infringing sites rather than the internet's technical infrastructure."

**The Bills Were Bad. The Problem Is Real.**

The defeat of SOPA and PIPA this week is being celebrated as a triumph of the open Web over Hollywood. As someone who opposed both bills, I share the relief. But I am uneasy about the triumphalism, because the underlying problem these bills tried, clumsily, to address has not gone away.

Foreign sites whose entire business model is the unauthorized distribution of American films, software, books, and music are a genuine harm. They are unreachable by ordinary U.S. process. Their operators face no consequences. Their advertising and payment processing often flow through American intermediaries. Pretending this is not a real problem — or that every download is a sale the studios never would have made anyway — is intellectually dishonest. Creators, including many who are not movie moguls, lose income to commercial-scale infringement.

What was wrong with SOPA and PIPA was not their ambition but their architecture. DNS filtering would have fractured the security work being done on DNSSEC and pushed users toward offshore resolvers that the government cannot see at all. The private right of action invited abuse by any rights holder willing to threaten payment processors and ad networks. The vague "facilitation" language would have chilled legitimate user-generated-content sites that already comply with the DMCA. These are design flaws, and design flaws can be fixed.

A better bill would do three things. First, it would limit any new remedy to the narrow category of sites that are demonstrably and overwhelmingly dedicated to infringement — the standard the Justice Department already uses for ICE seizures of domestic domains. Second, it would route every action through a court, with adversarial process, before any cutoff of payment or advertising. Third, it would drop DNS-level blocking entirely and stick with the "follow the money" tools that even most critics concede are reasonable.

The OPEN Act introduced by Senator Wyden and Representative Issa is roughly that bill. It is narrower, court-supervised, and aimed at commercial intermediaries rather than the Internet's plumbing. Critics of SOPA who simply wanted no bill at all should be careful: the next attempt, after the next high-profile infringement scandal, may be less considered than this one. Better to lock in a reasonable framework now than to wait for an angrier Congress.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Foreign-based commercial-scale infringement causes genuine economic harm to creators and remains unreachable by standard U.S. legal processes.
  2. 02SOPA and PIPA were fundamentally flawed because their use of DNS filtering threatened internet security and their vague language risked chilling legitimate user-generated content.
  3. 03Effective anti-piracy legislation should be limited to sites overwhelmingly dedicated to infringement to avoid overreach.
  4. 04Due process must be maintained by requiring adversarial court proceedings before any financial or advertising cutoffs are implemented.
  5. 05Focusing on 'following the money' through payment processors and ad networks is a more reasonable and less disruptive approach than blocking domain names.
  6. 06Passing a moderate alternative like the OPEN Act now prevents the future risk of more reactive and poorly designed legislation from an angry Congress.
Diversified Gemini

"The best way to combat online piracy is to pass the OPEN Act, which targets the financial revenue of rogue websites rather than compromising the technical infrastructure of the internet."

The backlash against SOPA and PIPA has successfully demonstrated that the public will not tolerate legislation that compromises the Domain Name System (DNS) or threatens the basic functionality of the open web. However, foreign "rogue websites" dedicated entirely to the distribution of stolen American intellectual property remain a serious economic threat. To protect content creators without breaking the Internet, Congress should focus not on censoring the web, but on starving these illicit operations of their revenue. We must "follow the money."

Piracy on a commercial scale is not a hobby; it is a highly profitable business model. The operators of massive offshore torrent trackers and illegal streaming sites rely on American financial networks and advertising syndicates to monetize their stolen traffic. They process subscriptions and donations through PayPal, Visa, and MasterCard, and they run display ads served by American ad networks.

The Online Protection and Enforcement of Digital Trade (OPEN) Act, currently being championed by Senator Ron Wyden and Representative Darrell Issa, offers a much more targeted and constitutional alternative to SOPA and PIPA. Rather than forcing Internet Service Providers to block websites at the DNS level—a move that cybersecurity experts warn could destabilize the web—the OPEN Act empowers the International Trade Commission (ITC) to investigate foreign sites accused of massive copyright infringement. If a site is found to be dedicated to piracy, the ITC can issue cease-and-desist orders directing payment processors and ad networks to cut off business with the infringing site.

This approach addresses the root cause of commercial piracy: the profit motive. By severing the financial lifelines of rogue websites, we can effectively shut them down without opening the door to domestic censorship or undermining the technical integrity of the global internet. The entertainment industry deserves protection for its investments, but we do not need to rewrite the rules of the Internet to provide it. Following the money is a surgical, effective, and internet-friendly solution to a complex economic problem.

  1. 01Commercial piracy is a profit-driven business model that relies on American payment processors and advertising networks to survive.
  2. 02The OPEN Act provides a constitutional alternative to SOPA and PIPA by empowering the International Trade Commission to investigate and cut off the financial lifelines of infringing sites.
  3. 03Targeting financial flows is a surgical approach that avoids the cybersecurity risks and potential for censorship associated with DNS-level blocking.
  4. 04Severing the profit motive effectively shuts down illicit operations without undermining the technical integrity of the global internet.

"The best way to protect against online piracy is through a surgical, court-centered approach that targets the financial infrastructure of rogue sites rather than interfering with the technical architecture of the internet."

The best alternative to SOPA and PIPA is not a softer slogan but a better procedure. Online piracy is real, especially on foreign “rogue” sites built around selling unauthorized movies, music, software and counterfeit goods. But the cure should not be a broad power to make lawful sites disappear, interfere with the domain-name system, or expose platforms to liability for what users post before anyone has had a fair chance to contest the charge.

Congress should begin with a narrow, court-centered approach. If a copyright owner believes a foreign site is dedicated to infringement, it should have to make that case to a federal judge, with a clear definition of the target: a site whose primary purpose is infringement, not a search engine, blog, social network, cloud service or payment processor that has some infringing activity somewhere on it. Remedies should be proportional and directed at business relationships rather than at the architecture of the Internet.

The most promising path is “follow the money.” Sites trafficking in pirated material often depend on advertising networks, credit-card companies, online payment services and search visibility. A court order requiring major payment and advertising intermediaries to stop doing business with a proven rogue site is much less dangerous than DNS blocking or broad de-indexing mandates. Money is where piracy businesses are vulnerable; the domain-name system is where everyone is vulnerable.

Any such law also needs safeguards that SOPA and PIPA lack or blur. There should be notice to the accused site when feasible, an opportunity to respond, penalties for false or reckless accusations, expedited review, and protection for fair use and lawful speech. The law should distinguish between willful commercial piracy and ordinary users remixing, quoting, backing up or sharing in ways that may be legal or at least not comparable to running a counterfeit marketplace.

The Internet’s success rests on low barriers to entry. A statute that makes startups screen all user behavior or fear being cut off because of a complaint will protect incumbents more than artists. Piracy enforcement is legitimate, but it must be surgical. We can punish businesses devoted to infringement without giving private parties and prosecutors a switch that can shut down speech, innovation and competition. The answer is due process, narrow targeting and economic pressure—not Internet blacklists.

  1. 01Enforcement should be limited to sites whose primary purpose is infringement, excluding general platforms like search engines, social networks, and cloud services.
  2. 02Remedies should focus on 'following the money' by requiring payment processors and advertising networks to sever ties with proven rogue sites.
  3. 03Anti-piracy measures must include robust due process safeguards, such as notice to the accused, penalties for false accusations, and protections for fair use.
  4. 04Legal frameworks must distinguish between willful commercial piracy and non-commercial user activities like remixing or sharing to avoid stifling innovation and speech.
  5. 05Enforcement should be handled through federal courts to ensure that remedies are proportional and based on clear legal definitions rather than broad administrative powers.

"Congress should replace SOPA and PIPA with a more narrowly tailored anti-piracy statute that targets the financial infrastructure of foreign infringing sites while ensuring due process and protecting the technical integrity of the internet."

The online protest against SOPA and PIPA should not obscure a stubborn fact: large-scale piracy is theft in a modern form, and the United States has been too passive about it. A foreign site that builds a business by offering unauthorized films, music, television shows, games or software is not engaging in free expression. It is exploiting creative labor while hiding behind borders and the technical openness of the Internet.

That said, the bills now before Congress have been drafted too broadly and defended too carelessly. The right response is not to abandon anti-piracy legislation but to write a tighter bill. The Digital Millennium Copyright Act was designed for a different era. Notice-and-takedown works tolerably for isolated files, but it is weak against sites that remove one link while immediately posting another, or that operate outside American jurisdiction while selling advertising to American audiences.

A better statute would identify foreign sites primarily dedicated to infringement and subject them to fast judicial review. Once a judge makes that finding, American companies should not have to help those sites make money. Payment processors, credit-card networks and ad brokers should be required to cut ties. Search engines could be required, in narrow circumstances, not to promote a site already adjudicated as a pirate enterprise. These are not radical ideas; they are ordinary refusals to subsidize unlawful commerce.

Opponents are right to worry about DNS blocking, vague definitions and private censorship. Those provisions should be removed or constrained. But it is not censorship to stop a criminal marketplace from using legitimate American intermediaries. Nor is it an attack on innovation to ask Internet companies to cooperate when a court has found systematic infringement. The technology sector’s rhetoric sometimes treats any obligation as a mortal threat to the Web. That is no more persuasive than Hollywood’s claim that every unauthorized download equals a lost sale.

Creative industries employ people far beyond famous actors and executives: set builders, editors, musicians, writers, designers, programmers and small labels. Their work deserves protection. If Congress retreats entirely, it will send the message that organized piracy is an acceptable cost of doing business online. It is not.

The answer is a revised anti-piracy bill: narrow definitions, real due process, no DNS tampering, remedies aimed at money, and penalties for abuse. We can defend the Internet without surrendering the rights of creators.

  3. 03Effective enforcement should focus on requiring American payment processors and ad brokers to cut ties with adjudicated pirate sites rather than using broad DNS blocking.
  4. 04Legislation must include strict judicial review and narrow definitions to prevent private censorship and protect the rights of legitimate internet users.
  5. 05Protecting intellectual property is essential for supporting the diverse workforce of the creative industries, including set builders, programmers, and designers.
