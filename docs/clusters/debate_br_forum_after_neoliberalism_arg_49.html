"The Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) manifesto unfairly scapegoats market-oriented economics for societal ills while proposing a technocratic, interventionist agenda that ignores the historical success of global markets and the inherent risks of government overreach."

The manifesto by Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman is a fascinating exercise in historical revisionism and disciplinary self-flagellation. In their eagerness to distance themselves from the political bogeyman of “neoliberalism,” the authors construct a cartoonish narrative of the last forty years, blaming market-oriented economics for every societal ill from stagnating wages to the “illiberal, nativist turn in our politics.” In doing so, they threaten to discard the very principles that have driven the greatest expansion of human prosperity in recorded history.

Let us start with the alleged failure of the "neoliberal policy framework." The authors claim we live in an age of "astonishing inequality" and that the prevailing framework has "failed society." This is a profoundly parochial, Americentric view. If we look at the world as a whole, the era of so-called neoliberalism (roughly 1980 to the present) has been a period of miraculous convergence. More than a billion people have been lifted out of extreme poverty, primarily in China, India, and other parts of the developing world that embraced the very "hyper-globalization" and market reforms the authors now deride. Global inequality has actually fallen. To look at the late 20th and early 21st centuries and see only "monumental failure" requires blinding oneself to the most spectacular reduction in human misery ever achieved.

The authors argue that "market fundamentalism" is a "simplistic perversion" of economics. But who, exactly, are these market fundamentalists? Milton Friedman and F.A. Hayek, whom they explicitly target, were not advocates of a lawless, unregulated free-for-all. They recognized the need for a strong state to enforce property rights, maintain the rule of law, and provide a basic safety net. What they warned against—presciently—was the hubris of technocrats who believe they can centrally manage complex economic systems to achieve specific distributional outcomes.

Ironically, the EfIP agenda embodies precisely this technocratic hubris. The authors promise a menu of "policy free lunches" and "precision tweaks to the tax code" that will magically produce inclusive prosperity without sacrificing efficiency. Anton Korinek wants governments to "steer technology" and promote AI systems that have "desirable distributive properties." Does anyone seriously believe that government bureaucrats have the foresight to micromanage the trajectory of artificial intelligence? The history of industrial policy is littered with spectacular failures, cronyism, and regulatory capture. Why should we expect the state to successfully plan the cognitive technologies of the 21st century?

Similarly, Dani Rodrik proposes "social safeguards" to restrict trade when it threatens the "domestic social contract." This is nothing more than a sophisticated intellectual gloss for protectionism. In 2019, as we witness the destructive consequences of the Trump administration's erratic tariff wars, it is astonishing to see prominent economists providing top cover for policies that invite retaliatory spirals and raise prices for the poorest consumers. We know what happens when trade policy is subjected to domestic political grievances: entrenched, concentrated interests win, and the diffuse public loses.

The authors celebrate the empirical turn in economics, suggesting that "systematic empirical evidence is a disciplining device against ideological policy prescriptions." Yet they selectively marshal evidence that supports an interventionist agenda. They highlight Arindrajit Dube’s work on wage boards while waving away the massive empirical literature on the disemployment effects of high minimum wages and rigid labor market regulations in Europe. They embrace Gabriel Zucman’s proposals for taxing multinationals without grappling with the vast literature on how corporate taxes depress capital formation and are ultimately borne by workers.

Economics has indeed evolved, and it should absolutely concern itself with market failures, externalities, and the institutional foundations of markets. But the core insights of the discipline—that incentives matter, that trade is positive-sum, that prices convey essential information, and that government intervention often produces unintended consequences—are not "primitive" or "simplistic." They are hard-won truths that protect society from the utopian schemes of social engineers.

The EfIP economists want to rebrand economics for an era of populist anger. But the job of the economist is not to cater to the zeitgeist or to validate "legitimate grievances" by endorsing bad policies. The job is to patiently explain the unseen costs of political interference in voluntary exchange. By tossing the classical liberal tradition under the bus to appease the political left, Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman are not saving economics. They are surrendering its most valuable intellectual inheritance.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The authors' claim that the neoliberal era was a failure is a narrow, Americentric perspective that ignores the historic reduction in global poverty and inequality achieved through market reforms and globalization.
  2. 02The proposed policy solutions, such as steering the trajectory of AI or implementing 'social safeguards' on trade, represent a dangerous technocratic hubris that invites protectionism, cronyism, and regulatory capture.
  3. 03The manifesto selectively uses empirical evidence to support an interventionist agenda while dismissing established economic research on the negative consequences of high taxes and rigid labor regulations.
  4. 04By framing core economic principles like incentives and free trade as 'simplistic perversions,' the authors are abandoning the discipline's most valuable insights to appease current political trends.
  5. 05The authors mischaracterize classical liberal economists like Friedman and Hayek, who recognized the necessity of the state but warned against the very social engineering the EfIP project now advocates.
