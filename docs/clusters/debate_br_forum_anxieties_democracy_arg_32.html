"Katznelson’s defense of representative democracy is limited by a liberal framework that prioritizes procedural correctness over the substantive moral values and democratic education necessary to ensure citizens make wise choices."

The most striking feature of Katznelson's essay is its normative ambiguity. He clearly believes democracy is in trouble, but he never quite says what he thinks should be done. His conclusion calls for "a robust debate about these questions" but offers no answers. This reticence may reflect scholarly caution, but it also reflects a deeper uncertainty about what the good life requires.

The essay operates within a liberal framework that treats democracy as instrument rather than end. Democracy is valuable because it produces good policy, respects rights, and enables legitimate collective decision-making. But what if democracy produces bad policy? What if majority preferences are illiberal or foolish? What if citizens prefer redistribution to the wealthy over their own interests? The liberal framework has no answer.

Perhaps democracy is valuable regardless of its consequences. This is the position of classical republicanism, which valued citizenship and participation as ends in themselves. But Katznelson shows little interest in this tradition. He focuses on procedural correctness—"formal, procedural correctness without distinction of content"—without asking whether the procedures might produce undesirable content.

Consider the Tea Party. Its members were exercising political rights as Katznelson would define them. They participated in elections, formed organizations, and articulated policy positions. Yet they advanced policies—abolishing the Department of Education, defaulting on the national debt, repealing healthcare reform—that would have harmed many of their own supporters. Were they exercising democracy or undermining it?

Katznelson would say they're exercising democracy, albeit misguidedly. But this answer requires a theory of democratic education that he doesn't provide. How do citizens learn to prefer good policies? How does democracy correct its own errors? The New Deal's success suggests democracy can learn from experience. But the persistence of bad preferences despite abundant evidence suggests otherwise.

Maybe democracy requires a morally serious citizenry that doesn't yet exist. This is a uncomfortable conclusion, but Katznelson's silence on substantive values points toward it. If democracy requires background consensus on fundamental questions, and that consensus has dissolved, then democratic renewal requires not just institutional reform but moral renewal. Katznelson doesn't tell us what moral framework might unite divided citizens.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The essay’s conclusion is normatively ambiguous because it calls for debate without offering a vision of what the 'good life' or a moral framework for society should look like.
  2. 02A purely procedural view of democracy fails to address the problem of citizens using democratic rights to advocate for illiberal or self-harming policies.
  3. 03The success of democratic institutions depends on a morally serious citizenry and a shared consensus on fundamental questions, which institutional reform alone cannot provide.
  4. 04Katznelson neglects the classical republican tradition that views political participation as an inherent end rather than just a tool for policy production.
