"While Brandon Terry correctly identifies Martin Luther King Jr. as a radical thinker, his interpretation risks distorting King's legacy by underplaying the extent to which King’s power was rooted in a sincere appeal to American liberal and constitutional traditions."

Brandon Terry has written a learned and often bracing essay against the domestication of Martin Luther King, Jr. He is right that King was more than the soothing civic figure regularly invoked each January. King was a demanding moralist, a critic of complacency, a man who endorsed civil disobedience and came to speak with increasing severity about poverty and war. Any honest account must include the King who wrote “Letter from Birmingham Jail,” the King who went to Memphis, and the King who denounced the Vietnam War.

But Terry’s corrective risks producing a distortion of its own. In rescuing King from sentimental nationalism, he sometimes moves too quickly toward a King useful to contemporary radicalism. The resulting portrait is richer than the schoolhouse myth, but it underplays the elements of King’s thought that were genuinely rooted in America’s liberal and constitutional tradition. King was not a conservative in the partisan sense, and he certainly was not an advocate of colorblind complacency. Yet neither was he simply a prophet standing outside the American project. His power came from the fact that he appealed to principles Americans already professed and demanded that they be honored.

That appeal should not be dismissed as naïve or merely tactical. King’s invocation of the Declaration of Independence, the Constitution, the prophets, and the Christian conscience was central to his political genius. When he said America had given black citizens a bad check, he did not say the bank itself was fraudulent beyond repair. He said the promissory note had to be cashed. Terry worries that this makes King “backward looking and even conservative.” But there is a difference between complacent reverence for the past and principled appeal to inherited commitments. Without the latter, democratic reform becomes either raw power struggle or moral exhortation without a common language.

This matters now because our politics is suffering from a collapse of shared civic vocabulary. The Trump era has exposed ugly forces in American life: nativism, racial resentment, contempt for norms, and indifference to truth. But the answer cannot be to abandon the language of constitutional democracy in favor of totalizing accounts of white supremacy, capitalism, or empire. Terry himself warns against theories of racism that explain too much and distinguish too little. That warning should apply equally to the tendency to portray the United States as so corrupted from inception that its ideals are nothing more than masks for domination.

King did not believe that. He believed America was morally disfigured by racism, but also that its democratic creed had real content. This duality is not weakness. It is the ground on which reform politics stands. If the nation’s stated principles are merely fraudulent, then appeals to equal citizenship are foolish. If those principles are fully realized, then protest is unnecessary. King occupied the morally demanding middle ground: America’s ideals are real enough to indict America’s practices.

Terry is at his best when he emphasizes King’s precision about racism. King understood racism as fear, ideology, social structure, and moral blindness. That complexity is badly needed. Contemporary debate often alternates between two unsatisfactory poles. On one side are those who deny racism unless a smoking-gun slur or explicit discriminatory intent can be found. On the other are those who label virtually every disparity or disagreement an expression of white supremacy. King’s approach resists both. He took racism seriously without making it the single cause of every social problem.

This distinction is essential for public policy. Racial disparities in wealth, incarceration, education, health, and housing are grave and often rooted in histories of discrimination. But the remedies must be carefully matched to causes. Some inequalities require antidiscrimination enforcement; some require better schools, safer neighborhoods, and economic growth; some require criminal justice reform; some require family and community support; some require changes in zoning and housing policy. If every problem is described in the same language, policy becomes symbolic rather than effective.

I part company with Terry, however, in his enthusiasm for mass civil disobedience as the necessary engine of economic transformation. Civil disobedience has an honorable place in democratic life, especially when ordinary channels are blocked and fundamental rights are denied. The civil rights movement’s willingness to break unjust laws and expose brutality was morally justified. But civil disobedience is a dangerous instrument. It deliberately creates crisis, disrupts public order, and pressures institutions outside normal democratic procedures. That may be necessary in extreme cases; it should not become the default model of politics.

King himself recognized the need for discipline, publicity, negotiation, and willingness to accept punishment. These conditions matter. Much contemporary protest lacks them. Blocking highways, shouting down speakers, occupying buildings, or treating disagreement as violence may dramatize anger, but it does not automatically deepen democracy. Indeed, when citizens conclude that activists are trying to coerce rather than persuade them, backlash follows. Terry rightly criticizes humiliation and social media vengeance. The same concern should make us cautious about romanticizing disruption.

This caution is especially important when the issue is economic inequality. Terry notes that King believed civil disobedience could address poverty as well as civil rights. Perhaps. But economic policy in a vast modern society involves tradeoffs that cannot be resolved by moral fervor alone. A guaranteed income, full employment program, housing subsidies, labor law reform, and health care expansion all require design, taxation, administration, and democratic consent. Mass protest can put issues on the agenda, but it cannot substitute for legislation, budgeting, and institutional competence.

King’s late economic views deserve attention, but we should not treat them as beyond dispute because he held them. A guaranteed income may reduce poverty; it may also weaken work incentives or prove fiscally unsustainable depending on design. A federal job guarantee may offer dignity and income; it may also create bureaucratic inefficiencies and political patronage. Stronger unions may lift wages; they may also protect insiders or burden struggling firms. These are not reasons to reject economic justice. They are reasons to treat policy as more than moral declaration.

Similarly, King’s critique of militarism must be engaged without assuming that his judgments settle every question of foreign policy. The Vietnam War was a disaster, and King’s moral courage in opposing it is now easier to see than it was in 1967. But the fact that America has misused power does not mean American power is always malign. In a world of authoritarian regimes, ethnic slaughter, terrorism, and nuclear danger, the United States cannot simply withdraw into innocence. Prudence, alliances, and sometimes force are necessary. King’s warning against militarism should humble policymakers; it should not become a reflexive anti-Americanism.

Where Terry’s essay speaks most powerfully to our moment is in its defense of character. He is right that King’s emphasis on love, courage, humility, and self-restraint should not be caricatured as respectability politics. Rights do not depend on virtue; criminals, addicts, and the irresponsible possess human dignity. But a free society cannot endure without civic virtues. Citizens must be able to hear criticism, tell the truth, restrain hatred, forgive, and deliberate with opponents. King’s insistence that one must not seek to humiliate the opponent is a rebuke to our entire political culture.

This may be the King most needed in 2018. Not the harmless dreamer, but also not the patron saint of permanent disruption. Rather, the King who combined moral urgency with civic seriousness. He condemned unjust laws but respected law enough to accept punishment. He attacked racism without denying the humanity of racists. He demanded equality while appealing to a shared moral order. He spoke prophetically but built organizations and negotiated practical gains.

Terry is right that conservatives have often misused King to oppose policies he might well have supported. It is especially dubious to quote one line from the “I Have a Dream” speech as though King’s hope for a society beyond racial stigma means indifference to entrenched racial disadvantage. But progressives can misuse King too, by turning him into a warrant for every radical ambition. The real King belongs neither to colorblind complacency nor to revolutionary romance.

The better lesson is more demanding: America is neither innocent nor irredeemable. Its ideals are neither fully realized nor empty. Protest is sometimes necessary, but persuasion and lawmaking remain indispensable. Racism is real and deep, but not all-explanatory. Economic inequality is a moral problem, but policy requires prudence. Anger can awaken, but hatred deforms. If we remember that King, we may recover not only a truer historical figure but a more usable democratic politics.
