"While Brandon Terry successfully rescues King from conservative sanitization, his attempt to retrieve King’s ethical framework of redemptive suffering and nonviolent love places an unfair and potentially ineffective moral burden on the oppressed."

Brandon Terry asks us to rescue King from canonization. I share the impulse. The state-approved King is intolerable: a man murdered while supporting striking sanitation workers has been remade into a mascot for patience; a critic of American militarism is quoted by politicians who vote for war budgets; an advocate of disruptive direct action is invoked against athletes who kneel during the national anthem. Terry is right that this King must be dismantled.

But I am less persuaded by the King Terry wants to retrieve. Or rather, I am persuaded that Terry has given us a more serious King, but not that this King can bear the political and ethical weight Terry places on him. The problem is not that King was insufficiently radical in the ordinary sense. He was more radical than American memory allows. The problem is that some of the deepest elements of King’s thought—especially his account of redemptive suffering, his ethic of love toward enemies, and his hope for interracial democratic transformation—remain more troubling than Terry acknowledges.

Terry knows the objections. He cites Kenneth Clark’s worry that it is “too much” to ask victims of cruelty to love those associated with their oppression. He notes Malcolm X’s accusation that King’s nonviolence benefited from the threat of violence by others. He recognizes that King’s Christianity and his gender politics are obstacles to contemporary embrace. Yet Terry ultimately reaffirms the ethical grandeur of King’s project: anger must be organized rather than indulged; hatred corrodes judgment; humiliation of opponents forecloses reconciliation; nonviolent love discloses new possibilities for living together.

This is a beautiful vision. It may also be an unfair demand.

The unfairness begins with the distribution of moral labor. King’s ethic asks the injured to perform extraordinary acts of discipline in the face of humiliation. They must absorb blows without returning them, dramatize suffering without being consumed by it, and keep open the possibility of the oppressor’s redemption. Even if we agree that such discipline can be politically powerful, we should ask why the burden of moral pedagogy falls so heavily on those already burdened by domination. White Americans have repeatedly required black suffering to become visible, orderly, and morally legible before recognizing even partial claims to justice. That requirement is itself part of the injustice.

Terry might respond that King did not impose a duty on every oppressed person to become a saint. He treated nonviolence as a political vocation and a movement discipline. Fair enough. But in practice, the line between strategy and moral expectation blurs. Once nonviolent suffering becomes the privileged language of democratic appeal, those who cannot or will not speak it are easily judged immature, irresponsible, or self-defeating. The grieving mother who screams, the teenager who throws a bottle at police, the prisoner who refuses respectability, the poor woman who is too exhausted to perform magnanimity—all become foils for the disciplined protester.

This is why the charge of respectability politics cannot be dismissed too quickly. Terry usefully distinguishes between proving fitness for citizenship and cultivating virtues necessary for struggle. But historically these have not remained neatly separated. Calls for self-command, cleanliness, chastity, thrift, and dignity have often functioned as internal policing within black communities, with women, queer people, the poor, the incarcerated, and the sexually nonconforming bearing the brunt. King’s own patriarchal assumptions were not incidental blemishes. They shaped what counted, for him and for many in his milieu, as moral seriousness.

Terry acknowledges sexism as a “fourth evil” that must be added to King’s triad. But adding sexism to racism, poverty, and militarism does not fully address the problem. King’s leadership model was masculinist not only because he held conventional views of family and gender, but because charisma itself was gendered. The movement depended on women’s organizing labor while elevating male preachers as prophetic voices. To retrieve King as a model of political judgment without making this structure central risks repeating the pattern: women’s labor as background, male moral genius as foreground.

There is another difficulty: King’s confidence that racism can be transformed through confrontation, truth, and love. Terry criticizes racial pessimists for making anti-blackness seem permanent, “a force of nature.” He is right that fatalism can become politically disabling. But optimism has dangers too. It can misrecognize repeated defeats as temporary delays on the road to redemption. It can turn black endurance into evidence of national possibility. It can make hope compulsory.

In 2018, after the election of Donald Trump, the resurgence of open white nationalism, the routine killing of black people by police, the gutting of voting protections, and the durable racial wealth gap, one need not believe anti-blackness is metaphysical to doubt the redemptive story. Perhaps American democracy contains a “democratic spirit,” as King thought. But it also contains a capacity to absorb revolt, commemorate it, and leave underlying hierarchies largely intact. The civil rights movement destroyed de jure segregation and won monumental legislative victories. Yet many black children attend segregated and underfunded schools; black neighborhoods remain vulnerable to disinvestment, predatory lending, environmental hazard, and police occupation; black political power remains subject to suppression; black wealth remains a fraction of white wealth. How many partial victories must coexist with structural reproduction before hope begins to look like a trap?

Terry would likely say that King’s hope was not naïve. He knew failure, betrayal, and death. He worried about white moderates, condemned capitalism’s brutality, and named America the world’s greatest purveyor of violence. That is true. But King’s hope was theological before it was empirical. It rested on a moral universe bending toward justice, even if human beings had to help bend it. For those who do not share that metaphysics, the question remains: why believe that love is more than a noble posture in a world where power so often honors only force?

The answer cannot simply be that nonviolence works. Sometimes it does; sometimes it does not. The civil rights movement’s victories depended on many conditions: black institutions, Cold War pressures, federal-state conflict, television, disciplined local organizing, economic boycotts, legal advocacy, electoral calculations, and yes, the looming possibility of urban rebellion. King’s nonviolent campaigns were part of an ecology of pressure, not a magic key. Terry recognizes this, but his admiration for King’s ethical architecture still risks overstating the independent power of agape.

Nor can the answer be that hatred corrupts the hater. Hatred can indeed narrow perception. But so can premature forgiveness. So can the demand to see the full humanity of those who refuse to see yours. So can reconciliation talk that arrives before repair. There are circumstances in which refusal, contempt, separatism, and withdrawal are not moral failures but forms of self-preservation. The oppressed may need spaces where they are not obligated to translate pain into terms acceptable to oppressors. They may need rage that is not immediately conscripted into coalition.

This does not mean celebrating nihilism. Terry is right that anger alone is not politics. Denunciation is not organization. Aestheticized despair can become a luxury, especially among elites who convert pessimism into cultural capital. But between Kingian love and nihilistic rage lies a wide field of political emotion: grief, suspicion, pride, solidarity, impatience, refusal, tenderness toward one’s own, and cold strategic hostility toward institutions that destroy life. Not all of these emotions aspire to reconciliation. Some aspire to survival.

The most valuable part of Terry’s essay, then, may not be its call to return to King but its insistence that King be argued with. A serious King is not one before whom contemporary activists must kneel. He is one whose categories can be tested against experiences he did not adequately understand: black feminism, queer politics, mass incarceration, the militarization of police, the decline of the black church, the fragmentation of media, and the exhaustion of appeals to national conscience.

If we are to retrieve anything from King, I would retrieve his willingness to revise under pressure. The late King was not the same as the early King. He became more radical because events taught him the inadequacy of earlier assumptions. He moved from appeals to conscience toward an analysis of power. He linked racism, poverty, and war because the world forced the connections upon him. A King faithful to that spirit might today listen more closely to those who reject redemptive suffering, who distrust charismatic male leadership, who refuse to love enemies on command, and who insist that the measure of justice is not interracial fellowship but material transformation and freedom from domination.

King may still be a profound interlocutor. But interlocutors do not get the last word. The danger of canonization is not only that conservatives make King harmless. It is also that admirers make him indispensable. We can honor King best by allowing the possibility that some of his most cherished beliefs must be left behind.
