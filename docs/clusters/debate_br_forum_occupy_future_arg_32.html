Diversified Gemini

"The lead essay’s optimistic historical framing of the Occupy movement ignores the unique and formidable structural barriers that make dramatic reform much harder to achieve today than in the past."

The editors of the Boston Review strike a hopeful, if somewhat deterministic, tone in their introduction to this forum. By aligning the Occupy movement with the triumphant narratives of abolition, women’s suffrage, and the civil rights movement, they imply that the current outrage over extreme inequality is the prologue to another chapter of inevitable, dramatic reform. As an observer deeply sympathetic to the frustrations animating Zuccotti Park and beyond, I fear this historical framing is dangerously optimistic and misreads the unique structural barriers of our current era.

To ask if we are entering "another moment in history" of dramatic reform requires us to look not only at past successes but at past failures. The more accurate historical parallel for Occupy Wall Street is not the civil rights movement of the 1960s, but the Populist movement of the 1890s. Like the occupiers today, the Populists faced an era of staggering Gilded Age inequality, corporate monopolies, and a political system entirely captured by the moneyed elite. They possessed immense grassroots energy and articulated a profound moral critique of capitalism. Yet, they ultimately failed to achieve their most transformative goals, outmaneuvered by entrenched power and absorbed into a political establishment that blunted their radicalism.

The barriers to dramatic reform today are arguably even more formidable than they were a century ago. The lead essay speaks of bringing our institutions into alignment with our principles, but it underestimates the sheer, unprecedented agility of globalized capital. Unlike the mid-20th century, when capital and labor were relatively bound within national borders—making it possible for the state to strike a grand bargain between them—today’s financial elites operate transnationally. If threatened with serious taxation or regulation, capital simply relocates. The "United States" as an enclosed entity capable of legislating a fair society is increasingly an anachronism.

Furthermore, the nature of work and class solidarity has fundamentally fractured. The industrial working class that provided the mass base for the New Deal has been decimated by automation and outsourcing. We are now a service and knowledge economy, marked by precarious, gig-based, and non-unionized labor. This makes organizing a sustained, powerful movement vastly more difficult than it was in the days of massive factory floors.

Finally, the ideological hegemony of neoliberalism remains profoundly resilient. Even in the wake of the 2008 crash—a crisis that should have thoroughly discredited the ideology of unfettered markets—the political response in Washington quickly reverted to a bipartisan consensus on deficit reduction and austerity. The very fact that Occupy had to invent the term "the 99%" to even introduce class consciousness back into the American vernacular shows how far we have fallen.

I hope the editors are right. I hope we are on the precipice of a new Progressive Era. But the energy of Occupy, while inspiring, lacks the institutional anchors—robust unions, a cohesive political party, a unified media ecosystem—that made past reforms possible. Acknowledging these severe structural headwinds is not defeatism; it is the necessary first step to building a movement that can actually survive the long, bitter winter ahead.

  1. 01The Populist movement of the 1890s serves as a more accurate historical parallel than the civil rights movement, demonstrating how grassroots energy can be outmaneuvered and absorbed by entrenched power.
  2. 02The unprecedented agility of globalized capital allows financial elites to evade national regulation and taxation, undermining the state's ability to legislate a fairer society.
  3. 03The shift to a service and knowledge economy has fractured class solidarity and decimated the industrial labor base necessary for sustained mass organizing.
  4. 04The resilient ideological hegemony of neoliberalism has maintained a political consensus on austerity even after the 2008 financial crisis.
  5. 05The Occupy movement lacks the essential institutional anchors, such as robust unions and a cohesive political party, that were required to secure past historical reforms.
