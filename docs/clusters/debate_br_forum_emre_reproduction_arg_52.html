Diversified Gemini

"While Merve Emre correctly identifies the exclusionary nature of 'natural' reproduction, her focus on individual access to assisted reproductive technology (ART) overlooks the systemic failure of society to support the labor of care."

In “All Reproduction Is Assisted,” Merve Emre provides a compelling and intimately reported look at the ways assisted reproductive technology (ART) intersects with the lives of women and queer people. By centering the narratives of individuals like S, B, and the couples N & K and Joanne & Lara, Emre illustrates how the biological imperative persists even when the traditional tools of hetero-normative reproduction are unavailable or undesirable. Emre concludes by suggesting that a radical embrace of ART could forge solidarity among women and marginalized groups, pushing past the exclusionary rhetoric of the "natural." However, by framing her argument almost entirely around the individual quest for biological offspring, Emre misses the broader, systemic crisis that makes ART so fraught in the first place: the total collapse of societal support for the labor of care.

Emre treats the desire for a biological child as a "fantasy" that is "fundamentally irrational," an a priori condition that we must simply accept and accommodate through technological means. But desires are not formed in a vacuum; they are shaped by the material conditions of the society we live in. Why are the women in Emre’s essay willing to endure such extreme financial, physical, and emotional costs to have a child? The answer lies not just in a mystical biological urge, but in the isolation of modern life, where the nuclear family is often the only sanctioned site for deep, enduring connection and care.

Consider S, the tech worker who freezes her eggs. Emre rightly pushes back against critics who frame S’s choice as anti-feminist, pointing out that egg freezing is a rational response to corporate demands. But what is truly dystopian about S’s situation is not the technology itself, but the timeline it is designed to manage. We live in an economy that demands total devotion during the exact decades when human bodies are most fertile. The solution offered by capital—and implicitly endorsed by the tech companies paying the $20,000 bill—is not to change the structure of work, but to pause the biological clock. This is an individualized, technological fix for a systemic, economic problem.

Similarly, B, the single mother by choice, is terrified of giving birth before she has worked at her new job for twelve months, as she would not qualify for paid maternity leave. Her reproductive timeline is completely hostage to the meager, employer-contingent benefits of the American labor market. Emre notes this as a constraint, but she doesn't position it as the core tragedy it is. If the United States had universal paid family leave, universal healthcare, and subsidized childcare, B’s journey would not be an agonizing race against the clock and her bank account.

Emre quotes Shulamith Firestone’s techno-utopian vision of artificial wombs freeing women from the "grinding work of pregnancy." But Firestone recognized something that the modern discourse around ART often forgets: technology alone does not liberate. The artificial womb is useless as a tool of liberation if the person who takes the baby out of the incubator is still forced to raise it in isolation, bearing the entire financial and emotional cost of social reproduction while working a forty-hour week.

This brings us to the core limitation of Emre’s essay. She brilliantly dismantles the "natural" as a lifestyle aesthetic, but she replaces it with a politics of access to the clinic. Emre writes, "all reproduction, even reproduction that appears ‘natural,’ is assisted," noting that if you have money, you don't realize conception can be costly. But the true assistance required for reproduction is not just the syringe of sperm or the petri dish; it is the village.

For decades, feminists of color, particularly Black feminists, have articulated a vision of reproductive justice that goes far beyond access to medical technology. While Emre cites Dorothy Roberts, she narrows Roberts’s expansive vision to fit the context of ART. True reproductive justice requires the transformation of society so that raising a child is no longer a private burden, but a collective responsibility. It requires affordable housing, a living wage, environmental justice, and the dismantling of the carceral state.

When we focus our political imagination solely on the right to access IVF and egg freezing, we accept the neoliberal premise that children are private luxury goods, and that the only injustice is that some people are priced out of the market. But the true injustice is that our society refuses to value the labor of caremaking unless it is tied to biological ownership.

If we want to build the solidarity Emre imagines, we must look beyond the fertility clinic. We must demand a society where B doesn't have to fear bankruptcy to have a child, where S doesn't have to freeze her eggs to prove her commitment to her job, and where non-biological forms of kinship and care are materially supported and culturally revered. Technology can help us conceive, but it cannot help us care. That requires a political revolution, not just a medical one.

  2. 02Corporate-sponsored egg freezing is an individualized technological fix that allows capital to demand total devotion from workers rather than restructuring work to accommodate human fertility.
