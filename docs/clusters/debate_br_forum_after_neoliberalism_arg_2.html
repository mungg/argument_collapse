Diversified GPT

"While the lead essay correctly identifies the failures of neoliberalism, a truly inclusive economic framework must move beyond a Western-centric perspective to address the specific structural, developmental, and institutional needs of the global South."

The lead essay is written primarily from the vantage point of the United States and other rich democracies. Its concerns—inequality, wage stagnation, financial instability, trade shocks, tax avoidance, and the decline of labor power—are urgent. But any economics after neoliberalism must also be judged from the perspective of the developing world. For many countries in Africa, Asia, and Latin America, the question is not only how to make prosperity inclusive after markets have produced wealth. It is how to achieve structural transformation in a global economy whose rules often constrain national development.

The authors are well aware that there is no single institutional path to growth. Rodrik’s own work has emphasized policy space, experimentation, and the dangers of one-size-fits-all globalization. The lead essay’s discussion of China is a useful reminder that property rights can take many forms. China did not grow by adopting a textbook model of private property and laissez-faire. It combined markets with state ownership, local experimentation, export discipline, industrial policy, and gradual reform. Whatever one thinks of its political system, its economic rise refutes the idea that development requires a standardized neoliberal template.

This lesson matters because neoliberalism in the developing world was often experienced not as an intellectual fashion but as conditionality. Structural adjustment programs pushed countries to cut public spending, privatize state enterprises, liberalize capital accounts, reduce tariffs, and focus on macroeconomic stabilization. Some reforms were necessary in countries suffering from inflation, corruption, or inefficient state control. But the package often went too far, too fast, with too little attention to history, institutions, and distribution. Public capacity was weakened. Industrial policy was stigmatized. Social spending suffered. Domestic firms were exposed to competition before they were ready. Capital flow volatility produced crises.

The lead essay argues that contemporary economics is richer and more empirical than market fundamentalism. That is true, but global economic governance has not fully absorbed the lesson. Trade agreements, investment treaties, intellectual property rules, and financial norms still limit the ability of developing countries to use tools that rich countries used in their own development. The United States, Germany, Japan, South Korea, and Taiwan all relied at various points on tariffs, subsidies, public banks, procurement, technology policy, and coordination between states and firms. Yet poorer countries are often told that such measures are inefficient or illegal.

An inclusive prosperity agenda must defend policy space for development. This does not mean endorsing every protectionist demand or state subsidy. Many industrial policies fail. They can be captured by elites, protect inefficient firms, and burden consumers. But the conclusion should be disciplined experimentation, not prohibition. Developing countries need room to discover comparative advantage, build capabilities, and move workers from low-productivity to higher-productivity activities.

The trade debate looks different from this angle. In rich countries, trade restrictions are often discussed as tools to protect workers from import competition. In poor countries, trade policy can be a tool of industrialization. The challenge is to design rules that distinguish socially useful development policy from pure rent-seeking. That is difficult, but the current system already makes distinctions—often in favor of intellectual property holders, investors, and multinational corporations. It is not neutral.

Taxation is another crucial area. Zucman’s proposal to tax multinationals by allocating profits according to sales could help rich countries recover revenue from tax havens. But developing countries may need a voice in how such formulas are designed. If profit allocation is based heavily on sales, large consumer markets benefit. Poor countries that host production but have smaller consumer bases may receive less. A fair system must consider employment, assets, users, and production, not only sales. The broader point is right: multinational tax avoidance drains public capacity. But the solution must not be written solely by and for rich countries.

Capital flows deserve similar attention. The old neoliberal view favored financial liberalization, but experience has shown that volatile capital flows can destabilize developing economies. The Asian financial crisis, Latin American crises, and repeated emerging-market turmoil demonstrated that capital account openness can produce vulnerability without delivering sustained investment. Economists have become more accepting of capital controls and macroprudential tools, but international norms still often privilege investor mobility. A post-neoliberal order should recognize the legitimacy of managing capital flows.

Migration is a striking absence in the lead essay. From a global welfare perspective, restrictions on labor mobility are among the most important economic barriers in the world. Workers can multiply their earnings by moving from poor countries to rich ones. Remittances support families and communities. Yet rich democracies are experiencing nativist backlash, and the authors understandably worry about the social contract. The challenge is to defend migrant rights while sustaining political support for welfare states. This requires labor standards that prevent exploitation and undercutting, paths to legal status, international cooperation, and public investment in receiving communities. It also requires acknowledging that borders create a massive inequality of opportunity.

Climate change is perhaps the greatest development issue. Rich countries industrialized by emitting carbon. Poor countries now face pressure to develop without following the same path, even though many still lack reliable electricity, transportation, and industrial employment. A fair climate policy must include technology transfer, climate finance, and green industrialization. Carbon pricing alone will not solve this. Developing countries need investment in grids, transit, adaptation, and resilient agriculture. They also need relief from the idea that ecological responsibility means permanent poverty.

The authors’ emphasis on institutions is useful here. Institutions cannot simply be imported. The best labor market system for Denmark may not work in India. Social insurance in a country with high informal employment requires different mechanisms than in a country with formal payroll systems. Tax capacity varies. State capacity varies. Trust varies. Economists should avoid the old habit of ranking countries by how closely they approximate an Anglo-American model. The right question is: what institutional arrangement can expand capabilities, reduce domination, and support transformation in this context?

This also means listening to economists and policymakers from the global South. The lead essay’s network consists of tenured academic economists, presumably largely based in elite institutions. Their expertise is real, but the conversation after neoliberalism must be more geographically plural. Latin American structuralists, African development economists, Indian public finance scholars, feminist economists, and practitioners of industrial policy have long challenged market fundamentalism. Many did so when mainstream economics was less receptive. They should not be treated as late additions to a debate now certified by Northern academia.

Finally, inclusive prosperity must confront the hierarchy built into the global monetary and financial system. The United States issues the dominant reserve currency and borrows on terms unavailable to most countries. Poor countries face balance-of-payments constraints, currency mismatch, and pressure from ratings agencies. Policy advice that is sensible for the United States—large fiscal expansion, for example—may be harder for countries that borrow in foreign currency. A global post-neoliberalism must include reforms to debt restructuring, development finance, and emergency liquidity.

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman are right that economics need not be neoliberal. But after neoliberalism, economics must also cease to be provincial. It must recognize that the rules of the global economy distribute power between nations as well as within them. The policy space demanded for American workers should also be demanded for developing countries. The fiscal capacity sought for rich democracies should also be defended for poor states. The right to choose institutions should not be reserved for those already wealthy.

Inclusive prosperity, if the phrase is to mean what it says, must be global.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Global economic governance must be reformed to grant developing nations the same 'policy space' for industrial experimentation and state intervention that wealthy nations used during their own development.
  2. 02International tax and capital flow reforms must be designed to protect the fiscal capacity and financial stability of poorer nations rather than solely benefiting large consumer markets or mobile investors.
  3. 03A post-neoliberal agenda must address global labor mobility and migration as primary drivers of international inequality and opportunity.
  4. 04Climate change policy must move beyond carbon pricing to include technology transfer and green industrialization support so that ecological responsibility does not mandate permanent poverty for developing states.
  5. 05The movement to reform economics must become geographically plural by incorporating the long-standing insights of scholars and practitioners from the global South who have historically challenged market fundamentalism.
  6. 06Inclusive prosperity requires restructuring the global financial hierarchy to provide developing countries with better debt relief, liquidity, and fiscal autonomy.

"While the Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) project offers valuable reforms for wealthy democracies, it remains a limited 'Northern' project that fails to address the historical failures of neoliberalism in the developing world or the specific needs of the global South."

The View from the South

The EfIP manifesto is a document of and for the rich world, and especially the United States. Its diagnosis is American (rising U.S. inequality, U.S. life expectancy, U.S. opioid deaths), its proposals are mostly designed for U.S. or rich-world implementation, and its political horizon is the recovery of a humane social settlement in the wealthy democracies. This is fine as far as it goes. I want to register, from a developing country perspective, what is missing.

The first observation is that "neoliberalism" looks different from where I sit. The authors describe it as a domestic policy regime—deregulation, financialization, welfare cuts, anti-labor reforms—that produced inequality and discontent. From the developing world the more salient face of neoliberalism is structural adjustment: the package of policies imposed on indebted countries by the IMF and World Bank from the early 1980s through the 1990s, which included rapid trade liberalization, capital account opening, fiscal austerity, privatization of state enterprises, and the dismantling of import-substitution industrialization. These policies were not, on balance, successful. Countries that implemented them most thoroughly often grew most slowly. Countries that resisted them—China, India, much of East Asia—grew faster by following heterodox strategies. The lesson here is straightforward: the neoliberal model failed development as a project of structural transformation, and the cost was borne by poor people in poor countries.

The EfIP project does not address this history. The international economic order it implicitly accepts—global trade rules, capital mobility, the international monetary architecture—is the same one that produced structural adjustment. Reforms are proposed at the margins (Zucman on corporate taxation, Rodrik on social safeguards) but the basic shape is not questioned. The poor world is asked to take on faith that a more equitable rich world will translate into a more equitable global order. The historical record gives little support to this faith.

The second observation is that the politics of inclusive prosperity look different when "inclusion" is understood globally. The authors are concerned, properly, about the working class in the United States. But the global working class—billions of people in low and middle income countries—has been the principal beneficiary of the trade integration the authors are now equivocal about. Rodrik's social safeguards, well-intentioned, threaten this. If rich-country publics can block imports whenever they feel their social contract is threatened, the result is likely to be a regression to a more closed global economy in which the poor of poor countries pay for the social peace of the rich. There is a genuine ethical trade-off here that the authors gesture at but do not really grapple with.

The third observation is about development as a topic. Development economics has been one of the most empirically active subfields, with the RCT revolution and the work of Banerjee, Duflo, and others getting Nobel-level recognition. But the EfIP project does not treat development as central. The proposals address labor markets, taxation, finance, and so on in the rich world. The corresponding questions for the developing world—how to industrialize in the twenty-first century, how to escape the middle-income trap, how to build state capacity, how to manage commodity dependence, how to navigate the relationship with China—are not on the agenda. This is a significant omission given that most of humanity lives in developing countries and that the global political economy is being reshaped by their trajectories.

The fourth observation is about climate, which I want to register briefly because others have addressed it more fully. Climate justice has a strong distributional dimension: the rich world has emitted most of the historical carbon and the poor world will bear most of the damage. Any serious framework for inclusive prosperity must include large transfers from rich to poor countries to finance adaptation and clean development. The EfIP project does not mention this.

I want to be fair. The authors are American economists writing for a primarily American audience about American problems. They are not obligated to solve every problem in the world in one essay. The work they are doing is valuable and the proposals they put forward would, if implemented, make the United States a more humane country. I support this work.

But I want to register that "Economics After Neoliberalism" as currently framed remains a recognizably Northern project, organized around Northern concerns, and inattentive to the ways in which Northern policy reverberates through the rest of the world. The intellectual tradition the authors come from—mainstream economics, with its universal claims and its mathematical apparatus—has a poor record of taking seriously the experience and analysis of economists from the global South. The structuralists, the dependency theorists, the heterodox development economists who worked on these questions in the postwar period have been largely written out of the discipline's memory. The EfIP project, in reaching back for an alternative to neoliberalism, could have drawn on this tradition. It does not.

There are signs of change. Justin Lin's new structural economics, Ha-Joon Chang's institutional development economics, the work of Mariana Mazzucato on the entrepreneurial state—all of these point toward a more pluralist economics that takes development seriously. The EfIP project could and should engage with these voices in future iterations. The poor world has been a laboratory for economic ideas for a century, often with great human costs. It has also produced thinkers who have much to teach economists in the rich world about the limits of the universal market model. A truly post-neoliberal economics will require their participation, not as junior partners but as colleagues whose intellectual traditions are taken seriously on their own terms. We are still some distance from this. The EfIP project, for all its merits, is one step on a longer road.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The EfIP manifesto defines neoliberalism as a domestic policy issue, ignoring the destructive legacy of structural adjustment programs imposed on developing nations.
  2. 02The project's focus on protecting the rich-world working class creates an ethical trade-off by potentially harming the global working class through protectionist trade safeguards.
  3. 03The manifesto neglects critical development challenges such as industrialization, state capacity, and commodity dependence that affect the majority of the global population.
  4. 04A framework for inclusive prosperity is incomplete without addressing climate justice through significant resource transfers from high-emitting rich nations to the global South.
  5. 05To truly move past neoliberalism, the discipline must move beyond its insular Western perspective and integrate heterodox economic traditions and voices from the global South.
Diversified Gemini

"While the 'Economics for Inclusive Prosperity' (EfIP) framework aims to move beyond neoliberalism, its policy proposals are fundamentally Eurocentric and risk harming the economic development of the Global South."

In "Economics After Neoliberalism," Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman articulate a vision for saving economics from its association with market fundamentalism. They seek a policy framework for "inclusive prosperity" that addresses the staggering inequality of the contemporary era. It is a noble project, but it is one that seems to stop at the borders of the developed world. Read from the perspective of the Global South, the EfIP agenda looks less like a universal blueprint for prosperity and more like the Global North pulling up the ladder of development.

The authors note that global inequality is a problem, but their proposed solutions are overwhelmingly tailored to the domestic political grievances of the United States and Europe. This is most glaring in Dani Rodrik’s proposal for "social safeguards" in trade agreements. Rodrik argues that advanced economies should have the right to restrict trade to prevent competition on "unjust" margins—such as weaker labor or environmental regulations in exporting countries—to protect the "domestic social contract."

For a developing nation attempting to export its way out of poverty, this sounds like a sophisticated justification for rich-country protectionism. The primary comparative advantage that many developing countries possess is precisely their lower labor costs. If the United States and the EU are empowered to slap tariffs on manufactured goods from Southeast Asia or Sub-Saharan Africa because those nations have not yet achieved the labor standards of a post-industrial social democracy, it will devastate the only proven pathway to rapid economic development. The "China Shock" may have disrupted communities in the US Midwest, but it also lifted hundreds of millions of Chinese citizens out of agrarian destitution. "Inclusive prosperity" must include them, too.

Similarly, consider Gabriel Zucman’s proposal to allocate the global profits of multinationals based on where they make their sales. While this elegantly prevents companies from booking profits in Caribbean tax havens, it systematically biases tax revenue toward rich countries with massive consumer bases. A developing country that hosts the manufacturing plants or mines the raw materials for a tech giant, but represents only a tiny fraction of its global consumer sales, would lose out on the right to tax the bulk of those corporate profits. The wealth would be redistributed, but it would be redistributed back to the capitals of the Global North.

The authors rightly criticize the IMF-style neoliberalism that forced fiscal austerity and rapid financial liberalization on the Global South, often leading to devastating crises (as they acknowledge). But their proposed alternatives are remarkably Eurocentric. They cite Arindrajit Dube’s wage boards, based on the Australian model, and point to the need to learn from the welfare states of the developed world.

But what does this mean for countries with massive informal economies where wage boards are impossible to enforce? What does Anton Korinek’s proposal to "steer AI" mean for countries whose entire development strategy is based on labor-intensive manufacturing that AI threatens to render obsolete?

The EfIP network claims that their higher-order principles are compatible with an "almost infinite variety of institutional arrangements." But the actual policy briefs they highlight suggest a very specific political economy: a defensive posture by wealthy democracies trying to shield their domestic institutions from the forces of global convergence.

If economics is to move beyond neoliberalism in a way that is truly inclusive, it must view the global economy from the perspective of the periphery, not just the core. A genuinely inclusive agenda would focus on technology transfer, debt jubilee for developing nations, reforming the WTO to allow for Southern industrial policy, and massive climate reparations from the historical emitters. Until the EfIP economists grapple with the development imperatives of the Global South, their "inclusive prosperity" is just nationalism with better math.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The proposal for 'social safeguards' in trade agreements acts as a sophisticated form of protectionism that would strip developing nations of their comparative advantage in labor costs.
  2. 02Redistributing corporate tax revenue based on sales location would unfairly divert wealth from manufacturing and resource-rich developing nations to large consumer markets in the Global North.
  3. 03The EfIP agenda focuses on institutional models, such as wage boards and AI steering, that are inapplicable or detrimental to countries with large informal economies or labor-intensive development strategies.
  4. 04A truly inclusive economic vision must prioritize global issues like technology transfer, debt relief, and climate reparations rather than focusing on shielding the domestic social contracts of wealthy democracies.
