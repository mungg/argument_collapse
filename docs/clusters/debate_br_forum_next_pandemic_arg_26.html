"The failure of the COVID-19 response was a catastrophic breakdown of political implementation rather than an epistemic failure of scientific prediction, meaning we should strengthen our preparedness tools rather than abandon them for 'technologies of humility.'"

In “Public Policy after Pandemic,” Sheila Jasanoff offers a sweeping critique of the technocratic mindset that dominated global public health prior to the arrival of COVID-19. She argues that the institutional architectures of preparedness—embodied in tools like the Global Health Security Index (GHSI) and the pandemic playbooks of successive administrations—failed because they were rooted in the hubris of prediction. As an alternative, she proposes a pivot toward precaution and “technologies of humility.” While Jasanoff is undeniably correct that the United States and the world fell tragically short in managing this crisis, her diagnosis misidentifies the disease. The failure we witnessed was not an epistemic failure of scientific prediction or preparedness. It was a massive, catastrophic failure of political implementation. By conflating the two, Jasanoff risks discarding the very tools that remain our only realistic defense against biological threats.

To understand why the critique of prediction misses the mark, we must remember what actually happened in the early months of 2020. The scientific apparatus of prediction did not fail; in fact, it performed with astonishing speed and accuracy. The genome of SARS-CoV-2 was sequenced and shared globally by early January. By late February, epidemiological models from institutions like Imperial College London accurately predicted the explosive exponential growth of the virus and the catastrophic toll it would take on human life if unmitigated. The predictive blueprints were correct. The warning lights on the dashboard, to borrow the playbook’s metaphor, were flashing a deep, unmistakable red.

The problem was that the people sitting in the driver’s seat simply ignored the dashboard. The Trump administration actively dismantled the pandemic response structures that had been left in place, downplayed the severity of the virus for political gain, and abdicated the federal government’s responsibility to coordinate a national response. The GHSI and the Obama-era playbook were not flawed because they relied on measurable indicators of lab capacity and hospital beds; they failed to save us because a blueprint cannot build a house if the contractor refuses to purchase the materials and fires the carpenters. You do not blame the architectural schematics when a building collapses due to willful vandalism.

Jasanoff singles out Harvey Fineberg’s April 2020 call for a wartime mobilization to “crush the curve” as a symptom of a militaristic, overconfident mindset that ignores social and political realities. She notes that Fineberg’s vision of a unified command and a compliant public did not map onto the chaotic, polarized reality of the United States. This is a fair empirical observation, but it is a strange basis for a normative critique of public health strategy. When an infectious pathogen is spreading exponentially, military-style logistics and centralized coordination are not just nice-to-have bureaucratic fantasies; they are absolute mathematical necessities. The fact that the United States was too politically fractured to execute such a mobilization is a tragic indictment of our civic culture, but it does not mean Fineberg’s prescription was wrong in principle. In countries that did manage to execute centralized, highly coordinated responses early on—such as South Korea, Taiwan, and New Zealand—the death tolls were kept vanishingly low for over a year.

Jasanoff asks us to abandon this “can-do” preparedness posture in favor of “technologies of humility,” which she defines as institutional mechanisms for incorporating memory, experience, and greater citizen participation. In the abstract, this sounds deeply ethical and profoundly democratic. But what does it actually mean in the crucible of an unfolding biological emergency? When emergency rooms are overflowing, when supply chains for personal protective equipment are collapsing, and when a novel virus is doubling its case count every three days, there is no time for the slow, deliberative processes of civic consultation.

Public health officials do not have the luxury of perfectly mapping out the historical grievances of every marginalized community before issuing a masking guideline or a stay-at-home advisory. They must act on imperfect information to stop the bleeding. The insistence on front-loaded humility and endless stakeholder participation during a crisis is a recipe for a veto-cracy. It transforms the necessary speed of crisis management into a paralyzing sludge of endless ethical deliberation. If we had waited to achieve a perfect, historically informed consensus on the rollout of vaccines, millions more would have died.

And it is precisely on the topic of vaccines that the preparedness paradigm proves its absolute necessity. The rapid development of mRNA vaccines was the crowning achievement of the pandemic, and it was entirely the product of predictive science and massive, centralized, “hubristic” preparedness. Operation Warp Speed did not rely on “technologies of humility”; it relied on staggering amounts of capital, predictive biomedical engineering, and military-style logistical distribution networks. If we had adopted a purely precautionary mindset, paralyzed by the unknown unknowns of a novel vaccine technology, we would still be locked in our homes today. It was the bold, unheroic, calculative power of science that provided our way out.

As we look toward the winter of 2021, the emergence of the Omicron variant underscores exactly why we need more predictive capacity, not less. We need better genomic surveillance to track its spread. We need rapid, centralized mechanisms to update our vaccines. We need robust data to predict its evasion of neutralizing antibodies. None of these challenges can be solved by reflecting on the limits of our knowledge or cultivating a stance of self-abnegation. They require hard data, rapid calculation, and decisive action.

Jasanoff is entirely correct that the pandemic revealed deep, festering inequities in American society, and that our public health institutions must do a better job of communicating with and protecting those at the margins. But the answer to these political and social failures is to fix our politics and repair our social safety net, not to abandon the scientific paradigm of preparedness. We must not allow the failures of politicians to shatter our faith in the indispensable tools of public health. To survive the next pandemic, we do not just need to be humble; we need to be ready.
