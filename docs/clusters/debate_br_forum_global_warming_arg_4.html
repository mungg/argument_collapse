"Aggressive near-term climate mitigation is a flawed insurance policy because it fails cost-benefit analysis and unfairly burdens the poor, whereas a more effective strategy focuses on economic growth, R&D, and adaptation."

The Wrong Insurance Policy

Kerry Emanuel ends his essay on a note that has become a kind of consensus refrain among scientifically literate commentators: even if we are unsure about the magnitude of climate damages, we ought to "make sacrifices as an insurance policy" against bad surprises. It is a reasonable-sounding sentiment, and it is the place where I want to push back hardest. Not because I doubt the basic atmospheric physics Emanuel describes—I do not—but because the insurance metaphor, applied carelessly, leads to bad policy. Insurance is something you buy when the expected payout, properly discounted, justifies the premium. We rarely insure against every conceivable contingency, and when we do, we have made ourselves poorer for no good reason.

Begin with Emanuel's own numbers. He projects warming of three to seven degrees Fahrenheit over the next century, an amount he himself compares to the climate difference between Boston and Philadelphia. He acknowledges, with admirable honesty, that some effects will be beneficial—lower heating costs, longer growing seasons at high latitudes, CO2 fertilization of crops—even as others will be harmful. He flags hurricanes (his specialty), accelerated hydrological cycling, and the possibility of ice sheet instability. What he does not do, and what climate scientists in general have been remarkably reluctant to do, is put the harms and the benefits onto a common scale so we can ask whether the proposed cure is worth its cost.

When economists have attempted that exercise—William Nordhaus most prominently, but also Richard Tol, Robert Mendelsohn, and others—the results are sobering in the opposite direction from what most readers expect. The net economic damages of a doubling of atmospheric CO2 over a century, expressed as a fraction of global GDP at the end of that century, are typically estimated at one to three percent. That is not a trivial number; it is, in absolute terms, very large. But the global economy in 2100 will, on any plausible growth path, be several times richer than today. A world that is, say, four times wealthier minus two percent is still about 3.9 times wealthier. To prevent that two-percent shortfall, we are being asked to consider transformations of the global energy system that, in the most aggressive proposals (Stern Review, released just months ago, comes to mind), would cost one to two percent of GDP indefinitely, starting now—when the world is poorer and the marginal utility of consumption higher.

This is not climate denial. It is a request that we apply to climate policy the same kind of cost-benefit reasoning we apply to highway safety, drug approvals, and pollution regulations of every other kind. The Stern Review reached its alarming conclusions only by adopting a near-zero pure rate of time preference, effectively treating the welfare of someone living in 2200 as equally weighty as someone living today. Nordhaus has shown that with conventional discount rates—the kind we actually use to evaluate, say, transportation infrastructure—the optimal carbon tax rises gradually over decades rather than spiking now. The "climate ramp" he advocates does not abandon mitigation; it sequences it sensibly.

Emanuel hints at, but does not engage with, the deepest problem with aggressive near-term mitigation: it is regressive across both time and space. Across time, because we are being asked to make people who are, on average, much poorer than their grandchildren will be, sacrifice consumption so that those richer descendants will be marginally richer still. Across space, because the marginal ton of carbon will, for the foreseeable future, be emitted in China, India, and other developing economies where energy is the precondition of escape from poverty. Tell the household in Bihar that just got its first lightbulb that the coal plant which powers it must close. The hurricane risk that worries Emanuel most—Bangladesh in 1970, Mitch in 1998—falls precisely on populations whose vulnerability is principally a function of poverty. Wealthy Floridians ride out Category 5 storms; impoverished Hondurans do not. The fastest way to reduce the human toll of hurricanes is to make poor countries less poor, which requires energy, which for the next several decades will mostly mean fossil fuels.

What about the catastrophic scenarios—the ice sheet collapse, the sudden climate jump that Emanuel rightly says we do not understand? Here I think the honest answer is that conventional cost-benefit analysis breaks down, and we have to think differently. But "differently" does not mean "spend whatever it takes." It means investing seriously in the things that hedge against the tail. Three priorities suggest themselves.

First, research and development. The most underfunded climate policy in the world today is basic energy R&D. Federal spending on energy research, in real terms, is a fraction of what it was after the oil shocks of the 1970s. The reason nuclear power, the only proven scalable low-carbon baseload technology, costs what it does is not physics; it is regulation and the absence of standardized designs. The reason solar photovoltaics cost what they do is that we have not yet pushed the learning curve far enough. A few tens of billions of dollars per year in public R&D, globally coordinated, would do more to bend the long-term emissions trajectory than any number of Kyoto-style emissions caps that wealthy countries will ratify and then quietly miss.

Second, adaptation. Sea walls, improved building codes, better hurricane forecasting (Emanuel's own field has contributed enormously here), drought-tolerant crops, and—above all—the economic growth that allows societies to redeploy resources in response to changing conditions. The Dutch have lived below sea level for centuries; Bangladesh can learn to do the same, if we let it become rich enough. Adaptation has the considerable advantage that its benefits accrue to those who pay for it, sidestepping the international free-rider problem that has bedeviled mitigation efforts since Rio.

Third, research into geoengineering. This is taboo, and I understand why. The prospect of deliberately injecting sulfate aerosols into the stratosphere, or fertilizing the oceans with iron, raises governance nightmares. But if we are genuinely worried about a sudden climate jump—the scenario Emanuel says he is "worried" about—we should want to know whether emergency interventions are possible, what their side effects would be, and how quickly they could be deployed. To rule out the research because we find the prospect distasteful is to insist on insurance while refusing to read the policy.

Emanuel's essay closes a circle that has become familiar: science is settled enough to warrant action, action means mitigation, mitigation means emissions caps, emissions caps mean Kyoto or its successor. Each step in this chain is contestable, and the science section, however solid, does not by itself justify any of them. I take the IPCC findings seriously. I take Emanuel's hurricane work seriously. What I do not take seriously is the claim that this body of evidence translates into an obvious policy prescription. A world that grows rapidly, invests heavily in energy innovation, prices carbon modestly and rising, and adapts pragmatically to the changes that come anyway will, I suspect, end the twenty-first century better off than a world that flagellates itself into stagnation now in pursuit of stabilization targets it cannot meet and may not need.

That is also, incidentally, the only kind of climate policy that has any chance of being adopted. Voters in democracies will not accept large reductions in living standards on the basis of model projections about 2100, and they should not be asked to. The countries that emit most of the future's carbon are not democracies and will not be coerced. Any climate policy that is not compatible with continued economic growth, in the developed world and—especially—in the developing world, is not a policy. It is a moral posture. We can afford a number of those, but we should not confuse them with the work of actually reducing human suffering, which is what an honest accounting of the climate problem ought finally to be about.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Economic models suggest that the net damages of global warming are relatively small compared to the projected growth of the global economy, making expensive immediate mitigation efforts economically inefficient.
  2. 02Aggressive near-term emissions cuts are regressive because they ask today's poorer populations to sacrifice for the benefit of much wealthier future generations.
  3. 03Prioritizing rapid economic development in poor countries is the most effective way to reduce vulnerability to climate-related disasters like hurricanes.
  4. 04Public investment in basic energy research and development is a more effective way to lower the long-term costs of carbon-free energy than setting emissions caps.
  5. 05Adaptation measures, such as sea walls and improved building codes, provide direct benefits to local populations and bypass the international cooperation problems inherent in mitigation.
  6. 06Research into geoengineering is a necessary hedge against the catastrophic 'tail risks' and sudden climate jumps that conventional policy cannot address.
  7. 07Climate policies that are incompatible with continued economic growth are politically unfeasible and will not be adopted by democratic voters or developing nations.
