"Public policy after the pandemic should not abandon preparedness for humility, but rather integrate the two into a 'humble preparedness' that builds uncertainty, social equity, and democratic legitimacy into the core of institutional planning."

Sheila Jasanoff is right that COVID-19 exposed the poverty of a purely technocratic imagination. The countries that looked best on paper did not necessarily protect their people best in practice, and the gap cannot be explained by virology alone. A pandemic is not simply a pathogen moving through bodies; it is a pathogen moving through workplaces, prisons, nursing homes, schools, households, media ecosystems, racial hierarchies, and political institutions. Any preparedness plan that treats those settings as background conditions rather than central mechanisms of transmission is bound to fail.

But I worry that contrasting preparedness with humility risks giving preparedness too little credit and humility too much work to do. The problem was not that governments prepared; it was that they prepared for the wrong things, in the wrong institutions, with too little attention to who would actually have to comply, sacrifice, trust, and bear the consequences. A more humble public policy after pandemic should not retreat from preparedness. It should remake preparedness so that uncertainty, inequality, and democratic legitimacy are built into it from the beginning.

The evidence is already visible in the pandemic record. South Korea, Taiwan, and Singapore were not successful early on because they disdained preparedness. They were successful because prior experience with SARS and MERS had left them with practical systems: testing capacity, contact tracing, border protocols, public communication routines, and legal authority that could be mobilized quickly. New Zealand’s elimination strategy likewise required decisive planning, border controls, income supports, and public trust. These cases do not prove that command-and-control models are sufficient; indeed, each raised serious questions about privacy, exclusion, and the burdens of isolation. But they do show that humility without capacity is not enough. One cannot deliberate one’s way into masks, tests, ventilation, paid leave, or vaccines after the emergency has already arrived.

The United States failed partly because it lacked humility, but also because it lacked a functioning welfare state. Asking infected people to isolate is a public health measure only if they can miss work without losing wages or housing. Closing schools is an epidemiological intervention only if children have food, internet access, safe homes, and adults available to care for them. Advising vaccination is easier when people have primary care providers, transportation, time off, and confidence that medical institutions see them as more than data points. The moral question “who might get hurt?” must therefore be attached to budgets, laws, and administrative routines. Otherwise humility becomes an admirable sensibility floating above the machinery that determines life chances.

This is especially important because many of the pandemic’s “surprises” were not unknown unknowns. The vulnerability of nursing homes was not mysterious. The dependence of hospitals on just-in-time supply chains was not unknowable. The higher risk borne by Black, Latino, Indigenous, poor, incarcerated, and front-line workers followed from durable facts about American society. The fragility of public trust was visible in declining confidence in institutions, partisan media fragmentation, and years of anti-vaccine activism. To call these failures failures of prediction may be too generous. In many cases, the knowledge existed but had little political force because the people most endangered possessed too little power.

Jasanoff’s “technologies of humility” are valuable precisely where they insist on widening the circle of relevant knowledge. Public health agencies should not consult communities only after policies meet resistance. They should involve workers, disability advocates, parents, teachers, nurses, religious leaders, immigrant organizations, and residents of highly polluted neighborhoods before crisis plans are finalized. Risk communication should not be an exercise in translating expert conclusions for lay consumption; it should be a two-way process that discovers what people fear, what they need, what they have reason to distrust, and what tradeoffs they regard as legitimate.

Still, participation cannot substitute for responsibility. Governments sometimes invoke uncertainty to delay action, diffuse blame, or pretend that every value conflict requires endless consultation. In March 2020, humility about limited evidence on masks should not have meant leaving the public confused while supplies were reserved through evasive messaging. Humility about school closures should not mean ignoring children’s losses, but neither should it mean pretending schools can operate safely without ventilation, testing, masks, vaccination, and support for families. Democratic humility must be compatible with decisions under pressure. It should make decisions more accountable, not less decisive.

The pandemic also suggests that we need a broader definition of infrastructure. Preparedness is usually imagined as stockpiles, surveillance systems, emergency operations centers, and expert dashboards. Those matter. But so do paid sick leave, universal health coverage, unemployment insurance, eviction prevention, broadband, public transit, occupational safety enforcement, and trusted local health departments. These are not merely social policies with incidental health benefits; they are pandemic controls. A society that forces millions to choose between infection and income has built viral spread into its political economy.

The task after COVID-19, then, is not to choose between heroic preparedness and chastened humility. It is to institutionalize humble preparedness: plans that assume they will be wrong in some respects; metrics that include inequality, trust, and administrative capacity; emergency powers bounded by transparency and review; supply chains designed for resilience rather than maximum efficiency; and public health agencies funded in ordinary times, not merely praised in emergencies. The next crisis will also exceed our models. That is not an argument against planning. It is an argument for plans designed by people who know that models fail, societies fracture, and harms fall unevenly unless policy is built to prevent them from doing so.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The failure of the U.S. response was caused as much by the lack of a robust welfare state as by a lack of humility, as public health measures like isolation are only effective if citizens have the financial and social support to comply.
  2. 02Many pandemic outcomes labeled as 'surprises' were actually predictable consequences of existing racial and economic hierarchies that planners chose to ignore because the affected groups lacked political power.
  3. 03Humility must be operationalized through material investments in infrastructure—such as paid sick leave and universal health coverage—rather than remaining a mere moral sensibility.
  4. 04Democratic participation and community consultation should be used to inform policy design before a crisis occurs, rather than being used as a tool for managing resistance after the fact.
  5. 05Humility should not be used as a justification for government indecision or the diffusion of responsibility during an emergency; it must be compatible with decisive, accountable action.
  6. 06True preparedness requires remaking supply chains for resilience and funding public health agencies consistently during ordinary times rather than only during active crises.
