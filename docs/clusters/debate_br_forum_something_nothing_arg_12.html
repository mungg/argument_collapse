"The failure of counterinsurgency (COIN) in Afghanistan is not a failure of doctrine or individual soldiering, but a failure of institutional design that assigns tactical units impossible political and social tasks they are not structured to perform."

What Rosen captures better than most strategic commentary is the gap between doctrine and the emotional reality of war. Counterinsurgency manuals speak in calm imperatives: protect the population, understand the environment, use minimum force, build host-nation capacity. On Highway 601 those imperatives must pass through men who have been shot at, who have watched Afghan partners die, who do not trust the police they mentor, who are counting the days until they go home, and who suspect every culvert and compound wall may hide a bomb. The result is not hypocrisy so much as overload.

This is why the essay should be read not only as a critique of COIN but as a critique of institutional design. The United States has assigned tactical units a mission that no tactical unit can perform. A captain is asked to be diplomat, police trainer, development officer, intelligence analyst, anthropologist, mayor, judge, and combat leader. An infantry sergeant is expected to move instantly from “kill the enemy” to “reassure the elder.” A National Guard team mentors Afghan police who lack pay, radios, literacy, equipment, discipline, and sometimes loyalty. Then Washington debates whether “counterinsurgency works,” as if the doctrine itself were the operative unit of history.

Rosen concludes that the American military and policy establishments are incapable of doing COIN because they lack curiosity and empathy. That is too sweeping. Many soldiers and civilians in Afghanistan have shown both. Some have learned languages, returned for multiple tours, built relationships, and risked their lives to protect Afghans. Rosen’s own portrait of Captain Westby is more complicated than his thesis. Westby is exasperated, profane in private, and sometimes patronizing, but he also listens, restrains, negotiates, and tries to keep his Afghan partners alive. The problem is not that Americans are uniquely incurious. It is that the system makes curiosity episodic and empathy optional, while making rotation, force protection, career incentives, and bureaucratic metrics permanent.

Take rotations. A unit arrives, learns its area at great cost, forms relationships, and leaves. Its replacement inherits maps, briefings, and perhaps a few introductions, but not trust. Afghans, who live there permanently, watch foreigners cycle through with new acronyms and promises. Insurgents can wait. Police commanders can manipulate the newcomers. Villagers can hedge. In such an environment, “lessons learned” become a ritual phrase masking structural amnesia. If the United States were serious about counterinsurgency, it would create long-term district teams with repeated deployments to the same area, robust language training, civilian specialists, and continuity across years. Instead it has tried to make general-purpose units perform specialized political labor.

Consider metrics. Military organizations count what they can report: patrols conducted, weapons found, enemies killed or captured, police trained, projects completed, dollars spent. But the decisive variables in Rosen’s story are harder to measure: whether villagers believe the police will steal from them, whether a commander’s cousin is Taliban, whether a mullah regards the district governor as legitimate, whether a family feud is being misread as insurgency. Because these variables are difficult, they are often converted into simplified categories: Taliban, pro-government, neutral; cleared, held, built; trained, partnered, independent. The map becomes cleaner as reality becomes more misunderstood.

Force protection is another contradiction. COIN requires living among the people. Armies want to protect their soldiers. Both impulses are morally serious. No officer wants to tell a family that their son died because headquarters wanted a more authentic village presence. Yet if troops remain in large bases, they cannot protect civilians at night or understand local politics. When they patrol from armored vehicles, they appear alien and frightening. When they dismount, they become vulnerable to IEDs and ambushes. Soldiers respond with suspicion; suspicion produces rough treatment; rough treatment produces resentment; resentment aids insurgents. Doctrine identifies the cycle but cannot abolish the fear behind it.

Rosen also shows the impossibility of mentoring broken institutions from the bottom up. Team Prowler can urge Afghan police to wear body armor, carry ammunition, read maps, and avoid abusing civilians. But the policemen’s behavior reflects higher-level failures: recruitment, pay, logistics, leadership, justice, and political patronage. A policeman who is not paid will extort. A commander appointed through factional ties will prey on rivals. A court that releases criminals for bribes will demoralize honest police and encourage arbitrary punishment. Training a police unit without reforming the system around it is like teaching a man to swim and then dropping him back into sewage.

Still, Rosen’s essay risks fatalism. Institutions can change, though slowly and unevenly. The U.S. military of 2009 is not the military of 2001. It has absorbed painful lessons about civilian casualties, detainee abuse, cultural ignorance, and the limits of firepower. McChrystal’s rules of engagement, whatever their flaws, represent institutional learning. The growth of provincial reconstruction teams, human terrain efforts, agricultural development teams, and embedded training missions reflects an attempt—often clumsy—to adapt. Critics should not deny that adaptation simply because it is incomplete.

The real question is whether the scale of adaptation required exceeds the political time available. Here Rosen is strongest. A true COIN campaign in Afghanistan would require a decade or more of consistent effort, with high-quality civilian participation, long-term language expertise, police and judicial reform, regional diplomacy, and sustained public support at home. The United States is already eight years into the war, economically strained, politically divided, and led by a president who has announced both an escalation and the beginning of a drawdown in 2011. That is not the foundation for a generational campaign. It is a compromise between strategic anxiety and domestic impatience.

If the United States cannot build the institutions required for COIN, it should not pretend that courage at the small-unit level can compensate. Soldiers should not be sent to die for a theory their government is unwilling or unable to implement fully. Nor should Afghan civilians be asked to trust a protection force that may leave before the Taliban decide the contest. Honesty demands matching aims to capabilities.

What would institutional honesty mean? It would mean narrowing missions. If a unit is there to train police, give it the language, time, authority, and civilian legal support to affect police behavior beyond marksmanship. If it is there to secure a population, require persistent presence and accept the casualties and political costs that follow. If the country will not accept those costs, do not call the mission population protection. It would mean reducing reliance on short tours and creating area specialization. It would mean promoting officers for political understanding, not only command aggressiveness. It would mean integrating State Department, USAID, Justice, Agriculture, and intelligence personnel in ways that are more than decorative. And it would mean telling the American public that “success” may look like managed instability, not victory.

Rosen’s soldiers speak in ugly ways, but they are also trapped in an ugly design. The Afghan police “suck,” as one sergeant says, but that fact is not a personality defect; it is the visible symptom of a political order built on corruption and fear. The villagers hedge not because they are ungrateful but because hedging is rational. The Taliban endure not because they are militarily brilliant but because they are embedded in a landscape of grievances and sanctuaries. A doctrine cannot fix these things unless institutions are built to practice it over time.

The lesson of Rosen’s essay is therefore not simply “leave” or “surge.” It is: stop assigning impossible tasks to people at the bottom while evading responsibility at the top. If Washington wants counterinsurgency, it must build a government capable of counterinsurgency—ours first, then Afghanistan’s. If it will not, it should scale down the mission before more captains are asked to solve all the world’s problems with a translator, a radio, and a watermelon.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The U.S. military system undermines COIN by prioritizing short-term rotations and bureaucratic metrics over the long-term trust and local expertise required for success.
  2. 02Tactical units cannot successfully mentor Afghan security forces when those forces are embedded in a larger, broken political system of corruption and patronage.
  3. 03The inherent contradiction between the need for force protection and the requirement to live among the population creates a cycle of suspicion and resentment that aids the insurgency.
  4. 04The U.S. government is politically and economically unwilling to commit to the decade-long, specialized effort that a true COIN strategy would necessitate.
  5. 05Washington must either build a government apparatus capable of sustained, specialized political labor or narrow the military's mission to match its actual capabilities.
