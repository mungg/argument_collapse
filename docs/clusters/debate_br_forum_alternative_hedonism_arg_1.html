Diversified MiniMax

"Kate Soper’s 'alternative hedonism' fails as a political program because it prioritizes the reformation of individual desires over the structural and material changes necessary to challenge the power of capital."

Kate Soper's essay is a testament to the poverty of contemporary left thought. Here is a writer who recognizes that we face an environmental crisis of civilizational scope, that this crisis is driven by the logic of capital accumulation, and that the lifestyle of affluent societies is neither sustainable nor fulfilling. And yet her response is to call for a "new hedonism"—for people to desire differently, to find pleasure in slower travel and local food and artisanal production. This is not a political program; it is a consumer guide.

I do not mean to be dismissive. The essay contains much that is valuable. Soper is right to challenge the equation of environmental politics with sacrifice, and her critique of the "hedonic treadmill" of consumer culture has empirical grounding. The concrete proposals she offers—a shorter working week, investment in public transit, the development of sharing economies—are worth pursuing for their own sake. And the concept of "structure of feeling" provides a useful bridge between cultural critique and political mobilization. But these valuable elements are subordinated to a framework that fundamentally misidentifies the problem and offers a correspondingly inadequate solution.

The core problem is the essay's individualism. Soper presents alternative hedonism as a matter of changing what we desire—choosing to walk rather than drive, to grow rather than buy, to share rather than possess. But this framing obscures the structural character of the problem. Consumer culture is not simply a matter of individual preference that can be reformed by better ideas or moral exhortation. It is embedded in material structures—urban design, labor market regulation, the organization of production, the logic of capital accumulation—that constrain the choices available to individuals. One can choose to walk to work only if the city provides safe pavements; one can choose to work less only if income support makes it possible; one can choose to grow food only if land is available. The essay acknowledges these constraints but treats them as incidental rather than central.

The political implications of this individualist framing are troubling. If the problem is that people desire the wrong things, then the solution is education and persuasion. The essay becomes, in effect, a piece of moral instruction: let us tell people that consumer culture is not as pleasant as they think, and they will choose differently. But this approach misunderstands how desire is formed. Desire is shaped by material conditions, not merely by ideas. When people work long hours to pay off consumer debt, when housing costs consume half their income, when public transit is inadequate and unsafe, they make choices that are rational within the constraints they face—not because they have been manipulated by advertisers but because they have no feasible alternatives. Changing their desires without changing their conditions will not liberate them; it will merely add guilt to the burdens they already bear.

A genuinely political response to the environmental crisis would start from the recognition that the current system generates desires that serve the interests of capital and the destruction of the planet. The response would not be to change desires but to change the system that generates them. This would require a political program: taxation of carbon and wealth, regulation of corporate behavior, public investment in sustainable infrastructure, reform of the labor market to reduce working hours, democratization of economic decision-making. These are demands that can be fought for through organizing, electoral politics, and social movements. They are demands that build shared identity and collective capacity. They are demands that address the material conditions within which desire is formed.

Soper gestures toward such demands—she mentions debt cancellation, reparations, and the regulation of corporate power—but they remain peripheral to an argument whose center of gravity is cultural. The result is an essay that offers a vision of the good life while leaving untouched the power relations that make the current system so difficult to change. Alternative hedonism may be an attractive destination, but it is not a route to the changes that would make it achievable.

There is also the question of political strategy. Soper hopes that a "structure of feeling" in favor of change is already emerging, and that alternative hedonism can give it expression. But structures of feeling do not translate automatically into political demands. They need to be organized, given institutional form, connected to material interests. The essay does not discuss how this organization might occur. It does not consider what institutional forms—unions, parties, social movements—might give expression to the vision it describes. It does not address the question of power: how the interests that benefit from the current system might be challenged and overcome. This is a fatal omission. A politics that does not address power is not a politics at all.

I want to be clear that I do not reject the essay's vision. A world of reduced work, slower travel, and communal consumption is, as Soper argues, more desirable than the current system in many respects. But the path to such a world runs through politics, not through the reformation of individual desire. We need to build political coalitions capable of demanding and winning the structural changes that would make alternative ways of living possible—not to convince people that they should desire differently but to create conditions within which different desires become feasible. This requires a politics of power, not a politics of pleasure.

The essay's individualist framework also has a troubling class dimension. Alternative hedonism is most readily available to those with the resources to opt out of the mainstream economy—to grow their own food, to work reduced hours, to choose slow travel. For those in the gig economy, those who must work multiple jobs to survive, those who live in areas without adequate public transit, the essay's prescriptions are not options but fantasies. Soper acknowledges that the consumerist lifestyle is "a regime in which we are all caught up," but she does not adequately grapple with what this means for the feasibility of her alternative. If everyone must change, but the conditions for change are not equally available, then the politics of pleasure risks becoming another form of middle-class self-expression.

In the end, the essay offers a vision without a strategy. This is the poverty of contemporary left thought: the recognition that something is badly wrong combined with the inability to identify how it might be put right. Alternative hedonism may be an attractive vision, but it is not a program. The environmental crisis requires a politics of power, not a politics of pleasure. We need to build the collective capacity to demand and win structural change. Anything less is not politics but its simulation.

Diversified DeepSeek

"While Kate Soper’s vision of 'alternative hedonism' is aesthetically appealing, it is politically insufficient because it prioritizes a cultural shift in consumer desire over the necessary structural struggle to dismantle the capitalist system of production."

Kate Soper’s essay is seductive. It paints a picture of a life richer in leisure, community, and sensual pleasure, freed from the treadmill of overwork and the clutter of meaningless consumption. As a vision, it is beautiful. As a political program, it is dangerously incomplete. By locating the central problem in “consumer culture” rather than in the capitalist regime of production that generates it, and by appealing to a universal “we” of affluent societies, Soper risks offering a palliative that soothes the conscience of the well-off while leaving the structural engines of exploitation and inequality untouched.

Let us be clear: the growth imperatives that drive environmental destruction are not, at root, a matter of collective desire gone awry. They are baked into the DNA of a system in which capital must continually expand or collapse. It is not that we, the people of the rich world, have made a simple mistake about where pleasure lies. It is that our lives are organized by firms that must sell ever more goods and services to survive, and by a labor market that compels most of us to work long hours under conditions we do not control. In such a system, even “alternative hedonism” can become a niche market — think of the expensive slow-travel tours, the high-end craft workshops, the farmers’ markets that have become status symbols for the professional classes. Soper gestures toward this danger but does not adequately confront it.

The biggest blind spot is class. When Soper speaks of “our” acceptance of consumerist ways, she conflates experiences that differ dramatically by income and security. A single mother working two jobs and relying on cheap, processed food and Amazon deliveries because she has no time to cook or shop locally is not a willing participant in consumer culture; she is a victim of it. Her “alternative hedonism” — the kind that requires time, space, and often disposable income — is structurally foreclosed. Telling her that she would actually be happier if she spent less, worked less, and consumed more sustainably is, at best, tone-deaf. At worst, it is a form of moralizing that lets the owning class off the hook: if only we all changed our desires, the system would crumble. But desire is not the primary engine; power is.

Soper’s own framework partly acknowledges this. She notes that even the breadline poor are caught up in consumer society’s demands. But her prescription — a citizens’ basic income, reduced workweeks, and a cultural shift toward slow living — requires a massive redistribution of wealth and power that she never seriously confronts. Who will fight for it? How will capital be forced to concede an end to growth? Historically, the only force capable of imposing such changes on an unwilling elite is a militant, organized working class with a vision of an entirely different mode of production. That vision must include ecological sustainability, but it cannot be reduced to a plea for more pleasant consumption. It must aim for democratic control over investment, production, and the very shape of the working day.

The labor movement in the West may have become too narrowly focused on wages and conditions, as Soper laments, but that is not an argument for abandoning its methods; it is an argument for radicalizing them. The fight for a shorter workweek without loss of pay, for example, has a long and honorable socialist pedigree. It is not a lifestyle choice; it is a class demand that directly attacks the power of capital to extract surplus value from our time. When Soper portrays this as part of an alternative pleasure agenda, she drains it of its antagonistic content. Real social transformation will not come because enough people read a persuasive book and decide to ride bikes; it will come because workers in logistics, manufacturing, and services — the very ones most brutalized by the current order — organize to shut down the machinery, not to “slow down,” but to seize it.

This is not to dismiss the importance of alternative visions of the good life. Far from it. Socialists have always insisted that capitalism alienates us from the pleasures of creative labor, genuine community, and uncommodified leisure. But those pleasures are not waiting for us just over the horizon of a cultural shift; they must be fought for through struggles over ownership, distribution, and the monopoly of corporate power over our collective future. The consumerist lifestyle Soper so eloquently critiques is not an autonomous cultural formation; it is the consequence of a world in which public provision has been gutted, working hours have been intensified, and the only remaining arena of apparent choice is the marketplace. Reversing that requires building a counterpower capable of taking on the corporations and the state.

Thus, while I share Soper’s impatience with a left that refuses to imagine the pleasures of a post-capitalist world, I worry that her “alternative hedonism” risks becoming exactly the kind of individualistic, middle-class solution that leaves the power of capital intact. The changes forced by climate crisis are indeed an opportunity, but the agency to seize it will not come from revised consumer desire alone. It will come from the organized, collective power of working people who have little to lose but their chains — and a world of pleasure to gain.

"While Kate Soper’s 'alternative hedonism' correctly identifies the need to frame sustainability as a desirable lifestyle rather than a sacrifice, its reliance on a hedonistic philosophical framework and cultural persuasion is insufficient to challenge the structural and political-economic forces driving environmental destruction."

Kate Soper's alternative hedonism rests on a fundamental premise that deserves scrutiny: that the good life is best understood in terms of pleasure, and that we can construct a politics around this concept that will be both appealing and adequate to the environmental challenges we face. I want to argue that this framework, while not without value, has significant limitations that the essay does not adequately address.

The hedonist tradition in philosophy has a distinguished history, from Epicurus to Bentham, and Soper draws on it effectively to challenge the equation of environmental sustainability with sacrifice. If the consumerist lifestyle is less pleasurable than it appears—if the hedonic treadmill means that additional consumption delivers diminishing returns—then perhaps sustainable living is not the deprivation it is made out to be. This is a useful corrective to fatalistic thinking about climate action. Yet the essay's uncritical adoption of hedonism as its normative framework creates problems that become increasingly apparent as the argument unfolds.

Consider, first, the philosophical poverty of hedonism as a theory of the good life. The tradition runs from Aristippus, who identified pleasure with the satisfaction of immediate desire, through Epicurus, who advocated the refined pleasures of friendship and philosophy, to the utilitarianism of Bentham and Mill, who sought to quantify and compare pleasures and pains. Each of these variants has been subject to devastating critique. Hedonism cannot explain why some pleasures are considered higher than others, why we sometimes sacrifice present pleasure for future gain, or why we value activities—caring for a dying parent, completing a difficult work project, raising children—that are not obviously pleasant. More fundamentally, hedonism treats the good life as consisting in subjective experience rather than in objective flourishing, which means it cannot distinguish between pleasures that are merely felt and pleasures that are genuinely good for us.

Soper is aware of these limitations. She notes the tension between "subjective" and "objective" approaches to well-being and acknowledges that people are "not always the best judges of their own well-being." Yet the essay does not provide a coherent resolution of this tension. It wants to argue that alternative hedonism offers a better account of what we should desire than conventional consumer culture, but it refuses to adopt the more robust normative framework that would be necessary to support this claim. The result is a hedonism that is both too permissive (it struggles to condemn pleasures that are environmentally destructive) and too restrictive (it cannot explain why we should value activities that are not immediately pleasant).

More troubling is the essay's treatment of the relationship between desire and political economy. Soper acknowledges that consumer culture is "structurally embedded" and that people lack the "time, space, and income" to consume more sustainably even if they wanted to. But her response to this constraint is essentially cultural: she wants to change what we desire rather than change the structures that constrain our desires. This approach is not only insufficient—desire is shaped by material conditions, not merely by ideas—but also politically conservative in its implications. If the problem is that people want the wrong things, then the solution is education and persuasion. But if the problem is that the political economy generates desires that serve corporate interests and environmental destruction, then the solution must be political: taxation, regulation, the restructuring of production and consumption.

The essay gestures toward structural change but does not make it the center of its argument. The discussion of work focuses on the pleasures and pains of individual workers rather than on the power dynamics of the labor market. The discussion of consumption focuses on the satisfactions of acquisition rather than on the corporate power that shapes the production and marketing of goods. The discussion of travel focuses on the pleasures of train travel rather than on the political economy of transportation policy. This is not to say that Soper ignores these issues—she does not—but that they remain peripheral to an argument whose center of gravity is cultural.

The political economy of alternative hedonism deserves more extended treatment than the essay provides. Soper acknowledges that "the changes forced by the climate crisis" will be necessary and that "greener technologies will help." But she does not seriously engage with the question of how a post-consumerist economy could be brought into being. What political forces would support such a transformation? What institutional changes would be required? What forms of resistance would need to be overcome? The essay is better at describing the destination than at mapping the route.

This is not merely a technical objection. If alternative hedonism is to be more than an utopian fantasy, it needs a political strategy. Soper rightly criticizes the "tech utopians" for their insufficient engagement with political economy, but her own alternative is subject to a similar criticism. The vision she describes—a world of reduced work, slower travel, communal consumption—will not emerge spontaneously from changed desires. It requires investment in public infrastructure, regulation of corporate behavior, reform of the tax and benefit system, and a fundamental reorientation of economic planning. These are political demands that need political strategies to be realized.

Yet the essay's culturalist slant means that it does not develop such a strategy. The "structure of feeling" Soper identifies may indeed be widespread, but feelings are not the same as political demands. The essay does not discuss how the inchoate intuition that something is wrong might be translated into organized political action. It does not consider what institutional forms—unions, parties, social movements, electoral coalitions—might give expression to the vision it describes. It does not address the question of power: how the interests that benefit from the current system might be challenged and overcome.

I want to conclude on a note of qualified appreciation. The essay's central insight—that we need to imagine sustainable living as a form of the good life rather than as sacrifice—is genuinely important, and Soper develops it with skill and imagination. The concrete proposals she offers are, for the most part, attractive and worth pursuing. And the concept of "structure of feeling" provides a useful bridge between cultural critique and political mobilization. Yet the essay would be strengthened by a more explicit engagement with political economy and a more robust normative framework than hedonism can provide. The good life is more than pleasure; the politics of the good life is more than changing desires. Without these clarifications, alternative hedonism risks remaining a vision for a better world rather than a program for achieving one.

  2. 02The essay lacks a coherent normative standard to distinguish between environmentally destructive pleasures and those that are genuinely beneficial for human well-being.
  3. 03By focusing on changing individual desires rather than the material conditions and power dynamics that shape them, the argument adopts a culturalist approach that risks being politically conservative.
  4. 04The proposal fails to provide a viable political strategy or map out the institutional changes—such as regulation, taxation, and labor reform—required to dismantle corporate power and realize a post-consumerist economy.

"While Kate Soper’s 'alternative hedonism' provides a necessary affirmative vision for environmentalism, its reliance on a hedonistic framework is philosophically and strategically insufficient to drive systemic change."

Kate Soper's essay makes a strong case that environmental politics must offer a vision of the good life rather than merely a catalog of sacrifice. If sustainability is to command broad popular support, it must engage with how people actually experience the world—the pleasures they seek, the satisfactions they value, the lives they want to live. Soper's alternative hedonism attempts precisely this kind of engagement, and in doing so it represents a genuine contribution to green thought.

The essay's strengths are considerable. Its critique of the hedonic treadmill—the idea that additional consumption delivers diminishing returns, so that the rat race of competitive acquisition cannot deliver lasting satisfaction—is empirically grounded and effectively deployed. Its challenge to the work ethic identifies a real source of dissatisfaction in contemporary life, and its proposals for a shorter working week deserve serious policy attention. Its vision of local economies, sharing schemes, and pedestrian-friendly cities is attractive and increasingly relevant as the costs of car culture become ever more apparent. And its insistence that the environmental crisis is an opportunity rather than merely a disaster provides the kind of affirmative politics that environmentalism has too often lacked.

But I want to push back on some aspects of the argument, and in particular on the essay's reliance on the hedonic framework.

The first problem is conceptual. Hedonism—the theory that the good life consists in maximizing pleasure and minimizing pain—is philosophically inadequate, and this inadequacy shows up in the essay's difficulties in distinguishing genuine from spurious pleasures. Soper argues that consumer culture offers "compensation" that falls "far short of making up for what has been lost through overwork." But this argument requires a measure of well-being that goes beyond subjective feeling. After all, many people report that they enjoy their consumer lifestyles, at least in part. Soper responds that their enjoyments are subject to hedonic adaptation—that the pleasure of new purchases fades, and the treadmill of acquisition leaves everyone dissatisfied. This is sometimes true, but it is not universally true, and the essay's treatment of hedonic adaptation is more confident than the psychological research warrants.

Moreover, hedonism has difficulty accounting for the kinds of satisfactions that are not obviously pleasant in the moment but are nonetheless valuable. Taking care of a sick family member, completing a demanding project, enduring hardship for a worthy cause—these activities are not pleasurable in any straightforward sense, but they contribute to human flourishing in ways that the hedonic calculus cannot capture. A politics premised on hedonism will have difficulty appealing to the satisfactions that are most distinctively human, and this is a significant limitation.

The second problem is political. Soper wants to build a politics around desire—to argue that if people desired differently, they would live differently and the system would change. But desire is shaped by material conditions, not merely by ideas. People work long hours because their material conditions require it, not because advertisers have seduced them. They drive rather than cycle because their cities have been built around the car. They shop at Amazon because there is no viable alternative in their communities. Changing desires without changing conditions will not transform the system; it will merely leave people frustrated that they cannot live up to ideals they intellectually accept.

This is not to say that cultural change is unimportant. Soper is right that "structures of feeling" precede and enable political change, and the essay's effort to articulate a vision of sustainable pleasure contributes to the cultural conditions for transformation. But cultural change and political change must proceed together. The essay would be strengthened by a more explicit account of the political demands that correspond to its cultural vision—the policies, regulations, and investments that would make alternative hedonism feasible for people who currently lack the resources to pursue it.

The third problem is strategic. Soper hopes that the environmental crisis will generate sufficient pressure to shift the system toward a post-consumerist future, and that alternative hedonism can provide the vision that animates this shift. But the essay does not adequately address the question of political agency. Who will make these changes? What forms of organization and mobilization will be required? What are the likely sources of resistance, and how might they be overcome? The essay offers a vision of the destination without a strategy for reaching it.

I do not want to be too critical. Soper's essay is a valuable contribution to environmental thought, and its central argument—that we need to imagine sustainable living as a form of the good life—deserves to be taken seriously. The concrete proposals it offers are worth pursuing, and the concept of "structure of feeling" provides a useful bridge between cultural critique and political mobilization. But the essay's reliance on hedonism limits its normative reach, its individualist slant obscures the need for structural change, and its political silence leaves the question of transformation unanswered. Alternative hedonism may be part of the answer, but it is not the whole answer. We need a politics that combines cultural vision with political strategy, normative clarity with practical judgment, and a sense of possibility with an honest assessment of power. Soper has given us materials for such a politics. The task of assembling them remains.

"While Kate Soper’s 'alternative hedonism' provides a vital and strategically superior reframing of environmentalism as a pursuit of pleasure rather than sacrifice, its effectiveness is limited by an overemphasis on individual desire at the expense of the structural political economy required for systemic change."

Kate Soper's essay offers a genuinely compelling reframing of environmental politics. Instead of treating sustainability as sacrifice—as mainstream discourse so often does—she asks us to imagine a future where reduced consumption and slower living actually deliver more satisfaction than the current treadmill of work and acquisition. This is not merely a philosophical refinement but a strategic intervention of considerable political significance.

Consider how environmental politics has typically been framed. The green case has long rested on a logic of renunciation: we must accept less because the planet cannot sustain our current appetites. This framing, however noble in intent, has proven politically catastrophic. It plays directly into the hands of those who would depict environmentalists as out-of-touch elites bent on denying ordinary people the pleasures they have earned. Soper's alternative is more ambitious: she argues that the consumerist lifestyle is itself the killjoy, that it promises pleasures it fails to deliver, and that a fundamentally different way of living would be more rewarding. The political implications are significant. If environmentalism can be recast not as the politics of less but as the politics of more—more time, more leisure, more genuine pleasure—then its appeal broadens considerably.

The essay's concrete proposals are where it is strongest. The case for a reduced working week is compelling on multiple grounds: it would free time currently consumed by commuting, enable more sustainable patterns of consumption, reduce the anxiety and stress that characterize contemporary work culture, and make possible forms of activity—gardening, craft, care work, cultural pursuits—that are themselves sources of satisfaction. The argument for investing in walking, cycling, and public transit rather than electric cars is similarly persuasive: beyond the environmental benefits, reclaiming urban space for pedestrians would transform the texture of daily life, enabling forms of sociability and sensory engagement that car culture forecloses. And the vision of local cooperatives, sharing schemes, and repair networks suggests that a post-consumerist economy could generate its own forms of pleasure—pleasures rooted in reciprocity and community rather than acquisition.

The essay also makes effective use of the concept of "structure of feeling." This is a rich notion, drawn from the cultural critic Raymond Williams, which captures the way in which inchoate intuitions about how life should be lived circulate through a society before they crystallize into articulated demands. Soper is surely right that such intuitions are at work in affluent societies: a sense that something has gone wrong with the pace of contemporary life, that the promises of consumer culture have not been fulfilled, that there must be something better. Alternative hedonism offers a language for giving expression to these intuitions—and, in doing so, a vision of what the good life might look like.

Yet the essay has weaknesses that limit its force. Most significantly, it undertheorizes the political economy of change. Alternative hedonism is not merely a cultural proposition but a political program: it would require massive investment in public infrastructure, regulation of corporate power, reform of the tax and benefit system, and reorientation of economic planning away from growth. Soper acknowledges that corporations and the state would resist such changes, but the essay provides little sense of how this resistance might be overcome. "Concerned consumers" can express preferences, but can they build the kind of political coalitions necessary to fundamentally reorient the economy? The essay gestures toward labor organizing, debt campaigns, and international solidarity, but these political dimensions are subordinated to what is essentially a cultural argument about desire and pleasure.

A related weakness is the essay's individualist slant. The alternative hedonism on offer is presented largely as a matter of individual choice—choosing to walk rather than drive, to repair rather than replace, to participate in sharing schemes rather than shop at Amazon. But consumer culture is not simply a matter of individual preference that can be reformed by better information or moral exhortation. It is embedded in material structures—housing policy, urban design, labor market regulation, the organization of production—that constrain the choices available to individuals. One can choose to walk to work only if the city provides safe pavements; one can choose to buy less only if alternatives to market provision exist; one can choose to work less only if labor market regulation and income support make it possible. The essay does gesture toward such structural changes, but its primary emphasis remains on changing desire—which may be the wrong order of operations. Structural change enables different choices, which in turn shape desire, rather than the reverse.

Finally, the essay's claim that consumer culture is "itself a killjoy" rests on contestable premises. The hedonic adaptation research cited is real, but its application to the critique of consumer culture is more complicated than the essay suggests. Hedonic adaptation is variable: some pleasures are more resistant to adaptation than others, and some people adapt more readily than others. More fundamentally, the essay conflates very different activities under the heading of "consumer culture." There is a vast difference between buying fast fashion to signal status and cultivating a garden, between working overtime to afford a bigger car and spending time with children. Many of the activities the essay identifies as more sustainable alternatives are themselves forms of consumption—consumption of time, space, and social connection. The argument would be strengthened by distinguishing more carefully between forms of consumption and their different relationships to satisfaction and sustainability.

These reservations should not obscure what is valuable in Soper's essay. The case for alternative hedonism is, in the end, not primarily philosophical but political. The claim is that environmental politics needs a vision of the good life if it is to mobilize popular support—that a politics premised on sacrifice will fail where a politics premised on liberation can succeed. This claim is worth taking seriously, and Soper's essay, for all its limitations, makes a contribution to articulating what such a politics might look like. The structure of feeling she identifies is real; the vision she outlines is genuinely attractive; the political stakes are high. Alternative hedonism may not be the whole answer, but it is a necessary part of any answer.
