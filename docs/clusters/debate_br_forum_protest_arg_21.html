Diversified Gemini

"The current defense of campus protesters is flawed because it fails to recognize that the moral power of civil disobedience depends on the protesters' willingness to accept the institutional consequences of their rule-breaking."

Alex Gourevitch mounts a vigorous defense of the Gaza encampments, arguing that severe administrative punishments—suspensions, expulsions, and arrests—are "massively disproportionate" and threaten the right to protest. While I agree with Gourevitch that the university commons must remain a space for robust political expression, his essay ignores the foundational premise of civil disobedience: the acceptance of consequences.

Gourevitch repeatedly invokes the civil rights movement and the anti-war protests of the 1960s to legitimize the disruptions of the past year. But the titans of those movements did not cry foul when the state or the institution punished them. When Martin Luther King Jr. wrote from a Birmingham jail, he did not argue that the city had violated his right to a public forum. He acknowledged that he had broken the law, and he accepted the penalty as the necessary cost of bearing moral witness against an unjust system. The power of civil disobedience stems entirely from the protester's willingness to suffer for their cause.

Today’s campus protesters have adopted a bizarrely consumerist approach to radicalism. They want to break the rules, occupy buildings, and disrupt the normal functioning of the university, but they demand complete amnesty from the administration while doing so. They want the aesthetic thrill of revolution with the risk profile of an intramural sports club.

Gourevitch enables this dynamic by arguing that universities should only issue "modest punishment" that "recognizes the conscientious nature of the protest act." This is a contradiction in terms. The university is a neutral arbiter of its own rules; it cannot assess the "conscientious nature" of a rule violation without endorsing the political content of the protest. If a university grants leniency to an encampment because administrators sympathize with the cause of Palestine, it must legally grant the same leniency to an encampment of neo-Nazis protesting immigration.

If protesters truly believe that their university's investments are funding a genocide, then pitching an illegal encampment and occupying an administration building are entirely rational acts. But if those acts are to carry any moral weight, the protesters must be prepared to face expulsion. The modern demand to "be hostile" without facing institutional hostility in return is not a defense of academic freedom; it is a demand for unearned privilege.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Historical movements for social justice derived their legitimacy from activists who accepted legal penalties as a necessary cost of moral witness.
  2. 02Modern campus protesters exhibit a consumerist approach to radicalism by demanding complete amnesty for disruptive actions that they frame as revolutionary.
  3. 03Universities cannot grant leniency based on the 'conscientious nature' of a protest without compromising their role as neutral arbiters and setting a precedent that would protect hateful groups.
  4. 04Demanding the right to be hostile toward an institution while expecting that institution to waive its disciplinary rules is a request for unearned privilege rather than a defense of academic freedom.
