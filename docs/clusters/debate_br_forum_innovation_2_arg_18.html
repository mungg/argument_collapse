"Dan Breznitz’s proposal for a stage-based innovation policy is a flawed form of central planning that risks locking local economies into static specializations rather than allowing market-driven experimentation to discover sustainable growth."

Dan Breznitz’s essay is a thoughtful contribution, but it is also a seductive trap. In his eagerness to replace the Silicon Valley fetish with his own stage-based taxonomy of innovation, he invites governments to become central planners of the innovation economy. The history of such efforts, from industrial policy in post-war Britain to the “picking winners” approach of many Latin American countries, is not encouraging. The knowledge that policymakers need to orchestrate the right stage of innovation for their region is simply not accessible in advance; it emerges only through market processes of experimentation and failure. Breznitz’s framework risks becoming the new cookie-cutter, just with four flavors instead of one.

Breznitz’s own examples undercut his prescription. He celebrates Taiwan’s success in stage-two and stage-three innovation, attributing it to public-private coordination and the Industrial Technology Research Institute (ITRI). But Taiwan’s rise was not a product of bureaucrats choosing which stage to pursue. It evolved organically from the decisions of thousands of firms responding to market signals. Giant became a leader in carbon fiber frames because it lost a major client and had to innovate to survive, not because a government agency designated bicycles as a strategic sector. The ITRI’s role was important but supportive, not directive. Similarly, the Italian shoe district of the Riviera del Brenta succeeded because entrepreneurial owners realized they could move upscale, not because a regional commission planned it. The spontaneous order of the market, not the design of the state, is the real engine behind these stories. Breznitz recasts them as triumphs of public leadership, but that is a misleading extrapolation.

The logical weakness of the essay is its assumption that the “right” innovation stage can be identified in advance and cultivated through policy. In a dynamic global economy, comparative advantages shift rapidly. A region that pours public resources into building stage-four assembly capabilities may find itself undercut by lower-cost locations within a few years, unless it has the flexibility to pivot. That flexibility, in turn, depends on the decentralized knowledge of entrepreneurs, not the strategic plans of government agencies. Breznitz’s framework, if adopted as policy doctrine, would lock communities into static specializations that may be tomorrow’s dead ends. The Silicon Valley model, for all its inequality problems, at least has the virtue of radical openness to new possibilities; it generates an enormous amount of chaff in hopes of a few grains of wheat, but the wheat can be transformative. A stage-based industrial policy tends toward pre-emptive pruning.

Breznitz is right that venture capital–backed startups concentrating on financial exit create inequality, but his alternative concentrates risk in a different way: by making the state the central coordinator of innovation capacity. Public officials are not immune to error, political pressure, or cronyism. The positive examples he cites—SiO2 Materials Science, Israel’s early tech push—involved government acting as an enabling customer or co-investor, not as a designer of the entire ecosystem. Operation Warp Speed purchased vaccines and vials; it did not dictate the innovation stage of the pharmaceutical industry. That distinction matters. Using the state’s purchasing power to solve specific problems is one thing; attempting to steer whole local economies into a particular innovation stage is quite another.

Moreover, Breznitz overlooks the role that Silicon Valley–style venture capital plays in funding tech teens. While he rightly notes that many such firms are undercapitalized, the VC model, for all its faults, remains a crucial mechanism for scaling ambitious young companies that banks and conventional lenders will not touch. The Israeli high-tech story, which he uses as a cautionary tale, also demonstrates that VC-funded firms can generate enormous wealth and knowledge that, over time, spill into the broader economy—if the link is made. The problem in Israel was not the stage-one focus per se, but the failure to connect the high-tech sector to the domestic economy through labor markets, supplier relationships, and tax policies. The solution is not to abandon stage-one ambition but to build those connective tissues, a point Breznitz himself almost makes with the Alabama example.

Ultimately, the essay’s call for local innovation ecosystems built around public goods sounds benign, but it risks substituting one top-down vision for another. The policy that would genuinely serve inclusive prosperity is not to pick a stage and build institutional supports around it, but to ensure a level playing field: enforce competition, lower regulatory barriers to entry, invest broadly in education and infrastructure, and let a thousand experiments bloom. The market, not the ministry, will discover which innovation stages a community can sustain.
