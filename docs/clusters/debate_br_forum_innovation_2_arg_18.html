Diversified DeepSeek

"Dan Breznitz’s four-stage innovation framework provides a vital roadmap for achieving inclusive local prosperity by moving beyond the flawed and exclusionary Silicon Valley model."

Dan Breznitz’s essay is a much-needed corrective to the simplistic narratives that dominate economic development policy. For decades, cities and nations have poured billions into chasing the Silicon Valley mirage, convinced that showering venture capital on high-tech startups is the surest path to prosperity. Breznitz systematically dismantles this myth, showing that innovation is not a monolith but a spectrum of activities, each capable of generating inclusive growth if matched with the right institutional supports. His framework of four innovation stages should be required reading for every mayor, governor, and economic development official.

I want to extend Breznitz’s argument by looking at a different sector entirely: agriculture. In many developing countries, the obsession with “tech hubs” and “disruptive startups” has diverted attention from the far more powerful potential of innovation in food systems. A stage-three innovation approach—making existing agricultural technologies better, more reliable, and more accessible—would do far more for local prosperity than yet another app-based delivery service run by a handful of engineers. Consider the work of organizations like the International Rice Research Institute or the success of Brazil’s Embrapa, which transformed the cerrado into productive farmland through decades of incremental innovation in soil management, crop varieties, and production techniques. That effort created millions of jobs across skill levels and lifted entire regions, exactly the kind of inclusive growth Breznitz champions.

Breznitz’s history of Cobalt, Ontario, is more than a colorful anecdote; it underscores a structural truth: extractive booms—whether of silver or of highly concentrated tech wealth—benefit a few while the community bears the long-term costs. But he might have drawn the parallel even more sharply. Silicon Valley itself can be seen as a kind of digital Cobalt, where the massive wealth generated by platform companies has largely enriched founders, early employees, and investors, leaving behind unaffordable housing, service-sector precarity, and a stark sorting between the coding elite and everyone else. The critical point is that the financial exit model, built on rapid scaling and acquisition, systematically channels the gains out of the local economy. Israel’s experience, so well recounted here, is Exhibit A: the money flows back to foreign investors, while inequality spikes.

What makes Breznitz’s alternative compelling is that it does not rely on fantasizing about a return to vertically integrated manufacturing in one locality, nor does it reject globalization. It works within the world we have. The examples of Shimano and Giant are particularly instructive because they demonstrate how firms in “unsexy” sectors can dominate global supply chains through sustained, stage-appropriate innovation, employing thousands of workers at various skill levels. This runs directly counter to the prevailing policy instinct, which says you must either attract a Google campus or give tax breaks to a battery gigafactory, never recognizing the intermediate capacities that make such facilities genuinely rooted. Giant’s collaboration with Taiwan’s public research institutes—the ITRI—is a model of public-private partnership that doesn’t just fund science but builds the ecosystem of suppliers, skills, and shared resources that stage-two and stage-three innovation requires.

One might object that Breznitz’s chosen examples are all from small, trade-dependent economies (Taiwan, Israel, specific Italian districts), and that they cannot be easily replicated in the sprawling, service-oriented United States. But this misses how deeply his framework applies to any locality that is willing to identify its existing strengths and deliberately build institutional supports around them. The SiO2 Materials Science example in Alabama—a “tech teen” focused on manufacturing—shows that the model can work in rural America. The challenge is not geography; it is political will and policy imagination. As Breznitz emphasizes, this requires an array of public and semi-public goods: education tailored to industry needs, shared specialized facilities, and collaborative networks. Those are precisely the investments that the American policy imagination, shaped by market fundamentalism, has systematically neglected.

Breznitz’s essay is also a subtle defense of industrial policy, a term long taboo in mainstream economics. By showing that different stages of innovation demand different public inputs—whether it’s the Israeli state embedding its tech sector in U.S. markets, or the Italian regional government supporting design skills for luxury shoes—he normalizes the idea that the state must be a strategic coordinator. The U.S. is belatedly rediscovering this truth with the CHIPS Act and other initiatives, but as Breznitz notes, those efforts come after we have “lost our abilities to innovate in those stages.” The real takeaway is not just to build a few fabs but to nurture the entire ecology of tech teens, training programs, and supplier networks that make stage-three innovation self-sustaining.

If there is a weakness in the essay, it is the relatively light treatment of the political economy of all this. Who opposes these sensible measures, and why? The answer, largely, is that the Silicon Valley model serves powerful incumbents—venture capitalists, large tech firms, real estate developers—who have captured the policymaking process and stand to lose from a more distributed model of innovation. Breznitz hints at this when he speaks of those who “profit by selling the Silicon Valley dream,” but a fuller analysis would confront the rent-seeking that makes inclusive innovation politically difficult. Nevertheless, as a diagnosis and a roadmap, the essay is superb. It reframes the entire debate, moving us from “How can we be the next Silicon Valley?” to “What kind of innovation fits our place and our people?” That is a question every community should be asking, urgently.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Silicon Valley model of venture capital and financial exits functions like an extractive boom, enriching a small elite while leaving local communities with inequality and precarity.
  2. 02Innovation should be viewed as a spectrum of activities—including design, incremental improvement, and production—rather than being narrowly equated with high-tech invention.
  3. 03Applying Breznitz’s stage-three innovation approach to sectors like agriculture could lift entire regions in developing countries more effectively than tech startups.
  4. 04Successful local growth requires the state to act as a strategic coordinator that provides public goods like specialized education and shared production facilities.
  5. 05The 'tech teen' model demonstrates that mid-sized, locally embedded firms are more effective at creating diverse jobs and sustained growth than chasing global tech behemoths.
  6. 06The primary obstacle to implementing these inclusive strategies is a political economy dominated by powerful incumbents who profit from the existing Silicon Valley narrative.
