"While economics is an essential tool for reform, a post-neoliberal framework must ensure that economic expertise serves democratic deliberation rather than replacing neoliberal technocracy with a progressive version that still insulates policy from popular control."

The most encouraging feature of the lead essay is also the source of my unease: it places economists back at the center of reform. Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman are among the economists one would want in that position—empirically minded, institutionally aware, skeptical of market fundamentalism, and attentive to power. Yet after decades in which economic expertise helped narrow democratic imagination, we should be cautious about replacing neoliberal technocracy with progressive technocracy.

The authors’ diagnosis is persuasive. The public has come to associate economics with a set of anti-government, pro-market prescriptions. Economists did not always deserve that reputation, but they did plenty to earn it. Too many presented trade liberalization, deregulation, privatization, and fiscal restraint as matters of technical necessity rather than political choice. Too many treated distributional concerns as secondary, to be handled by hypothetical compensation that rarely arrived. Too many spoke in the language of constraints when the constraints themselves were institutionally created.

The authors respond by saying: economics is richer than that. It can justify wage boards, stronger unions, social insurance, tax justice, financial regulation, and democratic reforms. This is true. But it raises a question: if economics can justify so many different institutional arrangements, what democratic role should economists play?

The answer cannot be that economists should design the post-neoliberal order and persuade the public to accept it. That would reproduce a central defect of neoliberalism: the displacement of democratic judgment by expert authority. Neoliberalism was not only a set of policies. It was a style of governance that insulated major economic decisions from popular control. Central banks, trade tribunals, fiscal rules, independent regulators, and global agreements often constrained elected governments in the name of credibility and efficiency. Even when some insulation was justified, the cumulative effect was to tell citizens that the most important economic questions were not really theirs to decide.

A democratic economics after neoliberalism must reverse that presumption. Economists should be servants of democratic deliberation, not managers of society. Their job is to clarify trade-offs, identify mechanisms, test claims, expose hidden costs, and expand the range of feasible options. It is not to decide the values.

This distinction matters because “inclusive prosperity” contains unresolved moral questions. How much inequality is acceptable? What should be guaranteed as a right rather than purchased as a commodity? How should we balance consumption today against climate stability tomorrow? When should communities be allowed to restrict trade or development? How much workplace authority should employers have? Should health care be universal even if taxes rise? Should inheritance be heavily taxed? Should workers have board representation? Should carbon-intensive jobs be phased out even before replacement jobs exist? Economics can inform these debates. It cannot settle them.

The lead essay acknowledges that economics does not provide distributional weights. But the policy discussion sometimes proceeds as if better evidence will reveal win-win reforms and thereby avoid conflict. There are indeed reforms that can improve both efficiency and equality. But democratic politics cannot be built on free lunches alone. Sometimes the rich must lose so the poor can gain. Sometimes consumers may pay more so workers can earn more. Sometimes fossil fuel assets must be stranded to protect the climate. Sometimes corporate discretion must be reduced to increase worker voice. Pretending that all good policy is efficiency-enhancing risks evading the moral core of politics.

This is why the revival of power analysis in economics is so important. Yet power must be understood not only as an object of study but as a condition of policy-making. Who gets invited to the workshop? Who defines the problem? Whose data count? Which communities are treated as cases and which as authors? A policy process dominated by tenured economists, however progressive, will miss knowledge held by workers, tenants, patients, teachers, caregivers, and local officials.

Take labor markets. Economists can estimate the employment effects of minimum wages, model monopsony, and design wage boards. But workers know which scheduling practices make life impossible, which managers retaliate, how subcontracting hides responsibility, why grievance procedures fail, and what forms of voice are credible. A wage board designed without worker participation may be technically sophisticated and socially thin. The same is true in housing, health care, climate policy, and finance. Patients understand insurance denials and medical debt. Tenants understand eviction threats. Farmers understand land and risk. Communities near refineries understand pollution. Expertise must be plural.

This does not mean all knowledge is equal in every respect. Causal inference matters. Budgets matter. Incentives matter. Some policies fail despite good intentions. Economists are right to warn against magical thinking. But democratic knowledge is not the enemy of rigor. It is a source of better problem definition and institutional design.

A post-neoliberal economics should therefore be participatory in method. Before recommending a policy, economists should ask: who is affected, and how have they been consulted? What administrative agency will implement it? What forms of capture are likely? What grievance mechanisms exist? How will the policy be revised when it fails in predictable and unpredictable ways? What data will be collected, and who will own it? Can local variation be used for learning without turning vulnerable communities into experimental subjects without consent?

The authors praise empirical economics as a disciplining device against ideology. It can be. But evidence-based policy has its own politics. Randomized trials and natural experiments often privilege interventions that are small, measurable, and administratively convenient. Structural reforms—unionization, desegregation, decarbonization, democratization of firms—are harder to evaluate with clean designs. They may be undervalued in a culture that prizes identification over transformation. The risk is that progressivism becomes a portfolio of clever tweaks rather than a democratic reconstruction of institutions.

Teaching economics must change accordingly. Introductory courses still shape public understanding. If students leave Econ 101 believing that markets are natural, government is distortionary, and equity is separate from efficiency, later qualifications may never reach them. From the beginning, students should learn that markets require law, power matters, information is imperfect, externalities are pervasive, and distribution is built into institutional design. They should read economic history and competing schools of thought. They should learn humility as a professional norm.

The authors are right that economists have indispensable tools. But indispensability should not become dominance. The social sciences need a new division of labor. Economics brings models of incentives, strategic behavior, general equilibrium, and empirical evaluation. Sociology brings institutions, networks, stratification, and culture. Political science brings collective action, parties, state capacity, and representation. History brings contingency and memory. Law brings rights and institutional architecture. Philosophy brings justice. Public health brings embodiment and population well-being. The people most affected bring lived knowledge. No single discipline can govern the rest.

The challenge after neoliberalism is not only to produce better policies but to rebuild democratic trust. Trust has been damaged by elites who said that globalization would lift all boats, that financial innovation was safe, that austerity was responsible, and that workers should adapt. Progressive economists can help repair that trust, but only if they speak differently: less as priests of necessity, more as translators of possibility.

The lead essay is an important step. It opens economics to institutions, power, and moral purpose. The next step is to open economic policy-making itself to democracy. Inclusive prosperity should not mean prosperity designed for the people. It should mean prosperity designed with them.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Economists should act as servants of democracy who clarify trade-offs and expand feasible options rather than managers who decide societal values.
  2. 02Focusing primarily on 'win-win' efficiency gains or 'free lunches' risks evading the fundamental moral and distributive conflicts at the heart of politics.
  3. 03Economic policy-making must be participatory, incorporating the lived knowledge of workers and communities rather than relying solely on the technical analysis of tenured experts.
  4. 04The current professional preference for clean empirical designs and measurable interventions may inadvertently undervalue necessary large-scale structural transformations.
  5. 05Economics education must be reformed from the introductory level to teach that markets are legally constructed and that distribution is inherent to institutional design.
  6. 06To rebuild democratic trust, economists must move away from being 'priests of necessity' and instead collaborate as equals with other academic disciplines and the public.
