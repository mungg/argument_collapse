Diversified Gemini

"To make incarceration productive and reduce recidivism, the government must restore access to higher education for prisoners by reinstating Pell Grant eligibility."

To make prison truly productive, we must bring higher education back behind bars.

In 1994, Congress passed a crime bill that, among other punitive measures, stripped incarcerated individuals of their eligibility for Pell Grants. Overnight, hundreds of thriving college-in-prison programs were decimated. We have spent the last two decades paying the price for that short-sighted political posturing.

The data on correctional education is overwhelming and unequivocal. Inmates who participate in educational programs are significantly less likely to return to prison than those who do not. Education transforms a prisoner’s self-concept from that of a societal outcast to a capable, future-oriented citizen. Furthermore, it equips them with the critical thinking skills and credentials necessary to compete in an unforgiving job market upon release.

When we warehouse individuals in idle isolation, we virtually guarantee their failure upon reentry. They return to their communities with the same lack of opportunity that often contributed to their incarceration in the first place, now compounded by the stigma of a felony conviction.

State budgets are currently buckling under the immense weight of mass incarceration. If policymakers are serious about reducing prison spending, they must invest in the front-end interventions that prevent recidivism. Restoring Pell Grant access for incarcerated people is the most economically rational and morally sound step we can take. We cannot expect people to change for the better if we actively deny them the tools required for self-improvement.
