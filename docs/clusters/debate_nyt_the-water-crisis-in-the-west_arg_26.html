"Western cities must achieve water security and independence by transitioning to a resilient, closed-loop urban water management model that prioritizes conservation and local supply generation."

The future of water in the West will not be secured by hoping for rain or endlessly fighting over dwindling river flows. It will be secured in our cities, through aggressive urban conservation, mandatory recycling, and a fundamental shift in how metropolitan areas manage their resources. Cities like Los Angeles, San Diego, Phoenix, and Las Vegas must recognize that the era of importing massive amounts of water from hundreds of miles away is drawing to a close. Urban resilience is the only path forward.

First, the ornamental lawn must be completely phased out of the Western landscape. It is an ecological absurdity to cultivate thirsty, non-native turf grass in regions that receive fewer than 15 inches of rain annually. Las Vegas has set a strong example with its "cash for grass" rebate programs, paying residents to replace lawns with desert landscaping. Every western municipality should adopt and aggressively fund similar mandates. Municipal codes must be updated to explicitly ban ornamental turf in new developments and commercial properties.

However, conservation alone is insufficient; we must fundamentally change our water supply portfolio. The psychological barrier to wastewater recycling—often derisively called "toilet to tap"—must be broken. Modern reverse osmosis and advanced oxidation technologies can purify municipal wastewater to a standard that is cleaner than most bottled water. Orange County has already proven this with its Groundwater Replenishment System, which purifies millions of gallons of wastewater daily to recharge local aquifers. Large-scale potable reuse should become the baseline expectation for every major western city, effectively creating a closed-loop system that drastically reduces the need for imported surface water.

In coastal areas, desalination must also be part of the portfolio. While historically expensive and energy-intensive, desalination technology is improving. The Carlsbad desalination plant currently under construction in San Diego County represents a necessary step toward drought-proof water independence. If paired with renewable energy sources, desalination can provide a reliable baseline supply that is entirely immune to the unpredictable snowpack of the Sierra Nevada or the Rockies.

Sharing the water means cities must stop acting like parasites on the distant watersheds of the Colorado and Sacramento rivers. By capturing stormwater, recycling wastewater, tearing out turf, and developing local supplies, our metropolitan areas can decouple their economic growth from the natural water cycle, leaving more water in the rivers for agriculture and the environment.
