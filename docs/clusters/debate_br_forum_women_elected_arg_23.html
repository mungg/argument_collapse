"The proposal to import gender quotas to the United States is impractical because such measures are fundamentally incompatible with the country's single-member, winner-take-all electoral system."

Jennifer Piscopo’s elegant history of passive suffrage and gender quotas is a welcome corrective to the tired “women don’t run” mantra. She rightly insists that structural barriers, not individual deficits, explain women’s underrepresentation. But her prescription—that the United States should import positive action for women candidates—overlooks a more basic structural failure that makes most quota designs nearly impossible to transplant: the United States uses single-member, winner-take-all districts, while virtually every country that has successfully implemented legislative gender quotas relies on proportional representation.

The mechanics are straightforward. In a closed-list proportional system, a party can simply place women in winnable positions on its list, and the ballot’s design guarantees that if the party hits a certain vote threshold, a predictable number of women will be seated. That is why Argentina’s 1991 quota law rapidly boosted women’s representation from under 10 percent to over 30 percent: parties complied by slotting women into list slots, and voters had no way to reject them individually. The same logic operates in Belgium, Spain, Costa Rica, and Senegal. In the United States, however, every congressional district elects a single person. There is no list to reorder. A party rule requiring that 50 percent of candidates be women would mean little if women are overwhelmingly nominated in unwinnable districts against entrenched incumbents, while men are cleared for the safe seats. You can mandate candidate slates, but you cannot mandate electoral outcomes without turning primaries on their head.

Piscopo gestures at the challenge, noting that parties do not control ballot access here. But the problem runs deeper. Even if a party aggressively recruited women and cleared primary fields for them—something the DNC has no formal power to do—those women would still have to win single-member, gerrymandered districts, many of which were drawn by male incumbents to protect themselves. The 2018 “blue wave” brought a record number of Democratic women to Congress, but they disproportionately won in suburban districts previously held by moderate Republican men, while men continued to dominate safe urban and rural seats. The ambition gap may be closing, but the structural gatekeeping of district geography remains.

Proponents of quotas sometimes point to the British Labour Party’s use of all-women shortlists as a counterexample. The United Kingdom also uses single-member districts, yet Labour sharply increased its proportion of women MPs by requiring that in certain seats, only women could be considered as the party’s candidate. But this approach depends on a highly centralized party with the authority to impose such shortlists on local constituency associations, a power that neither the Democratic nor Republican Party possesses. American parties are weak, porous, and decentralized by design; any attempt to enforce candidate quotas from the top would splinter them and trigger endless litigation over the associational rights that the Spanish Constitutional Court, as Piscopo notes, ultimately limited in the name of substantive equality—but which the U.S. Supreme Court might well protect, as it did by striking down California’s blanket primary system.

The more honest conversation, then, is not about quotas but about electoral system design. If we truly believe that legislatures should mirror the population, we should be debating the Fair Representation Act, which would establish multi-member districts with ranked-choice voting for the House. Such a system would allow women and candidates of color to campaign on their strengths without being confined to a single winner-takes-all geography. It would also create incentives for parties to put forward diverse slates, because voters could rank multiple candidates and a party would want to appeal to various demographic constituencies. Not coincidentally, multi-member districts have been correlated with higher levels of women’s representation in U.S. state legislatures historically, before they were largely eliminated.

Even beyond structural hurdles, Piscopo’s faith in party-level positive action underestimates the role of money. Candidate-centered campaigns mean that women aspirants must not only be recruited, but must individually raise millions of dollars from donor networks still dominated by men. Waiving filing fees is a trivial fix when the cost of a competitive House race runs into the millions. And while EMILY’s List has demonstrated that early funding can level the playing field, it also reveals that women’s candidacies depend on a parallel, private-sector welfare system—a kind of privatized quota that reinforces exactly the negative-rights framework Piscopo critiques. The state remains absent.

The centennial should indeed be an occasion for rethinking women’s political citizenship, but the lesson from Latin America is not simply that quotas work; it is that quotas work when they are embedded in an electoral system that makes representation a collective outcome. Until American reformers take proportional representation seriously, the most well-intentioned calls for positive action will crash against the single-member district wall. The right to be elected remains, for the vast majority of women, the right to beat the odds, not to share power.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Unlike proportional representation systems where parties can guarantee outcomes by placing women on candidate lists, the U.S. system makes it impossible to mandate results without controlling individual district elections.
  2. 02The decentralized and porous nature of American political parties prevents them from enforcing the kind of centralized candidate shortlists used successfully in other single-member district systems like the United Kingdom.
  3. 03The U.S. Supreme Court is more likely to protect the associational rights of political parties and voters than to uphold the principle of substantive equality used to justify quotas in other nations.
  4. 04Meaningful progress in women's representation requires a shift toward multi-member districts and ranked-choice voting rather than the mere adoption of candidate quotas.
  5. 05The candidate-centered nature of U.S. elections and the necessity of massive private fundraising create financial barriers that simple policy fixes like waiving filing fees cannot overcome.
