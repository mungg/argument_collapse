"Merve Emre’s claim that 'all reproduction is assisted' is a conceptual oversimplification that dangerously conflates routine diagnostic tools with invasive medical interventions."

I find myself skeptical of Merve Emre's central argument, despite appreciating its rhetorical power. The claim that "all reproduction is assisted" is clever but ultimately confusing—it conflates very different kinds of assistance under a single conceptual umbrella.

Consider what counts as "assistance" in Emre's account. A pregnancy test is assistance because it detects hCG levels through manufactured antibodies. An ultrasound is assistance because it images the fetus. But these forms of assistance are not equivalent to egg freezing, IVF, or surrogacy, either in their technological complexity, their costs, or their relationship to the reproductive body.

The distinction matters because Emre's argument risks normalizing assisted reproductive technology by showing that "all reproduction" involves some technology. This move may actually strengthen the hand of those who want to expand ART without adequate regulatory attention to its risks and costs.

The essay's case studies reveal the problem. S's egg freezing involves months of hormone injections, invasive procedures, and significant risks including ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome. B's IVF includes surgical retrieval under anesthesia, preimplantation genetic screening, and the emotional toll of multiple cycles. These are not equivalent to taking a pregnancy test or getting an ultrasound. They are major medical interventions with significant physical and psychological impacts.

Emre treats the similarity between these experiences as the point—all reproduction involves technology, so no form is more "natural" or legitimate than another. But this argument obscures the differences between interventions that merely observe reproduction and those that fundamentally transform it.

There's also a methodological problem with the essay's case studies. Emre's subjects are all white or light-skinned women (with the exception of cárdenas, who appears briefly), all college-educated, most working in knowledge industries. This profile is not representative of women who use assisted reproduction. Poor women, women of color, and women without employer health coverage also use ART—but their experiences are absent from the essay.

This selectivity matters because the essay's argument depends on showing how "the natural" functions as an ideology. But if the essay only examines women for whom "natural reproduction" was never an option—because of their class position, their sexuality, their relationship status—then it can't show how the ideology operates for those who do have access to "natural" reproduction.

Emre might respond that this is precisely the point: the natural is an ideology of privileged women, one that excludes everyone else. But this response would require engaging more seriously with the experiences of poor women and women of color, which the essay doesn't do.

The essay's invocation of Firestone is complicated by these concerns. Firestone's techno-enthusiasm was always a product of her position as a middle-class woman imagining technological liberation from domestic labor. Whether such liberation is available to working-class women and women of color is a different question, one that Emre's essay doesn't adequately address.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The essay fails to distinguish between technologies that merely observe reproduction, like pregnancy tests, and those that fundamentally transform the body, like IVF.
  2. 02Normalizing assisted reproductive technology (ART) by labeling all reproduction as 'assisted' may inadvertently discourage necessary regulatory scrutiny of the risks and costs associated with major medical interventions.
  3. 03The argument relies on a narrow demographic of subjects—primarily educated, middle-class women—which ignores how class and race dictate access to and experiences of reproductive technology.
  4. 04By focusing only on those for whom 'natural' reproduction was never an option, the essay fails to adequately demonstrate how the ideology of 'the natural' actually operates across broader society.
  5. 05The essay adopts Shulamith Firestone’s techno-utopianism without addressing whether such technological liberation is actually accessible or beneficial to working-class women and women of color.
Diversified MiniMax

"Merve Emre’s claim that 'all reproduction is assisted' is analytically unstable because it relies on an overly broad definition of technology that fails to distinguish between observational and transformative interventions."

I want to engage with Merve Emre's essay on its own philosophical terms, setting aside for the moment the political implications that others have emphasized. The question of whether "all reproduction is assisted" is, I think, more complicated than Emre allows.

Emre's argument is that technology mediates all reproduction: pregnancy tests, ultrasounds, prenatal screening, hospital births. These interventions are so ubiquitous that distinguishing "natural" from "assisted" reproduction makes little sense. Both involve technology; the difference is only which technologies and who controls them.

This argument is philosophically coherent, but it depends on a very broad concept of technology. If we define technology broadly enough—any tool or technique for accomplishing a goal—then almost all human activity is technological, and the distinction between natural and artificial dissolves entirely. But this conceptual move sacrifices precision for rhetorical effect.

A more rigorous analysis would distinguish between technologies that merely observe or monitor reproduction and technologies that fundamentally transform it. Pregnancy tests and ultrasounds are observational: they tell us about the state of a pregnancy without changing it. IVF and egg freezing are transformative: they alter the reproductive process itself, bypassing or modifying biological steps.

This distinction matters because the risks, costs, and ethical implications differ dramatically. Observational technologies are relatively low-risk and widely accessible, at least in wealthy countries. Transformative technologies involve significant medical intervention, substantial costs, and complex ethical questions about the status of embryos and the commodification of reproductive labor.

Emre's case studies focus almost exclusively on transformative technologies—egg freezing, IVF, IUI. The essay doesn't consider the more common forms of technological mediation that most pregnant people experience: the standard prenatal tests, the routine ultrasounds, the hospital-based births that are the norm in much of the world. These experiences are less dramatic but more universal.

The philosophical point is that "assisted reproduction" as a category includes both observational and transformative technologies, and we need to be precise about which we're discussing. Emre's essay conflates these different types, using examples of transformative technology to make claims about all technological mediation.

This imprecision creates rhetorical effects but analytical confusion. If "assisted reproduction" means any technologically mediated reproduction, then it includes virtually all reproduction in contemporary societies. But if it means specifically the transformative technologies of IVF and egg freezing, then it applies to a much smaller population with very different experiences.

Emre seems to want both moves simultaneously: universalizing the concept to show that "all reproduction is assisted" while focusing on specific populations whose experiences illustrate particular problems. This combination produces an essay that is intellectually stimulating but analytically unstable.

A more careful approach would specify which claims apply to which technologies and which populations. We might conclude that some forms of reproduction are more technologically mediated than others, that this variation has political implications, and that we should work toward equitable access to the technologies that people need. This conclusion would be less striking than "all reproduction is assisted" but more useful for political analysis.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Defining technology so broadly that it encompasses all human activity sacrifices the conceptual precision necessary to distinguish between natural and artificial processes.
  2. 02A rigorous philosophical analysis must differentiate between observational technologies that monitor pregnancy and transformative technologies that fundamentally alter biological steps.
  3. 03The risks, costs, and ethical implications of transformative technologies like IVF are significantly more complex than those of routine observational tools like ultrasounds.
  4. 04Emre’s essay suffers from a logical conflation, using the dramatic experiences of a small population using transformative tech to make universal claims about all reproductive experiences.
  5. 05A more useful political and analytical framework would acknowledge that reproduction exists on a spectrum of technological mediation rather than declaring all forms identical.
