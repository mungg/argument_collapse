What the Old Words Were Doing

Merve Emre's essay aims to retire a word — "natural" — that she believes has done feminism more harm than good. The retirement is intended both as critique of conservative ideology and as opening for new reproductive arrangements. I want to make the case, as gently as I can, that the word was doing some work the new vocabulary will not do as well, and that the cost of retiring it is higher than Emre's essay suggests.

Begin with what the word actually marks. "Natural" in the reproductive context is not, despite Emre's framing, primarily an ideological gloss on women's suffering. It is a folk-anthropological term that marks continuity with the biological life of the species. It says: this body is a mammal, this child grew the way mammal children grow, this relationship is the relationship that has obtained between gestating bodies and their offspring across evolutionary time. The word is doing the work that "tradition" does in other domains — naming the shape of practice that obtains before any deliberate intervention. Like "tradition," "natural" is sometimes used to defend bad practices. Like "tradition," it is also sometimes used to mark something genuinely valuable that deliberate intervention may unbuild.

Emre's reading of the word as essentially reactionary depends on her selection of examples. The natural-birth Instagram blogger reciting Adrienne Rich; the prenatal supplement label promising Eden; the manufacturer of expensive birthing tubs — these are easy targets, and they deserve mockery. But they are not, in fact, the people for whom the word "natural" is doing serious work. The people I have in mind are: women who have given birth several times across several decades and who can tell you that some of the routine medical interventions they were subjected to were unnecessary, painful, and bad for them and their children; midwives in continuous traditions of practice who carry knowledge that the obstetrical profession spent the twentieth century actively suppressing; mothers in indigenous and immigrant communities whose embodied knowledge of pregnancy, labor, and lactation has been treated as superstition by clinicians who could not match it; and, frankly, ordinary women who notice that the institutional management of their bodies seems organized for institutional convenience rather than their own. When these people invoke "natural," they are not, mostly, signing on to the School of Great Experience. They are pushing back against a medical apparatus whose presumptions about their bodies have been wrong often enough to warrant skepticism.

The history of obstetrics in the twentieth century vindicates a great deal of this skepticism. Twilight sleep, the routine use of forceps, the over-resort to episiotomy, the dorsal lithotomy position adopted for surgeon convenience, the rates of unnecessary C-section, the historical exclusion of fathers and other support figures from delivery rooms, the management of birth as a sequence of timed interventions on a labor curve invented by a single physician with a small dataset — all of this was done in the name of modern medicine and over the objection of women who said something was wrong. The pushback against these practices was, in significant part, articulated in the vocabulary of the natural. That vocabulary was instrumentally productive. It built midwifery into hospitals, returned doulas to the standard birthing team, banned routine episiotomy in many institutions, and produced clinical research showing that several "naturalist" preferences — upright labor positions, intermittent rather than continuous fetal monitoring, skin-to-skin contact after birth, immediate breastfeeding — improved outcomes by the medical profession's own metrics.

Emre's essay treats "breast is best" as an example of natural ideology that "occludes the many individuals who carry or care for children." This is a specific and contested claim. The slogan was developed in response to a documented history of aggressive infant-formula marketing — most notoriously by Nestlé in the Global South — that contributed to infant deaths from contaminated water used to mix formula in conditions where breastfeeding would have been safer. The slogan emerged from a real public-health movement with real evidentiary support, and its appropriation by judgmental middle-class mommy culture in the United States is a distortion of its purpose, not its purpose. To collapse the slogan with the lifestyle culture it has been twisted into is to give the lifestyle culture the last word. A more discerning critique would distinguish the public-health insight from its bourgeois moralization.

The same holds for several other items on Emre's list. Fertility-tracking apps are sometimes promoted as natural alternatives to hormonal contraception, with all the moralizing baggage that implies. They are also, at the basic level, just information — information about a body that women have historically had limited access to, that the medical profession has historically managed on their behalf, and that they are now able to gather and interpret themselves. Hormonal accounts of maternal bonding can be deployed to coerce reluctant mothers; they can also be empirically correct accounts of postpartum neuroendocrinology, of considerable interest to women who experience the changes and want a name for them. The blanket dismissal of these accounts as ideology drops information that some women want.

What I am asking for is a sharper instrument than Emre's essay provides. The instrument should be able to distinguish: (a) the use of "natural" to coerce, shame, or exclude — bad, and to be opposed; (b) the use of "natural" to defend valuable embodied practice against institutional encroachment — defensible, and to be supported; (c) the use of "natural" as a marketing category attached to overpriced products — silly, and to be ignored; (d) the use of "natural" as an honest descriptor of continuity with mammalian and human-historical reproductive life — neither good nor bad in itself, but doing real semantic work that no other word in current English does as well.

Without that instrument, the call to retire the word lands as a call to abandon the second and fourth uses along with the first and third. The cost is that the political case against unnecessary medicalization, against the dismissal of embodied knowledge, and against the corporate capture of reproductive options loses one of its principal vocabularies. The case can be made in other words, of course. But the other words are not, yet, doing the work.

The deeper point is this. Emre's essay tilts, in spite of its careful narrative method, toward a particular orientation: the assumption that more technological mediation of reproduction is, on net, the direction of feminist progress. I am skeptical of that assumption, not because I am committed to nature-mysticism but because I have read too much medical history to believe that technological mediation is, by default, in patients' interests. It is sometimes in their interests. It is sometimes not. The discernment between the two requires exactly the kind of body-grounded skepticism that has, in the twentieth-century American context, often been expressed in the language of the natural. Retire the word and you are likely to retire the skepticism with it. That would be a loss.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The term 'natural' functions as a folk-anthropological marker of continuity with biological and evolutionary history that other vocabularies cannot easily replace.
  2. 02The vocabulary of the 'natural' has been instrumentally productive in reforming modern obstetrics, leading to better clinical outcomes and more humane birthing practices.
  3. 03Emre’s critique unfairly conflates legitimate public health insights and pushback against corporate exploitation with superficial middle-class lifestyle consumerism.
  4. 04A blanket dismissal of the 'natural' risks abandoning the necessary skepticism required to evaluate whether technological interventions are actually in a patient's best interest.
  5. 05The word 'natural' provides a necessary semantic framework for women to reclaim authority over their own bodies from institutional and corporate encroachment.

"While all reproduction is indeed assisted, the concept of 'the natural' should not be dismissed as a reactionary fiction because it can represent a valid feminist desire for bodily autonomy and a resistance to unnecessary medicalization."

In Defense of the Natural (Sort Of)

Merve Emre's essay is a beautifully constructed argument, and it earns its central provocation: yes, all reproduction is assisted, and the unmarked category of "natural" childbirth lets a great deal of ideology smuggle itself in under the cover of biology. I agree with much of her diagnosis. I want to push back, though, on the rhetorical move that runs through the piece — the suggestion that "the natural" is essentially a reactionary fiction, useful mostly as a stalking horse for sexism, racism, and heteronormativity. That move is too clean. It throws away something worth keeping.

Consider what people often mean when they reach for the word "natural" in this context. Sometimes they do mean the moralizing, lifestyle-brand version Emre rightly mocks: the $500 hypnobirthing tutorial, the Edenic prenatal supplement, the Instagram doula in a clean white kitchen. But often they mean something more modest and defensible: a preference for interventions that have been used long enough to be well understood, a wariness of unnecessary medicalization, a desire for continuity with one's body, one's animal life, one's mammalian inheritance. These preferences are not irrational and they are not, intrinsically, anti-feminist. The women who fought to bring midwives back into American hospitals in the 1970s, who exposed the routine over-prescription of episiotomies, who pushed back against unnecessary C-sections — they were not Rousseauean fantasists. They were doing concrete, hard-won feminist work against a medical establishment that had treated childbirth as something to be sedated through.

Emre treats Firestone's "shitting a pumpkin" passage as a kind of foundational truth-telling. It is funny and bracing. But it is also, as a description of childbirth, partial and tendentious. Plenty of women experience labor as painful, boring, traumatic, alienating; plenty of others experience it as something else — intense, transformative, even, yes, meaningful — and these are not all dupes of the School of Great Experience. To grant that some women have been ideologically coerced into glorifying their suffering is not to grant that any positive valuation of embodied gestation is false consciousness. Firestone's contempt for the body has its own ideological history, and it is not obviously more emancipatory than the natural-childbirth pieties she ridicules.

There is also a quieter empirical problem. Emre's frame is "the natural is invisible privilege." But the technologies she profiles are not free of their own asymmetric distributions and harms. Egg freezing is a procedure with modest live-birth yields per thawed egg, performed on healthy women who undergo hormonal hyperstimulation and surgical retrieval. IVF treatments increase the rate of multiple pregnancies, low birthweight, and OHSS — which Emre's interviewee B nearly suffered. Preimplantation genetic screening, which Emre describes with notable tenderness ("I have two girls and a boy"), is an instrument that decides which embryos live, on what genetic criteria, with what consequences for our collective tolerance of disability. To treat these procedures simply as liberatory technologies awaiting wider distribution is to repeat Firestone's optimism rather than to interrogate it.

I want to suggest a friendly amendment. The opposition is not natural vs. assisted; Emre is right that this is a false dichotomy. But the opposition that actually does feminist work is something more like: which assistances do we want, on what terms, for whom, accountable to what? A midwife is an assistance. So is a doula, a birthing pool, a doctor, an epidural, an IUI catheter, a frozen embryo, a paid maternity leave, a co-parent, a state. Some of these assistances are continuous with longstanding human practice; some are very new. Some are administered by communities and kin; some by corporations and clinics. To say "all reproduction is assisted" is true but flattening. The political question is what kinds of assistance we are building, and Emre's strongest examples — N and K, B, micha cárdenas — are not really arguments for more technology so much as arguments for more solidarity, more capacious kin networks, less hostile clinics, more honest doctors. The technology is incidental; the relationships are not.

This matters because the "natural" critique, pushed too hard, has a way of arriving at conclusions that are not actually feminist gains. If the body is just one more substrate to be hacked, why not corporate egg freezing as a perk? Why not gestational surrogacy at scale in countries where women are cheap? Why not commercial gamete markets that disproportionately recruit poor women as donors and wealthy women as buyers? The xenofeminist answer is: universalize access, distribute the technologies more equitably. But the technologies as currently configured carry an economic and ideological freight that more equitable distribution doesn't simply dissolve. They were developed inside an infertility industry that treats women's bodies as raw material, and they reinforce a particular form of life — the late-twentieth-century professional career structured around postponed reproduction — that itself deserves criticism.

This is why I think the most important sentence in Emre's essay is not the title but the line near the end, quoting Dorothy Roberts: reproductive justice is not just the right not to have a child but the right to have children and raise them "with dignity in safe, healthy, and supportive environments." Roberts's framework explicitly refuses the choice between celebrating technology and celebrating nature. It says: the question is whether the material conditions of reproduction — housing, healthcare, paid leave, freedom from violence, freedom from coercive sterilization, freedom from family separation — make raising children possible. From that vantage, the corporate egg freezing perk and the natural-birth Instagram empire are mirror-image symptoms of the same underlying failure. Both are private workarounds for the absence of a society that supports parents.

I do not want to defend the "School of Great Experience." Firestone's mockery still bites, and it should. But I do want to defend a more modest claim: that human beings are mammals, that gestation and birth and lactation are mammalian capacities, that wanting to know and inhabit those capacities — when they are available to you, when you want them — is not, in itself, ideological capture. The natural is a contested word with a long history of misuse, but the body is real, and a feminism that cedes the body to its enemies, or that treats the body as merely a problem to be solved by better engineering, is a feminism with a thin grip on most women's lives.

The right horizon, then, is neither Firestone's ectogenesis nor the lifestyle-natural's aestheticized return to Eden. It is the unglamorous infrastructure of mutual aid Emre's own narratives keep gesturing toward: the friends who drove B home from her retrieval, the queer TTC Facebook group, the supervising ob-gyn who held B's hand, the trans women who taught each other to bank sperm. That is the assistance worth scaling.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The preference for 'natural' methods often reflects a defensible wariness of a medical establishment that has historically over-medicalized and sedated women during childbirth.
  2. 02Positive valuations of embodied gestation and labor are not necessarily products of ideological coercion or 'false consciousness.'
  3. 03Treating reproductive technologies as purely liberatory ignores the physical risks, modest success rates, and ethical complications inherent in procedures like egg freezing and IVF.
  4. 04The 'natural vs. assisted' dichotomy is flattening; the real feminist task is determining which forms of assistance—whether communal, medical, or state-led—are accountable to women's needs.
  5. 05A techno-utopian focus on 'hacking' the body can inadvertently validate exploitative corporate practices and the commodification of women's reproductive labor.
  6. 06True reproductive justice depends more on the material and social infrastructure of mutual aid and solidarity than on the advancement or distribution of technology itself.
  7. 07A feminism that treats the mammalian realities of the body as merely a problem to be solved by engineering loses touch with the lived experiences of most women.
