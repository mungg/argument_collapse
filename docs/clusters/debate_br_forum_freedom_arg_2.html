Vanilla GPT

"While reclaiming freedom from the right requires building institutions of collective solidarity, a successful left project must also protect individual agency by checking arbitrary power within both private and public institutions."

Aziz Rana is right that the American language of freedom has been warped by conquest, racial exclusion, and empire—and right, too, that the left should not surrender the word to those who define it as tax cuts, gun maximalism, and the right to dominate weaker people. But reclaiming freedom requires more than substituting solidarity for individualism. It also requires taking seriously why many Americans hear progressive invocations of collective power as potential threats to their own agency. A democratic left freedom must be capacious enough to include not only freedom through shared institutions, but freedom from arbitrary power wherever it appears: in markets, in police departments, in employers, in borders, but also in bureaucracies, parties, schools, unions, and states.

Rana’s central historical claim is compelling. The United States has repeatedly offered real freedoms to some by denying them to others. White settlers experienced landownership as independence because Indigenous nations were dispossessed. White workers could imagine themselves as self-made because racialized labor and imperial markets subsidized their security. Cold War prosperity helped many Americans identify private employment, employer-based benefits, and consumer choice with freedom, even as U.S. power constrained self-determination abroad. This history helps explain why the right’s freedom talk has often meant a shield for insiders and a sword for outsiders.

Yet the same history also warns against making “collective self-rule” do too much work on its own. Majorities can dominate. Solidarities can exclude. Institutions built in the name of the people can become insulated from the people. The New Deal, which Rana rightly treats as a moment when freedom and democracy were joined more robustly, also entrenched racial exclusions in agricultural and domestic labor, accommodated segregationist power, and built welfare and labor regimes that often depended on male breadwinner assumptions. Industrial unions were schools of democracy for millions, but some were also hostile to women, Black workers, immigrants, and internal dissent. None of this refutes Rana’s argument. It deepens it: freedom requires institutions of collective power, but also rights, checks, contestation, and exit.

That point matters because the contemporary left’s difficulty with freedom is not only institutional weakness. It is also a credibility problem about coercion. Many Americans experience unfreedom through employers, landlords, medical debt, student loans, algorithmic scheduling, and the fear of losing health insurance with a job. But they also experience unfreedom when public systems seem opaque, punitive, or contemptuous. A parent fighting a school bureaucracy for a disabled child’s services, a small contractor navigating licensing rules written for larger firms, a tenant trapped in a public housing authority’s indifference, or a dissident student worried about ideological conformity may not think “solidarity” captures the remedy they need. They may want voice, but they also want limits on power.

The right exploits these anxieties cynically, often redirecting anger away from corporate domination and toward migrants, teachers, trans people, or racial justice protesters. But the anxieties are not all manufactured. During the pandemic, for example, Americans saw both the necessity of collective action and the costs of institutional mistrust. Public health measures saved lives; they also revealed how unevenly burdens were distributed, how poorly authorities sometimes communicated uncertainty, and how little say many families and workers felt they had in decisions that transformed daily life. A left freedom politics that treats all resistance to public authority as selfish libertarianism will fail. The better answer is to democratize authority, make it accountable, and be honest about tradeoffs.

This is why messaging cannot be so neatly separated from institution building. Rana is surely right that slogans alone cannot create lived experiences of collective agency. But political language is one of the ways people decide which institutions are worth building and which sacrifices are legitimate. The civil rights movement did not merely construct organizations; it named the country’s contradictions in biblical, constitutional, and democratic terms. Labor movements did not only bargain; they taught workers to see the eight-hour day, the weekend, and workplace safety as freedom rather than favors. Feminists changed law and workplaces partly by changing the meaning of privacy, equality, bodily autonomy, and dependence. Words do not substitute for power, but they organize perception. They make some injuries intolerable and some futures imaginable.

A persuasive left account of freedom should therefore speak in several registers at once. It should insist that nobody is free if illness means bankruptcy, if a boss can fire workers for organizing, if police can kill with impunity, if pregnancy is compelled by the state, or if citizenship status makes a person permanently exploitable. It should also insist that people are not free when decisions affecting their neighborhoods, schools, and workplaces are made by distant authorities they cannot influence. And it should defend civil liberties without embarrassment: speech, association, due process, religious liberty, privacy, and protection against arbitrary punishment are not bourgeois distractions from material justice. They are tools the powerless have repeatedly used to organize, dissent, and survive.

Rana’s anti-imperial emphasis is indispensable, especially after Iraq and Afghanistan, and amid the horror in Gaza after October 7. A country habituated to treating other peoples’ self-rule as secondary will struggle to cultivate democratic humility at home. The scale of U.S. military spending also plainly crowds out social investment. Still, an anti-imperial freedom politics must avoid becoming a reflexive inversion of Washington’s preferences. Ukrainians resisting Russian invasion, Taiwanese people facing coercion from Beijing, Iranian women confronting theocratic repression, and Palestinians demanding equal dignity all speak languages of freedom. The question is not whether American power is good or bad in the abstract. It is how to support self-determination without domination, how to use restraint without indifference, and how to distinguish solidarity from geopolitical campism.

The most promising part of Rana’s essay is his call to rebuild everyday sites of agency. Unions matter not only because they raise wages, but because they let people practice democracy where they spend much of their lives. Tenant unions, cooperatives, community land trusts, participatory budgeting, public banking, school boards that are genuinely representative, and immigrant worker centers can all make freedom tangible. So can universal programs—health care, child care, paid leave, housing supports—that reduce dependence on employers and intimate partners. The point is not to replace individual freedom with collective freedom, but to show that secure individuality often depends on collective provision.

The left should reclaim freedom, then, but not by denying the American fear of domination. It should vindicate that fear and redirect it. The problem is not that Americans care too much about being pushed around. The problem is that our politics has taught many to see domination only when government acts, and not when capital, inherited hierarchy, racial violence, or imperial power does. A better freedom would make people less governable by bosses, bigots, monopolies, police, creditors, and unaccountable officials alike. It would be solidaristic because domination is shared, and pluralist because power is dangerous even when exercised in the name of justice. That freedom is harder to build than to brand. But it is also harder for the right to counterfeit.

Diversified GPT

"To reclaim freedom as a solidaristic practice, the left must look beyond national policy and messaging to revitalize the 'middle layer' of civic life—the local associations and institutions where Americans can practically experience and learn collective agency."

Aziz Rana is right that freedom cannot be reclaimed by messaging alone. Where I would push his argument is toward the middle layer of American life: the associations, congregations, cooperatives, clubs, libraries, mutual aid societies, parent groups, neighborhood councils, ethnic organizations, and local institutions that sit between the isolated individual and the national state. If Americans lack an everyday experience of collective agency, the problem is not only weak unions or antidemocratic national structures. It is also the thinning of civic life.

Freedom as self-rule requires practice. People do not become democratic citizens only by voting every few years, nor by consuming national political media, nor by expressing opinions online. They become citizens by running meetings, arguing over budgets, caring for shared spaces, electing officers, organizing food drives, bargaining with city agencies, creating rituals, teaching children, welcoming newcomers, and resolving conflicts with people they cannot simply block. These mundane activities are not distractions from politics. They are the texture of democratic freedom.

Rana’s account of settler freedom emphasizes land, empire, race, and markets. That history is essential. But another part of the American story is associational abundance. Many excluded communities built their own institutions because dominant institutions denied them equal membership. Black churches, mutual aid societies, women’s clubs, immigrant lodges, labor halls, settlement houses, freedom schools, and cooperative ventures did more than provide services. They cultivated leadership and solidarity. They made freedom visible in daily life. The civil rights movement did not emerge from moral insight alone; it emerged from dense networks of churches, colleges, local NAACP branches, unions, youth groups, and family ties.

The erosion of such institutions helps explain why freedom now so often appears as personal autonomy or resentment. If your main experiences of collective life are bureaucracy, employment discipline, consumer platforms, and national partisan conflict, then collective power will feel either remote or threatening. If the homeowners’ association is the most immediate form of local governance, freedom may come to mean keeping others out. If online spaces replace physical associations, political identity may become performance without responsibility. If congregations decline and unions shrink, people lose places where they can practice solidarity across difference.

This civic erosion has many causes: suburbanization, long work hours, television and digital entertainment, economic precarity, professionalized nonprofits, distrust of institutions, geographic mobility, and the privatization of leisure. Neoliberalism matters, as Rana says, but the remedy cannot be only national policy. We need civic reconstruction.

What would that look like? First, public libraries should be understood as freedom institutions. They provide access to knowledge, meeting space, internet, children’s programming, job assistance, and a rare noncommercial public environment. Attacks on libraries and librarians are attacks on democratic life. A country serious about freedom would fund libraries as civic infrastructure and defend them against censorship.

Second, schools should be community institutions, not merely testing sites or culture-war battlegrounds. Public schools are among the few places where families of different backgrounds encounter one another through a shared institution. They can teach democratic habits through student councils, debate, service learning, parent participation, and curriculum that treats history honestly. But schools cannot do this if they are underfunded, segregated, or governed by panic. The fight over education should not be framed only as parental freedom versus bureaucratic authority. It should ask how schools can become places of shared self-rule while protecting vulnerable students from majoritarian abuse.

Third, local journalism matters. Without reliable information about city councils, school boards, courts, employers, landlords, police, and developers, citizens cannot govern. The collapse of local news has nationalized attention and empowered rumor. People know every outrage in Washington but not who is shaping zoning decisions in their town. Public or nonprofit support for local journalism should be part of a freedom agenda.

Fourth, cooperatives and mutual aid deserve more than romantic praise. Worker cooperatives, credit unions, food co-ops, community land trusts, and mutual aid networks can give people direct experience with economic democracy. They are not substitutes for unions or welfare states; they are complements. A community land trust, for example, can protect housing affordability while giving residents a say. A credit union can embed finance in local accountability. These institutions make interdependence practical.

Fifth, religious and moral communities should not be written off. Many progressives are understandably wary of conservative religion’s role in restricting abortion, LGBTQ rights, and education. But religious institutions have also powered abolition, civil rights, sanctuary work, labor organizing, and antiwar activism. A left freedom politics that cannot speak to congregational life will miss millions of Americans who learn solidarity through faith. The goal should not be to sacralize politics but to welcome institutions that cultivate service, discipline, and moral imagination while firmly defending pluralism and equal rights.

Sixth, national service in a democratic, nonmilitary form deserves reconsideration. Not compulsory militarism, and not a cheap labor scheme, but well-paid opportunities for young people and others to work on climate resilience, elder care, tutoring, public health, conservation, and infrastructure. Such programs could mix people across region and class while producing tangible public goods. Freedom is strengthened when citizens can point to something they built together.

Rana might respond that all this risks nostalgia for civil society while ignoring structural power. That is a fair warning. Civic associations can be exclusionary, parochial, racist, patriarchal, or captured by local elites. The small town is not automatically democratic. Neighborhood control can become NIMBYism. Parent groups can become vehicles for censorship. Churches can abuse authority. Nonprofits can depoliticize struggle. Associations need rights, equality norms, and broader public frameworks.

But the answer to bad civil society is better civil society, not reliance on national politics alone. The left sometimes speaks as if power exists only in the state and the economy. Yet culture is produced in repeated association. Trust is built through doing. People who have successfully organized a tenant association or restored a park may be more open to unions, public goods, and democratic reform because they have felt collective agency in their hands.

Rana’s anti-imperial point also has a civic dimension. Empire weakens local democracy by directing imagination outward toward enemies and upward toward commanders. It teaches citizens to identify with national power rather than local responsibility. A less militarized country could invest not only in social programs but in civic capacity: disaster preparedness, climate adaptation, public health corps, refugee welcome networks, and city-to-city international partnerships based on reciprocity rather than dominance.

The right has been effective at building freedom institutions of its own: gun clubs, homeschooling networks, churches, legal organizations, talk radio audiences, business associations, and local activist groups. Progressives sometimes mock these spaces, but they help produce a lived meaning of freedom. If the left wants a different freedom, it needs places where people can belong before they agree on everything, act before they master the theory, and experience obligation as empowering rather than punitive.

Freedom is learned in public. Not only in the grand public of Congress and courts, but in the small publics where voices shake, votes are counted, coffee is poured, minutes are taken, and neighbors decide what to do next. Rana’s call for institution building is right. Let us make sure we build at human scale too.
