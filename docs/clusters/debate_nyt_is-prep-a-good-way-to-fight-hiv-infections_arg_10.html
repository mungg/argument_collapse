Diversified Gemini

"Promoting PrEP as a primary public health strategy is flawed because it medicalizes a complex social crisis and ignores the systemic root causes of H.I.V. transmission."

By elevating PrEP to the center of our H.I.V. prevention strategy, the CDC is falling into the trap of medicalizing a complex social problem. We are increasingly looking to a daily pill to solve a crisis driven by poverty, homelessness, lack of education, and pervasive stigma.

H.I.V. does not exist in a vacuum. The populations most at risk—including marginalized youth, injection drug users, and sex workers—are often dealing with compounding traumas. When public health agencies funnel millions of dollars into purchasing pharmaceuticals from a highly profitable corporation like Gilead, they are diverting resources away from the holistic, community-based interventions that actually address the root causes of risk.

Instead of asking whether we should put hundreds of thousands of healthy people on a daily antiretroviral drug, we should be asking why we aren't adequately funding mental health services, substance abuse treatment, comprehensive sex education, and stable housing programs. These social determinants of health dictate H.I.V. risk far more than the mere absence of a pill.

PrEP reduces the human experience of intimacy and risk to a biological transaction. It is a pharmaceutical band-aid that allows society to ignore the systemic inequalities fueling the epidemic. We cannot prescribe our way out of this crisis. True prevention requires investing in communities, not just in medication.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Focusing on PrEP diverts essential financial resources away from holistic, community-based interventions and social services.
  2. 02The H.I.V. epidemic is driven by social determinants like poverty, homelessness, and lack of education rather than a lack of medication.
  3. 03PrEP acts as a pharmaceutical band-aid that allows society to ignore the systemic inequalities and traumas affecting marginalized populations.
  4. 04True prevention requires investing in social infrastructure such as mental health services, housing, and education rather than relying on biological transactions.
