Diversified Claude

"To ensure justice and fairness in campus rape cases, policy must be based on accurate data rather than ideological narratives, shifting the adjudication of felony allegations to the legal system while restoring due process for the accused."

We are watching a moral panic deflate in real time, and the question is whether anything will be learned from it. For three years, the dominant narrative has been that American campuses are sites of an epidemic of sexual violence—a "rape culture" so pervasive that one in five women will be assaulted before graduation. This figure, derived from a single online survey at two universities using an expansive definition of assault, has been cited by the President of the United States. It has driven federal policy. And it does not survive comparison to the Justice Department's own crime victimization data, which suggests rates of campus sexual assault closer to 6 per 1,000—still too high, but not an epidemic, and notably lower than rates of sexual assault among non-college women of the same age.

Into this overheated environment came Sabrina Rubin Erdely's article, which described a premeditated gang rape on broken glass as part of a fraternity initiation. The story was implausible on its face, contained obvious internal contradictions, and was published without contacting any of the accused. It was nonetheless embraced because it confirmed what people wanted to believe. That is the textbook structure of a moral panic.

The costs are not abstract. The Phi Kappa Psi fraternity at UVA was vandalized. Its members were threatened. Students who had nothing to do with any of this were tarred by association. And nationally, young men accused of assault under campus procedures have faced expulsion through hearings that resemble no recognizable form of justice. Recent lawsuits at Vassar, Columbia, Duke, and elsewhere describe panels that ignored exculpatory evidence, denied access to witnesses, and applied a 50.01% standard to charges that can end a career before it starts.

To recognize all this is not to deny that rape happens or that real victims have been failed. It is to insist that policy should be built on evidence rather than ideology. The honest reforms are clear: separate support from adjudication, so victims can get help without triggering a process they may not want; refer felony allegations to law enforcement, which is constitutionally designed to handle them; provide accused students with notice, counsel, and the right to confront the evidence against them; and stop using federal funding threats to coerce universities into lowering procedural protections.

The Rolling Stone story is a gift, in a perverse way, because it makes denial harder. The question is whether the people who built the current framework are capable of revising it, or whether the lesson will simply be that next time, the fact-checking should be better.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The widely cited statistic that one in five college women are victims of sexual assault is an exaggeration that contradicts Department of Justice crime victimization data.
  2. 02The uncritical acceptance of the Rolling Stone article demonstrates how a 'moral panic' can lead to the public vilification of individuals and organizations without factual basis.
  3. 03Current campus disciplinary procedures lack fundamental fairness by ignoring exculpatory evidence and using a low standard of proof that can unfairly end a student's career.
  4. 04Victim support services should be separated from the formal adjudication process to allow victims to receive help without being forced into a legal proceeding.
  5. 05Felony sexual assault allegations should be referred to law enforcement and the criminal justice system, which are better equipped and constitutionally designed to handle such cases.
  6. 06The federal government should stop using the threat of withholding funds to pressure universities into reducing procedural protections for accused students.
