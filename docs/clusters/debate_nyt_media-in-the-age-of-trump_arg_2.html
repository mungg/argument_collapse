"To survive an antagonistic administration that exploits media competition to punish dissent, news organizations must adopt a strategy of collective solidarity and coordinated resistance."

**Solidarity, or Picked Off One by One**

The single most important fact about Donald Trump's war on the press is that it depends entirely on the press's willingness to be divided. When Trump pulls The Washington Post's credentials, the rest of the press corps shows up at his rallies. When he berates a CNN reporter, the Fox News correspondent gets the next question. When he punishes one outlet with frozen access, the others fill the vacuum. This is how a single politician — even a president — can systematically discipline an industry that is, on paper, far more powerful than he is. The remedy is obvious and old: solidarity.

Imagine a different scenario. The new press secretary holds his first briefing and refuses to call on a designated "opposition" reporter. Every other journalist in the room sets down their pen. No one asks a question until that reporter does. The briefing ends in silence. The story the next day is not whatever the administration wanted spun; it is the spectacle of an administration that could not get a single question answered because it tried to pick a target. Do this twice and the tactic dies.

This kind of coordination is not a fantasy. The White House Correspondents' Association has occasionally flexed collective muscle, and foreign press corps facing authoritarian governments have used similar tactics. What has historically prevented American newsrooms from acting in concert is competitive pride: the fear that a rival will scoop you while you stand on principle. That instinct is suicidal in the present environment. Trump's team understands exactly how to exploit it — by dangling exclusive interviews, leaks, and access to outlets that play along, and starving the rest. Unless news organizations make a binding commitment to refuse to be the beneficiaries of another outlet's punishment, they will be played against one another indefinitely.

Solidarity should extend beyond access. It should include shared legal defense funds for reporters facing libel suits, which Trump has telegraphed he intends to encourage. It should include collaborative investigations — the model of the Panama Papers, where more than 100 news organizations across 80 countries pooled documents and expertise, is the right one. The conflicts of interest emerging from the Trump Organization's global holdings will be impossible for any single newsroom to map. They are a natural target for cooperative reporting.

It should also include solidarity with non-traditional journalism: local papers being squeezed financially, online outlets without the legal resources of The Times, foreign correspondents whose visas may be used as leverage, and yes, even outlets the establishment press has long held at arm's length. The First Amendment does not distinguish between a Times columnist and a blogger, and the courts that will adjudicate the coming battles will not either.

There is a self-interested case for all of this. Subscription numbers at major papers have surged since the election; readers, it turns out, will pay for journalism that takes itself seriously. But solidarity is also a moral obligation, owed by the strong outlets to the weak. The Times and the Post will survive Trump. Smaller publications, freelancers, and individual reporters may not, unless the institutions with resources defend them.

A press that hangs together stands a fighting chance. A press that does not will hang separately. There is no third option.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Journalists should engage in collective action during briefings, such as refusing to ask questions until a blacklisted peer is recognized, to neutralize the administration's ability to pick off individual outlets.
  2. 02Newsrooms must overcome competitive pride and refuse to accept exclusive access or benefits that result from the punishment of a rival organization.
  3. 03Media outlets should pool resources for shared legal defense funds and collaborative investigative reporting to effectively tackle complex stories like the president's global conflicts of interest.
  4. 04Established media institutions have a moral and practical obligation to protect smaller publications, freelancers, and non-traditional journalists from government pressure.
  5. 05The surge in reader subscriptions demonstrates that the public will financially support a press corps that takes a principled and unified stand against intimidation.
