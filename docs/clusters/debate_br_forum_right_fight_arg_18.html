Diversified Claude

"The United States should establish a dedicated domestic security intelligence service separate from law enforcement rather than relying on the existing federal-local police structure for counterterrorism."

Daniel Richman's essay is, at bottom, an argument for muddling through: keep the existing federal structure, deepen its dependence on the locals, and hope the resulting friction generates accountability as a byproduct. I want to argue that this is exactly backwards, and that the United States needs to do what the British, the French, the Germans, the Canadians, the Australians, and the Israelis have all done in one form or another — establish a dedicated domestic security intelligence service separated from law enforcement.

Richman briefly disposes of the MI-5 option by quoting the 9/11 Commission's worry that such an agency would either have to develop its own local relationships from scratch or be ineffective. This is far too quick. MI-5 does not have a national arrest power; it works alongside Special Branches embedded in local police forces. The "golden thread" Richman himself invokes is precisely that arrangement. The British model is not "MI-5 versus the locals." It is MI-5 plus the locals, with the crucial difference that intelligence work is done by people whose tradecraft, oversight regime, and institutional incentives are all designed for intelligence work — not by FBI agents whose institutional DNA is built around criminal prosecution.

This matters because the failure mode of the FBI as a domestic intelligence agency has been on display for at least three decades. The Bureau is structurally a case-making organization. Its agents are rewarded for indictments, convictions, and statistics. Pre-9/11, the Phoenix memo and the Moussaoui investigation both foundered on a culture that could not value intelligence collection that did not lead to a near-term prosecution. Asking that same institution to be the hub of a national counterterrorism intelligence network — and to manage the delicate, slow, patient relationships that effective counterterrorism intelligence requires — is asking a hammer to do the work of a stethoscope.

Richman's local-cops-as-intelligence-collectors model exacerbates this problem rather than solving it. Local police are even more case-making than the FBI. Their performance metrics are arrests, clearance rates, and Compstat numbers. Telling a precinct commander in Dearborn or Jersey City to spend resources cultivating long-term relationships in the Yemeni community in the hope that, three years hence, something useful might come of it, is utterly contrary to how police departments are organized, funded, and judged. We will get, instead, a great deal of low-quality "intelligence" — names dumped into databases on the basis of street stops and traffic tickets — and very little of the human-source work that actually matters.

There is also a civil-liberties argument for the MI-5 model that Richman misses. A dedicated domestic intelligence agency without arrest powers must be more transparent about its mandate, because it cannot hide behind the criminal-procedure framework. The British have an Intelligence Services Act, a parliamentary committee with real powers, a tribunal for citizen complaints, and a regime of independent commissioners. None of these are perfect, but they are vastly more developed than what we have in the U.S. for domestic intelligence, where the relevant legal framework is a patchwork of grand jury secrecy rules, national security letters, FISA, and Attorney General guidelines that the Attorney General can change at will. By keeping domestic intelligence inside the FBI and now inside thousands of local police departments, we make it impossible to build a clean oversight architecture around the function. There is no there to oversee.

Richman acknowledges the limited reach of judicial and statutory oversight at the end of his essay — and then concludes that the answer is to scatter the function further. This is a counsel of despair dressed up as federalism. If we cannot oversee a centralized intelligence function with statute and courts, what reason is there to believe we can oversee a function dispersed across the FBI, fifty state police agencies, eighteen thousand local departments, hundreds of JTTFs, and a constellation of new fusion centers? The Church Committee could at least map the abuses of COINTELPRO because they were largely centralized at the Bureau. A successor committee charged with auditing the post-9/11 intelligence apparatus would have to chase its quarry into every county sheriff's office in America.

The serious question raised by 9/11 was not "how do we use cops to do intelligence?" but "how do we do intelligence at all on our own territory, in a constitutional system?" Richman's essay never quite answers that question. The MI-5 model — adapted to our federal structure, statutorily authorized, parliamentary-style oversight, no arrest powers, narrow targeting authorities — is a better answer than the muddling through he proposes. It is also, not incidentally, the answer most likely to be politically sustainable, because it is the only answer that allows the public to know roughly what its government is doing in its name.

Richman fears that creating such an agency would mean walking away from the manpower and street-level knowledge of local police. But it would not. Special Branches, fusion centers, or state-level intelligence officers can be the interface, with strict rules on the kind of information that can flow in either direction. What we would gain in clarity, oversight, and tradecraft would more than offset what we lose in the kind of diffuse, accidental information-gathering Richman celebrates. We should pick the right fight — and that fight is for an honest institutional architecture, not for the genteel exhaustion of trying to repurpose the FBI and the local beat cop into something neither was built to be.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The FBI and local police departments are structurally designed for criminal prosecution and case-making, making them culturally and institutionally ill-suited for the patient, long-term work of intelligence collection.
  2. 02A dedicated intelligence agency without arrest powers allows for a more transparent and robust oversight architecture than a system hidden behind the complexities of criminal procedure.
  3. 03Relying on local police for intelligence gathering will likely result in a high volume of low-quality data derived from routine law enforcement activities rather than meaningful human-source intelligence.
  4. 04Dispersing intelligence functions across thousands of local and federal agencies makes effective democratic accountability and auditing nearly impossible compared to a centralized, statutorily authorized service.
  5. 05The 'MI-5 model' can still utilize local police knowledge through specialized interfaces without requiring the police to act as the primary intelligence agents themselves.
