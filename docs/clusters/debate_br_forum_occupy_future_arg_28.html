"The Occupy movement must be understood as a node in a global wave of protest against a transnational financial order rather than as an isolated phenomenon rooted solely in American political traditions."

**Tahrir, Madrid, Zuccotti**

One of the most striking things about Occupy Wall Street is how American commentators have discussed it almost entirely in American terms. The Boston Review's lead essay is no exception: it situates Occupy within the American tradition of abolition, suffrage, and civil rights. That tradition matters, but it cannot account for what is actually happening on the ground in lower Manhattan and a thousand other places. Occupy is a node in a global wave, and any serious analysis has to begin there.

Consider the timeline. In December 2010, Mohamed Bouazizi set himself on fire in Tunisia. By January, the Tunisian government had fallen. By February, Hosni Mubarak had been forced from power in Egypt after the occupation of Tahrir Square. Bahrain, Yemen, Syria, Libya followed in various forms. In May, the indignados of the 15-M movement occupied Madrid's Puerta del Sol, taking direct inspiration from Tahrir and explicitly objecting to a political system they described as corrupted by financial interests. Athens has been in near-continuous protest since 2010 over austerity and the role of European financial institutions. By summer, the camps had spread across Europe, including major actions in Israel against housing costs. In September, Adbusters' call to occupy Wall Street drew on this transnational repertoire and explicitly invoked Tahrir.

To treat Occupy as an isolated American phenomenon is to miss what is most distinctive about it: the synchronous emergence, across very different political contexts, of a politics of square occupation, horizontal assembly, refusal of representational politics, and identification of finance and political corruption as the central enemies. This is not a coincidence. It reflects shared material conditions—the global financial crisis, the imposition of austerity on populations who did not cause the crisis, the ongoing transfer of resources from public to private hands under the guise of crisis management—and shared cultural infrastructure—social media, transnational youth networks, a generation educated for futures their economies cannot provide.

The American conversation, including the Boston Review's framing, sometimes treats this global context as background. I would argue it should be foreground. Several implications follow.

First, the diagnosis cannot be purely about American inequality. The countries where Occupy-like movements have flourished include some with much less internal inequality than the United States (Spain, Greece) and some with profoundly different political traditions (Egypt). What they share is not a domestic Gini coefficient but a particular relationship between national publics and a transnational financial order. Bondholders demand austerity; governments comply; populations are told that there is no alternative. The "1%" in this frame is not just the American rich; it is a global creditor class whose interests are protected by an international architecture of trade agreements, central bank policies, and conditionality.

Second, the strategic options available to American activists are different from those available in Tunisia or Egypt. The Tahrir occupation succeeded in toppling a dictator because the regime had limited democratic legitimacy and because the military ultimately declined to defend it. The American political system, however captured, is not Mubarak's Egypt. Occupation alone will not produce comparable institutional change, and pretending otherwise sets the movement up for disappointment. The American context requires the harder, slower work of contesting elections, building organizations, and changing laws within a system whose formal legitimacy is broadly accepted.

Third, the global frame should make American activists more humble and more curious. The Spanish indignados have been at this longer than Americans have, and they have generated sophisticated debates about the relationship between square occupations and political party formation. The Egyptian revolutionaries have learned painful lessons about what happens after the dictator falls and the organized forces—the military, the Muslim Brotherhood—reassert themselves. The Greek experience is instructive about how quickly mass mobilization can be drained by repeated cycles of austerity and broken promises. American activists should be reading and talking to their counterparts elsewhere, not reinventing wheels.

Fourth, there is a real opportunity in the global frame to build solidarities that have been mostly absent from American left politics. The trade-union movement has long resisted such solidarities, sometimes for understandable defensive reasons. But a politics that recognizes the international financial architecture as the common adversary opens possibilities for cooperation across borders that did not exist when the framework was purely about American jobs versus foreign jobs.

I do not want to overstate the analogy. American protesters are not facing live ammunition. The stakes for Egyptian or Syrian activists are incomparably higher than for those of us sleeping in Zuccotti. But the political imagination is shared, the tactics are shared, the analysis of who runs the world is increasingly shared. The Boston Review's series will be valuable if it sometimes lifts its eyes from the American context to see what the rest of the world has been doing for the past year. Occupy is part of something larger than itself. Understanding that something is the first step toward making it more effective.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The movement's emergence is synchronized with global protests in Tunisia, Egypt, and Europe that share a common repertoire of square occupations and horizontal assemblies.
  2. 02The core grievance is not merely domestic inequality but a shared global condition where national publics are subordinated to the interests of a transnational creditor class.
  3. 03While the movement's grievances are global, American activists face unique strategic challenges because the U.S. political system possesses a level of formal democratic legitimacy not found in autocratic regimes like Mubarak’s Egypt.
  4. 04American activists should look to international counterparts in Spain, Greece, and Egypt to learn from their more mature debates regarding political organization and the limits of mass mobilization.
  5. 05Framing the movement globally allows for new international solidarities against a common financial architecture, moving beyond the traditional protectionist focus of the American left.
