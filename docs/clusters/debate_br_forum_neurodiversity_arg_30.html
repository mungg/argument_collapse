"While supporting the shift from liberal reformism to 'Neurodivergent Power,' the movement must move beyond identity politics to focus on building a material infrastructure of care and universal rights that protects those who cannot be made profitable by capitalism."

Robert Chapman is right to insist that neurodiversity has reached a crossroads. A concept coined in the late 1990s to name a political fact—that neurological differences are part of human variation and that social arrangements disable some minds more than others—now appears everywhere from human resources slide decks to influencer bios. Its diffusion is not a simple victory. When a corporation celebrates “neurodivergent talent” while maintaining punishing productivity targets, precarious contracts, surveillance software, and open-plan offices that produce sensory distress, it has not absorbed neurodiversity’s liberatory core. It has learned a new dialect of recruitment.

The most important contribution of Chapman’s essay is to distinguish recognition from power. Liberal neurodiversity has given many people, especially autistic and ADHD adults, a language with which to reject shame. It has helped win accommodations, improve representations, and challenge cruel practices. But the person who can ask for noise-canceling headphones at a tech company is not the whole movement. The person restrained in a psychiatric ward, excluded from school, sleeping rough, cycling through jail, or dying early from preventable illness is also neurodivergent. A politics that cannot reach them is not yet a politics of liberation.

Still, the path from liberal neurodiversity to Neurodivergent Power needs more specification. “Power” can mean many things: community control, organized labor, disability justice, mutual aid, abolition, institutional redesign, or revolutionary party politics. Chapman invokes the Black Panthers and Kwame Ture to suggest a militant shift from rights to collective self-determination. The analogy is useful insofar as it reminds us that antidiscrimination law can coexist with continuing domination. But the analogy also raises questions. Black Power emerged from communities with shared histories of racialization, segregation, and colonial violence. Neurodivergence is a looser category: autism, ADHD, dyslexia, intellectual disability, epilepsy, Tourette’s, bipolarity, schizophrenia, traumatic brain injury, and dementia do not map neatly onto one shared experience or one political constituency. Some people embrace neurodivergent identity; others experience their condition primarily as illness, terror, pain, or loss. Some require quiet and predictability; others need stimulation and flexibility. Some demand freedom from institutions; others need intensive lifelong support.

This diversity is not an argument against Neurodivergent Power. It is the reason such power must be built around material guarantees rather than a single cultural identity. Neurodivergent politics should not depend on proving that we have “superpowers,” but neither should it depend on proving that all of us share the same oppression in the same way. It should start from a simpler claim: every person is entitled to the conditions necessary for a livable life, whether or not they can be productive, charming, articulate, independent, employable, or intelligible to bureaucracies.

That means Chapman’s list of demands—freeing people from prisons and psychiatric hospitals, housing for all, ending imperial wars, unionizing against neuronormativity—is a good beginning. But we should add a disability justice infrastructure that is less glamorous and just as radical: public care work paid at living wages; accessible communication supports; respite without abandonment; supported decision-making; non-police crisis response; guaranteed income; desegregated education with real staffing; assistive technology; peer-run spaces; transportation; sensory-accessible public design; and medical care that does not treat cognitively disabled people as disposable. These are not mere “services.” They are the material base of freedom.

The strongest part of Chapman’s critique is the exposure of “strengths-based” rhetoric as a double-edged sword. It is understandable that autistic people, dyslexic people, and others have wanted to contest deficit-only accounts. Many of us grew up hearing only what was wrong with us. To learn that our forms of attention, perception, memory, honesty, or pattern recognition can be valuable is not trivial. Pride can be lifesaving. But the corporate version of this message narrows dignity into utility. Autism is welcomed when it helps quality assurance. ADHD is praised when it produces entrepreneurial energy. Dyslexia is celebrated when it belongs to a CEO who can tell a triumph-over-adversity story. The person who cannot work a forty-hour week, who melts down, dissociates, hears voices, needs help bathing, or communicates through behavior rather than speech remains outside the frame.

This is why the movement should retire “superpower” as its dominant public metaphor. Not because neurodivergent people lack gifts, but because gifts are not the basis of rights. A person’s claim on the world does not increase when their difference becomes profitable. We do not need to show that autism improves innovation or ADHD increases creativity in order to deserve housing, care, love, education, and freedom from violence. Indeed, one of the deepest forms of neuronormativity is the assumption that human worth must be translated into contribution.

Chapman is also right that incarceration must be central, not peripheral, to neurodiversity politics. Schools often punish disability before they name it. Children who cannot sit still, regulate emotion, process language quickly, or comply with arbitrary authority are suspended, restrained, isolated, or handed to police. Later, prisons fill with people marked by learning disabilities, traumatic brain injuries, psychosis, addiction, ADHD, and autism. Liberal inclusion in elite workplaces does little for those trapped in this pipeline. If the movement’s public image remains the quirky coder, it will fail those most harmed by neuronormativity.

Yet I would complicate Chapman’s call to free people from psychiatric hospitals. The institution is often violent; coercive psychiatry has a long record of abuse; confinement can strip people of autonomy and community. But many families and disabled people also know crises in which there is no safe place to go. The demand should not be simply “close institutions” unless it is inseparable from building robust alternatives: housing with supports, peer respite, crisis sanctuaries, mobile teams that do not involve police, long-term accompaniment, and noncoercive treatment options. Otherwise abolition becomes a slogan borne by those with the fewest resources, while middle-class people quietly purchase private care.

A politics of Neurodivergent Power must therefore be prefigurative: it must build the world it demands. The early autistic online communities mattered because they created spaces where autistic people could speak to one another without translation through parents or clinicians. The next stage requires more durable institutions: cooperatives, unions, independent media, accessible assemblies, legal defense funds, research collectives, and schools of political education. Chapman’s call for neurodiversity studies departments is valuable, but universities are not enough. Some of the most excluded neurodivergent people will never enter a seminar room. Movement knowledge must be produced in day programs, prisons, group homes, kitchens, Discord servers, union halls, clinics, libraries, and encampments.

The question of capitalism cannot be avoided. A market society measures minds by speed, flexibility, emotional performance, and output. It creates scarcity, then requires people to prove impairment to survive scarcity. It celebrates independence while depending on underpaid care labor. It turns accommodations into individualized exceptions rather than redesigning the pace and purpose of work. In that sense Chapman’s anticapitalism is not ornamental; it follows from the problem. If neurodivergent people are valued only when we can be efficiently exploited, then liberation requires changing what value means.

But anticapitalism must also become practical enough to organize around. Many neurodivergent people need immediate protection from eviction, benefit cuts, workplace discipline, school exclusion, and medical neglect. Rights-based tools remain useful in those fights. The ADA, the Equality Act, individualized education plans, disability benefits, and antidiscrimination law are limited, but limits are not uselessness. A mature movement should not counterpose rights and power as if one must choose. Rights without power are fragile; power without rights can become rhetorical. We need both: litigation and direct action, accommodations and universal provision, pride and care, identity and class struggle.

Chapman ends with the phrase “individually, we are disabled; united, we hold a form of strength.” I would amend it only slightly. Individually, many of us are disabled by environments, institutions, economies, and sometimes by our own bodies and minds. United, we can demand not that every mind be celebrated, but that no mind be abandoned. That is the future of neurodiversity worth fighting for: not a more inclusive labor market for the exceptional few, but a society organized around interdependence, where even those who cannot be made useful are understood as fully, nonnegotiably human.
