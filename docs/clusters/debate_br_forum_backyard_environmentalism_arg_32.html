"The proposed 'rolling-rule regime' is a legally dangerous framework that undermines the rule of law, democratic accountability, and constitutional principles in favor of unpredictable, ad hoc governance."

The legal framework of the United States administrative state is fundamentally premised on predictability, accountability, and the rule of law. Since the passage of the Administrative Procedure Act (APA) in 1946, we have operated under a system where regulatory agencies must provide clear notice of proposed rules, allow for public comment, and ultimately issue binding regulations that are subject to judicial review. In “Beyond Backyard Environmentalism,” Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen propose upending this foundational structure in favor of a “rolling-rule regime.” While their vision of fluid, continuous problem-solving is rhetorically appealing, from a legal perspective, it represents a dangerous retreat from the rule of law and a potential constitutional quagmire.

The authors champion a regulatory architecture where local units set their own environmental performance targets and revise them continuously based on new information and pooled experiences. They proudly note that programs like the Chesapeake Bay Program rely on “joint executive decrees of dubious legal pedigree and status” that are considered morally, rather than legally, binding. They argue that this fluidity allows for rapid adaptation to complex ecological realities, bypassing the “notorious inflexibility of centralized command systems.”

However, inflexibility in the law is often a feature, not a bug. It is what we call “certainty.” Regulated entities—whether they are small businesses, large corporations, or municipal governments—require certainty to plan their investments and operations. If environmental standards are continually “rolling,” subject to constant revision by informal networks of stakeholders and central monitors, how can any entity know what the law requires of them today, let alone five years from now?

Moreover, this proposed regime poses severe challenges to democratic accountability and judicial oversight. Under traditional command-and-control regulation, if the EPA sets an emission standard that is arbitrary or capricious, an aggrieved party can sue under the APA, and a federal judge will review the agency’s administrative record. But how does a court review a “rolling rule” that emerges from informal, continuous deliberation among local and national actors? What is the administrative record? Who is legally responsible when a “tributary team” formulates a strategy that fails to meet statutory requirements?

The authors seem to view legal formalities as mere obstacles to pragmatic problem-solving. But these formalities exist to protect citizens from arbitrary state power. When the Fish and Wildlife Service negotiates Habitat Conservation Plans (HCPs) on a case-by-case basis, as the authors celebrate, it operates with massive discretion. Without strict, binding national standards, there is a severe risk of unequal application of the law. Two landowners in similar situations might face radically different requirements depending on the negotiating skills of their local “deep users” or the whims of the local agency field agent. This ad hoc approach violates the basic principle that the law should be applied uniformly.

The authors look to legislative validation, such as the proposed Miller Bill (HR 960), as the solution to legitimize these new architectures. They suggest that Congress should shift from setting specific public goals to merely “authorizing and conferring pluralist political legitimacy on the constitutive framework.” This sounds dangerously close to an unconstitutional delegation of legislative power. Congress cannot simply write a blank check to agencies and private stakeholders, authorizing them to figure out the law among themselves as they go along. The non-delegation doctrine, though historically dormant, exists precisely to prevent the legislature from abdicating its responsibility to make the hard policy choices.

Furthermore, the authors' reliance on consensus and collaboration obscures the reality of environmental conflicts. Environmental law is fundamentally about resolving competing values and interests—economic growth versus conservation, private property rights versus public health. These are not merely technical problems waiting to be solved by the “disciplined consideration of alternative policies.” They are zero-sum political conflicts. The traditional adversarial system, with formal rule-making and litigation, recognizes this reality and provides a structured, transparent forum for resolving these disputes.

Replacing this system with a “collaborative and mutual accountability” model threatens to push the real decision-making into opaque, quasi-official working groups. When rules are made continuously by those with the time and resources to participate deeply, the broader public is inevitably shut out. A rolling-rule regime may be more adaptable to ecological surprises, but it sacrifices the legal predictability and democratic transparency that legitimize the administrative state. Before we celebrate the dismantling of the old regulatory architecture, we must ask whether the new one will truly be governed by the rule of law, or merely the rule of the most persistent stakeholders.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The continuous revision of standards deprives regulated entities of the legal certainty and predictability necessary for long-term planning and investment.
  2. 02Fluid, informal deliberation makes judicial oversight nearly impossible by obscuring the administrative record and blurring legal responsibility.
  3. 03The shift toward localized, case-by-case negotiation risks the unequal application of the law and exposes citizens to arbitrary state power.
  4. 04Legislative authorization of such a flexible framework may violate the non-delegation doctrine by abdicating Congress's responsibility to make specific policy choices.
