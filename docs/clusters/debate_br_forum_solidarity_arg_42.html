Diversified DeepSeek

"The secular left should adopt spiritual and liturgical practices, such as ritual and moral formation, to build the thick cultural habits necessary for sustained social endurance and solidarity."

Mie Inouye’s “Solidarity Now” has a quiet subversiveness. It refuses to resolve the tension between the organizer’s need for discipline and the intellectual’s impulse to resist; between the materialist insistence on class interest and the everyday reality that people often show up for reasons they can’t fully articulate. I admire Inouye’s willingness to sit with these tensions rather than collapse them. As someone who came to organizing through the Catholic Worker movement, I find her Mormon formation resonant, and I want to press further on the role of spiritual and moral formation in building solidarity. Inouye is ambivalent about Mormonism’s legacy, but she borrows a crucial insight: belonging and transformation happen in the context of regular, often boring, collective rituals. The left, I believe, needs to take ritual and liturgy more seriously.

The secular left tends to be allergic to anything that smells like religion. Meetings are functional; they have agendas, action items, and facilitation stacks. We don’t sing together, we don’t share silence, we rarely tell stories of our heroes and martyrs in a way that constitutes a collective memory. The result is that our spaces often feel thin and transactional. People come to get something done, not to be formed. Inouye’s Mormon youth group on Wednesday nights, scripture study before school—these were practices that shaped her personhood over years, not just informed her politics. They made endurance not a heroic choice but a habit.

The left could learn from this without mimicking the authoritarian structures of the Mormon church. I think of the way the civil rights movement was sustained by mass meetings in churches, where singing, preaching, and testimony created a collective body capable of facing down dogs and firehoses. Ella Baker famously critiqued the charismatic leadership model, but she did not dismiss the importance of the spiritual and cultural dimensions of organizing. Today’s movement ecology, with its decentralized affinity groups and social media platforms, lacks those thick cultural practices. We have memes and hashtags, but we do not have a common liturgy.

Inouye’s proposal of “social endurance” is a step toward recognizing this, but she frames it mostly in terms of conflict and epistemic humility. I would reframe it as a practice of moral formation. To show up when you don’t like people is to practice forgiveness, patience, and hope—virtues that are not automatic and must be cultivated. The left has a rich tradition of such cultivation: the labor colleges of the early twentieth century, the political education schools of the Communist Party, the Freedom Schools of SNCC. These were spaces where people learned not just analysis but also how to be together, often through shared cultural production. We need to reinvent such spaces for our time.

Moreover, Inouye’s account of conflict as potentially transformative resonates with a theological understanding of repentance and reconciliation. In Christian social thought, the beloved community is not a community without conflict but one where conflict is handled through practices of truth-telling, confession, and repair. The problem with deference politics, from this perspective, is not that it asks the privileged to listen but that it often lacks a practice of accountability and restoration. Listening without power-sharing is paternalism; genuine solidarity requires a mutual submission to a common purpose, a kind of covenant. Inouye’s epistemic humility comes close to this, but it leaves out the element of mutual accountability. In a covenant, I don’t just remain open to having my mind changed; I commit to being accountable to others for my actions, and I have a stake in their transformation as well.

Uncle Gerald’s story is heartbreaking, and Inouye’s wondering what a different form of solidarity might have done for him is a profound theological question. The left cannot save everyone, but it can build communities that reach people before they are lost to reactionary despair. My Catholic Worker house in a rural town does that by offering meals, a place to talk, and a consistent presence of people who will argue with you about politics without writing you off as a deplorable. It’s slow work, and it doesn’t scale, but it plants seeds. That kind of work requires an endurance that is rooted not in abstract political commitment but in a daily practice of hospitality. Inouye’s essay reminds me that such endurance is a gift, and that the left must learn to receive it from the traditions it too often dismisses as backward.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Leftist organizing spaces often feel thin and transactional because they prioritize functional agendas over the rituals and storytelling that create collective memory and personhood.
  2. 02Social endurance should be reframed as a practice of moral formation where virtues like forgiveness, patience, and hope are cultivated through habit rather than heroic choice.
  3. 03The left needs to reinvent spaces for political education that focus on shared cultural production and learning how to be together, similar to the labor colleges or Freedom Schools.
  4. 04True solidarity requires moving beyond epistemic humility toward a 'covenant' model of mutual accountability and restoration, where members are responsible to one another for their actions and transformations.
  5. 05Building communities rooted in daily practices of hospitality can reach people lost to reactionary despair more effectively than abstract political commitments.
