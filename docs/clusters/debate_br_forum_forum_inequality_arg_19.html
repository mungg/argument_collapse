Diversified Gemini

"While David Grusky correctly identifies that inequality is driven by market structures rather than just tax policy, his solution of expanding higher education to create a competitive market is a neoliberal fantasy that ignores the necessity of collective labor power."

David B. Grusky is entirely correct to pull our gaze away from the tax code and direct it toward the structural forces generating massive pre-tax inequality. For too long, the Democratic Party establishment has operated under the delusion that we can let corporations pillage the workforce from 9 to 5, so long as we redistribute a few crumbs through the Earned Income Tax Credit on April 15. The Occupy Wall Street movement was indeed a cry against the systemic rigging of the American economy, not merely a lobbying effort for higher marginal tax rates.

But if Grusky’s diagnosis of the disease is a breath of fresh air, his proposed cure—a massive expansion of higher education to create a “perfectly competitive” labor market—relies on the very same neoliberal fantasies that got us into this mess.

Grusky’s argument rests on a fundamental misunderstanding of power in the workplace. He laments the “bottleneck” in higher education, arguing that if we just produced more college graduates, the oversupply of educated workers would drive down elite wages, while the resulting scarcity of uneducated workers would naturally drive up wages at the bottom. This is the classic neoclassical fallacy: the belief that wages are dictated primarily by the laws of supply and demand in a frictionless vacuum.

But someone still has to clean the office buildings. Someone still has to stock the grocery aisles, care for our aging parents, and harvest our food. These jobs are not going to be automated out of existence, nor will they suddenly disappear because we hand every high school graduate a bachelor’s degree. By framing the problem as a "reserve army of unskilled labor" that needs to be educated, Grusky ignores the millions of jobs that do not require a college degree but still demand hard work and deserve a living wage.

Why did a high school-educated autoworker in 1965 make a middle-class wage, with excellent benefits and a pension, while a similarly educated retail worker today lives in poverty? It wasn’t because autoworkers were artificially scarce. It was because they had a union.

The spectacular rise in pre-tax inequality since the late 1970s correlates almost perfectly with the systematic destruction of organized labor in the United States. When workers are stripped of collective bargaining power, employers will extract every ounce of "rent" they can from their employees' labor, regardless of how many workers have college diplomas. We already see this happening today: the post-2008 economy is littered with college graduates working as baristas and unpaid interns. Expanding the supply of college graduates doesn't magically empower them to demand higher wages; without collective bargaining, it merely results in credential inflation, where employers demand a B.A. for entry-level clerical work that paid a living wage to high school graduates a generation ago.

Grusky’s vision of “market repair” assumes that if we just tweak the rules, the invisible hand will finally distribute wealth fairly. But the invisible hand is inherently blind to human dignity. The rules of the market are written by those with the power to write them. Grusky accurately points out that CEOs capture enormous rents by stacking corporate boards with cronies. But his solution—vague "institutional reforms" to make CEO compensation more competitive—ignores the obvious mechanism to check executive greed: labor power. In countries like Germany, where workers hold seats on corporate boards through codetermination, CEO pay is a fraction of what it is in the United States, and inequality is vastly lower.

True institutional reform is not about building a mythical, perfectly competitive market. It is about building countervailing power. We need to overhaul labor law to make union organizing a civil right, penalize corporations that engage in union-busting, and raise the federal minimum wage to a level that guarantees a decent standard of living.

We should absolutely reform our education system to ensure equal opportunity, as Grusky suggests. Every child, regardless of zip code, deserves access to higher learning. But we must stop pretending that education is a substitute for labor rights. Equalizing opportunity just means everyone has a fair shot at winning the lottery; it does nothing to change the fact that the lottery's losers are still condemned to poverty. If we want an economy that works for the 99 percent, we must abandon the illusion that markets, left to their own devices, will reward hard work. We must demand a labor market where power is shared, not just competitively fought over.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Wages are determined by power dynamics and collective bargaining rather than the simple neoclassical laws of supply and demand.
  2. 02Increasing the supply of college graduates will not raise wages for essential low-skill jobs or prevent credential inflation in the absence of labor protections.
  3. 03The decline of organized labor, rather than educational bottlenecks, is the primary driver of the rise in pre-tax income inequality since the 1970s.
  4. 04Market repair through competition is insufficient because the 'invisible hand' is blind to human dignity and the rules are always written by those with power.
  5. 05True institutional reform requires building countervailing power through labor law overhauls, such as codetermination and making union organizing a civil right.
  6. 06Education should be a right for all, but it cannot serve as a substitute for the labor rights needed to ensure a living wage for those who do not win the 'educational lottery.'
