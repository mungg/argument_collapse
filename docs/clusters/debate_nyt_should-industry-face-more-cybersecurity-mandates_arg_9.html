"The federal government should not impose broad cybersecurity mandates on private industry because it lacks the technical competence to set effective standards and such regulations pose risks to civil liberties."

I run a small manufacturing firm, and I want to push back on the comfortable assumption — shared by Secretary Panetta and the senators who agree with him — that the federal government is in any position to tell my industry how to do cybersecurity. Has anyone read the GAO reports on federal information security? Year after year, agency after agency, the government cannot secure its own networks. OPM, State, Commerce, even DoD components have been repeatedly and embarrassingly compromised. The proposal on the table essentially asks the institution that lost the plans to the Joint Strike Fighter to write the rulebook for everyone else.

This is not a rhetorical jab; it goes to the core competence question. Effective cybersecurity is a fast-moving, deeply technical discipline. Talent is scarce, expensive, and overwhelmingly concentrated in the private sector. The notion that DHS or NIST can produce standards that meaningfully outpace what a competent CISO at a Fortune 500 firm is already doing is fanciful. What they can produce is paperwork, and paperwork is what we will get.

Meanwhile, the actual hard problems get ignored. Most successful intrusions exploit not the absence of standards but the presence of users who click phishing links, vendors who ship insecure code, and supply chains that no one audits. None of these are solved by a mandate that a chemical plant adopt "appropriate" controls. They are solved by changing the software industry's liability environment, by training, and by genuine information sharing — the part of the Senate bill that everyone actually agreed on but which got dragged down by the mandates fight.

I will grant that the Chamber of Commerce sometimes opposes regulation reflexively, and that some industries — looking at you, electric utilities — have earned skepticism about their willingness to invest. But the answer there is sector-specific action by sector-specific regulators who know the technology, not a horizontal mandate covering anything DHS chooses to designate.

There is also a quieter concern worth airing. A mandate regime creates a new lever for government access to private networks. Once DHS is auditing your security controls, it is a short walk to DHS asking why you have not installed its preferred monitoring tools, or sharing what it learns with intelligence agencies. The civil liberties community noticed this and was right to. The same bill that protects the grid can quietly normalize a surveillance architecture we will regret.

Better information sharing, yes. Standards mandates, no. The threat is real; this particular cure is not.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The federal government's poor track record in securing its own agencies demonstrates that it lacks the expertise to dictate cybersecurity standards for the private sector.
  2. 02Cybersecurity is a rapidly evolving field where technical talent is concentrated in the private sector, making government-issued standards likely to result in ineffective paperwork rather than improved security.
  3. 03Mandates fail to address the root causes of cyber vulnerabilities, such as human error and insecure supply chains, which are better solved through liability reform and information sharing.
  4. 04Broad horizontal mandates are less effective than sector-specific oversight conducted by regulators who understand the unique technologies of particular industries.
  5. 05Imposing cybersecurity mandates creates a dangerous precedent for government surveillance by providing a mechanism for federal agencies to monitor private networks.
