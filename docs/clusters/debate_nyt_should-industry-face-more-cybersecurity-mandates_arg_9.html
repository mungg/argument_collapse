"The government should shift from a mandate-heavy approach to a liability-and-incentive model that holds critical infrastructure companies responsible for cybersecurity outcomes while providing flexibility in how they achieve them."

The question should not be framed simply as mandates versus voluntary cooperation. The deeper issue is responsibility. At present, too many cyber risks are treated as externalities: a company saves money by delaying security improvements, while customers, suppliers and the public absorb much of the harm if something goes wrong.

That does not automatically mean a large new rulebook is the best answer. Cybersecurity mandates can become obsolete quickly, and regulated industries are skilled at turning rules into paperwork. A checklist may tell a board what it must do to satisfy an auditor, not what it must do to defeat an adversary. In an area this dynamic, incentives may work better than detailed prescriptions.

The federal government should define a clear standard of care for critical infrastructure. If a company operates systems whose failure could have serious public consequences, it should have to demonstrate that it took reasonable, recognized precautions. That standard could draw on existing technical guidance, sector practices and independent audits. Firms that meet it would receive a safe harbor from certain liabilities. Firms that ignore it would face real consequences after a breach or disruption.

Insurance can reinforce this system. Cyber insurance is still developing, but insurers are better positioned than many regulators to ask hard operational questions: Do you know your assets? Do you control remote access? Do you test backups? Do executives see incident reports? Premiums can translate vague cyber risk into dollars. If the government helps clarify liability and disclosure rules, the insurance market can push companies toward better behavior.

There is also a role for targeted public support. Some critical infrastructure depends on old control systems that cannot be secured cheaply. If the government wants rapid modernization of water systems, small utilities or regional transport networks, it should consider grants, loans or tax incentives rather than simply issuing commands.

Secretary Panetta is right that the threat is serious. Foreign intelligence services, criminal groups and possibly terrorists are probing networks that matter. But a mandate-heavy response risks producing brittle compliance without genuine security. A liability-and-incentive model would preserve flexibility while ending the fiction that cybersecurity failures are merely private misfortunes.

Industry should be required to do more, but the requirement should be to manage risk responsibly and prove it—not merely to obey yesterday’s technical checklist.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Traditional cybersecurity mandates often become obsolete quickly and encourage a culture of paperwork compliance rather than genuine security against dynamic adversaries.
  2. 02The federal government should establish a clear standard of care that grants safe harbor from liability to firms that demonstrate reasonable precautions while penalizing those that ignore them.
  3. 03The development of a robust cyber insurance market can help translate vague risks into financial incentives and push companies toward better operational security practices.
  4. 04Targeted public support, such as grants and tax incentives, should be used to help modernize aging infrastructure that cannot be secured cheaply through private investment alone.
  5. 05Current cyber risks are often treated as externalities, allowing companies to save money on security while the public absorbs the harm of potential failures.
