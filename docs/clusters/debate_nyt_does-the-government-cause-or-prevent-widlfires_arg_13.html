"The government can best prevent wildfire damage by implementing and enforcing local land-use regulations, zoning, and building codes rather than focusing solely on federal forest management."

Most wildfire damage is not really a fire problem. It is a housing problem.

A wildfire burning through uninhabited brush may be dramatic, but it is not a disaster in the same way a fire entering a subdivision is. The difference is the presence of homes, roads, propane tanks, wooden decks and evacuation routes. Public debate focuses on forests because forests are visible and federal agencies are easy targets. But many losses occur because we have allowed ordinary residential development in places where fire is predictable.

Government policy can prevent much of this damage through mundane rules: zoning, building codes and inspection. Require noncombustible roofs. Require defensible space. Limit dead-end roads. Make sure firefighters have water access and room to turn engines around. Restrict new development in the most hazardous canyons and ridges. These are not glamorous reforms, but they save homes.

The common response is that such rules violate property rights. But fire risk is not confined to one parcel. A poorly maintained lot can endanger neighbors and firefighters. A house built at the end of a narrow road can force a dangerous rescue. When public crews are expected to defend private property, the public has a legitimate interest in how and where that property is built.

Federal forest policy matters, and climate change matters too. Still, if we want fewer billion-dollar fire seasons, we should stop pretending that all solutions lie in distant national forests. Counties and municipalities hold many of the keys. The government that prevents wildfire damage may not be the Forest Service in Washington, but the planning board that says: not here, not like that.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Wildfire disasters are primarily a housing problem caused by residential development in fire-prone areas rather than a forest management problem.
  2. 02Mundane local reforms such as noncombustible roofing requirements and defensible space mandates are effective tools for saving homes from fire.
  3. 03The public has a legitimate interest in regulating private property development because individual fire risks and poor planning endanger neighbors and emergency responders.
  4. 04Responsibility for fire prevention lies with local planning boards and municipalities rather than just federal agencies like the Forest Service.
