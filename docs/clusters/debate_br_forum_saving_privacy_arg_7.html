"To preserve democracy in the digital age, we must move beyond the false 'privacy versus security' tradeoff and establish structural legal constraints that treat privacy as a collective political condition rather than a matter of individual property or consent."

Reed Hundt is right to reject the familiar “privacy versus security” bargain. That bargain is usually presented to citizens at the wrong moment and in the wrong form: after the architecture of surveillance has already been built, after secret legal interpretations have already been adopted, and after both private firms and public agencies have become dependent on the continuous flow of personal information. At that point, the citizen is asked whether she would like to be safe or free. Unsurprisingly, she answers: safe. But democracy cannot survive if its most basic choices are staged as emergency referenda conducted in the shadow of fear.

The most valuable part of Hundt’s essay is his insistence that privacy is not merely a personal preference, like wanting to close the curtains at night. It is a political condition. Without privacy, association becomes risky, dissent becomes legible to power, and due process is replaced by classification. This is the heart of the matter. The injury of mass surveillance is not exhausted by whether any particular analyst reads any particular email. The injury lies in building a system in which the state can sort people before it has reason to suspect them, and in which those people cannot know the rules by which they are sorted.

That is why the response to the Snowden disclosures cannot be limited to better manners in the intelligence agencies. We need structural constraints. The split federal court opinions on the NSA’s telephony metadata program show how unsettled the law remains. Judge Leon saw a program that looked alien to the Fourth Amendment; Judge Pauley saw a tool arguably relevant to preventing another catastrophic attack. Their disagreement reflects not simply different legal temperaments, but the inadequacy of old doctrines for a world in which “metadata” can be more revealing than content. Who I call, when, from where, and in what pattern can disclose religion, illness, political activity, intimate relationships, and professional confidences. Treating such data as categorically unprotected because it has been “shared” with a phone company is no longer plausible.

Still, I am less confident than Hundt that a digital bill of rights should lean heavily on the idea of “digital property rights.” Property is an appealing metaphor because it suggests control: my data, my choice. But information does not behave like land or a car. It is relational. My phone record is also the record of the person I called. A photograph of me at a protest may also identify everyone standing nearby. A genetic datum tells a story about siblings, parents, and children. If we frame privacy too strongly as individual ownership, we may miss the collective dimension of the problem.

The better foundation is not property but power. Privacy law should ask: who has the power to collect, infer, decide, and punish? What must they disclose? What must they justify? What may they never do? These questions point toward due process, transparency, and minimization rather than merely consent. The fiction of consent is already one of the great failures of the commercial Internet. No ordinary person can meaningfully negotiate with Google, Facebook, Amazon, Yahoo, a mobile carrier, a bank, a health insurer, and every application on a phone. Clicking “I agree” is not self-government.

Hundt’s proposal for transparency about agreements between firms and government is therefore essential. The public should know far more about the legal demands made on companies, the categories of information sought, the number of users affected, and the interpretations of law under which agencies operate. There will always be operational secrets in intelligence work, but secret law is an affront to republican government. If citizens cannot know what the government claims the law permits, then elections cannot discipline policy and courts cannot reliably protect rights.

I also share Hundt’s view that technology must be part of the answer. Encryption, Tor, and other privacy-preserving tools are not toys for paranoiacs. They are modern equivalents of envelopes, locked filing cabinets, private meeting rooms, and anonymous pamphlets. The American political tradition has never required citizens to conduct all lawful activity in a condition of perfect traceability. Anonymous and pseudonymous speech have played honorable roles in democratic life, from the Federalist Papers onward.

But here again, technology should not become an excuse for public abdication. If only experts can protect themselves, privacy becomes a luxury good. Julia Angwin’s point about the cost and difficulty of secure tools is crucial. A right that requires unusual technical sophistication is not a right in practice. Government should not sabotage encryption standards, and it should not demand universal backdoors. A backdoor for the “good guys” is also a vulnerability for criminals, foreign governments, and abusive insiders. But government also has a role in making secure tools usable, auditable, and widely available.

The hardest question is law enforcement. Hundt is correct that encrypted spaces cannot be immune from lawful investigation. Criminals do use anonymity. The Silk Road was not a fantasy invented by surveillance hawks. Yet the existence of crime does not justify general exposure. The analog world already knows how to handle this. Police may investigate, use informants, conduct undercover operations, and obtain warrants. They may not install microphones in every home on the theory that some homes contain criminals. The digital equivalent should be clear: targeted access based on individualized suspicion, not systemic weakness imposed on everyone.

A digital bill of rights should therefore include at least four commitments. First, no bulk collection of Americans’ communications records without individualized suspicion and judicial authorization. Second, no secret law: significant interpretations of surveillance authority must be public, with only operational details withheld. Third, strong protection for encryption and anonymity, subject to targeted lawful process. Fourth, real remedies for abuse, including notice where possible and compensation for those wrongly harmed by government data practices.

Hundt’s essay asks, “Who rules?” The answer cannot be “the engineers,” though they have indispensable work to do. It cannot be “the intelligence agencies,” though they perform necessary functions. And it cannot be “the platforms,” whose business models are built on extraction. The only democratic answer is: citizens rule through laws they can know, institutions they can contest, and technologies they can actually use. Privacy will be saved not by nostalgia for a pre-digital past, but by refusing to let the digital future be governed by secrecy.
