"While the lead essay correctly identifies the limits of ideological moderation, a successful anti-authoritarian strategy must move beyond a simple left-versus-center debate to focus on building a broad, disciplined, and credible pro-democracy majority."

Bonica and Grumbach are right about the most important thing: “move to the center” is not a strategy adequate to the crisis. It is a reflex, and often a stale one. In a nationalized, polarized, low-trust politics, the image of a candidate sliding along a left-right line until she reaches the median voter is much too simple. It cannot explain Donald Trump’s hold on his coalition, the weakness of many painstakingly calibrated Democratic candidates, or the fact that voters routinely reward authenticity over ideological tidiness. Nor can it answer the central challenge of an authoritarian movement: how to generate the urgency, solidarity, and institutional courage needed to defend self-government.

But if the old moderation gospel is inadequate, Democrats should be wary of replacing it with an equally simple counter-gospel. The lesson of the last decade is not that moderation never matters, or that persuasion is a distraction, or that the party can mobilize its way out of every defeat by becoming more confrontational. The lesson is that “moderation” has been asked to do too much conceptual work. Sometimes it means ideological centrism. Sometimes it means cultural reassurance. Sometimes it means procedural restraint, coalition-building, or a refusal to indulge activist jargon. Sometimes it means little more than donor comfort. These are not the same thing, and anti-authoritarian politics needs some of them even as it rejects others.

The strongest part of Bonica and Grumbach’s argument is their critique of candidate-level moderation as a magic electoral variable. It is plainly true that individual Democratic candidates have less room than they once did to outrun the national party brand. A Democrat in rural Maine, a Democrat in the Central Valley, and a Democrat in suburban Atlanta are all now interpreted through nationalized media, partisan identity, and social conflict. Ticket-splitting has declined. Local newspapers have weakened. Voters know less about their House candidates and more about the latest national outrage. Under those conditions, the marginal benefit of a candidate’s carefully curated ideological positioning is likely to be small, inconsistent, and easily overwhelmed by presidential approval, inflation, immigration, Dobbs, or the behavior of national party figures.

Yet that finding does not settle the broader question. Candidate ideology and party reputation are related but distinct. A moderate nominee in a swing district may not gain much from a slightly more centrist roll-call profile, but the national party can still pay a price when voters come to associate it with unpopular views, institutional arrogance, or indifference to their sense of disorder. The fact that Jared Golden or Kyrsten Sinema could not save themselves through triangulation does not prove that cultural trust, issue salience, and party image are unimportant. It proves that individualized distancing is often too weak to overcome a damaged brand.

This is where some versions of anti-popularism go too far. Polls are not commandments, and politics is not consumer marketing. But democratic leadership is not the opposite of responsiveness. It requires listening to citizens as they are, not merely summoning them to become the electorate one wishes existed. Calling popularism “pandering” captures a real danger—the hollow politician who says whatever tests well—but it can also become an excuse for dismissing genuine public concern. Voters who worry about border disorder, crime, inflation, school disruption, or ideological conformity are not necessarily dupes of right-wing media. Some are describing real experiences. Others are registering anxieties that any successful democratic coalition must address rather than shame.

The most promising anti-authoritarian politics would therefore combine moral clarity with democratic humility. It would say: Trumpism is corrupt and dangerous; political violence, election subversion, and government by personal loyalty are unacceptable; and also the public is entitled to demand competence, order, affordability, and respect. That is not the politics of Liz Cheney photo opportunities. It is the politics of building a majority large enough to isolate authoritarians rather than merely denounce them.

The turnout-versus-persuasion debate is another place where the lead essay usefully corrects a consultant cliché but risks creating a false choice. Democrats do have a turnout problem. The decline in Black participation, youth participation, and Democratic registration advantages in 2024 was catastrophic. A party that depends on irregular voters cannot win by speaking only to high-propensity suburban moderates. Year-round organizing, labor revival, campus and community infrastructure, and candidates rooted in working-class institutions matter enormously.

But nonvoters are not a hidden army of left-progressives waiting for a sufficiently bold message. Many are alienated, cross-pressured, cynical, socially conservative, economically populist, inattentive, or simply exhausted. Some lean Democratic; some lean Republican; many dislike both parties. Mobilizing them requires more than ideological intensity. It requires trust. And trust is built through presence, performance, and respect—showing up before the campaign, delivering material benefits, solving local problems, and speaking in a language that does not sound imported from elite nonprofits or cable panels.

That is why anti-corruption is such a valuable frame. Bonica and Grumbach are right that corruption can reorganize politics around legitimacy rather than left-right placement. It connects democracy to daily life: the billionaire’s gift to a justice, the stock trade made by a member of Congress, the defense contractor’s revolving door, the private equity roll-up of a hospital, the crypto donor’s access, the corporation that converts supply shocks into permanent price hikes. It explains why government seems remote and rigged. It can appeal to progressives, independents, and conservatives who believe the powerful play by different rules.

Still, anti-corruption is not self-executing. In a low-trust environment, “they are corrupt” can quickly become “everyone is corrupt,” which benefits the most shameless actor. Trump has survived scandal in part because his supporters view accusations against him as part of a corrupt establishment’s war on them. To pierce that shield, Democrats need more than an indictment of elite impunity. They need visible sacrifice and institutional consistency: ban congressional stock trading; impose real Supreme Court ethics rules; strengthen disclosure; police conflicts of interest in both parties; reform permitting, procurement, and contracting so government can actually build; reject the fundraising gimmicks that treat small donors like marks; and stop pretending that every problem can be solved by a tax credit designed for a stakeholder coalition.

Anti-establishment credibility also cannot mean anti-institutional nihilism. The democratic opposition must distinguish between captured institutions and institutions as such. Authoritarians want citizens to believe that courts, elections, civil servants, scientists, universities, and the press are merely factions in disguise. Democrats should expose corruption within institutions while defending the possibility of impartial rules. That is a harder message than “burn it down,” but it is essential. A democracy cannot be saved by teaching people that all authority is fake.

Comparative experience reinforces this point. Successful anti-authoritarian coalitions are usually broad, disciplined, and ideologically mixed. In Brazil, Lula defeated Jair Bolsonaro not by running as the maximal version of the left but by forming a democratic front that included Geraldo Alckmin, his former center-right rival. In Poland, the 2023 opposition victory depended on cooperation among liberals, centrists, agrarians, and the left, all focused on restoring rule of law after years of democratic erosion. In Turkey’s municipal contests, opposition gains came through pragmatic coalition-building, local competence, and candidates who could speak beyond the already converted. These movements did not win by “moderating” in the empty consultant sense. But neither did they treat breadth as betrayal. They understood that the first task in an authoritarian crisis is to split the regime’s coalition and unite everyone who still wants competitive politics to survive.

That has implications for the Democratic Party. It should not ask every candidate to sound the same, hold the same positions, or appease the same donor class. But it should impose a different kind of discipline: do not gratuitously alienate people who might join an anti-authoritarian majority. Defend transgender Americans’ dignity without forcing every local candidate into the least popular activist formulation. Support immigration and asylum while insisting that borders and legal processes must be orderly. Demand police accountability while taking public safety seriously. Pursue climate action through abundance—cheaper clean energy, faster building, reliable infrastructure—not only sacrifice and regulation. Stand with labor without implying that educational achievement or entrepreneurship are suspect. None of this requires surrendering principle. It requires translating principle into a majority language.

The same applies to “kitchen-table issues.” Bonica and Grumbach are right that a thin list of poll-tested affordability proposals cannot meet a democratic emergency. But democracy and material life should not be separated. Authoritarianism is not only a threat to norms; it is a mechanism of theft. When public power becomes personal power, ordinary people pay—in higher prices, worse services, arbitrary enforcement, crony contracts, and rights dependent on loyalty. Democrats should not choose between saying “Trump is a danger to the republic” and “your rent is too high.” They should explain that a rigged republic produces rigged markets, rigged courts, and rigged opportunities.

The party also needs to be honest about governance. Voters do not reward democracy’s defenders merely for having better values. They want proof that democratic government can deliver. The Biden years contained major legislative achievements: infrastructure, clean energy investment, industrial policy, prescription drug reforms. Yet many benefits were delayed, diffuse, or invisible, while inflation was immediate and punishing. A renewed Democratic strategy must therefore treat implementation as politics. Build faster. Make benefits legible. Reduce bureaucratic friction. Govern in ways that people can feel before the next election, not in white papers released after the loss.

Bonica and Grumbach’s call for experimentation is exactly right, but experimentation should be institutionalized rather than romanticized. Let candidates adapt to their states and districts. Invest in county parties, union halls, churches, community colleges, veterans’ networks, and local media. Recruit nurses, electricians, teachers, small-business owners, veterans, and parents who have fought school closures or hospital mergers—not as props, but as leaders with their own authority. Test messages, but do not let testing replace judgment. Study losses without assuming the answer in advance. Above all, stop treating campaigns as episodic advertising operations and start treating them as membership-building organizations.

The deepest problem with “moderation” as conventionally preached is that it often asks Democrats to become less vivid. It counsels caution when the moment calls for definition. But the deepest problem with some anti-moderation rhetoric is that it can mistake vividness for reach. A politics capable of defeating authoritarianism must be sharp enough to name the threat and generous enough to welcome latecomers, skeptics, and former opponents. It must be insurgent against corruption and conservative about constitutional democracy. It must mobilize the base and persuade the uneasy. It must be angry at impunity without becoming contemptuous of the public.

Democrats will not save democracy by sanding every edge off their agenda. Nor will they save it by assuming that every demand for reassurance is cowardice. The question is not whether to move left or center on an abstract line. The question is how to build a pro-democracy majority in a country that distrusts elites, fears disorder, resents unfairness, and still contains many millions of people who can be reached. Anti-corruption can be the bridge, but only if joined to competence, pluralism, and a willingness to share the coalition with people who disagree on much else.

The old rules are not dead so much as insufficient. Elections are nationalized, but candidates still embody trust. Turnout is crucial, but persuasion still matters. Polls are limited, but public opinion is real. Moral clarity is indispensable, but so is coalition breadth. Authoritarians thrive when their opponents choose between purity and caution. They are defeated when a democratic coalition becomes at once broader, tougher, cleaner, and more credible than the regime it seeks to replace.
