"While David Grusky’s proposal for structural market repair is theoretically appealing, dismissing progressive taxation is a strategic error because redistribution is both a practical necessity for funding reform and a proven tool for curbing economic inequality."

In "What to Do About Inequality," David B. Grusky offers a compelling vision of structural market reform, arguing that we should fix the root causes of pre-tax inequality rather than merely applying a redistributive "band-aid." It is a seductive argument. Who wouldn't prefer a naturally egalitarian economy over the messy, politically fraught process of taxing and transferring wealth? But in his rush to dismiss the "tax the 1 percent" agenda, Grusky throws the baby out with the bathwater, dangerously undermining the most proven, practical tool we have for mitigating economic extreme disparity.

Grusky claims that progressive taxation is politically vulnerable and operates as a blunt instrument. He suggests that if we simply fix our institutions—namely by vastly expanding college access and reforming corporate boards—we can engineer an economy that generates fair outcomes organically. This "market repair" framework, while theoretically appealing, is utterly disconnected from political and economic reality, and it fundamentally misunderstands the purpose of the modern welfare state.

First, Grusky's dismissal of tax policy relies on a false dichotomy between "market repair" and taxation. How exactly does Grusky propose we finance the "radical overhaul of our education system" he champions? Expanding access to high-quality primary, secondary, and higher education for millions of disadvantaged children will require a massive infusion of public funds. In an era of crushing budget deficits and austerity politics, you cannot achieve structural educational reform without aggressively taxing the very demographic that has captured all the economic gains of the past thirty years. Redistribution is not the alternative to equal opportunity; it is the prerequisite for it.

Second, Grusky gives short shrift to the profound role that top marginal tax rates play in shaping pre-tax behavior—the very behavior he wants to change. He acknowledges Emmanuel Saez and Thomas Piketty’s argument that high marginal tax rates deter executives from rent-seeking, but he brushes it aside as a "slight reduction" in the incentive to pursue rent. The historical data suggests otherwise. During the mid-20th century, when top marginal tax rates in the United States hovered between 70 and 90 percent, pre-tax inequality was at its lowest. Why? Because when the government takes 80 cents of every dollar earned over a few million, CEOs and their boards have virtually no incentive to ruthlessly suppress worker wages or game corporate governance just to grant the executive a massive raise. The tax code itself acted as a powerful instrument of market repair, establishing an effective maximum wage and encouraging companies to reinvest profits into their workforces and infrastructure rather than executive compensation.

Furthermore, we must look at Grusky’s proposed institutional fixes with a heavy dose of pragmatism. Reining in CEO pay through corporate governance reform is a noble goal, but how long will it take to legislate, implement, and enforce? Decades? The same applies to his educational agenda. Even if we passed a sweeping, universally accessible higher education bill tomorrow, it would take a generation for those students to graduate and alter the macroeconomic supply of labor.

We do not have a generation to wait. The economic pain being felt by working-class Americans today—stagnant wages, underwater mortgages, shrinking pensions—demands immediate relief. Taxation and redistribution can do tomorrow what Grusky’s educational bottleneck reforms will take twenty years to achieve. We can immediately expand the Earned Income Tax Credit, bolster unemployment insurance, and fund infrastructure jobs simply by letting the Bush tax cuts for the wealthiest Americans expire.

Finally, Grusky's assertion that Americans have "never much liked" redistribution is belied by the enduring popularity of Social Security and Medicare—the largest redistributive programs in our history. The public is not inherently hostile to taxation, provided they see the revenue being used to fortify the middle class and secure the social safety net.

We should absolutely pursue institutional reforms to make our markets fairer and more competitive. But to frame taxation as a capitulation to a broken system is a massive strategic error. Every successful, egalitarian democracy in the industrialized world relies on a robust system of progressive taxation and wealth transfer to balance the inherent inequities of capitalism. The tax code is not a band-aid; it is the very foundation of the social contract. To abandon it in pursuit of a utopian, perfectly competitive market would be a disastrous mistake.
