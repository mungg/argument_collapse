"The claim that behavioral economics provides a universal set of solutions for global poverty is premature and risks being applied recklessly without sufficient evidence from diverse cultural contexts."

Reading “Small Changes, Big Results,” one could be forgiven for thinking that development economics has finally found its universal grammar: small fees discourage, small incentives work, procrastination is a global plague. Glennerster and Kremer use a blizzard of evidence from Kenya and India to assert that “strikingly similar patterns of behavior” hold across cultures. But this claim of generalizability is premature and, if taken as a mandate for policy, potentially reckless. The world is more various than a couple of countries, and the development mantra “context is everything” deserves a more respectful burial than the authors give it. We need far more evidence from far more settings before we can confidently prescribe nudges as a global remedy.

The authors admit that most of their evidence comes from Kenya and India, and that we have not done enough studies to assert truly universal patterns. Yet the entire rhetorical momentum of the essay pushes the opposite message: “all over the world,” “strikingly similar,” “how can we feel so different and be so much alike?” They invoke behavioral economics as if it were a set of immutable psychological laws. But the field itself is largely based on laboratory experiments with undergraduates in American universities—a population that is profoundly unrepresentative of humanity in either poverty or cultural background. The few studies that have replicated classic behavioral findings across cultures show substantial variation. For example, ultimatum-game offers differ widely depending on local norms of fairness and gift exchange. It is entirely plausible that the effectiveness of a lentils-for-immunization program in Rajasthan draws on specific norms of reciprocity and food-sharing that would not exist in, say, pastoralist communities in Ethiopia or urban slums in Brazil.

The chlorine dispenser example is impressive, but it embeds many context-specific assumptions. It relies on communal water sources. In many parts of Africa, women collect water from multiple points, and the idea of a single, public dispenser might clash with private property norms or clan tensions. The peer-pressure mechanism that the essay credits—public placement to facilitate social-norm formation—could easily backfire in a community where health behaviors are private family matters and public display is seen as boastful or shameful. In some cultures, adding chlorine to water might be resisted because it makes water taste “chemical” or because it is associated with outside interference; in others, the visual reminder of the dispenser might be a daily irritant. The essay’s technocratic optimism sweeps such possibilities aside.

Even within the two study countries, the evidence is far from uniform. The essay notes in passing that “in other cases, providing information is not effective. Teaching children how to prevent worm infections did not change their behavior.” That is a rare moment of acknowledging heterogeneity, but it gets lost in the triumphal narrative. The fact that an information campaign failed for deworming should raise caution flags: it suggests that the determinants of behavior are not just about information or cost, but about deeply held beliefs about disease causation, disgust, or bodily boundaries that vary dramatically. Worm infections carry different cultural meanings than diarrhea or HIV, and the same nudge toolkit may not work across the board.

The broader risk is that policy makers and donors, attracted by the simplicity of the message, will push for “best practice” nudges without investing in the local knowledge necessary to adapt them. We already see this in the rush to scale up conditional cash transfers around the world. Mexico’s Oportunidades was a success, but by the time it was exported to ten other countries, the label “conditional cash transfer” covered a multitude of program designs, some far less effective. The lesson is not that nothing generalizes, but that generalization requires deep local insight and continuous experimentation, not a top-down reading of behavioral principles.

Glennerster and Kremer might reply that what they are asking is for the development community to test these ideas in new contexts through more randomized trials. That is fine. The problem is that the framing of the essay—its confident tone, its clean narrative of progress—will too easily be taken as an answer rather than a hypothesis. Before we accept that small changes produce big results everywhere, we need to see the results in places with different kinship systems, different colonial legacies, different economic structures, different political regimes. We need to know whether the dramatic drop in demand for chlorine when a small price is charged holds in Buddhist Laos, Muslim Mali, or indigenous Guatemala. We need to understand how incentive programs interact with local patterns of gift-giving and obligation, where receiving material rewards for health behaviors might be seen as bribery or create unsustainable expectations.

The danger of premature generalization is that it becomes a new form of intellectual imperialism: a handful of behavioral laws, validated in a few sites, get applied across the globe with insufficient sensitivity. Development is hard precisely because human beings are meaning-making creatures embedded in dense social fabrics. The authors’ own nod to the need for adaptation—“lentils are a great incentive in India, but you probably would not use them in Boston”—undercuts their case. If we can’t assume lentils work even between India and the U.S., why assume that the underlying behavioral mechanism is universal? Let’s do the studies, but let’s hold off on the global pronouncements until we have genuinely global evidence. Context might not be everything, but it is still a lot more than this essay allows.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The evidence presented is limited to a few specific regions like Kenya and India, which cannot represent the vast cultural and social diversity of the entire developing world.
  2. 02Behavioral economics itself is largely based on unrepresentative populations, and cross-cultural studies often show significant variation in how people respond to norms of fairness and exchange.
  3. 03Interventions like chlorine dispensers or incentive programs rely on social mechanisms, such as peer pressure or reciprocity, that may function differently or even backfire in different cultural settings.
  4. 04The failure of certain information campaigns, such as those for deworming, suggests that deeply held local beliefs and cultural meanings can override simple behavioral nudges.
  5. 05A top-down application of 'best practice' nudges by policymakers risks ignoring the local knowledge and continuous experimentation required to make development programs effective.
