"To effectively distinguish the rich from the middle class, federal tax policy should focus on the distinction between income derived from labor and income derived from wealth and assets."

The most important distinction is not between households that earn $180,000 and those that earn $220,000. It is between people who live mainly from labor and people who live mainly from wealth.

That is where tax policy should focus. A surgeon, a two-lawyer couple or a successful small-business owner may have high annual income. They are not poor, and they should pay more than a median-income family. But the durable advantages of the rich come from assets: stock portfolios, closely held businesses, inherited wealth, real estate, deferred capital gains and the ability to borrow against appreciating property. Much of the inequality debate since the recession has centered on precisely this fact. The recovery has been far kinder to asset owners than to wage earners.

The tax code often treats wealth gently. Long-term capital gains and dividends are taxed at lower rates than ordinary wages. Unrealized gains can go untaxed for years. The “stepped-up basis” at death can erase capital gains tax entirely. Estate taxes now apply only to very large estates. Carried interest allows some investment managers to treat compensation as capital gains. These are not middle-class provisions, even if defenders can always produce a sympathetic edge case.

Compared with those issues, 529 plans are a small battlefield. But they are revealing. A 529 is useful only if a family has money left over to save and enough tax liability to make the shelter valuable. That does not describe most families. Still, college saving is a legitimate goal, so the solution should not be to abolish the benefit without replacement. Congress could cap the amount that receives tax-free treatment, prevent huge accounts from becoming estate-planning devices, or phase out the benefit for very high-income households. The revenue should go toward Pell Grants or refundable education credits that help students whose parents cannot pre-fund tuition.

If tax policy wants to distinguish rich from middle class, it should look at balance sheets as well as paychecks. The middle class owns a home, a retirement account if fortunate, and maybe a college fund. The rich own claims on future income streams and have the flexibility to time, transform and transmit income. A tax system that ignores that difference will overtax some high earners while undertaxing some of the truly wealthy.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The durable advantages of the wealthy stem from assets like stock portfolios and real estate rather than high annual wages alone.
  2. 02The current tax code disproportionately favors the wealthy by taxing capital gains and dividends at lower rates than ordinary labor income.
  3. 03Provisions such as the stepped-up basis at death and carried interest allow the truly wealthy to avoid or significantly reduce their tax liability.
  4. 04Tax benefits like 529 plans should be reformed through caps or phase-outs to ensure they support legitimate savings goals without becoming estate-planning tools for the elite.
  5. 05A tax system that focuses solely on paychecks rather than balance sheets will inevitably overtax high-earning workers while undertaxing those with vast accumulated wealth.
