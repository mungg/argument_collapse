"The political potency of anti-migration sentiment is driven less by cultural conflict than by a 'capacity crisis' resulting from governments using migration as a substitute for proactive social planning and infrastructure investment."

Lea Ypi’s essay identifies a moral scandal: migration policy in rich democracies is often harshest toward the people with the least power and most accommodating toward those with the most money. That scandal is real. But there is another, more mundane scandal that helps explain why anti-migration politics has become so potent: governments have used migration as a substitute for planning.

They have not built enough housing. They have not funded schools in proportion to enrollment. They have allowed health systems to run hot and then recruited migrant staff to patch the gaps. They have let employers rely on low-wage labor instead of raising productivity. They have underinvested in language instruction and credential recognition. They have concentrated asylum seekers in poor municipalities. Then, when the predictable stresses appear, politicians discover “community concerns” and blame the newest arrivals.

This does not refute Ypi’s class analysis; it makes it concrete. The border crisis is also a capacity crisis. Societies can absorb newcomers well or badly. The difference is not cultural destiny. It is policy.

Start with housing. In many high-income countries, the most visible conflict around immigration is not over abstract identity but over rent. Population growth, whether from births, internal movement, or immigration, requires homes. If a country adds workers and students but does not add apartments, prices rise. The political result is poisonous: migrants are blamed for a scarcity created by homeowners, planning vetoes, speculative investment, construction bottlenecks, and state failure.

The solution is not to pretend migration has no effect on demand. It obviously does. The solution is to make housing supply part of migration policy. If a government wants to issue work visas, expand university recruitment, resettle refugees, or regularize undocumented residents, it should also have a housing plan: zoning reform, social housing, infrastructure grants, faster permitting, tenant protections, and anti-speculation taxes where appropriate. “Welcome” without shelter is not generosity. It is negligence.

The same logic applies to public services. Migrants often contribute more than they receive over time, especially when they are young and employed. But fiscal averages do not comfort a school principal who needs translators next week or a clinic whose waiting room is overflowing now. National governments reap tax benefits while localities bear immediate costs. A functional system would automatically direct funds to municipalities based on population change and need. It would not leave local officials to beg after the fact.

Labor markets require equal realism. Ypi is right that employers prefer migrants without rights, not migration as such. But employers also lobby for migration when it relieves pressure to improve wages and training. A hospital that cannot retain nurses may need international recruitment; it may also need better pay and staffing ratios. A farm that depends on seasonal labor may be viable; it may also be built on poverty wages and bad housing. A technology firm may genuinely need specialized talent; it may also prefer importing workers to training domestic ones.

The answer is not blanket restriction. It is conditional openness. Labor migration should be tied to enforceable labor standards. Migrants should receive the same minimum wage, overtime, safety protections, and union rights as citizens. Visas should be portable across employers within a sector so workers can leave abusive jobs. Recruitment fees should be banned and liability should extend to the firms that benefit from them. Employers seeking migrant labor should contribute to training funds, housing funds, or local service funds where shortages are acute. If an industry says it needs workers, it should be willing to support the communities in which those workers live.

This is not anti-migrant. It is anti-exploitation. A migration system that ignores labor standards will eventually create the very backlash restrictionists exploit. Citizens see employers gaining workers while wages stagnate and conclude that migrants are the problem. Migrants experience the country as a place that wants their labor but not their dignity. Both groups lose; employers win.

Ypi’s critique of points-based systems also deserves nuance. It is true that such systems privilege education, language, wealth, and youth. They can reproduce global inequality and drain skills from poorer countries. But selection is not inherently unjust. Democracies may reasonably ask how migration intersects with labor needs, demographic change, and fiscal capacity. A country with an aging population may need care workers. Another may need construction workers. Another may need agricultural labor or engineers. The injustice lies not in planning admissions, but in planning them around the preferences of capital alone.

A better points system would value social need rather than merely market salary. Care work, for example, is often underpaid precisely because it is feminized and publicly neglected. If admission criteria privilege high wages, they undervalue indispensable labor. Likewise, credential requirements can exclude skilled people whose qualifications are not recognized because they come from poorer countries. Migration policy should be linked to credential bridging, apprenticeships, and wage floors, not simply to employer demand.

The asylum system requires a different framework. Here the question is not economic utility but protection. Still, capacity matters. A fair asylum system needs enough adjudicators, lawyers, interpreters, housing, trauma support, and return procedures to function. Underfunded systems generate backlogs; backlogs generate informal work and public anger; public anger generates punitive laws; punitive laws generate more irregularity. This cycle is not inevitable. It is the product of deliberate neglect.

Fast and fair processing should be a progressive demand. So should early work authorization. So should safe routes for people with strong protection claims. So should humane returns after due process for those who do not qualify. It is not compassionate to let people spend five years in limbo and then declare their claim invalid. Nor is it compassionate to create procedures so restrictive that only those who risk death can access them.

The most provocative part of Ypi’s essay is her call to move beyond political membership. I agree with the impulse but not the conclusion. Residence should matter more than it does. Long-term noncitizens should have local voting rights, secure status, and paths to citizenship. Children raised in a country should not be treated as foreigners in the only home they know. But national citizenship still matters because fiscal and democratic institutions are organized nationally. The task is to make membership easier to earn and harder to buy.

On this point Ypi is unanswerable: golden visas are indefensible. If a country denies residence to a caregiver because her income is too low while granting it to an investor because his portfolio is large, it has abandoned any civic account of belonging. These programs should be abolished or radically narrowed. Investment can be welcomed without selling political membership. The fact that some governments treat citizenship as an asset class while lecturing poor migrants about integration is among the clearest signs of democratic decay.

Yet the politics of migration will not be repaired by moral clarity alone. People judge systems by outcomes. Do wages rise? Are homes available? Are schools stable? Are streets safe? Are rules intelligible? Are newcomers learning the language? Are employers punished for abuse? Are citizens told the truth? Are migrants treated with respect? A politics that cannot answer these questions will lose, even if its ethical premises are superior.

Ypi warns that progressives will not beat the right by copying its ethnocentrism. Correct. But progressives also will not beat the right by seeming indifferent to implementation. The right thrives when state failure becomes visible. A tent camp under a bridge, an overcrowded asylum hotel, a school without language support, a labor market full of wage theft, a neighborhood where rents jump faster than wages: these are not just policy problems. They are narrative opportunities for demagogues. Competence is anti-fascist.

A practical egalitarian migration agenda would have several planks.

Build first: housing, transit, schools, clinics, and digital administration should be scaled for population growth.

Rights from day one: labor protections, wage enforcement, and access to courts should not depend on citizenship.

Status with mobility: no employer should own a worker’s visa.

Local compensation: municipalities receiving newcomers should receive automatic, predictable funding.

Integration as infrastructure: language classes, credential recognition, civic education, and anti-discrimination enforcement should be treated as core public goods.

End citizenship markets: no passport sales, no investor shortcuts that mock ordinary naturalization.

Legal pathways: reduce irregular migration by making lawful movement possible for work, family, study, and protection.

International responsibility: climate finance, fair recruitment, debt relief, and peacebuilding should be understood as migration policy.

This agenda does not resolve the philosophical argument between cosmopolitanism and national democracy. But it lowers the temperature by making solidarity practical. It tells citizens that migration will not be used as an excuse to degrade their living standards. It tells migrants that they will not be admitted only to be exploited. It tells employers that labor shortage is not a license for a rights shortage. It tells local governments that they will not be abandoned.

The real border crisis, then, is partly a crisis of imagination, as Ypi says. We have allowed the right to define migration as a threat and the market to define migrants as inputs. But it is also a crisis of execution. Liberal democracies have tried to run twenty-first-century mobility through twentieth-century bureaucracies and nineteenth-century housing politics. They have combined moral universalism with administrative scarcity. The result is backlash.

A better politics would be neither “open” nor “closed” in the slogans’ usual sense. It would be planned, rights-based, and solidaristic. It would recognize that people move, that communities need stability, that employers require discipline, and that democracy requires visible fairness. Ypi gives us the moral horizon: a world where nobody is forced to leave home and nobody who moves is treated as less than human. The work now is to build the institutions that make that horizon credible.
