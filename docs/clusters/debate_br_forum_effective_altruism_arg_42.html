"While effective altruism aims to maximize global well-being, it ultimately undermines democratic life by replacing collective political responsibility with the private, unaccountable whims of wealthy individuals."

Peter Singer’s "The Logic of Effective Altruism" is an elegant synthesis of utilitarian philosophy and modern data analysis, but it inadvertently reveals a troubling shift in how we conceive of democratic life and the public good. The movement Singer champions relies heavily on the individual wealth of affluent millennials—many working in high-finance or technology—redirecting their private capital toward global problems. While the impulse to reduce extreme poverty is commendable, the structural consequence of effective altruism is the further privatization of social welfare and the abdication of democratic responsibility.

At its core, effective altruism asks: "How can *I*, with my personal wealth, do the most good?" This hyper-individualistic framing bypasses the collective, political mechanisms that have historically been responsible for the greatest leaps in human well-being. The eradication of smallpox, the establishment of universal education, and the creation of the modern welfare state were not the result of optimized private philanthropy; they were the achievements of governments, funded by progressive taxation and driven by democratic mandates.

By framing global poverty as a problem to be solved by the enlightened charity of Wall Street arbitrageurs and Silicon Valley engineers, effective altruism subtly endorses a technocratic, oligarchic worldview. It accepts a world where massive concentrations of wealth are inevitable, and simply asks that the winners of this economic lottery be rational in their beneficence. This shifts the power to determine global priorities away from democratic institutions and local populations, placing it firmly in the hands of wealthy Western donors and their favored econometric evaluators.

When a young professional decides to "earn to give" on Wall Street, they are participating in a system that resists taxation, fights regulation, and actively weakens the capacity of the state to provide public goods. If that individual then donates millions to the Against Malaria Foundation, they are lauded as a hero by Singer. But society has effectively traded a democratic process of resource allocation for the whims of a private individual. The donor gets to decide which diseases are cured, which communities are saved, and which problems matter, utterly bypassing the agency of the people affected.

This anti-democratic streak is evident in Singer's dismissal of local institutions. He argues that donating to Princeton or Yale is ineffective compared to global health charities. While it is true that elite universities have bloated endowments, the broader implication is that local public goods—the arts, civic institutions, local infrastructure—are poor investments. But these institutions are the connective tissue of a functioning democracy. They foster the educated, engaged citizenry required to hold governments accountable and demand systemic change. By directing all civic energy outward into mathematical optimization of distant suffering, effective altruism hollows out the local civic sphere.

We do not need a new breed of hyper-rational philanthropists; we need a more robust, equitable, and democratic global order. Philanthropy, no matter how "effective," is inherently undemocratic because it is accountable only to the donor. If the millennials Singer praises truly want to do the most good, they should not seek to become benevolent oligarchs. They should use their skills to advocate for fair global taxation, international wealth redistribution, and the strengthening of democratic institutions capable of providing for the public good without relying on the charity of arbitrageurs.
