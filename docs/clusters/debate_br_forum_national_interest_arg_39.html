"While the responding essay agrees that American primacy is best preserved through a strategy of offshore balancing and strategic restraint, it argues that the primary challenge lies in overcoming the domestic political obstacles to implementing such a realistic foreign policy."

Stephen Walt has written an essay that will please some readers and disturb others. On balance, I find myself persuaded by its core argument: American primacy is best preserved through strategic restraint rather than ambitious global leadership. Where I have reservations is on the practical implementation of this insight.

The essay's analysis of the Bush administration's foreign policy is devastating. The embrace of preventive war, the disdain for multilateral institutions, the conviction that American power could remake the Middle East by force—these were not merely tactical errors but fundamental misjudgments about what American power can accomplish. Iraq is the proof: a military campaign that removed a dictator but created a quagmire, a democracy project that empowered sectarian factions rather than liberal reformers, and a strategic opportunity that was squandered through hubris and incompetence.

Walt's alternative—offshore balancing—offers a more realistic assessment of American capabilities. The United States is powerful but not omnipotent; it can influence outcomes but cannot control them. Strategic restraint acknowledges these limits while preserving American ability to act when vital interests are at stake. The key is to distinguish genuine interests from aspirational ones—the defense of American territory and the prevention of great power hegemony from the promotion of democracy and the transformation of hostile societies.

The proposal for a nuclear grand bargain deserves serious consideration. Walt is right that American hypocrisy undermines nonproliferation efforts. The United States cannot credibly demand that other states forswear nuclear weapons while maintaining thousands of its own and developing new ones. A mutual approach—reductions accompanied by verification and enforced by consequences for cheating—would strengthen rather than weaken American security. The alternative is a world in which more states acquire nuclear weapons, increasing the risk of nuclear terrorism and great power competition.

The analysis of Israeli-Palestinian policy is the essay's most controversial section and, paradoxically, its most important one. Walt correctly identifies unconditional American support for Israel as a source of anti-Americanism throughout the Islamic world. This support has not made Israel safer; it has instead entrenched Israeli maximalism and provided ammunition to extremists. American pressure for a genuine two-state solution would serve both American and Israeli interests in the long run, even if it generates short-term resistance from domestic constituencies.

The implementation challenge is significant. American foreign policy is not made by strategic thinkers in isolation; it is made by politicians responding to interest groups, media, and public opinion. Walt's recommendations face powerful opposition from the Israel lobby and from those who believe that American power should be used without restraint. Changing course requires not only intellectual argument but also political mobilization—a coalition willing to support a different approach.

Walt's essay is an important contribution to that coalition-building effort. It correctly diagnoses the pathologies of current policy and points toward a more sustainable approach. Whether that approach can command sufficient political support is an open question, but the essay moves the debate in the right direction.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Bush administration's pursuit of global hegemony through preventive war and forced democratization proved that American power is not omnipotent and can easily lead to strategic quagmires.
  2. 02Offshore balancing provides a superior framework by distinguishing between vital national interests and aspirational goals like global social engineering.
  3. 03Addressing American hypocrisy regarding its own nuclear arsenal is a necessary prerequisite for a credible and effective global nonproliferation regime.
  4. 04Ending unconditional support for Israel and pushing for a two-state solution is essential to reducing anti-Americanism and serving long-term regional stability.
  5. 05The transition to a strategy of restraint requires political mobilization to counter the influence of domestic interest groups and the prevailing preference for unrestrained power.
