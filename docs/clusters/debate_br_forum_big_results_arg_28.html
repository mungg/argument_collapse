"While randomized controlled trials (RCTs) provide valuable insights into behavioral interventions, the success of these small-scale pilot projects rarely translates to national policy due to the institutional and logistical failures of government bureaucracies."

In their essay "Small Changes, Big Results," Rachel Glennerster and Michael Kremer advocate passionately for the use of randomized controlled trials (RCTs) to test behavioral interventions in the developing world. They argue that small tweaks in cost, convenience, and timing can yield massive improvements in health and education outcomes. As a practitioner who has spent over a decade implementing development programs on the ground, I find their evidence compelling and their focus on cost-effectiveness refreshing. However, the essay glosses over the most perilous chasm in the development landscape: the massive gap between a successful, NGO-run pilot project and a sustainable, government-run national policy. The "small changes" that work so beautifully in an RCT often fail spectacularity when subjected to the realities of scaling up.

The authors note that the "new wave of smaller-scale evaluations" was largely spearheaded by NGOs, contrasting them favorably with governments by stating that NGOs "proved to be highly flexible and open to experimenting." This is entirely accurate. NGOs are typically agile, highly motivated, and focused on specific outcomes. When an NGO partners with academic researchers from elite institutions to run an RCT—say, testing a new chlorine dispenser at communal water sources—the project operates under greenhouse conditions. The supply chain for the chlorine is meticulously managed. The field staff who monitor the dispensers are well-paid, highly trained, and intensely supervised. Data is collected rigorously, and any hiccups in implementation are quickly resolved by dedicated project managers.

But what happens when the academic paper is published, the NGO declares victory, and the intervention is handed over to the state to be scaled nationally? The greenhouse is shattered, and the intervention must survive in the harsh climate of developing-world bureaucracy.

Governments in impoverished nations are fundamentally different beasts than nimble NGOs. They are frequently constrained by severe budget deficits, Byzantine procurement rules, and entrenched political patronage systems. If a Ministry of Health takes over the chlorine dispenser program, the reliable supply of chlorine may suddenly fall victim to a stalled public tender process. The field staff responsible for maintaining the dispensers are no longer highly motivated NGO workers, but underpaid, overworked civil servants who may go months without a paycheck and have little incentive to travel to remote villages to ensure the dispensers are functioning. Within a year, the dispensers are broken, empty, or stolen. The "small change" that produced a 90 percent adoption rate in the pilot yields zero results at scale.

This is not a hypothetical concern; the history of development is littered with successful pilots that died upon scaling. The authors mention the challenge of abolishing user fees, noting that "many countries have abolished them on paper only to see informal fees creep back." This passing acknowledgment points to exactly the kind of institutional reality that thwarts behavioral interventions. Why do informal fees creep back? Because front-line health and education workers are often trying to survive in a broken system. If you remove their ability to charge formal fees without drastically reforming government payroll systems to ensure they receive a living wage on time, they will inevitably find other ways to extract rent from consumers. You cannot nudge a consumer to use a free clinic if the nurse is demanding a bribe at the door.

Furthermore, the political economy of scaling up behavioral interventions is fraught with difficulty. Take the celebrated example of providing a kilogram of lentils to rural Rajasthani mothers who complete their children’s immunization schedules. In the context of an RCT, this is a brilliant, cost-effective way to overcome procrastination. But imagine a state government trying to implement this as a universal policy. The logistical nightmare of procuring, storing, and distributing millions of kilograms of lentils without massive leakage and corruption is staggering. Politically, the government would immediately face backlash from families whose children were already immunized without the incentive, or from citizens demanding lentils for other civic behaviors. What works as a temporary, targeted experiment often becomes an unwieldy entitlement when translated into public policy.

Glennerster and Kremer are right to push the development community toward evidence-based interventions. We absolutely must stop wasting money on programs that do not work. But generating evidence on consumer behavior is only half the battle. If we want these "big results" to be permanent and widespread, the next frontier of development economics must move beyond the individual consumer and focus relentlessly on institutional capacity and the mechanics of implementation.

We need rigorous evaluations not just of whether a bag of lentils motivates a mother, but of how to reform procurement systems so government clinics don't run out of vaccines. We need to study how to motivate middle-management bureaucrats, how to insulate supply chains from political interference, and how to maintain quality control when a program expands from ten villages to ten thousand. Until the RCT movement applies the same level of scientific rigor to the messy plumbing of public administration as it does to the psychology of the poor, the promise of "small changes" will remain confined to the artificial perfection of the pilot project.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01NGO-led pilot projects operate under 'greenhouse conditions' with high-quality supervision and resources that are impossible for resource-constrained governments to replicate at scale.
  2. 02The transition from pilot to policy often fails because underpaid and unmotivated civil servants lack the incentives and oversight that make NGO field staff effective.
  3. 03Institutional realities, such as broken payroll systems and informal rent-seeking by workers, can undermine behavioral nudges like the abolition of user fees.
  4. 04Scaling behavioral incentives, such as food rewards, creates massive logistical challenges and political backlash that are not present during a controlled experiment.
  5. 05Development economics must shift its focus from individual consumer psychology to the rigorous study of institutional capacity and the 'messy plumbing' of public administration.
