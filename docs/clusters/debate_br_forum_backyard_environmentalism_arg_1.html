"While the 'rolling-rule' regime offers a superior, information-based approach to environmental protection, its success depends on institutionalizing equality and maintaining strong central oversight to prevent local deliberation from becoming a tool for domination by the powerful."

Archon Fung, Charles Sabel, and Bradley Karkkainen have done something rare in environmental argument: they have noticed a genuine institutional invention while it is still in progress. Much public debate in the 1990s has been trapped in stale oppositions—jobs versus owls, Washington bureaucrats versus local common sense, command-and-control versus markets. The lead essay rightly insists that the most interesting work is occurring in the spaces between those categories. The Toxics Release Inventory, Massachusetts’s toxics-use planning, the Chesapeake Bay Program, and the better Habitat Conservation Plans are not simply softer regulation. Nor are they deregulation in homespun clothes. They are efforts to make environmental protection more intelligent by making it more public.

The great strength of the “rolling-rule” idea is that it takes ignorance seriously. Much environmental law was built on a heroic but often unavoidable fiction: that regulators could identify a pollutant, set a limit, require a technology, and then move on to the next problem. This worked better than its critics admit. Rivers no longer catch fire in the way they once did, many municipal and industrial dischargers have been brought under control, and national statutes created a floor beneath which states could not easily race. But the success of that first generation of law has revealed the limits of its categories. Nonpoint runoff, habitat fragmentation, cumulative chemical exposures, and ecosystem decline do not fit neatly into the old model of a pipe, a permit, and a numerical limit. The authors are persuasive that these problems require institutions that can learn.

Their best example is the Chesapeake Bay. The Bay’s troubles cannot be fixed by ordering one factory to install one device. The sources of nutrient pollution are spread across farms, suburbs, sewage treatment plants, and atmospheric deposition. The ecological effects are mediated through tributaries, wetlands, fisheries, and weather. The relevant knowledge is therefore also dispersed. Farmers know things about soil and drainage that modelers do not. Watermen know things about the Bay that agencies cannot acquire from satellite images. Scientists know things about nutrient loading and dissolved oxygen that local users may not see. No one knows enough alone. A system that forces these forms of knowledge into contact is not a sentimental gesture toward participation; it is a practical necessity.

The same is true of toxics-use reduction. TRI’s genius was not that it commanded emissions reductions. It made invisible practices visible. It changed the questions asked inside firms and outside their gates. Managers who had treated wastes as an unavoidable cost of doing business suddenly had to explain why their facility released more of a chemical than a competitor’s. Neighbors had a basis for inquiry. Reporters had something to print. Investors had something to discount. The information requirement created a politics of comparison. TURA went further by asking firms to examine use, not just release. That is a crucial distinction. End-of-pipe regulation asks, “How shall we capture the poison after we have created it?” Toxics-use reduction asks, “Why are we using so much poison in the first place?” That is a more radical and frequently more economical question.

Yet precisely because the lead essay is right about the promise of these experiments, we should be sober about their conditions of success. Deliberation is not magic. It can discover common interests, but it can also conceal domination. The phrase “local stakeholders” often sounds democratic until one asks who has time to attend evening meetings, who can afford technical consultants, who controls the agenda, and who fears retaliation from an employer or landlord. A chemical plant manager, a county supervisor, a homeowner, a migrant farmworker, and a conservation biologist may all be “at the table,” but they do not arrive with equal resources. If rolling-rule regulation is to be more than negotiated accommodation among the already powerful, it must institutionalize equality, not merely invite participation.

That means several things. First, information must be public, intelligible, and timely. TRI works because the data do not remain locked in agency files or trade-association reports. The same principle must govern HCPs and watershed plans. Monitoring results, assumptions, models, permit terms, compliance histories, and adaptive-management decisions should be available in forms that citizens can use. The authors are right to worry that many HCPs are still private bargains between a permit applicant and a Fish and Wildlife Service field office. Such bargains may occasionally be wise, but they cannot claim democratic legitimacy unless outsiders can inspect and challenge them.

Second, participation requires resources. The civic capacity needed for deep use is expensive. Citizens need access to independent science, legal advice, translation, childcare, transportation, and time. Without support, “collaboration” will favor those who participate as part of their paid work. Environmental groups and agencies should help train local monitors and conveners, but they should also fund participation by affected communities. Otherwise the new system will replicate the defects of old administrative hearings: everyone may speak, but only a few can make themselves heard.

Third, rolling rules need enforceable floors. The authors wisely distinguish their proposal from voluntarism, but the distinction must be guarded fiercely. Performance-based regulation can improve on rigid rules only if minimum standards remain non-negotiable. Local experimentation should concern how to exceed floors, how to adapt means to circumstances, and how to revise standards upward as knowledge improves. It should not permit communities desperate for employment or dominated by polluters to bargain away basic health and ecological protections. The point of national environmental law was to prevent precisely that kind of local sacrifice.

Fourth, the center must learn how to be a center again. The lead essay criticizes centralized omniscience, but the answer is not centralized passivity. EPA, Interior, and state agencies must become organizers of comparison. They should collect local plans, publish performance rankings, identify best practices, expose failures, and revise guidance in light of evidence. This is not a lesser role than command regulation; in some ways it is more demanding. It requires scientific sophistication, political courage, and administrative memory. The troubling fact that Washington had not even collected HCPs already negotiated is more than an anecdote. It is a warning that decentralization without organized learning becomes amnesia.

The authors’ democratic ambition is also worth defending. Critics will say that ordinary citizens cannot master chemical inventories, biological monitoring, or land-use tradeoffs. But the history recounted here refutes that condescension. Citizens forced disclosure laws onto the agenda because experts and officials had failed them. Communities around Love Canal, Woburn, and countless less famous sites did not begin with credentials; they began with sick children, dead fish, strange odors, and unanswered questions. Over time many became sophisticated investigators. Their experience demonstrates that competence is often produced by participation, not merely a precondition for it.

Still, the new environmental democracy should not be romanticized as consensus. Conflict will remain, and sometimes it should. There are real clashes between development and habitat, between short-term income and long-term ecological resilience, between private gain and public health. Deliberation can clarify those conflicts and sometimes transform them, but it cannot abolish them. A good rolling-rule regime must preserve adversarial tools: citizen suits, judicial review, enforcement actions, and national campaigns. Collaboration works best in the shadow of credible enforcement.

The lead essay’s most important contribution is to show that environmental regulation is not exhausted. At a time when many people speak as if government can only command clumsily, trade permits, or get out of the way, these experiments suggest another path: government as convener, benchmarker, guarantor, and learner. The challenge is to build a structure sturdy enough to support real public authority and open enough to absorb local intelligence. If that can be done, backyard environmentalism will indeed have moved beyond “not in my backyard” toward a more demanding proposition: this is our backyard, and we will govern it together.

"While performance-based 'rolling-rule' regimes offer valuable flexibility and learning, they must be grounded in a formal legal architecture to ensure they do not sacrifice the rule of law, national standards, and enforceable public rights."

The lead essay is animated by justified dissatisfaction with rigid environmental administration. But in its enthusiasm for “rolling-rule” regimes, it risks undervaluing two achievements of the old environmental law: legality and equality before national standards. The Clean Air Act, Clean Water Act, Endangered Species Act, and related statutes are not merely clumsy central commands. They are public commitments enacted through representative institutions, enforceable in court, and intelligible to citizens as law. A regulatory future built on negotiated plans, adaptive management, performance benchmarks, and informal directives must be judged by whether it preserves those commitments.

The authors acknowledge the danger. They criticize Habitat Conservation Plans that become backroom deals between a Fish and Wildlife Service field agent and a permit seeker. They praise proposed reforms requiring measurable biological goals, monitoring, reporting, and broader participation. Still, the problem goes deeper than incomplete information. It concerns the rule of law. If environmental obligations are continuously revised through local deliberation, who has legal authority to decide? What counts as compliance? When may a citizen sue? What prevents agencies from using flexibility to excuse failure? What prevents regulated parties from using uncertainty to postpone duties indefinitely?

Traditional command regulation is often caricatured. Yes, technology-based rules can freeze methods and ignore unregulated harms. But statutes and regulations also create clarity. A discharger knows its permit limit. A citizen group can compare reported effluent with that limit. A court can order compliance. A listed species cannot be “taken” merely because a local committee thinks development benefits outweigh biological risk. These features are not incidental. They democratize enforcement by allowing outsiders to invoke law against both private violators and reluctant agencies.

By contrast, performance-based regimes often rely on standards such as “continuous improvement,” “adaptive management,” “best practices,” and “feasible targets.” These terms may encourage learning, but they can be difficult to enforce. Suppose an HCP promises monitoring and adjustment if a species declines. The species declines. The permit holder argues that the decline was caused by drought, wildfire, or neighboring development, and that the plan’s measures remain reasonable. The agency, short of staff and anxious to preserve cooperation, agrees. What remedy does the public have? If the plan contains precise triggers and mandatory responses, law can operate. If not, adaptive management becomes adaptive excuse-making.

The Chesapeake Bay Program illustrates both the promise and the legal ambiguity. Its multi-state agreements and executive directives have produced impressive cooperation. But the authors themselves describe some directives as of “dubious legal pedigree.” That should trouble democrats. Moral commitments by governors and agency heads may be useful, but they are not substitutes for law when burdens must be allocated. Farmers, municipalities, developers, and taxpayers may all be asked to change behavior or spend money. On what authority? If elected legislatures have not specified obligations, agencies may govern through negotiated understandings that are visible to insiders but opaque to ordinary citizens.

This is not an argument for returning to administrative rigidity. It is an argument for constitutionalizing flexibility. If rolling-rule regimes are to govern, they need formal legal architecture. Congress and state legislatures should authorize them explicitly, define their permissible scope, require transparent procedures, preserve judicial review, and establish enforceable minimum duties. The authors’ discussion of the Miller Bill points in this direction. Its importance is not simply that it would improve HCP monitoring. It would convert an improvisational administrative practice into a publicly validated legal framework.

Such frameworks should include at least six elements.

First, statutory floors. Local experimentation should not be allowed to waive basic environmental protections. The Clean Water Act’s water-quality goals, the Clean Air Act’s health-based standards, and the ESA’s commitment to species survival represent national judgments. Rolling rules may improve implementation but should not dilute these judgments under pressure from local economic interests.

Second, measurable obligations. Plans should include clear targets, deadlines, monitoring methods, and contingency measures. Where uncertainty is unavoidable, the plan should specify who bears the risk. Too often “uncertainty” is used to let development proceed while conservation benefits remain speculative. If a plan authorizes habitat destruction today in exchange for restoration tomorrow, law should require security, funding, and enforceable triggers.

Third, public records. Every plan, data set, model, permit condition, monitoring report, and agency review should be available to the public. The authors correctly emphasize information pooling, but legal rights to information matter. Without them, transparency depends on administrative grace.

Fourth, participatory rights. Notice-and-comment after a deal is made is insufficient. Affected citizens and national interests must be able to participate early enough to shape alternatives. But participation should also be structured. Agencies cannot simply convene whoever appears and call the result democratic. Procedures should require balanced representation, disclosure of conflicts, written responses to significant comments, and explanation of departures from scientific recommendations.

Fifth, independent review. Rolling-rule regimes depend on data generated by regulated parties and local collaborators. That data must be audited. Scientific panels, inspectors general, citizen suits, and courts all have roles. Peer review is not hostile to collaboration; it is what makes collaboration credible.

Sixth, remedies. If a plan fails, there must be legal consequences. Permit modification, penalties, injunctive relief, and suspension of authorizations should remain available. Otherwise the public is asked to trade certain statutory rights for uncertain promises.

The lead essay’s democratic theory also warrants caution. The authors contrast pluralist democracy, with its interest-group bargaining, to directly deliberative problem solving. But pluralism has virtues. It recognizes durable disagreement. It channels conflict through elections, legislation, litigation, and public advocacy. It does not require citizens to transform their identities before their claims count. Direct deliberation may produce wiser solutions in some settings, but it can also pressure participants to mute principled objections in the name of consensus. Environmental law needs both deliberation and adversarial accountability.

Indeed, some of the best examples in the essay depended on adversarial foundations. TRI emerged from public outrage after Bhopal and domestic toxic disasters. The Chesapeake effort began with advocacy. HCPs exist because the ESA’s take prohibition gives landowners and developers a strong reason to negotiate. TURA’s planning works because reporting is legally required. Collaboration flourishes in the shadow of law. Remove the shadow and the powerful may lose interest.

The authors are right that agencies cannot possess “modest omniscience.” But courts and legislatures do not require omniscience to perform their functions. Legislatures can set purposes and procedures; courts can require reasoned decision making and enforce commitments. Administrative law has long struggled with how to reconcile expertise, discretion, and democratic control. Rolling-rule regulation is a new chapter in that struggle, not an escape from it.

The central question, then, is not whether environmental regulation should learn. Of course it should. The question is whether learning will be governed by law or by managerial discretion. A rolling rule that no one can enforce is not a rule. A benchmark that carries no consequence is advice. A participatory process that can be ignored is therapy. If the new environmentalism is to deserve public trust, it must translate its pragmatism into rights, duties, and remedies.

The lead essay ends with a stirring hope for democratic renewal. That hope is plausible only if the new institutions are made answerable to the old democratic forms they seek to supplement: statutes, elections, public records, courts, and enforceable rights. Environmental law should become more experimental, but it must remain law.

"While performance-based, collaborative regulation offers a promising democratic and practical evolution in environmental policy, it can only succeed if it remains anchored in enforceable national laws, baseline rights, and material support for disadvantaged participants."

Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen are right to notice something important: American environmental regulation is no longer adequately described by the old contest between centralized command and deregulation. Watersheds, habitat plans, toxics-use reduction, community right-to-know laws, and firm-level pollution prevention all show that information, participation, and iterative learning can do work that neither fixed technology standards nor simple markets can do. Their essay is especially persuasive in arguing that citizens are not merely consumers of expert judgment. In many environmental settings, citizens possess indispensable knowledge about land use, workplace routines, neighborhood illness patterns, local streams, seasonal changes, and institutional evasions. A regulatory system that ignores that knowledge will often be blind.

But the authors’ enthusiasm for “rolling-rule” regulation risks underestimating the virtues of the very adversarial environmentalism they hope to move beyond. Backyard environmentalism did not arise because citizens failed to appreciate deliberation. It arose because ordinary people had repeatedly been lied to, ignored, or patronized by firms and public agencies. Love Canal, Woburn, and Bhopal generated a politics of mistrust for good reason. The question, then, is not whether collaborative, performance-based regulation can supplement command-and-control law. It plainly can. The harder question is whether it can do so without dissolving the coercive public commitments that made collaboration worth pursuing in the first place.

Consider the examples in the essay. TRI works not because it convenes everyone in a trusting conversation, but because it compels disclosure. Firms do not volunteer toxic-release information out of civic generosity; they report because law requires them to report, and because communities, investors, journalists, workers, and regulators may use the information against them. TURA likewise works because Massachusetts put a legal structure around planning, certification, technical assistance, and public evaluation. The Chesapeake Bay Program has been successful partly because the Bay has unusually high public salience, a long-standing advocacy community, and an institutional structure capable of keeping governors, EPA, scientists, farmers, and citizen groups at the table. Habitat Conservation Plans, by contrast, show how quickly “local flexibility” can become private bargaining between a permit applicant and an under-resourced field office.

This suggests a less romantic lesson than the authors draw. Experimental regulation works best in the shadow of enforceable law. Without a credible threat of prohibition, penalty, or permit denial, deliberation easily becomes delay. Without baseline rights to information and participation, collaboration privileges those who already have lawyers, consultants, and access. Without national floors, local experimentation can produce local sacrifice zones. The rolling rule must roll forward, not sideways or backward.

The authors acknowledge this danger, especially in their discussion of Responsible Care and weak HCP oversight. But they do not give enough weight to inequality among participants. “Deep use” sounds empowering, but deep participation is expensive. Developers attend meetings with engineers, attorneys, biologists, and financial models. Chemical firms employ compliance staff. Farmers may participate only after a day’s work, and low-income residents may lack transportation, child care, technical training, or confidence that their testimony will matter. Communities already overburdened by pollution often have the least capacity to become the “deep users” on whom the new regime depends.

This is not a theoretical worry. Environmental justice advocates have spent the last decade showing that poor communities and communities of color often bear disproportionate exposure to toxic facilities, waste sites, and degraded urban environments. A performance-based system that relies on local bargaining may reproduce those disparities unless it contains affirmative supports: funding for independent technical assistance, translation, citizen monitoring, intervenor participation, and legal representation. If regulatory innovation simply tells disadvantaged communities to deliberate with better-organized adversaries, it may convert exclusion into a more polite procedure.

There is also a problem of measurement. Performance-based regulation depends on reliable indicators, but environmental harms are often cumulative, delayed, synergistic, and uncertain. Toxic releases can be estimated inaccurately. Biological monitoring may detect decline only after damage is difficult to reverse. Species recovery cannot always be reduced to near-term measurable goals. Non-point pollution can be notoriously difficult to attribute. The authors are correct that traditional regulation often pretended to know too much. Yet experimental regulation can make the opposite mistake: mistaking what can be conveniently measured for what matters most.

Adaptive management is attractive because it promises correction in light of experience. But adaptation after irreversible loss is not enough. If an HCP permits destruction of habitat on the assurance that monitoring will reveal whether mitigation works, what happens if the mitigation fails? If a watershed program allows continued nutrient loading while models are refined, what happens to fisheries in the meantime? Precaution is not the enemy of learning. In many ecological settings, it is the condition for learning responsibly.

That is why the authors’ call for congressional validation is important, but it should be more demanding. Congress should not merely bless experimental local problem-solving. It should define minimum substantive obligations: no avoidable jeopardy to listed species, no undisclosed toxic use, no weakening of existing health-based standards, no exemption from citizen suits, no secrecy in negotiated compliance arrangements. It should also require independent auditing of data, public access to plans and results, and periodic review by agencies with enough money and staff to do the job. A national information infrastructure is not a decorative supplement to rolling-rule regulation; it is the mechanism that prevents decentralization from becoming fragmentation.

The authors are also too quick to dismiss market mechanisms as information-hungry and inadequate. The sulfur dioxide trading program created under the 1990 Clean Air Act Amendments has shown, at least so far, that cap-and-trade can achieve substantial reductions at lower-than-expected cost when the pollutant is measurable, sources are identifiable, and the cap is firm. Markets are no panacea, and they are poorly suited to many toxics, habitat, and justice concerns. But the right lesson is not that markets fail where deliberation succeeds. It is that different environmental problems require different institutional mixtures: fixed bans for some hazards, technology standards for others, tradable permits where emissions are fungible and monitored, disclosure where reputational pressure matters, and collaborative planning where ecological complexity demands local knowledge.

The most promising part of the essay is its insistence that democracy can be practical rather than merely expressive. Citizens should not be confined to voting every few years or speaking for three minutes at a public hearing after decisions have effectively been made. They can monitor streams, challenge emissions inventories, help design habitat corridors, and bring local knowledge into regulatory judgment. That is a democratic advance.

But democracy also requires conflict, rights, and enforceable public authority. The citizen who says “not in my backyard” may be narrow, but she may also be the first person telling the truth. The task is not to move beyond backyard environmentalism so much as to preserve its moral energy while building institutions capable of asking the next question: if not this, then what; if not here, then where; if development proceeds, under what binding safeguards; and who will know whether promises are kept?

Performance-based environmentalism deserves a future. It will earn one only if collaboration remains accountable to law, science remains open to challenge, participation is materially supported, and national standards prevent local experiments from becoming local defeats. The backyard still needs defending, even when its defenders are invited inside the meeting room.

"While the proposed 'rolling-rule' regulatory framework offers an attractive vision of collaborative democracy, it risks exacerbating social inequalities and undermining environmental protection unless it explicitly addresses power imbalances and maintains enforceable legal safeguards."

The lead essay offers an attractive vision: citizens, experts, firms, and agencies sit together, compare evidence, learn from one another, and revise environmental rules as experience accumulates. It is a vision of democracy without the shouting, science without arrogance, regulation without rigidity. But those of us shaped by environmental justice struggles should be wary. The first generation of “backyard environmentalism” was not merely a primitive stage on the way to enlightened collaboration. It was a response to betrayal. People had to fight because corporations concealed hazards, agencies looked away, experts spoke in soothing abstractions, and poor communities bore risks that wealthier communities avoided. Any new regulatory architecture that forgets this origin will reproduce the very inequalities it claims to overcome.

The authors are right that citizens must increasingly help decide what should occur in their backyards, not simply what should be kept out. But the question is: whose backyard? In American environmental politics, the backyard has never been a neutral space. Some backyards are defended by lawyers, homeowners’ associations, newspaper editors, and congressional representatives. Others are inhabited by renters, public-housing residents, non-English speakers, tribal communities, farmworkers, and industrial laborers whose political voice is fragile. A process that depends on “local deliberation” may empower the first group and further expose the second.

Consider TRI. The authors properly praise the right-to-know movement and the discipline created by public disclosure. But disclosure does not operate equally everywhere. A community with a university nearby, an active newspaper, and residents trained in law or engineering can turn emissions data into pressure. A low-income community already struggling with unemployment, poor schools, and unsafe housing may receive the same data and lack the means to act on it. Worse, disclosure can become a substitute for protection. It is one thing to know that a plant releases benzene or toluene; it is another to make it stop. The right to know is indispensable, but it is not the right to breathe.

TURA’s planning process raises similar questions. If firms often discover that pollution prevention saves money, excellent. But what happens when it does not? What happens when a reduction measure would protect workers and neighbors but impose real costs on a company? Planning requirements without enforceable reduction obligations can become a sophisticated form of delay. The lead essay emphasizes that 86 percent of firms surveyed would continue planning even absent legal compulsion. That tells us planning is compatible with managerial interest. It does not tell us whether the most vulnerable communities are protected when their interests conflict with managerial interest.

The authors distinguish rolling-rule regulation from voluntarism, but many affected citizens will reasonably ask how firm that distinction is. Responsible Care is the cautionary tale. The chemical industry’s voluntary program borrows the language of continuous improvement, best practices, and disciplined self-monitoring. Yet, as the authors concede, it is vulnerable to public-relations manipulation because the trade association cannot credibly sanction its members when consensus breaks down. This is not an accidental defect. It is what happens when public power is displaced by private governance. A community living beside a chemical facility should not have to depend on the moral seriousness of an industry association.

Habitat Conservation Plans present a different but related danger. The authors are intrigued by HCPs because they can bring developers, conservationists, scientists, and agencies together to design regional habitat protections better than rigid enforcement of the Endangered Species Act. Perhaps some do. But “negotiated” plans can also legalize destruction. Developers come to the table with land, capital, engineers, and the threat of political backlash. The Fish and Wildlife Service comes with limited staff and pressure to prove that the ESA can be flexible. Local conservationists may lack resources. Broader publics may enter only after the deal is largely made. Calling the result adaptive and deliberative does not change the power imbalance.

The Miller Bill’s proposed requirements—measurable biological goals, monitoring, reporting, public participation—move in the right direction. But even here we must insist that participation not be confused with attendance. A public meeting after maps have been drawn, assumptions embedded, and concessions exchanged is not democratic governance. Real participation means involvement in framing the problem, choosing the data, selecting experts, defining alternatives, and deciding what risks are unacceptable. It also means the ability to say no. If “deep users” are invited only to improve implementation of an already chosen compromise, the concept becomes decorative.

Environmental justice also complicates the authors’ celebration of local knowledge. Local knowledge is real and often superior to professional abstraction. Residents know which drainage ditch children play in, when odors appear, how asthma clusters, where illegal dumping occurs, which promises have been broken. But local knowledge can be internally divided. In a company town, some residents may fear that strict controls will cost jobs; others may fear cancer. Homeowners may prioritize property values; renters may prioritize immediate health. Farmers upstream and fishers downstream may disagree. A deliberative process must not pretend that “the community” speaks with one voice.

Nor should we assume that collaboration transforms self-interest benignly. It can, but it can also tame dissent. Agencies and firms often prefer “reasonable” community representatives—those willing to accept the language of tradeoffs and feasibility. Angry mothers, militant neighborhood groups, and uncompromising activists may be dismissed as uninformed or obstructionist. Yet many environmental victories began with precisely such refusal. Love Canal did not become a symbol because residents politely joined a stakeholder committee. It became a symbol because people would not accept official reassurance.

The lead essay’s historical framing underestimates the continuing value of adversarial politics. Citizen suits, statutory deadlines, technology standards, and permit limits are not embarrassing relics of command regulation. They are tools that enable weaker parties to compel attention. Without them, deliberation occurs at the pleasure of the powerful. If a facility knows that failure to reduce emissions will bring litigation, penalties, or permit denial, it has reason to deliberate seriously. If not, collaboration may be cheaper than compliance.

A better rolling-rule regime would begin with environmental justice principles. First, national law must establish enforceable health and ecological floors, including cumulative-impact analysis in communities already burdened by multiple hazards. Second, disclosure must be accompanied by technical assistance controlled by communities, not merely by agencies or universities. Third, participation funds should be built into permitting, HCPs, watershed programs, and toxics planning. Developers and polluters who can pay consultants can also pay into independent public-participation funds. Fourth, monitoring should include community-designed indicators as well as expert metrics. If residents report odors, illnesses, fish kills, or dust episodes, those observations should trigger investigation, not be dismissed as anecdote. Fifth, no adaptive-management plan should allow uncertain future corrections to justify irreversible present harm. Extinction and childhood neurological damage cannot be adaptively managed after the fact.

The authors’ democratic aspiration is admirable, and environmental justice movements should not reject it wholesale. Indeed, many of the most innovative forms of monitoring, mapping, and participatory science have come from communities fighting toxic exposure. These communities know that expertise is necessary; they simply refuse to let experts monopolize judgment. They know that local participation matters; they simply refuse local processes captured by local elites. They know that command regulation can be clumsy; they also know that a clumsy rule enforced by law may protect people better than a beautiful plan nobody must obey.

The future of environmental regulation may well lie beyond backyard environmentalism, but it must not move beyond the moral anger that made backyard environmentalism necessary. The demand was never only for a place at the table. It was for safety, respect, and power. Rolling rules will deserve democratic legitimacy only if they strengthen those demands rather than soften them into consultation.

Don't Bury Command-and-Control Yet

There is a fashion in regulatory scholarship for declaring traditional environmental regulation obsolete. The Fung-Sabel-Karkkainen essay is the latest and most sophisticated entry in this genre, but the genre itself rests on a misreading of the historical record. Before we move beyond command-and-control, we ought to be honest about what command-and-control has actually accomplished.

Between 1970 and the late 1990s, the United States cut emissions of the six criteria pollutants under the Clean Air Act by about a third even as the economy grew by more than a hundred percent and vehicle-miles-traveled more than doubled. Lead in ambient air fell by about ninety-eight percent. Sulfur dioxide emissions fell by roughly forty percent. Stratospheric ozone-depleting substances were phased out under the Montreal Protocol and the 1990 Clean Air Act Amendments, in what is arguably the most successful global environmental treaty in history. Major rivers that were biologically dead in 1970—the Cuyahoga, the lower Hudson, the Connecticut—now support fisheries and recreation. The bald eagle has been delisted in much of its range, the peregrine falcon delisted entirely, and the alligator long since recovered. None of this happened by accident, and none of it happened because EPA was running deliberative workshops with regulated entities.

It happened because Congress, in a series of landmark statutes from 1970 to 1990, set substantive standards backed by enforcement, technology-forcing requirements, deadlines, and citizen-suit provisions. The agency promulgated rules. Industries complied, often grumbling. When they did not comply, they were sued, by the agency or by citizens, and they paid. This is the unglamorous architecture that produced the environmental gains we have. To read Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen, you would think this whole apparatus has been failing for decades and that we are now lurching toward a more enlightened model. The data say otherwise.

Let me address the authors' specific complaints. They argue that command-and-control "attempts too little and too much." Too little, because it draws sharp lines around what is regulated. Too much, because it locks in particular technologies that may become outdated. Both criticisms contain a kernel of truth, but neither is decisive.

On the "too little" point: yes, the Clean Water Act has done more for point sources than for non-point sources. But this is partly because point sources were the easier problem and were addressed first, and partly because non-point source regulation runs into property-rights and federalism objections that have nothing to do with the limitations of command-and-control as such. The 1987 amendments to the CWA created the Section 319 nonpoint source program, and the Total Maximum Daily Load program is now beginning to bite on impaired waterways. The architecture is extending itself.

On the "too much" point: technology-based standards have indeed sometimes locked in suboptimal approaches. But the New Source Performance Standards under the CAA are revisable, and have been revised, on the basis of demonstrated improvements. The "Best Available Control Technology" determinations are made case-by-case in PSD permitting and explicitly take into account technological evolution. The system is not as rigid as the authors imply. And where it has been rigid, the rigidity has often been a feature, not a bug: it has compelled industry to find solutions it claimed were impossible. The phase-out of leaded gasoline, the catalytic converter, the reduction of acid rain precursors—these all involved compulsion, not deliberation.

I take the authors' point that programs like TRI have been valuable. But TRI is not an alternative to command-and-control; it is a complement to it, riding on top of the substantive obligations of RCRA, CERCLA, and the CAA. Strip away the underlying enforcement architecture and TRI becomes voluntary corporate sustainability reporting—useful, but no substitute for binding rules.

The Habitat Conservation Plans the authors celebrate are, in my view, the weakest part of their case. The HCP program is a workaround for the Endangered Species Act's strict take prohibition. It exists because landowners and developers complained about ESA enforcement, and because the Clinton administration wanted to defuse the political backlash that the spotted owl litigation had provoked. Whether HCPs produce better conservation outcomes than strict Section 9 enforcement is genuinely contested among ecologists, and the authors' own footnotes acknowledge serious monitoring and accountability problems. To hold up this fraught and recent experiment as the leading edge of a new regulatory paradigm is premature.

The authors also misunderstand, I think, the politics of the moment. They write as if environmentalism is now a settled fact and the only question is how to refine it. But the 1990s have seen sustained, well-funded attacks on the entire structure of federal environmental regulation: the Contract with America's regulatory reform agenda, the Lucas decision on regulatory takings, the unfunded mandates legislation, the persistent attempts to weaken the ESA. The rolling-rule rhetoric of decentralization, flexibility, and stakeholder negotiation is being deployed not only by sincere reformers but by interests that want to gut environmental protection while appearing reasonable. Anyone who proposes to dismantle existing enforcement architecture without securing alternative protections is, intentionally or not, providing cover for that project.

What I would urge instead is a more modest narrative. Command-and-control has accomplished an enormous amount and remains the indispensable core of American environmental regulation. Information-disclosure programs like TRI are valuable supplements that intensify pressure for performance. Market-based instruments, where well-designed (the acid rain SO2 trading program is the obvious success story), can deliver compliance more cheaply than uniform technology standards. Collaborative regional processes like the Chesapeake Bay Program have a role in addressing problems that don't fit neatly into existing statutory categories. All of these are tools in a toolkit. None of them, individually or collectively, replaces the substantive regulatory commitments that have produced the gains of the last thirty years.

What worries me about essays like the one under discussion is not the empirical observations, most of which are accurate. It is the framing: the suggestion that we are leaving an era of command-and-control behind. We are not, and we should not. The eagles, the rivers, and our own lungs are evidence enough.

The Charm of the New and the Uses of the Old

Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen have written a captivating piece, and that is exactly what worries me. The essay glides past hard questions about enforcement, capture, and political power because it has fallen in love with its own architecture. Anyone who has actually litigated under the Clean Water Act or watched a state department of environmental quality slowly suffocate under industry pressure will recognize the seduction—and the danger—of the "rolling-rule regime" the authors describe.

Let me concede what is worth conceding. The Toxics Release Inventory has indeed been one of the most successful environmental statutes of the last quarter century, precisely because it harnessed reputational pressure rather than relying solely on administrative penalty. Aggregate toxic releases as reported under TRI have fallen substantially since 1988, and we have a generation of environmental journalists and community organizers who would not exist without that data. The Massachusetts TURA program builds on that base intelligently. The 1987 Chesapeake Bay Agreement marked a real institutional achievement. None of this is in dispute.

What is in dispute is the inference the authors draw from these cases—that we are witnessing the birth of a new regulatory paradigm that supersedes both command-and-control and market-based approaches, and that we should accelerate the transition. I think this inference rests on three confusions.

The first is a confusion about what made these programs work. TRI works because behind it sits the residual threat of substantive command-and-control statutes—RCRA, CERCLA, the Clean Air Act—that give the disclosed information bite. A firm that releases enormous quantities of a TRI chemical is not merely embarrassed; it is also exposed to citizen suits, to potential listing decisions, to scrutiny under permit programs. Strip away that surrounding architecture and TRI becomes a press-release exercise. Similarly, the Chesapeake Bay Program operates against the backdrop of point-source permitting under the CWA. The "directives" that the authors describe as having "dubious legal pedigree" are persuasive precisely because the agencies signing them have real legal authority elsewhere. The rolling-rule regime is, in this sense, parasitic on the very command-and-control system the authors are eager to declare exhausted.

The second confusion is about Habitat Conservation Plans, which the authors hold up as their most ambitious example. The reality of HCPs as practiced in the 1990s is considerably uglier than this essay lets on. The Service has issued plans with "No Surprises" assurances locking in landowner obligations for fifty or even one hundred years—periods over which we cannot remotely predict the trajectory of climate, hydrology, or species behavior. Take Babbitt's flagship Southern California plans: the gnatcatcher reserves were drawn on maps largely by developers, with biologists brought in to bless the result. The authors themselves admit, in a footnote that deserves more attention than they give it, that as of July 1998 no one in Washington had even collected the HCPs that had been negotiated, much less read them. This is not "deep use." It is decentralization without the conditions that would make decentralization accountable. To call this proto-democratic is to mistake an enforcement vacuum for a participatory triumph.

The third confusion is about deliberation itself. The authors imagine a process in which "protagonists discover unanticipated solutions provisionally acceptable to all" through "disciplined consideration of alternative policies." This is a beautiful picture. It is also one that ignores everything we know about the bargaining power differentials that structure environmental conflicts. The Pacific Lumber HCP, the Plum Creek HCP, the Murray Pacific HCP—these were not Habermasian conversations. They were negotiations between a highly capitalized resource extraction firm with experienced counsel and a federal agency under enormous political pressure to "demonstrate the workability" of flexibility, in the authors' own approving phrase. The local conservation groups that the authors imagine as equal participants either lacked the technical capacity to engage meaningfully, or were deliberately excluded, or—most often—participated in good faith and were rolled. John Kostyack's work for the National Wildlife Federation, which the authors cite, makes this point with considerably more bite than the authors acknowledge.

There is a deeper issue here about the politics of regulatory reform. The case for command-and-control was never that bureaucrats in Washington possessed "modest omniscience." It was that diffuse, vulnerable interests—future generations, voiceless species, downstream communities, poor neighborhoods—are systematically underrepresented in any bargaining-based process, and that the function of national rules is to set non-negotiable floors that protect those interests against the immediate pressures of local politics. The genius of the Clean Air Act was not that EPA knew best; it was that EPA could insist on lead-free gasoline, on the phase-out of CFCs, on acid rain controls, over the heads of industries and state governments that would have bargained those gains away locality by locality. Lead in children's blood fell by roughly three-quarters between 1976 and the early 1990s because of a command, not a conversation.

I am not against learning. I am not against monitoring, benchmarking, or local experimentation. I am against the rhetorical move that uses the genuine successes of information-disclosure programs to argue for the dismantling of mandatory standards. And I notice that this move is being made not only by Sabel and his co-authors but by precisely those regulated industries that have the most to gain from rolling rules that, in practice, roll only in one direction.

The authors anticipate this objection and respond that pluralist politics has not in fact produced "exquisite balance" but rather "a welter of clashing rules" and "self-canceling swings of policy." Fair enough. But the answer to dysfunction in pluralist democracy is not to replace it with an unaccountable network of stakeholder negotiations whose outputs no elected body has ratified. The authors notice this problem, gesture toward the Miller Bill, and hope Congress will somehow bless the new architecture. Yet they do not engage seriously with the possibility that Congress, looking at the actual record of HCPs, might decide to constrain rather than authorize them—and that this would not be a failure of political imagination but a reasonable response to the evidence.

What I would propose instead is something more modest than the authors' transformative vision. First, retain mandatory national floors—including substantive standards for endangered species, ambient air and water quality, toxic releases—as the non-negotiable baseline. Second, embrace information-disclosure regimes like TRI as supplements that intensify pressure for performance above the floor, not as replacements for the floor itself. Third, demand from any locally-negotiated arrangement (HCP, watershed plan, etc.) the procedural safeguards the Miller Bill outlines—objective biological goals, monitoring, public participation, periodic review—and the legal authority to revoke approvals when those safeguards fail. Fourth, fund the national environmental groups and academic centers that can serve as independent monitors, because without them the "pooling of experience" the authors celebrate will be done by the regulated industries themselves.

This is not a sexy program. It does not promise a "novel form of democracy." It does promise that when a developer in San Diego County wants to take the last reasonable habitat for a listed species, we will say no, and mean it.

The authors close with an inspiring quotation from John Dewey, the spirit of which I share. Dewey believed that democracy required ongoing inquiry and experimentation. He also believed, less often quoted, that publics form around problems they cannot solve through immediate face-to-face deliberation, and that the function of the state was to constitute those publics. The administrative state we have, for all its flaws, is the institutional sediment of that insight. Before we celebrate its supersession, we should ask whether what is replacing it really has more democratic credentials, or merely a more flattering vocabulary.

"The proposed shift from command-and-control regulation to collaborative 'rolling-rule' regimes is a dangerous abdication of federal responsibility that prioritizes procedural participation over substantive environmental protection."

The seductive narrative spun by Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen presents a world where environmentalism has matured from angry NIMBYism into a rational, collaborative process of continuous improvement. It is a vision of “rolling-rule regimes” where local stakeholders, industry, and experts sit together, pooling information and setting their own goals, overseen by a benignly coordinating center. It sounds democratic, efficient, and almost too good to be true. That is because it is. The authors have mistaken a set of carefully curated anecdotes for a generalizable model, and in doing so, they gravely underestimate the dangers of dismantling the command-and-control system that has been the backbone of American environmental protection for three decades.

The central flaw is the conflation of participation with protection. The essay celebrates the Chesapeake Bay Program’s tributary teams and Southern California’s HCPs as exemplars of deep use. But look under the surface: the Chesapeake Bay is still profoundly degraded. Nutrient reduction targets have been repeatedly missed. The voluntary, collaborative model has proven agonizingly slow in producing real, measurable improvements in water quality, while thousands of farms and suburban lawns continue to send excess nitrogen and phosphorus into the water. The 40% reduction goal by 2000 will almost certainly not be met. The reason is simple: local autonomy, in the absence of a firm regulatory stick, tilts overwhelmingly toward the interests of those with the most to lose from mandatory restrictions—developers, large agricultural operations, and industrial users. The narrative of cooperation masks a power imbalance that citizen volunteers, however well-intentioned, cannot overcome.

The HCP story is even more troubling. The authors acknowledge that many HCPs are little more than bilateral deals between a permit-seeker and a field agent, lacking rigorous monitoring or meaningful public participation. They pin their hopes on a Miller Bill that may never pass, and on new FWS guidance that is, by their own description, tepid. But the structural problem is not that the rolling-rule mechanism hasn’t been fully built; it’s that the very premise of local deal-making undermines the foundational principle of the Endangered Species Act: that no economic interest should be allowed to push a species to extinction. The ESA’s Section 9 is rigid because extinction is irreversible. By turning that prohibition into a bargaining chip—offering an “incidental take” permit in exchange for some negotiated conservation measures—we shift the burden of proof onto the species. The authors praise the San Diego multi-species plan, yet many conservation biologists remain deeply worried that the reserve design is inadequate, that buffer zones are too narrow, and that the adaptive management triggers are too weak. The plan may simply be a more sophisticated way of writing a death warrant for the gnatcatcher and a dozen other species, dressed in the language of collaborative governance.

The Toxics Release Inventory is a genuine success story of information disclosure, but it is not a replacement for regulation. Public shaming worked because it augmented, not supplanted, the underlying threat of enforcement actions. Companies reduced emissions partly to avoid bad press, but also because they suspected that high reported numbers would invite lawsuits and tighter legal restrictions. TURA in Massachusetts adds planning requirements, but as the authors note, it imposes no penalties for failing to act on those plans. The 20% reduction in toxics use is encouraging, but it is a reduction from a very high baseline, and the easiest, most cost-effective reductions are always harvested first. What happens when the low-hanging fruit is gone, and firms face the need for truly expensive process changes? Voluntary planning, without a mandatory backstop, will stall. The claim that 86% of firms would continue to plan even absent legal requirements is a survey response, not a revealed preference. It is easy to say one would voluntarily do the right thing when the legal requirement remains in place as a safety net.

The nuclear industry example is particularly misleading. INPO succeeded not because it was a model of collaborative self-governance, but because the Nuclear Regulatory Commission stood behind it with formidable enforcement power. The NRC could pull an operating license; that ultimate authority gave INPO’s peer reviews teeth that Responsible Care, a purely private Chemical Manufacturers’ Association program, conspicuously lacks. The essay admits that Responsible Care faces a “stalemate” and risks becoming a public relations maneuver. Exactly so. And that is the far more likely outcome for expanded rolling-rule systems that lack a robust, enforceable federal floor. In the case of non-point source water pollution, there is no federal floor at all. The Clean Water Act’s failure to regulate agricultural runoff is precisely the reason the Chesapeake Bay is in trouble. Turning that regulatory void over to tributary teams, no matter how thoughtfully convened, is an abdication of federal responsibility.

The authors call for Congress to give legislative legitimacy to this new architecture. But the legislation they envision—exemplified by the Miller Bill—would essentially codify a system of procedural benchmarks (planning, reporting, monitoring) without substantively requiring actual environmental results. It would be a “process” law, not an “outcome” law. The history of administrative law teaches that procedural mandates are easily captured and gamed by sophisticated repeat players. A developer can hire a team of consultants to produce a volumious monitoring report and a beautifully adaptive management plan, while the habitat continues to shrink. The promise of transparency and public participation, absent the resources for local communities to hire their own experts and lawyers, becomes a hollow ritual.

The first generation of backyard environmentalism was born of a deep and justified distrust of distant corporations and captured agencies. It demanded that government stop harmful activities, not merely negotiate over how much harm is acceptable. The rolling-rule regime, for all its talk of democratic deepening, risks returning us to a world where the fox and the henhouse jointly design a collaborative poultry management plan. Environmentalists should be not opponents of innovation, but they must be defenders of the non-negotiable bottom lines—numeric water quality standards, prohibitions on taking endangered species, and enforceable caps on toxics—that the command-and-control system, for all its flaws, has incrementally put in place. The new architecture may have a role as a supplement, a way to achieve above-and-beyond performance in willing communities. But as a replacement for the backbone of regulatory obligation, it is a recipe for erosion, not progress.

Vanilla Claude

"While the proposed 'rolling-rule' regulatory framework identifies a significant shift toward performance-based environmentalism, it overstates the success of its case studies and fails to acknowledge that these collaborative regimes still depend on traditional coercive legal authority and face serious distributive inequities."

Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen have identified something real. The regulatory experiments they describe—TRI, TURA, the Chesapeake Bay Program, the southern California HCPs—do exhibit family resemblances that conventional categories of "command-and-control" versus "market mechanism" fail to capture. The authors deserve credit for naming a pattern that has been emerging piecemeal and for taking seriously the democratic possibilities latent in it. But the essay oversells its cases, underestimates the persistent role of coercion and adversarial politics in making any of these regimes work, and skates past distributive questions that should make us cautious about declaring a new democratic dawn.

Consider how the essay reads its own evidence. TRI is treated as a triumph of "benchmarking" and "alarming information." Yet TRI works precisely because it is a mandatory federal disclosure statute, backed by citizen-suit provisions, layered on top of the Clean Air and Water Acts and the Superfund liability regime. Take away those background commands and the "right-to-know" loses its bite: firms reduce reported emissions because lawsuits, share-price reactions, and the threat of additional command regulation impose real costs. The deliberative architecture is parasitic on the hierarchical one. The authors hint at this when they concede that Responsible Care has stalled in the absence of public authority, and that INPO became effective only when the NRC adopted its standards. The honest reading of these paired cases is not that "rolling-rule" regimes substitute for command, but that they require it as scaffolding.

The Chesapeake Bay case raises similar doubts. The 1987 Agreement committed the signatories to a 40-percent reduction in nutrient loadings by 2000. As of 1999, with the deadline a few months away, nitrogen reductions remain well short of that figure, and the program has quietly re-described its targets in tributary-specific terms—an institutional move the authors celebrate as adaptive learning, but which an unsympathetic reader could call goal-shifting in the face of failure. "Adaptive management" is a real virtue when it tracks new ecological knowledge; it is a vice when it absorbs political failures into ever-revised baselines. The essay never specifies how, in a rolling-rule regime, one distinguishes the two.

The HCP case is where the argument is most vulnerable, despite—or because of—the authors' enthusiasm. The 1994 "No Surprises" policy, codified in regulation earlier this year, guarantees permittees that once an HCP is approved, the federal government will not require additional land, water, or financial commitments if circumstances change, even if a covered species declines further. Permits run for periods of up to a century. This is the opposite of rolling-rule logic: it locks today's scientific understanding into a long-term contract precisely so that landowners will come to the table. The authors acknowledge that the southern California plans depend on "adaptive management" provisions, but they do not grapple with how those provisions interact with No Surprises, nor with the criticisms—voiced by Kareiva and colleagues in the very report they cite—that biological monitoring in most HCPs is too weak to detect failure in time to act. The Miller Bill might fix some of this, but it has not passed and shows no signs of imminent passage; building an architectural theory on prospective legislation is a thin foundation.

A second blind spot is distributive. The "deep user" sounds appealing in the abstract, but participation in tributary teams, planner certifications, and habitat-plan negotiations is enormously time-intensive. It systematically favors organized landowners, agricultural interests, and professionalized environmental groups over poor and minority communities whose backyards bear disproportionate environmental burdens. The environmental-justice scholarship of the past decade—Bullard's work on toxic siting, the United Church of Christ studies, the executive order on environmental justice—has documented that local deliberative bodies can entrench rather than correct these inequities. The essay's section on Jacksonian and Progressive antecedents is elegant, but it omits the populist tradition's darker side: that local control was historically a mechanism for racial exclusion as often as for democratic empowerment. A theory of deliberative environmental democracy that does not address who actually shows up at the table, and who pays the costs of showing up, is not yet a theory of democracy.

Third, the essay's pluralist counterfactual is a straw man. The authors mock the Interior Department's faith that national groups will police local under-enforcement and property owners will police over-enforcement. But this is what is actually happening: it is the National Wildlife Federation, the Center for Biological Diversity petitions, and litigation under the ESA that have forced FWS to draft the guidance the authors approve of. The pluralist machinery has not been displaced; it has produced the very correctives the essay celebrates.

None of this defeats the core insight. Performance-based regulation that pools information, requires self-monitoring, and revises standards iteratively is a genuine advance over both rigid command and naive market simulation. But its democratic credentials depend on hard law, robust monitoring, real sanctions for non-performance, and explicit attention to who participates. Without those, "rolling-rule" risks becoming what its critics already suspect: a sophisticated name for negotiated under-enforcement.

Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen have written a landmark essay, not just for its intellectual ambition but for its painstaking engagement with the messy, on-the-ground reality of environmental policy. They identify a genuine and widespread shift away from rigid command-and-control regulation, and they give it a name and a theoretical coherence. The “rolling-rule regime” is a powerful analytical lens. But as a practitioner who has worked with some of these programs, I see a wide gulf between the elegant architecture the authors describe and the gritty, under-resourced world in which these experiments must actually function. The question is not whether the model is desirable, but whether it can be made to work at scale without a massive infusion of resources and capacity that currently seems politically implausible.

The authors’ own case studies reveal the fragility of the new institutions. The Chesapeake Bay Program is cited as the most mature example, yet its nutrient reduction goals are behind schedule, its governance relies on legally dodgy “directives,” and its tributary teams are a very recent innovation whose effectiveness is entirely unproven. The Bay Program has been building its collaborative framework for fifteen years, supported by a unique combination of regional civic pride, strong philanthropic support from the Chesapeake Bay Foundation, and the sustained attention of EPA Region III. Can this model be replicated in the hundreds of other watersheds across the country that lack these advantages? The EPA is indeed trying to model new programs on the Chesapeake experience, but the agency’s budget has been largely flat or declining in real terms for years. State environmental agencies are even more strapped. The pool of trained facilitators, scientists willing to engage in community-level work, and citizen volunteers with the time and dedication to serve on tributary teams is limited. The rolling-rule model demands a dense institutional infrastructure—technical assistance offices, training institutes, information-pooling databases, peer inspectorates—that Massachusetts’ TURA provides, but that is completely absent in most states and for most environmental problems.

Consider the HCP experience. The authors note the highly variable quality of plans, with many being little more than bilateral deals. They place hope in a new FWS database and the Miller Bill’s reporting requirements. But a database is not a functioning system of pooled learning. Someone has to analyze the data, extract lessons, and feed them back to local actors. The FWS field office staff are already overwhelmed with processing permits and conducting basic consultations. They have no time to become sophisticated knowledge managers. The insight from INPO—that private pooling only became effective when it coupled benchmarking with the authority to suspend uncooperative members and when it was backed by the NRC’s hard regulatory power—is sobering. The FWS has no comparable ability to “suspend” a bad HCP without revoking the permit, an action that would provoke an immediate legal firestorm. The political economy of habitat conservation planning is one of extreme pressure to approve plans and keep the development pipeline moving. The center’s capacity for disciplined, evidence-based review is simply not there.

The Toxics Release Inventory is the most robust success, but it is a uniquely light-touch tool. Its power comes from the simplicity of requiring firms to report a limited set of numbers, which are then made public. It piggybacks on the pre-existing reputational and financial market incentives of large publicly traded firms. But when the problem moves beyond large industrial emitters to small, diffuse non-point sources—farms, households, small businesses—this mechanism fails. A thousand dairy farms are not going to be disciplined by a Greenpeace press release in the way DuPont is. The rolling-rule approach for non-point sources requires direct, face-to-face engagement: tributary teams, agricultural extension agents, community workshops. That is labor-intensive, slow, and requires a completely different skill set within agencies that have traditionally been staffed by engineers and enforcement lawyers, not community organizers and adult educators.

The democratic promise of “deep use” is also deeply demanding. The authors imagine active citizens who move from NIMBY opposition to co-management, using expert protocols to monitor their own streams and negotiate with farmers. I have seen such citizens in action, and they are extraordinary—early retirees with scientific backgrounds, passionate local activists who can afford to attend six-hour meetings on weeknights. They are not the norm. For most working people, especially in the low-income and minority communities that bear the heaviest environmental burdens, the barriers to this kind of participation are near-insurmountable. Time, transportation, childcare, and the sheer technical complexity of the issues all stand in the way. If we shift the locus of environmental protection from a federal agency that can be sued for failing to enforce the law, to a local collaborative body that meets on Tuesday evenings, we will effectively disenfranchise a huge swath of the population unless we also fund a parallel system of community-based advocacy and technical support. The Toxics Use Reduction Institute and the Office of Technical Assistance in Massachusetts cost money. Most states, and certainly most federal programs, do not have comparable line items.

There is also a more prosaic concern: the potential for collaborative overload. The same industries, agencies, and citizens that are being asked to participate in an HCP for endangered species are simultaneously being convened for watershed planning, air quality forums, transportation land-use planning, and a dozen other participatory processes spawned by the “new governance” trend. Collaboration fatigue is real, and it disproportionately affects the smaller, less well-staffed stakeholders—the environmental NGO with two employees, the tribal government, the small farmer. The rolling-rule model presumes a boundless supply of civic energy and attention. In reality, these processes compete for a limited pool of goodwill, and the danger is that they will all become perfunctory exercises, producing plans that sit on shelves while the underlying environmental problems continue.

None of this is to say the project should be abandoned. The authors are right that command-and-control has hit its limits, and that markets alone cannot solve problems like non-point source pollution and habitat fragmentation. The rolling-rule model is the best conceptual framework we have for the next stage. But its proponents must be honest about the scale of investment required to build the scaffolding. That means substantial new funding for the EPA and state agencies, not for more rule-writers in Washington, but for field-level coordinators, information systems, training programs, and public participation grants. It means Congress must do more than pass a Miller Bill; it must appropriate the money to support the monitoring and technical assistance that the bill would require. And it means that the new regime must be built in parallel with the old, not as a replacement for enforceable minimum standards, but as a way to achieve the above-and-beyond performance that will be necessary to meet the huge environmental challenges—climate change, biodiversity loss, persistent toxics—that the rigid, fragmented statutes of the 1970s were never designed to address. The vision is compelling; the implementation is daunting. We need to focus now on the unglamorous work of capacity-building.

The lead essay presents the "rolling-rule regime" as a triumph of practical deliberation over the limitations of both command regulation and market-based approaches. Yet a closer examination reveals that what is being celebrated may be less a novel democratic innovation than a systematic weakening of environmental protections dressed in the language of participation and adaptation.

Consider the Habitat Conservation Plans examined in the essay. These plans emerged because Section 9 of the Endangered Species Act threatened to halt development wherever endangered species were found. Landowners, developers, and local governments recoiled at this prospect, and Congress responded by creating the HCP mechanism as a escape valve. The result has been a systematic trading of endangered species protection for development permits. The "incidental take" permits that HCPs provide allow developers to proceed with projects that harm listed species, provided they set aside some habitat and implement mitigation measures. The authors acknowledge that "many of these agreements produce more, better, and more sophisticated ecosystem management regimes than would emerge from even the strictest application of Section 9." But this comparison is misleading. The question is not whether HCPs outperform the polar alternative of rigid enforcement; it is whether they achieve adequate protection relative to what the law actually requires.

The "adaptive management" rhetoric that pervades HCP discourse functions as a legitimating smokescreen for inadequate conservation. When a plan fails to achieve its biological objectives—if species continue to decline, if habitat restoration proves insufficient—proponents respond that management must "adapt." But adaptation can become an endless deferral of accountability. Each failure becomes evidence that more learning is needed before tough decisions can be made, while the species at risk continue their slide toward extinction.

The Chesapeake Bay Program presents a similarly troubling picture. While the authors celebrate the program's evolution toward participatory tributary teams, they acknowledge that "the very fluidity of the internal governance of the program becomes a liability, as external interlocutors seek, in vain, to determine the authoritative voice of an institutional ensemble that adjusts precisely by not having one." This institutional opacity makes accountability extremely difficult. When nitrogen loading targets are not met, when fish populations continue to decline, when agricultural operations evade their obligations—where is the mechanism for corrective action? The program's governance structure seems designed to diffuse responsibility so thoroughly that no actor can be held accountable for failure.

The broader philosophical commitments underlying the rolling-rule regime also warrant scrutiny. The authors embrace what they call a "pragmatist" approach that "rejects immutable principles." But environmental law embodies fundamental commitments—to preventing extinction, to protecting public health, to ensuring that polluters bear the costs of their contamination. These are not bureaucratic rigidities to be transcended through clever institutional design; they are moral commitments that constrain the range of acceptable outcomes. A regime that treats these commitments as merely provisional, subject to revision through deliberative processes among affected stakeholders, may prove unable to resist the relentless pressure that economic interests bring to environmental debates.

The authors note that some environmentalists prefer "strict application of Section 9" and dismiss this position as insufficiently sophisticated. But there is wisdom in this preference. When statutory protections can be traded away through negotiated agreements, when minimum standards are understood as merely temporary benchmarks subject to continuous revision, when accountability is diffused across multiple levels of governance—environmental protection becomes whatever powerful interests will allow. The rolling-rule regime may be innovative, but innovation in service of accommodating development is not necessarily progress.

The vision of a “rolling-rule regime” outlined by Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen is an important and in many ways attractive departure from the stale debate between rigid command-and-control regulation and the false promise of market-based panaceas. They are right that the old models have failed to cope with complexity, discouraged local innovation, and, in the case of tradable permits, demanded levels of information that regulators cannot muster. Their case studies—from the Toxics Release Inventory to the Chesapeake Bay Program—genuinely show that pragmatic, information-driven collaboration can produce environmental improvements that top-down rules alone could not. The essay’s optimism about the transformative power of deliberation and local autonomy glosses over deep problems of power, equity, and democratic accountability that, left unaddressed, could make the “rolling-rule regime” a vehicle for well-intentioned failure or, worse, a legitimating cover for inaction.

The first difficulty is that the essay treats “backyard environmentalism” as if it were a single, homogeneous tradition. In fact, the community-based struggles of Woburn and Love Canal were profoundly shaped by inequalities of race and class. The emerging environmental justice movement has made clear that low-income communities and communities of color disproportionately bear the burdens of toxic exposure, and they are often the very communities least able to mount the sophisticated, resource-intensive forms of participation the new regime demands. The “tributary teams” of the Chesapeake and the multi-stakeholder negotiations of the Southern California HCPs sound admirably inclusive, but they tend to draw on people with the time, expertise, and political connections to sit at the table. It is no accident that the most celebrated successes, such as the Massachusetts TURA, occurred in a wealthy, well-organized state with a strong public-interest infrastructure. Without a deliberate effort to build capacity in overburdened communities and to ensure that their voices are not merely present but genuinely influential, performance-based regulation may simply shift power from distant bureaucracies to local elites.

A related concern is enforceability. The essay rightly insists that the new framework is not voluntarism, and that central authorities must hold local units accountable. But it is remarkably vague about the mechanisms. When a firm or a tributary team fails to meet its own performance targets, what happens? The authors point to peer pressure, reputational penalties, and the publication of comparative data as the primary discipline. TRI’s effect on stock prices is offered as proof that information alone can drive improvement. But stock markets react to many factors; the initial TRI releases led to headline-grabbing drops, but as investors grew accustomed to the data, the shock value likely diminished. More fundamentally, many of the actors in environmental governance are not publicly traded companies: they are private landowners, small farms, or municipalities. For them, public comparison may sting but not compel if the cost of compliance is high. The Nuclear Regulatory Commission retained formal authority to impose sanctions on power plants that failed INPO standards. By contrast, the Chesapeake Executive Council’s directives are of “dubious legal pedigree,” and TURA penalizes only willful violations of reporting duties, not failure to meet reduction targets. If the rolling-rule regime is to avoid the fate of Responsible Care’s vacillation—where a trade association couldn’t bring itself to discipline its own members—it must be backed by real regulatory teeth. The essay envisions Congress eventually conferring legitimacy on the new architecture, but until such a legislative framework is in place, the line between accountability and voluntarism remains dangerously thin.

The essay also makes a grand democratic claim: that the new regulatory form points toward a “directly deliberative participatory democracy” that resolves the century-old tension between efficiency and self-rule. The ambition is bracing, but the evidence falls short. In many of the examples, the circle of genuine decision-makers is narrow. HCPs, as the authors concede, often amount to a deal between a permit applicant and a field agent, with little meaningful public oversight. Even where participation is broader, it frequently mimics expert knowledge: citizens are trained to monitor water quality according to scientific protocols, effectively becoming “quasi-experts.” That is a valuable civic contribution, but it is not the same as deliberating about values, priorities, or the distribution of environmental harms and benefits. The risk is that this style of participation privileges technical learning over the messy, adversarial politics through which marginalized groups can challenge entrenched interests. The essay dismisses pluralist interest-group bargaining as prone to wild swings between over- and under-enforcement, but pluralism at least provides multiple access points for organized dissent. A system that funnels conflict into collaborative problem-solving teams may, in practice, suppress exactly the sort of fundamental criticism that the early backyard environmentalists wielded so effectively against corporate and governmental power.

Perhaps the most telling blind spot concerns the role of science itself. The essay repeatedly emphasizes that we can never have enough information and that surprise is inevitable. That is a wise starting point. But it then places enormous faith in benchmarking, monitoring, and adaptive management to generate continuous improvement. Yet the history of environmental policy is littered with monitoring programs that produced torrents of data but only modest action. The Chesapeake Bay Program is again instructive: despite two decades of agreements, ambitious targets, and an army of citizen volunteers, by 1999 nutrient loadings have not fallen nearly enough, and the 40 percent reduction goal for 2000 will almost certainly be missed. The problem is not that the collaborative process lacks intelligence, but that the economic and political incentives running against tough action remain formidable. A rolling-rule regime can refine standards over time, but it cannot by itself create the political will to impose costs on powerful agricultural or development interests.

None of this is to reject the pragmatist impulse that infuses the essay. The current regulatory system is indeed overloaded and often counterproductive, and the experiments with information pooling, local experimentation, and collaborative planning deserve to be expanded and refined. But they should be seen as supplements to, not replacements for, a strong federal regulatory floor. The most successful innovations—like TRI—worked because they were built on statutory disclosure requirements with clear penalties for noncompliance, and because they armed citizens with information they could use in traditional legal and political arenas. A genuine democratic renewal in environmental policy will require not just better collaboration between citizens and experts, but also robust legal rights, genuine enforcement capacity, and an unwavering commitment to ensuring that the poorest and most vulnerable backyards are not sacrificed to a consensus they had little part in shaping. Anything less risks turning a promising reform into a technocratic mirage.

Archon Fung, Charles Sabel, and Bradley Karkkainen offer a deeply appealing vision of environmental governance for the twenty-first century. There is no denying that TRI has been a powerful tool, substituting the heavy hand of direct emission limits with the "sunlight" of mandatory disclosure. But information pooling does not automatically yield improved performance unless there is a mechanism to translate data into leverage. TRI works largely because poor environmental performance can depress stock prices and invite massive public relations crises. This relies on a specific set of market conditions—usually involving consumer-facing corporations vulnerable to public shaming. When the polluter is a local monopoly, a municipality, or a diffuse network of agricultural operations, information alone does not alter behavior. Even the Institute of Nuclear Power Operations (INPO), which the authors cite as a model of private standard-setting, only achieved its safety improvements because the Nuclear Regulatory Commission stood behind it, holding the ultimate authority to shut down non-compliant reactors.

In a performance-based regime, local units are tasked with setting targets, devising metrics, and monitoring outcomes. Participating effectively in a Habitat Conservation Plan negotiation or a Chesapeake Bay tributary team requires immense resources: attorneys, hydrologists, biological modelers, and, most importantly, time. Corporate stakeholders and large developers possess these resources and treat them as standard business expenses. For local citizens and volunteer environmentalists, this level of engagement represents a staggering, often unsustainable burden.

By demanding "deep use," the rolling-rule regime effectively privatizes the costs of regulatory design and enforcement, shifting them onto civil society. Over time, this dynamic is highly susceptible to local regulatory capture. As citizen volunteers inevitably exhaust their limited resources and burn out, well-funded corporate interests are left to dominate the supposedly collaborative tributary teams and working groups. When the center devolves authority to the periphery without heavily subsidizing the capacity of ordinary citizens to participate, "direct deliberation" simply becomes a venue where the most well-resourced voices dictate the terms of their own regulation.

The authors also dismiss pluralist interest-group politics as anachronistic, suggesting that disciplined deliberation can lead protagonists to "discover unanticipated solutions provisionally acceptable to all." This fundamentally misreads the nature of many environmental disputes, which are often rooted in zero-sum conflicts over values and material interests. A real estate developer’s financial imperative to pave a coastal sage scrub habitat and a conservationist’s moral imperative to preserve it for the California gnatcatcher cannot always be harmonized through "continuous benchmarking" or "adaptive management." Pluralist politics, with all its messy, centralized legislative and judicial battles, exists precisely to arbitrate between competing values that cannot be seamlessly integrated at the local level.

Ultimately, the innovations the authors describe—flexible compliance, information disclosure, and local adaptation—are highly valuable administrative tools that are already improving how the EPA and the Department of the Interior do their jobs. But they do not constitute a new architecture of democracy that can stand independent of centralized command. They are, instead, specialized grafts on the sturdy, if inflexible, trunk of the bedrock environmental statutes of the 1970s. To ensure that "backyard environmentalism" serves the public interest rather than the interests of the regulated, we must maintain the unyielding federal standards that force private actors to the table in the first place.

The Clean Air Act, the Clean Water Act, and the Endangered Species Act establish minimum requirements that states may exceed but not undercut. This structure reflects constitutional allocations of authority and political judgments about the appropriate distribution of regulatory responsibility. The rolling-rule regime proposes something different: broad local autonomy within general guidelines, with accountability achieved through information pooling and performance comparison rather than hierarchical command.

The Chesapeake Bay Program illustrates the institutional complexity this entails. The program's signatories include EPA, the governors of Maryland, Virginia, and Pennsylvania, and the mayor of the District of Columbia. The Executive Council and implementation committee operate through consensus, with directives that possess "dubious legal pedigree and status" yet are considered "morally binding." The program's flexibility enables adaptation to local conditions, but it also enables evasion of accountability.

The rolling-rule regime operates through less visible processes. Local deliberation among affected stakeholders determines what counts as acceptable performance.   1. 01The lack of clear legal authority in collaborative frameworks like the Chesapeake Bay Program makes it difficult to compel corrective action when environmental targets are missed.
  2. 02The administrative capacity required for national authorities to effectively monitor and pool information from thousands of local experiments is currently non-existent or severely limited.
  3. 03The shift from transparent, judicial-reviewed federal rulemaking to less visible local deliberations reduces the public's ability to hold specific officials accountable for regulatory failures.
  4. 04The rolling-rule model assumes a shared commitment to environmental goals that may not exist when powerful economic interests dominate local stakeholder negotiations.
  5. 05The 'dubious legal pedigree' of the directives used in these new regimes creates a constitutional and practical vacuum regarding who is ultimately responsible for enforcing standards.
