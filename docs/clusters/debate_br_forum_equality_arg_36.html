Diversified Gemini

"While Sypnowich correctly critiques the harshness of luck egalitarianism, her proposal to mandate equal outcomes (functionings) rather than equal capabilities (real freedoms) risks paternalistic overreach and violates individual agency."

In her essay "Is Equal Opportunity Enough?", Christine Sypnowich successfully exposes the moral bankruptcy of the prevailing "luck egalitarian" consensus. She rightfully criticizes a political philosophy that obsessively parsing human misery to determine what was "chosen" and what was "circumstance," offering a compelling vision of socialism rooted in Amartya Sen’s concept of capability and human flourishing. However, from the perspective of the Capabilities Approach itself, Sypnowich’s argument conflates vital distinctions, misreading Sen and Martha Nussbaum by advocating for equality of *functionings* (outcomes) rather than equality of *capabilities* (real freedoms). This distinction is not mere semantic quibbling; it is the crux of how we balance justice with human dignity and pluralism.

Sypnowich asserts that Sen and Nussbaum have "tailored their view to take account of choice and responsibility," criticizing Nussbaum for focusing on the "capability to function, not functioning per se." She argues that this is essentially a regression to "opportunity talk," complaining that "the political community steps back" once capabilities are provided, washing its hands if people fail to translate those capabilities into actual flourishing. Sypnowich wants the state to ensure the flourishing itself.

To understand why Sypnowich is mistaken, we must clarify what capability theorists mean. A *functioning* is an active realization of a state of being or doing: being well-nourished, being educated, participating in the community. A *capability* is the genuine, substantive freedom to achieve that functioning. The classic example in capability literature is the difference between a person who is starving due to famine and a person who is fasting for religious or political reasons. Both are experiencing the exact same "outcome" or "functioning" (undernourishment). If a state mandates equality of outcomes, it must force-feed the fasting person. If the state focuses on capabilities, it ensures that both individuals have abundant access to food, but respects the agency of the person who chooses to fast.

Sypnowich dismisses this as an unhealthy squeamishness about "taking a stand on how people should live." But forcing the fasting person to eat is not "taking a stand"—it is a violation of bodily autonomy and religious freedom. By arguing that we must ensure equality of actual flourishing (functionings) rather than the capability to flourish, Sypnowich advocates for a society that overrides the individual’s right to define their own path.

Sypnowich attempts to soften the authoritarian implications of her argument by insisting we should use "incentives, education, and supporting institutions" rather than "out and out coercion." She uses the example of music education, arguing that making lessons available isn't enough; we need "compulsory arts and music education" to ensure equal flourishing. But how far does this logic extend? What if the capability in question is bodily health, and an individual chooses to eat a poor diet and not exercise? A capabilities approach ensures they have access to healthy food, safe places to exercise, and healthcare, but respects their choice not to use them. Does Sypnowich’s outcome-oriented society mandate morning calisthenics and ban sugar to ensure the "flourishing" outcome?

Furthermore, Sypnowich’s critique that capabilities theory is just another form of "opportunity talk" misunderstands how radically demanding the capabilities approach truly is. Equalizing capabilities is not the thin, formal "equality of opportunity" of the neoliberal market. To give someone the genuine capability to be politically active, well-nourished, educated, and free from violence requires an absolute transformation of the material and social structures of capitalism. It requires eradicating systemic racism, guaranteeing material wealth, and dismantling patriarchal structures. It requires, as Sypnowich desires, addressing alienation, loneliness, and environmental degradation. The capabilities approach does not "wash its hands" at the starting line; it actively structures society so that the threshold for a dignified life is accessible to all.

Sypnowich is right that humans are socially embedded creatures and that our choices are shaped by our environments. She is also correct to criticize luck egalitarians for their harsh, punitive approach to those who make "bad" choices. The capabilities approach agrees: we do not let the imprudent starve, because the capability to be nourished and housed must be permanently guaranteed, regardless of past choices.

But acknowledging social conditioning does not mean we must extinguish the concept of agency altogether. As Nussbaum has argued, practical reason—the ability to form a conception of the good and to engage in critical reflection about the planning of one's life—is an architectonic capability. It is what makes us human. Sypnowich’s proposal to mandate outcomes treats citizens as passive vessels to be filled with state-approved "flourishing," explicitly rejecting their practical reason if it leads them away from the societal consensus.

Equalizing capabilities, rather than functionings, walks the narrow, necessary path between two disastrous extremes. It rejects the cruel indifference of laissez-faire opportunity, demanding robust material justice and structural transformation. But it also rejects the paternalistic overreach of mandated outcomes, preserving the individual’s right to fast, to decline the music lesson, and to author their own life.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Focusing on outcomes rather than capabilities fails to distinguish between those deprived of a good and those who choose to abstain from it for personal, religious, or political reasons.
  2. 02Mandating actual flourishing necessitates a violation of bodily autonomy and the right to define one's own path in life.
  3. 03The capabilities approach is not 'thin' opportunity talk; it requires a radical transformation of material and social structures to ensure the substantive freedom to flourish is accessible to all.
  4. 04Acknowledging that choices are socially embedded does not justify extinguishing individual agency or the 'architectonic' capability of practical reason.
  5. 05Equalizing capabilities provides a necessary middle ground that demands robust material justice while preserving the individual's right to author their own life.
