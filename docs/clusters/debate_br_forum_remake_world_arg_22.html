Diversified DeepSeek

"While Walter Johnson’s critique of racial capitalism is historically profound, it fails to provide a viable path toward justice because it neglects the essential role of the state in implementing material remedies like reparations."

There is a ghost haunting Walter Johnson’s essay, and it is the ghost of Karl Marx. Johnson invokes Marx explicitly—the critique of rights in “On the Jewish Question,” the distinction between political and human emancipation—and then, through the figure of Cedric Robinson, sets out to correct Marx’s blind spots on race and slavery. But the ghost is not so easily exorcised. For all the power of Johnson’s account of racial capitalism, and for all the importance of Robinson’s insistence that capitalism and racism were “inseparable” from their origins, Johnson’s essay ultimately reproduces a fundamental limitation of the Marxist tradition he seeks to escape: an inability to reckon seriously with the role of the state in both producing and potentially remedying racial injustice.

Let me be clear about what Johnson gets right. His critique of the “dehumanization” trope in slavery historiography is overdue and devastating. The idea that slaveholders had to believe their slaves were less than human in order to exploit them is not only historically false but morally evasive. It turns a specific, institutionally organized form of violence into a psychological failing, as if the problem with slavery was that slaveholders had bad thoughts about their captives rather than that they bought, sold, worked, and bred them for profit. Johnson is right to insist that slavery depended on the humanity of enslaved people—their intelligence, their desires, their fears, their capacity to suffer and to bear witness. A hoe handle jammed down a throat is not an act of dehumanization; it is an act of torture, and torture is something humans do to humans.

Johnson is also right to place the history of slavery at the center of any account of modern capitalism. The numbers he cites are staggering and incontrovertible: enslaved people as both labor and capital, their value in 1860 equal to all the capital invested in American railroads, manufacturing, and agricultural land combined. The cotton that enslaved people picked financed the industrialization of Britain and the financialization of New York. The land they worked was stolen from Native nations through a legal regime that Johnson traces, with surgical precision, to the Supreme Court’s decision in Johnson v. McIntosh. This is the story of American capitalism, and Johnson tells it with commendable clarity and passion.

But then comes the turn. Having established that slavery was integral to capitalism, and that the legacy of slavery persists in contemporary patterns of racial inequality, Johnson proposes an “account of justice rooted in the history of slavery and racial capitalism.” This account, he tells us, would have six virtues: it would view injustice from the standpoint of the Global South; it would focus on resource extraction and distribution; it would link present privilege to past wrongs; it would attend to gender, sexuality, and reproduction; it would refuse the separation of race and class; and it would connect the instrumentalization of human beings to the instrumentalization of nature. What this account would not do, apparently, is make any specific claims on the state. Johnson does not mention legislation, regulation, taxation, or public investment. He does not discuss the administrative apparatus that would be required to implement reparations or redistribute resources. He does not engage with the question of political power—who wields it, how it is legitimated, and how it might be transformed.

This is not an oversight; it is a structural feature of the intellectual tradition Johnson is working within. The black radical tradition, as Robinson describes it, is deeply suspicious of the state. This suspicion is well-founded. The American state was built on slavery and Native dispossession. Its legal system sanctified property in human beings. Its military enforced the slave regime. After emancipation, the state facilitated the reimposition of white supremacy through Black Codes, convict leasing, Jim Crow, redlining, and mass incarceration. To look to the state for justice can seem, from this perspective, like asking the arsonist to put out the fire.

And yet. And yet the state is also the only institution in modern society with the capacity to collect and redistribute resources on the scale that reparations would require. It is the only institution with the legal authority to define and enforce rights. It is the only institution that can, at least in principle, be held democratically accountable. The Movement for Black Lives platform that Johnson approvingly cites is, whatever its anarchist and abolitionist inflections, a set of demands on the state: demands for legislation, for executive action, for judicial remedies. When Black Lives Matter activists call for an end to police violence, they are calling for changes in the laws and practices of state agencies. When they call for reparations, they are calling for a massive transfer of public resources. These are not demands that can be met by the spontaneous democratic practices of the oppressed, important as those practices are for building solidarity and consciousness. They require engaging with the grubby, compromised, inherently frustrating work of politics.

Johnson’s essay, for all its radicalism, is strangely apolitical. It offers a powerful critique of liberal rights discourse, but it does not offer an alternative theory of political obligation or institutional design. It gestures toward a “just world” without specifying how that world would be governed, by whom, or according to what procedures. This is a problem not only for Johnson’s argument but for the broader intellectual movement of which it is a part. The “new history of capitalism” has done invaluable work in demonstrating the centrality of slavery to American economic development. But it has had less to say about what follows from that demonstration. If slavery was foundational, then its legacy is everywhere—not only in the racial wealth gap and the carceral state but in the very structure of property rights, tax law, and federalism. Addressing that legacy requires more than a change in consciousness or a revival of the black radical tradition. It requires a program.

What might such a program look like? I can only sketch it here, but the outlines are visible in the work of scholars and activists who take the history of racial capitalism seriously while also engaging the question of the state. A reparations program, for instance, would require not only a determination of who is owed what but a mechanism for delivery: a federal agency, perhaps, modeled on a truth and reconciliation commission but with the power to make binding awards. It would require a funding source, which in turn would require a reckoning with the tax code—a code that has, since the founding, been shaped by racial considerations, from the three-fifths clause to the mortgage interest deduction. It would require a theory of federalism, because so much of the infrastructure of racial inequality—school funding, zoning, criminal justice—is organized at the state and local level. None of this is glamorous. None of it offers the satisfactions of a purely moral critique. But it is the necessary, painstaking work of translating insight into action.

Johnson closes with Du Bois’s warning about the cost of missed opportunities. It is a warning that should resonate for the left in the present moment. The Movement for Black Lives has, against all odds, succeeded in placing the question of racial justice at the center of American politics. The intellectual groundwork Johnson surveys—the work of Robinson, Du Bois, Williams, Davis, and their inheritors—has never been more widely read or discussed. The question is whether this energy can be channeled into concrete, achievable demands that change the material conditions of people’s lives. If it cannot, if it remains trapped in a posture of critique that refuses engagement with the state as inherently corrupt, then we will indeed miss an opportunity, and the blood of subsequent generations will be on our hands. The ghost of Marx will have the last laugh.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Johnson correctly identifies that slavery relied on the human capacities of the enslaved rather than their 'dehumanization' and was foundational to the development of global capitalism.
  2. 02The black radical tradition's inherent suspicion of the state, while historically justified, creates a structural inability to propose concrete political or institutional solutions.
  3. 03The state is the only modern institution with the scale, legal authority, and potential for democratic accountability required to redistribute resources and enforce restorative justice.
  4. 04A purely moral or historiographical critique of racial capitalism is insufficient without a specific program for legislative, regulatory, and administrative action.
  5. 05Failing to translate the insights of the 'new history of capitalism' into a practical political agenda risks missing a critical historical moment for achieving material change.
