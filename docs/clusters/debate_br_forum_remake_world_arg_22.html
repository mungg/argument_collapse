"A truly radical understanding of racial capitalism must center the experiences of enslaved women, as slavery’s expansion depended on the forced collapse of the distinction between economic production and biological reproduction."

Walter Johnson’s essay asks us to rethink slavery not from the perspective of abstract humanity but from the historical experience of racial capitalism. I want to accept that invitation and press it further. The most radical implications of his argument lie in gender, reproduction, and care. If slavery forces us to rethink capitalism, it does so not only because enslaved people were labor and capital at once, but because enslaved women’s bodies exposed the false separation between production and reproduction on which much political economy still depends.

Johnson notes that racial capitalism “swung on a reproductive hinge.” That sentence should reorganize the entire debate. In the United States after the constitutional end of the transatlantic slave trade, the expansion of slavery depended on forced reproduction. Enslaved children inherited the status of their mothers. The wealth of slaveholders increased not only through crops harvested but through children born into bondage. Enslaved women were workers in the fields and houses, but they were also made into reproducers of property. Their pregnancies, births, lactation, sexual vulnerability, and maternal attachments were absorbed into the market.

This is not an appendix to the history of capitalism. It is one of its foundations. Conventional economic analysis often asks who owns the means of production, who controls labor, who receives wages, who takes profits. Slavery asks another question: who controls the means of reproduction? Under slavery, the answer was brutal. The law denied enslaved women authority over their own bodies and children. Owners claimed the right to sell infants, separate spouses, coerce sex, punish pregnancy when inconvenient, encourage pregnancy when profitable, and convert kinship into asset growth. The marketplace reached into the womb and the nursery.

That fact complicates any easy distinction between labor exploitation and sexual domination. For enslaved women, these were not separate systems. A woman could be forced to work while pregnant, punished for protecting a child, raped by an owner or overseer, compelled to nurse white children while her own went hungry, and sold away from family. Her reproductive life was not private; it was governed by property law. Her sexuality was not merely violated by individual men; it was structured by a regime that made consent legally incoherent. Her motherhood was not recognized as a protected relation; it was treated as an obstacle or instrument of ownership.

Johnson is right to criticize the language of “dehumanization” when it suggests that enslaved people’s humanity was lost. But from the standpoint of enslaved women, we should also notice how often “humanity” has meant masculinity. The liberal human subject is imagined as autonomous, self-owning, contract-making, and unencumbered. He stands before the law as an individual. But pregnancy, childbirth, nursing, dependency, childhood, disability, aging, and care all disturb that fantasy. No human being enters the world as an autonomous rights-bearer. Everyone is born dependent. Everyone survives through care. Slavery’s violence lay partly in its power to commandeer these dependencies and turn them into profit.

A justice rooted in the history of slavery must therefore be a reproductive justice. It must ask not only whether individuals possess formal rights, but whether communities have the material conditions to bear, raise, protect, and sustain children. This includes freedom from sexual violence, access to health care, safe housing, clean water and air, education, freedom from state seizure of children, freedom from coercive sterilization, freedom from police terror, and the resources necessary for family life. It also includes the right not to have children and the right to bodily autonomy. The history of slavery teaches that domination often operates by controlling reproduction, not only by exploiting labor.

This lesson reaches beyond the plantation. Black women in the United States have long encountered state power through contradictory accusations: blamed for having too many children, blamed for not forming proper families, blamed for dependency, blamed for poverty, blamed for the violence inflicted on their communities. Welfare policy, family policing, sterilization abuse, prison visitation rules, public hospital neglect, school closures, and punitive child-support enforcement all carry echoes—not simple repetitions, but echoes—of a society that has treated black reproduction as a public problem to be managed rather than a human good to be supported. A reparative politics must confront those continuities.

Johnson’s essay also invites a critique of the historiography of slavery. He worries that the new history of capitalism can write slavery without enslaved people. I share that worry, but I would put it more sharply: it can write slavery without enslaved women. Even when women appear, they too often appear as demographic variables, reproductive capacity, sexual victims, or illustrations of family separation. We need histories that recover enslaved women as thinkers, strategists, healers, workers, mothers, rebels, lovers, and theologians, while never transforming survival into a sentimental celebration.

The concept of agency is tricky here. Johnson is right that enslaved people’s humanity does not need to be proven by heroic resistance. But the corrective should not be to abandon agency. For enslaved women, agency often appeared in forms that liberal political theory is poorly equipped to recognize: preserving a child’s life; sharing food; feigning illness; transmitting herbal knowledge; negotiating domestic labor; choosing silence; refusing a sexual demand when refusal was dangerous; killing an infant rather than seeing that child enslaved; running away while pregnant; maintaining fictive kin; testifying after emancipation; naming the dead. These acts do not fit neatly into “independent will and volition,” but neither are they reducible to victimization.

Indeed, a black feminist reading of slavery challenges both liberalism and some versions of Marxism. Liberalism begins with the individual owner of himself. Slavery shows that self-ownership was denied through race, gender, and law. Marxism begins with labor and production. Slavery shows that reproduction, sexuality, and kinship are not secondary to production but constitutive of political economy. A theory of racial capitalism adequate to slavery must be feminist at its core, not merely inclusive of women as an additional category.

Johnson’s genealogy—Du Bois, C. L. R. James, Eric Williams, Cedric Robinson—is indispensable, but it should be read alongside scholars and activists who foreground black women’s experience. Deborah Gray White, Angela Davis, Darlene Clark Hine, Barbara Ransby, Jacqueline Jones, Jennifer Morgan, Stephanie Camp, Saidiya Hartman, and many others have taught us that slavery’s political economy cannot be understood apart from gendered domination. Angela Davis, whom Johnson names near the end, is especially important because she links slavery, domestic labor, prison, and reproductive control in ways that remain urgent.

The contemporary implications are plain. The Movement for Black Lives platform’s call for reparations is not only about compensation for unpaid labor, though it is certainly about that. It is about the conditions under which black life can flourish. Reparations should include resources for maternal health, community clinics, mental health care, childcare, schools, housing, protection from environmental toxins, and freedom from criminalization. It should include repair for families separated by slavery, by convict leasing, by migration forced through economic deprivation, by prisons, and by foster systems that punish poverty. It should include investment in black women’s leadership, because black women have so often carried movements while being asked to wait for their own liberation.

Racial capitalism also helps us see how care work remains devalued. The afterlife of slavery is not only in prisons and police; it is in the low wages of domestic workers, nursing assistants, home health aides, childcare workers, and service workers, many of them black women and immigrant women. The intimate labor of sustaining life is treated as naturally cheap because it has been associated with women, with racialized women especially, and with servitude. A society that pays extravagantly for speculation while underpaying care has not escaped the moral economy of slavery as completely as it imagines.

Here the language of human rights can be both useful and insufficient. The right to bodily integrity, the right to family, the right against forced labor, and the right against discrimination are essential. But a woman may formally possess these rights and still face a world in which the hospital dismisses her pain, the landlord neglects her home, the school suspends her child, the police threaten her son, the employer pays poverty wages, and the state surveils her household. Rights must be embedded in material guarantees. Otherwise they become declarations hovering over deprivation.

Johnson’s refusal of “dehumanization” has another implication for gender. Enslaved women were not dehumanized in the sense of being rendered nonhuman; they were hyper-humanized in the most violent way. Their capacity to bear children, to feel shame, to love, to fear, to nurse, to desire, to grieve, to endure pain—all were targeted. The system did not fail to see them as human. It saw them as human beings whose most intimate capacities could be appropriated. That is why a politics of justice cannot stop at recognition. Recognition can coexist with domination. One can recognize a woman’s fertility and exploit it. One can recognize a mother’s love and use it to discipline her. One can recognize intelligence and prohibit literacy. What matters is power.

A reproductive justice approach would also change how we think about the environment. Communities reproduce themselves in places. Poisoned water, toxic air, food deserts, dangerous housing, and climate vulnerability are assaults on the capacity to sustain life across generations. If slavery joined domination over people to domination over land, then repair must join bodily autonomy to ecological repair. The plantation exhausted soil and people together. Modern extraction continues to distribute ecological harm along racial and class lines. To defend black life is to defend the conditions of life.

Johnson ends by invoking moments when the world might be remade. We are in such a moment, though not necessarily a hopeful one. The new administration has already signaled hostility to immigrants, Muslims, protesters, and the fragile gains of racial justice movements. But moments of danger can clarify. A politics rooted in the history of slavery must not be satisfied with diversity in elite institutions or formal inclusion in an unjust economy. It must ask how labor, land, sexuality, reproduction, policing, borders, and ecology are organized.

To remake the world from the standpoint of enslaved women is to reject every system that turns dependency into profit, care into servitude, sexuality into domination, and children into property or risk. It is to say that justice is not merely the right to be left alone. Justice is the collective capacity to live, love, reproduce, refuse, remember, and flourish without being made material for another’s wealth.
