"The most effective approach to literacy is a genuinely balanced model that combines explicit, systematic phonics instruction with a content-rich curriculum that builds background knowledge and vocabulary."

The argument should not be “balanced literacy” versus “phonics,” as if children must choose between loving books and learning how print works. The right approach is a genuinely balanced one: explicit, systematic instruction in the alphabetic code in the early grades, coupled with abundant reading, writing, discussion and knowledge-building across literature, science, history and the arts.

The evidence is strongest on the basics. The National Reading Panel found that young children benefit from direct teaching in phonemic awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary and comprehension. Many children will infer some patterns from exposure, but many will not, especially those who arrive at school with fewer books, fewer words and less familiarity with academic language. For them, waiting for discovery is not progressive; it is neglect. Teachers should know how to teach sound-spelling relationships, decoding, spelling and oral reading fluency in a planned sequence, and schools should monitor whether children are actually mastering these skills.

But phonics is necessary, not sufficient. A child who can sound out “photosynthesis” still needs to know what it means. Reading comprehension depends heavily on vocabulary and background knowledge. That is why literacy instruction should not shrink the school day into generic “reading strategies” practiced on random leveled texts. Students need coherent units that build knowledge over time: myths, the solar system, civil rights, animal habitats, immigration, poetry. They should read aloud, listen to challenging texts, write frequently and talk about ideas worth discussing.

Balanced literacy has often been appealing because it treats children as real readers and writers rather than worksheet-completers. That instinct is valuable. Classrooms should contain rich libraries, daily independent reading, student choice, conferences and opportunities to write for real audiences. But if “balanced literacy” means vague mini-lessons, guessing from pictures, and little correction when children cannot decode words, then it is not balanced at all. It privileges children who already have strong support outside school.

New York City should avoid another swing of the pendulum. The city needs clear expectations, strong teacher training and curricula that integrate foundational skills with content-rich reading and writing. Principals should not be left to choose between ideology and test preparation. The goal is simple: children should crack the code early, read widely, learn a great deal about the world and come to see themselves as capable, thoughtful readers.

Vanilla Claude

"The most effective approach to literacy instruction is a synthesis that combines systematic, explicit phonics with a content-rich curriculum designed to build background knowledge."

The debate between balanced literacy and direct, explicit instruction tends to be framed as a clash of pedagogies, but the evidence accumulated over the past two decades points toward a more practical synthesis. The National Reading Panel's 2000 report, still the most comprehensive review we have, identified five pillars of effective reading instruction: phonemic awareness, phonics, fluency, vocabulary, and comprehension. None of those pillars is optional, and the trouble with pure balanced literacy as practiced in many New York classrooms is that it tends to underweight the first two.

Lucy Calkins's workshop model, which Chancellor Fariña is reintroducing, has real virtues. Children write daily, choose books at their level, and confer with teachers about their thinking. For students who arrive at school already steeped in print, this looks wonderful. But for the roughly one-third of American children who struggle to crack the alphabetic code—disproportionately poor children and English learners—discovery-oriented approaches and "three-cueing" strategies that encourage guessing from pictures and context can entrench bad habits. Cognitive scientists like Mark Seidenberg and Stanislas Dehaene have shown that skilled reading is built on rapid, automatic decoding, and that decoding has to be taught, systematically, to most kids.

The phonics-versus-whole-language framing, however, obscures what may be the bigger problem: comprehension. Once children can decode, what holds them back is not a generic "comprehension skill" but knowledge. E.D. Hirsch has been making this argument for thirty years, and recent work by Daniel Willingham reinforces it: you cannot comprehend a passage about the Civil War without knowing something about the Civil War. The relentless focus on "strategies" like predicting and inferring, divorced from substantive content, leaves students—again, especially disadvantaged ones—unable to make sense of grade-level texts as they move into the upper elementary grades and beyond.

The right approach, then, is neither Calkins's workshop nor a return to drill-and-kill. It is systematic, explicit phonics instruction in the early grades, paired with a content-rich curriculum that builds vocabulary and background knowledge across history, science, and the arts. Independent reading and writing matter, but they belong on top of that foundation, not in place of it.

Vanilla DeepSeek

"The most effective approach to literacy instruction must prioritize explicit, systematic instruction in phonics and the building of background knowledge over the student-led, immersion-based models of balanced literacy."

The debate over reading instruction often frames the choice as a stark one: joyful, student-led exploration versus rigid, skill-and-drill phonics. This is a false dichotomy that has, for too long, allowed a romantic vision of literacy to eclipse the realities of cognitive science. The balanced literacy programs making a resurgence in New York City promise the best of both worlds, but in practice, they frequently shortchange the very skills that make authentic reading possible—leaving too many children, particularly those from less language-rich homes, adrift.

By 2014, the evidence base is clear and voluminous. The National Reading Panel’s synthesis, replicated by subsequent research, demonstrates that explicit, systematic instruction in phonemic awareness and phonics is not an enemy of comprehension; it is its indispensable foundation. For the novice reader, the brain must forge new neural pathways, connecting visual symbols to the sounds of spoken language. This process does not happen by osmosis through immersion in quality literature alone. Skimming over letter-sound correspondences in a "teachable moment," as many balanced literacy models encourage, simply does not provide enough repetitive, structured practice for the majority of students to achieve automaticity. Without that automaticity, cognitive bandwidth is consumed by decoding, leaving little room for meaning-making.

Understanding the importance of background knowledge also complicates the romantic narrative. Comprehension is not an abstract skill that can be applied to any text once a child "learns to love reading." It is intricately tied to factual knowledge about the world. An approach that privileges student choice and minimizes direct instruction in history, science, and the arts inadvertently widens the knowledge gap between students who absorb such facts at home and those who do not. Those who advocate for a purely student-led approach sometimes mistake a child’s natural curiosity for a self-sufficient curriculum. A true commitment to equity demands that we systematically build the store of vocabulary and facts students need to grasp complex texts in the later grades, not simply hope they acquire it during independent reading time.

Restoring the balance in literacy does not mean banishing authentic literature or killing a love of stories. It means acknowledging the science of how children actually learn to read. It means recognizing that a teacher’s most profound act of empowerment is not to be a silent facilitator beside a reading nook, but to be an expert guide who directly, unapologetically, and joyfully equips every child with the code and the knowledge to unlock the world of ideas.

"The most effective approach to improving student literacy is a balanced integration of systematic phonics instruction and rich, student-led literary experiences rather than an ideological adherence to a single method."

The debate between balanced literacy and explicit phonics instruction often frames the issue as an either/or proposition, but the most effective approach to improving student literacy requires both elements working in concert. Research conducted before 2010 has consistently shown that skilled readers draw upon multiple sources of information simultaneously, and our instruction should reflect this reality.

The National Reading Panel's landmark findings in 2000 established that systematic phonics instruction produces measurable advantages in word recognition and spelling skills, particularly for beginning readers and students experiencing difficulties. This evidence has not been superseded or meaningfully challenged in the years since. However, phonics alone does not produce fluent, engaged readers. Comprehension depends equally on vocabulary development, background knowledge, and the motivation to engage with text—areas where rich literary experiences and student choice play important roles.

The criticism of balanced literacy that focuses on insufficient explicit instruction has merit. When "student-led" becomes an excuse for inadequate guidance, students—particularly those from disadvantaged backgrounds who arrive at school with limited vocabulary and print exposure—suffer. These students need more intensive, explicit instruction, not less. The answer is not to abandon meaningful reading and writing experiences, but to ensure they are undergirded by systematic skill instruction.

What New York City's schools need is not a pendulum swing from one extreme to another, but thoughtful integration guided by assessment data. Skilled teachers, provided with proper preparation and ongoing professional development, can make these distinctions in practice. The focus should be on teacher quality and the thoughtful implementation of research-based approaches, rather than ideological adherence to any single method.

"The most effective approach to literacy instruction is a teacher-led model that prioritizes explicit, systematic phonics and a content-rich curriculum over the student-led balanced literacy approach."

New York City’s decision to revive the "balanced literacy" approach relies on a romantic but ultimately flawed vision of how children learn to read. Balanced literacy is undeniably appealing in its rhetoric. It emphasizes independent reading, student choice, and cultivating a lifelong love of literature. However, while surrounding children with engaging books is a wonderful goal, it is not a substitute for directly teaching them how to read the words on the page.

Decades of cognitive science and the findings of the 2000 National Reading Panel have settled the debate on how the human brain acquires written language. Unlike speaking, reading is not a biologically natural process that children absorb simply by being immersed in a text-rich environment. Children must learn to decode the English language by mapping sounds to letters. For the vast majority of students, this requires explicit, systematic phonics instruction, not a brief "mini-lesson" where phonics is treated as just one of several optional strategies for guessing a word based on context clues or pictures.

The shift away from direct instruction is profoundly an equity issue. Some children arrive at kindergarten having been read to thousands of times, possessing the rich vocabulary and print awareness to seemingly "catch" reading with minimal explicit guidance. Balanced literacy often works well enough for them. But for students from disadvantaged backgrounds, who may lack that early exposure, the absence of systematic phonics is devastating. Direct, teacher-led instruction levels the playing field, ensuring that every child is explicitly taught the fundamental mechanics of reading.

Furthermore, literacy extends beyond decoding; it requires deep comprehension. Critics of balanced literacy are right to point out that students need "facts." Comprehension is heavily dependent on background knowledge and vocabulary. When reading instruction consists primarily of students practicing abstract strategies—like "finding the main idea"—on isolated books they choose themselves, they miss out on the systematic knowledge-building that comes from a shared, content-rich curriculum.

We all want students to love reading, but they cannot love what they cannot do. True literacy requires a solid foundation built on explicit phonics and rich, factual content. Pretending otherwise will only continue to leave our most vulnerable students behind.

"The most effective approach to literacy instruction is a writing-centered apprenticeship that integrates explicit instruction in phonics and grammar with meaningful student practice and content knowledge."

The most important word in the reading debate is not “balanced,” “phonics” or “facts.” It is “writing.” Too often, literacy reform treats children as consumers of text rather than makers of meaning. If New York wants stronger readers, it should build classrooms where students write every day, revise seriously, discuss texts with evidence and read like writers.

This does not mean ignoring phonics. In the early grades, children need direct instruction in sounds, letters and spelling patterns. A first grader cannot write or read confidently if the code remains mysterious. But writing is one of the best ways to make that code visible. When children stretch words, notice patterns, learn high-frequency spellings, combine sentences and read their own work aloud, they are not merely expressing themselves. They are studying language.

Balanced literacy deserves credit for keeping writing workshop alive in many classrooms. Students need time to choose topics, draft, confer, revise and publish. A child who writes a personal narrative, an animal report, a poem and an argument over the course of a year learns that language has purposes and audiences. That sense of purpose is often missing from heavily scripted reading programs.

But writing workshop can also become too loose. “Write more details” is not enough instruction. Students need explicit teaching in sentence construction, grammar, paragraphing, organization, evidence and style. They need models from real literature and nonfiction. They need teachers who can show how a sentence by Langston Hughes, E.B. White or a science writer works. They need to learn that revision is not simply fixing spelling but rethinking meaning.

The same is true of reading. Children should have some choice, but not all reading can be self-selected. A teacher-led class discussion of a challenging story, speech or historical document teaches students how to attend to words, structure and implication. Small-group and independent reading then give students room to practice. The best classrooms move back and forth between explicit instruction and independence.

Facts matter because writing without knowledge is thin. Ask students to write an argument about a topic they know little about and they will produce slogans. Let them study the American Revolution, bridges, insects or neighborhood history, and their writing gains precision. Content is not the enemy of creativity; it is its material.

So the right approach to literacy is an apprenticeship. Teachers model how readers and writers work. Students practice with guidance, then with increasing independence. Phonics is taught, but not isolated from reading and writing. Knowledge is built, but not through memorization alone. Choice is honored, but not confused with curriculum. In a city as varied as New York, children need more than a program label. They need classrooms where language is studied, used, argued with and enjoyed every day.

The phrase “balanced literacy” sounds sensible, but in practice it too often evades the central problem: many children in New York City are not being taught to read in a systematic way. The city should make explicit, sequential reading instruction the foundation of early literacy, especially in kindergarten through second grade.

This is not a call for dull classrooms or scripted teaching from morning to afternoon. It is a call for honesty about how reading develops. Skilled readers recognize words rapidly and accurately. That automatic recognition does not arise simply from exposure to books. It is built through knowledge of speech sounds, letter-sound correspondences, spelling patterns and repeated practice. A child who cannot decode “ship,” “shop,” “thin” and “then” reliably is not ready to compensate by looking at the picture, guessing from context or asking whether the word “makes sense.” Those habits may look like reading in the early grades, but they become traps as texts grow more complex.

The children most harmed by loose instruction are the children whom public schools most need to serve well. Middle-class children often arrive at school with thousands of hours of language exposure, books at home and parents who can intervene when school instruction is weak. Low-income children are less likely to have that safety net. If schools decline to teach phonics explicitly because it feels old-fashioned, they effectively privatize reading instruction.

A serious literacy plan would include universal screening in the early grades, systematic phonics, decodable texts aligned with what children have been taught, handwriting and spelling instruction, fluency practice and prompt intervention for students who fall behind. Teachers should be trained to understand phonological awareness, dyslexia risk, and the structure of English words. The city should stop pretending that all programs are equal if they are delivered with enthusiasm.

But phonics alone is not enough. The critics of balanced literacy sometimes speak as if facts can be poured into children apart from language and literature. Reading comprehension is knowledge-dependent, and schools should build knowledge deliberately through history, science, the arts and rich read-alouds. Yet that strengthens the argument for explicit teaching, not weakens it. Students need both the code and the content.

New York has already lived through reforms that overpromised. Mandates came and went; publishers prospered; teachers were blamed. The next move should be less theatrical: adopt a clear evidence-based early reading framework, allow schools some flexibility in materials, and hold teacher preparation programs responsible for teaching reading science. Once children can read, give them abundant literature, debate, writing and inquiry. But do not ask six-year-olds to lead themselves across a bridge adults have failed to build.

"Balanced literacy is a rigorous and effective instructional philosophy that should be supported in New York City schools, provided it is accompanied by proper investment and teacher training."

I taught fourth grade in the Bronx for seven years, and I want to push back on the way this debate is being framed. The critics of balanced literacy talk as though the alternative—"closer instruction from teachers and more work on phonics and facts"—is self-evidently rigorous, and as though workshop classrooms are loose, undisciplined places where children drift. That is not what I saw, and it is not what my colleagues did.

A well-run reading workshop is one of the most demanding instructional formats there is. The teacher delivers a focused minilesson, then circulates and confers with individual students, diagnosing in real time what each child needs—this one is skipping over multisyllabic words, that one is decoding fine but missing inferences, a third has stalled at chapter books because she lacks stamina. The teacher keeps running records, plans small strategy groups, and adjusts daily. This is not the absence of instruction. It is instruction at a level of differentiation that whole-class phonics drilling cannot match.

The caricature of balanced literacy as "let the children discover reading" comes mostly from people who have not spent recent time in good balanced-literacy classrooms. Phonics is taught in those rooms—through word study, through interactive writing, through shared reading. It is just not the only thing taught, and it is not taught as an end in itself.

What went wrong in New York under the prior administration was not that balanced literacy was tried and failed. It is that it was layered on top of a high-stakes testing regime that pushed teachers toward test prep, and rolled out without enough trained literacy coaches to support new teachers. When a brand-new teacher with no mentor is told to "do workshop," of course it falls apart. That is a problem of investment, not of philosophy.

The phonics-first crowd points to scientific consensus, and they are right that decoding instruction matters, particularly for the youngest students and for children with dyslexia or other reading difficulties. No serious balanced-literacy advocate disputes this. The disagreement is about what comes next—whether a child who can decode is a reader, or whether reading also requires the dispositions of choice, stamina, response, and authentic writing. I believe it requires those dispositions, and I believe they cannot be programmed in from a script.

Chancellor Fariña's return to workshop methods is welcome, provided it comes with what was missing before: smaller class sizes, full-time literacy coaches in every school, and time for teachers to plan. Method without support is theater. With support, balanced literacy is, in my experience, what good teaching looks like.

The renewed interest in balanced literacy in New York should not be dismissed as nostalgia for “whole language.” At its best, balanced literacy is a humane and intellectually ambitious way to run a classroom. It treats children not as test scores or decoding machines but as readers and writers with interests, voices and choices. That matters, especially in schools where too many students experience reading as a sequence of worksheets, levels and punishments.

Children learn to read by reading, writing, listening and talking in meaningful contexts. A classroom rich in books, daily read-alouds, writing workshops, conferences with teachers and opportunities to choose texts can create the habits that sustain literacy beyond third grade. We should not underestimate the importance of motivation. A child who sees herself as a reader will read more; a child who reads more will gain fluency, vocabulary and knowledge. No phonics worksheet can substitute for that virtuous cycle.

Critics are right about one point: balanced literacy can be implemented badly. If teachers are told merely to set up bins of leveled books and let children “use strategies” while guessing at unfamiliar words, many students will flounder. Explicit instruction belongs in a balanced classroom. Young children need to hear sounds in words, connect those sounds to letters, learn common spelling patterns and practice reading decodable and familiar texts. Older students need direct teaching in vocabulary, morphology, grammar, text structure and revision. But these elements should serve real reading and writing, not replace them.

The false debate is between freedom and instruction. A good reading workshop is not a free-for-all. It is highly structured: a focused mini-lesson, teacher modeling, guided practice, independent reading at an appropriate level, individual conferences, small groups and shared reflection. The teacher is not absent; she is everywhere, listening closely, diagnosing, nudging and teaching. Students are not simply “leading” themselves; they are gradually taking responsibility for work that the teacher has made visible.

Nor should “facts” be used as a club against child-centered teaching. Knowledge is indispensable, but children can gain knowledge through coherent, engaging units that include both informational text and literature. A class studying immigration, ecosystems or ancient civilizations can read, write, debate and research in ways that build vocabulary and content while preserving student agency.

New York’s task is not to choose a camp but to support teachers in doing balanced literacy well. That means smaller classes where conferences are possible, well-stocked libraries, time for independent reading, strong phonics in the early grades, and curricula that do not reduce children to test preparation. If “balanced literacy” becomes an excuse to avoid teaching the code, it will fail. If it means balancing explicit instruction with authentic reading and writing, it is exactly what many classrooms need.

There is a dangerous nostalgia creeping into our reading debates. When critics attack balanced literacy as too loose, too student-led, and insist on a return to teacher-directed, phonics-heavy instruction, they forget what school felt like for millions of children under the old factory model. Balanced literacy, at its best, treats reading not as a set of drills to be endured but as a deeply human act of making meaning. It trusts teachers to design vibrant classrooms where kids devour real books, write for authentic audiences, and learn the code in context — not in joyless isolation.

The charge that balanced literacy abandons phonics is a straw man. Every credible balanced literacy framework includes word study, phonemic awareness, and guided reading groups in which a teacher can target decoding skills precisely when a child stumbles. The difference is pedagogical: rather than a scripted sequence of letter-sound drills delivered to the whole class regardless of need, the balanced literacy teacher weaves phonics into the fabric of a rich literacy block. She pulls a small group to work on vowel digraphs using a beloved picture book, or she nudges a writer to stretch out a word in his story. This responsive teaching respects child development: some five-year-olds crack the code with minimal direct instruction, while others need repeated, systematic exposure. A skilled teacher in a well-stocked balanced literacy classroom can provide exactly that, without making every child march in lockstep.

What the critics really object to, I suspect, is the trust placed in teachers. Balanced literacy demands enormous skill — the ability to listen to a young reader’s miscues and know, in the moment, whether to prompt for meaning, syntax, or visual information. It resists the deskilling of the profession that comes with scripted programs. In New York City, where balanced literacy is regaining ground, the goal is to elevate teacher expertise, not impose a one-size-fits-all method. And let us not forget motivation. Children who see reading as a source of joy and agency — who get lost in a series book or pour their heart into a personal narrative — read more. Volume of reading is one of the strongest predictors of long-term achievement. Stripping classrooms of choice and authentic literature to make room for more worksheets and decodable text may raise scores on the DIBELS nonsense-word fluency test, but at what cost? We cannot afford to win a phonics battle and lose the war on literacy. Balanced literacy, imperfect as it may be in practice, keeps our eyes on the prize: not just accurate word-callers, but thoughtful, passionate readers.

"New York City schools should reject balanced literacy in favor of a scientifically grounded approach that emphasizes explicit phonics instruction and a content-rich curriculum."

The return of balanced literacy in New York City schools is a troubling step backward. After decades of cognitive research, we know with certainty how skilled reading develops. It is not a natural process, like learning to speak. Reading is an artificial construction that requires explicit, systematic instruction in the alphabetic code. When we embrace a student-led philosophy that privileges meaning and context over the mechanics of decoding, we fail the very children who most need our teaching — and we widen the achievement gap.

The evidence is overwhelming. The National Reading Panel’s report, now well over a decade old, confirmed the necessity of phonemic awareness and systematic phonics for beginning readers. More recently, functional brain imaging has shown that effective reading instruction literally rewires the brain, creating neural pathways that integrate visual, phonological, and semantic processing. This rewiring does not happen through osmosis or by guessing at words from pictures.   5. 05The debate between phonics and balanced literacy is a false dichotomy, as students need both technical skills and the intellectual engagement found in real books.
