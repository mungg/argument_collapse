"The problems associated with modern parenting stem not from the ideology of full-time mothering or an excess of care, but from a loss of moral discipline and the corruption of parental responsibility by status-seeking and market values."

Nancy Hirschmann offers a sharp and often justified criticism of indulgent parenting, but she misidentifies the culprit. The problem is not that mothers remain at home, nor that they believe mothering is important. The problem is that many parents—mothers and fathers alike—have lost the older understanding that love must be joined to discipline, duty, and moral formation.

A mother who lies to help her daughter avoid an exam is not caring too much. She is caring badly. A parent who imagines a summer in Paris as appropriate after a child has killed someone while driving drunk is not an example of domestic devotion; it is an example of moral decadence. A mother who excuses plagiarism because her son has a job at an investment firm has not been corrupted by the ideology of full-time motherhood. She has been corrupted by status worship, careerism, and the belief that success outranks character.

On this point, conservatives and feminists might find unexpected agreement: the family can become selfish. It can become a machine for advancing its own members at the expense of the community. But the remedy is not to weaken family commitment or urge women to “care a little less.” The remedy is to recover a richer account of parental responsibility. Parents are not supposed to be their children’s defense attorneys in every circumstance. They are supposed to raise decent human beings. Sometimes that means letting a child suffer consequences.

Hirschmann treats stay-at-home motherhood as politically suspect because it may reinforce women’s dependence and men’s irresponsibility. There is truth in the concern. A man who treats his wife as a servant while congratulating himself as breadwinner is failing as a husband and father. A woman who has no access to money, no legal protection, and no standing in family decisions is vulnerable. Divorce can leave women who sacrificed careers in precarious circumstances. Any serious defense of the family must confront these facts.

But Hirschmann’s answer leans too heavily on paid employment as the path to dignity and power. This is a striking concession to the very market values that have eroded family life. If a woman spends her day managing a hedge fund, the culture recognizes power. If she spends it raising three children, caring for an elderly parent, cooking meals, organizing a household, volunteering at church, and sustaining neighborhood ties, the culture calls her dependent. Feminists did not create this devaluation, but they sometimes reinforce it by implying that the workplace is the true arena of citizenship.

The paid workplace is not necessarily a school of virtue. It may teach ambition, punctuality, and cooperation, but it may also teach greed, manipulation, and indifference. The recent financial collapse was not caused by too many people caring for children at home. It was caused in part by highly educated people in prestigious paid positions pursuing profit without prudence. If we are worried about producing good citizens, we should not assume that attaching mothers more firmly to the labor market will solve the problem.

Nor should we assume that children benefit from less parental presence. Many do not. Children need time, attention, supervision, and moral instruction. Schools and peers cannot substitute fully for family. A mother or father at home can provide stability that is difficult to purchase. This is especially true when children are young, numerous, ill, disabled, or struggling. The fact that some stay-at-home mothers become status-obsessed or overprotective does not discredit the vocation itself any more than corrupt lawyers discredit law.

That said, defenders of at-home parenting must avoid sentimentalism. A household centered on children can become child-ruled. The modern cult of self-esteem has sometimes displaced the formation of conscience. Parents who hover over every grade and activity may produce anxious, entitled young adults. The old virtues—honesty, courage, self-command, gratitude, respect for elders, responsibility to neighbors—require more than affection. They require authority. The mother who refuses to lie for her child is exercising better care than the mother who smooths every path.

The father’s role is crucial. Traditional family life at its best never meant that fathers merely earned money and disappeared. It meant paternal authority, example, sacrifice, and instruction. Today many fathers are physically or emotionally absent, even in affluent households. Others outsource the moral life of the family to mothers while retaining final authority when convenient. This is not tradition; it is abdication. If mothers are overfunctioning, it is often because fathers are underfunctioning.

Hirschmann is correct that men should do more household labor and childcare. But equality need not mean identical roles in every season. Families may reasonably divide labor in different ways: one parent home full time, both working reduced hours, grandparents involved, father at home, mother at home, alternating arrangements. What matters is that the arrangement is just, freely embraced, and ordered toward the good of all family members—not merely the husband’s career or the child’s résumé.

Public policy should support families without dictating one model. The tax code should not punish marriage or caregiving. Divorce law should recognize the sacrifices of a spouse who left paid work for children. Parents should have more choice in education, including homeschooling, though homeschooling should be serious and accountable. Workplaces should permit flexibility for both mothers and fathers. At the same time, welfare policy should encourage responsibility, work when feasible, and stable family formation. The state cannot love children; it can only create conditions in which families and communities can do their work.

Hirschmann worries that if care is treated as a public good, the state will claim oversight. Here she is right to be cautious. The state is a blunt instrument. It may protect children from abuse and neglect, enforce schooling requirements, and ensure basic rights, but it cannot define good mothering in detail without becoming intrusive. Many of the most important forms of care—prayer, conversation, discipline, forgiveness, example—are beyond bureaucratic measurement. A society that asks the state to validate parenting may end with the state policing parenting.

The better source of accountability is community: extended family, religious congregations, schools, neighbors, civic associations. These institutions can correct parental excess more humanely than distant bureaucracies. They remind parents that children belong to families but also to a moral order larger than the family. A mother tempted to excuse her son’s cheating needs a community that says, “No, love requires truth.” A father tempted to measure his worth only by income needs a community that says, “Your children need your presence and example.”

The deepest disagreement with Hirschmann concerns the meaning of dependence. She sees dependence on a husband as a threat to women’s individuality. It can be. But human beings are not meant to be radically independent. Marriage, rightly understood, is mutual dependence disciplined by vows. The answer to selfish husbands is not to make wives less dependent by making everyone more market-dependent. It is to make husbands more faithful, wives more secure, and marriage more just.

Feminism has rightly exposed abuses hidden in the private family. Conservatives should admit that. But feminism should also admit that the family remains one of the few institutions capable of resisting the reduction of persons to workers, consumers, and competitors. The task is not to care less. It is to care better: with discipline, humility, fidelity, and a sense that our children are not trophies but souls entrusted to us for a time.

  1. 01The instances of parental misconduct cited by Hirschmann are examples of moral decadence and status worship rather than a failure of care feminism or stay-at-home motherhood.
  2. 02Prioritizing paid employment as the sole path to dignity and power unfairly devalues the essential social and civic contributions made by those who manage households and care for family members.
  3. 03The workplace is not a reliable school of virtue and can often foster negative traits like greed and manipulation, making it an insufficient remedy for producing better citizens.
  4. 04Children require significant parental time and moral instruction that cannot be fully substituted by schools, peers, or the market.
  5. 05True parental responsibility requires joining love with authority and discipline, meaning parents should allow children to face consequences rather than acting as their defense attorneys.
  6. 06Gender equality in the home should be achieved through mutual sacrifice and justice rather than forcing women into market dependence to escape domestic dependence.
  7. 07Accountability for parenting should come from local communities and moral institutions rather than state oversight, which is a blunt and intrusive instrument.
  8. 08The solution to domestic power imbalances is to strengthen the mutual dependence and fidelity of marriage rather than pursuing radical individualism.
