"While procedural and technical security measures are necessary to protect democracy from information attacks, true resilience requires deep institutional reforms that address the underlying unfairness and inequality that make disinformation effective."

Henry Farrell and Bruce Schneier are right to resist the fashionable fatalism that treats democracy as helpless before the black arts of bots, trolls, and microtargeting. Their most important move is to shift attention from sinister outsiders to the institutional conditions that make manipulation effective. Russian operatives did not invent American polarization, distrust of elections, racial resentment, or cynicism about public expertise. They exploited weaknesses already present. If democracy is to survive information attacks, the answer cannot be merely to swat at each new lie. It must be to make democratic institutions worthy of trust.

But this is also where I would push their argument further. They distinguish the problem of making democracy fairer from the problem of making democracy more resilient to being gamed. Analytically, that distinction is useful. Politically and sociologically, it is much harder to sustain. A democracy that is unfair will be vulnerable to attacks on its legitimacy precisely because the attacks will contain enough truth to travel. You cannot secure a rotting bridge by improving the signage around it. You have to fix the bridge.

The authors’ framework of “common political knowledge” is persuasive. Elections, public comment processes, the census, and expert agencies all depend on a degree of shared confidence that procedures are basically real: that votes are counted, that comments are not fabricated, that population figures are not manipulated, that agencies are not merely laundering decisions already made by the powerful. When these forms of common knowledge collapse, partisan disagreement stops being productive and becomes existential. If the other side is not merely wrong but illegitimate, then the incentive to respect democratic rules diminishes.

Yet common political knowledge is not produced by procedure alone. It is produced by lived experience. The citizen who sees districts gerrymandered beyond recognition, polling places closed, voter identification laws applied selectively, donors enjoying privileged access, and agencies staffed by industry lobbyists does not need a Russian Facebook ad to suspect that the game is rigged. The ad may amplify the suspicion, but it does not create it. A politics of democratic cybersecurity that treats distrust chiefly as a vulnerability to be patched risks mistaking a symptom for a cause.

Take the census example. The citizenship question controversy is indeed a striking illustration of how a technical administrative instrument can become a partisan weapon. The census is supposed to be the mundane infrastructure of representation and resource allocation. If households fear that answering will expose family members to immigration enforcement, then the count will be distorted and political power will shift. But the fear is not irrational in the abstract. It arises in a climate of aggressive immigration enforcement, family separation, and presidential rhetoric that casts immigrants as threats. Public education campaigns are necessary, but they cannot fully repair trust when the government’s own conduct teaches vulnerable communities to be afraid.

The same is true of elections. Paper ballots, audits, and improved coordination among federal, state, and local authorities are indispensable. But electoral legitimacy also depends on whether citizens believe that the rules of participation are fair. In the United States that belief is strained not only by foreign hacking attempts but by domestic fights over voter purges, felony disenfranchisement, partisan redistricting, campaign finance, and the malapportionment of the Senate and Electoral College. Some of these are constitutional structures; others are statutory choices; all shape whether citizens regard democratic competition as genuinely open. If one party can win power while receiving fewer votes, or maintain legislative control through district maps designed to predetermine outcomes, then the losing side’s suspicion is not simply a cognitive error induced by disinformation.

This matters because resilience depends on what engineers might call redundancy. A society with high trust, broad inclusion, and fair institutions can absorb a certain amount of misinformation. Lies will circulate, but they will not necessarily become politically decisive. By contrast, a society in which large groups believe, for good reasons, that institutions do not hear them is brittle. Falsehoods can attach themselves to real grievances and become difficult to dislodge. The attack surface of democracy is therefore not only informational; it is distributive, racial, geographic, and institutional.

Farrell and Schneier acknowledge this in places, especially when they emphasize domestic actors and “powerful actors” advancing their interests at the expense of the body politic. But their proposed remedies sometimes lean toward procedural security: authentication, auditing, institutional insulation, transparency. These are needed. Still, democracy cannot be secured by better verification alone. We also need reforms that reduce the incentives for political actors to destroy common knowledge.

Consider public comments at the FCC. The fake comment scandal was not merely a technical failure of identity verification. It reflected a deeper problem: agencies are often formally required to solicit public input while remaining highly responsive to concentrated economic power. Telecommunications companies had strong incentives to create the appearance of public support for repealing net neutrality because they knew that the appearance of participation matters. A more secure commenting platform would help prevent stolen identities and bot-generated submissions. But it would not solve the imbalance between diffuse public interests and concentrated industry resources. For that, we need stronger conflict-of-interest rules, more transparent meetings between regulators and regulated firms, better funding for public-interest advocates, and perhaps a more deliberative model of agency consultation that does not confuse mass comment counts with democratic judgment.

Nor is the problem limited to the Internet. The United States has lived through earlier eras of partisan newspapers, conspiracy pamphlets, yellow journalism, and demagogic radio. What is distinctive today is not merely the cheapness of speech but the collapse of many mediating institutions that once helped organize public conflict. Local newspapers have been gutted. Parties have weakened as membership organizations even as they have hardened as partisan identities. Unions, churches, civic associations, and local clubs have declined in many communities. Nationalized media ecosystems now turn every controversy into a battle for existential control. Social media did not create this landscape, but it accelerates its worst tendencies.

This suggests that one of the most important democratic security measures is investment in civic infrastructure. Local journalism should be treated as a public good, not because journalists are infallible but because communities need institutions capable of producing shared facts about school boards, police departments, state legislatures, and local economies. Public libraries, universities, civic associations, and deliberative forums can help rebuild habits of disagreement that are neither technocratic nor tribal. If people encounter one another only as avatars in national partisan combat, then every issue becomes a proxy war. Democracy needs places where disagreement is concrete, repeated, and consequential.

The authors’ emphasis on “negative feedback loops” is especially fruitful here. A healthy democracy needs mechanisms that cool conflict without suppressing it. Independent election administration is one. Fair districting is another. So are public financing systems that reduce dependence on large donors, ranked-choice voting experiments that may reward coalition-building, and legislative procedures that prevent temporary majorities from entrenching themselves permanently. Even a renewed commitment to congressional capacity—the kind lost when the Office of Technology Assessment was abolished—would help. Legislators who lack independent expertise become more dependent on lobbyists, partisan media, and ideological think tanks. That dependence weakens common knowledge.

There is, however, a danger in speaking too clinically about equilibrium. Some democratic instability is justice arriving late. The civil rights movement destabilized a settled order that many Americans experienced as normal. Labor organizing, feminism, gay rights, and immigrant rights movements all challenged existing common sense. A democracy that values resilience too highly may mistake righteous disruption for pathology. Farrell and Schneier are aware of this danger, and their dynamic model is meant to preserve disagreement. The challenge is operational: who decides which disruptions strengthen democracy and which undermine it?

The answer cannot be entrusted to a ministry of truth or a council of experts. It must be found through democratic processes themselves. But those processes can be designed to distinguish between contestation and fraud. Protest, persuasion, boycotts, leaks in the public interest, litigation, and organizing are legitimate democratic weapons. Fabricated identities, forged documents, deceptive suppression campaigns, and deliberate sabotage of public input are not. The line will sometimes blur, but the existence of hard cases does not eliminate the need for rules.

The deeper point is that democracy’s informational dilemma is also a moral dilemma. People accept losses when they believe they have been treated as equal participants in a continuing system of self-government. They reject losses when they believe the system has no place for them, or when leaders teach them that compromise is betrayal. Technology changes the speed and scale of manipulation, but legitimacy remains rooted in equality.

So yes: democracies need paper ballots, audit trails, secure census systems, authenticated public comments, and better defenses against automated deception. But these will not be enough if citizens experience democracy as a spectacle staged by donors, consultants, lobbyists, and algorithmic platforms. The most effective disinformation often succeeds because it whispers: the system is rigged. The only durable answer is to make that claim less true.
