"Redirecting AI toward socially beneficial outcomes requires moving beyond broad political-economic critiques to address the specific technical, institutional, and incentive-based details of the research field."

As someone sympathetic to the AI research community, I welcome Daron Acemoglu’s challenge. Researchers and engineers should think more seriously about the social consequences of their work. But the essay sometimes describes AI research as if it were more unified, more automation-obsessed, and more directly responsive to corporate preferences than it really is. The path to better AI requires understanding the technical and institutional details of the field, not only its political economy.

First, “AI” is not a single technology. It includes machine learning, symbolic reasoning, robotics, computer vision, natural language processing, planning, optimization, human-computer interaction, privacy-preserving computation, causal inference, and more. Even within machine learning, the same technique can be used for many purposes. A model that classifies images can help diagnose tumors, identify endangered species, sort waste, or enable face surveillance. A language model can assist writing, improve accessibility, generate spam, or support customer service automation. The social meaning of a system often emerges from its deployment context, not from the algorithm alone.

This makes the automation-versus-complementarity distinction harder to operationalize than Acemoglu suggests. Researchers frequently work on general capabilities: better prediction, robustness, sample efficiency, interpretability, transfer learning, privacy, or optimization. These capabilities may later be incorporated into products that substitute for labor or augment it. A paper on reinforcement learning for robotic manipulation might eventually support warehouse automation, surgical assistance, prosthetics, or disaster response. It is not always possible to know in advance.

That does not absolve researchers of responsibility. But it changes the form responsibility must take. Instead of asking every project whether it is “automation” or “new task creation,” we should build evaluation practices around likely domains of use, misuse potential, affected stakeholders, and governance requirements. Some research areas—mass facial recognition, emotion detection for policing, autonomous weapons, manipulative personalization—raise red flags at the outset. Others require careful deployment rules. The challenge is to avoid both naïve neutrality and overly broad condemnation.

Second, AI researchers are already more engaged with ethics than the essay implies. In recent years, the field has seen major growth in fairness, accountability, transparency, interpretability, privacy, robustness, and human-centered machine learning. Conferences now include broader impact statements, ethics review processes, and workshops on social implications. Researchers have exposed racial and gender bias in facial analysis, criticized predictive policing, developed differential privacy, and studied algorithmic discrimination in hiring, lending, and criminal justice. Tech workers have protested military contracts and surveillance applications. These efforts are imperfect and sometimes superficial, but they are real.

The problem is not simply lack of awareness. It is that awareness often lacks institutional force. A graduate student can write a fairness paper, but a company may deploy a biased system anyway. A conference can require a broader impact statement, but researchers may treat it as boilerplate. An engineer can object internally, but management controls product strategy. An academic lab can propose privacy-preserving methods, but firms may prefer data-hungry models because they support advertising and lock-in. Norms matter, but without regulation, procurement standards, liability, and funding, they will not be enough.

Third, the incentive structure of AI research is not only corporate. It is also academic. Researchers are rewarded for benchmark performance, publication counts, novelty, and technical elegance. Many widely used datasets and benchmarks encourage abstraction from real-world context. A model that improves accuracy by one percentage point on a standard task is publishable; a careful participatory design process with teachers or nurses may be harder to evaluate and less prestigious. This pushes the field toward problems that are easy to formalize, not necessarily problems that matter most.

If we want AI that complements human workers, we need new research infrastructures. Consider education. Building a system that genuinely helps teachers requires long-term collaboration with schools, attention to pedagogy, privacy protections for children, integration with classroom practice, and evaluation over time. That is slow and messy. It does not fit neatly into the dominant conference cycle. Health care is similar: useful AI must fit clinical workflows, reduce rather than increase documentation burden, handle distribution shifts, earn trust, and improve outcomes. These are sociotechnical problems, not just prediction problems.

Public funding could make a major difference here. Grants should support interdisciplinary teams embedded in real institutions. Computer scientists should work with nurses, teachers, social workers, disability advocates, labor unions, and domain experts. Evaluation should include effects on workload, autonomy, equity, and quality of service. Funding agencies should reward maintenance, replication, negative results, and community-governed datasets. These are not glamorous, but they are essential.

Fourth, Acemoglu’s concern about productivity deserves technical nuance. The fact that AI has not yet produced large measured productivity gains does not prove that the market is choosing the wrong technologies. General-purpose technologies often take time to diffuse and require complementary organizational changes. Electricity transformed factories only after production processes were redesigned. Computers took decades to reshape business operations. AI may follow a similar pattern. It is possible that current investments look disappointing because firms have not yet learned how to use the tools well, not because the tools are inherently misdirected.

At the same time, there is a real risk of “so-so automation,” to use Acemoglu’s phrase from his broader work: systems that replace humans imperfectly, generate modest productivity gains, but reduce labor demand. Some chatbots, self-checkout systems, automated scheduling tools, and content moderation systems may fit this pattern. They save labor costs without improving service much. The research community should take this seriously. Technical benchmarks should not be confused with social value.

Fifth, the essay’s proposed labor-share measure is intriguing but insufficient. A technology that increases the labor share in one firm may reduce welfare elsewhere. A tool that improves worker productivity might increase wages only if workers have bargaining power. Conversely, a technology that lowers labor share in a particular process may produce large consumer benefits or eliminate dangerous work. We need a portfolio of metrics: wages, employment, job quality, autonomy, safety, inequality, consumer welfare, environmental impact, privacy, and democratic risk. No single number can guide AI policy.

Sixth, we should distinguish between AI that makes decisions and AI that supports decisions. In many high-stakes domains, the best near-term approach is decision support: systems that provide information to humans who retain authority. But “human in the loop” can become a fig leaf. If a worker is expected to rubber-stamp an algorithm under time pressure, human oversight is meaningless. Research should focus on effective human-AI collaboration: when humans should defer to models, when they should override them, how uncertainty is communicated, how explanations are designed, and how responsibility is assigned.

Seventh, open science matters. Corporate concentration is partly a result of data and compute concentration. Large models require resources many universities lack. If only a few companies can train frontier systems, independent scrutiny weakens. Public compute resources, open datasets with strong governance, and shared evaluation platforms would help. But openness has risks too: models can be misused. The research community needs norms for staged release, access controls, and red-teaming, especially for systems that can generate persuasive text, code, or synthetic media.

Finally, researchers need democratic partners. Engineers cannot decide society’s values alone. Nor should they. The backlash against “ethics washing” is justified when companies use ethics language to avoid regulation. But it would be a mistake to dismiss technical work on fairness, privacy, interpretability, and safety as mere distraction. Law often needs technical tools to become enforceable. Data protection requires privacy engineering. Anti-discrimination law requires auditing methods. Due process requires explanation and contestability. Democratic governance and technical research should reinforce each other.

Acemoglu is right that AI’s future is open. But redirecting it will require more granular mechanisms than telling researchers to stop automating and start complementing humans. We need better funding structures, better benchmarks, public compute, interdisciplinary institutions, domain-specific regulation, worker participation, and liability for harmful deployment. We need to identify applications that should not be built, applications that should be built only under strict rules, and applications that deserve public investment.

The AI community is neither innocent nor omnipotent. It is a field of people responding to incentives, ideals, curiosity, career pressures, and institutional constraints. Change those constraints, and many researchers will gladly work on more socially valuable problems. But we should ask them to do so with precision. The future will not be improved by treating AI as magic or as menace. It will be improved by making the social consequences of technical choices part of the technical work itself.

  3. 03Academic incentive structures, which prioritize technical novelty and benchmark performance over messy, real-world sociotechnical integration, must be reformed to encourage research that supports human workers.

  5. 05The lack of measured productivity gains from AI may reflect a standard delay in the diffusion of general-purpose technologies rather than an inherent misdirection of the market.

  8. 08Addressing corporate concentration in AI requires public investment in compute resources and open data to ensure independent scrutiny and broader access to frontier systems.
  9. 09Technical research into fairness and interpretability should be viewed as a necessary partner to democratic governance, providing the tools required to make laws and rights enforceable in a digital context.
