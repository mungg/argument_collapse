"To improve recruitment, police departments must integrate systemic reform with community-focused outreach to transform policing into an honorable, accountable, and sustainable profession."

Police departments will not solve their recruiting problem by simply widening the mouth of the funnel. In many cities, the young people most needed in policing — residents with local knowledge, language skills and credibility in heavily policed neighborhoods — have grown up seeing the job as alien or even hostile. Recruitment therefore begins long before the application: with departments proving that officers will be expected to serve lawfully, be held accountable and work in partnership with the public.

That means reform and recruitment cannot be separated. A department known for unconstitutional stops, weak discipline or a code of silence will struggle to attract the very applicants it claims to want. Stronger supervision, transparent discipline, early-warning systems and serious civilian oversight are not anti-police measures; they are hiring tools. Good candidates want to join an honorable profession, not inherit a tarnished culture.

Departments should also change where and how they recruit. Too many rely on military-style pitches, civil service exams and networks that reproduce the existing force. Agencies should build cadet and explorer programs in local high schools, create paid internships, partner with community colleges and historically black colleges, and recruit social workers, teachers, veterans and multilingual residents who may not have considered policing. Residency incentives, tuition assistance and clear career ladders can help candidates who cannot afford a long unpaid hiring process.

At the same time, departments should not lower standards to fill vacancies. They should raise the standards that matter: emotional maturity, communication, judgment under stress and respect for constitutional limits. Screening should look beyond criminal disqualifiers to bias, history of violence and ability to de-escalate. Training should emphasize problem solving, crisis intervention and procedural justice, not just command presence.

Finally, cities must make policing a sustainable job. Reasonable pay, predictable schedules, mental health support and modern equipment matter. Recruiting better officers requires offering a better profession — one rooted in legitimacy, accountability and public trust.

"To improve recruitment, police departments must modernize their selection criteria, actively cultivate local talent, and reform institutional practices that discourage diverse and qualified candidates."

Recruiting better officers begins with rethinking who departments are looking for, and how. For decades, police hiring has filtered candidates primarily through physical fitness tests, a clean record, and a willingness to do shift work. Those screens are necessary but insufficient. Departments should weight empathy, emotional regulation, language skills, and community ties as heavily as marksmanship—using structured interviews and situational judgment tests that the Police Executive Research Forum and others have validated. Psychological screening should look not only for instability but for indicators of bias and authoritarian temperament.

Second, departments must recruit where they want officers to serve. The DOJ findings in Baltimore and Ferguson, and Chicago's recent task force report, all describe forces culturally disconnected from the neighborhoods they police. Cadet and Explorer programs in city high schools, tuition-forgiveness partnerships with community colleges, and residency incentives can build a pipeline of local, diverse applicants. Lateral entry from social work, teaching, and military medical corps would broaden the talent pool beyond the traditional applicant.

Third, fix the disqualifiers that quietly skew hiring white and suburban. Blanket bans on old marijuana use or minor juvenile records exclude disproportionately black and Latino applicants without evidence they predict bad policing. Credit-history screens have similar effects.

Finally, recruitment cannot be separated from reputation. In a year of viral videos from Baton Rouge to Falcon Heights, qualified candidates—especially candidates of color—are weighing whether the job is honorable. Visible accountability, body cameras, and consent-decree reforms aren't just oversight tools; they are recruiting tools.

"To improve recruitment, police departments must address their crisis of legitimacy by shifting from a warrior to a guardian mindset and prioritizing candidates with high emotional intelligence and service-oriented backgrounds."

The most urgent obstacle to police recruitment is not a shortage of bodies but a crisis of legitimacy. After Ferguson, Baltimore, and a damning Department of Justice report on Chicago, many Americans—particularly young people of color—see policing as an adversarial, even hostile, profession. Departments tempted to lower standards to fill classes are chasing the wrong fix. Instead, they must fundamentally reimagine whom they seek and what the job represents.

The President’s Task Force on 21st Century Policing urged a shift from a “warrior” to a “guardian” mindset. Recruitment should reflect that by actively targeting candidates with demonstrated emotional intelligence, conflict-resolution skills, and a service orientation. Police forces can forge pipelines with university social work, psychology, and communications programs, and offer loan forgiveness or tuition incentives to attract liberal-arts graduates who might never have considered a badge. At the same time, arbitrary disqualifiers—minor, decades-old drug offenses or credit blemishes—must be reexamined; they disproportionately bar minority applicants without making departments safer, and they signal a culture stuck in rigid exclusion.

Diversifying the force requires more than job fairs. Departments should embed cadet programs in high schools within underserved neighborhoods, creating a visible pathway from the community into the department. Panels that include neighborhood residents in the final interview stage lend credibility and surface the interpersonal qualities a resume cannot. Peer recruiters from minority backgrounds should be compensated and professionally developed, not just borrowed for photo ops.

Underneath all this, the real product being marketed is workplace culture. Chicago’s report revealed officers with disciplinary histories moving between departments, shielded by absent national transparency. A true recruiting overhaul requires a national decertification database, along with early academy training—in implicit bias, mental-health crisis intervention, and de-escalation—that is rigorous and unskippable, not a check-the-box afterthought. The goal is to attract candidates who want to join a profession where safeguarding life and dignity is the core metric, not arrest numbers. Until departments signal that they will reward integrity over blind loyalty, no bonus or billboard can fix the deficit of trust that keeps the people they most need away from the application portal.

The recruitment crisis facing American police departments is inseparable from the current crisis of public trust. Following the deeply troubling Justice Department reports on Baltimore and Chicago, it is clear that the traditional "warrior" image of policing is not only alienating minority communities, but it is also discouraging compassionate, community-minded individuals from applying. To recruit well-qualified, diverse officers, departments must fundamentally change their pitch, rebranding policing as a "guardian" profession focused on public health and community service rather than urban combat.

To achieve this, agencies must broaden where they look for talent. Currently, many departments rely heavily on recruiting from military bases and traditional criminal justice programs. While these candidates bring valuable skills, the pool remains narrow. Police agencies should actively recruit from sociology, psychology, and social work programs at local universities. Officers today spend the vast majority of their time responding to mental health crises, domestic disputes, and substance abuse issues. Emphasizing these realities in recruitment materials—and perhaps offering student loan forgiveness for graduates in these fields—will attract candidates equipped with the interpersonal and de-escalation skills our cities desperately need.

Finally, departments must dismantle the structural barriers that inadvertently filter out diverse, local talent. The modern hiring process is notoriously cumbersome. Lengthy unpaid or low-paid academy training periods, arbitrary application fees, and rigid zero-tolerance background checks for minor juvenile infractions disproportionately exclude young people of color. By subsidizing academy pay, streamlining the painfully slow hiring timeline, and evaluating candidates' life experiences holistically rather than punitively, departments can finally build a workforce that genuinely reflects and understands the neighborhoods it is sworn to protect.

The most important recruiting tool for police departments is not a better brochure. It is a better occupation. Talented people with options look at policing and see modest pay, unpredictable hours, intense public scrutiny, physical danger, weak training in many jurisdictions and a workplace culture that may punish officers who speak up. It should surprise no one that many departments struggle to attract the best candidates.

America asks police officers to exercise enormous power with far less preparation than many licensed professions require. In some states, becoming a barber or an electrician can require more hours of formal training than becoming an officer with a badge and gun. If cities want better recruits, they should raise the status of the job by raising standards: more education, more scenario-based training, stronger field supervision and continuing professional development. A college degree should not be the only path into policing, because that can exclude working-class applicants. But departments can require meaningful postsecondary coursework, subsidize it and create apprenticeships that combine classroom learning with supervised practice.

Pay matters too. We cannot demand maturity, restraint and constitutional sophistication while treating policing like a low-bid public service. Higher salaries tied to higher standards would make departments more competitive with private-sector employers. Loan forgiveness, housing assistance and child-care support could help attract applicants who otherwise cannot afford to enter the profession.

Equally important, cities must make clear that good officers will not be trapped in organizations that tolerate misconduct. A department known for covering up excessive force will repel precisely the recruits it should want: ethical, thoughtful people who do not want their names associated with abuse. Transparent discipline, early-warning systems and protection for whistle-blowers are therefore recruitment policies as much as accountability policies. The same is true of getting rid of officers who repeatedly lie, use unnecessary force or generate large numbers of complaints. Nothing discourages a principled recruit like seeing bad officers remain untouchable.

Racial diversity is essential, but it should follow from a broader commitment to excellence and legitimacy. Communities need officers who are representative, yes, but also skilled, calm and honest. Recruitment will improve when policing becomes a true profession with serious preparation, fair compensation and a culture that honors restraint as much as bravery.

Pay Them Like Professionals, Train Them Like Them Too

If we want police departments to attract the talented, college-educated, emotionally mature recruits that the Baltimore and Chicago reports cry out for, we have to stop pretending that a 19-year-old with a high-school diploma and six months at an academy is ready to exercise the most awesome power the state grants any civilian: the discretion to take a life.

Start with money. In many midsize cities, starting officer pay is barely above what Target pays an assistant manager, and the pension promise that once made up the difference has been hollowed out by two decades of municipal budget crises. A young person weighing a career in law enforcement against nursing, teaching, or the trades sees a job with worse pay, worse hours, and far more legal and physical risk. Raising starting salaries by even 15 to 20 percent — funded, if necessary, by shrinking bloated overtime budgets — would dramatically widen the applicant pool.

Then raise the educational floor. The President's Task Force on 21st Century Policing recommended this last year, and the research backs it up: officers with four-year degrees generate fewer complaints, use force less often, and are more likely to de-escalate. Some chiefs object that a degree requirement would shrink the pool of minority applicants, but that is an argument for subsidizing the degree, not abandoning the standard. Departments could partner with community colleges and HBCUs to fund tuition in exchange for service commitments — essentially an ROTC model for policing.

Finally, lengthen and deepen training. American academies average about 19 weeks; German police train for two and a half years. Our compressed model produces officers who know how to clear a room but not how to talk a suicidal teenager off a ledge. A longer, paid training pipeline would itself be a recruiting tool, because it signals that the job is serious and that the institution is investing in the person.

None of this is cheap. But the cost of the status quo — consent decrees, civil settlements, lost public trust — is far higher. Recruitment problems are downstream of professional status. Fix the status, and the applicants will come.

  4. 04Lengthening the training pipeline to mirror international models would better prepare officers for complex social interactions and signal that the department is invested in their professional development.
  5. 05Investing in professionalization is more cost-effective than the current status quo of legal settlements and lost public trust.
Diversified DeepSeek

"To improve recruitment, police departments must first reform their toxic internal cultures and lack of accountability, which currently drive away ethical officers and deter qualified applicants."

The scramble to find more and better police recruits often misses an uncomfortable truth: departments don’t primarily suffer from a hiring problem, but from an exit problem. Talented, reform-minded officers leave or are driven out by toxic internal cultures, while the survivors adapt to a code of silence that protects abuse. Until police agencies clean house, recruitment campaigns are just pouring water into a leaking bucket.

Look at the data on retention. A 2016 Bureau of Justice Statistics report on local police departments showed that voluntary separations have been rising, particularly among officers with less than five years of service. Exit interviews rarely point to pay or danger as the top complaint; instead, officers cite a lack of leadership accountability, a culture where harassment of whistleblowers is tolerated, and a sense that doing the right thing will get you shunned by your squad. In Chicago, the Police Accountability Task Force noted that even veteran officers expressed fear of retaliation for reporting misconduct. When the system punishes integrity, the decent recruits you finally persuade to join will hand in their badge within a few years.

Consequently, any recruitment strategy that doesn’t simultaneously address departmental culture will fail. It’s not enough to invite diverse, service-oriented applicants into an institution that rewards aggression and discourages speaking up. Baltimore’s consent decree, negotiated this year, begins to tackle the problem by mandating early intervention systems that identify problematic behavior patterns, but far too many departments resist such transparency because they see it as a threat to officer solidarity. That solidarity is the very obstacle.

The most effective recruitment tool is a reformed workplace. When officers in a department can point to real accountability—when a colleague who chokes a suspect is fired and prosecuted rather than protected by the union and supervisors—the message travels faster than any glossy ad campaign. Potential recruits who are currently dissuaded by the profession’s reputation will instead see an institution where their values can thrive. The question is not first how to find better applicants; it’s whether police leaders have the courage to make the job one that good people don’t have to abandon.

The Recruitment Problem Is a Symptom, Not the Disease

Every time a department lands in the headlines for beating, shooting, or systematically harassing the people it serves, the reform conversation drifts toward recruitment. If we just hired better people — more diverse, more educated, more "community-minded" — the thinking goes, the problems would ebb. This is a comforting story, and it is mostly wrong.

The Justice Department's findings in Baltimore are damning precisely because they describe institutional behavior, not individual bad apples. Officers were trained to make unconstitutional stops, supervisors rewarded the numbers those stops produced, and the few cops who objected were sidelined. You can recruit a saint into that system and within two years she will be writing the same bogus loitering tickets as everyone else, or she will quit. Chicago's code of silence, documented in the city's own task force report, works the same way: it punishes the conscientious and protects the abusive.

This is why I am skeptical of recruitment-centric reform. It assumes the bottleneck is the input when the real problem is the machine. Departments hire from the same labor pool as the military, fire services, and corrections, and the demographic data suggest plenty of qualified candidates of color apply — they just wash out at higher rates during background checks that disqualify them for offenses (marijuana use, unpaid debts, a cousin with a record) that have nothing to do with whether they would be a good officer.

If we are serious, we should focus on what happens after the hire. Strong civilian oversight with subpoena power. An end to qualified immunity's most generous applications. Decertification databases so fired officers cannot simply move one county over. Union contracts that no longer wall off discipline from public view. Real consequences for supervisors whose units rack up complaints.

Do those things and the recruitment problem partially solves itself, because the people you want — the ones with options, the ones the community already respects — will believe the job is something other than a liability magnet. Skip them, and no amount of glossy "We Want You" advertising at the local job fair will change a thing.

Rethink Who Gets Disqualified, and Stop Selling the Wrong Job

A surprising amount of America's police recruiting crisis is self-inflicted, baked into screening rules and marketing materials written for a different era.

If the job is focused on preventing violence, investigating serious crime and protecting people in genuine emergencies, departments can recruit people suited to that mission. If other public-safety roles are created, cities can attract applicants who want to serve their communities but do not want to carry a weapon or join a paramilitary culture. That is not anti-police; it is pro-competence.

This shift would also help diversity. Many residents of heavily policed neighborhoods do not reject public service. They reject joining an institution they associate with stop-and-frisk, fines, surveillance and force. Offer them paid routes into violence interruption, restorative justice, victim services, forensic analysis, emergency response and eventually sworn policing if they choose, and the talent pool changes.

For sworn officers, departments should then recruit for calm, humility and judgment. Training should emphasize de-escalation, constitutional law, local history and procedural justice. Recruiters should be candid that the new model values fewer arrests when problems can be solved without them.

The United States has spent decades expanding the police mandate and then blaming police when they perform social work badly. Better recruiting requires a more honest division of labor. Hire armed officers for the tasks that truly require armed authority, and build other civic professions for the rest. Only then will the right people see public safety as a calling rather than a contradiction.
