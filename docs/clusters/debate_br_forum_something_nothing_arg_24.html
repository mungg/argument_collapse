Diversified Gemini

"While Nir Rosen correctly identifies that the Obama administration’s counterinsurgency (COIN) strategy is a doomed attempt to engineer a functional state in Afghanistan, the U.S. should replace it with a scaled-down, lethal counterterrorism (CT) strategy rather than withdrawing entirely."

Nir Rosen’s “Something from Nothing” provides a desperately needed corrective to the sanitized, PowerPoint-driven optimism emanating from the Pentagon and Washington think tanks. By embedding with Team Prowler and the Afghan police, Rosen exposes the fatal conceit of the Obama administration’s Afghan surge: the assumption that a foreign military can engineer a functional state in a society fundamentally resistant to centralized control. However, while Rosen’s diagnosis of the counterinsurgency (COIN) delusion is flawless, his dismissal of the security threat that drew us to the region in the first place is naive. The failure of COIN does not negate the necessity of counterterrorism (CT).

Rosen eloquently demonstrates why the “clear, hold, build” framework is destined to fail in Afghanistan. As his anecdotes reveal, the U.S. military can "clear" an area with relative ease, but the "hold" and "build" phases rely entirely on the Afghan National Police and the government in Kabul. This is the Achilles heel of the McChrystal strategy. We are trying to build a modern, centralized security apparatus on a foundation of illiteracy, deeply ingrained corruption, and tribal factionalism. You cannot graft a Weberian state onto a society that has historically organized itself around decentralized, local power structures.

The military advocates of COIN treat the corruption of the Karzai government as a manageable variable, a glitch that can be fixed with better "mentoring" and more money. Rosen correctly identifies that corruption is not a glitch; it is the operating system. When Afghan cops fleece locals or abandon checkpoints, they are acting rationally within a system that rewards patronage and survival over national loyalty. Pouring 30,000 more American troops and billions of dollars into this system will only inflate the corruption and increase the resentment of the local population.

But where Rosen goes astray is his conclusion regarding al-Qaeda and American security interests. In his final paragraphs, he asks whether a "few hundred angry, unsophisticated Muslim extremists really pose such grave dangers to a vigilant superpower." This is a profound misreading of the adversary. Al-Qaeda may be unsophisticated in conventional military terms, but their asymmetric capabilities, intent to acquire mass-casualty weapons, and ability to exploit ungoverned sanctuaries are well documented. September 11 was not a one-off stroke of luck; it was the culmination of years of planning conducted in the safe haven of a Taliban-ruled Afghanistan.

Rosen argues that al-Qaeda is already in Pakistan, making the Afghan war irrelevant. He is half-right. The core of al-Qaeda *has* relocated to the tribal areas of Pakistan. But his assumption that they would not return to Afghanistan if the Taliban reclaimed power ignores the deep, symbiotic relationship between the two groups. The Taliban are not global jihadists, but they are ideologically sympathetic hosts who have shown absolutely no willingness to break with Osama bin Laden.

Therefore, we are faced with a strategic dilemma. COIN is a fool's errand that will trap us in an unwinnable nation-building project for decades. But complete withdrawal, as Rosen seems to advocate, invites the reconstitution of a transnational terrorist sanctuary.

The solution, which Vice President Joe Biden reportedly championed during the administration’s internal debates last fall, is a dramatically scaled-down Counterterrorism (CT) strategy. Often termed "CT-Plus," this approach acknowledges that we cannot fix Afghanistan, but we can protect the United States.

A CT-centric strategy would abandon the pretense of population-centric COIN. We would stop trying to pacify every village in Helmand. We would stop pretending that we can turn the Afghan National Police into a Western-style constabulary. Instead, we would radically reduce our conventional footprint, maintaining a small, agile force composed primarily of Special Operations Forces, intelligence operatives, and air assets.

The objective would be narrow and ruthless: disrupt and dismantle terrorist networks. When al-Qaeda or affiliated groups attempt to establish training camps or operational hubs, we eliminate them using drone strikes, standoff weapons, and targeted Special Forces raids. We would continue to fund and support the Afghan National Army to prevent the immediate collapse of Kabul, but we would disengage from the quixotic mission of local governance reform.

Proponents of COIN argue that you cannot conduct counterterrorism without the intelligence that comes from protecting the population. There is some truth to this; a smaller footprint means less situational awareness. But the technological advancements in signals intelligence and persistent aerial surveillance over the last eight years mitigate this loss. Furthermore, a massive troop presence generates its own massive intelligence noise, much of it false information fed by local actors settling tribal scores—as Rosen’s story about the innocent religious student, Zeibullah Agha, perfectly illustrates.

Rosen is absolutely right that our heavy footprint fuels the insurgency. The Taliban’s most potent recruiting tool is the presence of foreign troops kicking down doors and dropping bombs that kill civilians. By shrinking our presence, we remove the primary irritant. We allow the localized, nationalist elements of the insurgency to fracture, rather than uniting them against a common occupier.

In January 2010, the Obama administration has committed to the McChrystal surge. It is a tragic misallocation of blood and treasure. We are treating a counterterrorism problem with a nation-building solution. We must accept the harsh reality that Afghanistan will not be a functional democracy in our lifetimes. It will likely remain a violent, fractured landscape. Our goal should not be to build a nation from nothing, but simply to ensure that the chaos of that nation does not reach our shores. A lean, lethal, offshore counterterrorism posture is the only viable path forward.

  3. 03A 'CT-Plus' strategy focusing on Special Operations and drone strikes would allow the U.S. to disrupt terrorist networks without the quixotic and costly mission of local governance reform.
