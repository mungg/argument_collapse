Diversified Gemini

"To recapture those drifting away from religious institutions, congregations must abandon exclusionary political and social ideologies in favor of radical inclusion and unconditional justice."

As we wring our hands over the Pew Research data showing a drift away from traditional religious attendance, we must confront the elephant in the room: the American culture wars. For the last thirty years, institutional religion in the United States—both Christian and Jewish—has frequently aligned itself with rigid political and social ideologies that actively alienate vast swaths of the population.

Younger Americans are fleeing the pews because they associate institutional religion with exclusion. In the Christian world, the relentless focus on anti-gay politics, opposition to women's reproductive rights, and a general hostility toward science has pushed millions of compassionate, thoughtful people out the doors. They still believe in God, and they still identify as Christians, but they refuse to associate with institutions that preach intolerance.

In the Jewish world, the dynamics are slightly different but parallel. Synagogues that rigidly police interfaith marriages, or that demand unquestioning adherence to specific geopolitical stances regarding Israel, are finding that younger, progressive Jews simply opt out. When an institution makes you choose between your deeply held ethical convictions about human rights or family, and your religious heritage, people will choose the former.

How can congregations win these people back? The answer requires institutional repentance. Religious leaders must boldly and publicly declare their sanctuaries to be zones of radical inclusion. This means fully welcoming LGBTQ individuals into leadership, embracing interfaith families without conditions, and decoupling the pulpit from partisan culture-war politics.

You cannot capture a generation by telling them that their basic sense of justice and fairness is a sin. Until the dominant narrative of American religion shifts from one of judgment to one of unconditional love and justice, the pews will continue to empty.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The alignment of institutional religion with rigid, partisan culture-war politics has alienated younger generations who value compassion and science.
  2. 02Religious institutions often force members to choose between their religious heritage and their deeply held ethical convictions regarding human rights and family.
  3. 03Strict policies regarding interfaith marriage and geopolitical litmus tests drive progressive members away from synagogues.
  4. 04Religious leaders must publicly decouple the pulpit from partisan politics and fully welcome marginalized groups, such as the LGBTQ community, into leadership roles.
