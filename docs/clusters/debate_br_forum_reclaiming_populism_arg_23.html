Populism Is the Wrong Word for What We Need

Adom Getachew's framing essay is generous, careful, and politically serious. It would be churlish to disagree with most of its conclusions: that the left must build a multiracial working-class coalition; that the defeats of Morales, Corbyn, and Sanders demand a comparative analysis; that COVID-19 has exposed the political economy of disposability in ways that should reshape what is politically possible. I want to push, however, against the central rhetorical move of the forum — the project of "reclaiming populism" — not because I disagree with the political content the term is meant to carry, but because I think the term itself is the wrong one to carry it. The argument I want to make is that populism, even in its most generous theoretical formulations, smuggles in a set of assumptions about politics that the contemporary left would do well to leave behind. What the left needs is not a populism but a class politics adequate to the twenty-first century — and these are not the same thing.

Let me say first what I take to be the strongest case for the populist framing, because I want to do it justice before quarreling with it. The strongest case, which Ernesto Laclau spent much of his career elaborating, runs roughly like this: politics is irreducibly a matter of constituting collective subjects, and "the people" is the name of one such constitution. Because society does not have a pre-given unity — because the working class, for instance, is fractured by race, sex, sector, region, citizenship status — political subjects must be built through what Laclau called the articulation of disparate demands into a chain of equivalence. Populism, on this account, is not a deviation from democratic politics but its constitutive form: the moment in which scattered grievances are bound together into a "we" that can act. To refuse populism is to refuse the work of constituting collective subjects at all, which means ceding the field to whichever forces are willing to do that work — which today means the right.

I think this argument is partly right and partly misleading. It is right that collective subjects must be built and that they do not pre-exist political action. But it is misleading in its suggestion that the only or best way to build such subjects is through the populist form — the binary of people-versus-elite, the moralized opposition, the mobilization around affect rather than interest. The history of the left contains many other modes of subject-formation: the patient organizing of industrial unions in the 1930s, which built solidarity across ethnic and craft lines through institutional rather than charismatic means; the civil rights movement's church-based, locally rooted, theologically dense mass mobilization; the various twentieth-century socialist parties of Europe, which created entire counter-publics — newspapers, choirs, sports clubs, mutual aid societies — through which class identity was lived rather than performed. These were not populist projects in any meaningful sense. They built peoples without calling themselves populist, and the peoples they built were arguably more durable than the peoples populism builds.

Why is this distinction worth insisting on? Because populism, even at its theoretical best, tends to organize political identity around the figure of the leader and the moment of the rally — around what Laclau himself called the "empty signifier" that gives unity to disparate demands. This emphasis has consequences. It produces movements that are remarkably mobilized but institutionally thin, that can fill a stadium but cannot run a school board, that depend on the affective charisma of a particular figure and decline precipitously when that figure exits the stage. Look at what is happening to the Sanders movement now that his campaign has ended. Some of it will persist through pre-existing institutions — DSA, the unions that supported him, the progressive PACs — but a great deal of the energy will simply dissipate, because it was attached to him personally and to the campaign as a vehicle. Compare this to what Getachew rightly celebrates in Chicago: the slow accretion of power by the CTU, the patient running of candidates, the welding of strike action to community organization to electoral politics. That is not populism. That is something older and better. It is organizing.

The COVID-19 moment, which Getachew identifies as transformative, sharpens this point. What has the pandemic exposed? It has exposed, first, the brutal political economy of essential work: the fact that the people who keep our societies running — grocery clerks, delivery drivers, warehouse workers, nurses, home health aides, meatpackers, transit workers, custodians — are paid least, protected least, and respected least. It has exposed, second, the racial and gendered structure of that disposability: the disproportionate Black death rates in Chicago, Milwaukee, Detroit, and New Orleans; the over-representation of women, and especially women of color, in the most exposed care work. It has exposed, third, the catastrophic consequences of the long neoliberal hollowing-out of public capacity: the lack of testing, the inability to distribute protective gear, the impotence of public health departments that have been defunded for decades. And it has exposed, fourth, the global character of these crises: that no country can be safe while others are unsafe, that the production chains that link the world also transmit pathogens, that sanctions which deny medical supplies to Iran or Venezuela are not merely cruel but epidemiologically suicidal for everyone.

What political form is adequate to these exposures? I would submit that it is not populism. It is socialism, properly understood — a politics oriented toward the democratic ownership and control of social wealth, the decommodification of essential goods, and the building of international working-class solidarity. The populist frame, with its emphasis on people-versus-elite, tends to obscure the fact that the political economy of disposability is not a matter of bad elites who can be replaced by good ones; it is a matter of structural arrangements — wage labor, capital mobility, the international division of labor — that must be transformed at the root. A populism that treats the problem as one of corrupt elites rather than of capitalism as such ends up either tilting at windmills or, worse, finding scapegoats in immigrants, foreigners, and racialized minorities who can be coded as part of the elite's clientele.

This is not a paranoid worry. It is exactly what is happening on the right, and it is the structural risk the left runs whenever it adopts the populist form. Trump's response to the pandemic has been to call it the "Chinese virus," to halt funding to the WHO, to escalate sanctions on Iran, to expel asylum seekers under public health pretexts. Bolsonaro's response has been to deny the crisis entirely and to attack governors who try to manage it. Modi's response has been to use lockdown to discipline Muslim communities. In each case, the populist form has served as a vehicle for ethnonationalist response, and in each case the form is not incidental to the content. The people-versus-elite binary is intrinsically prone to ethnicization, because once you posit a singular authentic people, the question of who counts and who doesn't becomes definitive.

Some on the left will respond that this is precisely why we need a left populism: to occupy the populist form before the right monopolizes it. I have two answers. First, the empirical record of left populism is mixed at best. Where it has succeeded — in mid-century Latin America, for instance — it has often done so by accepting compromises with national bourgeoisies and by building patronage networks whose durability has been questionable. Where it has more recently been attempted — Syriza, Podemos, Sanders, Corbyn — it has either lost or, when it won, been forced into retreat by capital flight and institutional resistance. Second, and more importantly, the populist form is not the only mobilizational form available, and the alternatives are arguably stronger. Mass-membership parties with internal democracy, militant unions with strike capacity, community organizations with deep local roots, and tenant federations with the ability to coordinate rent strikes are all forms that mobilize without depending on the leader-affect-stadium dynamic that populism relies on.

What would a non-populist left politics look like in the United States in 2020? I think it would look something like this. It would build, first, from the strike. The wave of teacher strikes that began in West Virginia in 2018 and continued through Los Angeles, Oakland, Chicago, and elsewhere has been the most generative source of left energy in years, and it has been generative precisely because it has been institutional: rooted in unions, durable in its organizational form, capable of producing leaders who go on to other offices and other struggles. It would build, second, from the workplaces that COVID has revealed as essential: the Amazon warehouses, the Instacart shops, the meatpacking plants, the hospitals where nurses are being asked to reuse single-use protective equipment. These are sites of class struggle in the most classical sense, and they are also sites where the multiracial, multinational character of the contemporary working class is undeniable. It would build, third, from the tenant struggle that COVID is making unavoidable: with 26 million unemployment claims in five weeks and rent due on the first of every month, the conditions for a mass tenant movement are emerging whether the left organizes them or not. It would build, fourth, from a renewed internationalism: an end to sanctions, debt relief for the Global South, support for global vaccine and medicine access, opposition to the militarization of the pandemic response.

None of this is populist. All of it is socialist, in the sense that it is oriented toward the democratization of economic life and the decommodification of essentials. The fact that the language of socialism is now sayable in American politics for the first time in nearly a century — that "socialism" polls better than "capitalism" among Americans under thirty — is the actual opening that the left should be exploiting. To reach for "populism" instead is to reach for a weaker concept that the right is better positioned to wield.

I want to close by registering my agreement with Getachew on what I take to be her deepest point: that the failures of the last six months — Bolivia, Britain, the United States — must be analyzed together rather than separately, and that the comparative frame discloses problems that the national frame conceals. What it discloses, I would say, is that the international left in 2020 confronts a capitalist class that is more mobile, more financialized, and more politically organized than at any point in postwar history, and a state apparatus in most countries that has been gutted of the capacities needed to discipline that class. No rhetorical reanimation, populist or otherwise, will substitute for the slow work of rebuilding the institutional capacity — both inside and outside the state — to confront capital on something like equal terms. That work is unglamorous, but it is the work. Calling it populism does not make it any easier; it only makes it less honest about what it actually is.
