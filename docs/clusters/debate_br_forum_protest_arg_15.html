**The Therapeutic Frame Is Real, and It Cuts Both Ways**

The single most valuable contribution of Gourevitch's essay is its diagnosis of a culture that has "redefined the right to be safe as a right to feel safe." This shift, accelerated over the last fifteen years on campuses, has been catastrophic for civic life. Gourevitch correctly traces its arc: the language of trigger and trauma migrated from clinical settings into student affairs, was institutionalized by an expanding administrative class, and is now being weaponized by the right against the very universities that gave it shelter. He is right that we cannot escape this trap by simply switching which group's feelings get to count.

But I want to register a worry about how Gourevitch deploys this critique. The therapeutic frame is real, and it has done real damage. It is also, in his essay, doing some heavy lifting that I am not sure it can bear.

Consider the structure of his argument. Jewish students report feeling unsafe; Gourevitch responds that subjective feelings cannot establish objective hostility, and cites the higher rate of Muslim students who also report feeling unsafe to suggest that the standard, when applied even-handedly, would be incoherent. The implicit conclusion is that we should mostly discount the feelings-claims of both groups and focus on whether there were "credible, imminent threats." This is a defensible position, but it elides a question: what work is the category of "feeling unsafe" actually doing in students' lives?

In my own experience teaching for two decades, the phrase "I felt unsafe" is doing several different things at once, and sorting them out matters. Sometimes it means "I was afraid I might be physically harmed"—a factual claim about anticipated violence that can be evaluated against evidence. Sometimes it means "I felt my place in the community was being denied"—a claim about belonging that is harder to evaluate but not less real. Sometimes it means "I felt the institution failed to acknowledge my distress"—a claim about institutional recognition that is really a political demand in psychological language. Sometimes, frankly, it means "I encountered views I disagree with and I want them suppressed"—a claim that should be rejected.

Gourevitch's argument tends to treat all four meanings as instances of the same therapeutic-consumerist confusion. But the second and third are not confusions. They are real claims about what kind of community a university should be, dressed in the only vocabulary the institution makes available. When a Jewish student says "I felt unsafe walking through the encampment," she may be making a factual claim about her physical safety (probably false) or she may be saying "this protest is structured around the proposition that my people are uniquely evil, and I do not understand why my university tolerates it." The second claim deserves an answer. "Subjective feelings do not establish objective harm" is not an answer.

The same applies in reverse. When a Muslim student says she felt unsafe after October 7 because of campus rhetoric, she is often not making a claim about anticipated assault but a claim about belonging in an institution that, just days earlier, was sending out solidarity emails about her people's killers. The institution owes her an answer too, and "subjective feelings do not establish objective harm" is no better an answer in her case.

What Gourevitch's framework needs, and lacks, is a category for institutional moral address that is neither legal liability nor administrative discipline. Universities used to do this through deliberation: presidents made speeches, faculty held teach-ins, departments hosted forums where the relevant issues could be argued out. Some of this still happens, but it has been crowded out by a regime in which every claim of distress becomes a Title VI complaint and every institutional response becomes either a crackdown or a press release. Gourevitch is right that we need to roll back the legal and administrative apparatus. But rolling it back will not, by itself, restore the deliberative culture it displaced. That requires building something, not just dismantling something.

I also worry that Gourevitch's tonal stance—the wry, slightly resigned, "Democracy is demanding" voice—does not quite reckon with what it costs to be on the receiving end of public hostility for months on end. He acknowledges this at the end, somewhat defensively, with his line about not wanting to sound like an "insensitive finger-wagger." But the acknowledgment is brief. The longer arc of the essay does ask people to endure quite a lot, and to endure it without expecting institutional comfort. That ask is defensible in principle but underspecified in practice. What do we say to the freshman who came to college expecting to learn and finds herself, six weeks in, the daily target of a movement that wishes her grandparents had stayed in Europe? "Develop more resilience" is not enough. Neither is "you should counter-protest." Some answer is required, and Gourevitch's essay, for all its sophistication, does not quite supply one.

The therapeutic frame is real. So is the experience it sometimes mislabels. The hard work is distinguishing them in practice, on the ground, with humility about how often we will get it wrong. Gourevitch has made the case for one half of that work admirably. The other half remains.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The phrase 'feeling unsafe' often masks valid political and social claims about community belonging that cannot be dismissed simply as therapeutic confusion.
  2. 02Dismissing subjective feelings as a basis for administrative action does not provide a necessary answer to students who feel their place in the university community is being fundamentally denied.
  3. 03Dismantling the administrative and legal apparatus of 'safetyism' is insufficient without simultaneously rebuilding a deliberative culture of speeches, teach-ins, and forums.
  4. 04The demand for students to simply develop resilience in the face of prolonged public hostility is an inadequate institutional response to the genuine distress caused by campus conflict.
  5. 05A complete framework must distinguish between clinical 'trauma' and the real experience of social exclusion rather than treating all psychological language as a bad-faith demand for censorship.
