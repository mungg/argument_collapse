"Libraries should evolve by expanding their core mission as public infrastructure for an informed society rather than merely attempting to replicate the retail model of defunct bookstores."

Libraries should not try to become bookstores with weaker cash registers. The disappearance of Borders leaves a real gap: fewer places to browse, to encounter books serendipitously, and to sit without being hurried along. Public libraries can and should occupy some of that space. But their purpose is broader than retail’s old “third place.” A library is one of the few institutions built on the premise that access to knowledge should not depend on ability to pay.

That mission matters more, not less, in a digital age. In 2012, the Internet is abundant but unevenly distributed. Many job applications, government forms, school assignments and health searches now assume broadband access, a working computer and basic digital skill. Libraries provide all three. They also provide trained people who can help patrons distinguish a reliable source from a rumor, a database from a search result, and a book worth reading from an algorithmic suggestion.

Libraries should evolve by expanding, not abandoning, their core functions: lending books in print and electronic form; preserving local history; teaching children to read; helping adults learn English, find work or navigate public services; and offering quiet public space. They should experiment with e-books, media labs, lectures, homework help and community programming. They should also defend reader privacy and resist becoming mere distribution points for commercial platforms.

The danger is not that libraries will become obsolete. It is that we will misunderstand them as nostalgic warehouses for paper. A good library is public infrastructure for an informed society. Bookstores may come and go with the market. Libraries answer to a different need.

Vanilla Claude

"Libraries should evolve by deepening their civic functions and guaranteeing universal access to information rather than merely attempting to replace the retail role of defunct bookstores."

Libraries have never been just warehouses for books, and the temptation to recast them as bookstore replacements misreads both their history and their present. Andrew Carnegie's libraries were civic infrastructure: places where immigrants learned English, workers self-educated, and citizens encountered ideas they could not afford to buy. That mission is, if anything, more urgent now than in 1900.

Consider what libraries already do that Borders never did. They provide free internet access — a lifeline for the roughly one in four Americans without broadband at home, and increasingly a prerequisite for job applications, unemployment claims, and school assignments. They host story hours, ESL classes, tax-help volunteers, and after-school programs. The Pew Internet study released earlier this year found that the majority of library users value these services as highly as the books themselves.

So filling the "void" left by Borders is fine as a marketing line, but it sells libraries short. A bookstore's job is to move inventory; a library's job is to guarantee access — to information, to literacy, to the digital tools required for modern citizenship, and to a noncommercial public space that is becoming rarer in American life.

The right evolution, then, is not toward retail ambience but toward deeper civic function: better e-book lending (the current publisher standoffs are an embarrassment), maker spaces, digital literacy training, and partnerships with schools. Libraries should not imitate the stores that died. They should outlast them by being something stores never were.

The temptation to recast libraries as benevolent bookstores is understandable. As Borders vanishes, a store-shaped hole appears on Main Street, and libraries—with their shelves and reading rooms—seem a natural fit. But to see libraries merely as a replacement for commercial booksellers is to mistake a vital public square for a shop. Libraries are not failed bookstores; they are fundamentally different institutions, and their future lies not in mimicking retail, but in deepening their democratic mission.

The core purpose of a library has never been to distribute product. It is to ensure free, equitable access to information, culture, and the tools of self-education. In 2012, this mission is more urgent than ever. For the millions of Americans still without home broadband or the means to purchase e-readers and databases, the library is the only portal to the digital world. It’s the place where a job seeker crafts a résumé online, where a student without a computer completes homework, where a new immigrant learns English in a community class. A bookstore sells you a book or a latte; a library provides you the space and resources to become a more capable citizen.

As libraries evolve, they should lean into this role as community infrastructure. Instead of curating a cozy browsing experience with bestseller pyramids, they can become technology hubs offering digital literacy training, workshops on everything from coding to small business planning, and flexible gathering spaces for civic dialogue. The shift means fewer duplicates of the latest James Patterson and more investment in public terminals, broadband capacity, and skilled guidance. Libraries still need books—but they need staff who are knowledge navigators more than they need to be booksellers.

The bookstore void is real, but it’s a commercial void. Communities’ deeper need is for neutral, non-commercial spaces where everyone, regardless of income, can access the tools of modern life. Libraries that chase the bookstore model risk abandoning the very people who need them most. They must evolve, but their compass should remain fixed not on the market, but on the public good.

Libraries Should Not Try to Be Bookstores

The temptation for libraries to fill the Borders-shaped hole in American downtowns is understandable, but it would be a strategic mistake. Bookstores failed, in part, because the economics of selling physical books to browsers cannot compete with Amazon's warehouses and the Kindle. Libraries that lean harder into "browsing the new releases like at Barnes & Noble" are chasing a business model that the market has already rejected.

What libraries offer that bookstores never did is something more fundamental: a non-commercial public space. You do not have to buy anything to sit there. You do not have to be a customer. In an economy where nearly every square foot of indoor space is rented to people with money to spend, that civic function is rare and precious. Teenagers without a car, job-seekers without broadband, immigrants studying for citizenship tests, retirees on fixed incomes — these are not Borders' lost customers. They are the library's actual constituents.

The right evolution is to deepen that civic identity rather than mimic retail. That means more reference librarians, not fewer; more programming around literacy, citizenship, and job training; more meeting rooms; better public computers and Wi-Fi; and serious investment in digital lending infrastructure so that patrons without e-reader budgets are not locked out of contemporary reading. The American Library Association's recent fights with publishers over e-book lending terms are precisely the right battles — they are about preserving public access in a digital age, not about staging author signings.

If libraries try to be cozy bookstores, they will be judged by bookstore metrics — foot traffic, bestseller turnover, café receipts — and they will lose. If they double down on being the last truly public indoor space in most American towns, they have a future that no Kindle can disrupt. The Borders comparison flatters libraries in the short term and misreads their purpose in the long term.

The Question Is Wrong

"Do we still need libraries?" is the kind of question that gets asked about institutions only after they have already proved indispensable. We do not ask whether we still need fire departments because most houses do not burn down. The Pew Internet survey released earlier this year found that 91 percent of Americans over 16 consider public libraries important to their communities, and use of library computers and Wi-Fi has surged precisely as Borders has collapsed. The premise of the debate is, frankly, a thin one drawn from the assumption that books equal libraries.

They never did. Andrew Carnegie did not fund 1,600 buildings so people could borrow novels; he funded them because he believed access to information was a precondition of democratic citizenship. That mission is more urgent now, not less. The digital divide is real: roughly a third of American households still lack reliable home broadband, and that share is much higher among the poor, the elderly, and rural residents.
