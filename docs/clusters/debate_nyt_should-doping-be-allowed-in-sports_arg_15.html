Diversified Gemini

"The public's demand for superhuman athletic performance and record-breaking entertainment makes society complicit in the doping culture it claims to condemn."

Whenever an Olympics rolls around and the inevitable doping scandals emerge, the public reaction follows a predictable script of shock, outrage, and condemnation. We point fingers at the disgraced race walker or the suspect swimmer, shaking our heads at their lack of morals. Yet, the truth is far more uncomfortable: the sports consumer is entirely complicit in the doping culture. Our demand for superhuman entertainment created this monster, and our outrage is nothing more than collective hypocrisy.

As a society, we do not tune in to the Olympics to watch ordinary people do ordinary things. We want the impossible. We demand that the 100-meter dash gets faster every four years. We want swimmers to shatter world records by absurd margins. We want gymnasts to perform routines that defy the laws of physics. The entire multi-billion-dollar sports-entertainment complex—the television networks, the corporate sponsors, the ticket buyers—is built on the promise of relentless, linear progression in human athletic achievement.

But the un-enhanced human body has biological limits. You cannot continually break world records purely on grit, broccoli, and a good night's sleep. When athletes realize that the difference between lifetime financial security and being forgotten is a fraction of a second, and when they know the viewing public will only reward the record-breakers, the incentive to dope becomes overwhelming.

The testing regime exists largely as theatrical appeasement. It allows sponsors to maintain a family-friendly image and allows fans to feel good about what they are watching. When a few athletes are caught—often the ones who simply couldn't afford the most advanced, undetectable regimens—they are offered up as sacrificial lambs to prove the system "works." Then, the games continue, and we go right back to cheering for performances that defy all physiological logic.

We must stop pretending that doping is a localized disease caused by a few bad apples. It is a systemic feature of modern elite sports, driven by our insatiable appetite for the spectacular. Until we as spectators are willing to accept slower times, fewer home runs, and the reality of human limitations, we have no right to feign moral outrage when athletes give us exactly what we paid for.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The sports-entertainment complex is built on the unrealistic expectation of relentless, linear progression in human achievement.
  2. 02The biological limits of the human body mean that athletes cannot meet the public's demand for constant record-breaking without chemical enhancement.
  3. 03The financial and social rewards for winning create an overwhelming incentive for athletes to dope in order to avoid being forgotten.
  4. 04Doping tests serve as a form of theatrical appeasement that allows sponsors and fans to maintain a facade of fair play while continuing to consume spectacular performances.
  5. 05Doping is a systemic feature of modern elite sports driven by spectator appetite rather than a localized moral failure of individual athletes.
