Diversified Claude

"The Democratic Party’s recent economic agenda was not a 'near-miss' of a transformative program, but rather a structurally flawed effort designed to appease affluent suburbanites while failing to build durable working-class power."

The Bait and the Switch Were Always Built In

Hacker and Pierson tell a story of near-triumph: a bold, redistributive agenda that almost made it through, foiled at the last minute by Joe Manchin and the density tax. Their account is meticulous and, in many respects, correct. But the framing — "remarkable just how close the agenda came to succeeding" — gets the politics backward. Build Back Better did not narrowly miss. It was structurally engineered to miss, and the structural engineers were Democrats themselves.

Consider the basic architecture of the 2021 effort. The largest, most ambitious legislation Democrats have proposed in fifty years was constructed entirely under the constraint of budget reconciliation, with no serious attempt to abolish or even meaningfully reform the filibuster. The Child Tax Credit expansion was made temporary at the outset, not because that was politically necessary but because permanence would have required compromises Democratic leaders would not make. Universal pre-K, the care economy investments, paid leave — every one of these "transformative" programs was sunset early to fit a ten-year budget window. The PRO Act, the only piece of legislation that would have shifted the underlying balance of class power, was never seriously fought for. Medicare-for-All was treated, even rhetorically, as outside the realm of polite conversation.

When Hacker and Pierson write that "the overwhelming majority of the Democratic caucus" supported a bold agenda, they conflate roll-call votes with political will. A caucus that knows a bill will not survive Manchin can vote for anything. The question is what leadership fights for when the stakes are real. And the record there is clear: Chuck Schumer did not threaten Manchin; the White House negotiated against itself for months; the progressive caucus was talked down from withholding infrastructure votes that were its only leverage. This was not a tragedy of institutions. It was a series of choices by people who preferred a half-loaf within the existing donor and consultant ecosystem to a confrontation that might have produced a full loaf or a fight worth losing.

Hacker and Pierson acknowledge the awkward fact that "few if any of [the Democrats'] initiatives seem to have built power through policy." They puzzle over this, then offer some hopeful conjectures: maybe time will help, maybe organizations will get stronger, maybe the media environment makes it impossible. What they will not say is the obvious thing. You cannot build durable working-class power through means-tested, time-limited transfers administered through the tax code, designed in the first instance to be invisible to recipients and unobjectionable to suburban professionals. The CTC was a beautiful policy and an inert political instrument. People received deposits in their bank accounts and did not know whom to thank. When Manchin killed the extension, there was no mobilization, because no mobilization had ever been built. That isn't a tragic accident; it's the consequence of designing the program for the comfort of the Democrats' upscale wing rather than the political activation of its downscale one.

This is what Heather Gautney was pointing at with the phrase "bait and switch," and Hacker and Pierson's response is essentially to insist that the bait was sincerely offered. Perhaps it was. But sincerity of intention is beside the point when the political form of an agenda systematically undermines its political durability. Universal programs build constituencies. Means-tested ones build resentment, paperwork, and quiet attrition. Programs run through state bureaucracies and visible institutions — Social Security, Medicare — anchor partisan loyalty across generations. Programs delivered through Treasury deposits or insurance subsidies vanish into the background hum of household finance.

There is a deeper problem with the Hacker-Pierson account that becomes visible once you stop accepting their framing of the U-shaped coalition as a fact of nature. The "Brahmin left" they want to distinguish from Piketty's version is still, in important respects, Piketty's left. The wealthiest counties in America did not vote for Biden because they had been won over to redistribution. They voted for Biden because Trump made the Republican brand intolerable for educated professionals while leaving the underlying material interests of those professionals — low capital gains taxes, asset-friendly monetary policy, residential zoning, selective school admissions, the carried interest loophole — substantially untouched. None of these were on the BBB table. The agenda's actual revenue side — partial repeal of the 2017 tax cuts, modest corporate minimum taxes — left the structural mechanisms of upward redistribution intact. This is not an accident. It is what the U-shaped coalition will tolerate, and the authors are right about that. They are simply wrong to call the result "bold."

What would a genuinely bold agenda look like? It would attack opportunity hoarding directly: federal preemption of exclusionary zoning, conditioned on housing and transportation dollars; an end to the mortgage interest deduction beyond modest caps; full taxation of capital gains at death; a wealth tax that the Brahmin left would actually feel. It would rebuild labor power through card check and sectoral bargaining, not through subsidies that nudge unionization at the margins. It would attack the price of housing, education, and health care — the three goods whose inflation makes professional-class anxiety politically explosive — rather than subsidizing demand within unreformed markets. Hacker and Pierson concede most of this in passing, especially on zoning, and then explain why national Democrats cannot do any of it. They are describing the limits of the coalition while telling us how transformative the coalition has become.

There is something almost touching about the authors' insistence that the story should not be lost in the noise. They want us to recognize the genuine break with Clintonism, the seriousness of industrial policy, the realness of Lina Khan. I grant all of this. The party is to the left of where it was in 2010, and that matters. But "to the left of Larry Summers in 2009" is a low bar, and the test of a political movement is not how it compares to its own worst recent self but whether it can produce durable change in the distribution of power. By that test, the Biden era has so far been a near miss on the policy side and a clear miss on the political-economic side. The donor class has not been displaced. Labor density continues to fall. Housing has become more, not less, unaffordable. The professional-managerial class remains the party's center of gravity, and its preferences continue to shape what is thinkable.

The honest version of the Hacker-Pierson story would be this: Democrats have rediscovered Keynesianism and industrial policy under the pressure of a pandemic, a wave of progressive activism, and competition with China. They have done so in a way carefully calibrated not to threaten the underlying class structure of their own coalition. That is genuinely better than Rubinomics. It is not a transformation, and treating it as one risks letting the party's leadership coast on the gap between its rhetoric and the lives of the people it claims to serve. The next opportunity, when it comes, should be greeted with more pressure and less gratitude.

Diversified DeepSeek

"The Democratic Party’s recent economic shift is a fragile 'sleight of hand' rather than a durable transformation because it avoids broad-based taxation of the affluent, maintaining a neoliberal commitment to technocratic engineering over collective solidarity."

Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson have provided a compelling narrative of strategic adaptation: Democratic elites, they argue, have navigated the treacherous waters of a U-shaped coalition by boldly turning left on economics, using broad-based benefits to bind together affluent suburbanites and less well-off urban voters. The story is alluring, casting today’s Democrats as the savvy heirs to a political tradition that can outmaneuver structural constraints. But the entire framework is built on a fragile and ultimately unconvincing premise—that the party’s recent leftward turn on spending represents a durable, transformative break with its neoliberal past. In truth, the 2021 agenda, for all its ambition, was less a sharp U-turn than a remarkably effective sleight of hand, one that buried a crucial continuity beneath a mountain of new spending: the continued, almost theological, commitment to avoiding broad-based taxation of the affluent. This is not a detail. It is the foundational flaw that makes the “new” Democratic economics both politically brittle and philosophically hollow.

Hacker and Pierson are upfront about the tax constraint. They note that Biden’s $400,000 pledge essentially ruled out new taxes on all but the richest 2 percent of Americans. They acknowledge this as a “real cost,” primarily because it makes contributory social insurance programs hard to design. But they treat this constraint largely as an electoral necessity, a clever and, for now, workable concession to the coalition’s affluent wing. “Ironically,” they write, the incredible concentration of wealth means the necessary revenues can come exclusively from corporations and the very rich. This is the core of the shell game. The party can promise a European-style welfare state on a American-style tax base that asks almost nothing of the professional-managerial class that forms its new backbone. The implication is that this is a sustainable political equilibrium—a kind of free-lunch social democracy.

This is a profound misreading of both the politics and the policy of the welfare state. Historically, the most durable and popular social programs—from Social Security to Medicare to public education—have been universal in both their benefits and their burdens. They are contributory. Citizens pay in and see a direct return. This creates a deep sense of ownership and political invincibility that means-tested or general-revenue-funded programs, no matter how generous, simply cannot match. The expiration of the expanded child tax credit is a perfect illustration. It was a pure transfer, a check from the government, and when it disappeared, the political mobilization to save it was anaemic compared to what would greet any serious threat to Social Security. A welfare state funded by the top 2 percent is a welfare state that the other 98 percent can easily be persuaded is a form of charity for the poor, not a contract of mutual obligation. It is a welfare state perpetually on sufferance, one election away from being branded as redistributionist overreach.

Politically, the strategy of taxing only the ultra-rich is a trap of the party’s own making. It cedes the ideological battlefield entirely. By accepting that taxes are an unmitigated evil to be shielded from as many voters as possible, Democrats validate the entire conservative worldview that government is a parasitic force from which citizens must be protected. They cannot make a case for the public good as a shared enterprise. Instead, they are left with a technocratic appeal: you get good stuff, and somebody else—a tiny, demonized elite—pays for it. This might work for a cycle or two, but it is a fragile foundation for a majority coalition. It offers nothing to the suburban professional who sees their bloated tax bill and asks, reasonably, “What am I getting for all this?” The answer, under the Biden model, is effectively “more for you, paid for by a much richer person across town.” That is a message of individual consumerism, not collective solidarity, and it is easily shattered the first time the government needs a broader revenue base to fund its ambitions, or when Republicans successfully reframe the debate around the deficit.

The great irony is that Hacker and Pierson document the end of “Rubinomics,” yet their new Democratic Party has not escaped the long shadow of Robert Rubin’s most fundamental error: the belief that targeted technocratic interventions could float a progressive economic program without the messy, difficult work of building popular consent for a genuinely shared project of taxation and public investment. A true pivot from neoliberalism would require not just spending more, but taxing differently—asking affluent professionals to make a visible, tangible contribution to the common good. That would be politically difficult, even dangerous. But until Democrats are willing to take that risk, their “new” economic agenda is just a more colorful iteration of the old one: an attempt to painlessly engineer a good society from the top down, one that ultimately lacks the political foundations to survive the next economic downturn. The U-turn hasn’t failed to materialize. It just hasn’t been attempted.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01By pledging not to tax anyone earning under $400,000, the party creates a 'free-lunch social democracy' that asks nothing of the professional-managerial class forming its new electoral backbone.
  2. 02The lack of a contributory tax model makes new social programs politically vulnerable and prevents them from achieving the 'invincibility' of universal programs like Social Security.
  3. 03The strategy of taxing only the ultra-rich cedes the ideological battlefield to conservatives by framing government as a parasitic force rather than a shared public enterprise.
  4. 04A genuine break from neoliberalism requires the politically difficult work of building popular consent for shared taxation and public investment rather than relying on targeted, top-down spending.

"The Democratic Party's supposedly bold economic agenda is actually a 'Brahmin left' compromise that avoids addressing the structural mechanisms of inequality to protect the interests of its affluent suburban voters."

What Democrats Cannot Touch Tells You What They Are

There is a moment in Hacker and Pierson's essay that, once you notice it, reorganizes the whole argument. They acknowledge that residential zoning, exclusionary local land use, and what they call "opportunity hoarding" are the deepest source of inequality in metropolitan America. They concede that affluent suburban Democrats are "highly protective of these arrangements even when they profess economically and racially progressive values." And then they pivot to the observation that "the localized, low-profile character of these policies facilitates the national party's efforts to keep them far from its core agenda."

This sentence is doing enormous work. It is doing more work than the authors seem to want to acknowledge. Read carefully: the national Democratic Party is choosing to leave undisturbed the single largest mechanism by which wealth, opportunity, and racial advantage are reproduced in the United States — because to do otherwise would split its coalition. The U-shaped coalition the authors celebrate is held together by an unspoken pact in which the affluent leg gets a quiet veto over the policies that most directly threaten its position, while the lower leg gets the visible programs that do not touch the underlying allocation of land, schools, and neighborhoods.

This is not a minor caveat. It is the central feature of the coalition, and it determines what "bold economic agenda" can actually mean. Biden's plans, ambitious as they were on paper, did not include federal preemption of exclusionary zoning. They did not include conditioning transportation or housing dollars on local upzoning. They did not include reforms to the property-tax-financed school system that allows wealthy suburbs to functionally hoard educational resources. They did not touch the mortgage interest deduction in any serious way, or the step-up in basis at death, or the carried interest loophole, or the preferential treatment of capital gains. Hacker and Pierson are correct that the agenda would have done good things for poor families. They underplay how carefully it avoided doing anything that would have meaningfully reduced the wealth of the affluent suburban families on whose votes the coalition depends.

This is what a "Brahmin left" actually is. It is not a left that refuses redistribution. It is a left that practices redistribution in forms that pose no threat to the assets, neighborhoods, schools, and credentialing systems through which the upper-middle class secures its advantages. Spending on infrastructure, climate, manufacturing, child care, and health care can all be financed by taxes on corporations and the very rich. The top 2 percent — Wall Street, the actual capital owners — pays the bill. The top 20 percent — the professional class that constitutes the upscale leg of the U — gets handed redistributive credit without ever being asked to give up the things they actually care about: their houses, their school districts, their professional licensure cartels, their college admissions advantages.

This is a much more cynical reading than Hacker and Pierson's, and I want to be clear that I do not think it requires bad faith from anyone involved. It is a coalitional equilibrium, and equilibria do not require malice. But it has consequences. The most important consequence is that, even on its own terms, the agenda Hacker and Pierson admire cannot solve the problems it identifies. You cannot reduce metropolitan inequality without changing land use. You cannot make housing affordable without breaking the political grip of incumbent homeowners. You cannot equalize educational opportunity while leaving the school district boundary map intact. You cannot reduce wealth inequality while leaving the structural advantages of the top decile untouched and taxing only the top 2 percent.

The housing crisis is the clearest test case. American metropolitan rents and prices have decoupled from incomes over the past three decades, driven primarily by local restrictions on supply. This is not contested. It is true in San Francisco and Los Angeles and Boston and New York and Seattle and Washington. These are the wealthiest, most productive, most Democratic metros in the country. The Democratic Party's local affiliates have, with rare exceptions, been responsible for the regulatory architecture that produces the shortage. There is now a YIMBY movement within the party trying to change this. The Biden administration has gestured at the issue — the federal "housing supply action plan" of 2022, some carrots for local zoning reform. But the gestures are precisely calibrated to avoid actually overriding local autonomy, because to do so would shatter the coalition.

Hacker and Pierson's response is essentially that this is a tragedy, but a manageable one, because national politics can keep the issue out of the spotlight. I would argue the opposite. The shortage is no longer manageable. Housing costs are the dominant material reality of the lives of younger metropolitan voters, including those toward the upscale end of the U-curve. They are the reason millennials feel poorer than their parents despite higher nominal incomes. They are a major driver of fertility decline, of the inability of young families to live near their parents, of the political alienation of the under-forty cohort that is increasingly visible in the data. A party that cannot speak to this issue because its coalition forbids it is a party with a strategic vulnerability that will not stay hidden.

The same point applies to occupational licensing, to the legal cartelization of medicine and law and dentistry, to the protectionist credentialing systems that allow professionals to capture rents. These are bipartisan failures, but they are particularly damning for a center-left party that claims to care about inequality. They are also untouchable for exactly the same reason zoning is untouchable: the coalition will not bear it.

What this means for the Hacker-Pierson thesis is something stronger than a friendly amendment. The "bold economic agenda" they celebrate is bold along precisely those dimensions where the U-shaped coalition has no internal conflict — namely, taxing the very rich and corporations to fund transfers and investments. It is timid, indeed silent, along every dimension where the coalition has a real internal conflict — namely, the protected position of the upper-middle class in housing, education, and labor markets. To call this a transformation overstates what has happened. To call it Piketty's Brahmin left disguised in industrial-policy clothing understates it only modestly.

A genuinely transformative Democratic agenda would begin where Hacker and Pierson stop: at the local arrangements that produce metropolitan inequality. It would dare to split its own coalition along the lines that actually matter. Until it does, "bigger, bolder, broader" will mean bigger transfers from the very rich to the very poor, with the middle of the U-curve receiving symbolic redistribution while keeping its actual privileges intact. That is better than nothing. It is not the new New Deal.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The party's coalition is held together by an unspoken pact that grants the professional class a veto over policies that would threaten their exclusive access to housing, schools, and neighborhoods.
  2. 02By focusing exclusively on taxing corporations and the top 2 percent, Democrats avoid asking the top 20 percent to sacrifice the 'opportunity hoarding' privileges that drive metropolitan inequality.
  3. 03The Biden administration’s agenda deliberately ignored essential reforms like federal preemption of exclusionary zoning and changes to property-tax-financed education to avoid fracturing its U-shaped voter base.
  4. 04The party's inability to address the housing supply crisis—caused by local regulations in Democratic strongholds—reveals a strategic vulnerability that undermines its claim to be a champion for the working class.
  5. 05A truly transformative agenda would require the party to confront the internal conflicts of its coalition by challenging the legal cartels and land-use restrictions that benefit its upscale supporters.
Diversified MiniMax

"The Democratic Party's reliance on affluent suburban voters creates a fragile coalition that risks prioritizing the interests of the wealthy over genuine democratic accountability and redistributive goals."

The most striking claim in the lead essay is that "Democrats are now much less clearly the party of the 'have nots.'" The authors support this claim with impressive data: Democrats now represent 24 of the 25 wealthiest congressional districts, win counties producing 71 percent of GDP, and dominate the 24 most economically productive metro areas. This is a genuine transformation worth taking seriously.

But what does it mean for democracy? The authors implicitly assume that Democratic success is democratic success—that a party governing for the affluent while maintaining support among the poor is better than the alternative. This assumption deserves scrutiny.

Consider the policy implications of Democratic reliance on affluent suburban voters. These voters are, by definition, beneficiaries of the existing economic order. They have mortgages to protect, retirement accounts to preserve, and neighborhood arrangements to maintain. Their policy preferences may align with Democratic redistribution in the abstract, but they also include resistance to housing construction that might lower property values, opposition to tax increases that might affect their marginal rates, and ambivalence about redistribution that might be perceived as benefiting "others" more than themselves.

The authors acknowledge these tensions when they discuss opportunity hoarding and local control. But they treat these as problems to be managed rather than contradictions to be resolved. The party's ability to "organize issues out" of politics is presented as a strategic success; it might equally be described as a failure of democratic accountability. When affluent suburban interests are protected by political design rather than democratic deliberation, the result is quiet plutocracy dressed in progressive clothing.

The authors also understate the degree to which affluent Democratic voters may be motivated by cultural rather than economic factors. College-educated suburbanites may support Democratic economic policies because they believe in redistribution, but they may also support Democrats as a cultural signal—as the party of tolerance, professionalism, and cosmopolitanism opposed to the cultural backwardness of Republicans. If the latter motivation is stronger, then the coalition's commitment to redistribution is more fragile than the authors assume.

This matters for 2024 and beyond. If affluent suburban support for Democrats is primarily cultural rather than economic, then a Republican Party that moderates on cultural issues—or a Democratic Party that is perceived to have gone too far on redistribution—could peel off these voters rapidly. The economic commitments the authors celebrate may be epiphenomenal, contingent on cultural alignment that cannot be assumed to persist.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The policy preferences of affluent suburbanites, such as protecting property values and resisting tax increases, fundamentally conflict with the party's stated redistributive ambitions.
  2. 02The strategy of 'organizing issues out' of politics to avoid alienating wealthy voters represents a failure of democratic accountability rather than a strategic success.
  3. 03Affluent suburban support for Democrats may be driven more by cultural signaling and cosmopolitanism than by a genuine commitment to the party's economic agenda.
  4. 04The Democratic coalition is highly vulnerable to a Republican shift toward cultural moderation or a perception that Democratic economic policies have become too radical for the wealthy.
Diversified MiniMax

"The responding essay argues that the lead essay's celebration of the Democratic Party's economic shift is overly optimistic and fails to critically examine how the party continues to prioritize affluent suburban interests over transformative redistribution."

The lead essay is primarily a defense of Democratic Party strategy, and as such it performs the ideological work of legitimating that strategy. This is not a criticism—it is an observation about genre. Forum essays are typically contributions to ongoing political debates, and contributors typically take positions. But readers should understand that the perspective offered is a particular one: from within the coalition the authors analyze, looking outward at electoral and policy possibilities.

Consider what a critique from the left might look like. The authors celebrate Democratic boldness on economics but do not ask whether that boldness was sufficient. The American Jobs Plan called for $2 trillion in infrastructure spending over eight years—approximately 1 percent of GDP per year. The Green New Deal, which inspired elements of Biden's approach, called for $10-15 trillion over ten years. By the standards of the crisis Democrats claimed to be addressing—climate change, infrastructure decay, care economy collapse—the Biden agenda was modest, not bold.

Similarly, the authors treat the shift in Democratic economic policy as a break with the past, but that break was partial and reversible. Clinton's deficit reduction and welfare reform remained uncontested. The 2017 tax cuts were slated for partial rather than full repeal. Financial regulation remained weaker than pre-2008 levels. The party that the authors describe as pursuing "the most extensive package of economic benefits for working- and middle-class families the Democrats had proposed since at least the 1960s" was simultaneously a party that had not reversed any major rightward shift of the previous four decades.

A left critique would also question the coalitional analysis. The authors argue that Democrats successfully managed tensions between affluent suburbanites and poorer urban voters. But one could argue that affluent suburban interests have not merely been accommodated—they have been prioritized. The failure to address zoning reform, the retention of state and local tax deductions that disproportionately benefit high-tax affluent jurisdictions, the modest proposals on housing subsidy reform: these suggest that when push came to shove, Democratic protection of affluent voter interests trumped redistribution to the poor.

None of this means the authors are wrong about the direction of Democratic change. The party has clearly shifted left on economics, and this shift is worth documenting and analyzing. But it is worth analyzing critically, with attention to what the authors do not say as well as what they do. The New Blue Divide is a real phenomenon. Whether it represents genuine transformation or rhetorical adjustment remains, as the authors themselves admit, to be seen.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Biden administration's economic proposals were modest and insufficient when measured against the actual scale of the climate and social crises they aimed to address.
  2. 02The Democratic Party's recent policy shift is a partial and reversible adjustment that leaves the major rightward economic shifts of the Clinton and Obama eras largely intact.
  3. 03The party's failure to tackle issues like zoning reform and tax deductions for the wealthy suggests that affluent suburban interests still take precedence over the needs of the poor.
  4. 04The lead essay functions more as an ideological defense of current party strategy than as a rigorous critical analysis of the party's limitations.
