What Does It Take to Get Women Elected? · Cluster 2 of 17

"Achieving true political representation for women requires restructuring the household economy and domestic gender roles to alleviate the disproportionate labor burdens that prevent women from participating fully in public life."

"To achieve true gender parity in elected office, the United States must move beyond recruitment and funding to address the structural barriers created by the organization of work and caregiving."

Jennifer Piscopo argues that the United States should move beyond celebrating women’s right to vote and confront women’s unequal right to govern. I agree, but I would put more weight on a barrier that often hides in plain sight: the organization of work and care. Women are not merely underrecruited by parties or underfunded by donors. They are constrained by a political economy that treats caregiving as private, campaigns as all-consuming, and officeholding as a job for people with either money, a spouse at home, or no one depending on them.

This matters because political ambition is not formed in the abstract. A woman deciding whether to run for city council, state legislature, or Congress is not simply asking, “Am I qualified?” She may be asking: Who will take care of my children during evening meetings? Can I leave my hourly job to canvass? Will campaign travel endanger my pregnancy or my health? Can I afford unpaid time off? Will my aging parent lose care if I spend weekends at events? Will I be punished by voters for having young children in a way a father would not be? Will I face online threats that spill over onto my family? Will I have health insurance if I quit my job to campaign?

A wealthy candidate can buy answers to some of these questions. Many working- and middle-class women cannot. This is why the representation debate cannot be separated from the care crisis. The United States has long relied on women’s unpaid and underpaid labor to sustain families and communities. It then designs politics as if candidates have no bodies, no dependents, no household responsibilities, and no need for income. The result is a system that selects for people insulated from ordinary life.

The COVID-19 pandemic has made this impossible to ignore. As of August 2020, schools and childcare centers are disrupted across the country. Women are disproportionately bearing the burden of remote schooling, childcare, eldercare, and job loss. Many essential workers are women, especially women of color, and they are risking their health in hospitals, grocery stores, nursing homes, public transit, and care facilities. At the same time, the public decisions being made—about unemployment insurance, paid leave, school reopening, eviction, health care, and workplace safety—are precisely the decisions in which women’s experiences should matter. Yet those experiences remain underrepresented in the rooms where policy is made.

Piscopo is right that exhorting women to “run” can become a way of blaming them for their absence. Nowhere is this clearer than with caregiving. A candidate training can teach message discipline; it cannot create affordable childcare. A donor network can help a congressional candidate; it cannot make a state legislative salary livable. A recruitment program can tell a mother that her voice matters; it cannot stop reporters from asking who is watching her children while never asking fathers the same question. Encouragement is not enough when the structure of political life assumes a male breadwinner model.

So what would a care-centered right to be elected look like?

First, campaign funds should be usable for caregiving expenses. The Federal Election Commission has already permitted some candidates to use campaign funds for childcare under certain circumstances, but the principle should be clear at every level of government. If a candidate incurs childcare or eldercare costs because she is campaigning, those are campaign expenses. Treating them as personal indulgences privileges candidates with a stay-at-home spouse, nearby relatives, or enough money to pay out of pocket. Care is infrastructure for democracy.

Second, public financing should be expanded and designed to help candidates who cannot self-fund or rely on wealthy networks. Small-donor matching systems are not only campaign finance reforms; they are representation reforms. They allow candidates rooted in ordinary communities to compete without spending every waking hour courting affluent donors. For women with caregiving responsibilities, time is not an abstraction. A system that requires endless call time imposes a hidden tax on those whose hours are already claimed by unpaid labor.

Third, legislative jobs themselves must change. Many state legislatures pay low salaries, meet part-time, and require travel to state capitals. This arrangement is often defended as citizen-legislator democracy. In practice, it favors retirees, the independently wealthy, lawyers with flexible practices, business owners, and people with spouses who can absorb family responsibilities. If we want working parents and caregivers in office, legislatures need adequate pay, predictable schedules, parental leave, remote participation rules where appropriate, lactation rooms, childcare support, and anti-harassment systems. Professionalizing legislatures is often criticized, but amateurism has its own class and gender bias.

Fourth, political parties should treat caregiving as a recruitment issue. When local party meetings happen at times impossible for parents or shift workers, the party is choosing who can participate. When candidate interviews occur without attention to accessibility, transportation, or family schedules, the party is narrowing the field. Parties should provide childcare at major meetings, vary meeting times, use remote participation, and compensate organizers where possible. These reforms sound mundane, but democracy is made or broken in mundane places.

Fifth, media norms must change. Women candidates are still asked to narrate their family arrangements in ways men are not. Mothers of young children are treated as suspect: too distracted, too ambitious, too neglectful. Fathers are often praised for the same family life that burdens women politically. During the 2020 presidential campaign, Elizabeth Warren’s policy expertise did not spare her from gendered electability doubts; Kamala Harris faced scrutiny shaped by both race and gender; Amy Klobuchar was judged through familiar tropes of toughness and likability. These dynamics are not only about presidential politics. They trickle down to local races, where voters and journalists may apply even more traditional expectations.

Sixth, the care workforce itself should be represented. It is not enough to elect women who can overcome care barriers by virtue of professional status. Domestic workers, nurses, teachers, home health aides, childcare workers, service workers, and family caregivers understand the policy failures of the United States from the inside. Yet low wages, irregular schedules, immigration status, and lack of political connections keep many far from candidacy. Unions, worker centers, and community organizations can play a crucial role here. Candidate recruitment should not be limited to lawyers and nonprofit executives. If care is central to public life, caregivers should be central to public office.

This care-centered approach complements Piscopo’s argument about positive rights. The right to be elected should not be imagined only as a right to appear on the ballot or occupy a party list. It should include the material conditions that make candidacy and officeholding possible. A formal right that can be exercised only by those with private support is not equal citizenship. It is a door technically open but practically guarded.

At the same time, care politics helps avoid one weakness in some parity arguments. Calls for more women in office sometimes rely on the idea that women are naturally more nurturing or collaborative. That is both unfair and unnecessary. Women do not deserve representation because they are better caregivers by nature. They deserve representation because society has assigned them disproportionate responsibility for care, and that assignment shapes their lives, opportunities, and political knowledge. Men who are caregivers also suffer under a politics built for the unencumbered. A care-centered democracy would expand freedom for everyone.

It would also expose the class dimension of representation. A Congress with more affluent women is preferable to a Congress with fewer women, but it will not fully address the exclusion of those who cannot afford to run. The same is true for state legislatures and city councils. If the only women who can enter politics are those who have already solved the care problem privately, then public policy will continue to treat care as private trouble rather than collective infrastructure.

The suffrage centennial comes at a moment when the country is being forced to see dependence. People depend on nurses, aides, grocery clerks, teachers, parents, neighbors, and public health workers. They depend on unemployment checks, food assistance, schools, hospitals, and local governments. The fantasy of the autonomous citizen—unburdened, self-sufficient, always available for public life—has never been true. It has simply described the people whose dependence was hidden by someone else’s labor.

Getting women elected will require party reform, money, recruitment, and perhaps in some places quotas or targets. But it will also require rebuilding political life around the realities of care. Until campaigning and governing are compatible with human dependency, the right to be elected will remain formally equal and substantively unequal. Women do not need another lecture about ambition. They need a democracy that does not require them to have a wife.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current political system is designed for candidates who have no household responsibilities or dependents, effectively excluding working- and middle-class women.
  2. 02Campaign finance rules should be reformed to allow the use of funds for childcare and eldercare expenses to level the playing field for caregivers.
  3. 03Legislative roles must be professionalized with adequate pay, predictable schedules, and parental leave to make officeholding accessible to those who are not independently wealthy.
  4. 04Political parties must modernize their recruitment and meeting structures to accommodate the schedules and needs of parents and shift workers.
  5. 05Media and voter norms must be challenged to end the double standards and gendered scrutiny applied to mothers and women candidates regarding their family lives.
  6. 06The right to be elected should be redefined as a positive right that includes the material conditions and infrastructure necessary to make candidacy possible for everyone.
  7. 07A care-centered approach to representation addresses class-based exclusion by ensuring that political office is not reserved for those who can solve care needs privately.

  1. 01The historical 'maternalist' strategy used by suffragists to gain the vote reinforced a division of labor that still limits women's political involvement to 'family issues.'
  2. 02Despite women's increased participation in the workforce, they continue to perform the vast majority of emotional, physical, and cognitive labor at home.
  3. 03The lack of intellectual space and free time resulting from domestic burdens constitutes a primary barrier to women seeking and holding political office.
  4. 04Meaningful representation requires public policies such as universal healthcare, funded daycare, and mandated family leave for men to equalize the domestic sphere.
  5. 05Men must move beyond being mere allies and take active responsibility for domestic life to create the necessary space for women to enter politics on equal terms.
