"To avoid repeating the historical exclusions of the suffrage movement, any modern agenda for women's political representation in the United States must be explicitly intersectional and address the structural barriers of both patriarchy and white supremacy."

The central strength of Jennifer Piscopo’s essay is also the place where American feminists must be most careful. She argues that women’s suffrage should be understood not only as the right to vote, but as the right to elect and be elected. She draws on Latin American parity movements to show how women transformed a formal legal right into a claim for actual governing power. That is a vital intervention. But in the United States, any movement for women’s representation that does not begin with race will reproduce the exclusions that have haunted women’s politics from the start.

The Nineteenth Amendment is often narrated as “women” winning the vote in 1920. Piscopo rightly complicates this by noting that many Black, Native, Latina, and Asian American women remained excluded by law, violence, citizenship restrictions, language barriers, and Jim Crow. But the problem is deeper than delayed inclusion. The mainstream suffrage movement itself often made strategic bargains with white supremacy. White suffragists argued that enfranchising white women would preserve white political power. Some treated Black men’s voting rights as an insult to educated white women. The centennial cannot be disentangled from this history.

That history matters for today’s representational agenda. “Women” are not underrepresented in the same way. White women have had access to certain forms of social power, donor networks, media sympathy, and party legitimacy that Black women, Latinas, Native women, Asian American women, Muslim women, immigrant women, disabled women, and working-class women have not. If the reform goal is simply more women in office, the beneficiaries may be those already closest to power.

This is not a theoretical concern. American politics has often converted diversity demands into opportunities for the least disruptive representatives of excluded groups. A corporate board adds a white woman and declares gender progress. A party recruits women candidates but looks first to lawyers, prosecutors, wealthy professionals, and relatives of male politicians. A donor network celebrates “electability” while filtering out candidates who lack elite credentials. Even the rhetoric of “qualified women” can become a trap if the standard of qualification is built around institutions that excluded many women in the first place.

Piscopo’s critique of the ambition narrative is therefore essential. Women of color are not absent from politics because they lack ambition. Black women have been among the most consistent organizers, voters, and movement builders in American democracy. Latina and Native women have led fights over labor rights, education, land, immigration, and sovereignty. Asian American women have organized across language and citizenship barriers. The issue is not whether these women are politically engaged. The issue is whether parties and donors recognize their leadership as electoral leadership.

This distinction is especially clear in 2020. The Democratic Party depends heavily on Black women voters. Black women helped produce the 2017 Senate victory in Alabama. They were central to the 2018 midterms. They are organizers, strategists, church leaders, union activists, and community anchors. Yet Black women remain dramatically underrepresented in statewide and executive offices. Stacey Abrams’s 2018 gubernatorial campaign in Georgia showed both the promise of a multiracial pro-democracy coalition and the ferocity of structural barriers, including voter suppression. Ayanna Pressley’s victory in Massachusetts, Ilhan Omar’s in Minnesota, Rashida Tlaib’s in Michigan, Deb Haaland’s in New Mexico, Sharice Davids’s in Kansas, Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez’s in New York, and Lauren Underwood’s in Illinois expanded the public imagination of who can serve in Congress. But their victories were not gifts from party establishments. Many had to defeat the assumptions of those establishments.

A serious right-to-be-elected agenda in the United States must therefore be intersectional not as a slogan, but as institutional design. First, parties should collect and publish data about recruitment, endorsements, staffing, vendor contracts, and funding by gender and race together. It is not enough to say a party supported women if most of that support went to white women. It is not enough to say it supported candidates of color if women of color were told to wait. Transparency would not solve exclusion, but it would make denial harder.

Second, public financing should be central. Wealth tests are racial and gender filters. Candidates from communities with less accumulated wealth face a donor gap before they make their first call. Small-donor matching systems and democracy vouchers can help candidates who have broad community support but lack access to max-out donors. Filing fees and ballot access costs should be lowered across the board, not only for women, because poverty is not evenly distributed by gender alone. Candidate childcare expenses should be eligible campaign expenditures, as the Federal Election Commission has allowed in some cases, and that principle should be made clear and universal.

Third, harassment must be understood through both misogyny and racism. Women in politics face sexualized abuse, but women of color face abuse that is also racist, xenophobic, Islamophobic, and sometimes tied to threats against their communities. The attacks on Ilhan Omar and Rashida Tlaib, for example, are not generic sexism. They question national belonging. They cast Muslim women as enemies. Likewise, Black women candidates are often framed as angry, ungrateful, or illegitimate. If parties want women of color to run, they must build rapid response systems, legal support, digital security training, and public norms that treat threats as anti-democratic conduct rather than background noise.

Fourth, reforms should reach local offices. The pipeline metaphor can be misleading because it implies a smooth route upward, but local office does matter. School boards, city councils, county commissions, sheriffs’ offices, prosecutors’ offices, and state legislatures shape daily life and create future candidates. These arenas often receive little media scrutiny while being governed by informal networks. Women of color are frequently encouraged to organize for candidates but not recruited as candidates. Party committees, unions, civic groups, and advocacy organizations should invest in local leadership long before a congressional seat opens.

Fifth, representation must include agenda-setting power. Descriptive representation without power can become display. Women of color should not be asked merely to diversify the photograph while leadership, committee chairs, consultants, and donors remain unchanged. Congresswoman Pressley’s statement that “the people closest to the pain should be closest to the power” is more than a campaign line; it is an institutional test. Who writes the platform? Who chairs the committee? Who controls the budget? Who is considered a messenger on “universal” issues such as the economy, health care, and national security, rather than confined to identity topics?

Piscopo is right to be wary of instrumental arguments that women should be included because women will make politics kinder or cleaner. That burden falls even more heavily on women of color, who are often expected to save democracy while being denied authority within it. Black women, in particular, are praised as the “backbone” of the Democratic Party in ways that can sound admiring but function exploitatively: backbone, not head; labor, not leadership. The claim should not be that women of color will redeem American politics. The claim is that American politics has no democratic legitimacy while it marginalizes them.

What about quotas? I am not opposed to them in principle. In many countries, they have worked. In the United States, however, a gender quota that does not account for race could deepen inequality. A race-conscious quota would face severe constitutional and political obstacles, especially in a judiciary increasingly skeptical of affirmative action. But voluntary party rules could be bolder. Parties could require diverse candidate slates for open seats. They could set targets for women of color in appointments, leadership, and endorsed races. They could refuse to spend in open primaries unless their process included real outreach beyond established networks. They could treat the absence of women of color from candidate pools as evidence of institutional failure, not demographic accident.

The question “What does it take to get women elected?” must be followed by “Which women, elected by whom, to do what, with what power?” Without those questions, parity risks becoming a ceiling for some and a cover story for others. The goal should not be a Congress where half the members are women but the class composition, racial hierarchy, militarism, and donor dependence of American politics remain untouched. Nor should the goal be symbolic firsts that leave communities unrepresented once the celebration ends.

The suffrage centennial arrives amid a pandemic that has exposed the racialized and gendered structure of American life. Women are disproportionately essential workers, caregivers, and job losers. Black, Latino, and Native communities are suffering devastating health and economic harms. Decisions about paid leave, unemployment insurance, schools, evictions, prisons, policing, and health care are decisions about whose lives count. Representation is not ornamental in this context. It is about who is in the room when survival is negotiated.

Piscopo’s essay gives us the right starting point: voting is not enough. But in the United States, the right to be elected must be built as a right against both patriarchy and white supremacy. Anything less would commemorate the Nineteenth Amendment by repeating its failures.

Race Is Not a Footnote

Jennifer Piscopo's essay arrives at a moment when American politics is being rearranged, in real time, by a mass mobilization against police violence that has put racial justice at the center of national debate in a way it has not been for decades. It is striking, then, that her account of women's representation treats race as a complication to be acknowledged near the end rather than as a feature central to the whole story. I want to spend this response essay placing race back at the center, because I think doing so changes both the diagnosis and the prescription.

Piscopo's narrative arc goes like this: in the nineteenth century, Latin American constitutions defined citizenship in terms of "the right to elect and be elected"; twentieth-century suffragists in those countries used this language to demand both vote and office; late-twentieth-century activists revived the framework to push for gender quotas, with great success. The American story is a parallel one in which the same conceptual move was, for historical reasons, not made. Piscopo's prescription is to make it now.

The trouble is that the parallel is partial. Latin American gender-quota campaigns succeeded in part by treating "women" as a unified political category whose claims could be advanced without reference to the racial, class, and indigenous hierarchies that also structure those societies. Piscopo cites Ruth Rubio-Marín making essentially this observation, and acknowledges that the rhetorical separation of gender from race "worked elsewhere" but is harder in the United States, where "white supremacy and patriarchy are intertwined." She then moves on. I want to stay with this point, because I think it is the crux of the American question.

When American women first organized for suffrage in the nineteenth century, the movement was already racially fractured. The white-led wing, exemplified by Stanton and Anthony, made strategic decisions to exclude Black women and Black suffrage demands, sometimes explicitly. When the Nineteenth Amendment passed in 1920, southern Black women remained effectively disenfranchised through poll taxes, literacy tests, and white terror for another four and a half decades. Native women had to wait until 1924 for citizenship and longer for the franchise in many states. Asian American women's access to citizenship was constrained by immigration and naturalization law until the 1940s and beyond. The 1920 centennial is, accurately understood, the centennial of white women's enfranchisement; for many other American women, the more meaningful date is 1965, or even later.

This history matters for the quota debate because it determines which "women" American advocates are talking about when they talk about women's representation. The 2018 wave of Democratic women elected to Congress was a genuine moment of change, and it was disproportionately a moment of change for women of color. Ayanna Pressley, Ilhan Omar, Rashida Tlaib, Sharice Davids, Deb Haaland — these are the names that mattered. The "wave" was not a wave of generic women; it was a wave of specifically situated women whose campaigns were rooted in racial-justice and immigration-justice constituencies. The structural exclusions they were overcoming were not only gendered but racialized. The local party leaders Piscopo mentions, who told women aspirants to wait their turn, were not just sexist. They were operating within party machines whose racial assumptions about who can win are deeply entrenched.

This suggests that any positive-action framework for the United States must be intersectional from the start, not as an afterthought. A gender quota that does not specify racial composition would, in the U.S. context, likely produce a Congress whose women members were disproportionately white, because white women have greater access to the donor networks, party connections, and educational credentials that current candidate-development infrastructure relies on. We have evidence for this. State legislatures with the highest percentages of women are not, on average, the state legislatures with the highest percentages of women of color. The two dimensions of representation are partially independent, and a reform aimed at one without attention to the other can entrench inequality on the other.

There is a deeper point here too. The reason positive-action frameworks have struggled in the United States is not just that the constitutional tradition is built on negative rights. It is that the most visible American experiment with positive action — race-conscious affirmative action — has been under sustained legal and political attack for forty years, has been narrowed by the Supreme Court in case after case, and is now hanging by a thread. American jurisprudence has moved steadily toward a "colorblind" reading of the Fourteenth Amendment that treats any race-conscious remedy as constitutionally suspect. To then introduce a sex-conscious remedy — a gender quota — into this legal environment is to invite a doctrinal collision. Opponents will argue, with some legal force, that if race-conscious remedies require strict scrutiny and narrow tailoring, sex-conscious remedies require at least intermediate scrutiny, and that a sweeping quota law fails both. The Latin American countries Piscopo discusses do not face this jurisprudential challenge in the same form, because their constitutional traditions accept "compensatory inequalities" as a legitimate framework. American constitutional law does not, except in narrow remedial contexts.

This means that any serious American positive-action campaign for women's representation must either (a) limit its ambitions to measures that survive existing constitutional doctrine — voluntary party rules, public-financing incentives, recruitment programs — or (b) commit to the much larger project of changing American constitutional doctrine to accept positive group rights. The latter would require revisiting the trajectory of affirmative-action jurisprudence and, in effect, winning the political and legal fight for race-conscious remedies as well. You cannot, in the American context, secure sex-conscious positive action while race-conscious positive action is being dismantled. The two stand or fall together.

This is, in some ways, a forbidding prospect. But it is also clarifying. It tells us that the path to gender quotas in the United States runs through, not around, the politics of racial justice. And it tells us that white-led feminist organizations whose strategic instinct has been to keep the gender question separate from the race question — to make the quota debate easier by not entangling it with affirmative action — are misreading the political terrain.

The encouraging news is that the women-of-color-led feminist organizing of the past decade has already begun to chart this integrated path. Higher Heights, Latino Victory Project, EMILY's List's increasing attention to women of color candidates, the Black Women's Roundtable's representation work — these organizations are doing the kind of intersectional candidate development that an honest American positive-action framework requires. They have built the infrastructure that produced the 2018 wave and that will produce future waves. If Piscopo's reframing of representation as a political right is to take root in the United States, it will take root through their work, not through an imported Latin American template.

I want to close by underlining what I take from Piscopo's essay, even as I push back. She is right that the American obsession with women's individual ambition is inadequate. She is right that representation is properly understood as a political right rather than a personal achievement. She is right that the centennial moment invites a deeper conversation than the one we are having. I just want that conversation to begin from the recognition that American women have never been a single political category, that the suffrage history she narrates has been racially uneven from the start, and that the representation we should be fighting for is one that takes that unevenness seriously. Otherwise, we will end up with a more demographically diverse version of the same exclusion.
