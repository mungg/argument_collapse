Diversified MiniMax

"Merve Emre’s argument that 'all reproduction is assisted' is politically incomplete because it centers the experiences of those seeking pregnancy while conspicuously omitting the foundational role of abortion rights in the reproductive justice framework."

The most striking thing about Merve Emre's essay is what it doesn't say. In 7,500 words about reproductive technology and feminist theory, the word "abortion" appears only once, in a historical reference to Sherri Finkbine. This omission is conspicuous and revealing.

Emre's argument that "all reproduction is assisted" implicitly universalizes assisted reproduction as the paradigmatic form of reproduction. But this framing centers the experiences of women who want to become pregnant—a relatively privileged position in the reproductive hierarchy. The essay's silence on abortion rights, especially given the political context of August 2018, suggests a troubling gap in Emre's reproductive justice framework.

At this moment—three months before the Kavanaugh hearings that would imperil Roe v. Wade—the right to abortion was under renewed attack across the United States. Emre mentions abortion only as a historical curiosity, a practice that prompted 1960s doctors to worry about artificial wombs creating life without maternal consent. She doesn't engage with contemporary abortion politics or the ways assisted reproduction intersects with abortion access.

This matters because the "natural/artificial" binary Emre critiques operates differently in abortion debates than in assisted reproduction debates. Opponents of abortion frequently invoke the "natural" to delegitimize abortion as an interference with "natural" pregnancy and birth. Meanwhile, supporters of abortion rights often claim that abortion is a "natural" part of reproductive life, that women's bodies have always regulated reproduction through miscarriage and miscarriage alone. The politics of "natural" are more complicated than Emre's critique suggests.

Emre's invocation of Dorothy Roberts's reproductive justice framework is welcome but underdeveloped. Roberts has been clear that reproductive justice includes the right to have children, the right not to have children, and the right to parent children in safe and sustainable environments. Emre's essay emphasizes the first right but largely neglects the second, which is under far more immediate political threat in August 2018.

The essay's case studies also reveal a blind spot. S, B, N, and K all want to become pregnant and are trying various forms of assisted reproduction. The essay doesn't include anyone who has chosen abortion—or who has been denied abortion access, or who has faced coercion to continue unwanted pregnancies. These experiences are as much a part of reproductive justice as access to IVF.

Emre is right that reproductive justice requires more than abortion rights. But abortion rights remain the foundational demand of reproductive justice, the precondition for all other reproductive choices. An essay about assisted reproduction that doesn't engage with abortion politics in 2018 is incomplete at best.

The essay's philosophical sophistication shouldn't distract from its political implications. If "all reproduction is assisted" becomes a rallying cry for reproductive justice, we need to ask: assisted by what, for whom, and under whose control? Technology is not neutral. The same techniques used to help women conceive can be used to surveil them, control them, or deny them care. A politics of reproduction must grapple with this complexity rather than celebrating technological mediation as inherently liberatory.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The essay’s silence on contemporary abortion politics is a significant oversight given the immediate legal threats to reproductive autonomy at the time of its publication.
  2. 02The 'natural/artificial' binary operates differently and more complexly in abortion debates than it does in the context of assisted reproductive technologies.
  3. 03By focusing almost exclusively on the right to have children, the essay neglects the equally vital right not to have children, which is a core pillar of Dorothy Roberts’s reproductive justice model.
  4. 04The case studies provided in the essay reflect a privileged blind spot by excluding the experiences of individuals who have sought abortions or faced reproductive coercion.
  5. 05Technological mediation in reproduction is not inherently liberatory and can be used as a tool for surveillance and control rather than just assistance.
