"The Democratic Party should move beyond debating ideological positioning and instead focus on structural transformation, building a grassroots-led movement that earns anti-establishment credibility through deep community organizing and internal democratization."

The lead essay gets one thing absolutely right: the Democratic Party’s strategic conversation has been hijacked by a stale debate between “moderates” and “progressives,” both of whom assume that the main task is to position the party correctly on a left-right spectrum. That assumption itself is the problem. Bonica and Grumbach’s proposal to shift the axis to corruption represents a creative attempt to break out of that trap. But it remains trapped in another: the assumption that framing alone can build a durable electoral majority. What the moment demands is not a new frame but a new party structure—one capable of a genuinely popular, grassroots-led movement that can channel anti-system anger into democratic renewal, not Trumpist authoritarianism.

The authors are undoubtedly correct that the old electoral laws no longer hold. They cite extensive research showing that candidate ideology has little independent effect in an era of nationalized, identity-sorted politics. But if ideology doesn’t matter much, then the choice between “moderate” and “progressive” is a red herring: either type of Democrat will be painted as a radical socialist by the right-wing media machine, and either will be judged by the national party brand. So the party’s core challenge is not which positions to adopt but how to build a brand that can withstand that machine and connect with voters who currently distrust all institutions, including the Democratic Party itself.

The authors’ anti-corruption axis is a plausible start, but it suffers from an inescapable weakness: it asks voters to trust a party to fight corruption when that party has been part of the same corrupt system for decades. The public knows that Democrats take corporate cash, that they enrich themselves through insider stock trading, that they make promises on the campaign trail and then govern as technocrats beholden to donors. The authors recommend “genuine, costly reform”—rejecting corporate PAC money, banning stock trading. These are worthy measures, but they are also small and defensive. They don’t fundamentally alter the party’s relationship to power. To truly earn anti-establishment credibility, the party would need to democratize itself: open its primary processes, reduce the influence of superdelegates, empower small-dollar donors over bundlers, and, most critically, devolve strategic decision-making to local chapters and affiliated social movements, rather than hoarding it in consultant class war rooms in D.C.

Here, the essay’s call for “authentic, working-class candidates” is oddly at odds with its top-down framing approach. Candidates don’t emerge from focus groups; they emerge from movements. If the party wants nurses and teachers in office, it needs to make it possible for them to run by providing stipends, childcare, and independent fundraising infrastructure that doesn’t rely on personal wealth or elite networks. But more than that, it needs to create a political ecosystem in which being a Democratic candidate means being embedded in ongoing organizing—tenant unions, strike support, mutual aid groups—so that voters encounter the party not as a parachute campaign every two years but as a constant presence that fights for them. That kind of organizing-heavy, relationship-based politics is the only antidote to the toxic combination of right-wing propaganda and Democratic absenteeism that plagues rural and exurban areas.

This points to a deeper shortcoming in the essay’s analysis of turnout. The authors note that the turnout gap was devastating in 2024 and that progressive candidates slightly boost base turnout. But they treat turnout almost as a mechanical outcome of campaign messaging: energize the base with an anti-corruption message, and they will show up. But nonvoting is not merely apathy; it is often a rational response to a political system that has failed to deliver. The most reliable predictor of nonvoting is low socioeconomic status, precarious employment, and a past negative experience with government services. Getting those voters to turn out requires more than a compelling slogan; it requires demonstrating that voting can change their material conditions. That means the party must govern differently where it already has power—cities and states—and show what Democratic control can actually do: free public transit, social housing, community land trusts, universal pre-K. When people in Milwaukee see that Madison has fixed their streets and provided good jobs because of a progressive state government, they have a reason to vote. That’s the mobilization the authors gesture at but don’t flesh out.

The essay’s historical and comparative references are also selective. The authors mention European center-left parties flailing against the far right when they adopt anti-immigration positions. True enough. But they ignore the successful models in Denmark and elsewhere where social democrats have combined tough immigration positions with robust welfare state expansion, thereby undercutting the far right’s populist appeal. That strategy isn’t “moderation” in the American sense—it’s an uncompromising defense of the welfare state paired with cultural assertiveness. The Democrats could learn from this: champion a universal economic program (Medicare for All, a job guarantee, massive investment in child care) while also breaking with the activist groups that insist on expansive policies on policing, transgender athletes, and border enforcement that have little public support and serve as a constant drag on the party’s brand. This is not popularism as the authors caricature it; it is strategic prioritization that refuses to let the perfect be the enemy of the winnable.

The anti-corruption frame, as described, tends to elide this cultural dimension entirely. It presumes that voters who are disgusted by insider trading will set aside their concerns about crime, immigration, and changing social norms. But in the real world, those concerns are often more salient and emotionally potent. The party that aims to unite people against corruption must also credibly address those fears without simply dismissing them as racist or transphobic. A candidate who says, “I’m going to go after the billionaires who rigged the economy and I’m going to make your neighborhood safe and I’m going to secure the border so that workers’ wages stop falling” is delivering a coherent, all-of-the-above populist message that doesn’t require picking a side in intra-left culture wars. That message is neither simply moderate nor uniformly progressive; it’s a new synthesis that could rebuild the blue-collar multiracial coalition the party once had.

Ultimately, the essay’s fatalism about strategy—the admission that “nobody knows for certain” what to do—is both refreshing and paralyzing. Yes, there is deep uncertainty. But the path forward is not to choose between a poll-tested moderation and a poll-tested anti-corruption frame; it’s to invest in the kind of long-term organizing that makes the party less dependent on the whims of the political moment. When the party is rooted in communities, it can survive the messages the other side hurls at it. When it has a clear, popular economic program, it can afford to be patient on cultural change. And when it has internal democracy, it earns the trust to lead.

History will indeed judge whether Democrats fought hard enough. But how they fight matters. A fight led by consultants armed with new frames will lose. A fight led by millions of organized people who have seen the party stand with them in their daily struggles has a chance. That is the transformation the authors dance around but never fully embrace.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The party's core challenge is not choosing between moderate or progressive policies, but building a brand resilient enough to withstand nationalized right-wing media attacks.
  2. 02To credibly claim an anti-corruption stance, the party must undergo costly internal reforms that reduce the influence of donors and consultants while empowering local chapters.
  3. 03Authentic working-class candidates must be cultivated through movements and material support rather than being selected by top-down party apparatuses.
  4. 04Increasing voter turnout requires more than a new message; it requires demonstrating the material benefits of Democratic governance in cities and states where the party already holds power.
  5. 05The party should adopt a strategic synthesis that pairs a universal economic program with a willingness to break from unpopular activist positions on cultural issues.
  6. 06Long-term community organizing is the only way to make the party less vulnerable to the volatility of national political tides and propaganda.
