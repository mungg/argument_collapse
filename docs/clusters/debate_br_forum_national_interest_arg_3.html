Diversified Gemini

"The United States should reject offshore balancing in favor of a strategy of deep integration that leverages economic globalization and interdependence to maintain its global primacy."

Stephen M. Walt’s "In the National Interest" is a masterclass in traditional international relations theory, offering a compelling critique of the Bush administration’s neo-imperial blunders. Yet, reading Walt’s essay in 2005 is somewhat like reading a brilliant treatise on naval strategy on the eve of the aviation age. His worldview is thoroughly state-centric and military-focused. He defines power almost entirely in terms of troop deployments, regional hegemony, and nuclear warheads. In doing so, he misses the actual taproot of American primacy in the 21st century: economic globalization and the intricate web of interdependence that binds the world to the U.S. economy.

Walt’s proposed strategy, "offshore balancing," envisions the United States as a geopolitical mechanic, stepping back to let regional powers balance each other and only intervening militarily when a hegemon threatens to emerge in Europe, Asia, or the Persian Gulf. This 19th-century model of geopolitics completely ignores the reality that in today’s world, security and economics are inextricably linked, and the greatest threats to American stability are often non-military.

Consider the regions Walt identifies as vital. He argues we should draw down forces in Europe because it is "reliably democratic" and faces no military threats. But the U.S.-European relationship is not just a military alliance (NATO); it is the central artery of the global economy. Transatlantic trade and investment drive global growth. Maintaining this relationship requires constant, deep engagement, not an "offshore" posture. If the U.S. steps back politically and militarily, it risks the economic divergence of the EU, which is increasingly flexing its regulatory and economic muscles.

Even more glaring is Walt’s treatment of Asia. He views Asia primarily through the lens of containing a rising China via military balancing. But China is not the Soviet Union; it is not economically isolated behind an Iron Curtain. As of 2005, China is rapidly becoming the factory of the world and a massive holder of U.S. Treasury securities. The American and Chinese economies are engaged in a state of mutually assured economic dependence. If the U.S. pursues a classic containment strategy, leaning heavily on military alliances with Japan and India to box China in, it risks triggering an economic decoupling that would devastate the American standard of living.

The true grand strategy for the United States should not be offshore balancing, but deep integration. American primacy is sustained not just by our aircraft carriers, but by the fact that the U.S. dollar is the global reserve currency, that Wall Street is the financial hub of the world, and that American technology companies dominate the global information architecture. When other nations grow rich, they do so largely by integrating into a system where the rules were written by Washington.

Walt expresses concern about nations "balancing" against the United States. But traditional military balancing against the U.S. is practically impossible given our massive defense spending. The real balancing happening today is economic and institutional. It is the formation of the G-20, the push for regional trade blocs that exclude the U.S., and efforts to price oil in Euros instead of dollars. Walt’s military-centric strategy offers no tools to manage this soft balancing.

Furthermore, Walt’s realism leads to a dangerous apathy toward the internal governance of other states, so long as they don't threaten regional balances. He criticizes the Bush administration’s democracy promotion as a "fool’s errand." While the invasion of Iraq was indeed a disastrous method of spreading democracy, the underlying premise—that autocratic, economically stagnant societies breed instability—is sound.

The Middle East is volatile not just because of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, as Walt heavily emphasizes, but because of a profound demographic and economic crisis. Millions of young Arabs are entering the workforce with no job prospects and no political voice in corrupt, authoritarian regimes. Walt’s strategy of relying on these very regimes as "local actors to uphold the regional balance" only perpetuates this stagnation. The U.S. should not impose democracy at the point of a gun, but it must use its economic leverage—trade agreements, foreign direct investment conditions, and integration into the WTO—to force economic liberalization, which historically paves the way for political reform.

Finally, Walt’s brief mention of the WTO and global warming reveals how secondary these issues are to his thinking. He suggests offering a "fair-minded proposal" on Kyoto simply as a public relations move to make U.S. hegemony look better. But climate change, energy dependency, and global financial stability are core security issues. A pandemic originating in Asia or a collapse of the global financial system poses a more immediate threat to the American way of life than a regional hegemon in the Persian Gulf.

If America wants to preserve its primacy, it cannot do so by playing hard-to-get and retreating to an offshore balancer role. It must double down on globalization. It must lead the Doha Development Round of trade negotiations, reform the IMF and World Bank to give emerging markets a stake in the system, and spearhead a technological revolution in alternative energy. American power is most effective not when it is poised over the horizon waiting for a war, but when it is woven so deeply into the fabric of the global economy that our prosperity and the world’s prosperity are indistinguishable.

  2. 02Offshore balancing fails to address 'soft balancing' where nations challenge the U.S. through economic blocs and institutional shifts rather than military competition.
  3. 03Withdrawing militarily from vital regions like Europe risks economic divergence and the loss of influence over the central arteries of the global economy.
  4. 04A classic military containment of China is counterproductive because the U.S. and Chinese economies are characterized by mutually assured economic dependence.
  5. 05The U.S. must use economic leverage and trade integration rather than military force to encourage liberalization in autocratic regions that breed instability.
  6. 06Transnational threats like climate change and financial instability are core security issues that require constant global leadership rather than an over-the-horizon military posture.
Diversified Gemini

"The United States must move beyond the narrow, amoral realism of offshore balancing to make soft power and moral legitimacy the core of its foreign policy rather than a mere secondary tool."

In his compelling essay, Stephen M. Walt diagnoses the central paradox of contemporary American foreign policy: the United States possesses unparalleled power, yet this very primacy generates fear, resentment, and resistance globally. Walt’s prescription is “offshore balancing,” accompanied by a plea for diplomatic restraint and improved public diplomacy. While Walt rightly criticizes the Bush administration’s abrasive unilateralism, he treats the struggle for hearts and minds as an appendage to his grand strategy, a mere lubricant to reduce the friction of American military and economic dominance. In truth, the crisis of American legitimacy is the central strategic challenge of our time, and “soft power” must be the core, not the corollary, of our foreign policy.

Walt defines American power primarily in traditional, hard terms: military force, economic leverage, and the ability to maintain regional balances. He acknowledges that “American power is most effective when it is seen as legitimate,” but his solutions—better public relations, Arabic-language broadcasting, and university exchanges—betray a superficial understanding of how legitimacy is generated and sustained in the information age.

Public diplomacy, as Walt frames it, is fundamentally reactive. It is about explaining our policies better or applying a softer rhetorical gloss to our geopolitical maneuvers. He suggests that if we had just spun our rejection of the Kyoto Protocol differently, or if we marketed our Middle East policies with more “care and respect,” anti-Americanism would wane. But as Walt himself notes briefly, “any public diplomacy effort... needs a good product to sell.” The problem is not our marketing; the problem is the product. The product is a foreign policy perceived as fundamentally hypocritical.

The United States cannot win the war of ideas while practicing the cold, amoral realism that offshore balancing often requires. Walt argues we should return to relying on “local actors” to uphold regional balances, which in the Middle East has historically meant propping up autocrats in Saudi Arabia and Egypt. He simultaneously calls for a “broad-based and sustained public-information campaign” to win over Arab youth. This is cognitive dissonance. Arab youth are not angry because they lack access to American websites; they are angry because American actions contradict American ideals. They see the U.S. preaching democracy while funding the secret police that oppress them. No amount of Arabic broadcasting on Alhurra or Radio Sawa can obscure that reality.

Furthermore, Walt’s recommendation to explicitly use international institutions like the UN and NATO merely as a “de facto ‘buddy system’ to regulate the large-scale use of [U.S.] military power” is deeply cynical and easily seen through by the rest of the world. Soft power is not about tricking other nations into feeling consulted; it is about genuinely binding oneself to shared rules and norms. Legitimacy arises when the hegemon agrees to operate within a system of international law, even when it is occasionally inconvenient. When the U.S. uses institutions merely as a rubber stamp for decisions already made in Washington, it delegitimizes both the institution and itself.

To truly rebuild American soft power, we must move beyond the transactional mindset of offshore balancing. First, we must align our policies with our proclaimed values. In the Middle East, this does mean, as Walt bravely argues, abandoning unconditional support for Israeli expansionism, which is a massive drain on our moral authority. But it also means gradually decoupling from Arab dictatorships. We must accept the short-term instability of democratic transitions in the Arab world rather than clinging to the false stability of authoritarian “balancers.”

Second, the U.S. must reclaim its role as the provider of global public goods. Walt’s strategy focuses on preventing bad things (regional hegemons, nuclear terrorism). But American primacy was built on doing good things: Marshall Plan aid, securing sea lanes for free trade, eradicating diseases. In 2005, a true soft power strategy would make the U.S. the undisputed leader in combating the HIV/AIDS pandemic in Africa, the largest investor in green technologies to address climate change (not just offering “fair-minded proposals”), and the primary engine for global poverty reduction. When the U.S. military is deployed for disaster relief—as it recently was following the devastating 2004 Indian Ocean tsunami—American approval ratings skyrocket in places like Indonesia. That is the true taproot of global influence.

Finally, we must recognize that in a digitized world, non-state actors, NGOs, and global public opinion have strategic weight. Osama bin Laden did not out-communicate us because he had better broadcasting equipment; he did so because his narrative of victimhood resonated with lived realities. Defeating this narrative requires more than state-to-state diplomacy; it requires demonstrating that the American-led order offers a better, more just future for ordinary people.

Walt is right that global hegemony and preventive war are disastrous. But his offshore balancing is too narrow, too focused on the chessboard of nation-states, to secure American interests in the 21st century. Persuasion is indeed more powerful than compulsion, but persuasion requires moral authority. Until the United States is willing to act not just as a global balancer, but as a genuine global citizen, our power will continue to provoke the resistance that threatens our security.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Public diplomacy is ineffective if it merely markets a 'product' or foreign policy that is perceived globally as fundamentally hypocritical.
  2. 02The strategy of relying on authoritarian local actors to maintain regional balances directly contradicts and undermines American efforts to win the hearts and minds of oppressed populations.
  3. 03Using international institutions as a cynical 'buddy system' to rubber-stamp unilateral decisions delegitimizes the U.S. and the international order.
  4. 04True soft power requires the U.S. to genuinely bind itself to international law and shared norms, even when doing so is inconvenient to its immediate interests.
  5. 05The U.S. must reclaim its global influence by becoming the primary provider of global public goods, such as leading the fight against pandemics and climate change.
  6. 06In a digitized world, the U.S. must compete with the narratives of non-state actors by demonstrating that an American-led order offers a more just and prosperous future for ordinary people.
Diversified Gemini

"The strategy of offshore balancing is a flawed policy of retreat that would create a global power vacuum, embolden enemies, and undermine American security."

Stephen M. Walt’s “In the National Interest” is a sophisticated attempt to disguise a strategy of retreat as a masterstroke of grand strategy. Writing as the United States is engaged in a difficult but necessary war to reshape the Middle East, Walt advises us to cut our losses, pull back our forces, and trust in a strategy of “offshore balancing.” While his critique of the messy realities of Iraq hits home in 2005, his proposed alternative is fundamentally flawed. It relies on a naive trust in “regional allies,” a profound misunderstanding of deterrence, and a willingness to cede American sovereignty to an international “buddy system.”

The core mechanism of Walt’s offshore balancing is reliance on local actors to uphold the balance of power, with the U.S. intervening only when those actors fail. This sounds elegant in a political science seminar, but it is disastrous in practice. Who exactly are these reliable regional balancers in the Middle East? Are we to trust a fragile, post-Saddam Iraq to balance against Iran? Are we to rely on the House of Saud, a regime that simultaneously relies on our protection while exporting the Wahhabism that fueled 9/11?

The reality is that regional actors in the Middle East are weak, duplicitous, and inherently unstable. By stepping back and expecting them to manage their own security, we do not create a stable balance; we invite a chaotic scramble for dominance. When the U.S. signaled a hands-off approach in the late 1980s, Saddam Hussein invaded Kuwait. If we pull back now, as Walt suggests, the resulting vacuum will not be filled by a neat balance of power, but by Iranian hegemony, proxy wars, and the unchecked proliferation of terror networks.

Walt’s disdain for the Bush administration’s doctrine of preemption (or preventive war) forms the crux of his argument. He claims it makes the U.S. look "eager to use force." But Walt fails to grasp the post-9/11 reality: deterrence, as constructed during the Cold War, does not work against non-state actors who welcome martyrdom, nor does it work reliably against rogue regimes that might slip a dirty bomb to those actors.

Walt asks, “would any leader simply give [WMDs] to terrorists when that leader could not control how the terrorists might use the weapons...?” The answer is, we don't know for sure, and that uncertainty is unacceptable. We know that Pakistani scientist A.Q. Khan ran a global nuclear black market under the nose of his government. We know that state sponsors of terror frequently use proxies precisely for plausible deniability. Waiting for the “imminent threat” that satisfies international lawyers means waiting for a mushroom cloud in Manhattan. The willingness to strike first—and the global knowledge that we will do so—is the only true deterrent against the nexus of rogue states and terror networks.

Even more troubling is Walt’s suggestion that the U.S. should subject its military actions to a “de facto ‘buddy system’” like the UN Security Council. He claims this isn't ceding control, but merely a tool for "reassurance." This is a distinction without a difference. The UN Security Council gave France and Russia—nations with deep financial ties to Saddam Hussein—a veto over American national security. To suggest that the United States must seek a permission slip from Jacques Chirac or Vladimir Putin before defending its interests is a dereliction of sovereignty. The world respects strength and resolve, not a superpower that ties itself down like Gulliver with multilateral red tape.

Walt’s extensive attack on U.S. policy toward Israel is equally wrongheaded. He blames the U.S.-Israel relationship for Arab anti-Americanism and demands we force Israel to its 1967 borders. This buys into the terrorists' own propaganda. Al Qaeda attacks us not because of where Israel draws its borders, but because we are the primary obstacle to their radical Islamic vision. Israel is our most capable ally in this fight. Threatening to abandon them, as Walt advocates, would be viewed by our enemies not as an act of “evenhandedness,” but as a catastrophic failure of American resolve.

Finally, Walt’s vision of America “playing hard-to-get” is dangerously arrogant. He assumes that if we threaten to leave Europe or Asia, our allies will “bend over backward” to appease us. The opposite is far more likely. If we demonstrate that our commitments are conditional and our staying power is weak, nations like Japan, South Korea, and Poland will not grovel; they will either accommodate regional bullies (like China or Russia) or rapidly militarize, potentially acquiring their own nuclear weapons.

We do not need to nation-build everywhere, and the execution in Iraq has been undeniably flawed. But the solution is not to retreat to the oceans and hope the world balances itself. The United States must maintain overwhelming, forward-deployed military primacy. We must punish our enemies swiftly and decisively, without feeling the need to rebuild their societies afterward, and without asking the UN for permission. We must support our genuine allies, not threaten them. Walt’s “offshore balancing” is a recipe for a more dangerous, chaotic world where American power is diluted and American enemies are emboldened.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Relying on weak or duplicitous regional actors to maintain a balance of power is unrealistic and invites regional instability or hegemony by hostile states.
  2. 02Traditional deterrence is ineffective against non-state actors and rogue regimes, making the ability to conduct preventive strikes a necessary security tool in a post-9/11 world.
  3. 03Subjecting American military action to an international 'buddy system' or multilateral institutions like the UN is a dangerous surrender of national sovereignty.
  4. 04Conditioning support for Israel based on territorial concessions validates terrorist propaganda and signals a catastrophic lack of American resolve to allies and enemies alike.
  5. 05Reducing America's global military footprint would cause allies to militarize or accommodate regional bullies rather than encouraging them to cooperate more closely with the United States.
  6. 06The United States should maintain forward-deployed military primacy and the willingness to punish enemies decisively without the burden of nation-building or seeking international permission.
