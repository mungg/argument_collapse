"While experimentalist governance is a valuable framework for climate policy, it must be expanded into a more pluralistic 'climate realism' that addresses adaptation, diverse energy technologies, and the management of long-term climate emergencies under deep uncertainty."

The great virtue of David Victor and Charles Sabel’s essay is humility. Not passivity, not fatalism, but humility about what governments and diplomats can know in advance. Climate policy is often discussed as though the central task were to identify the correct master plan and then summon the will to implement it. Victor and Sabel remind us that no such plan exists. Deep decarbonization requires discovery. It requires institutions that can try, fail, compare, revise, and scale.

That is the right starting point. But the logic of humility should be pushed further. A serious climate strategy must be even more pluralistic than the lead essay suggests. It should include not only sectoral mitigation experiments, but also adaptation, carbon removal, nuclear power, land-use reform, resilience, and perhaps even research governance for solar geoengineering. To “fix the climate” is not simply to cut emissions through industrial transformation, although that is central. It is to manage a long emergency under conditions of deep uncertainty.

The mitigation challenge is already daunting. Electricity must become nearly carbon-free while demand rises from vehicles, heating, industry, and digital infrastructure. Transportation must shift toward electricity, cleaner fuels, and better urban design. Buildings must be retrofitted. Methane must be controlled. Heavy industry must find new processes. Agriculture and land use must change. The world must do all this while expanding energy access, maintaining reliability, and navigating geopolitical competition.

Experimentalist governance is well suited to many of these tasks. It makes little sense to imagine that a single global rule could tell India, California, Germany, Kenya, and China how to integrate renewables into their grids. Local conditions differ: demand patterns, weather, institutions, fuel mixes, land availability, public acceptance, and finance. But it does make sense to create mechanisms for comparing experience. Which market designs reward flexibility? Which storage technologies work at which durations? How do distribution utilities manage rooftop solar? What happens to reliability when coal plants retire? What rate structures protect poor households? These are empirical questions that can be answered only by doing.

The same is true of buildings. A heat pump mandate may be sensible in one region and premature in another if housing stock, electricity prices, insulation, and winter temperatures differ. Retrofit programs can fail if they ignore landlords, renters, contractors, or upfront costs. Here the experimentalist emphasis on ground-level knowledge is indispensable. Decarbonization will enter basements, kitchens, roofs, and monthly bills. Policies must learn from the people who live with them.

Still, an experimentalist climate agenda should avoid narrowing the field of acceptable options too early. In some climate debates, “innovation” is invoked as an excuse to delay deployment of existing technologies. That is a real danger. But the opposite danger also exists: assuming that today’s favored technologies can carry the whole burden. Solar, wind, batteries, and efficiency have made remarkable progress, and their growth is essential. But a resilient zero-carbon system may also require advanced nuclear power, geothermal energy, long-duration storage, carbon capture for certain industrial processes, synthetic fuels, and negative emissions.

Nuclear power is the most obvious test of climate pragmatism. It is deeply contested, for understandable reasons: cost overruns, waste, safety, weapons proliferation, and public distrust. Yet it remains one of the largest sources of zero-carbon electricity in the United States and Europe, and some countries have used it to maintain relatively low-carbon grids. An experimentalist approach would not settle the nuclear debate by ideology. It would ask where existing plants can safely operate longer, whether new designs can actually reduce cost and risk, how regulation can be rigorous without being paralyzing, and whether nuclear can complement variable renewables. It would also be willing to conclude, in particular cases, that nuclear is too expensive or too slow. The point is to test rather than anathematize.

Carbon capture deserves similar treatment. It has often been oversold, especially by fossil fuel interests that present it as a reason to keep burning coal, oil, and gas indefinitely. Many projects have disappointed. But some industrial emissions are difficult to eliminate, and carbon capture may be needed for cement, chemicals, hydrogen production, or negative emissions when paired with sustainable biomass or direct air capture. The experimentalist question is not whether carbon capture is a universal solution; it is where, if anywhere, it is technically credible, economically sane, and environmentally safe. Public support should be milestone-based and transparent, not a blank check for incumbents.

Carbon removal raises even harder governance questions. Most pathways remain expensive, uncertain, land-intensive, energy-intensive, or ecologically risky. Yet climate models that limit warming often assume large-scale negative emissions later this century. That should worry us. It would be reckless to use speculative future removal as a license for slower emissions cuts today. But it would also be reckless not to explore removal options seriously. The world may need them to balance residual emissions and perhaps eventually reduce atmospheric concentrations. Experimental governance could help distinguish genuine promise from hype.

Adaptation should be elevated from the margins to the center of climate strategy. Even rapid mitigation will not prevent all warming, and current impacts are already severe. Wildfires, hurricanes, heat waves, floods, droughts, and sea-level rise are not future abstractions. They are present stresses on infrastructure, insurance systems, public health, agriculture, and migration. A governance model oriented around learning is highly relevant here because adaptation is local and uncertain. Which coastal defenses work? When is retreat wiser than protection? How should cities reduce heat deaths? How should water rights adapt to drought? How can wildfire risk be reduced without destroying ecosystems or bankrupting households? These questions demand experimentation, monitoring, and revision.

The Covid-19 pandemic should make this lesson impossible to ignore. Societies that looked efficient often proved brittle. Supply chains, hospitals, public agencies, and social safety nets were tested by shock. Climate change will bring repeated shocks, some acute and some gradual. A climate strategy focused only on emissions curves may miss the institutional resilience needed to protect people. Public health departments, emergency management agencies, local governments, water districts, utilities, and community organizations are climate institutions too.

There is also a security dimension. Climate impacts can aggravate instability, but climate policy itself will reshape geopolitics. Clean energy supply chains depend on minerals, manufacturing capacity, shipping routes, and standards. Oil and gas exporters face uncertain futures. Competition between the United States and China may accelerate clean technology in some areas and obstruct cooperation in others. Sectoral clubs may help, but they must be designed with geopolitical realism. A world of rivalry will not produce frictionless global governance.

That realism also applies to trade. Victor and Sabel are right that standards and market access can discipline laggards. But penalties can escalate. Carbon border adjustments may be necessary to prevent leakage and protect firms investing in cleaner production. They may also provoke accusations of green protectionism. The answer is not to avoid them but to make them transparent, linked to actual carbon performance, and paired with assistance for poorer trading partners. Experimentalism must operate within a world where trust is limited.

One area the lead essay does not discuss much is solar geoengineering. That omission is understandable; the subject is fraught. Deliberately reflecting sunlight to cool the planet would not solve ocean acidification, would carry uncertain regional effects, and could create severe governance problems. It could be misused as an excuse to slow emissions cuts. Yet as warming risks grow, the pressure to research or even deploy such technologies may increase. The worst outcome would be ignorance combined with panic. We need public, international, transparent research governance now—not deployment, but rules for studying risks, monitoring unilateral activity, and ensuring that vulnerable countries have a voice. Humility requires preparing for uncomfortable possibilities.

The lead essay’s critique of Paris is broadly persuasive, but I would frame Paris less as a failed engine and more as a weak constitution for a crowded ecosystem. It cannot decarbonize steel or redesign grids. But it can set shared goals, structure expectations, and provide legitimacy for more specialized regimes. The task is not to choose between Paris and sectoral experimentalism. It is to connect Paris, national policy, city networks, industrial standards, development finance, and civil society pressure into a system that learns faster.

The Biden administration will have an opportunity to rebuild U.S. credibility, but credibility will not come from rejoining Paris alone. It will come from performance: cutting domestic emissions, investing in technology, supporting workers, financing poorer countries, and cooperating where possible even with rivals. It should create demonstration programs for hard-to-decarbonize sectors, strengthen appliance and vehicle standards, support advanced energy research, reform transmission policy, and use federal procurement to create early markets. It should also invest in adaptation and resilience with the same seriousness it brings to mitigation.

The public should be wary of anyone offering a single answer. Carbon prices are useful but insufficient. Regulation is necessary but imperfect. Innovation is vital but not automatic. Protest is powerful but not a deployment plan. Markets can scale solutions but also misprice risk and ignore justice. Governments can coordinate action but can be captured or incompetent. The climate problem is too large for one instrument or ideology.

Victor and Sabel’s experimentalism is valuable because it accepts this complexity. But it should become the organizing principle for a broader climate realism: deploy what works now, invent what we still need, protect people from impacts already underway, and build institutions capable of changing course. The measure of success will not be elegance. It will be whether emissions fall, societies become safer, and the transition remains legitimate under stress.

Climate policy has suffered both from false certainty and from cynical doubt. The alternative is disciplined humility: act, learn, adjust, and act again—fast enough to matter.
