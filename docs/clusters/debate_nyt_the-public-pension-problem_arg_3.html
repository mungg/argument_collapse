Diversified Claude

"The defined-benefit public pension model is a fundamentally broken institution that must be replaced with defined-contribution or hybrid plans to ensure long-term fiscal stability."

The defined-benefit pension, as it has been practiced in American state and local government, is a fundamentally broken institution. It is broken because it asks elected officials with four-year horizons to fund obligations with forty-year horizons, and it is broken because it relies on actuarial assumptions that no honest financial economist endorses. Detroit and Illinois are not aberrations; they are the leading edge.

Consider the basic mechanics. A government promises a worker a pension. The cost of that promise is discounted to present value using an assumed rate of return on plan assets—usually 7.5% or 8%. This makes the obligation look small today. The politician who makes the promise gets the political benefit; the cost is deferred and disguised. Meanwhile, financial economists (Rauh, Novy-Marx, and many others) have argued that because the pension promise itself is essentially riskless—it's guaranteed, constitutionally in some states—it should be discounted at a riskless rate, perhaps 3–4%. Doing so roughly doubles or triples the reported liabilities.

This isn't an accounting curiosity. It is the difference between a system that is "mostly funded" and one that is catastrophically underwater. The honest measure is closer to $3–4 trillion in unfunded liabilities, not the $1 trillion typically reported.

So when commentators ask whether Wall Street caused this problem: no, not primarily. Wall Street collected fees, sold bad swaps, and did its usual rent extraction, but Wall Street did not invent the fiction that politicians could promise a guaranteed return on a portfolio of risky assets. That fiction is the heart of the public DB pension model.

The solution, painful as it is, is to migrate away from this model. Future hires in most state and local governments should be enrolled in either pure defined-contribution plans (like the federal Thrift Savings Plan) or hybrids with a small DB floor and a larger DC component. Utah did this in 2010. Rhode Island, under Gina Raimondo's reforms, moved partially in this direction in 2011. The result is not a loss of retirement security—DC plans, properly designed with low fees and lifecycle funds, produce perfectly adequate retirements—but a loss of the political incentive to underfund.

Existing retirees and accrued benefits are a harder question. Some impairment, as Detroit shows, may be unavoidable. But the larger lesson is forward-looking: stop digging the hole. The defined-benefit public pension was an experiment, and the experiment has failed.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current system creates a moral hazard by allowing elected officials to reap political benefits from pension promises while deferring the actual costs to future generations.
  2. 02Public pension funds rely on dishonest actuarial assumptions and inflated discount rates that hide the true scale of unfunded liabilities.
  3. 03The primary cause of the pension crisis is the structural flaw of the model itself rather than external factors like Wall Street investment performance.
  4. 04Transitioning to defined-contribution plans removes the political incentive to underfund retirement obligations while still providing adequate security for workers.
  5. 05The severity of the current funding gap makes the impairment of some existing benefits an unavoidable reality in the reform process.
Diversified DeepSeek

"The only sustainable solution to the public pension crisis is to replace defined-benefit plans with defined-contribution plans for all future public employees."

The only lasting answer to the public pension problem is to stop digging the hole deeper by replacing defined-benefit plans with defined-contribution plans for all future public workers. Every debate we have ignores the structural flaw: promises that extend decades into the future, based on investment guesswork and political convenience, will inevitably be broken. Michigan, among other places, has already shifted new hires into hybrid or 401(k)-style plans, protecting both workers and taxpayers from the vagaries of market swings and legislative whims. Workers get portable, owned accounts that cannot be raided by politicians; governments get predictable costs that don’t balloon with each market downturn. This week’s chaos only underscores the point. If Detroit had converted to a defined-contribution system decades ago, its retirees wouldn’t be facing cuts now, because their money would already be theirs, not a political IOU dependent on the city’s solvency. Those who call this a race to the bottom forget that the current defined-benefit system has already proven to be a trap for the very workers it purports to protect. Transitioning to a funded, individually owned model is pro-worker realism, not a Wall Street plot.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Defined-benefit plans are structurally flawed because they rely on long-term investment guesswork and political convenience that lead to broken promises.
  2. 02Defined-contribution plans protect workers by providing portable, individually owned accounts that cannot be raided by politicians or affected by a city's insolvency.
  3. 03Shifting to 401(k)-style plans benefits taxpayers and governments by creating predictable costs that do not balloon during market downturns.
  4. 04The current defined-benefit system acts as a trap for workers rather than a protection, as evidenced by the benefit cuts currently facing retirees in bankrupt cities.
Diversified Gemini

"The defined-benefit pension system is an obsolete model that must be replaced by defined-contribution plans to ensure the long-term fiscal solvency of state and local governments."

The debate over who is to blame for the public pension shortfall—Wall Street fat cats or pandering politicians—misses the deeper structural reality. The defined-benefit pension system itself is an obsolete mechanism that is entirely incompatible with the modern economic and demographic landscape.

When defined-benefit plans were popularized in the mid-20th century, the math worked. The workforce was growing, retirees lived for perhaps ten to fifteen years after leaving their jobs, and bond yields were high enough to provide safe, steady returns. Today, every one of those variables has inverted. People are living significantly longer, meaning a worker who retires at 55 might draw a pension for over 30 years. Concurrently, since the 2008 crisis, the Federal Reserve has kept interest rates near zero. In this low-yield environment, it is practically impossible to generate the 7 or 8 percent returns required to keep these funds solvent without taking on immense, irresponsible risk.

Consequently, pension funds did chase riskier investments on Wall Street, and politicians did underfund the systems. But both were reacting to a structural flaw: the defined-benefit model demands a predictability that the 21st-century global economy simply cannot provide.

The solution is not to litigate the past, but to structuralize the future. We must transition public employment away from defined-benefit pensions and toward defined-contribution plans, similar to the 401(k)s used by the vast majority of the private sector. This shift ensures that governments only promise what they can actually afford to contribute today, eliminating the creeping menace of unfunded liabilities.

For current retirees and those nearing retirement, governments will have to find a middle path, honoring the core of the benefits while perhaps capping automatic cost-of-living increases. But for all new public employees, the era of the guaranteed pension must end. It is the only mathematical way to ensure that our cities and states remain solvent and capable of providing basic services to their citizens.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Increased life expectancy means retirees are drawing benefits for much longer than the defined-benefit model originally intended.
  2. 02The current low-interest-rate environment makes it impossible to achieve the high investment returns necessary to keep pension funds solvent without taking on excessive risk.
  3. 03Transitioning to defined-contribution plans prevents the accumulation of unfunded liabilities by ensuring governments only promise what they can afford to contribute in the present.
  4. 04The structural flaws of the defined-benefit system, rather than just political mismanagement or market volatility, are the primary cause of the pension crisis.
  5. 05Maintaining the current pension system threatens the ability of cities and states to provide essential public services to their citizens.
