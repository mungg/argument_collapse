"The cosmopolitan progressive stance on migration, while intellectually sophisticated, is a condescending and politically disastrous position that alienates the working class and fuels the rise of the far right."

**The Cosmopolitanism of Frequent Flyers**

Lea Ypi's essay is, in many respects, a sophisticated and learned restatement of the cosmopolitan progressive position on migration. It comes with all the customary intellectual furniture: Lessing, the Enlightenment, the Washington Consensus, dependency theory, gestures toward Arrighi, denunciations of Meloni and Trump, and the obligatory hint at the Holocaust ("first they came for the Goldschmidts and the Levis"). It is fluently written and morally serious. And it is, I want to argue, exactly the kind of argument that has done more than any other to discredit the left among the working-class voters it claims to speak for, and to deliver Western politics into the hands of Trump, Meloni, Farage, Le Pen, and the AfD.

Begin with the strange use of the word "we." Ypi writes as though "we" — her cosmopolitan-progressive readership — face a binary choice between her position and "ethnocentrism and immigrant scapegoating." This is the rhetorical move that has poisoned this debate for thirty years. It treats every voter who has worries about the scale, pace, or character of immigration as morally indistinguishable from a fascist. Working-class voters notice this, and they have noticed it long enough that they no longer trust the people who do it. The collapse of social-democratic parties from Hamburg to Stoke is not, as Ypi suggests, the result of those parties having compromised too much with anti-migration sentiment. It is the result of those parties having, for a generation, refused to take that sentiment seriously and instead lectured their former voters about Lessing.

Consider the Calhoun line Ypi cites with apparent self-awareness: cosmopolitanism as "the class consciousness of frequent travelers." Ypi quotes this against the rich, but the line is just as cutting against her. The contemporary cosmopolitan-progressive position on migration is held overwhelmingly by people whose own jobs, neighborhoods, and children's schools are not in the front line of demographic change. It is held by people who can afford to live in the parts of London or New York or Berlin where high-skilled migration is a cultural amenity rather than a competition for housing. The British factory town that voted Leave in 2016 was not voting against Lessing; it was voting against thirty years of being told by people insulated from the consequences that its concerns were ignorant. The diagnosis was wrong then and it is wrong now.

Now to the substance. Ypi's argument has a sleight of hand at its core. She wants to deny that there is any meaningful distinction between "deserving" and "undeserving" migrants — the asylum seeker and the economic migrant, the political refugee and the chancer — on the grounds that such distinctions are tools of class power. But the distinction is not invented by capital; it is a moral distinction that ordinary people, including ordinary migrants, recognize and respect. The Albanian who fled Hoxha was a refugee. The Albanian who paid Ben the Lame in 1997 to take him to Italy because life there paid better — and let's be honest, that is a much more accurate description of most of Ben's clientele than Ypi allows — was an economic migrant. Both have claims; the claims are not identical; and pretending they are identical does not strengthen either claim, it weakens both.

Notice how Ypi handles Ben the Lame himself. He is described affectionately as a man helping his neighbors realize their dreams. This is the framing of the smuggler's family, not the framing of the people whose dinghies sink in the Adriatic. People-smuggling is one of the largest organized criminal industries on earth. It killed thousands of people in the Mediterranean last year, as it did the year before. The smugglers operating out of Libya hold migrants for ransom, sell women into prostitution, and dump people overboard when patrol boats appear. To present this trade as a kind of folk-heroic resistance to closed borders is to romanticize an atrocity. It is true that closed borders create the conditions for the smuggling industry. It is also true that opening borders to whoever wishes to come — which Ypi's argument requires, even if she avoids saying so — would generate consequences she does not discuss: massive labor-market displacement at the bottom end of rich-country economies, fiscal pressure on welfare states whose net beneficiaries Ypi insists on classifying as "vulnerable citizens" without explaining how their interests will be protected, and demographic transitions on a scale that no democratic society has ever sustained.

Ypi's response to these concerns is to deny that they are real, and to attribute them to "narrative," "hegemony," and "ideology." This is the move that empties her argument of empirical content. When polls show that 81 percent of Germans think immigration is too high, she does not consider that 81 percent of Germans might know something about their own country that she does not. She assumes they have been manipulated. This is the standard cosmopolitan-progressive move, and it is not respectful of democracy. It is condescending toward it.

There is a deeper philosophical issue here. Ypi treats the bounded democratic state as essentially a vehicle for class power, useful or harmful depending on whose hands it is in. But the bounded democratic state is also the only form of political organization in which ordinary people have ever exercised meaningful authority over their conditions of life. The labor movements Ypi celebrates were built within national frameworks. The welfare states she presupposes were built within national frameworks. The civil-rights victories she cites were won within national frameworks. The transnational labor solidarity she gestures toward — the Make Amazon Pay campaign — is, by any honest assessment, marginal, episodic, and incapable of substituting for the national institutions on which working-class power has historically depended.

What Ypi proposes, stripped of its rhetorical adornment, is that working-class voters in rich countries should accept large-scale migration, accept the resulting pressure on wages and services, accept the rapid transformation of their neighborhoods, and accept that any objection on their part will be classified as ethnocentrism — all on the promise that one day, through cross-border labor organizing, a global working class will materialize. This is not a serious political program. It is a confession of faith.

The honest version of Ypi's position would acknowledge several things. First, that her preferred policy will have material costs for some working-class natives, and that those costs are not adequately addressed by structural analysis. Second, that the rich countries' welfare states were built on assumptions of cultural and institutional continuity that her preferred policy strains. Third, that the political coalition required to defend her position does not exist in any rich country, and the trend lines are getting worse, not better. Fourth, that the actual existing alternative to her position is not Trumpian fascism but rather a defensible center-left politics of bounded democratic solidarity — the politics of, say, Denmark's Social Democrats — which she lumps in with the right and dismisses.

I do not write any of this in defense of Trumpian cruelty, the Platinum Card, the chained deportation flights, or any of the other genuine outrages Ypi rightly identifies. Those are wrong, full stop. But the answer to those outrages is not the dissolution of bounded democratic membership. It is the strengthening of it, for everyone inside, on terms the demos can recognize as fair. That is the politics that has historically delivered what little freedom, equality, and solidarity actually exist in the world. It is the politics Ypi cannot bring herself to defend, and so we are left with her elegant essay and the steady advance of the very forces it laments.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The essay's rhetorical framing unfairly treats legitimate working-class concerns about the scale and pace of migration as morally equivalent to fascism or ethnocentrism.
  2. 02The author’s perspective reflects the class consciousness of 'frequent travelers' who are insulated from the negative socioeconomic consequences of high-volume migration.
  3. 03The attempt to erase the moral and legal distinction between political refugees and economic migrants weakens the legitimacy of both claims in the eyes of the public.
  4. 04Romanticizing human smugglers as folk heroes ignores the reality that they lead a violent, criminal industry responsible for immense human suffering.
  5. 05Dismissing public anxiety over immigration as mere 'ideological manipulation' is a condescending rejection of democratic sentiment and lived experience.
  6. 06The bounded democratic state is the only proven framework for labor rights and welfare, and transnational solidarity is an insufficient substitute for national institutions.
  7. 07The proposed solution—sacrificing national stability for the hope of future global labor organizing—is an unrealistic 'confession of faith' rather than a viable political program.
