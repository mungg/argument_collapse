Vanilla Claude

"Lowering the voting age to include teenagers under 18 would fundamentally shift political campaigns and policy priorities toward long-term issues that directly impact the younger generation."

If teenagers under 18 could vote, the most immediate change would be in how candidates talk—and where they show up. We'd see fewer rallies in retirement-heavy Florida condo communities and more campaign stops at high schools, community colleges, and on platforms like YouTube and Tumblr, where younger Americans already gather. Both Obama and Romney have courted youth this cycle, but mostly through college outreach. Expanding the franchise downward would force a real conversation with people who don't yet have a vote, a paycheck, or a tax bill, but who live with the consequences of every long-term decision adults make.

The issues would shift accordingly. Student loan debt, now past $1 trillion, would move from a talking point to a central plank. The cost of college, the quality of public high schools, and the federal role in education (think No Child Left Behind and the Common Core rollout) would get sustained attention. Climate change—conspicuously absent from this year's debates until Hurricane Sandy—would be harder to ignore, since young voters consistently poll as more worried about it. Gun violence in schools, immigration policy affecting classmates under DACA, and LGBT equality would likely gain ground too.

There's a fair worry that teens would simply mirror their parents, but research on adolescent civic development suggests otherwise: voting habits formed early stick. The bigger risk isn't that young voters would distort politics—it's that candidates, faced with an electorate stretching down to 16, would finally have to plan past the next quarterly poll.
