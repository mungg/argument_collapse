Vanilla GPT

"Free societies must accept terrorism as a persistent risk to be managed through sober security measures rather than allowing fear to transform democratic nations into illiberal security states."

To “live with terrorism” sounds, in the raw aftermath of Nice, like an insult to the dead. No democratic government can accept the deliberate murder of civilians as a normal cost of public life. It must investigate, disrupt networks, protect vulnerable gatherings and punish those responsible. But if the phrase means that free societies must learn to endure a continuing risk without remaking themselves into security states, then Manuel Valls is stating an unpleasant truth.

Terrorism is designed to produce political effects disproportionate to its physical capacity. A truck, a rifle or a homemade bomb can turn ordinary civic spaces — a concert hall, a promenade, a train station — into scenes of horror. No police force can make every sidewalk, school, market or national celebration invulnerable. The promise of perfect safety is therefore not merely false; it is dangerous, because it invites governments to claim extraordinary powers that will still fail to eliminate the threat.

Europe has already lived through earlier eras of political violence: the I.R.A., ETA, the Red Brigades, far-right attacks, anarchist bombings before them. The United States has endured Oklahoma City, the Boston Marathon bombing and repeated mass shootings whose tactical resemblance to terrorism is obvious even when the ideology differs. Israel has long faced attacks in public places. None of these societies defeated violence by panic alone. They adapted: better intelligence, emergency medicine, public vigilance, targeted policing, and a refusal to let attackers dictate the terms of national life.

That last point matters most. If the answer to each atrocity is mass surveillance without oversight, indefinite emergency rule, religious profiling, closed borders to desperate refugees, or the abandonment of due process, terrorism succeeds in deforming the society it attacks. France’s state of emergency after the Paris attacks may have been understandable, but emergency powers are habit-forming. Raids, house arrests and bans on demonstrations must remain exceptional and accountable, not become the ordinary grammar of democracy.

Living with terrorism also means speaking honestly about scale. Terrorist attacks are terrifying because they are public, intentional and symbolic. But in most Western countries they remain a small source of mortality compared with traffic deaths, ordinary homicide, suicide or preventable disease. This does not diminish the victims; it helps prevent fear from becoming policy. A frightened public is more easily persuaded to trade liberty for measures that offer theatrical reassurance rather than real security.

The right approach is neither resignation nor militarized hysteria. Governments should improve intelligence-sharing, monitor known extremists, secure large public events intelligently, strengthen community trust, and address the prison radicalization and online recruitment that have fed jihadist violence. They should also protect the social inclusion of Muslim citizens, because treating millions as a suspect class is both unjust and strategically foolish.

So yes, we may have to live with terrorism in the sense that we live with other grave risks: soberly, actively and without illusion. We should not live for terrorism — organizing our politics, our laws and our imagination around the terrorists’ desire to make free people afraid of their own freedom.

Vanilla Claude

"Societies must accept a certain level of terrorist risk as a permanent reality to avoid the self-destructive erosion of civil liberties and the politics of perpetual emergency."

Manuel Valls was clumsy, but he wasn't wrong. The phrase "live with terrorism" grates on the ear because it sounds like resignation, and in the immediate shadow of the Promenade des Anglais, resignation feels obscene. But strip away the rhetoric and what Valls described is simply the truth: there is no policy, no surveillance regime, no military campaign that will reduce the risk of terrorism to zero, and pretending otherwise leads to worse outcomes than acknowledging it.

Consider what "not living with terrorism" would actually require. France has been under a state of emergency since November 2015. Police can conduct warrantless searches; assembly can be restricted; suspects can be placed under house arrest without judicial review. Valls had just announced, hours before the Nice attack, that the state of emergency would finally be allowed to lapse. Now it will be extended again. Each extension makes the exceptional ordinary. Each new attack ratchets the baseline of normal state power upward, because no politician wants to be the one who relaxed vigilance before the next truck or bomb. This is the asymmetry terrorism exploits: democracies impose permanent costs on themselves in response to episodic violence.

The mathematics of the threat reinforce the point. Even counting Paris, Brussels, and Nice, a European is vastly more likely to die in a car accident, by suicide, or from ordinary homicide than from jihadist violence. We "live with" all of those, organizing our lives around managing rather than eliminating them. The peculiarity of terrorism is that it is designed to be cognitively unbearable — to make us feel that ordinary statistical reasoning is callous. That feeling is precisely the weapon. To refuse to live with terrorism, in the sense critics of Valls seem to want, is to grant the attackers' premise that their acts are categorically different from other tragedies and warrant categorical responses.

This is not an argument for complacency. France has real intelligence failures to address; the perpetrator in Nice was apparently known to police for petty crime, and the broader pattern of radicalization within French banlieues demands serious policy attention, from prison deradicalization to integration of marginalized communities. "Living with" terrorism means doing the unglamorous work of policing, intelligence-sharing, and social policy without promising the public an impossible deliverance.

What it rules out is the politics of perpetual emergency — the Trumpian temptation, visible across the Atlantic, to promise that strong enough walls and harsh enough measures will banish the threat. They won't. The honest choice facing democracies is between absorbing a low but real level of violence while protecting the openness that makes them worth defending, or eroding that openness in pursuit of a security that cannot be achieved. Valls, awkwardly, named the choice correctly.

Vanilla DeepSeek

"Open societies must accept a certain level of terrorist threat as an unavoidable reality to prevent the erosion of the democratic freedoms and civil liberties that define them."

The immediate shock of the Nice atrocity—a truck deliberately plowing through families celebrating Bastille Day—naturally provokes a fierce refusal to accept such carnage as normal. Prime Minister Valls’s remark that France must “live with terrorism” sounded to many like a capitulation dressed in bureaucratic language. Yet behind the poor phrasing lies an uncomfortable truth that open societies have been grappling with for decades: total safety from terrorism is a fantasy, and the pursuit of it can inflict deeper wounds on our democracy than the attacks themselves. We can, and arguably must, learn to live with a certain level of terrorist threat if we are to preserve the freedoms that define us.

To “live with” terrorism is not to surrender to it, nor to abandon all protective measures. It is to acknowledge that in a free, mobile society, a determined individual with a vehicle or a kitchen knife can always find soft targets. After the 2015 Paris attacks, France declared a state of emergency that remains in place, expanding police powers of search and detention. Yet a man in Nice still drove a 19-ton truck past barriers and killed 84 people. Security services can foil complex plots—as they have done many times since Charlie Hebdo—but they cannot guarantee the routine safety we crave without turning public space into a network of checkpoints and surveillance that would be unrecognizable as a free society. The IRA’s bombing campaigns in Britain killed hundreds; Londoners adapted with a grim resolve, accepting that some risk was the price of not militarizing everyday life. That is the kind of “living with” that builds resilience rather than panic.

The greater danger posed by terrorism is not the body count, horrific as it is, but the provocation to overreact. Terrorists want to goad liberal democracies into betraying their own principles: mass internment, religious targeting, pervasive surveillance, a permanent culture of suspicion. The U.S. response to 9/11—torture, indefinite detention, the Iraq war—demonstrated how catastrophic such an overcorrection can be. Today, the rise of anti-Muslim rhetoric and the normalization of emergency powers across Europe risk doing exactly what ISIS and al-Qaeda want: alienating Muslim citizens and framing the struggle as a clash of civilizations. If we jettison due process and equal treatment under law, we hand the terrorists a strategic victory. Living with terrorism means refusing that trade-off. It means accepting that we cannot prevent every truck attack without dismantling the open society we believe in.

This is not a call for passivity. Sensible measures—better sharing of intelligence, closing loopholes in gun and explosive access, protecting large public gatherings with unobtrusive means—are essential. But the rhetoric of “never giving in” must be matched by a commitment not to give in to fear-driven policy. The day after Nice, people were back in cafes. That is the right instinct: to grieve, to be vigilant, but to carry on. That is precisely what “living with” terrorism looks like—not complacency, but the quiet defiance of ordinary life, which is the ultimate rejection of the terrorist project. We can live with it, because the alternative is to let terrorism dictate the terms of our existence.

Vanilla MiniMax

"Societies must adopt a strategy of resilient acceptance toward terrorism to avoid the destructive overreactions and civil liberty erosions that follow from attempting to eliminate the threat entirely."

Manuel Valls was right to emphasize resilience, but his framing obscured a harder truth: societies already do live with terrorism, and the real question is how consciously we choose to do so.

The word "live with" suggests resignation, but what the prime minister apparently meant was something closer to strategic acceptance. No democracy can eliminate the threat of asymmetric violence entirely, just as we cannot eliminate traffic deaths or natural disasters. The post-9/11 overreach—sacrificing civil liberties, launching catastrophic wars, normalizing mass surveillance—proved that panic produces worse outcomes than the original threat. The intelligence failures that preceded Paris and Nice were not failures of willingness to intrude on privacy but of coordination, sharing, and attention. Overreaction hollowed out the very values terrorists seek to undermine.

Yet "living with" terrorism cannot mean complacency or withdrawal. The phrase carries danger when leaders use it to deflect from hard choices about resources, integration policy, and counter-extremism programs. France's security services were stretched thin; the national conversation about social alienation in marginalized communities remained insufficient. Acceptance of risk does not mean acceptance of avoidable risk.

The deeper problem is that "living with" is often a rhetorical escape hatch. It lets governments avoid the scrutiny that should accompany expanded powers. If we truly accept terrorism as a permanent feature of modern life, we should apply the same rigorous cost-benefit analysis we apply to other persistent risks—aviation safety, public health, highway policy—rather than making exceptions for security theater that trades liberty for the appearance of action.

Living with terrorism means neither surrendering to fear nor pretending threats don't exist. It means building security that is targeted, accountable, and proportionate—precisely the standard that panicked responses almost never meet.

Vanilla Gemini

"Societies must learn to live with terrorism as a manageable risk because the pursuit of absolute security is both impossible and destructive to the democratic values we seek to protect."

French Prime Minister Manuel Valls’ assertion that "France is going to have to live with terrorism" was swiftly condemned as defeatist by his political opponents. But this criticism misinterprets realism for resignation. To "live with" terrorism is not to surrender to it; rather, it is to acknowledge an uncomfortable truth about the modern asymmetric threat landscape, and to refuse to let the pursuit of absolute security destroy the very liberties we claim to protect.

The horror of the Bastille Day attack in Nice, alongside recent tragedies in Paris, Brussels, and Orlando, demonstrates a terrifying shift in terror tactics. We are no longer dealing solely with complex, multi-layered plots that intelligence agencies can readily intercept through traditional surveillance. The weaponization of a rental truck requires minimal planning, no sophisticated network, and practically no funding. Eradicating this type of decentralized, low-tech threat entirely is impossible without transforming open democracies into totalitarian police states. If the standard for victory is zero attacks, democratic societies are setting themselves up for perpetual failure.

When politicians promise to completely eliminate the terror threat, they inevitably invite policies that undermine our fundamental democratic values. France has been under a continuous state of emergency since the November 2015 Paris attacks, yet even these sweeping, rights-restricting powers could not stop a lone extremist with a truck. Demanding absolute safety invariably leads to mass surveillance, border closures, and the stigmatization of entire religious communities. Such overreactions play directly into the hands of groups like the Islamic State, whose explicit strategy is to eliminate the "grayzone" of coexistence and provoke Western governments into alienating their Muslim citizens. By tearing up our own civil liberties in a futile quest for zero risk, we hand terrorists the power to rewrite our laws and fracture our social cohesion.

"Living with" terrorism must instead be understood through the lens of civic resilience. We live with the threat of violent crime, natural disasters, and industrial accidents. We take reasonable, evidence-based precautions—bolstering community policing, securing public infrastructure, and disrupting terrorist financial networks—but we do not bring daily life to a halt. To live with it means treating it as a risk to be managed, not an existential panic to be endlessly sustained.

Accepting terrorism as a chronic condition rather than an existential threat deprives terrorists of their primary weapon: our own terror. Valls was right. We must mourn the victims, relentlessly pursue the perpetrators, and remain vigilant. But true defiance lies in continuing our daily lives, preserving our open societies, and accepting that while we cannot prevent every tragedy, we fully control our response.

Diversified GPT

"Democracies should adopt a resilient, public-health-oriented approach to terrorism that focuses on intelligent prevention and social cohesion rather than overreaction or fatalistic acceptance."

The debate over whether we can “live with terrorism” often assumes that toughness and freedom are opposites. They need not be. The real question is what kind of social immune system a democracy builds after repeated attacks. A brittle society overreacts, turns on minorities and grants the state powers it later regrets. A resilient society absorbs shocks, learns and adapts.

France’s predicament is severe. Since January 2015, it has endured attacks on journalists, Jews, concertgoers, café patrons, police officers and now families gathered for Bastille Day fireworks. It is natural that citizens want dramatic action. But terrorism is designed to produce precisely that demand. The spectacle of violence is meant to convince the public that normal politics has failed and that only collective anger remains.

A better model is public health. We do not “accept” disease because we admit that disease will exist. We collect data, identify risk factors, intervene early, treat outbreaks and communicate honestly with the public. Terrorism likewise requires layered prevention: competent intelligence, local policing that communities trust, mental-health and family-reporting channels where appropriate, prison de-radicalization programs that are evaluated rather than merely announced, and rapid emergency medical response. Some of this is unglamorous. Much of it is more useful than symbolic crackdowns.

The language of war is sometimes unavoidable, especially when dealing with the Islamic State’s territory and command structures abroad. But many recent attackers in Europe are not conventional soldiers. Some are petty criminals, unstable men, self-radicalized loners or loose contacts of extremist networks. If we imagine every attacker as a commando sent from Raqqa, we may miss the local pathways that turn grievance, masculinity, criminality and ideology into violence.

Living with terrorism also means living with one another. France’s Muslims cannot be treated as a suspect class without deepening the divide extremists seek to exploit. At the same time, community leaders cannot be asked merely to denounce attacks after the fact; they should be full partners in prevention, education and civic life. The state must defend secular republican principles while also recognizing that exclusion and humiliation are combustible materials.

The hardest discipline after Nice is to speak in two registers at once: morally, the attack is intolerable; practically, some risk will remain. Democracies routinely manage that tension. They can improve security without promising miracles. They can grieve without surrendering judgment. They can refuse both complacency and panic.

So yes, France and other democracies must live with terrorism in the sense that they must continue living. But they should do so intelligently, not fatalistically.

Diversified Claude

"Societies must treat terrorism as a chronic background risk to be managed through rational, proportionate cost-benefit analysis rather than as an existential threat that justifies unlimited sacrifices of liberty and resources."

The framing of this debate — "live with" versus "refuse to live with" — obscures what is actually at stake, which is the question of proportion. Every society lives with risk. The relevant question is never whether to accept a given risk but how much we are willing to pay, in money and in liberty, to reduce it, and by how much.

By that standard, the post-9/11 Western response to terrorism has been wildly disproportionate. The United States has spent, by various estimates, somewhere between three and six trillion dollars on the wars in Iraq and Afghanistan, plus the standing apparatus of the TSA, DHS, expanded NSA surveillance, and so on. The civil liberties costs — warrantless surveillance, indefinite detention, the normalization of drone strikes against citizens — have been substantial. And the actual annual death toll from jihadist terrorism in the West, even in a bad year like 2015 or 2016, remains in the low hundreds across a population of nearly a billion.

This is not to dismiss the suffering in Nice or Paris or Brussels or San Bernardino. It is to ask whether the current allocation of resources and rights actually saves lives at a reasonable cost per life saved, compared to alternatives. Would another billion euros spent on road safety save more French lives than another billion spent on counterterrorism? Almost certainly yes. Would lifting some of the more theatrical security measures at airports and stadiums measurably increase risk? Almost certainly not.

Valls's formulation is awkward because politicians cannot speak in these utilitarian terms without sounding callous. But the underlying logic — that terrorism is a chronic background risk to be managed at sustainable cost, not an existential threat justifying any sacrifice — is correct. The truly dangerous posture is the one that treats every attack as a civilizational emergency, because that posture leads inexorably to overreach: to wars that create more terrorists than they kill, to surveillance regimes that corrode democratic legitimacy, to communal suspicion that drives the next generation of recruits into the arms of the recruiters.

"Living with" terrorism, properly understood, is not resignation. It is the mature recognition that a free society must price risk rationally, even — especially — when the risk involves blood on the pavement and grieving families on the evening news. The harder discipline is not to be brave but to be calm.

Manuel Valls was not being complacent. He was being honest in a way that politicians rarely manage, and the backlash against him reveals more about our own immaturity as citizens than about any failure on his part.

Diversified GPT

"Societies must accept terrorism as a persistent risk while refusing to sacrifice democratic principles and civil liberties in a futile pursuit of absolute security."

The honest answer is that we already live with terrorism. The question is whether we will live with it as citizens or as frightened subjects. Terrorism has existed in many democratic societies: anarchist bombings, anti-colonial violence, the Red Brigades, the Baader-Meinhof Group, the I.R.A., ETA, white supremacist attacks, jihadist attacks. The methods and ideologies differ, but the democratic dilemma is familiar. How much power should we hand to the state in exchange for a promise of safety it cannot fully keep?

After Nice, that question is agonizing. Eighty-four dead, including children, is not an abstraction. It is understandable that people want barriers, soldiers, raids, deportations, surveillance and harsher laws. Some of these may be warranted. Large events require better planning. Intelligence services need resources. People already known to authorities should not fall between agencies. No serious defender of liberty should sneer at practical security.

But the record since September 11 should make us cautious. The United States built enormous surveillance and detention systems, invaded Iraq on false premises, normalized secret watch lists and spent vast sums. Some plots were stopped; some policies were illegal, ineffective or counterproductive. France, too, has been under a state of emergency since the November 2015 attacks, yet Nice still happened. This is not an argument against policing. It is an argument against believing that exceptional powers are magic.

A free society is not one without risk. It is one in which the government must justify coercion, courts remain open, minorities are not collectively punished and public life continues. Terrorism attacks all of these habits. It tells majorities to fear their neighbors and tells governments to treat constitutional restraints as luxuries. When we comply, the terrorists gain more than the body count they inflicted.

There is also a moral hierarchy to maintain. We should not compare every inconvenience to tyranny, but neither should we dismiss civil liberties as procedural niceties. Freedom of movement, privacy, due process and equal treatment are not ornaments on the state; they are among the reasons the state is worth defending. If Muslim citizens feel they are permanently under suspicion, the republic becomes less liberal and less secure.

So yes, we must live with terrorism—but not meekly, and not forever resigned. We live with it by investigating, disrupting and prosecuting terrorists. We live with it by refusing to let fear govern law. We live with it by remembering that security agencies are tools of democracy, not replacements for it. The measure of France after Nice will not be whether it can eliminate danger overnight. It will be whether it can defend life without losing liberty.

"Societies must endure the reality of terrorism without sacrificing democratic freedoms or overreacting in ways that undermine the rule of law."

Democracies have lived with terrorism before. Britain lived for decades with the I.R.A. Spain lived with ETA. Israel has lived with recurring attacks for much of its history. None of these examples is comforting, and none offers a simple model. But they show that terrorism can be endured without allowing it to define every institution or every citizen’s identity.

The first lesson is patience. Terrorist campaigns often end not with one decisive raid but through years of policing, intelligence work, political adjustment, public exhaustion and internal failure among the militants themselves. Governments that promise quick victory usually disappoint. Governments that keep law, politics and policing working over time have a better chance.

The second lesson is that overreaction is not a side issue; it can become part of the conflict. Britain made grave mistakes in Northern Ireland, including internment without trial and abusive security practices. Those policies did not simply violate rights; they damaged legitimacy. Spain succeeded against ETA not by suspending democracy indefinitely, but by combining police pressure with political isolation of violence and the strengthening of democratic norms. The details differ, but the broad point travels: lawfulness is not softness.

The third lesson is that public rituals matter. Terrorism aims to make normal life feel impossible. When people stop gathering, traveling, arguing, voting, celebrating and criticizing their leaders, the damage spreads beyond the attack. After Nice, the point is not to pretend nothing happened. The point is to mourn publicly and then insist that public life belongs to citizens, not murderers.

There should be visible security where it helps. Crowds can be protected by barriers against vehicles. Intelligence agencies can be better funded and better coordinated. Known violent extremists should be watched closely. But a society that responds to every attack by expanding suspicion to entire religious or ethnic communities will not become safer in the long run. It will become more brittle.

So can we “live with terrorism”? We must, if the alternative is the fantasy that a free country can make itself invulnerable. But the word “just” should be rejected. We do not just live with it; we resist it, investigate it, punish it, reduce it, and care for those it wounds. We also refuse to let it become an argument for abandoning the freedoms that make democratic life worth defending.

The harder courage is not only running toward danger, though police and emergency workers do that. It is also constitutional courage: the willingness to remain a free society while frightened.
