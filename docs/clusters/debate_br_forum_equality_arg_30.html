"While Sypnowich’s focus on solidarity offers a valuable alternative to the deficiencies of liberal egalitarianism, the concept requires significant development to address its potential for exclusion and its tension with individual liberty."

Sypnowich's invocation of solidarity as a central criterion of an egalitarian society is the most original and least developed thread in her argument. It deserves deeper analysis, both because it offers genuine resources for an alternative to liberal egalitarianism and because it reveals the limitations of the flourishing approach as she develops it.

The solidarity argument runs as follows: equality is not merely a matter of institutional design but of the quality of social relationships; a just society is one where members care about each other's well-being and are prepared to act accordingly; this caring is not a complement to equality but constitutive of it. Sypnowich draws on Marx's principle—"from each according to ability, to each according to need"—to articulate this vision: the egalitarian community is one where flourishing is shared, where the well-being of each is bound up with the well-being of all.

This is an attractive vision, and it correctly identifies a deficiency in liberal egalitarianism. Rawlsian justice, with its emphasis on principles to which free persons might agree, has difficulty explaining why justice requires not just institutional fairness but active concern for others. The difference principle permits inequalities so long as the least advantaged benefit, but it does not require the advantaged to care about the worse off as fellow members of a shared community. Similarly, luck egalitarianism holds individuals responsible for their choices, which introduces a moralized judgment about desert that sits uneasily with community.

Sypnowich's solidarity is meant to fill this gap. The egalitarian community cares about the flourishing of all its members, not because of institutional requirements or principles of justice but because of the bonds of fellowship that bind them together. This is genuinely valuable, and it points toward a vision of equality that transcends the instrumental rationality of liberal theory.

But the solidarity argument also has a darker side that Sypnowich does not adequately explore. Solidarity can be exclusive as well as inclusive—it can bind insiders together while excluding outsiders. The history of socialist and communitarian movements is replete with examples of solidarity that demanded conformity within the community while reinforcing boundaries against those outside it. The Nazi concept of Volksgemeinschaft, for instance, was explicitly solidaristic—a community bound together by shared fate and mutual care—but it was also profoundly hierarchical and exclusivist. Even when solidarity does not take such pathological forms, it can prioritize the well-being of the community's members at the expense of outsiders, particularly the global poor.

Sypnowich addresses global inequality only in passing, but it is central to any adequate account of solidarity. The global poor—those living on less than a few dollars per day—face deprivation that is arguably more severe than anything within affluent societies. Do members of an egalitarian community have obligations of solidarity to these distant others? Liberal theory has difficulty answering this question, and the flourishing approach as developed here does not address it directly. Solidarity is usually understood locally—toward one's compatriots, co-religionists, or fellow community members. But if equality is genuinely universal, if all persons have equal claim to flourishing, then solidarity cannot stop at national borders.

This suggests that the solidarity argument requires development in a more cosmopolitan direction. Global solidarity would require not only that members of a community care for each other but that they extend this care to all human beings, regardless of nationality or location. This is a demanding conclusion, but it follows from the premises of the flourishing approach: if flourishing is the metric and all persons have equal claim to it, then the barriers of national sovereignty cannot legitimately block the flow of resources and assistance that solidarity requires.

Sypnowich might resist this extension on grounds that solidarity must be rooted in shared institutions and history to be meaningful. But this response would concede too much to particularism. The flourishing approach seeks to articulate universal standards of justice that apply to all human beings; it cannot quietly limit those standards to those who share our national citizenship or cultural heritage. Global solidarity is not merely an optional extension of domestic solidarity; it is a test of whether the solidarity argument can bear the weight that Sypnowich places on it.

A second limitation of the solidarity argument concerns its relationship to freedom. Sypnowich argues that solidarity requires the flourishing of all, not merely the equality of formal opportunity. But it is not clear how solidarity relates to the freedom of individuals to pursue their own conceptions of the good. If solidarity demands that everyone flourish, it might also demand that individuals be guided toward flourishing—prevented from making choices that lead to floundering. This is the paternalism problem that I raised earlier, but it appears here in a different form. Solidarity does not merely require institutions that enable flourishing; it requires that individuals actually flourish, which seems to require interference with their liberty.

Perhaps solidarity is compatible with freedom if it is understood not as a demand that individuals flourish but as a disposition of mutual care that provides the background against which individual flourishing occurs. On this interpretation, solidarity does not justify paternalism but rather explains why we care about equality in the first place: not because of abstract principles but because we care about others and want them to thrive. This is plausible, but it raises the question of what to do when individuals do not want to thrive in ways that solidarity would require. Must they accept guidance, or can they opt out of solidarity's demands?

These are not rhetorical objections but genuine puzzles that any solidarity-based approach must address. Sypnowich has opened an important line of inquiry; it remains to be fully developed.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Sypnowich correctly identifies that liberal egalitarianism fails to account for the active concern and social bonds necessary for a truly just community.
  2. 02The concept of solidarity is historically prone to being exclusive and parochial, necessitating a more cosmopolitan framework to address global inequality.
  3. 03A solidarity-based approach must resolve the 'paternalism problem' by clarifying whether the demand for universal flourishing justifies interfering with an individual's freedom to make poor choices.
  4. 04The argument for solidarity needs to distinguish between a disposition of mutual care and a coercive requirement for individuals to achieve specific flourishing outcomes.
