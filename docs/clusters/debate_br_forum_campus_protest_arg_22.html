"The modern university should be preserved as an institution dedicated to the disinterested pursuit of truth and objective inquiry, rather than being treated as either a therapeutic safe space for students or a site for radical political sabotage."

Robin D. G. Kelley’s essay captures a defining conflict of our time: the clash between student activists demanding a utopian, "safe" university, and radical academics urging the use of the university as a staging ground for revolution. From my perspective, however, both Kelley and the students he critiques are fundamentally mistaken about the nature and purpose of higher education. The modern university is neither a therapeutic "family" meant to shield students from trauma, nor is it a bourgeois fortress waiting to be sabotaged by "guerrilla intellectuals." It is, at its core, an institution dedicated to the disinterested pursuit of truth, a mission that is actively undermined by the intense politicization both sides of this debate demand.

Let us first address the student activists. Kelley correctly identifies the therapeutic turn in modern activism, noting how the language of oppression has been replaced by the vocabulary of psychology: "trauma," "PTSD," "triggers," and "microaggressions." He is right to be skeptical of this shift. When subjective emotional pain becomes the primary metric of institutional failure, the university is forced into the role of a hyper-vigilant parent rather than an intellectual crucible. The demands for "safe spaces," trigger warnings, and speech codes represent a profound anti-intellectualism. They rely on the assumption that students are fragile entities who must be protected from ideas, historical facts, or literature that might cause them discomfort.

However, Kelley’s objection to the students is not that they are too radical, but that they are not radical enough. He chides them for wanting to be included in an institution that he views as a "cog in the neoliberal order." His alternative, drawn from Stefano Harney and Fred Moten, is the "undercommons"—a subversive network of academics and students who view the university not as a place of enlightenment, but as an enemy entity from which one should simply "steal what one can."

This vision of the guerrilla intellectual is not just romantic posturing; it is profoundly destructive to the academic enterprise. A university cannot function if its faculty and graduate students view themselves as insurgents dedicated to the "sabotage" of the institution. The academic freedom that allows scholars like Kelley to teach, write, and advocate for radical causes is guaranteed precisely because the university, historically, has defended a zone of objective inquiry free from the immediate demands of political utility. When activists—whether they are students demanding mandatory cultural-competency training, or Marxists demanding the university model economic justice—insist that the academy must serve a specific political agenda, they erode the very foundation that allows free thought to flourish.

Kelley scoffs at the idea that the university possesses a unique teleology as "an enlightened space free of bias and prejudice." He writes off the entire structure as inherently racist, classed, and militarized. This totalizing cynicism leaves no room for the genuine triumphs of the liberal university. The scientific method, the rigorous evaluation of historical archives, the open debate of competing philosophies—these are not mere "regimes of professionalization" or tools of "counterinsurgency." They are the greatest mechanisms human beings have ever devised for discovering truth and lifting societies out of ignorance.

To say that the university should not be a political engine is not to say that education has no social consequence. Education transforms society by producing educated, critical individuals who then go out into the world to vote, organize, and build. But when the university itself is commandeered to be the direct agent of social transformation, it loses its ability to educate impartially. A curriculum mandated by student protesters to reflect specific social justice goals ceases to be an education and becomes indoctrination. Similarly, an "undercommons" that views traditional academic rigor as a bourgeois trap produces only activists, not scholars.

Kelley reminisces about his own college days, noting that he and his peers read widely and deeply, never refusing a text simply because it validated racism or free-market ideology. They engaged in a "ruthless criticism" of everything. This is highly commendable, but it is precisely this rigorous, wide-ranging intellectual engagement that is threatened by today’s climate. The students Kelley critiques are often the ones demanding that "problematic" texts be removed from syllabi.

If we want to preserve the university, we must rescue it from those who view it merely as a site of political contestation. We must reject the therapeutic demands of students who want to be protected from the world, and we must equally reject the radical demands of theorists who want to burn the institution down from the inside. The university’s true value lies in its commitment to scholarship, reason, and the free exchange of ideas. That is a heritage worth defending.
