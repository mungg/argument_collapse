**Ronald Is Right, and the Stakes Are Higher Than She Says**

I read Pamela Ronald's essay with the relief one feels when, after a long argument with one's relatives at Thanksgiving, somebody finally says the obvious thing clearly. Genetic engineering, as deployed in modern agriculture, is not a hazard to human health. There is no serious scientific dispute about this. The relevant question — the only relevant question, when we are talking about a hungry world — is whether the technology can be put to use solving the problems that actually face poor farmers in the global South, and whether the political reaction against it will prevent that from happening.

Let me press Ronald's argument further than she presses it herself. The opposition to GE crops is not just misguided. In its impact on the global poor, it is morally serious in a way that deserves to be named. Golden rice was developed in the late 1990s and donated, royalty-free, to public breeding programs in Asia. Vitamin A deficiency, according to WHO estimates, kills hundreds of thousands of children each year and blinds hundreds of thousands more. The technical case for golden rice as a complement to supplementation programs was made over a decade ago. It is still not in the field. Why? Because regulatory regimes shaped by European and American activist pressure have set the bar for approval impossibly high, because field trials have been physically destroyed by activists in the Philippines, and because anti-GMO NGOs have campaigned relentlessly against a product that would cost them nothing to allow and could save many lives.

I do not say this lightly. Reasonable people disagree about agricultural policy all the time, and a healthy debate about the role of corporations, IP, and biodiversity is appropriate. But somewhere along the way, opposition to GE crops shifted from "we should be careful" to "this technology should be prevented from existing," and that shift has consequences. Ronald is too polite to use the word, but I will: the people who blocked golden rice for ten years bear some moral responsibility for the children who went blind during those ten years. So do the European policymakers whose moratoria gave political cover to African governments who feared that adopting Bt cotton or virus-resistant cassava would jeopardize their export markets.

This is what is missing from too much of the GMO debate: the recognition that *not* deploying a technology is also a choice, with its own consequences, and that those consequences fall disproportionately on people who do not get a vote in Berkeley or Brussels. When Mama Moses loses her banana crop, the cost of regulatory caution is paid in her household, not in the affluent neighborhoods where anti-GMO sentiment flourishes.

Ronald rightly points out that anti-GMO sentiment correlates with educational attainment and income in a way that should embarrass its proponents. Marin County. Whole Foods shoppers. The professional class of the coastal United States. This is not a movement of the wretched of the earth. The wretched of the earth, when polled, tend to want better seeds. The disconnect between the people who would benefit from the technology and the people who organize against it is one of the more striking features of contemporary food politics, and it deserves more scrutiny than it gets.

A few smaller points. On labeling: I oppose mandatory GMO labels not because I want to hide information, but because they are designed to function as warning labels, and the warning is unwarranted. We do not label foods produced by mutagenesis, by wide-cross hybridization, by polyploidy induction, or by any of the other genetic interventions that fill the produce aisle. Singling out transgenesis for labeling is bad science communication dressed up as transparency.

On corporate concentration: real, and worth addressing through antitrust and public-research investment. But the answer is more public-sector biotech, not less biotech. The Gates Foundation and CGIAR have demonstrated that public-good GE crops are possible. Starving the public sector of resources while denouncing the private sector for monopolizing the field is incoherent.

On the precautionary principle: applied symmetrically, it counsels caution about *not* adopting technologies whose benefits are well-established. The status quo is not a safe default. Climate change, soil degradation, water scarcity, and a growing population will make the next several decades the most challenging period in the history of agriculture. We are going to need every tool, applied wisely. Ronald's essay is a clear-eyed account of one of those tools. The next step is for the rest of us to stop letting people who have never been hungry decide which tools the hungry are allowed to use.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Activists and regulatory regimes bear moral responsibility for the preventable deaths and blindness caused by the decade-long delay of Golden Rice.
  2. 02The cost of regulatory caution is paid by subsistence farmers like Mama Moses rather than the affluent Westerners who drive anti-GMO sentiment.
  3. 03Anti-GMO movements represent a disconnect between the professional class of the global North and the 'wretched of the earth' who actually desire better seeds.
  4. 04Mandatory GMO labeling is a form of bad science communication designed to function as an unwarranted warning rather than genuine transparency.
  5. 05The solution to corporate concentration in agriculture is to increase public-sector biotechnology research rather than abandoning the technology altogether.
  6. 06A symmetrical application of the precautionary principle requires considering the grave risks of not adopting GE technologies in the face of climate change and population growth.
