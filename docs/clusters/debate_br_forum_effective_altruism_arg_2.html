Diversified Gemini

"Effective altruism is a fundamentally flawed and conservative philosophy because it prioritizes quantifiable, short-term philanthropic metrics over addressing the systemic economic and political structures that cause global poverty."

Peter Singer’s opening anecdote about Matt Wage is meant to be an inspiring tale of moral awakening, but it reads more like a damning indictment of the movement he is championing. Wage, a bright philosophy student, decides that the most ethical path available to him is to take a lucrative job at a Wall Street arbitrage trading firm so that he can donate a massive portion of his income to effective charities. Singer presents this “earning to give” strategy as the pinnacle of rational altruism. In reality, it reveals the profound structural blindness at the heart of effective altruism. By focusing exclusively on the final destination of philanthropic dollars, Singer and his disciples conveniently ignore the mechanisms by which those dollars are accumulated in the first place.

Wall Street does not generate wealth in a vacuum. High-frequency trading, arbitrage, and the broader financialization of the global economy have devastating real-world consequences. They contribute to the volatility of global food prices, the stripping of assets from productive industries, and the exacerbation of the very global inequality that effective altruists are trying to mitigate. To argue that a person can live a "fully ethical life" by participating in a system of extraction, provided they donate the spoils to the victims of that same economic order, is deeply paradoxical. It essentially functions as an indulgence system for millennial elites: you can participate in the ruthless machinery of global capitalism, provided you pay your tithe to the Against Malaria Foundation.

Effective altruism treats poverty as an unfortunate natural disaster rather than the predictable output of a specific geopolitical and economic order. Singer asks how we can do the “most good,” and answers with a spreadsheet of cost-effective interventions: bed nets, deworming pills, direct cash transfers. These are undeniably helpful to the individuals who receive them, but they are band-aids on a gaping wound. Why are these populations lacking basic medical care? Why are their local economies unable to support public health infrastructure? The answers lie in histories of colonialism, crushing sovereign debt, unfair global trade agreements, and the extraction of resources by multinational corporations.

By steering the brightest minds of a generation away from public service, political organizing, or systemic advocacy, and toward Wall Street and Silicon Valley, effective altruism actively neutralizes the threat these individuals might have posed to the status quo. If Matt Wage had decided to dedicate his life to reforming the global financial system, he might have impacted the lives of millions by altering the structural conditions that produce poverty. But such systemic change is messy. It takes decades, it involves political compromise, and crucially, its outcomes cannot be easily measured in a randomized controlled trial (RCT).

This is the second fatal flaw of the movement: its measurability bias. Effective altruism privileges interventions that are easily quantified. A bed net distribution provides a clean, satisfying metric of "lives saved per dollar." Political lobbying to change international trade laws does not. But the history of human progress shows that the most dramatic improvements in living standards have come from messy political struggles—the labor movement, the civil rights movement, the push for universal healthcare—not from optimized philanthropy.

Singer is right that we should not be content with a “warm glow” when we give. We should care about effectiveness. But effectiveness cannot be reduced to what is immediately measurable. A truly effective altruism would recognize that poverty is a political problem, not just a logistical one. It would ask its adherents not just to redistribute the spoils of a fundamentally unjust system, but to dismantle the systems of extraction that make such extreme philanthropy necessary in the first place. Until effective altruism reckons with the origins of the wealth it seeks to distribute, it will remain a conservative philosophy dressed up as a radical one, comforting the comfortable while managing the symptoms of the afflicted.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'earning to give' strategy ignores the fact that wealth is often accumulated through extractive financial systems that exacerbate the very inequality altruists seek to solve.
  2. 02Effective altruism treats poverty as a logistical accident rather than a predictable outcome of historical and geopolitical factors like colonialism and unfair trade.
  3. 03The movement's 'measurability bias' unfairly privileges easily quantified interventions over complex political advocacy and systemic reform which historically drive greater human progress.
  4. 04By encouraging elite individuals to work within the status quo, effective altruism neutralizes potential challenges to the global economic order.
  5. 05A truly effective altruism would focus on dismantling unjust systems of extraction rather than merely managing the symptoms of global suffering through optimized donations.
Diversified Gemini

"While effective altruism claims to be a radical ethical movement, it is actually a conservative and individualistic framework that fails to challenge the systemic political and economic structures that cause global poverty."

In his essay, Peter Singer introduces effective altruism as a transformative philosophical movement that empowers individuals to "do the most good" through reason and evidence. While the core premise is logically sound, Singer's framework reveals a troubling philosophical contradiction at the heart of the movement. Effective altruism claims to be a radical ethical awakening, yet it relies entirely on a deeply conservative, individualistic theory of change. It asks us to optimize our personal consumer choices—specifically, our career paths and our charitable donations—while leaving the broader political and economic machinery of the world completely unchallenged.

Consider Singer's primary example: Matt Wage. Wage determines that he can do the most good not by engaging directly with the poor, nor by challenging the systemic causes of poverty, but by becoming an arbitrage trader on Wall Street. This "earning to give" model is the ultimate expression of neoliberal morality. It suggests that the market economy is an unalterable force of nature, and our only moral agency lies in how we spend the capital we manage to extract from it.

This logic absolves the individual of any complicity in the mechanisms of wealth generation. Arbitrage trading does not create societal value; it exploits market inefficiencies. The financial sector has been a primary driver of the staggering wealth inequality that defines the modern era. To argue that participating in this system is morally righteous as long as you donate a portion of your earnings is a spectacular exercise in moral compartmentalization. It is akin to a doctor working to spread a disease so that they can afford to donate more medicine to the victims.

Singer acknowledges the problem of causing harm to achieve good, stating that effective altruists recognize that breaking moral rules against harming innocent people has bad consequences. But his definition of harm is remarkably narrow. He warns against "speculative reasoning" that justifies atrocities, referencing Stalin and Mao. Yet he fails to recognize the systemic, diffuse harms perpetuated by the very industries his acolytes are entering. The extraction of natural resources, the exploitation of labor, and the destabilization of local economies by global finance are not "speculative" harms; they are the daily realities of global capitalism.

Furthermore, by framing ethics as a matter of consumer choice (which charity to buy, which career to select), effective altruism depoliticizes the fight against poverty. It shifts the burden of global welfare from the state and international regulatory bodies onto the shoulders of individual philanthropists. This is exactly the kind of privatization of public goods that conservative political movements have championed for decades. If the world’s problems can be solved by the benevolent charity of Wall Street traders, there is no need to demand fairer trade policies, international labor standards, or global wealth taxes.

Effective altruism is intellectually seductive because it offers a clean, mathematically solvable approach to a messy world. But true ethical living cannot be reduced to an optimization problem. If we truly want to eliminate extreme poverty, we must have the courage to examine not just where our money goes, but how the world is structured to produce such vast disparities in the first place. Doing the most good requires more than just opening our wallets; it requires fundamentally changing the systems that make our charity necessary.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'earning to give' model relies on a neoliberal morality that treats the market economy as unalterable and limits moral agency to personal consumer choices.
  2. 02Effective altruism allows individuals to ignore their complicity in the systemic harms caused by the industries, such as global finance, where they earn their wealth.
  3. 03Singer’s definition of harm is too narrow, focusing on direct atrocities while ignoring the diffuse, systemic harms of global capitalism like labor exploitation and resource extraction.
  4. 04By framing ethics as a matter of private philanthropy, the movement depoliticizes poverty and shifts the responsibility for global welfare away from states and international regulatory bodies.
  5. 05True ethical living requires addressing the structural causes of inequality rather than simply treating morality as a mathematically solvable optimization problem for the wealthy.
