The Fourth Evil Is Not an Appendix

Brandon Terry deserves credit for what most King scholarship still avoids: acknowledging, in plain language, that "any retrieval of King's legacy has to amend his triple evils to include a fourth: sexism." That sentence appears, in Terry's essay, almost as a parenthesis, in a paragraph that also gestures at King's "essentialist views on gender and normative views on the family" and their "severe logical and moral failures." Terry, who has co-authored important work on this with Shatema Threadcraft, certainly knows the scale of what he is conceding. But the form of the concession—as a brief admission embedded in a long defense—reveals a deeper problem in the essay's structure. The fourth evil is not an appendix. If you take it seriously, it reshapes the other three. And once it does, the picture of King that emerges is less the "profound interlocutor" Terry wants us to engage and more an intellectual whose categories require thorough reconstruction before they can do much work for us today.

Begin with the analysis of racism. Terry praises King's three-part model: irrational fear, sociopolitical structure, cognitive and empathetic failure. Each component is illuminating. But each was developed by a thinker who, in practice and in print, treated black women as the supporting cast of black liberation. The "irrational fears" King catalogues are fears coded in the public imagination as white male fears—loss of standing, contamination, retaliation—and the political object of those fears is also coded male: the black man who might vote, work, marry across the color line, retaliate. King's analysis is not wrong about this, but it is partial. The fears that drive the policing of black women—fears of black maternal "deviance," of welfare "queens," of black women's sexuality as a threat to white reproductive norms—operate by a different logic, and they have been at least as politically consequential in shaping American racial policy from Moynihan to the welfare reform debates Terry knows well. A theory of racism that does not metabolize gender at its analytic core will keep missing them.

The sociopolitical component fares no better. King's account of "congenital deformity" and the "democratic spirit" that opposes it is a story of public political contest: voting, legislation, civil disobedience, public discourse. It is a story whose actors are the kinds of people who appear in public—preachers, lawyers, soldiers, students, marchers. It is not equipped to see the sites where racial domination was, and is, reproduced through the everyday governance of households, bodies, and reproduction: the convict-leasing of black women, the sterilization campaigns of the mid-century, the domestic-worker exclusions from the New Deal labor regime, the welfare surveillance that turned black motherhood into a managed status. These are not addenda. They are the infrastructure of American racial capitalism. To leave them out of one's foundational analysis is to define racism in a way that systematically privileges the experiences of black men who could imagine themselves as protagonists of a public political drama.

The third component, the cognitive one, is in some ways the most damaging to leave ungendered. King's powerful argument that mass disciplined protest "dissolved the stereotype of the grinning, submissive Uncle Tom" and forced America to see black people as "active organs of change" is true—and it is true about a stereotype of black masculinity. The stereotypes that fixed black women were different: the Mammy, the Jezebel, the matriarch. None of them was dissolved by the march on Washington. Some have been intensified by the very dynamics of black male public heroism that the civil rights movement generated. When King's wife was made to play, even in his own household, the role of the supportive helpmeet to the great man, that was not a private failure. It was an expression of an ideological structure that the movement reproduced precisely as it was confronting its racial counterpart.

Once you see this, the theory of civil disobedience that Terry praises also looks different. Terry quotes King's celebration of nonviolent direct action as having a "magnificent universal quality" that transcends "gender, age, physical disability, education, and wealth." This is true in principle and was demonstrably false in practice. Ella Baker had to fight King and the SCLC leadership to be heard. Septima Clark's citizenship schools, which built the actual organizational base of the movement in much of the South, were undervalued in the SCLC budget while King's appearances were not. Dorothy Cotton's role in the Citizenship Education Program is barely remembered. The "magnificent universality" of nonviolent protest was, in the King-led wing of the movement, organized through profoundly hierarchical structures of charismatic male authority, often grounded in the gender norms of the black Baptist church. Terry mentions this. He does not let it touch the theory.

The ethics fares similarly. Terry's most lyrical pages defend King's emphasis on love, magnanimity, humility, and the disciplined channeling of anger. These virtues are not gendered in their content. But they have been gendered in their distribution. The demand that the oppressed not slip into "corrosive" anger, that they refuse to "humiliate the opponent," that they maintain ethical composure under conditions of degradation—this demand has been pressed hardest, in American public life, against black women. The "angry black woman" is not a stereotype invented yesterday. It has been deployed against figures from Ida B. Wells to Shirley Chisholm to the founders of the Movement for Black Lives. To recover, in 2018, an ethics that warns above all against the slippage of anger into hatred is to recover a posture that, however philosophically defensible in the abstract, is bound to be applied unequally in practice. Terry knows this; he wants the demand to "cut in two directions" and not to license complacency. But the cut never quite seems to fall the same way for both sexes.

I do not want to argue, against Terry, that King should be abandoned. The Movement for Black Lives' founders—three black women, two of them queer—have not abandoned him, even when they have chosen Audre Lorde or Assata Shakur as more proximate ancestors. They have done something more interesting: they have built an organizing practice that is decentralized, anti-charismatic, explicitly feminist, and explicitly indebted to King's tactical vocabulary. That is what taking King seriously while taking the fourth evil seriously actually looks like. It is not the addition of a sentence about sexism to a fundamentally untouched theoretical edifice. It is the willingness to rebuild the edifice when the additional weight reveals which beams were never load-bearing in the first place.

Terry ends his essay by saying the spirit of King is most alive when we "embrace the challenges" of new ideas, new sacrifices, new movements. I agree. The challenge his own essay underweights is the one his own footnote acknowledges: that the most generative inheritors of King are working in a register he himself did not learn to speak. To honor him is to keep going where he could not.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01King’s analysis of the 'irrational fears' driving racism is partial because it focuses on white male anxieties while ignoring the distinct gendered logic used to police black women’s bodies and reproduction.
  2. 02The sociopolitical component of King’s thought overlooks the domestic and reproductive sites where racial domination is maintained, privileging a public political drama centered on male actors.
  3. 03King’s cognitive theory of racism successfully challenged stereotypes of black masculinity, like 'Uncle Tom,' but left intact—and sometimes reinforced—harmful stereotypes of black womanhood.
  4. 04The 'magnificent universality' of King’s theory of civil disobedience was contradicted by the movement's actual practice, which relied on hierarchical, charismatic male authority that marginalized women leaders like Ella Baker.
  5. 05King’s ethical emphasis on channeling anger into love is historically applied unequally, often serving to silence black women who are uniquely stigmatized by the 'angry black woman' trope.
  6. 06Truly honoring King’s legacy requires following the example of modern movements that have rebuilt his tactical vocabulary into a decentralized, anti-charismatic, and explicitly feminist organizing practice.
