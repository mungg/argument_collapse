"While Brandon Terry successfully rescues Martin Luther King Jr. from sanitized national myths, his analysis downplays King’s late-career radicalism by prioritizing philosophical and ethical reflections over King's systemic, anti-capitalist critique of racial capitalism."

Brandon M. Terry’s “MLK Now” is a vital and timely intervention, rescuing Martin Luther King, Jr. from the anesthetizing grip of national mythmaking. Terry correctly identifies the tragedy of King’s contemporary canonization: by transforming a radical strategist into a harmless symbol of moral consensus, we have stripped his legacy of its utility for present-day struggles. However, while Terry does excellent work recovering King’s complex theories of racism, civil disobedience, and ethical character, his account subtly downplays the very engine of King’s late radicalism: his evolving, systemic critique of racial capitalism. By centering King’s philosophical reflections on "agape" and character, Terry risks replacing the sanitized liberal King with a philosophical moralist King, leaving the anti-capitalist revolutionary still waiting in the wings.

Terry notes that King viewed racism, militarism, and poverty as the “triple evils,” and he highlights King’s prescient warnings about automation, his calls for a guaranteed basic income, and the necessity of attacking unjust economic inequalities. Yet, in Terry’s framework, these material concerns are frequently subordinated to discussions of "cognitive and empathetic failures" and the ethical requirements of love and nonviolence. If we are to truly wield King’s ideas in 2018—an era defined by staggering wealth inequality, a predatory gig economy, and the resurgent power of monopoly capital—we must foreground King’s materialist analysis. The King of 1968 was not merely seeking to correct the empathetic failures of his opponents; he was seeking a radical redistribution of economic and political power.

In his final years, King increasingly recognized that moral suasion and the exposure of racist hypocrisies were insufficient to dismantle the structural foundations of inequality. The victories of the Civil Rights Act and Voting Rights Act cost the nation little in financial terms. As King observed, it is one thing to integrate a lunch counter; it is quite another to guarantee a living wage to the person serving the food. The shift from civil rights to human rights was, for King, a shift from constitutional legalism to a democratic socialist critique of the American economy. He explicitly identified the profit motive as the root cause of the exploitation of the poor, both black and white.

Terry suggests that King worried about black nationalism because it gave priority to race at a time when automation made the economic question fundamental for all workers. This is true, but it only tells half the story. King also recognized that the capitalist system fundamentally relies on racism to fracture working-class solidarity. The "irrational fears" that Terry rightly identifies in King's theory of racism are not merely psychological anomalies; they are cultivated and weaponized by economic elites to prevent the cross-racial alliance of the poor that King sought to build with the Poor People's Campaign.

Furthermore, Terry’s emphasis on civil disobedience as a mechanism to prompt “moral learning” and transcend "enmity" softens the deeply antagonistic nature of class struggle that King was beginning to embrace. When King went to Memphis in 1968 to stand with striking sanitation workers, he was not just engaging in a politics of character or attempting to win the friendship of the city administration. He was supporting a labor strike—a direct withdrawal of labor power designed to grind the city to a halt until material demands were met. He called for massive boycotts of Memphis businesses, leveraging economic pain to force political concessions. This is the "coercive" dimension Terry mentions, but it is specifically economic coercion, a tactic that requires recognizing irreconcilable material interests between the ownership class and the working poor.

In 2018, as we witness teachers striking in West Virginia and Oklahoma, and warehouse workers organizing against the brutal efficiency of Amazon, King’s economic radicalism is his most urgent legacy. The technocratic solutions to poverty that Terry rightly critiques—such as the Silicon Valley iteration of Universal Basic Income, which often serves as a Trojan horse for dismantling the social safety net—must be countered by King’s vision of a radical restructuring of the economy centered on human dignity and labor power.

If we are to retrieve King’s legacy, we cannot simply amend his "triple evils" to include sexism, as Terry suggests. We must elevate his critique of capitalism to the forefront. The persistent failures of reciprocity that King identified are not just ethical lapses; they are the required operating conditions of a capitalist system that bases distribution on market power rather than human need. To read King today without centering his call for a "radical redistribution of economic and political power" is to read him with one eye closed. We need the King who recognized that a society that prioritizes property rights over human rights is fundamentally bankrupt, and we need his militant, anti-capitalist organizing strategies more than his theological reflections on the nature of love.
