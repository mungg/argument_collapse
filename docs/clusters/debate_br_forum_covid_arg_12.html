"While the lead essay correctly identifies institutional and procedural failures during the pandemic, its central empirical claim that non-pharmaceutical interventions (NPIs) were ineffective is based on flawed state-level data analysis that ignores complex confounding variables."

What the State-Level Data Can and Cannot Tell Us

Macedo and Lee have done public discourse a service by insisting that the COVID-19 response deserves a proper reckoning. On the broad point—that we have not adequately interrogated our pandemic decisions, that institutions have been too quick to move on, that polarization stunted what should have been an ordinary process of expert dissent and revision—they are correct, and I am sympathetic to most of their normative claims. My objection is narrower but important: their central empirical argument relies on a methodology that cannot bear the weight they place on it.

Their Figure 2 is the load-bearing piece of evidence. It shows that, grouped by 2020 presidential vote, U.S. states had broadly similar cumulative per capita COVID-19 mortality through April 2021 despite very different policy stringency. From this they conclude that "more restrictive policies" did not "succeed in reducing death and serious illness." They reinforce the claim by citing a 2023 Lancet study that finds restrictions associated with lower infections but not lower mortality, and a Scottish review concluding insufficient evidence for most NPIs.

There are at least four problems with treating this evidence as decisive.

First, ecological inference. Comparing state-level stringency to state-level outcomes is a classic ecological design, and it is well known that ecological associations can misrepresent individual-level relationships. A state's stringency score is an aggregate of many distinct policies—school closures, business restrictions, gathering limits, travel rules, mask mandates—that may have very different effects. The Oxford Stringency Index sums these into a single number for tractability, but that number is not the "dose" of any particular intervention. Two states with identical scores can have radically different policy bundles. Two states with very different scores can have similar de facto behavioral environments, because formal policy and actual behavior diverge in complex ways. Macedo and Lee acknowledge none of this.

Second, confounding by initial conditions. The states that locked down hardest in spring 2020 were disproportionately the states hit first and hardest: New York, New Jersey, Massachusetts, Connecticut, California. These were also states with higher urban density, more international travel exposure, older populations in some cases, and substantial nursing-home infrastructure. Comparing their cumulative mortality to that of Wyoming or Mississippi without adjusting for these radically different starting conditions—the authors say they control for percent over 65, obesity, urbanization, and insurance, which is a partial set—does not tell us what we want to know. The lockdowns and the mortality were both responses to the same underlying epidemic intensity. Disentangling cause requires more than per capita comparisons.

Third, behavioral substitution. Lockdowns happened in a context where people were already changing behavior. Google mobility data from spring 2020 shows that mobility in Florida, Georgia, and other less restrictive states fell nearly as much as in heavily restricted states in March and early April, before reopening began. The marginal effect of a formal stay-at-home order on top of voluntary behavior change is much smaller than the effect of all the behavior change combined. State-level policy comparisons therefore systematically underestimate what NPIs as a category accomplished, because the comparison set is "more aggressive NPIs vs. less aggressive NPIs," not "NPIs vs. no behavioral change at all."

Fourth, temporal heterogeneity. Macedo and Lee themselves note that mortality diverged after the vaccine rollout, with Republican states experiencing 30 percent higher mortality by January 2023. This is important. It suggests that when the policy variable was vaccination rather than NPIs, large mortality differences did appear. The authors interpret this as supporting vaccines while indicting NPIs. But it is equally plausible that NPI variation in 2020 mattered less than vaccine variation in 2021–22 because (a) the NPI variation across states was smaller than it appeared once voluntary behavior is netted out, (b) the NPIs were implemented imperfectly and inconsistently, or (c) the dominant determinants of 2020 mortality were features like age structure and nursing-home protocols that NPI policy did not directly target. None of these would license the conclusion that NPIs "did not work" as a category.

The Lancet study they cite is more nuanced than their summary suggests. Stricter restrictions were associated with lower infection rates—a finding the authors mention but quickly elide. Lower infection rates, all else equal, should translate into lower mortality, particularly among the vulnerable. The reason the study did not find a clear mortality association is likely the same set of identification problems I have just described, not that NPIs fail to reduce mortality. Absence of evidence in observational designs is not evidence of absence.

What about the pre-pandemic planning documents? Here I think Macedo and Lee are on stronger ground in identifying a tension—pre-COVID consensus did not endorse society-wide lockdowns—but on weaker ground in treating that consensus as the gold standard against which 2020 policy should be judged. Those documents were written for influenza, not for a novel coronavirus with substantial asymptomatic transmission, R0 around 2.5–3, and apparent CFR (in February 2020) in the low single digits. Many of the planning assumptions were specific to flu and did not translate. The IFR of COVID-19 in the unvaccinated, particularly among the elderly, turned out to be in the range of 0.5–1 percent overall and dramatically higher in older cohorts—numbers that would have justified more aggressive intervention than influenza planning anticipated.

This does not vindicate every policy decision. Long school closures in many U.S. jurisdictions were clearly excessive in retrospect. By fall 2020, the evidence that K–8 schools were not major drivers of community transmission was substantial, and schools elsewhere in the OECD reopened. The U.S. failure here was not principally a failure of pandemic science; it was a failure of governance, in which teachers' unions had veto power and federal CDC guidance was excessively cautious for too long. Macedo and Lee are right to highlight this, and right to note its disproportionate burden on disadvantaged children.

Similarly, the suppression of debate about lab leak origins, the inconsistent messaging on masks in spring 2020, and the dismissive treatment of the Great Barrington signatories were genuine failures—failures of process and norms more than of specific policy choices. A scientific community confident in its position does not need to censor dissenters. The aggressive moralization of dissent that the authors describe was both intellectually unworthy and practically counterproductive. On these points the lead essay is largely correct.

But the leap from "process failures occurred" and "specific policies (long school closures) were wrong" to "NPIs as a class did not save lives" is where the essay overreaches. The first two claims are well-supported. The third is asserted on the basis of evidence that cannot establish it. And the difference matters, because what we tell ourselves about NPI effectiveness will shape the next pandemic response. If the conclusion is "NPIs don't work, don't bother," we will face the next novel pathogen having disarmed ourselves of tools that, properly targeted and time-limited, may indeed save lives. If the conclusion is "specific NPIs were misapplied, sustained too long, and imposed inequitable costs that were not adequately weighed," we will be better prepared.

A serious postmortem—the kind Macedo and Lee correctly call for—should foreground methodological humility. It should distinguish between policies (where evidence is mixed and intervention-specific), processes (where failures are clear), and norms (where suppression of debate damaged the scientific enterprise). It should resist the temptation, equally present on left and right, to extract a single clean lesson from a complex event. The pandemic was not a controlled experiment, and we will not get clean answers by pretending it was.

Macedo and Lee have asked the right question. The answer requires more care than they have given it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Comparing aggregate state stringency scores to mortality rates is a form of ecological fallacy that fails to account for the specific effects of distinct policies or the divergence between formal rules and actual behavior.
  2. 02The lead essay fails to adequately control for initial conditions, such as urban density and international travel exposure, which meant the hardest-hit states were also the ones most likely to implement strict lockdowns.
  3. 03State-level policy comparisons underestimate the impact of NPIs because they do not account for the significant voluntary behavior changes that occurred regardless of formal government mandates.
  4. 04Pre-pandemic planning documents cited by the lead essay were designed for influenza and were not necessarily applicable to a novel coronavirus with different transmission dynamics and higher fatality rates.
  5. 05The lead essay's dismissal of NPIs as a category is an overreach that risks disarming public health officials of potentially life-saving tools for future pandemics.
  6. 06A proper pandemic postmortem must distinguish between the failure of specific long-term policies, like school closures, and the general effectiveness of NPIs as a class of intervention.
