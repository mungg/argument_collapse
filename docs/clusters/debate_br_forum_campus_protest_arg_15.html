"While the university is structurally incapable of self-transformation, student activists should prioritize organized political struggle and strategic power-building over the 'undercommons' model of fugitive study and individual survival."

Kelley's essay represents the best traditions of black intellectual engagement with the university: rigorous, self-critical, historically grounded, and committed to liberation. I write as someone who has spent two decades inside academia, who has benefited from its resources and been wounded by its exclusions, and who finds in Kelley's argument both validation and challenge.

The central critique is undeniable: the modern university is structurally incapable of delivering on its promises. Built on land stolen from indigenous peoples, funded by endowments accumulated through slavery and exploitation, organized around hierarchies of race, gender, and class, the university reproduces the social order it claims to transcend. No amount of diversity training, no renaming of buildings, no multicultural curriculum can transform this fundamental character. Kelley is right to insist that activists understand this.

But I want to push back on what I see as an overreliance on the undercommons as political model. Harney and Moten offer a brilliant critique of university ideology, but their prescription—"steal what one can"—is ultimately a strategy for individual survival, not collective transformation. The undercommons provides refuge for the already radicalized, but it does not explain how to build movements capable of challenging power on a larger scale.

The history Kelley recounts supports a different model: the freedom school as political formation, connected to mass movement and oriented toward winning concrete changes. The Mississippi Freedom Schools were not just sites of alternative pedagogy; they were part of a campaign to destroy Jim Crow. The Institute of the Black World was not just an intellectual space; it was intended to mobilize black scholars in service of liberation movements globally. Even the group that produced Lessons from the Damned was organizing in Mt. Vernon, connecting study to the lived struggles of welfare mothers and unemployed workers.

What distinguishes these models from the undercommons is their connection to organizing. Political education that is not connected to organizing becomes a form of consumption, another way of acquiring cultural capital. We see this in the popularity of radical theory among graduate students who read Harney and Moten but never leave the library. The danger is that critique becomes an end in itself, a way of feeling radical without doing the harder work of building coalitions, confronting power, and risking failure.

Kelley writes that "winning is not always the point" and that "unveiling the university's exploitative practices can be profound acts of demystification." But demystification without organization is ultimately impotent. The question is not whether we can see through the university's ideology—we can—but whether we can build something that challenges it. This requires political education that takes seriously the question of power: who has it, how is it exercised, and how can it be contested.

The most radical student demands at Mizzou, UNC, and elsewhere did not come from fugitive study groups but from coalitions that connected campus to community, that made demands that could not be easily dismissed or accommodated, that used the university's own vulnerabilities—its concern for reputation, its dependence on football revenue, its fear of bad publicity—as leverage. This is not reformism; it is strategy.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'undercommons' strategy of 'stealing' from the university serves as a refuge for the radicalized but lacks a mechanism for large-scale collective transformation.
  2. 02Political education risks becoming a form of intellectual consumption or cultural capital if it is not explicitly tethered to community organizing and mass movements.
  3. 03Historical models like the Mississippi Freedom Schools demonstrate that radical pedagogy is most effective when it is part of a concrete campaign to dismantle oppressive systems.
  4. 04Demystifying university exploitation is insufficient unless it is paired with an organizational strategy that identifies and leverages the institution's specific vulnerabilities.
  5. 05Effective student activism requires moving beyond critique to engage in the difficult work of building coalitions and contesting power in the pursuit of winnable, structural changes.
