"Public pension crises are primarily caused by the failure of governments to fund their obligations rather than investment losses, and retirees should not be forced to accept retroactive benefit cuts to solve a problem created by political delinquency."

The public pension debate often begins at the wrong moment. It starts when the fund is short and asks what retirees should surrender. It should begin decades earlier, when governments decided not to pay the bills they had incurred.

A public pension is not a gratuity. It is part of compensation. When a teacher, police officer or clerk works for 30 years under a statute and contract promising a pension, that promise is a wage paid later. If the state fails to set aside the money, the worker has not become greedy; the state has become delinquent.

Illinois is the clearest example. Its pension crisis is not primarily the result of retirees living in luxury or one disastrous investment. The state repeatedly underfunded its systems while using the money for other public purposes, tax relief or simple budget avoidance. Now the legislature proposes to reduce benefits and calls the result reform. To many workers, it looks more like the state solving its own breach of faith by rewriting the contract after performance has been completed.

Yes, pension funds took losses in the financial crisis. Yes, some funds paid too much to Wall Street managers. Those abuses should be investigated and corrected. But investment losses do not explain why some states with similar market exposure are in far better condition. The crucial difference is whether elected officials made required contributions year after year.

The Detroit bankruptcy ruling is alarming because it suggests that state constitutional protections can be overridden in federal bankruptcy. But even if that is legally correct under Chapter 9, it should not become a political model. Bankruptcy is supposed to be an emergency process for genuinely insolvent municipalities, not a bargaining strategy for states that prefer cutting pensions to raising revenue.

The solution is to treat pension debt as real debt. States should adopt enforceable funding schedules, with courts empowered to compel contributions. They should dedicate reliable revenue sources to pension systems rather than depending on annual political will. They should stop granting tax breaks or making budget promises while pension contributions are unpaid. In Illinois, preserving adequate income tax revenue is a more honest option than cutting the inflation protection of elderly retirees.

There can also be prudent benefit reforms for future employees. Retirement ages, employee contributions and benefit formulas can be adjusted prospectively, so workers know the terms before they accept the job. What should not be done is retroactively confiscating benefits already earned.

Wall Street may have taken a cut from the system, and that deserves attention. But the deeper looting was political: legislatures borrowed from workers’ deferred wages without calling it borrowing. Retirees should not be forced to finance that deception in old age.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Pensions are deferred wages earned through service, and failing to fund them constitutes a breach of contract by the state.
  2. 02The primary cause of pension shortfalls is the political practice of diverting required contributions to other public purposes or tax relief.
  3. 03Investment losses and management fees do not explain the crisis, as states that consistently made required contributions remain in better financial condition despite market exposure.
  4. 04Bankruptcy and legal rulings should not be used as a political strategy to override constitutional protections for pensions in favor of avoiding revenue increases.
  5. 05The solution to pension debt is to implement enforceable funding schedules and dedicated revenue sources that treat pension obligations as mandatory debt.
  6. 06While benefit reforms can be applied prospectively to future employees, it is unjust to retroactively confiscate benefits already earned by current retirees.
