Diversified Gemini

"The proposal to integrate local police into a national counterterrorism intelligence network is dangerous because it co-opts the trust-based model of community policing to facilitate the systemic surveillance and coercion of Arab, Muslim, and South Asian American communities."

Daniel Richman’s “The Right Fight” attempts to harmonize the aggressive imperatives of the War on Terror with the progressive ideals of community policing. He argues that relying on local police for domestic intelligence will organically protect civil liberties because police must maintain good relations with the communities they serve. For Arab, Muslim, and South Asian Americans living in the shadow of 9/11, this argument is not just naive; it is profoundly dangerous. It dresses up systemic surveillance in the friendly uniform of the local beat cop, co-opting the language of civic engagement to build a Panopticon that targets our communities.

Richman cites instances of police outreach—chiefs meeting with Arab-American leaders, sheriffs guarding mosques—as evidence that local police can mediate between heavy-handed federal agents and vulnerable minorities. But what Richman interprets as healthy "mediation" is often experienced on the ground as coercion. When the FBI demands to interview thousands of young Middle Eastern men, and the local police chief offers to have his officers present to "smooth things over," the fundamental dynamic does not change. The community is still being targeted based on ethnicity and religion; the presence of a familiar local officer merely makes the racial profiling go down easier.

The deployment of "community policing" as a counterterrorism tool fundamentally corrupts the relationship between minority communities and local government. True community policing is supposed to be about empowering residents to set public safety priorities—reducing break-ins, fixing streetlights, clearing out drug houses. It is a reciprocal relationship based on improving the quality of life.

When local police are deputized into the national intelligence network, this relationship is inverted. The police no longer view the Arab-American neighborhood as a community to be protected; they view it as a threat matrix to be mapped. Every town hall meeting, every multicultural forum, every casual conversation between a beat cop and a local shop owner becomes an intelligence-gathering opportunity. Richman explicitly endorses this, stating, "Every felony arrest is an intelligence opportunity and should be recognized as such."

For our communities, this means living under constant suspicion. Community leaders are put in an impossible position: they are expected to act as informants on their own youth to prove their "loyalty" to the local police chief. If they refuse to participate in these one-sided "partnerships," they risk losing police protection for their mosques and businesses, or worse, being flagged as uncooperative and referred to federal authorities.

Richman argues that "electoral accountability" will prevent local police from alienating us. This ignores the reality of urban politics. Arab and Muslim Americans constitute a tiny fraction of the electorate in most cities. If a mayor or police chief decides that aggressive surveillance of our neighborhoods will play well with the frightened, broader majority, our votes will not save us. We do not have the political capital to "exact a toll" from the federal government, as Richman suggests local agencies will do on our behalf.

The idea that local police are the natural defenders of minority rights is contradicted by the daily reality of racial profiling that existed long before 9/11 and has only intensified since. We do not need local police to act as a buffer between us and the federal government. We need clear, enforceable laws that prohibit all levels of law enforcement from targeting individuals based on their religion or national origin. Richman’s reliance on the structural friction between government agencies to protect our rights is cold comfort. We refuse to be the raw material for a new domestic intelligence network, no matter how politely the local police ask for our data.
