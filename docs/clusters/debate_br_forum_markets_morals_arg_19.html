Diversified Gemini

"Sandel’s critique of market expansion is flawed because it prioritizes abstract moral purity and symbolic integrity over the practical necessity of using markets to solve large-scale human suffering."

Michael J. Sandel is perhaps our most gifted popular philosopher, and his critique of the creeping expansion of market values is characteristically eloquent. Yet, in his eagerness to demonstrate how markets "crowd out" moral and civic virtues, Sandel mischaracterizes the economic way of thinking and, more troublingly, turns a blind eye to the profound human costs of restricting markets in the name of moral purity.

The crux of Sandel’s argument relies on a dismissal of Kenneth Arrow and Lawrence Summers, who warned against relying too heavily on altruism. Sandel mocks the idea that altruism is a "scarce resource" like fossil fuel, preferring the metaphor of a muscle that grows stronger with use. It is a beautiful sentiment, but it fundamentally misunderstands the economists’ point. Arrow and Summers do not literally believe that a person possesses a fixed volumetric tank of benevolence. Rather, they recognize that human attention, emotional energy, and the capacity for self-sacrifice are bound by cognitive and evolutionary limits.

We are biologically wired to care deeply for our kin, our friends, and our immediate communities. Our "muscle" for altruism works exceptionally well in these small spheres. But when it comes to coordinating the production and distribution of food, medicine, and shelter for hundreds of millions of strangers, the muscle of altruism is insufficient. The genius of the market is not that it destroys virtue, but that it does not require it. By harnessing self-interest, the market ensures that strangers cooperate to meet each other’s needs. If we demanded that every transaction be fueled by civic virtue and mutual love, the global economy would collapse, and the most vulnerable would suffer immediately. Conserving our moral energy for where it is truly needed—our families, our communities, and the marginalized—is not a cynical retreat from virtue; it is a practical necessity.

Nowhere is the danger of Sandel’s "corruption" argument more glaring than in his brief mention of human kidneys. Sandel casually notes that we must weigh the moral arguments for and against organ sales, treating it as an abstract philosophical puzzle akin to buying a wedding toast. But this is not an abstract puzzle. Today, in 2012, more than 90,000 Americans are waiting for a kidney. Thousands will die this year because the supply of altruistically donated organs falls tragically short of the demand.

Our current system operates exactly as Sandel wishes: it relies entirely on the non-market norm of altruistic giving. And it is failing. Economists and medical professionals who advocate for a regulated market in kidneys do not do so because they wish to "commodify" the human body for its own sake. They do so because they believe that saving thousands of lives is a higher moral imperative than preserving an abstract sense of bodily sanctity.

Sandel worries that introducing money into this sphere would degrade the "meaning" of the good. But what is more degrading to human dignity: a regulated system in which a willing donor receives compensation for providing a life-saving organ, or a system that watches thousands of people perish on dialysis because we are too morally squeamish to allow a transaction? If saving lives requires us to tolerate a degree of "commodification," we should accept that trade-off without hesitation.

Sandel’s focus on the aesthetic discomfort of market transactions—his hand-wringing over gift cards, ghostwritten wedding toasts, and the creeping commercialization of holiday shopping—reveals a framework optimized for the anxieties of the affluent. It is a luxury to fret over whether giving cash instead of a sweater corrupts the spirit of Christmas. It is a luxury to debate whether legacy admissions at Ivy League universities violate the institutional integrity of higher education.

When Sandel does touch upon matters of life and death—such as the peasant selling a kidney or the sweatshop worker—he explicitly sets aside the "fairness" objection (which deals with poverty, coercion, and inequality) to focus on the "corruption" objection. But to the starving peasant, the problem with the market is not that it fails to honor the telos of the human body. The problem is starvation. By prioritizing the "corruption" of goods over the material welfare of human beings, Sandel elevates symbolic purity above actual human suffering.

Markets are not perfect, and they require robust regulation to ensure safety and prevent coercion. But the appropriate response to the limits of markets is to address the underlying inequalities that make them coercive, not to banish them from spheres where they could do tremendous good. Treating virtue as an infinitely renewable resource is a comforting illusion, but public policy must be built on the reality of human nature. When we prioritize the sanctity of the non-market sphere over the tangible benefits that markets can deliver, we are making a moral choice—and it is often a deadly one.
