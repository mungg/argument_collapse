"While Buddy Roemer correctly identifies the corrupting influence of crony capitalism and political favoritism, the solution lies in limiting the government's power to grant favors rather than primarily focusing on campaign finance reform."

Buddy Roemer’s essay should make conservatives uncomfortable in the best sense. He reminds us that free enterprise and crony capitalism are not the same thing. A market economy depends on profit and loss, competition, prudence, and failure. What Washington has too often produced is something different: private gain backed by public rescue, complexity written for insiders, and regulation shaped by the regulated. No conservative should defend that.

Roemer is especially convincing when he describes the tax code. A simple, broad-based, low-rate tax system would be far less hospitable to political manipulation than the current tangle. Every deduction, credit, phaseout, temporary extension, and industry-specific rule creates a constituency. Every constituency hires lobbyists. Every lobbyist understands that members of Congress need campaign funds. The result is a system in which lawmakers can posture as guardians of the public while quietly auctioning complexity.

His example of the research-and-experimentation credit is telling. If a policy is good, make it permanent or incorporate it into a broader pro-growth tax structure. If it is bad, end it. But Washington prefers temporary uncertainty because temporary uncertainty keeps supplicants coming back. Businesses then spend resources not on inventing, hiring, or competing, but on protecting themselves from Congress. That is not capitalism. It is tribute.

Roemer is also right that the financial crisis exposed an intolerable alliance between big government and big finance. The phrase “too big to fail” should offend anyone who believes in markets. If a firm can keep its gains in good times while shifting catastrophic losses to taxpayers in bad times, it is not operating in a free market. It is operating with an implicit government guarantee. Such guarantees encourage leverage, recklessness, and gigantism. They also punish community banks, local businesses, and prudent borrowers who do not have a direct line to the Treasury or the Federal Reserve.

The bailouts may have been necessary in the panic of 2008 to prevent a broader collapse. Reasonable people can disagree about what policymakers should have done in those frightening weeks. But emergency action must not become a permanent business model. The largest banks should face capital requirements that reflect the risks they impose. Their creditors should not be allowed to assume rescue. Resolution authority must be credible enough that failure is possible without bringing down the economy. If no regulator can unwind an institution, the institution is too large or too complex.

Where I part company with Roemer is in his suggestion that campaign-finance reform is the first reform, the master key that unlocks all others. Money flows to Washington because power flows through Washington. When Congress writes a tax code of thousands of pages, allocates subsidies, guarantees loans, regulates industries in minute detail, and can make or break firms with a sentence in an appropriations bill, organized interests will spend heavily to influence it. That spending may be ugly, but it is also predictable. If the state has the power to distribute favors, citizens and corporations will compete for favors.

The most durable campaign-finance reform is therefore to reduce the value of political favoritism. Simplify the tax code. End targeted subsidies. Eliminate earmarks permanently, not cosmetically. Stop using the tax system to micromanage economic life. Make regulations clearer and more general. Require that major rules pass rigorous cost-benefit analysis. Sunset programs unless Congress affirmatively reauthorizes them. Publish legislative text well before votes. If Washington has fewer favors to sell, fewer people will line up to buy them.

This does not mean there is no role for campaign rules. Disclosure should be immediate and searchable. Voters should know who is paying for ads, who is bundling contributions, and who is lobbying whom. PACs should not be used to hide the true source of money. Coordination rules should be enforced honestly. Members of Congress should disclose fundraising events, and lobbyists should not be able to bundle unlimited influence through layers of committees. Transparency is a conservative reform because it arms citizens with information without empowering bureaucrats to ration speech.

But we should be cautious about voucher schemes and public financing. Roemer is careful to design a voluntary system, and his desire to expand participation is admirable. Yet once government becomes the financier of campaigns, government must decide eligibility, compliance, fraud prevention, and enforcement. Those decisions will not be neutral for long. Incumbents will write rules that favor incumbents. Major parties will protect themselves against insurgents. Agencies will be pressured to punish disfavored speakers. Campaign-finance law is already complex enough to confuse ordinary citizens while employing armies of lawyers. We should not add another layer unless we are certain it will do more good than harm.

Nor should conservatives join the attack on Citizens United without thinking through the First Amendment stakes. Corporations can be powerful, but many corporations are also newspapers, publishers, nonprofit advocacy groups, and associations of citizens pooling resources. Unions also spend money. So do ideological organizations. The temptation to restrict “corporate” speech often becomes a temptation to let Congress decide who may criticize Congress close to an election. That is a dangerous power. The answer to bad speech is disclosure, debate, and more speech, not official permission.

Roemer’s personal $100 limit is honorable. It may even be politically clarifying. A candidate who refuses PAC money can speak with independence about industries that dominate both parties. But voluntary self-restraint is different from legal compulsion. We should celebrate candidates who choose small donors while preserving the freedom of others to organize, spend, and argue.

The deeper conservative lesson from Roemer’s essay is that concentrated power corrupts whether it sits on Wall Street or in Washington. Big banks used government power to protect themselves. Politicians used economic complexity to finance campaigns. Regulators and industry became too comfortable with one another. The remedy is not merely cleaner elections; it is constitutional government, limited favors, market discipline, and transparency.

Roemer’s warning deserves attention because it comes from someone who believes in enterprise, not from someone hostile to business. He is asking Republicans to choose between capitalism and corporatism. That choice is overdue. But if we want leaders free to lead, we also need citizens free from a government that sells too many privileges. Clean up the money, yes. But above all, stop minting the favors that make the money so eager to come.
