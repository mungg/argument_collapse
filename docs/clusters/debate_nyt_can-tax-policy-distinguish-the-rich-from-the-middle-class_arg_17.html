"To be politically viable and substantively fair, federal tax policy should distinguish between the truly wealthy and upper-middle-class earners by targeting accumulated privilege rather than ordinary aspirations like college savings."

The administration made a tactical and substantive mistake by reaching for 529 plans. A family earning more than $200,000 is not typical, and it is fair to ask such families to contribute more. But many of them are better described as upper-middle-class strivers than as “the rich” in the sense most Americans understand the term. They work for salaries, pay mortgages, save for tuition and worry about retirement. They are comfortable, not dynastic.

That distinction matters because tax policy depends on consent. If every household in the top 5 percent is treated as though it belongs to the same category as hedge-fund billionaires, reform will fail politically. The 529 proposal collapsed so quickly because it appeared to punish a virtuous behavior: saving for a child’s education. At a time when tuition has risen faster than family income, raiding college accounts was bound to look like a middle-class tax increase, regardless of the distribution tables.

Federal policy should draw the line higher and cleaner. Raise revenue from households with very high incomes, large estates and substantial investment income. Close carried-interest treatment. Limit deductions for those with incomes far above ordinary professional salaries. Enforce the estate tax. Those measures address real concentrations of wealth without telling parents that their child’s college fund is a loophole.

For the middle and lower-middle class, the better tools are Pell Grants, community college support, child credits and payroll tax relief. Those benefits can be targeted without undermining saving. Congress can also cap abusive uses of 529s rather than eliminating their basic tax treatment.

There is no magic income number that separates rich from middle class. But there is a political common sense: do not define “rich” so broadly that people who earn salaries and save for college think the government is coming after them. Aim at accumulated privilege, not ordinary aspiration.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Treating high-earning salaried professionals the same as billionaires creates political resistance and undermines the public consent necessary for tax reform.
  2. 02Taxing 529 plans is perceived as a penalty on virtuous middle-class behaviors like saving for education during a period of rising tuition costs.
  3. 03Revenue should be raised by targeting very high incomes, large estates, and investment loopholes rather than ordinary professional salaries.
  4. 04Support for the middle and lower-middle class should be provided through targeted benefits like Pell Grants and credits that do not discourage personal saving.
  5. 05Instead of eliminating broad tax benefits, the government should implement caps to prevent the abusive use of such accounts by the ultra-wealthy.
