"The United States should adopt a strategy of offshore balancing to preserve its primacy by husbanding its resources and intervening only when vital regional balances of power are directly threatened."

Stephen Walt’s central claim is both unfashionable and necessary: American power is most useful when it is held in reserve. In Washington, “leadership” has come to mean visibility, activism, and the constant display of resolve. Walt reminds us that a great power can lead in another way—by setting priorities, husbanding resources, and letting others carry burdens when their own interests are more directly engaged. That is not isolationism. It is strategy.

The strongest part of Walt’s argument is his insistence that primacy is a wasting asset if it is used promiscuously. The United States entered the post–Cold War era with advantages unmatched in modern history: no peer competitor, friendly neighbors, control of the seas, a dynamic economy, extraordinary universities, and a network of alliances built over decades. The question after 1991 was not whether America could knock over hostile regimes. Of course it could. The question was whether doing so would make the United States safer at tolerable cost. Iraq has supplied the answer. The U.S. military destroyed Saddam Hussein’s regime with astonishing speed, but the war’s political aftermath has exposed the limits of American power. Armies can seize capitals; they cannot easily manufacture legitimacy, reconcile sectarian communities, or erase nationalist resentment.

Offshore balancing is attractive because it begins with geography. The United States is not Prussia, France, Russia, or China. It is protected by oceans and bordered by weak or friendly states. It does not need to garrison the world to survive. It needs to prevent hostile domination of three key regions: Europe, East Asia, and the Persian Gulf. That goal has historically required American intervention only when local balances fail. In both world wars, the United States entered late, not because it was indifferent, but because it could afford to wait until the danger became unmistakable. During the Cold War, the Soviet Union’s power and ideology made permanent forward deployments more plausible. But the Cold War is over. A strategy designed for containing the Red Army in central Europe should not be treated as a permanent feature of nature.

Walt is also right that other states do not see American power as Americans see it. We tend to regard our power as benign because we know our own intentions. Others judge by capabilities, proximity, and experience. When U.S. officials reserve the right to overthrow regimes that might someday threaten us, they should not be surprised if threatened regimes seek the one deterrent that appears to work: nuclear weapons. North Korea’s lesson from Iraq is not subtle. A country without a credible deterrent may be invaded; a country with one receives negotiations. If we wish to reduce proliferation, we must reduce the incentives to proliferate. That does not mean trusting Kim Jong Il or the Iranian clerical regime. It means recognizing that their fear of American power is part of the problem.

Still, Walt’s essay is stronger as a diagnosis than as a full prescription. Offshore balancing can sound cleaner in theory than it is in practice. When exactly do local balances fail? How much warning will we have? How do we reassure allies sufficiently that they do not acquire nuclear arsenals of their own, while also persuading them to do more for themselves? A distant balancer must maintain credibility despite distance. If Japan or South Korea concluded that the United States was becoming “hard to get” in the wrong sense, the result might not be a healthier regional equilibrium. It might be a nuclear Northeast Asia.

The same problem arises in the Persian Gulf. Walt is right that the United States should not occupy the region or attempt to transform it by force. But a pure reliance on local balancing has often been difficult there because the local states are weak, divided, and internally brittle. The Gulf monarchies want American protection but fear American visibility. Iraq under Saddam was a threat to Kuwait, Iran, and Saudi Arabia; Iran’s size and revolutionary history worry its neighbors; Saudi Arabia’s internal politics pose their own dangers. A successful offshore strategy would require not simply withdrawal, but a careful architecture of access agreements, naval presence, intelligence cooperation, and rapid deployment capability. The term “offshore” should not obscure the amount of diplomatic work such a posture requires.

Walt’s discussion of Israel and Palestine is likely to provoke the sharpest objections, but his basic strategic point is hard to dismiss. The United States has allowed its Middle East policy to be identified with Israeli preferences to a degree that harms American interests and, arguably, Israel’s long-term interests as well. No serious peace will be possible without Israeli security, but no lasting peace is possible while Palestinians remain stateless, humiliated, and fragmented by settlements and barriers. The Clinton parameters remain the closest thing to a plausible settlement: two states, borders based on 1967 lines with swaps, a divided or shared Jerusalem, security guarantees, and a negotiated refugee solution. The United States should not pretend that settlement expansion is compatible with such an outcome. If American aid gives Washington leverage, then refusing to use that leverage is a choice.

Yet here, too, Walt may understate the political and moral complexity. Israel is not merely another small state. It is a democracy born after genocide, surrounded for decades by enemies, and still facing terrorism deliberately aimed at civilians. To say that American and Israeli interests are not identical is correct; to imply that pressure on Israel is the master key to Muslim opinion is too simple. Anti-Americanism in the Arab and Islamic world also draws on authoritarian propaganda, social stagnation, resentment of modernity, and internal political failures. Solving the Palestinian question would help enormously. It would not transform the region overnight.

Where Walt is most persuasive is on legitimacy. The United States cannot win a struggle of ideas while appearing indifferent to law, civilian casualties, and the opinions of friendly publics. Legitimacy is not a decorative extra; it is a force multiplier. The 1991 Gulf War succeeded not only because American arms were superior, but because Iraq had plainly committed aggression, the coalition was broad, and the war aim was limited. Kosovo was more controversial, but even there the United States acted with allies after exhausting diplomacy. Iraq in 2003 lacked that legitimacy. The cost has been measured not only in dollars and lives, but in trust.

A mature great power should know the difference between strength and restlessness. It should not confuse every hostile regime with Hitler, every crisis with Munich, or every doubt with appeasement. Nor should it imagine that restraint is weakness. Restraint is often what makes power sustainable. Walt’s offshore balancing needs refinement, especially in Asia and the Gulf. But its animating insight is right: America’s primacy will last longer if others see it as tolerable, predictable, and limited. The United States should be powerful enough to act when it must, wise enough not to act when it need not, and patient enough to let history do some of its work.

"The United States should adopt a conservative realism that preserves American primacy and freedom of action by narrowing global commitments and using military force more sparingly."

Stephen Walt has written a powerful indictment of strategic excess, and conservatives should not dismiss it simply because it criticizes the Bush administration. Prudence is a conservative virtue. Limited government abroad, like limited government at home, begins with an awareness of unintended consequences. The United States cannot occupy and reform every dangerous society. It cannot afford to confuse ambition with strategy. On these points, Walt is right.

But Walt’s cure contains too much faith in international approval, too little appreciation for the moral and political character of enemies, and an unnecessary tendency to turn restraint into apology. A conservative realism would reject both neoconservative overreach and liberal multilateral piety. It would preserve American freedom of action while using it more sparingly.

The Iraq war has exposed the limits of American planning. Even those who supported removing Saddam Hussein must admit that the occupation was mishandled. The failure to anticipate looting, insurgency, porous borders, and institutional collapse was not a minor administrative error; it was a strategic failure. A government that cannot secure ammunition dumps or understand the society it has conquered should be more modest about remaking regions. The doctrine of preventive war should not be treated as routine policy. If every potential threat becomes a justification for war, the United States will exhaust itself and unite enemies.

Yet Walt overstates the uselessness of force and the reliability of deterrence. Some regimes are deterred; some are reckless; some work through proxies. The world after September 11 does not permit us to wait comfortably for threats to become imminent in the old-fashioned sense. Intelligence will always be uncertain, and presidents will sometimes have to act under uncertainty. The lesson of Iraq should be that preventive war requires an exceptionally high evidentiary threshold, clear congressional and public debate, and a plausible postwar plan. It should not be that America renounces the option forever.

Walt’s “buddy system” for the use of force is also misguided. Allies are valuable. Legitimacy matters. But no president should make American security depend on the approval of the United Nations Security Council, NATO consensus, or any other external body. France, Russia, and China do not become guardians of wisdom because they possess vetoes. They pursue interests. Sometimes those interests align with ours; sometimes they do not. The United States should consult seriously and seek coalitions whenever possible, but it must retain sovereign judgment. Multilateralism is a tool, not a sacrament.

A similar problem appears in Walt’s list of treaties and gestures America should have embraced: Kyoto, landmines, biological-weapons verification, small arms. Some may have merits, but the implication that signing more agreements would have transformed America’s standing is doubtful. Kyoto imposed costs unequally and excluded major developing emitters from binding limits. The landmine treaty raised legitimate concerns for the defense of South Korea. Verification protocols can create illusions of security while exposing sensitive facilities. The United States should not reject agreements reflexively, but neither should it sign flawed treaties as reputational offerings.

Walt is strongest when he argues for reducing unnecessary military footprints. Permanent deployments can become magnets for resentment and excuses for allied dependency. Europe can do more for itself. The Gulf should not be treated as an American protectorate. Bases should serve current missions, not bureaucratic nostalgia. The United States should rely more on naval power, air mobility, prepositioned equipment, intelligence, and temporary access. It should make clear that American help is not an entitlement. Allies who want protection should contribute to common purposes.

But playing hard-to-get has limits. Alliances are not merely transactions. They are assets accumulated over time. If allies conclude that America is fickle, they may accommodate adversaries, pursue independent nuclear capabilities, or refuse cooperation when we need it. A conservative strategy should demand more from allies without turning reassurance into manipulation. Reliability is a form of power.

On the Middle East, Walt is right that the United States should not attempt imperial transformation. Democracy cannot be imposed by bayonets. But neither should we return to the old bargain of oil, bases, and silence. Friendly autocrats helped create the swamp in which radicalism grew. The United States should press for gradual reform, private property, women’s education, freer media, and accountable institutions. This should be done through diplomacy and conditional assistance, not invasion. Stability purchased by permanent repression is not stable.

Walt’s treatment of Israel, however, is too severe. Settlement expansion deserves American opposition, and a two-state settlement is in Israel’s interest as well as ours. But threatening to cut off Israel unless it accepts Washington’s terms misunderstands both Israeli insecurity and Palestinian dysfunction. Israel is a democratic ally facing terrorist groups that deliberately murder civilians. Palestinian leadership has repeatedly failed to control violence and build credible institutions. The United States should push both sides hard, but a public ultimatum to Israel would likely encourage Palestinian maximalists and Israeli hard-liners alike. Candid friendship is wiser than punitive distancing.

The nuclear section of Walt’s essay deserves serious attention. Nuclear terrorism is indeed the gravest danger. Securing fissile material should receive far more money and presidential energy. Cooperative threat reduction with Russia, reactor conversion, port detection, and interdiction are conservative investments in survival. Here the administration has not done enough. If we can spend vast sums in Iraq, we can spend what is needed to lock down bomb material.

Still, Walt’s proposed nuclear “grand bargain” may promise more than it can deliver. Reducing America’s arsenal to a few hundred warheads might be compatible with deterrence, but it will not persuade North Korea or Iran to abandon nuclear ambitions. They seek leverage, prestige, and regime security. Security assurances may be useful bargaining chips, but only if tied to intrusive verification and consequences for cheating. The United States should not weaken its extended deterrent in ways that frighten Japan, South Korea, or other allies. Nonproliferation requires not only disarmament gestures but credible protection.

Where Walt’s essay is most valuable is in its call for seriousness. American foreign policy has been too often driven by slogans, domestic politics, and ignorance. Conservatives should be the first to insist that government know what it is doing before it undertakes grand projects. Intelligence must be improved. Language training matters. The State Department and military must plan together. Congress must exercise oversight rather than cheerleading. Patriotism does not require pretending that every policy is wise.

A conservative realism would preserve American primacy by narrowing commitments, strengthening borders and homeland defenses, securing nuclear materials, maintaining military superiority, and using force decisively but rarely. It would respect allies but not be ruled by them. It would promote liberty by example, trade, and diplomacy more than by occupation. It would recognize that some enemies cannot be charmed, but also that not every enemy must be invaded.

Walt has performed a service by reminding Americans that power can be squandered. But American restraint should arise from confidence, not contrition; from judgment, not deference. The United States should be slower to draw the sword, but no less determined to keep it sharp.
