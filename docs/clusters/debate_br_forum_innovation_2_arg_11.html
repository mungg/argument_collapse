What This Looks Like From the Global South

Dan Breznitz's essay is written from a particular vantage—broadly, from the perspective of mid-income or rich communities deciding among innovation strategies. The cautionary tales are Cleveland, Lyon, the Bay Area. The success stories are Sakai, Taichung, Brenta, Shenzhen, and certain Israeli precincts. The implicit reader is a policymaker in a city with reasonable infrastructure, a functional state, and enough industrial history to plausibly choose among stages two, three, and four. I want to ask what his framework looks like from somewhere else: from Lusaka, from Dhaka, from rural Bolivia, from the parts of the world where the question is not "which innovation stage to specialize in" but "how to escape commodity dependence at all."

Begin with the most important fact about the global economy that Breznitz's framework leaves implicit: the four stages he describes are mostly downstream of access to a particular kind of basic capability. Stage four production in the Pearl River Delta did not arise from nowhere. It rests on decades of state investment in primary and secondary education that produced a workforce capable of operating modern factories; on infrastructure investment that produced ports, roads, and power grids; on macroeconomic management that kept the currency competitive; and on a domestic market large enough to support learning-by-doing before exports took off. Most low-income countries today have none of these preconditions. Telling Zambia to become the next Shenzhen is no more useful than telling Cleveland to become the next Palo Alto.

This matters because there is a real risk that Breznitz's framework, applied uncritically by international development institutions, will substitute one fashionable model for another. We have seen this cycle before. In the 1990s the World Bank pushed export processing zones; in the 2000s, cluster strategies; in the 2010s, innovation hubs and incubators. Each new template promises locally inclusive prosperity. Each tends to flatter the consultants who promote it more than the communities they advise. If we now add "stage two design hubs" and "stage three component innovation" to the menu of recommended strategies, we should at least be honest that almost no country in sub-Saharan Africa is in a position to execute on them in the next decade.

What does work in low-income settings? The honest answer is mostly the same things that worked historically: basic education, basic infrastructure, basic public health, basic state capacity, and selective protection of infant industries long enough for them to develop competitive capabilities. The East Asian developmental states whose successes Breznitz draws on did precisely this. Taiwan did not become a stage three innovator in 1972; it became one after thirty years of patient industrial policy that began with light manufacturing, moved through heavy industry, and only later moved up the value chain. South Korea's path was similar. China's, on a different scale, also. The lesson is sequencing, not stage selection.

The risk of Breznitz's framework for development practitioners is that it can be read as suggesting countries can simply pick an innovation stage and dive in. They cannot. Brenta's design capabilities rest on generations of accumulated craft knowledge in shoemaking. Shimano's gear innovation rests on a Japanese metallurgy tradition that goes back to swords. Even MediaTek, which feels like a more recent phenomenon, is the spinoff of an integrated circuit design capability that the Taiwanese state had been deliberately cultivating since the 1970s. You cannot teleport into stage three. You arrive there by working through stages four and three and back to two, slowly, with a state capable of guiding the process.

I would add a more pointed concern. The picture Breznitz paints of global production is one in which different localities specialize in different stages and all can win. This is a comforting story, but it leaves out the politics of who occupies which slot. The global semiconductor industry has perhaps a half-dozen meaningful nodes. The global luxury shoe industry has perhaps two. The slots are scarce, and getting one of them often involves displacing somebody else. When Schwinn lost its bicycle production capability to Giant, that was not a peaceful complementarity; that was competitive destruction. When MediaTek undercuts Qualcomm, somebody's wages get cut somewhere. The pluralist vision of "many roads to prosperity" is partially true and partially obscures the fact that the global division of labor is zero-sum at any given moment, even if it is positive-sum over time.

For low-income countries, this implies that the available paths are narrower than Breznitz's pluralism suggests. Ethiopia's bid to enter the global apparel value chain is a competitive challenge to Bangladesh's. Bangladesh's was a challenge to China's, which was once a challenge to South Korea's. Each entrant gains because the previous occupant has moved up the chain, but no country gets to skip the queue. The strategy that worked for Taiwan in the 1970s—labor-intensive light manufacturing financed by patient state-directed credit and protected by managed trade—works less reliably today because automation has reduced the labor intensity of much production and because the WTO regime constrains the policy tools that East Asian states once used freely.

So my reading of Breznitz is this. His critique of the Silicon Valley monoculture is welcome, and his stages framework is a useful corrective to "innovation equals startups." But his examples are drawn from middle- and high-income contexts where the basics are already in place, and his framework should not be exported as a development template without serious adjustment. For the poorest countries, the relevant menu is much shorter and much more dependent on getting the boring fundamentals right. For middle-income countries, the strategic question is less which stage to specialize in than how to manage the long climb up the value chain without being trapped on a single rung.

In the global development conversation, what we most need is not new strategy menus but the recovery of policy tools that the post-Bretton Woods consensus took off the table: managed trade, directed credit, capital controls, and the kind of activist industrial policy that East Asia practiced and that the WTO regime has progressively constrained. Without those tools, even the most cleverly designed innovation strategy is unlikely to succeed.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The four stages of innovation described are downstream of basic capabilities—such as primary education, infrastructure, and macroeconomic stability—that many low-income nations have yet to establish.
  2. 02Successful innovation hubs like Taiwan and Japan did not simply choose a stage to specialize in, but rather followed a long, patient sequence of industrial development starting from basic manufacturing.
  3. 03The framework risks becoming another failed development fad if it encourages countries to 'teleport' into advanced stages of innovation without the necessary generations of accumulated craft and technical knowledge.
  4. 04The global division of labor is often a zero-sum competition for scarce slots in the value chain, meaning one region's success frequently necessitates another's decline.
  5. 05For the Global South to achieve prosperity, the focus must shift from choosing innovation strategies to recovering activist policy tools—like managed trade and directed credit—that are currently constrained by international trade regimes.
