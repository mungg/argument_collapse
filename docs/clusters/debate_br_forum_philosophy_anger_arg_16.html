"Agnes Callard’s philosophical defense of grudges and revenge relies on an inaccurate armchair phenomenology of anger that is contradicted by extensive empirical psychological and neurological research."

**What the Psychology Actually Shows**

Agnes Callard's essay is a piece of armchair philosophy that makes empirical claims. It claims, in essence, that the phenomenology of anger has a particular structure: anger does not naturally fade in response to apology and restitution; the felt link between the other's evil and one's own good is constitutive; revenge fantasies are rational extensions of moral perception. These are not just conceptual claims. They are claims about how human minds actually work, and they can be checked against the substantial empirical literature on anger, forgiveness, and moral emotions.

I want to summarize what that literature tells us, because I think Callard's picture, while powerful as philosophy, does not match what the psychology actually shows.

First, on the relationship between apology and anger. There is now a robust experimental literature on apology and forgiveness, going back to early work by Robert Enright in the 1980s and developed extensively by Everett Worthington, Michael McCullough, and many others. The basic finding is that sincere apology, accompanied by restitution and demonstrated change, reliably reduces anger in the wronged party. Not always; not completely; not for all injuries; but reliably, and across cultures. This is not a fact that requires explanation in terms of irrational fizzling-out. It is exactly what you would expect if anger were tracking the present state of moral relations (rather than the bare past fact of injury) and updating when that state changed.

Callard's argument requires that any reduction in anger in response to apology be either irrational or merely a function of nonrational fizzling. This is empirically implausible. The reduction is not random; it is calibrated to features of the apology — its sincerity, its specificity, its acceptance of responsibility, its accompanying change in behavior. Anger updates on these features in fine-grained ways. This calibration is exactly what we would expect if the cognitive content of anger included something like "the wrongdoer remains in unrepented violation of moral relations." Apology and restitution change this content; anger updates accordingly.

Second, on the relationship between anger and revenge. The empirical literature on revenge — work by Kevin Carlsmith, Mario Gollwitzer, and others — shows that taking revenge does not, contrary to folk wisdom, reliably make people feel better. In fact, it often makes them feel worse, and prolongs rumination on the original wrong. Forgiveness, by contrast, is robustly associated with better psychological outcomes for the forgiver, including reduced rumination, lower cortisol, lower rates of depression. This is not a moral claim; it is a finding about human psychology.

Now, Callard might say: my argument is not about what makes people feel good. It is about what is rational. Revenge is rational regardless of its hedonic consequences. Fine. But notice that the psychological data tell against her descriptive picture of revenge as the natural completion of anger's logic. If revenge were the natural completion of anger, we would expect it to satisfy the angry person. It does not. What this suggests is that anger is more complicated than Callard's argument allows; that the relationship between anger and its supposed object (the making-bad-of-the-wrongdoer) is not as simple as she claims.

Third, on the cross-cultural variability of anger. The anthropological and cross-cultural psychological literature shows that the phenomenology Callard describes — the colonized fantasy life, the intimate hatred, the eternal grudge — is not a human universal. Cultures vary substantially in how anger is expressed, sustained, and dissolved. The work of Anna Wierzbicka on emotion vocabularies, of Catherine Lutz on the Ifaluk, of Paul Ekman and his critics on basic emotions, all suggests that what we call "anger" in English is not a single cross-cultural phenomenon but a family of related but distinct phenomena. Some cultures cultivate practices that reduce or transform anger; others amplify it. Callard's picture is recognizably ours — recognizably Mediterranean-honor-culture, recognizably modern individualist — but it is not the picture of human anger as such.

Fourth, on the trainability of emotional response. The contemplative neuroscience literature of the last twenty years — work by Richard Davidson, Tania Singer, and others — has shown that long-term meditation practice produces measurable changes in neural responses to negative emotional stimuli. Trained meditators show reduced amygdala activation in response to provocation, increased prefrontal regulation, and (relevant for our purposes) reduced behavioral expression of anger without reduced perception of injustice. This is exactly the combination that Callard says is a philosopher's fiction: clear moral perception of wrong, decoupled from the colonizing affective response.

I want to be careful here. None of this empirical literature settles the philosophical question. Callard could maintain that what the meditators have achieved is not "real" anger-without-vengeance but something else, perhaps moral indifference dressed up as virtue. She could maintain that the cross-cultural variability is variability in how anger is *expressed*, not in its underlying structure. She could maintain that the apology literature shows only that we are skilled at suppressing anger we still rationally have. These moves are available.

But the moves come at a cost. Callard's argument was supposed to be that purified anger is a fiction, that the actual phenomenon of anger has the structure she describes. If she has to discount the empirical evidence of trained, apologized-to, or culturally distinct individuals as not really having the relevant phenomenon, she is increasingly fitting the theory to a particular subset of cases — angry Western individualists in conditions where apology has not occurred — and presenting it as universal.

There is a related point about her Argument for Grudges that the empirical literature illuminates. Callard says the reason for anger is the past fact of wrong, which never changes. But the psychological literature on anger consistently finds that anger has present-tense intentional content: the wronged person represents the wrongdoer as currently culpable, currently threatening, currently in violation. When this present-tense content updates — through apology, through restitution, through demonstrated change — the anger updates with it. The persistence of the past fact does not, by itself, sustain the anger; the persistence of the present moral situation does. Callard's argument requires the past-fact construal, but the empirical evidence supports the present-situation construal.

What does this all mean for the philosophical argument? I think it means that Callard's essay, while elegant, is doing armchair phenomenology that has not been checked against the lab. The picture of anger she paints is recognizable; it captures something real about how anger sometimes goes. But it overstates the necessity of the structure it describes. Anger can be calibrated, updated, transformed, and (in some cases) released, without anyone needing to deny that the original wrong was a wrong, and without anyone falling into acquiescence.

This matters because Callard's conclusion has practical import. If we believe her — if we believe that the alternative to acquiescence is corruption, that purified moral response is a fiction, that the wronged are necessarily damaged — we will give up on practices like therapy, restorative justice, and contemplative training that aim at exactly the transformed response she calls fictional. We will become more pessimistic about the possibility of moral repair after grave wrong. We may even, perversely, license forms of vengeance on the grounds that they are the rational completion of justified anger.

The empirical literature does not support this pessimism. Human beings do, in fact, sometimes manage the difficult work of being wronged without being deformed by it. They do it imperfectly, partially, and with support. But they do it. And the practices that support this work are not fictions; they are real human achievements, with real (if modest) empirical track records.

Callard's essay deserves admiration as a piece of philosophical provocation. As a description of how anger actually works, it is partial in ways that matter. The phenomenology she captures is real but not exhaustive, and the conclusions she draws about the impossibility of purification go beyond what the actual data support. There is more room for moral repair than her essay allows, and the disciplines that pursue it are not chasing ghosts.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Experimental literature demonstrates that sincere apologies and restitution reliably reduce anger, suggesting that anger tracks the current state of moral relations rather than unchangeable past facts.
  2. 02Psychological data shows that revenge often increases rumination and distress rather than providing the rational satisfaction or 'completion' Callard describes.
  3. 03Cross-cultural studies reveal that the specific 'colonizing' and eternal nature of anger Callard depicts is a culturally specific phenomenon rather than a universal human constant.
  4. 04Neuroscience and contemplative practices prove that individuals can be trained to perceive injustice clearly without experiencing the vengeful affective responses Callard claims are inseparable from moral perception.
  5. 05Callard’s insistence that the past fact of a wrong necessitates a permanent grudge ignores the reality that anger's cognitive content is focused on the present moral situation, which can be updated.
  6. 06Accepting Callard’s view would harmfully undermine practical efforts toward moral repair, such as therapy and restorative justice, by framing them as psychological fictions.
