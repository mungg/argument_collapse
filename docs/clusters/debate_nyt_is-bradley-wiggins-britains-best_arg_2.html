"Bradley Wiggins should be celebrated as one of Britain’s many sporting greats rather than being ranked as the single best, as comparing excellence across different sports is reductive and illogical."

The question “Is Bradley Wiggins Britain’s best?” is irresistible, but it is also the wrong question. Sport is not a single examination in which a cyclist, a rower, a tennis player, a cricketer and a footballer sit the same paper. Wiggins has done something magnificent: he has become the first Briton to win the Tour de France, one of the most demanding prizes in world sport. But ranking him above Steve Redgrave, Daley Thompson, Fred Perry, Bobby Moore, Ian Botham, Chris Hoy or Kelly Holmes tells us more about our appetite for lists than about greatness.

Different sports create different kinds of excellence. A cyclist in the Tour must survive a moving battlefield for three weeks, while depending on teammates and tactics. A tennis player stands alone, exposed point by point. A rower repeats a punishing act to perfection for years. A footballer operates inside a game of improvisation, scarcity and luck. Comparisons across these worlds are fun over a pint, but they become silly when treated as verdicts.

What Britain should do instead is enlarge its idea of sporting honor. Too often national celebration is rationed to the last person standing: the winner, the gold medalist, the one who ends a drought. That explains the slightly strained mood around Andy Murray at Wimbledon. He reached the final, lost to perhaps the greatest grass-court player of his generation, and still somehow carried the burden of British sporting inadequacy. That is an unfair way to watch sport.

Wiggins’s victory should be celebrated fully, but not as a cudgel against other athletes or as proof that Britain has finally become “serious.” Britain has long produced great competitors. It has also produced sports cultures that prize amateurism, stoicism and eccentricity as much as winning. Some of that has held the country back; some of it is worth preserving.

As London prepares to host the Olympics, the country should honor heroes past and present by telling better stories about them. Put Redgrave beside Wiggins, not beneath him. Remember that Tanni Grey-Thompson’s achievements matter. Remember that women’s sport is too often noticed only during Olympic weeks. Remember coaches, clubs and volunteers.

Wiggins is not Britain’s best. He is one of Britain’s best. That should be enough. A mature sporting nation does not need a single king; it needs a crowded pantheon.

Britain should enjoy Bradley Wiggins’s Tour de France victory without turning it into another national anxiety attack. The assumption behind the question is that because Britain was the birthplace of so many sports, it “should” be producing more champions. But why should birth confer permanent ownership? Britain helped codify games and then gave them to the world. The world accepted the invitation.

That is one of the great successes of British sporting history, not a failure. Football is played beautifully in Spain and Brazil. Tennis champions come from Switzerland, Serbia, Spain, the United States and many other places. Cricket has been transformed by India, Australia, the West Indies, Pakistan, Sri Lanka and South Africa. Rugby, golf and athletics have similarly escaped any single national custody. If Britain invented or organized many modern sports, it also created the conditions for being beaten at them.

This does not mean Britain should settle for mediocrity. Wiggins’s win is glorious because it required excellence in a sport where Britain had no automatic privilege. But a medal table is a crude measure of a sporting nation. A country can win medals while leaving many citizens inactive, priced out or uninterested. It can also fail to dominate internationally while sustaining a rich culture of clubs, parks, weekend leagues and local loyalties.

London 2012 will tempt Britain to judge itself every evening by gold, silver and bronze. Some of that is harmless fun. But if the Olympics become merely a referendum on national virility, the country will have missed the better opportunity. The real question is not whether Britain produces enough champions to satisfy television panels. It is whether sport remains woven into ordinary life.

Honoring heroes should therefore mean more than elevating the latest winner. By all means celebrate Wiggins. He has earned the yellow jersey and the applause. But also honor the volunteer cricket scorer, the junior athletics coach, the swimming teacher, the groundskeeper, the parents driving to cold Saturday fixtures, and the aging five-a-side player who keeps showing up. They are part of Britain’s sporting inheritance too.

Is Wiggins Britain’s best? On a bicycle this July, unquestionably. In the grander sense, the argument is unresolvable. What should be resisted is the gloomy idea that every British defeat is a national flaw and every victory a national cure. Sport is richer than that. Wiggins has given Britain a wonderful moment. The task now is to make sure the country’s love of sport is not reduced to the hunger for more moments like it.

Bradley Wiggins's victory in Paris is a magnificent achievement, and the sideburned Mod from Kilburn deserves every ounce of the praise being heaped on him. But let's not let national giddiness obscure a simple truth: the question of whether he is "Britain's best" is essentially unanswerable, and asking it tells us more about our anxieties than about our athletes.

Best at what? Across what era? Sir Steve Redgrave won gold at five consecutive Olympics in a sport that demands brutal year-round commitment. Daley Thompson dominated the decathlon, the closest thing sport has to a measure of all-around athletic excellence. Nick Faldo collected six majors when European golf was supposed to be a sideshow. Lennox Lewis unified the heavyweight title. Sir Bobby Charlton lifted the World Cup. Each of these claims rests on different criteria, and ranking them is a parlour game, not an argument.

What makes Wiggins's achievement distinctive isn't that it outranks the others but that it punctures a particular myth: that British riders couldn't win the Tour. For over a century, the Grand Tours were the property of continental Europe. Tom Simpson died trying. Robert Millar came close in 1984. Now a Londoner has worn yellow into Paris, and he did it by leading the strongest team in the race. That is a categorical breakthrough, not merely an incremental one.

The deeper story, though, is institutional. Wiggins is a product of British Cycling's transformation under Peter Keen and Dave Brailsford, of National Lottery funding redirected toward Olympic disciplines after the Atlanta humiliation in 1996, and of Team Sky's Murdoch-financed assault on the road peloton. The "marginal gains" philosophy turned a cycling backwater into a superpower in roughly a decade. That is the real headline, and it bears directly on the Olympic question raised by the prompt: when British sport invests seriously, with patient money and ruthless professionalism, it produces champions in clusters, not isolated heroes.

So rather than asking whether Wiggo edges out Redgrave, ask why cycling and rowing churn out gold medallists while English cricket and football lurch from crisis to crisis. The answer isn't a shortage of talent. It's a shortage of the structures that turn talent into trophies.

No, Bradley Wiggins is not Britain's greatest athlete, and the rush to crown him reveals an unfortunate British habit: confusing novelty with supremacy.

Wiggins won the Tour de France, and that is genuinely historic. But "first Briton to win X" is not the same as "best Briton ever." By that logic, Virginia Wade would outrank Fred Perry simply because she's more recent, or any debut Olympic champion would dwarf a multiple gold medallist. The criterion has to be sustained excellence against the world's best, and on that measure several British athletes have a stronger case.

Start with Sir Steve Redgrave. Five Olympic golds in five consecutive Games, across two decades, in a sport that punishes the body more savagely than almost any other. He won in coxed fours, coxless pairs, and coxless fours, adapting to whatever crew and discipline circumstances demanded. There is no parallel achievement in British sport.

Consider also Sir Chris Hoy, who already has four Olympic golds and could add more in London next week. Or Tanni Grey-Thompson, with eleven Paralympic golds and a London Marathon record that stood for years. Or, looking further back, Jackie Stewart, three-time Formula One world champion who also dragged motor racing kicking and screaming into the safety era. Or Sir Ian Botham, whose 1981 Ashes performances remain perhaps the most extraordinary individual contribution to a Test series in cricket history.

Wiggins's yellow jersey is a stunning accomplishment, but he has won it once, at 32, with the help of the deepest team in the peloton and a teammate (Chris Froome) who looked capable of winning it himself. Compare that to Redgrave grinding out gold in Sydney, aged 38, after telling the world to shoot him if he ever went near a boat again.

The right way to honour Wiggins is to put his achievement in its proper context: a magnificent breakthrough in a sport Britain has only recently learned to dominate, and the latest fruit of a remarkable cycling programme. The wrong way is to use him as a stick to beat earlier generations, or to declare a contest closed when the man himself has, one hopes, years of competition left.

Britain’s rich sporting history is not a ladder with one rung at the top. It’s a mosaic. That’s a far healthier form of patriotism.
