Should Companies Tell Us When They Get Hacked? · Cluster 5 of 20

"Companies should be legally required to disclose security breaches because the data they hold belongs morally to the individuals affected, who have a right to know when their personal information is at risk."

Companies should disclose breaches because the data they lose is usually not theirs in any meaningful moral sense. It is ours.

Over the last two decades, businesses have built enormous databases from ordinary transactions. A bookstore knows what we read. A search company knows what we wonder about. A retailer knows our addresses, credit cards and buying habits. A hospital knows our bodies. A newspaper may know its subscribers, reporters’ contacts and internal deliberations. Consumers did not negotiate the security architecture behind these systems. In many cases, they had no realistic option but to hand over information. If that information is exposed, the company has no ethical right to keep the fact quiet.

Disclosure is a matter of consent and autonomy. People cannot protect themselves from risks they are not allowed to know about. If my password has been stolen, I need to change it, especially if I reused it elsewhere. If my debit card information is compromised, I need to watch my account. If my e-mail address and personal details are exposed, I should be alert for targeted fraud. Notice does not eliminate the harm, but it gives the victim some agency.

The business community often frames breach disclosure as punishment. That is the wrong frame. We do not require food companies to announce contamination because we hate food companies. We require it because the public has a direct stake in the risk. Data breaches are similar. A company that collects sensitive information assumes a duty of care. If it fails, or even if it is overcome by a sophisticated attacker despite reasonable care, it must warn those affected.

The objection that disclosure will damage reputations is precisely why voluntary disclosure is insufficient. Some firms will behave honorably, as several recent victims have by speaking publicly. Others will calculate that silence is cheaper. A rule creates a level playing field. It prevents the honest company from being the only one embarrassed.

Of course the law should be practical. Notices should be written in plain language. They should identify what information was at risk, what the company is doing, and what steps individuals should take. There can be narrow delays for active law enforcement investigations. But those exceptions should not swallow the rule.

We should stop treating cyber intrusions as private corporate misfortunes. They are failures in systems that hold pieces of our lives. When those systems are breached, the people whose lives are in the database have a right to know.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Companies have an ethical obligation to report breaches because consumers often have no choice but to provide their data and did not negotiate the security measures protecting it.
  2. 02Disclosure is essential for individual autonomy, as it allows victims to take proactive steps to protect themselves from further fraud or identity theft.
  3. 03Mandatory disclosure should be viewed as a public safety requirement similar to food contamination warnings rather than as a punishment for the company.
  4. 04A legal requirement for disclosure creates a level playing field that prevents honest companies from being disproportionately penalized for their transparency compared to silent competitors.
  5. 05While the law should allow for practical considerations like law enforcement delays, the primary focus must remain on providing clear and actionable information to the victims.
Diversified DeepSeek

"Companies should be legally required to disclose security breaches because personal data is the property of the individual, and custodians have a fiduciary duty to inform owners when their property has been compromised."

The debate over mandatory hack disclosure asks the wrong question. It frames the issue as a matter of corporate obligation, a regulatory burden to be imposed or resisted. But the real problem is a fundamental denial of basic property rights in the digital age. The data that companies collect about us — our purchase histories, location trails, intimate communications — is not theirs to lose silently. It is ours. Once you recognize that personal data is a form of digital property, the duty to notify becomes not a bureaucratic requirement but a direct consequence of a property violation.

Think of it this way: if someone broke into a bank and rummaged through your safe deposit box, you wouldn’t accept the bank’s decision to keep it quiet because it might cause a panic or embarrass their security team. You’d demand immediate notice because your property had been accessed without authorization. The same principle applies when a retailer’s database is breached and your financial and personal details are exposed. The company is the custodian of your information, not its owner. When that custody is violated, they have a fiduciary duty to inform you, the true owner, at once.

This shifts the entire conversation away from corporate cost-benefit analysis and toward individual rights. A company doesn’t get to weigh whether disclosure suits its public relations calendar. It must report the breach just as it would report a physical theft of tangible assets — promptly, to the victim, with full accounting of what was taken. The current state-by-state notification laws, weak as they are, at least implicitly acknowledge this property interest by tying notification requirements to the type of data lost, not the company’s preference. But they don’t go nearly far enough.

A robust federal standard, grounded in a clear legal declaration that individuals own their personal data outright, would change everything. It would mean you have standing to sue not just for actual fraud committed with your data, but for the breach of your property right itself. The harm is the unauthorized exposure, not the subsequent use. This creates an automatic, powerful incentive for companies to invest in real security: they would face liability directly to you, not just to the Federal Trade Commission.

We are long past the era where data is just exhaust from our transactions. It is the most intimate portrait of our lives. A society that takes property rights seriously cannot allow custodians to hide when that property is compromised. They must tell us because it’s ours, not theirs, and we have a right to know when it’s been touched by unwelcome hands.
