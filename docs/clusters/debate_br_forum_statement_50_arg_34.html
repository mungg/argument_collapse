"While the Port Huron Statement’s call for participatory democracy remains a universal human impulse, it is an ideologically neutral tool that is just as effectively—and perhaps more consequentially—harnessed by the political right as by the left."

Reading Tom Hayden’s commemoration, I found myself transported not to Zuccotti Park or to the immigrant rights marches but to a small rural town in western Massachusetts, where in the late 1990s I sat in a circle of neighbors trying to save our local post office from closure. We were not radicals; we were retired teachers, dairy farmers, and small business owners who had never read the Port Huron Statement and would have been baffled by its sweeping Cold War anxieties. Yet as Hayden describes the essence of participatory democracy—the joint creation of community and self—I recognize our campaign instantly. We organized public meetings, circulated petitions, confronted distant bureaucrats, and ultimately forced the Postal Service to reverse its decision. We did not achieve utopia, but we preserved a small piece of our common life. In that sense, I share Hayden’s conviction that the impulse to participate is universal and that it cuts across the tidy ideological categories we use to sort our fellow citizens.

But Hayden’s essay errs, I believe, in treating participatory democracy as a mainly progressive phenomenon, a legacy of the student left. The truth is that the most vibrant and sustained expressions of grassroots democratic participation in recent American life have erupted not from the left but from the right. The Tea Party movement, which Hayden conspicuously fails to mention, is arguably the most consequential instance of participatory democracy since the civil rights era, and it challenges everything his narrative assumes about who gets to claim the mantle of Port Huron.

The Tea Party emerged in 2009 as a decentralized, largely self-organizing rebellion against the financial bailouts and the perceived overreach of the Obama administration. It held mass rallies, many of which dwarfed the Occupy encampments in sheer numbers. It practiced a kind of rough consensus-building through local chapters and online networks. It was profoundly suspicious of established political gatekeepers—much more suspicious, in fact, than many on the left who still pine for Obama’s transformative promise. Tea Party activists worked tirelessly to master the mechanics of local government, showing up at town hall meetings, running for precinct committeeman positions, and systematically moving the Republican Party to the right through sheer participant energy. They did not merely demand a seat at the table; they rebuilt the table.

If participatory democracy is a timeless human need, then it cannot be the exclusive property of those who share Hayden’s policy preferences. And yet his essay implicitly treats the surge for Obama in 2008 as a vindication of participatory ideals while ignoring the surge for the Tea Party that followed immediately thereafter. The Occupy movement gets a paragraph, but the millions of Americans who flooded town halls in the summer of 2009 to protest the Affordable Care Act are invisible. This selectivity suggests that Hayden is not celebrating participation as such; he is celebrating a specific progressive coalition. That is perfectly legitimate as a political project, but it undermines the claim that Port Huron’s message is transcendent.

A more honest assessment would acknowledge that participatory democracy is ideologically neutral. It is a set of techniques—rallies, referenda, independent media, networked organizing—that can be harnessed by any movement with sufficient passion and organizational savvy. The same Internet that Hayden praises as a tool of liberation has also been central to the rise of the Tea Party and to the spread of conspiracy theories, birtherism, and the kind of populist anger that does not easily fit into a progressive frame. The Tools of direct democracy, such as the recall and referendum, have been used in California to cripple public finances through Proposition 13 and its successors, fueling exactly the crisis of public education that Hayden laments. Participation is not a magic wand; it is an amplifier, and what it amplifies depends on who shows up and what they want.

This recognition does not diminish the value of participatory democracy; it clarifies it. If we believe that ordinary people should have a meaningful say in the decisions that shape their lives, then we must accept that they will sometimes use that say to advance outcomes we deplore. The alternative is to retreat into a vanguardist position in which participation is encouraged only when it points toward the correct ideological destination—a position that is profoundly anti-democratic. The Port Huron authors, for all their youthful fervor, understood this better than some of their heirs. They were pluralists, convinced that democracy required genuine contention among diverse viewpoints. Hayden gestures toward this when he says that libertarians and even conservatives can “sometimes” fit into the participatory framework. But his essay does not do the hard work of imagining what it would mean to share a movement with people whose views on, say, abortion or taxation one finds abhorrent.

For my own part, I found in the post-office fight a microcosm of this challenge. Our little group included people who voted for George W. Bush and people who voted for Ralph Nader. We disagreed about nearly everything, but we agreed that our community should not be dictated to by a remote bureaucracy in Washington. That common ground was fragile, often frustrating, and entirely dependent on our willingness to bracket our larger metaphysical conflicts in favor of a concrete, achievable goal. It was participatory democracy in its most ordinary form, and it worked because it was local, bounded, and practical. Scaling this up to the national or global level is the great unresolved problem, and neither the Port Huron Statement nor Hayden’s essay offers a convincing answer.

So I accept Hayden’s core message but ask him to draw a more uncomfortable conclusion. If participatory democracy really is a big tent, then it must make room not only for the Dreamers and the Occupiers but for the Tea Partiers and the town-hall shouters, for the NIMBY activists and the tax revolt organizers. It must grapple with the fact that the people, when they speak, do not speak with one progressive voice. This is frightening, because it means that the expansion of participation may not lead to the world we want. But it is also liberating, because it reminds us that democracy is not a machine for producing correct outcomes; it is a way of living together amid irreducible disagreement. That is the deep wisdom hidden in the Port Huron Statement’s call for joint-creation, and it is a wisdom that Hayden’s essay, for all its grace, cannot quite bring itself to face.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Tea Party movement represents one of the most successful modern examples of participatory democracy, yet it is conspicuously ignored in progressive histories of the concept.
  2. 02Participatory democracy is a set of techniques and amplifiers rather than a guarantee of progressive outcomes, as evidenced by its use in tax revolts and the spread of populist anger.
  3. 03True commitment to participatory democracy requires accepting pluralism and the reality that increased public participation will often lead to outcomes that progressives find abhorrent.
  4. 04The most effective and sustainable forms of participatory democracy are often local, practical, and bounded, rather than the sweeping national movements Hayden celebrates.
  5. 05By framing participatory democracy as an exclusively progressive legacy, Hayden undermines the Port Huron Statement’s claim that the impulse to participate is a transcendent human need.
