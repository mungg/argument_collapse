Diversified Claude

"The responsibility of intellectuals remains rooted in the traditional liberal commitment to objective truth-telling and the defense of institutional norms rather than a shift toward partisan, 'organic' intellectualism."

Robin D. G. Kelley has written a powerful and learned essay, and like much of his work it should be read carefully even by those of us—I count myself among them—who disagree with significant parts of it. The recovery of Black antifascism in the 1930s and '40s is a genuine intellectual service; St. Clair Drake, William Patterson, and Claudia Jones deserve the central place Kelley gives them. So does his account of the donor-driven repression of pro-Palestinian speech on campus, which has been a slow-motion scandal of liberal cowardice for two decades. On that he is unambiguously right, and I have signed letters and joined defenses to that effect.

But the framing essay also asks readers to accept several large claims that, on inspection, I think do not hold up. They concern (1) the diminishment of truth-telling as a political resource, (2) the conceptual elasticity of "fascism" and "genocide," and (3) the implication that the proper response is to abandon what Kelley dismisses as the liberal posture of the autonomous intellectual in favor of frank partisanship within a movement. I want to take each in turn, and to defend a position Kelley treats as quaint: that Chomsky's injunction to expose lies remains the indispensable starting point, and that loosening it weakens rather than strengthens the antifascist project.

Start with the diagnosis of "post-truth." Kelley writes that "whatever power truth-telling might have had against fascism has been radically diminished," and lists a series of well-established facts—immigrants do not depress wages, the 2020 election was not stolen, the war in Gaza does not make Jews safer—that have failed to move the political needle. The implication is that since reasoned demonstration has proved insufficient, we should stop relying on it and turn to "casting our lot with the insurgent class." But notice what is happening in this argument. The very examples Kelley uses to prove that facts no longer matter are themselves invocations of facts, which he expects his readers to accept because they are true and well-supported. The essay's own form refutes its thesis. Kelley wins his case against the Heritage Foundation by reasoned exposure of Heritage's lies. If he believed the public sphere were really post-truth, he could not have written this essay; he would have written a manifesto instead.

The more accurate description of our moment, I would suggest, is not that truth has lost its power but that the institutional channels through which truth travels have been deliberately destroyed—local newspapers, public broadcasting, academic freedom, civil service expertise—and that rebuilding them is itself the central antifascist task. The intellectual's job in such a moment is *more* like Chomsky's 1967 job, not less. Norman Finkelstein's tenure case was lost because his colleagues did not stand up for the principle of evaluating his work on the merits. The Palestine encampments were dispersed not because facts failed but because administrators chose to ignore facts—about the protests' character, about the war's conduct, about international humanitarian law—that were perfectly available to them. "Speak the truth and expose lies" is not a quaint injunction. It is the load-bearing wall of any serious resistance.

Second, the word "fascism." Kelley uses it throughout, sometimes carefully (citing the Black radical tradition that named lynch terror as fascist) and sometimes loosely (writing that "we are living in fascist times" as a general description of the present United States). I do not deny that the Trump movement contains fascist currents: there are open white nationalists in its intellectual orbit, the second administration has plainly attacked the rule of law, and the targeting of legal residents for deportation over speech is among the gravest civil-liberties crises in living memory. But the moment we have arrived at is not, in fact, Mussolini's Italy or Hitler's Germany or even Franco's Spain. Courts have ruled against the administration repeatedly. The midterm elections will occur. Universities, however cowed, are not closed. The Times still prints what it prints. Hundreds of thousands of people protest without being shot.

Why does this matter? Because if we describe every authoritarian turn as fascism, we lose the conceptual ability to recognize the moment when actual fascism arrives—when courts are bypassed rather than defied, when elections are canceled rather than contested, when dissident professors are killed rather than fired. We also lose the ability to make the coalitional argument that wins democratic majorities: that a great many Americans who are not on the left have an interest in preserving constitutional government against this administration's depredations. The Black antifascists Kelley cites used "fascism" as a precise term of analysis, naming the kinship of Jim Crow lynching with European fascism through a shared logic of racial state violence. When the word becomes shorthand for "very bad current government," it can no longer do that work.

The same caution applies, with even more urgency, to the word "genocide." Kelley uses it as settled fact about Gaza. I think Israel's conduct in this war has been catastrophic, criminal in significant respects, and shameful in ways that will haunt the Jewish people for generations. I have said so publicly and at cost. But "genocide" is a legal term with a specific meaning, currently under adjudication at the International Court of Justice, and its application to this case is contested by serious scholars of genocide—not all of whom are propagandists for Israel. There are excellent historical and moral arguments on both sides of that classification. To treat the matter as closed and then to use that closure to indict American liberal institutions wholesale is to do exactly what Kelley accuses the right of doing: deciding what is true on the basis of one's prior political commitments. If the rule is that we choose our side and let truth follow, then the right's truth-following is not different in kind from ours, only different in content. That cannot be the standard.

Third, the figure of the organic intellectual. Kelley's Gramscian turn is the philosophical core of his essay: traditional intellectuals serve hegemony, organic intellectuals fight for their class, and the responsibility of the intellectual now is to be the second sort. There is something attractive in this; it has the bracing clarity of partisanship. But it also has costs that Kelley does not fully acknowledge. The organic intellectual model assumes that one's "side" is identifiable, coherent, and right—that the movement one joins has worked out its politics sufficiently that subordination to its line is intellectually defensible. This was not, in fact, the experience of the figures Kelley most admires. Du Bois's late embrace of Stalin's Soviet Union is not a small footnote; it is an embarrassment that should make us cautious about the model of the intellectual who has chosen a side and made discipline a virtue. Robeson, similarly, defended the Moscow trials. Claudia Jones operated under Comintern discipline that required her to reverse positions on the war between 1939 and 1941. These were brave and admirable people whose courage in standing against American racism was inseparable from their willingness to be wrong, sometimes catastrophically, about Stalinism. The "organic intellectual" who casts her lot with a movement and absorbs its discipline is not, historically, more reliable than the liberal truth-teller; she is differently unreliable, in ways that ought to give us pause.

This is why I want to defend, against Kelley, the much-maligned figure of the intellectual whose first loyalty is to honest description. Chomsky himself is the great example. He has been wrong about important things—Cambodia in 1977 most notoriously—and his stubborn factual seriousness has been what allowed his critics to identify and correct those errors, and what allowed him to correct them himself. The same temperament that produced his analyses of Indochina produced his unsparing accounts of Israeli policy decades before it was respectable to publish them. He was not picking a side; he was applying a method consistently. That method is precisely what is most precious about the liberal intellectual tradition, and what fascism, whether actual or aspirational, most wants to destroy.

None of this means that intellectuals should refuse solidarity or stay above politics. Kelley is correct that the choice to remain "neutral" is itself a political choice and almost always a complicit one. The professors who said nothing as their students were arrested, the administrators who let donors dictate hiring, the deans who suspended programs to placate a hostile administration—these are failures of nerve that deserve the condemnation Kelley delivers. But the alternative to complicit silence is not partisan commitment; it is principled commitment to specific norms—academic freedom, due process, factual accuracy, equal treatment—that can be defended across political coalitions and that the right is currently systematically destroying. The defense of those norms is not a centrist evasion. It is the substantive work.

Where Kelley and I most clearly agree is on the corruption of campus governance by donor money and federal blackmail, the disgraceful treatment of pro-Palestinian students and faculty, and the urgent necessity of organizing—through unions, through legal defense funds, through coalitions that link academic freedom to broader civil liberties. Where we differ is on the meaning of that organizing. For Kelley it is the work of an insurgent class fighting fascism. For me it is the work of constitutionalists, in something like the older American sense, defending the practices of free inquiry and democratic self-government against an administration that despises both. I think that frame draws a wider tent and offers a more durable politics. I also think it is what the situation actually demands. The responsibility of intellectuals in our moment is the one Chomsky named in 1967 and again in 2011: speak the truth, expose the lies, and accept the consequences. Everything Kelley wants—solidarity, courage, organization—follows from that, and is corrupted without it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'post-truth' diagnosis is flawed because the power of facts persists; the real crisis is the destruction of the institutional channels through which truth travels.
  2. 02Overusing the terms 'fascism' and 'genocide' as general descriptors for current events diminishes their analytical precision and hinders the ability to build broad democratic coalitions.
  3. 03The 'organic intellectual' model, which prioritizes movement loyalty over autonomous inquiry, historically leads to intellectual errors and the defense of authoritarianism.
  4. 04Principled commitment to academic freedom, due process, and factual accuracy provides a more durable and inclusive framework for resistance than insurgent partisanship.
  5. 05Chomsky’s original injunction to expose lies is not a 'quaint' relic but the essential foundation for any serious opposition to authoritarianism.
Diversified Claude

"Intellectuals must maintain independent judgment and a commitment to objective truth-telling rather than subordinating their work to movement discipline or political solidarity."

There is much to admire in Robin D. G. Kelley's essay, and I want to mark that at the outset because what follows is a sustained disagreement with one of its central moves. Kelley's recovery of the Black antifascist tradition—Drake, Cayton, Patterson, Jones, Du Bois, Robeson—is genuinely valuable. His account of Project Esther and the donor capture of campus governance is grimly accurate. His refusal to let universities off the hook for their treatment of pro-Palestinian students is morally correct, and I share it. But the essay also performs a maneuver that I think we should resist, even, and especially, when it comes from someone whose larger politics we admire. That maneuver is the claim that the responsibility of intellectuals in our moment is to "cast our lot with the insurgent class and enter the struggle"—that is, to subordinate intellectual work to movement discipline because the conditions are too grave for any other posture.

I want to argue, against this, that the gravest conditions are precisely those in which the discipline of independent judgment is most necessary, and that abandoning that discipline—even in the name of solidarity—is one of the principal ways twentieth-century intellectuals enabled the catastrophes they thought they were opposing.

Consider Kelley's own examples more closely than he does. W. E. B. Du Bois, near the end of his life, wrote that Stalin "was a great man; few other men of the twentieth century approach his stature." Paul Robeson defended the Moscow show trials. Claudia Jones operated within a Communist Party discipline that required her to support the Hitler-Stalin pact in 1939 and to oppose it in 1941—not because the facts had changed but because Moscow's line had. These were people of extraordinary courage who suffered grave injustices at the hands of the American state, and their critique of American racism and American empire was largely correct. But their willingness to be Communist Party loyalists at the height of Stalin's terror is not a footnote we can elide. It is part of what their choice of "the organic intellectual" model actually looked like in practice. The model worked best when one's chosen movement happened to be right about the most important thing. It worked very badly when one's chosen movement was complicit in mass murder, which the international Communist movement was, on a scale the participants spent decades denying.

This is not a gotcha. It is a reminder that the alternative to the autonomous truth-teller is not the heroic militant. It is the militant who must, sometimes, defend the indefensible because her side has committed it, and who has, by the logic of organic intellectuality, no place to stand outside her side from which to see this. The temptation of Kelley's framework is that it lets us imagine our movement will be different—our solidarity more honest, our discipline less corrosive. Maybe. But that hope cannot ground a general theory of intellectual responsibility. It can only be tested, painfully, case by case, by intellectuals who retain the capacity to see when their side has gone wrong.

I write as a Jewish scholar of the left, and I want to take up one place where I think Kelley's argument especially needs disagreement. He writes, of Dwight Macdonald's 1953 footnote on the Palestinian refugees: "Today this passage would be considered antisemitic, its author subject to investigation, libelous condemnation by the Anti-Defamation League." This is meant to demonstrate the closure of permissible speech in the present. But the passage is not antisemitic by any serious standard, and very few people I know on the Jewish left would call it so. Macdonald's footnote is a measured statement of fact and moral judgment that distinguishes carefully between the Nakba and the Holocaust ("This expropriation cannot, of course, be put on the same plane as the infinitely greater crime of the Nazis"). It is the kind of thing said routinely in *Jewish Currents*, in *+972*, in *Jewish Voice for Peace* spaces, at Jewish Studies conferences. The notion that uttering it now would invite ADL-led professional destruction is overstated, and the overstatement does political work: it frames criticism of Israel as automatically suppressed by a coordinated Jewish power structure, which is not quite the picture, and which feeds tropes we should be vigilant about.

The picture is more complicated and in some ways worse. There is real and escalating repression of pro-Palestinian speech on American campuses, and Kelley documents it well. But the agents of that repression are not principally Jewish institutions, however much certain Jewish institutions have shamefully participated. They are donors of varied backgrounds, congressional Republicans, Christian Zionists, university lawyers worried about Title VI suits, and—now—a federal administration whose interest in Jewish safety is, as Kelley himself notes, opportunistic at best and theologically annihilationist at worst. To name the ADL repeatedly while saying relatively little about the Christian Zionist apparatus that is the actual engine of state-level repression is to misallocate the analysis. It is also to risk reproducing, even unintentionally, the older conflation of Jews with the power they are alleged to wield.

I think Kelley would agree with this in conversation. The reason it matters in the text is that the essay is doing something subtler than its surface argument suggests. It is making the case that the antifascist tradition Kelley wants to recover is continuous with the present anti-Zionist movement, such that the latter inherits the moral authority of the former. Some of that inheritance is legitimate—the Non-European Unity Movement's comparison of South African racial rule to Nazism, Césaire's *Discourse on Colonialism*, Patterson's *We Charge Genocide!* all named structural likenesses that the postwar West preferred to ignore. But the inheritance is not unproblematic. Black antifascism in the 1930s and '40s was emphatically philosemitic in significant respects—the Communist Party USA was disproportionately Jewish, Patterson and Robeson were close to Jewish comrades, the Scottsboro defense ran through Jewish lawyers and Jewish-funded organizations, the antifascism of City College that Kelley invokes was a specifically Jewish working-class antifascism. The Black-Jewish radical alliance that built the prewar antifascist movement is one of the great achievements of the American left, and it was not built on the assumption that Jewish political agency was suspect. The current moment has frayed that alliance, and a serious essay on the antifascist tradition has to face that fraying squarely. Kelley's does not, quite.

This brings me to my broader disagreement. Kelley's essay treats "choosing a side" as the moral payoff of his argument. But the side it chooses is, on inspection, drawn fairly narrowly, and the boundaries are policed in ways that I think weaken rather than strengthen the antifascist coalition we actually need. The Jewish liberal who is appalled by Netanyahu, supports a ceasefire, and is unwilling to call Israel a settler-colonial entity in the simple sense; the labor unionist who is anti-Trump but skeptical of campus encampments; the moderate Republican judge enforcing injunctions against the administration; the African-American church-goer who is socially conservative and politically anti-fascist—these are the actual constituents of an antifascist majority in the United States in 2025. The Gramscian "organic intellectual" framework, as Kelley deploys it, has trouble making room for them. It tends to identify them, instead, as objects of correction—people whose consciousness is not yet sufficiently advanced.

If I have learned anything from the actual antifascist tradition Kelley invokes, it is that successful antifascism is broader than the left and uglier than we would like. The Popular Front of the 1930s, for all its compromises and Stalinist distortions, was right that fascism could be defeated only by an alliance that included people one did not agree with about most things. The same is true now. An antifascist coalition that requires its members to accept that Israel is committing genocide, that the United States is already a fascist state, that Zionism is racism, and that picking a side is the threshold of moral seriousness, is an antifascist coalition too small to win. It is, in fact, precisely the kind of left sectarianism that the Black antifascists of the 1930s sometimes practiced and sometimes overcame, depending on whether they were following Comintern instructions or their own judgment.

Where I think Kelley most needs to be answered is on the substitution of organization for truth-telling. He frames this as additive—we must do both—but his rhetoric tilts heavily toward the former, and his dismissal of Chomsky's injunction as "incomplete" risks reading as a permission slip for tendentious advocacy disguised as analysis. I want to insist the other way around: in this moment, the discipline of honest description is the most antifascist thing an intellectual can offer her movement. Fascism's signature epistemic move is the collapse of the distinction between description and allegiance—true things become true because our side says them. Any antifascism worth the name must refuse that collapse, even at the cost of being less effective in the short term and less popular within one's own coalition.

This is a hard position to hold, and it is unfair in obvious ways: it asks more discipline from the dissident than from the regime. But that asymmetry is built into the situation. The regime can lie because it has power; we can only build power by being trustworthy. The Black antifascists Kelley admires understood this. *We Charge Genocide* was a documentary indictment, footnoted to the hilt, written for a UN body that they knew would not act, precisely because they knew that the record would matter. *Black Metropolis* was a sociology before it was a politics. The reason these works retain their force is that they did not bend their facts to their commitments; they let their commitments be shaped by what the facts compelled.

The responsibility of intellectuals in the age of fascism and genocide, then, is not to choose a side and submit to its discipline. It is to do the work that makes a side worth choosing—the work of telling the truth about it, including about the parts that are inconvenient, and including especially when the truth costs us friends. That is what the best of Kelley's heroes did when they were at their best, and it is what the worst of them failed to do when they failed. It is also, I think, what Chomsky meant in 1967 and meant again in 2011. The injunction is not incomplete. It is hard. We should not weaken it because it is hard.
