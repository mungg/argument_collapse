"The United States should expand and diversify its official federal holidays to better reflect the nation's religious and cultural pluralism."

Yes, the United States should have more and different official holidays, because the present calendar still tells an old story about who counts as fully American. It is not enough to say that everyone is free to celebrate Mawlid, Lunar New Year, Orthodox Christmas, Diwali or Yom Kippur on private time. Public calendars are civic textbooks. Children learn from them which events stop the nation and which events their families must quietly fit around work, school and exams.

The answer is not to add every observance mentioned in a winter almanac. But a country as large and religiously varied as ours can afford a more generous and representative calendar. The federal government now recognizes ten annual holidays. That is not an extravagant number by international standards, and Americans are hardly overburdened with paid leave. For many workers, especially hourly workers, the real problem is not too many holidays but too few days in which family, worship and community can take precedence over the time clock.

A first step would be to add one or two holidays that reflect major communities not now visible in the federal calendar. Lunar New Year is an obvious candidate: it is celebrated by Chinese, Korean, Vietnamese and other Asian-American communities, and it is already a major public event in many American cities. For Muslims, Eid al-Fitr may be a better choice than Mawlid because it is more widely observed across traditions, though the question’s mention of Mawlid makes the same point: Islam is part of the American religious landscape, yet the federal calendar gives no sign of it. If adding days is politically impossible, then trade. Columbus Day, which honors a figure many Americans associate with conquest and dispossession, could be replaced. Washington’s Birthday could be folded into a broader civic commemoration that does not require a separate day off.

Recognition need not always mean a complete national shutdown. Congress and the president already create observances, commemorative months and proclamations. Schools and agencies can acknowledge holidays without closing every office. But paid holidays matter because time is the most concrete form of respect. A greeting from the president is nice; the ability to gather with one’s family without penalty is better.

The fear that inclusion will become an endless competition is real but overstated. Government makes line-drawing decisions all the time. Population, depth of observance, national distribution and civic significance are reasonable criteria. The current calendar is not neutral; it is merely familiar. Revising it would not fragment America. It would tell a truer story about a nation whose people worship, remember and celebrate in more than one historical key. A shared country does not require a uniform culture. It requires public institutions capacious enough to recognize the people who are already here.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Public calendars serve as civic textbooks that signal which communities and traditions are considered central to American identity.
  2. 02The current number of federal holidays is not excessive by international standards, and many workers lack sufficient time for family and community observance.
  3. 03Adding holidays like Lunar New Year or Eid al-Fitr would provide concrete respect and visibility to major communities currently ignored by the federal calendar.
  4. 04If adding new holidays is not feasible, the government should replace or consolidate existing holidays like Columbus Day to make room for more representative commemorations.
  5. 05A more inclusive calendar would not fragment the country but would instead create public institutions that accurately recognize the diversity of the American people.
