Diversified Gemini

"Joseph Carens’s time-based framework for amnesty is insufficient because it fails to address how the threat of deportation is weaponized to facilitate gender-based violence and exploitation against undocumented women."

Joseph Carens provides a valuable critique of the punitive nature of deportation, but his framework remains noticeably gender-blind. By focusing primarily on the traditional narrative of the male migrant worker (embodied by Miguel Sanchez) or the male breadwinner (Hiu Lui Ng), Carens overlooks the specific, often hidden ways in which the current immigration regime disproportionately harms undocumented women. A feminist analysis of immigration reveals that amnesty is not merely a matter of recognizing "social membership" over time, but an urgent necessity to protect women from systemic, gender-based violence and exploitation.

The vulnerability that Carens describes—the fear of deportation—does not manifest equally across genders. For undocumented women, this vulnerability is frequently weaponized by abusive partners and exploitative employers. In domestic violence situations, abusive partners routinely use the threat of calling Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) to trap undocumented women in violent homes. Because reaching out to law enforcement carries the risk of deportation and separation from their children (who are often U.S. citizens), undocumented women are effectively cut off from the legal protections and shelters available to native-born women.

Similarly, in the workplace, undocumented women are heavily concentrated in unregulated sectors like domestic work, childcare, and agriculture. These isolated environments are ripe for sexual harassment and wage theft. The fear of deportation ensures silence, creating a permanent class of women whose bodies and labor can be exploited with absolute impunity.

Carens argues that time should be the main criteria for regularization. However, waiting five to seven years for legal status is a death sentence for a woman trapped with an abusive partner or a predatory employer. A time-based threshold ignores the acute, daily dangers faced by female migrants.

Furthermore, Carens's reliance on "family ties" as an accelerator for legal status—such as marrying a citizen—reinforces patriarchal dependencies. While marriage should certainly protect families from separation, conditioning a woman's legal status on her relationship to a citizen spouse recreates the very power imbalances that lead to abuse.

A just immigration policy must recognize that the criminalization of migration creates unique vectors of gendered violence. Amnesty should not be framed merely as a reward for long-term assimilation; it must be understood as an essential mechanism for restoring bodily autonomy and basic civil rights to millions of women. We must advocate for immediate regularization not just to recognize their "membership," but to dismantle the legal architecture that enables their systematic abuse.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The fear of deportation is used by abusive partners to trap undocumented women in violent domestic situations by cutting them off from legal and social protections.
  2. 02Undocumented women in unregulated labor sectors, such as domestic work, face heightened risks of sexual harassment and wage theft because their legal vulnerability ensures their silence.
  3. 03A five-to-seven-year waiting period for regularization is dangerously long for women facing acute, daily physical dangers from predatory employers or partners.
  4. 04Conditioning legal status on marriage to a citizen reinforces patriarchal dependencies and creates power imbalances that can lead to further abuse.
  5. 05Amnesty should be framed as an immediate mechanism for restoring bodily autonomy and civil rights rather than a reward for long-term social membership.
