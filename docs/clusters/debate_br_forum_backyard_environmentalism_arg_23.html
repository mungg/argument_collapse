The Information Paradox in Performance-Based Regulation

Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen make information availability the centerpiece of their new regulatory architecture. The Toxics Release Inventory demonstrates how mandatory disclosure can discipline polluters through reputational mechanisms. TURA extends this logic by requiring firms to develop reduction plans based on their own monitoring data. The authors argue that information pooling across localities allows regulators to establish progressively more demanding standards.

Yet this emphasis on information reveals a fundamental paradox. The authors criticize market-based approaches partly on grounds that "artificial markets, created from the center, the information must first be accumulated (or specified) by the regulator." But the rolling-rule regime faces essentially similar challenges. How do local actors determine what data to collect? Who establishes the metrics by which performance will be assessed? How are baseline conditions determined against which improvement is measured?

Consider the Chesapeake Bay Program's evolution through successive phases of monitoring and target-setting. The 1987 Agreement established a 40 percent nutrient reduction goal by 2000. When further studies revealed that loadings in various tributaries had "differential impacts," parties revised their system-wide goals and developed tributary-specific targets. This adaptive revision is presented as a virtue—evidence that the regime responds to new scientific understanding. But consider what it implies. The original 40 percent target was established with apparently insufficient information about tributary-specific dynamics. Why should we have greater confidence in the revised tributary strategies? Each adjustment may itself rest on incomplete understanding that subsequent learning will reveal as inadequate.

The authors acknowledge that "the more we learn about the ecology of the Bay, the more surprising new findings will be." If this is true, then the entire project of establishing performance targets and monitoring progress becomes uncertain. If knowledge is fundamentally incomplete and subject to continuous revision, on what basis are current benchmarks established? The pragmatic philosophy the authors embrace offers no guidance beyond "institutionalize better ways of learning from the inevitable surprises that experience offers us." But this is circular—learning is defined as discovering what we need to learn to improve learning, and improvement is defined relative to learning. The framework provides no external criteria for evaluating whether particular learning processes are adequate.

Furthermore, the quality of information generated by local actors depends on their capacity and incentives. TURA works, in part, because Massachusetts established a Toxics Use Reduction Institute to develop planning curricula and provide technical assistance. Small firms in other states may lack comparable resources. The authors note that HCPs vary enormously in their monitoring quality—"local circumstance, seldom corrected by national discipline, determines whether an HCP monitors its progress well or poorly." If monitoring quality varies so dramatically, how can information pooling produce reliable standards? Poor local data will generate unreliable conclusions when aggregated and compared.

The INPO example the authors invoke is instructive. They argue that INPO succeeded only when it placed self-monitoring "under the aegis of public institutions and authority." But INPO operated in a domain—nuclear power generation—where firms had strong incentives to avoid catastrophic accidents that would destroy their investments and threaten their survival. Environmental compliance lacks comparable self-disciplining incentives. Firms may report favorable data to avoid regulatory scrutiny, to satisfy community expectations, or to maintain competitive positions. The authors acknowledge that TRI's reputational penalties give managers "strong incentives to either reduce their toxics emissions or shade their reporting estimates to appear cleaner than they are." If reporting can be "shaded" in this fashion, what confidence can we place in the information infrastructure on which the entire rolling-rule regime depends?

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The rolling-rule regime faces the same information-gathering challenges that the authors use to criticize market-based approaches, specifically regarding who sets metrics and baselines.
  2. 02The adaptive nature of the framework implies that current performance targets are likely based on insufficient information, making it impossible to determine if revised benchmarks are actually more accurate or effective.
  3. 03The pragmatist philosophy of 'continuous learning' lacks external criteria to evaluate whether the learning process itself is adequate or leading to genuine environmental improvement.
  4. 04The quality of information generated is highly dependent on local capacity and resources, leading to inconsistent data that cannot be reliably pooled to create national standards.
  5. 05Unlike high-stakes industries like nuclear power, environmental regulation lacks sufficient self-disciplining incentives to prevent firms from shading or falsifying their reported data to avoid scrutiny.
