"The strategy to defeat fossil capital must integrate racial and colonial justice into its first step rather than deferring them, because fossil capital and the state are inextricably linked through a system of racial capitalism."

Olúfẹ́mi O. Táíwò’s call for a twenty-first-century two-step—first dethrone fossil capital, then transform the world—is bracingly clear and strategically urgent. It avoids the fatalism of those who believe the state can never be countenanced, yet it refuses the naive electoralism that imagines winning a few elections can deliver climate justice. But Táíwò’s framing risks an abstraction that, if uncorrected, will reproduce the very exclusions that have long hamstrung left movements And it does so precisely because of how it treats the state and fossil capital as ontologically separate from racial capitalism.

Táíwò after rehearsing the debates between state-oriented and state-skeptical tendencies, casts the primary antagonism of our era as one between the public and fossil capital, calling on the public to first seize state power to dethrone that fraction of capital This narrative while compelling treats the public as a relatively undifferentited whole, elidiing the ways in which racism and colonialism are not side effects of fossil capital but co-constiutive with it. He nods to this by acknowledging that racial justice cannot be won in a world where fossil capital still reigns but his strategic conclusion is simply to get on with the first step: dethrone fossil capital. What he misses is that fossil capital in the Global North and the United States in particular, cannot be dethroned without simultaneously confronting the state’s deep investment in racial and colonial power.

Consider the extractive economy not as a separate sphere called fossil capital but as a racialized system of expropriation from its inception Oil refineries are placed in Black and brown communities; the enforcement of extraction is organized through police and military units disproportionately deployed against Indigenous land defenders and colonized peoples worldwide The U.S. state itself—its carceral and military infrastructure—is a vast consumer of fossil fuels and a defender of overseas extraction To dethrone fossil capital, would we not also have to dismantle the apparatus that consumes its product most voraciously? Táíwò’s two-step treats the state as a machine that can be captured and turned against one fraction of capital But what if the same racial capitalist state that enriches ExxonMobil also subjugates the Black, Indigenous, and post-colonial populations fighting for climate justice? Isn’t the state already aligned with certain fractions of capital, including racialized labour discipline? The “public” of Táíwò’s aspirations is deeply fissured, its solidification can’t be deferred to after the first step, because the very process of building power to dethrone fossil capital will set the pattern for the new order.

The Sunrise Movement and the Justice Democrats have learned this lesson the hard way: focusing on climate policy alone, without a clear and present commitment to racial and economic justice in the immediate ask, weakens the coalition Táíwò points to recent windfalls like the Arkansas solar-schools revenue story and New York’s public renewables act, but these victories were won not by a climate-only frame but by anchoring the energy transition in good union jobs and community benefits When Los Angeles set its 100% renewable target, it did so in tandem with equity and job-quality provisions The two-step as Táíwò articules it not only risks defrring justice to the second step, but misses that the first step can only succeed if it is already a just process Coalition scholars like Hana A. Giridharadas have shown that transformative change only sticks when the people building the new system have a material stake in its outcome.

This leads to a strategic imperative that modifies the two-step. To dethrone fossil capital, the coalition must be held together by a commitment to racial justice now, not later This means the first step must integrate demands for energy democracy with the dismantling of racist carceral state structures that secure fossil capital In Táíwò’s account, state capacities are not just destructive but creative. But if those capacities remain enclined to destructive racial capitalism, even the new public energy systems will reflect the same patterns of sacrifice zones—this time for lithium mines and damns. The two-step must be a unified step: we decardonize by building a care-full, reparative state that directly redreses the harms of racial ecocide rather than simply turning a previously dirty machine to clean ends

The strategic imperative for the climate left is not to wait for a post-fossil world to pursue racial justice Rather we need to build the coalitional power that makes racial justice central to the first step, recognizing that the state we need does not yet exist in full It will have to be assembled through struggle that simultaneously attacks fossil capital, transforms labour, and dismantles racist state apparatuses Not two steps, but one spiraling movement toward a just envionment that is impossible without the transformation of the state from a weapon to a steward Only by insisting on racial justice as a constitutive element of dethroning fossil capital can we build enough power to do any of it Táíwò’s genius is in his call for strategic clarity; but the strategy must be intersectional or it will fail.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Fossil capital is not an independent entity but a racialized system of expropriation that relies on the state's carceral and military infrastructure for enforcement.
  2. 02Treating the 'public' as an undifferentiated whole ignores the internal fissures caused by racism and colonialism that must be addressed to build a viable political coalition.
  3. 03Historical and contemporary climate victories demonstrate that transformative change only succeeds when energy transitions are anchored in immediate material benefits for marginalized communities and unions.
  4. 04Without dismantling the racist structures of the state during the decarbonization process, new green energy systems will simply reproduce the same 'sacrifice zones' and patterns of exploitation.
  5. 05The 'two-step' model should be replaced by a unified, intersectional movement that simultaneously attacks fossil capital and transforms the state from a weapon of racial capitalism into a steward of care.
