"While the responding essay agrees that state power is necessary for a climate transition, it argues that prioritizing the defeat of fossil capital without simultaneously dismantling imperialist structures will merely replace fossil capitalism with a 'green imperialism' that continues to exploit the Global South."

In “Climate, State, and Utopia,” Olúfẹ́mi O. Táíwò argues forcefully that the planetary climate crisis requires the strategic seizure of state power to defeat fossil capital. Drawing on the legacy of anti-colonial thinkers like Samir Amin, Táíwò calls for a planetary "delinking" from fossil fuels, prioritizing an aggressive, state-managed energy transition above all other immediate radical goals. While Táíwò’s intervention is a welcome departure from the localized fatalism of much contemporary climate discourse, his application of Amin’s "delinking" concept strips it of its vital anti-imperialist core. In his singular focus on dethroning fossil fuels, Táíwò risks endorsing a model of state power that replaces fossil capitalism with a "green" imperialism, leaving the fundamental structure of Global North exploitation intact.

Táíwò rightly observes that we do not need to relitigate twentieth-century debates about national bourgeoisie revolutions. However, we absolutely must interrogate the geopolitical position of the state he expects to lead this transition. When Táíwò speaks of "the state" enacting massive energy transitions, he is primarily referencing the capacities of core capitalist states—the United States, the European Union, and China. If these core states simply direct their immense capacities toward decarbonization without fundamentally altering their economic relationship to the Global South, the resulting "green state" will continue to rely on the brutal extraction of value and resources from the periphery.

Amin’s original concept of "delinking" was not merely about changing the technological basis of an economy (e.g., swapping coal for solar). It was fundamentally about subordinate nations breaking free from the unequal exchange forced upon them by the imperial core. By re-appropriating "delinking" to mean merely ending our reliance on carbon, Táíwò sidesteps the impending geopolitical crisis of the transition.

A renewable energy grid and electrified transportation system require astronomical quantities of critical minerals: copper, lithium, cobalt, nickel, and rare earths. The vast majority of these resources are located in the Global South—in places like the Democratic Republic of the Congo, Chile, Bolivia, and Indonesia. If the US or European states execute Táíwò’s "step one" and successfully defeat domestic fossil capital, their next imperative will be to secure these critical minerals to fuel their public renewables programs. Under the current international order, this means core states will use their diplomatic, financial, and military power to enforce extractive, low-value-added mining in the Global South, perpetuating the exact dynamic of core-periphery exploitation Amin sought to destroy.

We can already see the architecture of this green imperialism taking shape. The United States and the EU are scrambling to secure supply chains, often at the expense of local sovereignty and ecological health in the mining regions. Furthermore, the financial mechanics of the transition are overwhelmingly stacked against the Global South. While core states can deficit-spend billions to build out green infrastructure, countries in the Global South are trapped in a crushing cycle of sovereign debt, often forced to expand fossil fuel extraction or deregulate mining just to service loans from Western creditors.

Táíwò mentions debt cancellation briefly in his essay, noting that it might expand flexibility for state governments. But from an anti-imperialist perspective, sovereign debt cancellation and the dismantling of the unequal exchange system are not auxiliary tactics; they are the absolute prerequisites for a just planetary transition. If we do not address the imperial structure of the global economy simultaneously with the energy transition, we are merely building a "green fortress" in the Global North, powered by the continued immiseration of the Global South.

Táíwò asserts that his strategy is opposed to "carbon reductionism," yet by placing the defeat of fossil capital as the sole primary objective before broader world-transforming goals, he essentially endorses a temporal reductionism. He suggests that we can secure the energy transition first, and address the legacies of colonialism and imperialism later. But for a farmer displaced by a lithium mine in the Andes, or a child laborer in a cobalt mine in the DRC, the transition is not a step toward utopia; it is a continuation of the same colonial violence they have endured for centuries.

A truly twenty-first-century politics cannot simply aim to align the state’s interests with the "broad public" of the domestic nation. We must demand an internationalist climate politics that forces core states to finance the transition globally through massive technology transfers, unrestricted climate reparations, and the democratization of international financial institutions like the IMF and World Bank.

Táíwò is right that we cannot abandon the state; the scale of the crisis demands its utilization. But we must be fiercely clear-eyed about the nature of the states we are dealing with. If the climate movement in the Global North focuses only on domestic public power and domestic decarbonization, it will become complicit in a new era of green neocolonialism. The true first step must be an internationalist solidarity that recognizes that a green transition built on imperial extraction is no transition at all.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The author's redefinition of 'delinking' focuses narrowly on technological shifts while ignoring Samir Amin’s original intent of breaking the unequal economic exchange between core and peripheral nations.
  2. 02A state-led transition in the Global North risks driving a new wave of colonial violence through the aggressive extraction of critical minerals like lithium and cobalt from the Global South.
  3. 03The financial architecture of the transition, including sovereign debt and IMF/World Bank policies, ensures that the Global South remains trapped in a cycle of exploitation unless these systems are dismantled as a prerequisite.
  4. 04The 'two-step' strategy incorrectly assumes that the energy transition can be separated from anti-imperialist struggles, whereas for the Global South, the transition is often a continuation of colonial extraction.
  5. 05A truly internationalist climate politics must demand climate reparations and technology transfers rather than focusing solely on domestic public power within wealthy nations.
