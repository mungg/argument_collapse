Diversified MiniMax

"The flourishing approach to equality is conceptually indeterminate, practically unworkable, and unacceptably paternalistic compared to the more coherent framework of liberal egalitarianism."

Sypnowich's critique of liberal egalitarianism is spirited and often insightful, but it ultimately fails to vindicate the alternative she proposes. The flourishing approach faces insuperable difficulties that her essay does not adequately address. Liberal egalitarianism, despite its acknowledged limitations, remains the most coherent framework for thinking about justice in complex societies.

The first problem is conceptual. Sypnowich defines flourishing as the appropriate metric of egalitarian concern, but she never adequately specifies what flourishing consists in. She gestures at Amartya Sen's work, invokes Marx's "all-round development," mentions William Morris's aesthetic concerns, and raises the importance of friendship and love. But these diverse elements never cohere into a unified account. How do we weigh material security against aesthetic fulfillment? How do we compare the flourishing of a contemplative monk with that of an activist entrepreneur? The flourishing approach requires a metric—a way of comparing how well different lives are going—but Sypnowich provides no such metric. Without it, the approach cannot guide policy.

Liberals face a similar challenge with their account of opportunity, but they have at least a procedural response: let individuals define their own conceptions of the good and provide the means for pursuing those conceptions. This "neutral" approach is not without difficulties, but it sidesteps the need for a comprehensive theory of well-being. Sypnowich explicitly rejects neutrality, arguing that it is impossible and perhaps undesirable. But she offers no alternative method for determining what constitutes flourishing. How does democratic deliberation solve this problem? Deliberation can establish minimum thresholds—everyone should have adequate nutrition, shelter, and education—but it cannot rank comprehensive conceptions of flourishing against each other. Different communities will reach different conclusions, and there is no meta-procedure for adjudicating among them.

The second problem concerns freedom. Sypnowich acknowledges that the flourishing approach involves shaping preferences and guiding choices, but she treats this as unproblematic. "Influence is inevitable," she argues, so we might as well use it carefully and transparently. But this response conflates two very different things: the inevitable fact that social environments shape preferences, and the deliberate use of state power to promote certain preferences over others. The first is a feature of human existence that we cannot escape; the second is a political choice that can be resisted. Liberal egalitarianism does not pretend that social influence can be eliminated, but it insists that the state should not deploy its considerable coercive power to shape preferences in particular directions. This is not naivety about the impossibility of neutrality; it is a principled commitment to respecting citizens as autonomous agents capable of forming their own conceptions of the good.

Sypnowich might respond that autonomy itself is a social product and that the liberal commitment to it reflects particular historical circumstances and ideological commitments. This is true. But we can acknowledge the social embeddedness of autonomy without concluding that the state should therefore actively promote particular visions of flourishing. Indeed, recognizing how deeply social influences shape us provides reason to be humble about our ability to distinguish genuine flourishing from social conformity. The flourishing approach risks substituting the state's judgment for the individual's in determining what constitutes a good life, and history suggests that states are poor judges of human flourishing.

The third problem is practical. Sypnowich acknowledges that perfect equality of outcome is impossible and that some outcome disparities reflect legitimate differences in talent, temperament, and choice. But she offers no guidance for distinguishing legitimate from illegitimate disparities. Her approach seems to require that we eliminate all outcome differences traceable to social conditions while preserving those resulting from individual choice. But this requires exactly the choice-circumstance distinction that she rightly criticizes as unstable. If we cannot reliably separate choices from circumstances—if all choices are partly shaped by social conditions—then we cannot implement a policy that compensates for circumstance-based disadvantages while leaving choice-based disadvantages unaddressed.

In practice, the flourishing approach will either degenerate into a form of comprehensive redistribution that equalizes almost everything (and thereby violates individual liberty) or collapse into a variant of luck egalitarianism that excuses large outcome disparities (and thereby fails to deliver flourishing equality). Sypnowich does not resolve this tension.

Finally, the association of flourishing equality with socialism is tendentious. Many societies with robust welfare states and significant redistribution—including the Nordic countries that Sypnowich implicitly invokes—remain fundamentally capitalist. They have not abolished private property in the means of production or established democratic control over investment decisions. If these societies have achieved something like flourishing equality, then socialism is not necessary. If they have not achieved it, then socialism is not sufficient. Either way, the conclusion that socialism is required does not follow from the premises.

Liberal egalitarianism has real limitations, but the alternative Sypnowich proposes is too indeterminate to guide action and too paternalistic to respect human freedom. We should refine liberal egalitarianism, not abandon it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The essay fails to provide a unified metric or definition of flourishing, making it impossible to compare different lives or guide public policy.
  2. 02By rejecting state neutrality, the flourishing approach risks using coercive state power to paternalistically shape individual preferences and override personal autonomy.
  3. 03The flourishing approach fails to solve the practical problem of distinguishing between legitimate and illegitimate disparities in outcomes, a critique the author ironically levels against liberal egalitarianism.
  4. 04The claim that achieving equal flourishing requires socialism is logically unsupported, as capitalist welfare states can achieve similar goals and socialism itself does not guarantee flourishing.
  5. 05While liberal egalitarianism has limitations, its procedural focus on providing the means for individuals to pursue their own goals is superior to the state-mandated visions of a good life.
Diversified MiniMax

"Sypnowich’s focus on equal flourishing as an outcome should be rejected because it necessitates a paternalistic and potentially authoritarian state that undermines individual freedom and the pluralism of the good life."

Sypnowich's argument rests on a confusion between opportunity and outcome that, if accepted, would justify a degree of state interference in human life that undermines the very values she claims to advance. Liberal society rests on a distinction between formal equality and substantive equality that is not a defect but a constitutive feature of a free society. Her flourishing approach, whatever its intentions, leads inevitably toward paternalism and the diminution of human freedom.

The argument for equality of opportunity begins from the recognition that human beings differ in their talents, preferences, and aspirations. These differences are not themselves injustices; they are features of the human condition. What would be unjust is if these natural differences were amplified by social barriers—discrimination, inherited privilege, arbitrary exclusion—that prevented people from pursuing their ends according to their abilities. The opportunity principle addresses precisely this form of injustice: it demands that social positions be open to all and allocated on the basis of relevant criteria, primarily merit.

Sypnowich objects that even formal equality of opportunity is insufficient if some start from positions of disadvantage. This is true, and the appropriate response is to address those disadvantages directly—through education, healthcare, income support—but without abandoning the principle that positions should be allocated on the basis of merit. The goal is not to equalize outcomes but to ensure that everyone has the means to develop their talents and compete fairly. Once that is achieved, the results of competition must be accepted, even when they are unequal.

The flourishing approach rejects this conclusion and insists that actual outcomes matter, not merely opportunities. But this leads to a fundamental problem: who determines what constitutes flourishing? Sypnowich gestures at pluralism—"flourishing comes in many forms"—but this gesture is insufficient. If flourishing is genuinely plural, then there is no way to determine whether someone is flourishing or not without imposing a particular vision of the good life. The state would need to evaluate whether a person's life meets some externally determined standard of flourishing and intervene when it does not. This is the definition of paternalism.

Consider the implications. A person chooses to live simply, to work part-time, to devote time to family or community rather than career advancement. Are they flourishing or not? The flourishing approach seems to require some answer to this question, and that answer will inevitably reflect controversial judgments about the value of different ways of life. Similarly, a person makes a series of imprudent choices—dropping out of school, accumulating debt, avoiding work—and ends up poor. Is this a failure of flourishing requiring remedy, or a consequence of choices the person was free to make? The flourishing approach cannot answer this question without recourse to contested assumptions about human well-being.

The liberal approach avoids this difficulty by respecting the distinction between opportunity and outcome. The state ensures that everyone has the education, health, and resources necessary to compete on equal terms; it does not then evaluate whether those who succeed are "flourishing" or whether those who fail are entitled to further assistance on grounds of insufficient flourishing. This is not because the liberal fails to care about human well-being but because the liberal recognizes that well-being is ultimately a personal project. The state can create conditions conducive to flourishing; it cannot guarantee flourishing without becoming tyrannical.

Sypnowich claims that this liberal stance is impossible—that the state inevitably makes judgments about the good life and that it is better to do so transparently and carefully. But this is a false dichotomy. There is a significant difference between the state shaping social conditions in ways that influence preference formation and the state evaluating whether individuals are flourishing well enough to deserve continued support. The first is ubiquitous and largely unavoidable; the second is a distinctive form of authoritarianism that the flourishing approach seems to require.

I do not claim that the liberal approach is perfect or that existing inequalities are acceptable. The world is full of injustice, and many people face disadvantages that are not of their own making. The appropriate response is to expand genuine opportunity—to ensure that all children have access to quality education, that all citizens have adequate healthcare, that discrimination is rigorously prohibited and prosecuted. These are substantial goals that require significant resources and political will. But they do not require the comprehensive equality of outcome that Sypnowich seems to demand. They require a commitment to formal equality and its material preconditions—a commitment that can be honored without embracing the paternalistic statism that the flourishing approach entails.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The distinction between opportunity and outcome is a constitutive feature of a free society that respects the reality of diverse human talents, preferences, and aspirations.
  2. 02A flourishing-based approach requires the state to impose a specific, controversial vision of the 'good life' to determine whether an individual's outcome is satisfactory.
  3. 03While the state should address initial disadvantages like education and healthcare to ensure fair competition, it must accept unequal results once those fair conditions are met.
  4. 04Holding the state responsible for individual flourishing ignores the fact that well-being is a personal project and risks becoming tyrannical by evaluating private life choices.
  5. 05The flourishing approach fails to distinguish between the unavoidable social influence on preferences and the unacceptable state evaluation of whether an individual is 'flourishing enough' to deserve support.
