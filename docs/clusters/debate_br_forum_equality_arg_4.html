Diversified DeepSeek

"The pursuit of equal flourishing through state-mandated equal outcomes is an authoritarian project that destroys individual liberty, human agency, and the prosperity generated by a free market."

Christine Sypnowich’s lead essay is a usefully candid statement of the authoritarian implications of egalitarianism pushed to its logical conclusion. While most defenders of “equal opportunity” attempt to hide their redistributive ambitions behind vague language, Sypnowich openly declares that what really matters is equal outcomes, specifically “equal flourishing.” She calls for the political community to actively shape citizens’ values, steer them toward approved pursuits, and ensure that everyone lives equally well, regardless of their choices. This vision is not a benign correction to heartless liberalism; it is a blueprint for a comprehensive tyranny that would destroy individual liberty, market prosperity, and the very pluralism she claims to value. A free society rests on the principle of equal opportunity—understood as the removal of legal coercion and discrimination—not on the forced equalization of life results.

The foundation of any just social order is self-ownership. Each individual has an exclusive right to their own body, mind, and the fruits of their honest labor. When the state intervenes to transfer resources from one person to another for the sake of equalizing flourishing, it is engaging in theft, however well-intentioned. Sypnowich dismisses the distinction between chosen and unchosen disadvantages, arguing that all choices are socially shaped and thus not truly free. But this denies human agency altogether. In a market society with a framework of equal rights, individuals make genuine choices all the time. Some choose to work longer hours, invest in high-risk ventures, or acquire scarce skills; others prefer leisure, security, or less demanding occupations. The resulting income and wealth differences are not injustices; they are the outcome of voluntary transactions reflecting different preferences and contributions. To treat a high-earning entrepreneur and a surfer who lives modestly as equally “flourishing” in need of equal resources is to ignore the fundamental moral fact that people are responsible for their decisions.

Sypnowich’s positive program would require a state of almost unimaginable scope and intrusiveness. To equalize flourishing, the state would need to determine exactly what flourishing consists of for each person and then redistribute accordingly, while continually adjusting for the endless variations in taste, luck, and effort. This would mean a total command economy where the authorities decide which careers are valuable and worthy of support, which leisure activities enrich the soul, and which forms of family life are conducive to well-being. Sypnowich tries to soften this by saying that “out and out coercion” should be inadmissible, but she supports “incentives, education, and supporting institutions.” In practice, a state empowered to set incentives and shape education will inevitably coerce; it is impossible to direct citizens toward approved ends without penalizing those who dissent. Her example of mandatory music education for children is a small instance; once the principle is conceded, why not mandatory physical exercise, mandatory dietary guidelines, mandatory training in the state’s preferred morality? The history of socialist and communist regimes proves the point: once the state assumes the role of guardian of the people’s flourishing, gulags are not far behind.

Sypnowich’s critique of the market is unfounded. The market is not a neutral mechanism but a discovery process that coordinates decentralized knowledge and generates innovation, lifting countless people from poverty. The vast inequalities she deplores in rich Western countries are, in fact, largely the result of government intervention that protects incumbents, restricts housing supply, or grants crony privileges—crony capitalism, not free markets. A genuine equal opportunity regime would eliminate these distortions, ensuring that everyone can enter any profession, start any business, and keep what they earn, without discrimination. Beyond that, voluntary charity and mutual aid can address residual hardship, preserving the community and solidarity Sypnowich wants without the coercion of state redistribution.

Even Sypnowich’s concern with solidarity is ill-served by her outcome-focused social engineering. Real community arises from voluntary cooperation and shared values freely chosen. When the state tries to engineer solidarity through forced redistribution, it breeds resentment and division, pitting recipients against taxpayers. The friendships and mutual care she admires in small communities cannot be scaled up to a national level by bureaucratic diktat. The attempt to do so destroys the very social capital it seeks to foster.

Sypnowich ends by calling for socialism, but she offers no details on how the means of production would be owned or controlled. Would it be state ownership? Worker cooperatives? The record of state socialism is one of economic stagnation, repression, and ecological ruin. Worker cooperatives can exist within a market framework, but they do not guarantee equal outcomes; some co-ops fail, others thrive. The only way to guarantee equal flourishing is to give a central planner the power to override all individual choices—the very definition of totalitarianism.

In a free society, the goal of public policy should be to secure life, liberty, and property under a general rule of law, so that individuals can pursue their own happiness in their own way. That is the original meaning of equal opportunity: no one is legally privileged or penalized for their group identity. Sypnowich is right that some people will fare worse even with equal legal rights, but that is not an injustice that demands state remedy. It is a condition of human existence, and it calls forth the voluntary compassion that makes a society truly decent. Her vision of equal flourishing is a siren song that would lead us to a deadening equality of poverty and servitude. We should reject it in favor of genuine freedom.

  3. 03Achieving equal flourishing would require an intrusive, totalizing state to determine and enforce a singular vision of the good life, inevitably leading to coercion and tyranny.
  4. 04The market is a vital discovery process for innovation and poverty reduction, and true equality of opportunity should focus on removing legal barriers and cronyism rather than redistributing results.

Diversified DeepSeek

"The responding essay argues that shifting from equality of opportunity to a state-mandated goal of equal flourishing is a dangerous move toward paternalistic despotism that threatens individual agency and pluralism."

Christine Sypnowich’s ambitious essay seeks to reorient egalitarian thought away from opportunity and toward equal flourishing. Her central insight—that what ultimately matters is how people actually live, not just the chances they are given—has undeniable moral appeal. But in her eagerness to correct the perceived hand-washing of liberal egalitarians, Sypnowich opens the door to a far more dangerous problem: paternalistic state power over our deepest values and life choices. A society that aims at equal flourishing, as she conceives it, would require the state to make judgments about what counts as a good life, and to encourage, incentivize, and sometimes disincentivize certain choices. This is not a path to solidarity and freedom, as she suggests; it is a path to a soft despotism that undermines the very pluralism and individual agency that any decent society must protect.

The core of Sypnowich’s argument against neutrality is that choices are “never made in a vacuum” and that all societies shape values. Therefore, she maintains, we should not pretend the state can be neutral; we should instead “embrace influence and use it carefully, publicly, and transparently” to promote what is genuinely valuable and to discourage what is harmful. Her example is music education: simply making music lessons available is not enough; we must require them, or at least heavily incentivize them, because without initiation people will not know what they are missing. This line of reasoning, while seductive, is a classic perfectionist fallacy. It overlooks the crucial difference between a society that provides enabling conditions for diverse pursuits and one that actively seeks to shape souls according to a particular vision of the good.

Sypnowich’s argument that liberal societies already promote some values and discourage others—state support for education and the arts, discouragement of smoking—is not a proof that neutrality is impossible, but rather a warning that state action in the realm of values must be carefully constrained by non-perfectionist reasons. Funding education is justified by the need to prepare citizens for democratic participation and economic self-sufficiency, not by a comprehensive judgment that learning poetry is intrinsically superior to learning comic books. Discouraging smoking rests on the negative externality of secondhand smoke and the health burden on public systems, not on a judgment that the life of a smoker is less flourishing. Sypnowich’s framework, by contrast, explicitly invites the state to decide which ways of life “warrant social support” and which do not. The room for abuse is vast.

Consider who would get to define “flourishing.” Sypnowich says we should have “legitimate, democratic debate about what counts as flourishing,” but she gives no indication of how such debate would be insulated from majoritarian bias or elite capture. In a society with deep cultural divisions, the outcome of that debate might well be a labeling of sexual minorities, single parents, or religious minorities as not flourishing, or in need of redirection. Sypnowich might protest that her pluralistic vision would welcome diverse forms of life. But pluralism in the abstract is no guard against its concrete violation. Once the legitimacy of the state promoting particular conceptions of the good is conceded, the machinery of promotion—public schooling, tax incentives, licensing, media subsidies—becomes a prize to be captured by whichever faction has the most power. The history of state paternalism is not a pretty one: from the forced assimilation of Indigenous children to the pathologizing of homosexuality under the guise of “well-being,” the state has a miserable track record when it presumes to steer its citizens toward true happiness.

Liberal neutrality, as defended by thinkers like Jonathan Quong, does not demand that the state be silent on values. It demands that the state justify its actions on grounds that all reasonable citizens can accept, regardless of their comprehensive doctrines. This allows for many of the policies Sypnowich favors—universal healthcare, generous social insurance, public parks—without requiring the state to endorse a particular meaning of life. For instance, we can fund mental health services because we recognize that depression severely impairs people’s ability to pursue their chosen ends, not because we have decided that cheerful people are more flourishing than melancholic introverts. The distinction may seem subtle, but it is the difference between a society that treats citizens as autonomous agents and one that treats them as subjects to be perfected.

Sypnowich attempts to preempt the charge of coercion by saying that “out and out coercion, including punishment or threats to punish, should be inadmissible.” But she then endorses “incentives, education, and supporting institutions” that guide people. In practice, the line between incentive and coercion can become wafer-thin. If the state provides generous subsidies only for careers it deems valuable and withholds support from others, it is effectively punishing those who choose differently. If the curriculum is designed to inculcate particular values, it is coercive to the extent that schooling is compulsory. Sypnowich’s reply that liberal societies already do this is not a justification but an indictment of overreach; the proper response is to limit the state’s perfectionist intrusions, not to sanctify them with a theory of equal flourishing.

Moreover, Sypnowich’s outcome-focus risks exactly the “leveling down” she decries. If flourishing is defined by some metric and some people stubbornly refuse to meet it, the state will be under constant pressure to intervene more aggressively. This is not hypothetical: the eugenics movement of the early twentieth century was fueled by a similar conviction that society should promote the “flourishing” of its population and discourage the propagation of the “unfit.” I am not accusing Sypnowich of endorsing eugenics, but the logic of state-managed flourishing, once untethered from robust rights constraints, can lead to terrible places.

None of this is to deny that inequality inflicts profound suffering and that current liberal democracies fail many of their citizens. But the answer is to strengthen the safety net, enforce genuine equal opportunity, and ensure that everyone has the real capability to live as they see fit—not to task the state with making everyone flourish equally. Sypnowich’s call for “socialism” as the realization of the egalitarian ideal is not a magic wand; it would only shift the power to define and enforce flourishing to an even more centralized authority. True solidarity and community cannot be commanded from above; they must grow from a culture of mutual respect and freedom. That culture is better protected by a state that declines to take sides on the question of the good life than by one that sets itself up as the gardener of souls.
