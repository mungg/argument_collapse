"The current U.S. strategy in Afghanistan is destined to fail because it repeats the same structural mistakes that led to the Soviet Union's defeat, specifically the attempt to impose a centralized client state on a rural society that rejects it."

Reading Rosen's essay, I kept reaching for an old battered copy of Lester Grau's translation of the Soviet General Staff study, *The Soviet-Afghan War: How a Superpower Fought and Lost*. The parallels are not exact. History does not repeat itself; it pastiches itself. But the resemblances are uncomfortable enough that the burden of proof should fall heavily on those who insist this time is different.

Consider what the Soviets attempted in Afghanistan between 1979 and 1989. They came in with overwhelming conventional force and quickly secured the cities. They installed a friendly government — first Karmal, then Najibullah — whose legitimacy never extended much beyond the urban centers and the road network. They tried, with considerable resources, to build an Afghan national army, eventually fielding something like 300,000 troops on paper. They invested in development projects, particularly in education and women's literacy, well beyond anything the current intervention has attempted. They cycled through tactical doctrines, moving from large-unit sweeps to small-unit hunter-killer teams to "Afghanization" under Najibullah. They struggled with rural insurgents who could melt into Pakistan, with corruption in their client state, with police forces that often sold their weapons to the resistance.

They lost.

It is fashionable in American policy circles to insist that the comparison is unfair — that we are different because we have better technology, better intentions, better counterinsurgency doctrine, and (the polite formulation) better strategic patience. Some of this is true. The Soviets had nothing like the precision munitions or surveillance capabilities of the modern American military. Their tactical brutality, particularly the depopulation of contested rural areas, has no direct American equivalent (yet). And it is true that the mujahideen had a sanctuary in Pakistan with active CIA support and Stinger missiles, whereas the current Taliban has only the first of those things.

But the structural similarities are striking. Both interventions installed an unpopular client government dependent on foreign arms. Both struggled to extend that government's reach beyond Kabul and the major provincial capitals. Both faced a rural insurgency in a country where two-thirds of the population is rural. Both attempted to build national security forces by quantity rather than quality, with predictably mixed results. Both found themselves protecting infrastructure projects and convoys rather than civilians. Both saw their tactical battlefield success failing to translate into political consolidation. Both became dependent on increasingly absurd narratives of progress to justify continued commitment.

Rosen alludes to the Soviet experience in passing — noting that police corruption was a huge problem then as now — but does not push the comparison as hard as he might. Let me push it.

The Soviets at their peak deployed approximately 115,000 troops to Afghanistan. The McChrystal plan brings American forces to around 100,000, with another 40,000 or so from coalition partners, for a total comparable to the Soviet commitment. The Soviet client army peaked at higher numbers than McChrystal proposes for the ANSF. The Soviet aid effort, in real terms, was likely larger than the Western developmental effort has been. The Soviet brutality was greater, but that worked against them more than it worked for them; their ability to depopulate areas like the Panjshir Valley did not produce strategic success.

What ultimately defeated the Soviets was not the Stinger missile, though that helped. What defeated them was the fundamental impossibility of imposing political consolidation on Afghan rural society from above through a client government, on a timetable shorter than a generation, with foreign troops as the visible enforcer. The mujahideen did not need to win battles; they needed to outlast the Soviet political will. Najibullah, who in retrospect was a more capable leader than Karzai, held on for three years after the Soviet withdrawal — but only by transforming himself into a kind of national-reconciliation figure, and only until the Soviet aid stopped flowing in 1992.

Rosen's most quietly devastating point in the entire essay is that the Afghan security forces, even if they reach McChrystal's 400,000 target, cannot be paid by the Afghan state when the ISAF subsidy ends. This is precisely what destroyed Najibullah. The withdrawal of Soviet aid in 1992 collapsed the Afghan state within months. Whatever government the United States leaves behind will face the same arithmetic problem on the same timeline. We will be subsidizing the Afghan security apparatus indefinitely, or we will not, in which case it will collapse and the men with the guns will go back to the side of the warlords or the Taliban depending on which is paying them that week. There is no third option that any serious analyst has been able to articulate.

This is why I think Rosen, even in his pessimism, is not pessimistic enough about the plan. The McChrystal blueprint does not have an exit. It has a transition fantasy, in which Afghan forces stand up while ours stand down, paid for by an Afghan state that has no plausible revenue base, governing a population that has never accepted central authority on the scale required. The Soviets discovered, painfully, that this transition cannot be executed because its preconditions cannot be created.

Where I differ from Rosen is in his closing turn toward "addressing problems at their roots" and his suggestion that the al Qaeda threat has been overstated. The Soviets did not leave Afghanistan because they decided Islamist militancy was a manageable threat. They left because the Soviet system was collapsing under economic and political pressures unrelated to Afghanistan. The American system is not collapsing. The argument for withdrawal, if it is to be made, has to be made on the merits of withdrawal itself — what we accept about the consequences, what we do about the risks Rosen waves away too quickly. The Soviet withdrawal produced a brutal civil war, the rise of the Taliban, and eventually 9/11. That is not an argument against withdrawal. It is an argument for being clear-eyed about what withdrawal entails, and for serious diplomatic and regional preparation of the ground.

The deepest lesson of the Soviet experience is not "do not intervene." It is "do not intervene without a plausible theory of how the intervention ends." We do not have one. The McChrystal plan is not such a theory; it is a refusal to confront the absence of one. Rosen's essay correctly identifies this absence. It does not yet face up to what facing the absence honestly would require.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The structural parallels between the Soviet and American interventions—including the reliance on unpopular client governments and the inability to control rural areas—suggest a similar inevitable failure.
  2. 02The plan to build a massive Afghan security apparatus is a 'transition fantasy' because the Afghan state lacks the revenue base to sustain such a force once foreign subsidies inevitably end.
  3. 03Tactical advantages like superior technology and precision munitions cannot overcome the fundamental political impossibility of consolidating power through a foreign-enforced central government.
  4. 04The McChrystal plan lacks a plausible exit strategy, instead relying on a cycle of 'Afghanization' that historically leads to state collapse once the occupying power withdraws its support.
  5. 05While withdrawal carries significant risks of civil war and instability, the current strategy is a refusal to confront the absence of a viable theory for how the intervention can successfully end.
