Vanilla Claude

"While the lead essay correctly identifies the dangers of the 'right to feel safe' standard, a meaningful defense of campus protest requires rebuilding the structural power of students and faculty rather than simply appealing to administrators for better principles or more tolerance."

Gourevitch is right that the recent campus crackdowns are unprecedented in their severity and that the "right to feel safe" framing has metastasized into something genuinely dangerous to political life. But his essay underplays a tension at the heart of his own argument: the right to protest he wants to defend is not really, or not only, a right against administrators. It is a right that depends on the kind of robust civil society—labor solidarity, organized publics, mass disobedience—that a half-century of neoliberal restructuring has gutted. Defending the right by appealing to administrators to behave better, or by exhorting students to tolerate more, won't reconstitute the conditions that made tolerant campuses possible in the first place.

Consider the 1969 comparison he draws. Four thousand arrests out of millions of protesters wasn't just a kinder administrative culture; it reflected the simple fact that universities then could not credibly threaten to ruin a student's life with expulsion and visa revocation, because students had outside options, because legal exposure was lower, and because faculties had real governance power. Today's punishment regime is so disproportionate partly because it can be. The credentialing chokehold on professional life, the precarity of international students under an authoritarian executive, and the collapse of tenured faculty governance all mean that even modest sanctions function as catastrophic deterrents. Any honest defense of campus protest has to grapple with this asymmetry, not just with the doctrinal slippage from "safe" to "feeling safe."

Gourevitch's critique of the subjective-harm standard is largely correct, and his observation that the same logic gets selectively applied—Muslim and Arab students' fears ignored, elite Jewish institutional voices privileged—is devastating. But I'd push him further: the problem isn't only that "hostile environment" doctrine has been stretched. It's that the entire infrastructure of Title VI enforcement was built to address structural exclusion of historically subordinated minorities, and is now being weaponized by the executive branch to discipline anti-war speech. The lesson isn't merely to insist on objective standards; it's to recognize that civil rights law, once routed through a politicized federal apparatus and donor-captured boards, can be turned against the very constituencies it was meant to protect.

Finally, Gourevitch's call for a "culture of toleration" is necessary but insufficient. Toleration is a disposition; it doesn't organize anyone. What protected protest in earlier eras wasn't tolerance but countervailing power—unionized faculty, organized students, a press willing to dissent. Rebuilding that, not just clarifying principles, is the harder task ahead.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The unprecedented severity of modern crackdowns is a result of the 'credentialing chokehold' and the collapse of faculty governance, which allow universities to use even modest sanctions as life-altering deterrents.
  2. 02The weaponization of Title VI civil rights law by the executive branch and donors demonstrates that legal protections for marginalized groups can be easily inverted to suppress anti-war speech.
  3. 03The historical tolerance for protest was not a product of administrative benevolence but of countervailing power from organized labor and student groups that has since been gutted by neoliberal restructuring.
  4. 04Appealing to a 'culture of toleration' is insufficient because tolerance is a personal disposition that cannot substitute for the organized political strength needed to protect dissent.
