**Where Gourevitch's Symmetry Breaks**

Gourevitch closes his essay with a principle that, he insists, applies regardless of one's views on Gaza: "whatever right protesters claim for themselves is a right they are also claiming for their enemies." This is the moral hinge of the argument. It is also where I want to press.

The principle is logically sound. If you want a robust right to occupy a quad in protest of Israeli policy, you must also tolerate a quad occupation by, say, an anti-trans group, an anti-abortion group, or a group celebrating the deportation of undocumented students. The right is either viewpoint-neutral or it is not a right. Gourevitch knows this; his examples of pro-life "Genocide Awareness" displays and conservative bake sales show that he is willing to bite the bullet.

But the symmetry breaks down at the level of institutional practice in ways that Gourevitch's essay does not fully address. Consider three asymmetries.

First, asymmetry of administrative response. Gourevitch notes that pro-Palestinian protesters have been punished while pro-Israel counter-protesters have largely not been, even when they engaged in similar behavior (and in some cases, like the UCLA attack on the encampment, considerably worse). He treats this as evidence that administrators are applying their standards unevenly under political pressure. True. But the question is what to do about it. A purely formal commitment to viewpoint neutrality will not, by itself, produce viewpoint-neutral outcomes when administrators are subject to asymmetric pressures from donors, trustees, and federal agencies. You can have the most beautiful First Amendment doctrine in the world and still find that, in practice, only one side gets prosecuted.

Second, asymmetry of social cost. Gourevitch's "right to be hostile" assumes that the discomfort of being protested is, in some rough sense, equally distributed across political positions. But protest targets the powerful, or at least it is supposed to. When pro-Palestinian students protest a Hillel speaker, they are exercising countervailing pressure against an institution backed by enormous resources and political weight. When pro-life activists post graphic images outside a Planned Parenthood, they are exercising countervailing pressure against a vulnerable population (women seeking abortions, many of them already in distress) backed by far less institutional support. The "right to be hostile" looks different in the two cases. Gourevitch's formal symmetry obscures this material asymmetry.

Third, asymmetry of consequence. Gourevitch wants us to tolerate protest because the alternative is administrative overreach. Fair. But some protests are themselves attempts to bring about administrative overreach. The "antiwoke" right has spent five years using protest, complaint, and public pressure to demand the firing of professors, the cancellation of courses, the elimination of DEI offices, and the legislative gutting of academic freedom. Should we tolerate these protests on the same terms as we tolerate Gaza encampments? In one sense yes, because viewpoint neutrality. In another sense, no, because the content of one protest is "stop investing in this war" and the content of the other is "fire that professor for what she said in class."

A consistent absolutism on the right to protest cannot distinguish these cases at the level of the right. It can only distinguish them at the level of substance: this protest is correct, that one is wrong. Gourevitch is uncomfortable with this and tries to abstract from it. But the abstraction will not hold. When pro-life protesters at South Carolina display lynching photos to make their case against abortion, they are not just exercising a right; they are making a substantive moral argument that is, in my view, deeply wrong and that causes real harm to Black students and to women on that campus. Defending their right to do this is one thing. Pretending that defending their right is morally equivalent to defending the Gaza protesters is another.

This is where Gourevitch's essay, for all its rigor, performs a familiar liberal sleight of hand. It defends an absolutist right at the level of formal entitlement while quietly counting on substantive agreement at the level of which protests are worth defending. I share his substantive sympathies. I think the Gaza protests were morally serious and that the crackdown was a disgrace. But I would prefer an argument that owns its substance rather than launders it through procedural neutrality.

What would that look like? It would say: the right to protest is not viewpoint-neutral in the way liberal speech doctrine pretends, because some protests aim at justice and some aim at domination, and a healthy university distinguishes between them. The distinction will be contested, sometimes wrongly. But pretending we can do without it has produced a regime in which the only protests anyone defends in practice are the ones that confirm the priors of whoever happens to be in power. Gourevitch's essay is, in the end, a brief for substantive judgment dressed in proceduralist clothing. I would rather wear the substance plainly.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Formal viewpoint neutrality fails to produce equitable outcomes when university administrators are subject to lopsided political and financial pressures from donors and government agencies.
  2. 02The social cost of hostile protest is not equally distributed, as protests targeting vulnerable populations carry different moral weight than those targeting powerful institutions.
  3. 03An absolutist right to protest fails to distinguish between movements seeking justice and those specifically designed to trigger administrative overreach and the suppression of academic freedom.
  4. 04The lead essay's reliance on procedural neutrality is a 'liberal sleight of hand' that avoids the necessary task of making substantive moral judgments about the goals of specific protests.
  5. 05Universities should move away from pretending to be neutral and instead openly distinguish between protests that aim at justice and those that aim at domination.
