"Academic medicine must restore its integrity and public trust by strictly separating its scientific and educational missions from the commercial interests of the pharmaceutical industry."

Marcia Angell’s indictment of the pharmaceutical industry’s capture of academic medicine is powerful because it does not rest on the claim that drug companies are uniquely wicked. In fact, one of her most important points is almost banal: investor-owned firms exist to maximize shareholder value. The scandal is that universities and physicians—institutions and professionals whose legitimacy depends on public trust—have allowed that commercial mission to migrate into the heart of medical science, education, and judgment.

That distinction matters. It is too easy to turn this debate into a morality play about “Big Pharma.” Pharmaceutical companies have produced drugs that save lives, relieve suffering, and make once-devastating diseases manageable. No serious critic should deny that. But the question Angell asks is not whether medicines are useful. It is whether the system by which evidence about medicines is produced, interpreted, taught, and translated into practice can remain trustworthy when so much of it is financed and shaped by companies whose profits depend on favorable answers.

The evidence she cites is hard to dismiss. Industry-sponsored trials are more likely to favor the sponsor’s drug. Negative trials can disappear or be delayed. Published articles may be ghostwritten. Academic “authors” may be less authors than endorsers. Guideline panels often include physicians with financial relationships to companies whose products they evaluate. Continuing medical education is subsidized by the very firms that benefit when doctors prescribe more expensive brand-name drugs. Even if every individual involved believes himself objective, the structure is corrupting.

The defenders of the status quo usually respond in one of three ways. First, they say that conflicts of interest do not necessarily produce bias. That is true but irrelevant. A judge need not be demonstrably bribed in a particular case for us to insist that he not hear a lawsuit involving a company in which he owns stock. Conflict-of-interest rules exist not because we can prove that each decision was corrupted, but because institutions depend on confidence that decisions are made for the right reasons. Medicine should be at least as scrupulous as the judiciary.

Second, defenders argue that disclosure is enough. Again, disclosure is necessary, but it is not sufficient. If a professor tells an audience that he consults for the maker of the drug he is about to praise, the audience may discount his claims—or, perversely, may trust him more because he has been “transparent.” Disclosure can become a ritual absolution: I have confessed, therefore I may proceed. But the problem is not merely secrecy; it is dependence. The most important issue is not whether the tie is visible, but whether it should exist at all.

Third, defenders claim that strict separation would slow innovation. This is the most serious argument, but it is often overstated. Angell is not calling for an end to all collaboration. Companies must test drugs in humans, and academic investigators can play an important role in designing and conducting trials. The question is who controls the design, the data, the analysis, and the publication. A trial funded by a company need not be a company trial in the intellectual sense. Universities could insist on independent protocols, full access to data, publication rights, and statistical analysis insulated from marketing departments. If companies refuse those terms, that tells us something important.

The deeper issue is that academic medicine has mistaken proximity to money for proximity to progress. The Bayh-Dole era encouraged universities to think of discoveries as assets to be monetized. Technology-transfer offices became symbols of sophistication. Faculty entrepreneurs became local heroes. Some useful products emerged from this world, but it also encouraged a change in aspiration: from asking what knowledge the public needs to asking what intellectual property can be licensed. The two questions sometimes overlap, but they are not the same.

The opportunity costs are immense. We need comparative effectiveness research that asks whether new drugs are better than old ones, not merely better than placebo. We need research into prevention, public health, antibiotics, neglected diseases, and non-pharmaceutical interventions. We need trials that include the elderly, the chronically ill, women, and minorities in numbers sufficient to reveal real-world risks and benefits. These are not always the studies most attractive to drug companies, because the results may not expand markets. That is precisely why academic medicine must preserve an independent agenda.

Angell is especially persuasive on medical education. The idea that drug companies should subsidize doctors’ continuing education is indefensible. No one would ask a tobacco company to sponsor a course on lung cancer prevention, or a casino to design a seminar on gambling addiction. Yet physicians have tolerated lunches, dinners, speaker programs, and “educational” symposia because the gifts are normalized and often small. The smallness is part of the danger. A pen, a sandwich, or a subsidized conference does not feel like corruption. But the industry does not spend billions on physician access because it enjoys hospitality. It spends because it works.

Doctors should pay for their own education. Hospitals and professional societies should budget for education as a core responsibility, not as an optional luxury to be underwritten by vendors. Medical schools should teach students from the beginning that professional independence is not a decorative virtue but a condition of competent practice. The recent activism of medical students who object to pharmaceutical influence is encouraging, but it should not require students to embarrass their elders into behaving professionally.

The reforms Angell proposes are not radical; they are restorative. Faculty conducting clinical trials should not take personal payments from interested companies. Researchers should control data and publication. Doctors should refuse gifts. Guideline panels and FDA advisory committees should be populated, whenever possible, by experts without relevant financial ties. Patents arising from publicly funded research should be licensed in ways that protect access and downstream inquiry.

The predictable objection is that “everyone” has ties, so excluding conflicted experts is impractical. But that is an argument for changing the culture, not surrendering to it. If the only people qualified to judge a drug are those paid by its manufacturer, then academic medicine has already failed at cultivating independent expertise.

Medicine asks patients to accept uncertainty, risk, and sometimes painful treatment on the assurance that recommendations are based on evidence and judgment rather than salesmanship. That trust is not infinitely renewable. Angell’s essay is a warning that academic medicine has been spending down its moral capital. The solution is not hostility to industry, but distance from it. Collaboration without capture is possible. But it will require medical schools to remember what they are for.

"While Marcia Angell’s concerns about industry influence are valid, her proposed solution of total isolation between academia and the pharmaceutical industry is impractical because it ignores the necessary and productive division of labor required to translate basic science into usable therapies."

Marcia Angell is right to worry about conflicts of interest, but her essay risks leaving readers with a dangerously incomplete picture of how medicines come into being. The pharmaceutical industry’s influence on academic medicine should be scrutinized and limited. Yet the implication that a cleaner, more innovative system existed before the 1980s—and could be substantially recovered by pushing industry to arm’s length—underestimates both the complexity of drug development and the public benefits of industry-academic collaboration.

Start with a fact that critics sometimes acknowledge but do not fully absorb: turning a promising biological idea into an approved medicine is extraordinarily difficult. Basic science can identify a pathway, receptor, enzyme, or gene associated with disease. That is not the same thing as producing a safe, effective, manufacturable, stable, doseable, regulated therapy. The long path from discovery to medicine includes medicinal chemistry, toxicology, pharmacokinetics, formulation, manufacturing scale-up, multiple phases of clinical trials, regulatory negotiation, post-marketing surveillance, and distribution. Academic labs are not designed to do most of this. Large firms, for all their faults, possess infrastructure and expertise that universities generally do not.

Angell criticizes companies for relying on universities and start-ups for early-stage research. But why is that inherently bad? The modern biomedical ecosystem is more distributed than the old vertically integrated model. Universities are good at discovery; small biotechnology companies may be nimble at early development; larger pharmaceutical companies are often better at large trials, regulatory submissions, manufacturing, and global commercialization. That division of labor can be wasteful and distorted, but it can also be productive. If we insist that drug companies do all creative research internally, we may not get purer science; we may get less translation of publicly funded discoveries into usable therapies.

Bayh-Dole deserves criticism, particularly when publicly supported inventions are licensed on terms that lead to excessive prices or block downstream research. But it is too simple to say that discoveries should generally be placed in the public domain or licensed non-exclusively. For some inventions, especially platform technologies, nonexclusive licensing makes sense. The Cohen-Boyer recombinant DNA licensing model is rightly admired. But a molecule requiring hundreds of millions of dollars in development may not attract private investment without some exclusivity. The public domain is not a magic conveyor belt from bench to bedside. If no one has a defensible right to develop a product, no one may spend the money to test it.

That does not mean the present system is acceptable. It means reform must distinguish between bad conflicts and necessary incentives. A university scientist should not be paid personally to promote a sponsor’s drug. A company should not ghostwrite papers. Investigators should not be denied access to data. Negative trials should not be buried. FDA advisory committee members should not vote on products made by companies that pay them. Continuing medical education should not be a disguised sales channel. On these points Angell’s argument is compelling.

But some of her language blurs the difference between collaboration and corruption. A faculty member who consults on trial design may contribute real expertise. A scientist who helps found a company based on her laboratory’s discovery may be the person most capable of guiding its early development. A professional society may need outside support for educational programming, though that support should be pooled, blinded, and kept away from content. If the rule becomes “no financial contact except arm’s-length grants,” we may reduce bias, but we may also reduce the flow of expertise between science and development.

The right question is not whether money changes hands. It is whether money changes judgment, control, or public access. Personal gifts and speaker fees plainly threaten judgment. Sponsor control of data threatens science. Exclusive licenses with no access safeguards threaten the public interest. But carefully structured consulting, milestone-based institutional licensing, and transparent collaborative research may serve patients.

We should also be careful with the claim that the industry mostly produces “me-too” drugs. There is truth in the criticism. Too much effort goes into extending franchises, making minor molecular variations, and marketing new products as breakthroughs without comparative evidence. But follow-on drugs are not always useless. Drugs in the same class can differ in side effects, dosing, interactions, and patient response. Competition within a class can also lower prices once patents expire, though brand competition before generic entry may not. The problem is not that every second or third drug in a class is medically worthless; it is that our evidence and payment systems often reward novelty without proving added value.

Here, Angell’s critique should push us toward better comparative effectiveness research. Approval based on superiority to placebo may be appropriate when no therapy exists, but often physicians and patients need to know whether a new drug is better than an established generic, safer for particular patients, or worth its price. Public funding for head-to-head trials is essential precisely because companies have little incentive to ask questions whose answers may shrink markets. The new health reform law’s movement toward comparative effectiveness research is promising, though it will need protection from political pressure and industry lobbying.

Similarly, transparency should be strengthened. Trial registration, public reporting of results, disclosure of payments to physicians, and access to protocols and data would make manipulation harder. The Physician Payments Sunshine provisions enacted this year are a step in the right direction, though disclosure alone is not enough. Journals should require author access to data and meaningful contributions to manuscripts. Universities should prohibit ghostwriting and sales-oriented speakers bureaus. FDA advisory committees should minimize conflicts and explain waivers publicly.

Where I part company with Angell is in tone and emphasis. The pharmaceutical industry is not merely an intruder in medicine; it is one of the institutions through which modern medicine accomplishes difficult work. To treat its educational efforts skeptically is sensible. To treat its research partnerships as presumptively illegitimate is not. We need boundaries, not walls.

Academic medicine should recover its independence, but independence does not require isolation. Universities should negotiate from strength: no trial without publication rights, no data secrecy, no personal enrichment tied to favorable results, no company-written papers, no educational control by sponsors, no licensing without access provisions. Companies that want academic legitimacy should accept academic norms. Those that refuse should be left to contract research organizations and private marketing channels, without the prestige of university endorsement.

Angell has done a service by forcing a profession grown comfortable with industry money to look in the mirror. But reform will succeed only if it preserves what is valuable in collaboration while eliminating what is corrupting. Patients need trustworthy evidence. They also need new therapies. A reformed system must deliver both.

Vanilla GPT

"While collaboration between academic medicine and the pharmaceutical industry is necessary for drug development, medical institutions must establish strict boundaries and independent control over science and education to restore their institutional integrity and public trust."

Marcia Angell’s indictment is powerful because it is not anti-medicine and not even, in the usual sense, anti-industry. It is an argument about institutional character. A drug company is organized to sell drugs. A university medical center is organized—or should be organized—to discover truth, train physicians, and care for patients. When the second institution begins to borrow the habits, incentives, and language of the first, something important is lost even if no one commits fraud.

On the central point, Angell is right: disclosure is not enough. For years medicine has treated conflicts of interest as if they were a kind of footnote. The assumption has been that if readers, students, patients, or guideline users are told that a researcher consults for Pfizer, Merck, GlaxoSmithKline, or Eli Lilly, they can somehow discount the information properly. But no ordinary reader of a journal article can know how much the trial design, the choice of comparator, the dose, the endpoints, the statistical analysis, or the decision to publish were shaped before the disclosure statement ever appeared. Bias does not usually arrive wearing a black hat. It is often built into the question being asked.

The record of the last decade makes that hard to deny. Vioxx showed how a blockbuster drug could be widely prescribed before its cardiovascular risks were fully reckoned with. The controversies over antidepressant trials in children showed the damage done when negative or equivocal results remain unpublished or are presented too favorably. Litigation over Neurontin exposed the use of sponsored “education” and publication strategies to expand off-label prescribing. These are not isolated lapses by a few bad actors; they are predictable results of a system in which the evidence base for prescribing is often generated, filtered, interpreted, and promoted by companies with billions at stake.

Still, the conclusion should not be that academic medicine and industry must have no dealings with one another. The development of a useful drug requires chemistry, toxicology, manufacturing, clinical testing, regulatory work, and distribution on a scale that universities generally cannot supply. Industry has real expertise. It also takes real financial risks, including many failures for every success. A romantic picture of pure academic discovery followed by public-spirited dissemination will not produce the drugs patients need.

The question is therefore not whether collaboration should exist, but who controls the terms. Here Angell’s proposed line is the right one: industry may fund research, but it should not own the science. Academic investigators must have guaranteed access to complete data, independent statistical analysis, and the unqualified right to publish results within a defined time. Contracts that give sponsors veto power over publication should be unacceptable at any serious medical school. Institutional review boards should not approve trials unless these protections are in place. Journals, too, should refuse to publish industry-sponsored studies unless they can verify that named academic authors actually controlled and interpreted the work.

Clinical trial registration and reporting are essential parts of this architecture. The movement toward mandatory registration, strengthened by journal requirements and by the FDA Amendments Act of 2007, is one of the most important reforms in recent years. But registration alone is not enough if posted results are incomplete, delayed, or too technical to be useful. The public bargain should be simple: if human beings are asked to assume risk in a trial, the results belong in the public record. Negative findings are not commercial disappointments to be buried; they are medical knowledge.

Angell is also persuasive about medical education. There is no defensible reason for physicians to depend on drug companies for continuing medical education. The argument that doctors are too busy or too burdened to pay for their own education is embarrassing. Lawyers, accountants, pilots, and teachers manage to maintain professional competence without accepting subsidized dinners from companies whose products they may later buy or recommend. The fact that a lecture is scientifically accurate does not make the surrounding marketing harmless. Repeated exposure to branded messages, friendly representatives, free meals, and prestigious speakers changes norms. It teaches young physicians that dependence on industry is ordinary.

Some medical schools have begun to respond. Several have restricted gifts, samples, speakers bureaus, and ghostwritten articles. The Association of American Medical Colleges has urged tighter conflict-of-interest rules. The Institute of Medicine’s 2009 report called for a broad cultural change. The new federal health reform law includes “sunshine” provisions requiring public reporting of many industry payments to physicians. These are welcome steps. But transparency should be a floor, not a ceiling. If a payment would compromise trust when revealed, the problem is not secrecy alone; the problem is the payment.

Where Angell’s essay is most vulnerable is in its treatment of Bayh-Dole and commercialization of academic discoveries. She is right that universities have become too enamored of patents, start-ups, and royalty windfalls. Technology-transfer offices can create perverse incentives, especially when early scientific tools are locked up in exclusive licenses. The image of every laboratory as a potential company is corrosive. It encourages secrecy, delays publication, and tempts universities to measure success in licensing revenue rather than knowledge.

But Bayh-Dole should not be blamed for every distortion in modern biomedical research. Some discoveries do need private investment to become usable products. A promising molecule, diagnostic method, or biologic may require expensive development before it reaches patients. Without some predictable intellectual-property arrangement, many such projects would languish. The better reform is not to abolish university licensing, but to discipline it. Universities should favor nonexclusive licensing for research tools and platform technologies; reserve exclusive licenses for cases where they are truly necessary; include access provisions for poor patients and neglected diseases; and make far more serious use of the law’s requirement that publicly funded inventions be made available on reasonable terms.

The deeper issue is not patents themselves but mission drift. Academic medical centers have allowed financial scarcity to become a moral excuse. Because reimbursement is tight, because laboratories are expensive, because star faculty can leave, almost any industry arrangement is rationalized as necessary. But necessity is often exaggerated. A department that cannot function without consulting fees, ghostwriting, commercially shaped CME, or sponsor control of data is not being sustained by industry; it is being transformed by it.

Patients understand this more clearly than many physicians do. A patient entering a clinic assumes that the doctor’s recommendation is based on the best available evidence and the patient’s welfare. If that recommendation has been influenced by a paid lecture, a consulting contract, a guideline panel stacked with conflicted experts, or a published trial designed by a marketing department, the injury is not only a possibly inferior prescription. It is a breach of trust.

Medicine cannot eliminate money from medical research, nor should it pretend that commercial incentives never produce public benefits. But it can insist on boundaries. Industry can make drugs, test them under independently governed rules, and sell them honestly. Universities can study drugs, teach doctors, write guidelines, and advise regulators. What they cannot do is merge these roles without corrupting them.

Angell’s proposals may sound severe only because medicine has grown accustomed to arrangements that should always have seemed improper. The proper standard is not whether a brilliant physician can take industry money and remain personally honorable. Many can. The standard is whether the profession can design institutions that deserve public confidence even when individuals are fallible. On that test, academic medicine has been failing. It now has the chance—and the obligation—to recover its independence.

"While the pharmaceutical industry and academic medicine require aggressive reform to eliminate specific ethical abuses, the underlying architecture of their collaboration is a productive and necessary engine for medical innovation."

A Partial Defense of the Partnership

Marcia Angell writes with the moral clarity of someone who has watched abuses up close, and there is much in her essay that any honest observer of academic medicine must concede. The Vioxx panel scandal, ghostwriting, the more egregious consulting arrangements — these are real, and they need correction. But Angell's essay slides from documenting abuses to indicting the entire architecture of industry-academic collaboration, and on that broader claim I think she is wrong, in ways that matter for patients.

Start with the historical baseline she invokes. Angell repeatedly suggests that medical research before 1980 was "every bit as productive" as it is now, and that the Bayh-Dole regime has been a windfall-seeking distortion rather than a genuine engine of translation. This claim does not survive contact with the record. Before Bayh-Dole, the federal government held title to thousands of patents derived from public funding, and licensed almost none of them — fewer than five percent by most accounts. Promising compounds sat on shelves because no firm would invest the hundreds of millions required for development without exclusivity. The biotechnology industry as we know it — Genentech, Amgen, the entire monoclonal antibody revolution — emerged in the wake of Bayh-Dole and the Diamond v. Chakrabarty decision, not by coincidence. Imatinib, trastuzumab, the HPV vaccine, modern HIV therapeutics: these came out of the post-1980 ecosystem, not the pre-1980 one. One can argue that the system has accreted abuses, but one cannot seriously argue that the prior system was equivalently productive.

Second, Angell's portrait of pharmaceutical R&D as essentially imitative — "very little innovative research" — relies on a rhetorical sleight of hand. Yes, many approved drugs are me-toos, and yes, this is a problem worth addressing. But "innovative" research is not the same as "FDA approval of a novel mechanism." A great deal of genuinely innovative work — figuring out which targets are druggable, which leads can be optimized, which formulations cross which membranes — happens inside companies and never appears in the journals Angell read at the New England Journal. The conceit that universities do the creative work and companies merely package it has always flattered academics, but it is largely false. Drug development is itself a scientific discipline, and the people who do it well are mostly not in universities.

Third, the proposal that pivotal trials be conducted at arm's length from sponsors, with no consulting relationships, sounds clean but raises real questions about who is competent to design them. A trial of a kinase inhibitor in non-small-cell lung cancer requires intimate knowledge of the molecule's pharmacokinetics, its known toxicities from earlier phases, and the specific subpopulation likely to benefit. The people who have that knowledge are, almost by definition, the people who have worked closely with the sponsor. Angell would presumably reply that they can share information without taking personal payments. Perhaps. But the line between "scientific collaboration" and "consulting" is fuzzier in practice than her clean prescriptions suggest, and an enforcement regime built on that line will produce either evasions or a chilling effect that pushes the best clinical investigators out of the field.

Fourth, on continuing medical education, I will partially agree with Angell while pushing back on her framing. Industry-funded CME is indefensible in its current form, and the cozy arrangements between Madison Avenue agencies and "medical-education companies" are an embarrassment. But the alternative she proposes — doctors paying their own way — would in practice mean far less CME, especially among community physicians who are already stretched. If the medical profession truly took back education as its own responsibility, that would be wonderful. The track record on the profession actually doing this, however, is thin. Medical societies have themselves become substantially industry-funded, and there is no obvious financial substitute on the horizon.

What I want to defend is not the status quo but the basic legitimacy of cooperation between universities and industry. Translating a discovery from a research bench into a pill that a patient can swallow is enormously expensive, technically demanding, and risky. Most candidates fail. The institutions that bear that risk need a return when they succeed, or the risk-taking stops. The reasonable debate is about how exclusivity, price, and access are structured — not whether translation should occur at all. Angell sometimes writes as if the only honorable model were the Salk model, in which the discovery is given to humanity gratis. It is a beautiful story, but the polio vaccine was developed in an era of public funding levels we no longer maintain, with technical demands far lower than those of modern biologics. We cannot simply will ourselves back into that world by rhetoric.

So: reform aggressively, yes. Ban gifts, ban speakers' bureaus, ban ghostwriting, require comprehensive disclosure, and rebuild the firewall between industry and CME. Strengthen FDA conflict-of-interest rules on advisory panels. Require registration and publication of all clinical trials, including negative ones — a reform that, surprisingly, Angell does not emphasize, but that addresses publication bias directly. Push for comparative effectiveness research funded by public money, so that we know which drugs actually beat older ones. These are real reforms, mostly attainable, that would address most of the abuses Angell documents without dismantling a productive system.

The danger of Angell's framing is that, by treating the entire enterprise as corrupt, it makes the actually corrupt parts harder to isolate and fix. We need surgical excision, not amputation.

  2. 02Pharmaceutical companies perform essential, innovative scientific work in drug development that universities are not equipped to handle alone.
  3. 03Strictly prohibiting consulting relationships would hinder clinical trial design by distancing investigators from the specialized knowledge required to test complex new molecules safely.
  4. 04Eliminating industry-funded continuing medical education without a viable alternative funding model would likely result in a decline in physician knowledge and participation.
  5. 05The high cost and risk of modern drug development necessitate a system of exclusivity and return on investment to ensure that medical translation continues.

"While Marcia Angell correctly identifies the dangers of commercial influence in medicine, the solution is not to avoid industry entirely but to create rigorously governed, mission-driven partnerships that leverage private resources for public health goals."

Marcia Angell’s essay describes a real danger: the capture of academic medicine by commercial interests. But in reacting against that danger, we should not adopt a model of medical progress that is too nationally bounded, too university-centered, and too suspicious of all public-private cooperation. The world’s health needs are broader than the conflicts of interest in American teaching hospitals, and some of those needs will not be met without carefully structured partnerships that include industry.

Consider vaccines, HIV medicines, tuberculosis drugs, malaria treatments, and therapies for diseases that afflict people with little purchasing power. In these areas, the central problem is not always that companies are too involved. Often it is that they are not involved enough, because the expected return is too small. Angell notes the shortage of new antibiotics for resistant infections; the same market failure affects many global health priorities. If the only reliable engine of development is private profit, poor patients will wait indefinitely. But if we conclude that industry has no legitimate place in the development pipeline, we may also deprive ourselves of manufacturing capacity, regulatory experience, compound libraries, and distribution systems that can save lives.

The lesson is not “trust the market.” The lesson is “design the partnership around the public goal.” There is a difference between a university allowing a company to ghostwrite an article about its antidepressant and a nonprofit product-development partnership contracting with firms to develop a neglected-disease drug under access conditions. There is a difference between a drug company sponsoring continuing education to increase prescriptions and a public agency purchasing vaccines at negotiated prices for a national immunization program. The former corrupts judgment; the latter may implement public health.

Angell is strongest when she insists on mission clarity. Drug companies exist to make profits. Academic medical centers exist, or should exist, to produce knowledge, train professionals, and care for patients. Public health institutions exist to improve population health. Problems arise when these missions are confused—when marketing is called education, when promotional trials are called science, when patent revenue is called public service. But the fact that missions differ does not mean institutions can never cooperate. It means cooperation must be governed by the mission of the public or professional institution, not the mission of the firm.

This distinction is crucial for global health. A rigid anti-industry stance may unintentionally favor the status quo, in which profitable diseases receive investment and unprofitable diseases receive sympathy. To change that, governments and philanthropies may need to create incentives: advance purchase commitments, prize funds, patent pools, public grants, tax credits, or collaborative research consortia. Each mechanism has risks. Companies may capture subsidies, demand excessive exclusivity, or use partnerships for public relations. But those risks can be managed more effectively than the risk of doing nothing.

The same is true of Bayh-Dole and university licensing. Angell is right that universities too often chase lottery-like windfalls. Yet the answer should depend on the technology and the health need. Nonexclusive licensing is appropriate for broad research tools and platform discoveries. Humanitarian licensing is essential when products may be used in poor countries. March-in rights and access provisions should be meaningful, not decorative. But some products may require a period of exclusivity to attract development. The public interest lies not in a universal rule against patents, but in licensing strategies that ask: what arrangement will get this intervention to the people who need it, at a price they can afford, without blocking further research?

Industry-funded research in academic centers also needs differentiation. A company-designed trial whose purpose is to position a me-too drug in the market is one thing. A rigorously governed trial of a needed therapy, with independent academic control of protocol, data analysis, and publication, is another. The problem is not the presence of industry money alone; it is sponsor control over scientific judgment. Angell’s arm’s-length standard is a good starting point, but it should be incorporated into enforceable partnership models rather than treated as a reason to avoid collaboration altogether.

Medical education is where I would draw the brightest line. Unlike drug development, education does not require industry sponsorship.
