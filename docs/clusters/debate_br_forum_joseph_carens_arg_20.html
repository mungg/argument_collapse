"Amnesty for long-settled unauthorized migrants is not merely an act of mercy but a necessary act of legal honesty that recognizes the state's role in creating and benefiting from a population it labels as illegal."

Joseph Carens urges liberal democracies to recognize that long-settled unauthorized migrants become members over time. I agree with his conclusion, but I would place more emphasis on one historical fact: unauthorized status is not a natural condition. It is created by law. People do not arrive in the world as “illegal.” They become illegal when legal categories fail to accommodate human movement, labor demand, family life, and global inequality.

This does not mean borders are meaningless or every immigration law is illegitimate. It means we should be cautious about building moral judgments on legal labels. American immigration history is full of shifting categories that later generations view with embarrassment: Chinese exclusion, national-origin quotas, racial bars to naturalization, the exclusion of many Asian immigrants, and the mass deportations and “repatriations” of Mexicans and Mexican Americans during the Depression era. The law has often declared certain people unwanted, only to have history judge the declaration harshly.

The current unauthorized population is partly the product of similar legal construction. The United States has deep economic ties with Mexico and Central America, a long history of recruiting Mexican labor when convenient, and an economy that continues to demand low-wage workers. At the same time, it offers few lawful avenues for such workers to enter permanently. Family backlogs stretch for years. Employer visas are limited and often mismatched to actual labor markets. The result is not surprising. People move through the channels available, including unlawful ones.

Carens’s account of social membership corrects the legal fiction that unauthorized migrants remain perpetual outsiders. But we should also ask why the law insists on producing outsiders from people whom society has already incorporated. The answer is political convenience. Unauthorized workers are useful but deniable. They can be employed without being fully included, taxed without being represented, blamed without being heard. Their illegality is not merely a violation of the system; it is part of how the system functions.

This is why amnesty must be more than an act of mercy. It must be an act of truth-telling. Legalization says: we have lived with these people, worked with them, bought what they produced, relied on their care, taught their children, and welcomed their rent and taxes. We cannot now pretend they are strangers. The law must acknowledge the society it helped create.

Carens’s examples are carefully chosen. Marguerite Grimmond makes deportation look absurd because she lived in Britain for almost her whole life. Hiu Lui Ng makes it look cruel because he had a citizen wife, children, a home, and a career. Miguel Sanchez makes it ordinary because there are millions like him. The power of the examples lies in their ordinariness. They are not asking for rescue from persecution or reward for genius. They are asking not to have ordinary lives destroyed.

Opponents say that the first act matters: unlawful entry or overstay. It does matter. But moral judgment must consider proportion. The wrong of entering without authorization to work and support family is not equivalent to the harm of deporting a parent after a decade. The state’s response should be proportionate to the violation and attentive to its own role. A fine, registration requirement, or probationary period may be justified. Exile after years of residence often is not.

The statute-of-limitations analogy is helpful because it reframes amnesty as a normal legal response to time. We do not keep every legal threat alive forever. The law values repose because people build lives. Immigration law should do the same. A five- or seven-year rule may be imperfect, but it is better than a system in which a person can be deported after twenty years because an old violation never loses force.

Still, legalization must avoid reproducing hierarchy. If the path is too expensive, too complex, or too dependent on employer documentation, many of the most vulnerable will be excluded. Domestic workers paid in cash, day laborers, farmworkers, and people who have moved frequently may struggle to prove residence. Women doing unpaid caregiving may lack pay stubs. The evidentiary system should accept school records, medical records, affidavits, church records, remittance receipts, leases, and other realistic proof. Otherwise amnesty will favor the most formally documented among the undocumented.

The process should also protect applicants from enforcement if they are denied for technical reasons. Fear of coming forward undermined past programs and will do so again if people suspect a trap. Community organizations, unions, churches, and legal-aid groups should be involved. Translation and outreach are essential. Legalization is not just a change in statute; it is a relationship of trust between state and residents who have long feared it.

Carens notes European experiences with regularization, and Americans should pay attention. European countries that once imagined guest workers would remain temporary eventually had to accept settlement. The lesson is universal: when human beings live, work, marry, and raise children somewhere, temporary categories break down. States may write “temporary” on a visa or “unauthorized” in a file, but life does not remain temporary for fifteen years.

In 2009, the politics are difficult. The economy is in crisis. Many citizens are anxious. But hard times are also when scapegoating becomes most tempting. Blaming unauthorized migrants for structural economic failures will not restore jobs, regulate banks, or rebuild wages. It will only make vulnerable people more vulnerable. Legalization should be paired with labor protections and economic policies that help all low-wage workers.

The deeper issue is whether law will be used to clarify reality or conceal it. Current immigration law conceals a population that is present everywhere. It labels neighbors as illegal, workers as removable, and families as administrative problems. Carens asks us to let time and membership matter. We should accept that invitation and go further: we should recognize that illegality itself is often a political artifact, not a moral essence.

Amnesty is justified because settled migrants belong. But it is also justified because the society that calls them illegal has depended on their presence. Legalization would not be a national indulgence. It would be a long-overdue act of honesty.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Unauthorized status is a legal construction rather than a natural moral condition, often created when laws fail to account for actual labor demands and human movement.
  2. 02The state and economy are complicit in unauthorized migration by relying on the labor of workers who are useful to the system but denied legal inclusion for political convenience.
  3. 03Moral judgments regarding immigration must be proportionate, as the minor wrong of unauthorized entry does not justify the massive harm of destroying an ordinary life after years of residence.
  4. 04Amnesty functions like a statute of limitations, acknowledging that the law should value social repose and the fact that people build lives over time.
  5. 05To be effective and just, the legalization process must use flexible evidentiary standards to ensure that the most vulnerable migrants, such as domestic workers or caregivers, are not excluded.
  6. 06Legalization should be viewed as a way to clarify the reality of a society that already incorporates these individuals rather than a way to reward lawbreaking.
