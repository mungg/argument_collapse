Diversified Claude

"The moral case for amnesty must be reframed to include the mass deaths caused by border enforcement, as the state's right to control immigration is severely limited by the lethal consequences of its deterrent strategies."

There is something missing from Carens' essay that we should not let pass without comment: the dead.

Carens writes touchingly of Marguerite Grimmond, who was lucky enough to come to public attention and not be deported. He writes of Hiu Lui Ng, who died in detention. He writes of Miguel Sanchez, who lives in fear. The essay is structured around the figures of irregular migrants whose lives can be visited by deportation. But the boundary that produces this situation is also a killing apparatus, and the people killed by it do not appear in Carens' essay because they are not available to make claims for themselves.

In the year 2008 alone, the U.S. Border Patrol recovered the remains of at least 390 people in the southwestern desert. The total over the past fifteen years is well over five thousand. These are official, recovered numbers. The actual figure is certainly higher; many bodies are never found, and many are found by family or local authorities and never enter federal statistics. Similar numbers can be tallied from the southern coast of Spain, the waters off Malta and Lampedusa, the boundary between Morocco and the Spanish enclaves of Ceuta and Melilla. Tens of thousands of people have died in the past two decades trying to enter rich democracies. They died because the policies of those democracies pushed migration into ever more dangerous routes.

This is not a side issue. It is the principal moral fact about contemporary border enforcement. We have constructed, over the past fifteen years, a regime that produces mass death as a regular and expected outcome. The "Prevention Through Deterrence" strategy adopted by the U.S. Border Patrol in 1994 explicitly aimed to push migration into hazardous terrain on the theory that the hazards would deter migration. It did not deter migration. It only produced deaths. The deaths were predicted, accepted, and continued.

Carens mentions border deaths briefly, in the context of arguing that the United States and Spain may be going "too far" in enforcement. The treatment is brief and tucked into a discussion of state complicity. I think this is the wrong place to put it. The deaths are not a footnote to the amnesty argument; they are part of the same moral situation, and they reframe the argument in important ways.

Consider what it means that we have built a border regime that kills hundreds of people a year. We are spending billions of dollars on enforcement that does not significantly reduce migration but does significantly increase mortality. The people dying are mostly young men and women trying to find work; some are children; almost all of them are doing what their economic situations and family obligations require them to do. They die of exposure, of dehydration, of drowning. They die alone, in places where their bodies often go undiscovered. They die because we have decided that the integrity of our immigration laws is worth this price.

When we discuss amnesty for the eleven million who made it through, we are also implicitly discussing the policy that killed the others. The two cannot be separated. The migrants Carens describes are the survivors of a passage that killed many of their peers. To regularize them while continuing the enforcement that killed the others is not enough. It is a partial moral response to a comprehensive moral failure.

What follows from taking the deaths seriously? Several things.

First, the comparative weight of considerations shifts. Carens' essay is at pains to grant the state's right to control immigration and then to argue for the constraint on that right represented by amnesty. The deaths suggest a more aggressive position: that the state's right to control immigration is severely limited by the lives lost in the attempt to enforce it. A policy that produces this many deaths is not a legitimate exercise of state authority; it is a moral catastrophe that demands fundamental reform.

Second, the policy implications expand beyond amnesty. Amnesty addresses the survivors. The dead and the dying require a different kind of response: an end to the deterrent strategy that produces them, a substantial expansion of legal channels so that migration does not require deadly routes, an honest accounting of the costs of enforcement against its supposed benefits. Carens' essay focuses narrowly on amnesty. The deaths demand that we widen the lens.

Third, the moral standing of the receiving state to make demands of irregular migrants is compromised in a way Carens' essay does not capture. A state that has killed many of the migrant's peers is not in a strong moral position to insist on its right to deport the survivors. The state's hands are not clean. Its enforcement apparatus is implicated in deaths it has caused. The migrant who has made it through is, in a sense, owed something by the state — at minimum, the recognition that her presence cost lives the state was responsible for taking.

Fourth, the rhetoric of "rule of law" and "fairness to those who waited in line" looks different against this background. The line that the patient applicant is waiting in does not lead to entry for most of the people who would otherwise be applicants. The "rule of law" being defended is a law that, in its enforcement, produces mass death. When we ask how we should treat the irregular migrants among us, we cannot pretend that the alternative is the orderly operation of a just system. The alternative is the system that killed 390 people last year alone.

I do not want to be unfair to Carens. He is aware of these issues; he mentions them. But the structure of his essay treats them as supplementary to the central argument about social membership, when they should be part of the foundation. The dead at the border are not external to the question of amnesty. They are the reason the question has the urgency it does.

The case for amnesty is also the case against a border policy that kills. Both belong in the same essay. The next essay, perhaps, will be the one that puts them there.
