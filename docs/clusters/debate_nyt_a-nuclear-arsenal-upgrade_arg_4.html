"The United States should reject a full-scale nuclear recommitment in favor of a minimal deterrent force that prioritizes safety, reduces the role of nuclear weapons, and leads toward global abolition."

The phrase “nuclear modernization” sounds antiseptic, as if the United States were updating bridges or air-traffic software. But the issue is whether to spend roughly a trillion dollars sustaining and refining weapons capable of destroying cities and threatening civilization. The right answer is not a better nuclear arsenal. It is a smaller one, moving deliberately toward abolition.

The standard defense of nuclear weapons is that they deter nuclear war. That claim cannot be dismissed; the bomb has shaped great-power caution since 1945. But deterrence is also a gamble. It assumes rational leaders, perfect information, reliable technology and permanent luck. The historical record is full of close calls, false alarms, accidents and misread signals. A system that must work flawlessly forever is not safe.

Modernizing for safety and security is one thing. No one should want aging warheads or vulnerable command systems. But the current plan goes far beyond custodianship. It aims to preserve all three legs of the triad for decades, develop new delivery systems and, in some cases, make weapons more precise and supposedly more usable. That is not disarmament by another name. It is recommitment.

The United States should exclude new nuclear cruise missiles, new low-yield options and any weapons designed to fight a “limited” nuclear war. It should cancel plans that expand the role of nuclear weapons and declare that the sole purpose of the arsenal is to deter nuclear attack. It should adopt a no-first-use policy, take missiles off high alert and reduce deployed warheads below New START levels, inviting Russia to reciprocate but not allowing Russian obstruction to dictate every American moral choice.

Critics will say this is naïve because Russia, China and North Korea will not follow. But American overinvestment in nuclear weapons has never persuaded others to abandon theirs. In fact, it often strengthens the argument of hard-liners abroad who claim that nuclear arms are the currency of power. If the United States insists that it needs thousands of nuclear weapons for security, why should weaker countries believe they can do without them?

A serious modernization would modernize American thinking. It would recognize climate change, pandemics, cyberattacks and loose nuclear material as security threats at least as urgent as building a new generation of doomsday machines. The United States should maintain the minimum force necessary while leading, visibly and credibly, toward a world in which nuclear weapons are stigmatized, reduced and eventually eliminated.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The theory of nuclear deterrence is a dangerous gamble that relies on the impossible assumption of permanent human and technological perfection.
  2. 02Current modernization plans go beyond necessary maintenance to create more precise and usable weapons, which contradicts the goal of disarmament.
  3. 03The U.S. should adopt a no-first-use policy and unilaterally reduce its arsenal to demonstrate that nuclear weapons are not the primary currency of national power.
  4. 04Overinvesting in nuclear weapons provides a justification for adversaries and weaker nations to develop or maintain their own nuclear capabilities.
  5. 05National security resources should be redirected from nuclear expansion toward more urgent modern threats such as climate change, pandemics, and cyberattacks.
Diversified Claude

"The United States should abandon its trillion-dollar nuclear modernization plan in favor of a drastically smaller arsenal and a shift in focus toward nonproliferation and modern security threats."

We are sleepwalking into a new arms race, and the modernization plan is the somnambulist's stride. The fundamental question the country should be asking is not which warheads to build, but why — twenty-five years after the Cold War ended — we still maintain an arsenal designed to destroy human civilization several times over.

The official answer is deterrence. But deterrence of what? Russia is not going to launch a bolt-from-the-blue first strike against the United States, and we are not going to launch one against Russia. The scenarios in which our nuclear weapons are theoretically "used" — extended deterrence in Europe, response to tactical Russian use, escalation in a Taiwan crisis — are precisely the scenarios in which any use would likely lead to civilizational catastrophe. We are spending a trillion dollars to maintain options that no sane leader could exercise.

President Obama promised in Prague in 2009 to pursue "the peace and security of a world without nuclear weapons." The trillion-dollar plan is the repudiation of that promise. It commits the country to maintaining a Cold War-sized force structure until roughly 2080. It develops new categories of weapons — the LRSO, the B61-12 — that will inevitably be matched by Russia and China, and then by others. It signals to non-nuclear states, which renewed their Non-Proliferation Treaty commitments on the understanding that the nuclear powers would disarm, that the bargain is dead. Why should Iran or any other threshold state accept restraints we ourselves refuse?

A genuinely safe modernization would be drastically smaller. Move to a dyad of submarines and bombers. Retire the ICBMs, eliminating the launch-on-warning posture and the most accident-prone leg. Cap the deployed arsenal at 1,000 warheads, unilaterally if necessary, and invite Russia to reciprocate. Cancel the LRSO. Pour the savings into nonproliferation, securing fissile material worldwide, and intelligence on rogue programs — the genuine nuclear threats of this century.

The greatest risks today are not a Russian first strike but accidental launch, terrorist acquisition, regional escalation in South Asia or the Korean peninsula, and proliferation cascade. A trillion dollars spent on shinier ICBMs addresses none of these. It addresses only the institutional interests of the labs, the contractors, and the services. The next president should have the courage to say so.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current modernization plan maintains a Cold War-sized force structure that provides no practical utility since any use of these weapons would lead to civilizational catastrophe.
  2. 02Developing new categories of nuclear weapons encourages a global arms race and signals to non-nuclear states that the Non-Proliferation Treaty is no longer being honored by nuclear powers.
  3. 03A safer nuclear posture would involve retiring land-based ICBMs to eliminate the risks associated with launch-on-warning policies and accidental launches.
  4. 04The massive financial resources currently allocated for arsenal upgrades should be redirected toward securing fissile material and intelligence to combat the genuine threats of nuclear terrorism and rogue programs.
  5. 05The modernization plan serves the institutional interests of contractors and the military rather than addressing contemporary risks like regional escalation or proliferation cascades.
