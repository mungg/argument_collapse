Diversified Claude

"Sabel and Victor’s experimentalist governance model fails to account for the unique political and technical realities of carbon emissions that make the Montreal Protocol an inapplicable template for climate policy."

The Montreal Protocol has done a great deal of harm to climate policy thinking, and Sabel and Victor's essay, despite its many virtues, perpetuates the harm even while trying to correct earlier misreadings. The earlier misreading was institutional mimicry: build a UNFCCC in Montreal's image and the ozone miracle will repeat itself on carbon. Sabel and Victor correctly diagnose this as a category error. But they then proceed to a more sophisticated version of the same mistake: extract design principles from Montreal — thin initial consensus, devolved problem-solving, peer review, penalty defaults — and apply them to the climate case. The principles are good. The problem is that Montreal worked not because of these principles in the abstract but because of the particular political and technical conditions that made the principles effective. Those conditions do not obtain for carbon, and no amount of clever institutional design can manufacture them.

Consider what the ozone problem actually looked like in 1987. The number of significant CFC producers in the world was perhaps a dozen, concentrated in a handful of advanced industrial economies. The substances were used in identifiable consumer products (aerosols, foams, refrigerators) and industrial processes (cleaning, fire suppression) that represented a small share of total economic activity. Substitutes were technically demanding but conceptually straightforward: find a molecule that performs the same function without the chlorine atom that destroys ozone. The economic value of the substances being phased out was tiny relative to the value of the host industries continuing to operate. DuPont made most of its money from things other than CFCs; it could afford to be a hero. And — this is decisive — the United States was politically dominant, technologically leading, and committed to the regime. American sanctions threats were credible because America had something the rest of the world wanted access to.

Now consider carbon. The number of significant emitters is, depending on how you count, every firm on Earth or every household on Earth. Fossil fuels are not a marginal input into a few consumer products; they are the energetic basis of the entire economy. The substitutes are not chemically analogous molecules but entirely different physical infrastructures (grids, fleets, building stocks) that must be built at unprecedented speed and scale. The economic value of the substances being phased out is many trillions of dollars in reserves on the balance sheets of the largest companies on Earth, plus sovereign wealth in petrostates whose entire fiscal structure depends on continued extraction. There is no DuPont here — no major fossil producer that can credibly say it makes most of its money in something else and can afford to be the hero. And the United States, far from being the credible hegemonic enforcer, is the country that withdrew from the Paris Agreement and whose political system cannot pass climate legislation.

Every disanalogy here matters. Sabel and Victor's "penalty defaults" worked at Montreal because a small number of powerful states could credibly threaten a small number of relatively weak laggards. Who threatens whom on carbon? The EU can threaten its own importers with a border adjustment, and may well do so. But the EU cannot threaten China, India, or the United States with anything that would actually move them. The institutional setting in which credible sanctions could be wielded against major emitters does not exist and is not on the horizon.

The substitutes problem is even more decisive. Sabel and Victor are right that the Montreal sectoral committees did valuable work figuring out, on a case-by-case basis, which alternative refrigerants would meet the safety and efficacy requirements of metered-dose inhalers. The reason this worked is that the answer existed: there are alternative propellants that perform the function. For many decarbonization problems, the analogous answer does not exist or exists only at radically higher cost. Cement requires the calcination of limestone, which releases CO2 as a matter of basic chemistry; you cannot substitute the molecule the way you can substitute a refrigerant. Steel can in principle be made with hydrogen, but only at costs that are not yet competitive and only with hydrogen that does not yet exist at industrial scale. Long-distance aviation has no plausible non-combustion substitute on a 2050 timeline. Sectoral experimentalism can accelerate progress on these problems, but it cannot conjure substitutes that physics has not provided.

There is also a quieter assumption running through Sabel and Victor's essay that deserves scrutiny: the assumption that the international system is roughly capable of sustaining the kind of patient, multi-decadal, technocratic cooperation that experimentalist governance requires. Montreal was negotiated in 1987 at the end of the Cold War, in a moment of unusual Western confidence and unusual willingness of developing countries to be folded into a regime designed elsewhere in exchange for transfers. We do not live in that world. We live in a world of intensifying U.S.-China rivalry, of a paralyzed WTO that the authors themselves note, of rising nationalism in major emitters, and of a generalized erosion of the technocratic institutions that an experimentalist climate regime would have to rely on. The sectoral committees at the IMO, the standards bodies in aviation, the technical working groups at the IPCC — all of these are increasingly contested terrain in great-power competition rather than neutral fora for problem-solving. Sabel and Victor mention that "no global hegemon and no trusted technocracy" exists, but they do not draw the implication: the kind of governance they propose may require precisely the conditions that no longer obtain.

I do not want to leave the impression that the essay is wrong about everything. The diagnosis of Paris is correct. The critique of the false dichotomy between top-down and bottom-up is useful. The insistence on sectoral and place-based work is right; this is, in fact, where most actual decarbonization is happening, whether or not its participants describe it in Sabelian terms. The reframing of Montreal as a process rather than a treaty is salutary corrective to the lazy "Montreal for carbon" trope. And there is real wisdom in the observation that "binding global commitments" should be an outcome of cooperation, not a starting point.

But I would draw a different inference from these observations than the authors do. If the political and technical conditions for Montreal-style success do not obtain for carbon, then we should be more skeptical, not more hopeful, about institutional models built on the Montreal template. The honest implication is that climate change may not be solvable as a problem of cooperation at all — that it may be solvable only as the byproduct of national industrial competitions over the technologies of the next economy. China is decarbonizing its electricity sector not because the experimentalist machinery of an international regime is nudging it to do so, but because the Chinese state has decided that solar panels, EVs, and batteries are strategic industries it wishes to dominate. Europe is decarbonizing because the EU has made green industrial policy a core element of its post-pandemic recovery and its strategic positioning vis-à-vis both China and the United States. India is electrifying because solar PV is now cheaper than coal in many configurations.

These are not "experimentalist governance" outcomes; they are mercantilist industrial policy outcomes, accelerated by collapsing costs that are themselves the product of earlier industrial policy. The international cooperation involved is thin and largely about preventing carbon leakage and securing supply chains. If climate change is to be addressed, it will be addressed by this kind of strategic competition more than by the kind of patient sectoral learning Sabel and Victor describe. That is not a happy thought, because mercantilist competition has its own pathologies. But it is closer to the world we actually inhabit than the world of well-run sectoral committees solving problems in the spirit of Dewey.

Montreal was a beautiful exception to the normal pattern of international politics. Sabel and Victor have done a fine job of explaining why. We should be careful, though, of treating the exception as a template. The conditions that made it possible are not present on carbon, and pretending otherwise is its own kind of magical thinking.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The structural differences between the ozone and climate problems—specifically the vast number of emitters and the fact that fossil fuels are the basis of the entire global economy—render Montreal's design principles ineffective.
  2. 02Unlike the ozone crisis, many carbon-heavy sectors lack chemically analogous or cost-effective technological substitutes, meaning experimentalism cannot simply 'conjure' solutions that physics does not allow.
  3. 03The 'penalty defaults' used in the Montreal Protocol are unworkable for climate change because no single hegemon or institution possesses the credible authority to sanction major powers like China or the United States.
  4. 04The current geopolitical climate of U.S.-China rivalry and rising nationalism has eroded the neutral technocratic trust required for patient, multi-decadal experimentalist cooperation.
  5. 05Meaningful decarbonization is currently driven by mercantilist industrial policy and strategic national competition rather than the collaborative learning cycles of international governance.
