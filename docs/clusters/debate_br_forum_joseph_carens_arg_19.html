Diversified Claude

"Amnesty for irregular migrants should be justified not just by their social membership, but by the need to eliminate a vulnerable underclass that depresses the wages and bargaining power of low-wage native workers."

Carens writes movingly about Miguel Sanchez and the harm that deportation would do him and his family. But the essay almost entirely ignores the people standing on the other side of the labor market — the low-wage American workers, disproportionately African-American and native-born Latino, whose wages and bargaining power are depressed by the presence of eleven million workers who cannot quit, complain, or unionize. Any honest argument for amnesty has to grapple with this, and Carens does not.

I support legalization. I want to say this clearly so that what follows is not mistaken for nativism. The status quo — a vast deportable underclass, raids that tear apart families, deaths in the desert — is morally indefensible and economically destructive. But the case for amnesty cannot rest only on the harms suffered by migrants. It has to include a serious account of the harms suffered by the workers with whom they compete, and it has to be coupled with policies that actually address those harms. Carens' essay is silent on this entire dimension. It treats the migrants as the only moral agents whose interests are at stake.

George Borjas and others have argued, with substantial evidence, that the influx of low-skilled immigrant labor over the past three decades has reduced the wages of high-school dropouts by something on the order of eight percent. This effect is concentrated on the most vulnerable workers in the American economy: the workers who already had the least bargaining power, who were already losing ground to deindustrialization and the decline of unions. Card and others contest the magnitude, but no serious economist denies that there is a downward pressure. To pretend that the social cost of mass irregular migration falls only on the migrants — who, after all, came voluntarily because the alternative at home was worse — is to ignore the people for whom the alternative was a union job in a packing plant that no longer pays a union wage.

This matters for the amnesty debate in two ways. First, it complicates the simple morality tale that Carens tells. Yes, Miguel Sanchez has built a life here. But so has the African-American construction worker in Chicago whose hourly wage has been undercut for two decades by the willingness of undocumented workers to accept worse conditions because they have no legal recourse. Both are members of the community in Carens' sense. Both have moral claims. Carens' framework gives us no resources for weighing these claims against each other.

Second, it points toward a different kind of legalization argument — one rooted not just in the migrants' membership but in the structural function of irregular status. The reason low-wage employers love an undocumented workforce is precisely the workers' vulnerability. They cannot complain about safety violations. They cannot organize. They cannot demand overtime. They cannot leave a bad employer easily because their next employer will also know about their status. Legalization, in this view, is not just a mercy to the migrants but a precondition for any decent labor market in the sectors where they are concentrated. Cesar Chavez understood this in the 1970s, which is why the UFW initially opposed open borders: a bracero program, like an undocumented workforce, undercut the workers Chavez was trying to organize.

So I want a different argument for amnesty than the one Carens makes. I want an argument that says: the irregular status of these workers is bad for them and bad for native workers, and the way to fix it is to legalize the workers and then enforce labor law, including wage law, like we mean it. Legalize them, make them full participants in the labor market with full rights to unionize and complain, and then watch what happens to the wage differential at the bottom of the labor market. It will close, not because we deport people but because we stop subsidizing their employers with a captive workforce.

This is also why Carens' "social membership" frame, by itself, is insufficient. Social membership is a beautiful idea, but it can be invoked equally well by Miguel Sanchez and by the displaced American worker in his neighborhood. Carens needs to say something about why one trumps the other, or how they can be reconciled. The reconciliation, I think, runs through the workplace: legalization plus enforcement of wage and labor law equals a labor market that is not zero-sum, because employers can no longer game it.

There is also a worry that Carens' analysis underplays. He suggests that long-term legalization will not function as a magnet. I am not so sure. But more importantly, even if it would, the right response is not to refuse legalization but to combine it with rigorous workplace enforcement that takes the demand side seriously. E-Verify, real penalties on employers, end of the don't-ask-don't-tell culture of certain industries. The reason the United States has eleven million irregular migrants is not that they snuck across the border. It is that they were hired. Stop the hiring of new irregular workers — by punishing the employers, not the workers — and the flow will slow dramatically. Legalize those who are already here. This is the deal that has been on the table since Simpson-Mazzoli in 1986, except that we kept the legalization part and abandoned the enforcement part. We should do both this time.

Carens' essay is humane, and I do not want to be churlish about its real virtues. But the humanitarianism is one-eyed. It sees the migrant family in the Chicago neighborhood; it does not see the native-born family three blocks over. A politics of amnesty that does not see both will not build the coalition needed to actually pass an amnesty, and it will not produce a labor market the working class can live in. We have to do better than this.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The presence of a large undocumented workforce creates downward pressure on the wages of the most vulnerable native-born workers, such as high school dropouts and minorities.
  2. 02Irregular status functions as a subsidy for exploitative employers because undocumented workers lack the legal standing to unionize, complain about safety, or demand overtime.
  3. 03Legalization should be framed as a structural necessity for a healthy labor market, as granting full rights to migrants removes the competitive disadvantage faced by native workers.
  4. 04A viable amnesty policy must be coupled with rigorous workplace enforcement and penalties for employers to address the demand side of irregular migration.
  5. 05The 'social membership' framework is insufficient because it fails to reconcile the competing moral claims of migrants and the displaced native-born workers living in the same communities.
