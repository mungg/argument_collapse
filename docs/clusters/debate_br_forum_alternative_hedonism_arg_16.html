Diversified Gemini

"Kate Soper’s vision of 'alternative hedonism' is only viable and equitable if it explicitly centers the care economy and dismantles the gendered division of labor."

Kate Soper’s call for an “alternative hedonism” arrives at a crucial moment. The escalating climate crisis and the psychic toll of late-stage capitalism have laid bare the profound failures of our current economic model. Soper elegantly articulates what many of us feel: the treadmill of endless work and hyper-consumption is not just destroying the biosphere; it is making us miserable. However, in outlining her post-growth vision, Soper overlooks a central axis of human labor and flourishing that must be the foundation of any truly sustainable society: the care economy. A feminist reading of alternative hedonism reveals that the “new consumption” Soper desires is impossible without a radical restructuring of how we value, distribute, and perform care work.

Soper’s vision of a slower, more deliberate life involves a retreat from the convenience economy. She advocates for “self-provisioning, mending, and making do,” a return to “traditional artisanal modes of production,” and local community bartering. On the surface, this sounds charming and ecologically responsible. But we must ask a fundamental feminist question: when we give up the conveniences of modern consumer capitalism, whose time is sacrificed to make up the difference?

Historically and presently, the vast majority of unpaid domestic labor, social reproduction, and care work falls on the shoulders of women. The explosion of the fast-food industry, the reliance on Amazon Prime, and the proliferation of cheap, disposable clothing are certainly ecological disasters, but they are also deeply intertwined with the entry of women into the formal workforce. Without structural support for childcare, eldercare, and domestic labor, time-poor households have relied on the cheap, carbon-intensive conveniences of the market to survive.

If we are to embrace "slow working" and "self-provisioning"—growing our own food, cooking from scratch, mending clothes, and participating in time-intensive community networks—we risk implicitly reinstitutionalizing traditional gender roles, unless the gendered division of labor is explicitly dismantled. Alternative hedonism, left unexamined, could easily devolve into a neo-traditionalist trap where men enjoy the newly won leisure time of a four-day workweek to pursue hobbies, while women use that time to absorb the increased burden of sustainable domestic labor.

A truly progressive alternative hedonism must therefore place care—the work of sustaining human life and well-being—at the very center of its economic and political project. Care work is inherently low-carbon. Teaching, nursing, counseling, child-rearing, community building, and tending to the elderly are activities that generate profound human well-being and social cohesion, but they do not require massive extraction of fossil fuels or the endless production of physical commodities.

If we want to decouple pleasure and fulfillment from resource-intensive consumption, we must elevate care from its current marginalized, underpaid, and often invisible status to the core purpose of our economy. The current system values the production of plastic trinkets over the rearing of a child, and the coding of ad-tracking algorithms over the care of the elderly. This is the profound "spiritual poverty" that Nemonte Nenquimo points to, and it is a distinctly patriarchal capitalist poverty.

To realize an alternative hedonism that does not fall on the backs of women, we must implement policies that socialize the burden of care. Soper rightly mentions a universal basic income and a shorter workweek, but we must go further. We need universal, state-funded childcare; robust elder care networks that treat caregivers with dignity and pay them living wages; and deep investments in communal infrastructure that make "self-provisioning" a shared, public activity rather than isolated domestic drudgery. Neighborhoods should be designed with communal kitchens, shared laundries, and localized care centers, transforming what was once private burden into public conviviality.

Furthermore, redefining pleasure through the lens of care challenges the very notion of hedonism as an individual pursuit. Soper’s essay, while communal in its aspirations, still largely frames pleasure in terms of personal sensual enjoyment—the quiet of a train ride, the satisfaction of making something by hand. But the deepest human pleasures are relational. They are found in the profound connections forged through giving and receiving care, in the solidarity of community support, and in the shared joy of raising the next generation in a secure and loving environment.

A feminist alternative hedonism asserts that we do not need more stuff; we need more time for each other. It recognizes that the most radical thing we can do in a society obsessed with the accumulation of dead capital is to prioritize the flourishing of living beings. By centering the care economy, we can bridge the gap between Soper’s subjective "structure of feeling" and the objective requirement for a low-carbon, post-growth future, ensuring that our transition to a sustainable world is fundamentally just, equitable, and deeply humane.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The retreat from market conveniences toward self-provisioning risks disproportionately increasing the unpaid domestic labor burden on women unless care work is radically restructured.
  2. 02Care-based activities, such as nursing and teaching, are inherently low-carbon and should replace commodity production as the core purpose of a sustainable economy.
  3. 03To prevent alternative hedonism from becoming a neo-traditionalist trap, the state must socialize care through universal childcare, eldercare, and communal infrastructure.
  4. 04True hedonism should be redefined to prioritize relational pleasures found in community solidarity and caregiving rather than just individual sensual enjoyment.
