"The intense intellectual debate surrounding the Araki Yasusada hoax is disproportionate to the actual literary quality of the poems, which are ultimately derivative and mediocre works of workshop surrealism."

It is probably inevitable that a literary hoax of any ambition will generate vastly more commentary than the work itself could ever sustain. The Yasusada affair, now laid out in forensic detail by Marjorie Perloff, is no exception. Yet after reading the dozens of pages devoted to the hoax—its clues, its theoretical implications, its insult to the dead of Hiroshima, its exposure of editorial sloth—I find myself returning to the poems themselves, and I find them, on the whole, rather flimsy. That flimsiness is the real story, and it suggests that the furious debate over authenticity and Orientalism is largely a distraction from a simpler artistic failure.

The centerpiece of Perloff’s analysis, “Telescope with Urn,” is a case in point. It has an arresting opening line and a certain imagistic elegance, but its method—the disconnected declarative sentences, the faux‑Zen repetition, the oblique pathos—is formulaic of a strain of poetry that has been with us since at least the early Imagist translations. Strip away the Hiroshima frame and the biographical folderol, and what remains is a competent piece of workshop surrealism, the kind that might earn a respectful nod in a graduate seminar before being forgotten. The “Mad Daughter and Big‑Bang” poem, likewise, relies on a single, familiar surrealist conceit—the talking severed head—straight out of a thousand magic‑realist fictions. The renga pastiche, with its “sorority girls sing of fucking” and its mix of archaic flora and demotic profanity, is Pound‑lite, a remix of the Cantos’ more outrageous moments without the massive learning and ferocious musicality that made the originals stick to the mind.

This is not to say the hoax is worthless. It has provided a certain wicked pleasure, especially for those of us who have watched the academic humanities turn every poem into a deposition about identity. The spectacle of editors scrambling to return checks and renounce their own earlier enthusiasm is rich in Schadenfreude. But the sheer volume of intellectual energy now being poured into the case—a symposium, long essays, likely a forthcoming book—is out of all proportion to its literary weight. The real Araki Yasusada, had he existed, would likely have been a minor figure, the author of a few lovely but ephemeral lyrics. The fictional Yasusada is minor too, and the hoax’s success tells us less about the merits of the poetry than about the nervous hunger of the moment.

Perloff ends her essay by calling on us to “look searchingly and critically at what is always already there.” It is a fine phrase, but I wonder if it does not unintentionally betray a weariness with the whole exercise. What is already there is a great deal of remarkable poetry, some of it by living Japanese writers, much of it by Americans and Europeans of various stripes, that deserves reading whether or not it arrives with a notarized certificate of suffering. The Yasusada tempest is, finally, a sign of literary decadence—a situation in which the conditions of production and reception have become more fascinating than the art itself. Perhaps the most useful response to the hoax is a shrug: publish the poems if they’re good, expose the hoax if you must, and then let us get back to the work that matters.
