Diversified MiniMax

"While the authors successfully demonstrate that economics is not inherently neoliberal, their project for inclusive prosperity requires a more robust theory of political change and sustainability to ensure their policy proposals can actually be implemented and defended."

The authors offer a compelling rebuttal to the view that economics is simply the handmaiden of neoliberalism. Their central claim—that many dominant policy ideas are supported neither by sound economics nor by good evidence—deserves widespread recognition. The identification of economics with market fundamentalism is wrong, and the EfIP project provides concrete evidence that mainstream economists can contribute to a more inclusive vision of prosperity.

But the essay could go further in addressing the historical roots of the economics-neoliberalism conflation. The Mont Pelerin Society did bring together economists and intellectuals who were hostile to government intervention, and their influence on policy over the past four decades has been substantial. It is not enough to argue that their influence was based on a perversion of economic science; one must also explain why that perversion was so readily accepted and what made it so durable.

The answer, in part, is that the intellectual environment of the 1970s created fertile ground for market-friendly ideas. Stagflation, the failure of Keynesian demand management, and the rise of monetarism combined to undermine confidence in government intervention. Economists like Milton Friedman provided intellectual cover for a political agenda that was already gaining traction, and that agenda in turn shaped which economists were heard in policy circles. The feedback loop between ideas and politics was not one-directional; intellectual developments both reflected and reinforced the shift toward market-oriented policies.

Understanding this feedback loop is essential for anyone who wants to reverse it. The EfIP project can produce excellent policy proposals, but those proposals will not translate into policy unless the political environment changes in ways that make them politically viable. The authors recognize this implicitly when they discuss the need to "rebalance power" in labor and financial markets. But they do not provide a theory of political change that would explain how such rebalancing could actually occur.

The essay's treatment of institutional diversity is one of its strongest points. The authors are right that "plausible institutional diversity is not limited to existing practices" and that "we can—and will need to—develop new institutions." The examples they offer—of different property rights regimes, of wage-setting arrangements, of financial regulation—illustrate the range of possibilities that exist within a broadly market-based framework. This is a valuable corrective to the false choice between unconstrained markets and centralized planning.

However, the authors do not adequately address the question of which institutional arrangements are likely to be politically sustainable. The history of progressive economic policy is littered with reforms that were later reversed when political conditions changed. The New Deal gave way to the backlash of the 1970s; the golden age of collective bargaining was followed by the decline of unions; the regulatory apparatus constructed in the wake of the Great Depression was progressively dismantled. Preventing similar reversals will require not only good policy design but also political strategies that can defend reforms against organized opposition.

The authors write that "democratic political economy—where people's influence on policy is roughly equal and political preferences are arrived at through open, well-informed public debate—must be considered for any policy proposals in 2019." This is correct, but it is easier to state as an aspiration than to achieve in practice. The political economy of contemporary democracies is characterized by profound inequalities of resources and influence, and those inequalities shape which policies are adopted and which are rejected. Addressing them requires not merely technical expertise but also a commitment to building the kind of broad-based political coalitions that can sustain progressive reforms over time.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The authors fail to fully explain why the 'perversion' of economic science into market fundamentalism became so politically dominant and durable in the first place.
  2. 02Technical policy excellence is insufficient for change because ideas and politics exist in a feedback loop where the political environment dictates which economic theories gain traction.
  3. 03The essay lacks a strategy for how to achieve the necessary rebalancing of power in the face of organized political opposition.
  4. 04The authors do not adequately address how to make new institutional arrangements politically sustainable to prevent them from being dismantled like the New Deal or the regulatory era.
  5. 05Achieving the authors' goal of a democratic political economy requires building broad-based political coalitions rather than just providing technical expertise.
Diversified MiniMax

"While the Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) project successfully demonstrates that economics can address inequality, its success depends on building a robust institutional and political infrastructure to challenge the entrenched dominance of neoliberal ideas."

This essay offers a valuable perspective on the relationship between economic ideas and policy outcomes. The authors are correct that economics has been wrongly identified with neoliberalism and that mainstream economists have substantial resources for addressing inequality and market failures. The EfIP project provides concrete evidence of this potential.

However, the essay would benefit from a more sustained engagement with the political economy of economic ideas. The authors write that "conservative foundations and think tanks have monopolized the banner of economics in policy circles," but they do not fully explain how this monopoly was established or how it might be broken. Understanding the mechanisms of intellectual production and dissemination is essential for anyone who wants to change the ideas that circulate in public discourse.

The role of the Mont Pelerin Society in shaping post-1980 economic policy is especially important in this context. The society brought together economists, intellectuals, and business leaders who shared a commitment to market-friendly ideas and who worked to disseminate those ideas through academic positions, media appearances, and policy advice. Their success was not merely intellectual; it was organizational and financial. The conservative foundations that fund think tanks and academic programs created infrastructure for promoting market-friendly ideas that has persisted for decades.

Breaking this infrastructure will require similar efforts on the part of economists who favor more progressive policies. The EfIP network is a step in this direction, but it needs to be embedded in a broader ecosystem of research institutions, media outlets, and policy organizations that can amplify its message. The authors note that "the sociology of the profession—career incentives, norms, socialization patterns—often militates against engagement with the policy world," but they do not address how to change these patterns.

One approach would be to change the incentives facing academic economists. If tenure and promotion decisions gave more weight to policy-relevant research and public engagement, more economists would be likely to pursue such work. This would require changes in the criteria used by hiring and promotion committees, which in turn would require leadership from department chairs, deans, and professional associations. The authors are well-positioned to advocate for such changes, given their prominence in the profession.

The specific policy proposals in the essay are generally sound. The case for wage boards, for taxation based on sales rather than profits, for financial regulation that reduces systemic risk, and for social insurance that protects workers from market fluctuations—all of these reflect both good economics and progressive values. But the authors should recognize that good ideas are not sufficient to change policy; they must be embedded in political strategies that can build coalitions and sustain reforms over time.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The authors must move beyond intellectual arguments to understand and dismantle the organizational and financial infrastructure that conservative think tanks use to monopolize economic discourse.
  2. 02The sociology of the economics profession must be reformed by changing tenure and promotion incentives to reward policy-relevant research and public engagement.
  3. 03The EfIP network needs to be integrated into a broader ecosystem of research institutions and media outlets to effectively amplify its message to the public.
  4. 04Sound economic policy proposals are insufficient for change unless they are embedded in political strategies capable of building coalitions and sustaining long-term reform.
