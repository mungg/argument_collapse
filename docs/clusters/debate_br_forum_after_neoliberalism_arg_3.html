"While the authors successfully demonstrate that modern economics offers pragmatic, evidence-based alternatives to neoliberalism, their framework fails to account for the political power struggles and institutional decay that stand in the way of implementing such reforms."

As a financial journalist who has covered every major policy debate of the past two decades, I have learned to be skeptical of manifestos that promise to transcend old ideological battles through the magic of “evidence-based” policy. So when I began Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman’s essay, my guard was up. By the time I finished, much of my skepticism had melted away—not because I agree with every proposal, but because I recognize something rare and valuable: a serious attempt to bridge the gap between the academic frontier and the world of policy, without dumbing down the complexity or resorting to partisan cheerleading.

The essay’s greatest contribution is its demolition of the false binary between “pro-market” and “anti-market.” By insisting that markets are always embedded in institutions, and that the relevant question is always “what kind of institutions?” the authors reclaim the high ground from both the free-market fundamentalists and the knee-jerk anti-capitalists. The specific proposals—wage boards, financial regulation, global tax reform—are concrete enough to be debated and tested, yet flexible enough to accommodate different national contexts. This is the kind of pragmatic pluralism that American politics desperately needs.

However, as a close observer of the policymaking process, I am haunted by one question: where is the political subject? The essay assumes that “we” can design policies and “we” can convince the public. But who is the “we”? The EfIP network is a group of tenured economists, mostly at elite institutions. Their social and professional position grants them a platform, but it also insulates them from the brutal pressures of electoral politics, lobbying, and media cycles. The essay reads like a policy document from a technocratic administration-in-waiting—a President Elizabeth Warren’s economic brain trust, perhaps. But it never grapples with the fact that the current administration is actively dismantling the regulatory state, and that the left wing of the Democratic Party is more focused on Medicare for All and a Green New Deal than on the design of credit contracts.

The proposals, for all their empirical grounding, have an ethereal quality. They are optimal policies for an idealized polity. The wage board, for example, assumes a functioning tripartite structure that can negotiate in good faith. But where is the modern American labor movement that could supply workers’ representatives with the organizational heft to sit at that table? Union density is at a historic low, and the authors’ own Naidu acknowledges the “pervasive collective action problem.” Reviving unions is a precondition for wage boards to work, not a parallel policy that can be checked off a list. The essay treats these as separate items, but they are deeply interdependent.

Similarly, the proposal to steer technology toward labor-augmenting innovations presumes a government capable of nimble, sophisticated industrial policy. Yet the federal government has largely abandoned that capacity, and the private sector’s venture-capital model is hardwired to seek labor-saving automation. Building a new technology policy architecture is a project that would take decades, not a single administration.

I do not fault the authors for not having all the answers. But I think their intellectual framework, which emphasizes “free lunches” and institutional design, subtly obscures the necessity of conflict. Real change will come only when some groups lose power so others can gain it. The corporate tax reformer’s gain is the shareholder’s loss; the wage board’s gain is the employer’s loss; the financial regulator’s gain is the banker’s loss. There is no way around that, and no amount of clever economics can make those fights disappear. The essay’s tone is one of rational persuasion, as if the authors believe that, armed with the right evidence, they can win the debate on the merits. Perhaps they can, in the seminar room. But in the arena, the other side plays by different rules.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The essay effectively dismantles the false binary between pro-market and anti-market ideologies by showing that markets are always dependent on specific institutional designs.
  2. 02The authors adopt a technocratic 'administration-in-waiting' perspective that ignores the reality of current political polarization and the dismantling of the regulatory state.
  3. 03Many of the proposals are interdependent and rely on existing social structures, such as a robust labor movement, that have already been severely weakened.
  4. 04The framework's focus on 'free lunches' and rational persuasion obscures the fact that meaningful economic reform requires direct conflict with powerful groups who stand to lose from these changes.
  5. 05The proposed policies assume a level of government capacity and nimble industrial policy that the federal government has largely abandoned.

"While the Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) initiative offers a compelling progressive vision, its policy prescriptions are overly reliant on a state-centric framework that lacks a viable plan for international coordination or domestic political mobilization."

The Economics for Inclusive Prosperity initiative lands at a precarious moment. The authors astutely note the “nativist turn” in our politics, fueled by economic chasms that neoliberal policies have torn open. Their response is, in essence, to propose a progressive economic nationalism—a set of policies that would re-embed markets within national social contracts, tax global winners fairly, and give labor a seat at the table. This is a coherent and appealing vision. But as someone who works on global economic governance, I worry that it relies too heavily on a state-centric framework that is already being eroded by the very forces of digitization and capital mobility it seeks to tame.

Consider the centerpiece international proposals: Zucman’s plan to tax multinationals based on sales, and Rodrik’s “social safeguards” in trade agreements. Both are ingenious attempts to claw back policy space for nation-states. Zucman’s proposal, in particular, is an elegantly simple solution to a genuinely hard problem. But it presumes a level of international coordination that is, to put it gently, not forthcoming. The United States could adopt such a system unilaterally, but if other nations do not follow suit, we are back to tax competition, only now over sales thresholds rather than profit shifting. The digital economy, with its intangible assets and network effects, will find new ways to exploit jurisdictional gaps. A global minimum tax, which would require the consent of dozens of sovereign states, is a political non-starter in the current nationalist climate that the authors themselves lament.

Rodrik’s trade proposal faces a similar dilemma. Social safeguards sound reasonable, but who adjudicates what counts as a “just” margin for competition and what is mere protectionism? In a world where the United States is already imposing tariffs on national security grounds and threatening to withdraw from the WTO, adding a new, vague exception for social contracts could become a license for the kind of beggar-thy-neighbor policies that the authors presumably abhor. The essay’s implicit assumption is that a benevolent, democratically accountable government would use these safeguards wisely. But the essay also acknowledges the profound capture of the American state by special interests. Why would the social safeguards process be immune to the same pathologies?

This leads to a deeper tension: the EfIP project simultaneously diagnoses the American state as captured and broken, yet entrusts that same state with enormously expanded responsibilities—wage-setting, technology steering, financial regulation, social insurance. There is no theory of state transformation here, only a hope that better policies, once enacted, will prove durable and self-reinforcing. But the neoliberal era should have taught us that policies are only as solid as the political coalitions that support them. The 1945–75 social democratic compact was not the product of enlightened economic advice; it was the product of a militant labor movement, a devastated capital class seeking legitimacy, and the geopolitical pressures of the Cold War. Absent any analogous forces today, the authors’ policy prescriptions are blueprints for a palace that no one has the power to build.

Finally, the essay’s international dimension is curiously truncated. It focuses on trade and tax, but says nothing about immigration, development assistance, or global climate policy, all of which are inextricable from inclusive prosperity in an interconnected world. A truly inclusive prosperity cannot be built within borders alone, especially when the prosperity of the rich depends on the immiseration of the poor abroad. The authors’ economics may be after neoliberalism, but their frame remains stubbornly national. Perhaps that is a strategic concession to political reality, but it is a concession that limits the moral and practical scope of their vision.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01International tax and trade proposals, such as taxing multinationals by sales or implementing social safeguards, are vulnerable to jurisdictional exploitation and protectionist abuse without unlikely global cooperation.
  2. 02The initiative fails to explain how a state it describes as captured by special interests can be trusted to effectively manage expanded responsibilities like wage-setting and technology steering.
  3. 03The authors overlook the historical reality that major economic shifts require powerful political coalitions and social movements, not just superior academic policy blueprints.
  4. 04The framework's focus on national social contracts ignores critical global issues like immigration and development, limiting the moral and practical scope of its 'inclusive' vision.

"While the Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) network successfully demonstrates that modern economics supports progressive policy, its vision is undermined by a failure to account for the diminished administrative capacity and political dysfunction of the contemporary American state."

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman have written a provocative and largely persuasive brief. By drawing on cutting-edge empirical work, they convincingly demolish the notion that “economics” inevitably prescribes austerity, deregulation, and free trade. The proposal for wage boards, for instance, is a creative extension of the new monopsony literature, and the idea of taxing multinationals based on sales rather than profits elegantly sidesteps the game of jurisdictional hide-and-seek. As a fellow traveler in the campaign for a more realistic, evidence-based economics, I am heartened to see such prominent scholars take a public stand.

However, the essay inadvertently reveals a persistent blind spot in even the most progressive corners of the economics profession: its inability to grapple honestly with the state. The authors’ policy vision is profoundly state-centric. They call for wage boards, public provision of social insurance, government steering of technological innovation, prudential financial regulation, and public investment in education. On every front, the state is the primary agent of inclusive prosperity. Remarkably, the essay never pauses to ask whether the actually existing American state—captured by special interests, gridlocked by polarization, hollowed out by decades of underinvestment in administrative capacity—is capable of executing any of this agenda competently or equitably.

This is not the standard public-choice critique that government is inherently worse than markets. Rather, it is a historical and institutional observation: the U.S. state today is a very different creature from the mid-century state that built the interstate highways, administered the GI Bill, and enforced antitrust. The authors nod toward this reality via Kaplan’s piece on political inequality, but that acknowledgment remains quarantined in its own policy silo. They do not allow the depth of political dysfunction to temper their enthusiasm for the state as an instrument of inclusive prosperity. In effect, they are writing policy prescriptions for a governmental machine that no longer exists, or perhaps never did in the idealized form they imagine.

Consider the wage board proposal. In Australia, where such boards operate, they are embedded in a legal and institutional framework with a history of centralized arbitration and relatively strong unions, overseen by a competent, well-resourced bureaucracy. In the United States, any new wage board would immediately become a site of intense political warfare. Business lobbies would fight to pack the tripartite boards with industry-friendly representatives; administrative procedures would be gummed up by litigation; and the resulting wage schedules would likely reflect political clout as much as economic evidence. The same fate likely awaits any attempt to steer technology toward labor-augmenting innovation: the political muscle of Silicon Valley would render any government “steering” a laughable pipe dream.

These are not arguments against trying. But they are arguments for a much more integrated political-economic analysis. The authors want to design institutions; they should also design the political strategies that might bring those institutions into being and sustain them. That means thinking less like a technocrat offering free lunches and more like a political organizer building a coalition. The “social safeguards” in trade that Rodrik proposes require a broad social movement to demand them, not just an elegant Op-Ed. The EfIP network, composed of tenured academics, is a step toward changing the intellectual climate, but it is far removed from the brutal trenches of actual policymaking.

If economists want to serve inclusive prosperity, they must descend from the realm of policy design into the messy world of power, organizing, and state-building. Until then, their policy briefs will remain, like so many before them, a library of elegant solutions in search of a functional polity.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The authors' policy prescriptions are overly state-centric and assume a level of governmental competence and neutrality that does not exist in the current era of special-interest capture and polarization.
  2. 02Proposed interventions, such as wage boards or technology steering, would likely be co-opted or paralyzed by the political clout of business lobbies and Silicon Valley in the absence of a functional bureaucracy.
  3. 03The essay treats political inequality as a separate policy silo rather than a fundamental constraint that should temper the authors' enthusiasm for state-led solutions.
  4. 04To be effective, economists must move beyond technocratic design and integrate political strategy and coalition-building into their framework for institutional change.

"The authors' vision for 'inclusive prosperity' is a flawed technocratic project that overestimates the ability of experts to engineer social outcomes while ignoring the political and administrative constraints that lead to government failure."

It is a sign of the times that three of the most creative economists of their generation feel compelled to write a manifesto reclaiming their discipline from the simplistic market fundamentalism that has, in fact, been its public calling card for decades. Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman deserve credit for assembling a genuinely impressive set of policy proposals under the banner of “inclusive prosperity.” Yet for all its sophistication, this essay fundamentally misdiagnoses the problem. The issue is not that economics has been misunderstood or misapplied; it is that the progressive policies they endorse require a degree of state capacity and political consensus that their own framework systematically undermines.

The authors are right that modern economics is not a monolith. They point to the revival of economic history, behavioral economics, and the empirical turn. But notice what these have in common: they are all, in the end, technocratic correctives. They refine our understanding of market failures so that we can design better interventions—wage boards, Pigouvian taxes, prudential regulations. They remain firmly within what James C. Scott called the “high modernist” tradition: the belief that a scientifically informed state can engineer better outcomes. The essay’s repeated invocation of “free lunches”—policies that enhance both efficiency and equity—is a classic technocratic trope: the promise that we can have it all without hard tradeoffs, if only we are smart enough.

The real world, however, is defined by tragic trade-offs, not free lunches. Consider the wage board proposal. In a frictionless model, setting minimum wages at industry-occupation-region levels might reduce inequality without employment loss. But in a world of limited administrative capacity, regulatory capture, and geographically mobile capital, the outcomes will be messier. Firms will lobby to classify themselves into favorable categories; workers in declining regions will find their nominal wage floors undercut by informal employment; and investors will adjust their portfolios to avoid the most regulated sectors. The net effect on the least well-off is deeply uncertain. The authors acknowledge none of these implementation challenges because their framework encourages them to see only the “free lunch” in the diagram, not the messy politics of the lunch line.

This points to a deeper issue. The essay treats government as a black box that implements the economist’s design. But the same body of economic thought that yields these proposals also, in its public-choice variant, explains why those proposals will be distorted. If politicians and bureaucrats respond to incentives, why should we expect wage boards to be set by dispassionate experts rather than by the most powerful interests? The authors want to have it both ways: they embrace the rational-actor model when designing incentives for firms and workers, but assume a benevolent, omniscient planner when designing institutions. This schizophrenic view of human nature is the weak foundation upon which their inclusive prosperity edifice rests.

A more coherent approach would start from the insight that all human beings, including those in government, are fallible and self-interested, and that knowledge is dispersed and often tacit. From those premises, one would be far more skeptical of large-scale government redesign of labor markets, technological paths, and capital flows. One might still conclude that some interventions are warranted—a carbon tax, a basic social insurance floor—but one would do so with far more humility and a far greater emphasis on market-based mechanisms that do not presuppose bureaucratic wisdom.

The authors have, perhaps unwittingly, produced a manifesto for a technocratic progressivism that shares many of neoliberalism’s original sins: an overconfidence in expert knowledge, a blindness to political economy, and an insufficient appreciation for the creative, decentralized forces of markets. True economics after neoliberalism would not resurrect the planner’s toolkit with a progressive gloss; it would develop a genuinely institutional approach that takes constraints—including political constraints—as seriously as market failures.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The essay relies on the 'free lunch' trope to suggest that efficiency and equity can be balanced without hard trade-offs, ignoring the messy implementation challenges of the real world.
  2. 02The authors' framework suffers from a 'schizophrenic' view of human nature, assuming individuals are self-interested in markets but that government actors will behave as benevolent, omniscient planners.
  3. 03The proposed interventions require a level of state capacity and dispassionate expertise that is unrealistic given the inevitability of regulatory capture and political distortion.
  4. 04The manifesto repeats the 'high modernist' errors of neoliberalism by prioritizing top-down expert knowledge over the decentralized, tacit knowledge found in markets.
  5. 05A more robust post-neoliberal economics should be rooted in institutional humility and a realistic assessment of political constraints rather than a resurrection of the planner's toolkit.
