Diversified MiniMax

"The government should not restrict civilian drone use for privacy reasons because being observed from public airspace does not violate any established legal right to privacy."

Here's a radical proposal: maybe we shouldn't be asking whether drones should be restricted at all. Privacy advocates keep invoking the "peering over fences" scenario as if it's obviously problematic, but consider what they're actually arguing. If a neighbor climbs a ladder to look into your yard, that's rude but legal. If they fly a kite at rooftop level, that's fine too. A drone isn't meaningfully different—it's just a more convenient ladder.

The outdoor privacy argument rests on a category mistake. Privacy law protects against intrusion into private spaces, not against being observed from public ones. You don't have a reasonable expectation of privacy in your backyard any more than on your front porch. Courts have been consistent on this for over a century. The fact that drones make observation easier doesn't change the underlying principle—it just makes observers more efficient.

What privacy advocates really seem to fear isn't legal violations but social discomfort. But discomfort with new technologies is constant and usually transitory. When security cameras became cheap, everyone panicked. Today they're unremarkable. Drones will follow the same trajectory.

More importantly, heavy-handed regulation will simply move drone activity into informal channels. Amateur fliers won't register with the FAA; they'll just fly anyway. Better to accept drones as part of the landscape and let social norms develop organically. Eventually, persistent drone stalkers will face social opprobrium even if they face no legal sanction. That's how privacy norms actually form—not through legislation, but through practice.
