"The authors' proposal for defeating authoritarianism is insufficient because it remains confined to electoral messaging and framing rather than building the independent, material institutions necessary to sustain democratic life."

Bonica and Grumbach have written a rigorous critique of the popularist fantasy—the notion that Democrats can poll-test their way to victory in an age of authoritarian populism. But their own proposal, however well-intentioned and empirically grounded, suffers from a deeper blind spot: it remains a politics of the word, not the deed. They call for reframing, messaging, authenticity. What they do not call for, with nearly enough urgency, is the building of parallel institutions that can sustain democratic life even as formal institutions are captured. In the fight against authoritarianism, the most important work happens outside electoral politics altogether.

The essay correctly identifies corruption as a potent wedge issue. But corruption is not just a frame; it is a material reality that demands institutional counterweights. When the authors say that “successful anti-authoritarian movements don’t win by moderating their positions on a traditional left-right axis but by creating an entirely new one,” they gesture toward the crucial insight: politics is not a marketplace of policy preferences; it is a struggle over who controls the institutions that shape daily life. In a truly authoritarian trajectory, the formal political system—elections, legislatures, courts—can become tools of repression or, at best, empty formalities. What protects democracy in such moments is a dense fabric of independent associations: free and combative unions, independent media, cooperative businesses, faith communities that refuse to bow to state ideology, and networks of mutual aid that make people less dependent on a hostile government.

Consider the countries that have successfully resisted democratic backsliding. In Hungary, the primary organized resistance has come not from a rejigged electoral strategy but from a network of civil society organizations, independent universities like the Central European University (before it was forced out), and cultural institutions that sustain a pluralistic public sphere. In Poland, the revitalization of civil society after 2015—women’s strikes, independent courts of opinion, local government coalitions—was instrumental in keeping the democratic flame alive until an electoral opening appeared. In Brazil, the anti-Bolsonaro front was anchored not just in Lula’s Workers’ Party but in the powerful social movements of the MST (landless workers), the feminist and black movements, and the progressive sectors of the Catholic Church. In all of these cases, the electoral victory was downstream of years of organizing and institution-building outside the party system.

The Democratic Party’s weakness is not primarily that it has the wrong message; it’s that it has allowed itself to become a hollow, candidate-centered fundraising machine with shallow roots in communities. For decades, the party has offloaded its organizing responsibilities onto outside groups—MoveOn, the ACLU, Planned Parenthood—that can rally activists around specific issues but cannot build the long-term, place-based relationships that produce durable political power. The authors celebrate the idea of recruiting “authentic, working-class candidates” as if the politician is the unit of change. But the politician is the tip of the iceberg. Beneath must lie a mass of organized people who can hold that politician accountable, fund their own campaigns, and provide a base of power independent of the donor class. That requires a strategic reorientation toward base-building organizations: tenant unions, worker centers in the gig economy, rural cooperatives, community land trusts, and local media experiments.

The essay’s emphasis on turnout is a step in the right direction, but turnout is best understood as the outcome of organizational density, not of messaging. Research on civic engagement shows that people are far more likely to vote when they are embedded in networks where norms of participation are strong and where they see a direct link between collective action and improved circumstances. The decline in voter turnout since the mid-20th century is not primarily a story of apathy; it is a story of the collapse of mass-membership organizations—unions, churches, fraternal groups—that once bridged the gap between private life and public power. Rebuilding that associational infrastructure is a generational project, but it must begin now, because when formal democracy comes under direct attack, the only thing standing between a people and a dictatorship is their capacity for collective action outside the state.

An anti-corruption frame, by itself, doesn’t build this infrastructure. It’s a media narrative, a campaign theme. The authors might reply that their call for “genuine, costly reform” and a “thousand flowers blooming” is exactly about building credibility through action. But the actions they propose—rejecting corporate money, banning stock trading—are still entirely within the electoral sphere. They are good housekeeping, not transformation. To truly shift the axis of politics, Democrats would need to invest a significant portion of their campaign war chests into grantmaking for independent organizing, much as the early CIO unions funded organizing drives that later created the political basis for the New Deal coalition. They would need to encourage their elected officials to spend time not just at fundraisers but at strike lines, eviction defenses, and community meetings where they listen more than they talk. And they would need to accept that some of the groups they support will criticize them, because that’s what an autonomous civil society does.

The left has a romantic tendency to fetishize movements, and movements are insufficient without electoral vehicles. But the converse is also true: electoral vehicles without movements are empty. The authors recognize that the old rules are dead, but they then propose a new set of rules that are still confined to the electoral arena. The real shift is to recognize that in an era of democratic breakdown, the boundary between electoral politics and social movement organizing must be nearly porous. The party should become a platform for organizing, not just a machine for winning offices that it then cannot use because the Senate is malapportioned and the Supreme Court is captured.

One immediate implication: the Democratic Party should stop treating safe blue states and cities as ATMs for national campaigns and instead use them as laboratories for dual power. Let California, New York, and Illinois build public banks, establish community broadband, fund cooperative housing, and create state-level universal healthcare. These tangible achievements would serve as demonstration projects that a different kind of society is possible, directly improving lives and showing what the party stands for beyond rhetoric. They would also create constituencies—public employees, union workers, beneficiaries of new programs—with a material stake in defending democratic governance. That’s how you mobilize people: not by asking them to vote against corruption, but by giving them something to defend.

History will judge this moment harshly if Democrats respond to authoritarianism with only a better slogan. The essay’s call to “reframe the battlefield” is necessary but insufficient. The battlefield itself must be expanded to include the DSA chapter in a working-class suburb, the community newspaper in a rural county, the tenants’ union in a big apartment complex. These are the trenches from which democracy will be defended, and they cannot be built overnight. The party must invest in them now, even if they yield no immediate electoral payoff, because without them, all the framing in the world will not stop the bulldozer.
