Vanilla Claude

"While the behavioral insights and randomized evaluations presented by Glennerster and Kremer are valuable, their claim that a portable toolkit of small behavioral changes can reliably drive large-scale development results overlooks critical institutional and contextual complexities."

The case Glennerster and Kremer make is genuinely exciting, and the accumulated evidence from the J-PAL network and its collaborators over the past fifteen years has changed how serious people think about anti-poverty programs. They are right that the "you don't value what you don't pay for" intuition has been embarrassed repeatedly, and right that small frictions — a thirty-cent fee, a 3.5-minute walk, an unmet need for cash on the day school fees are due — have effects that no plausible rational-agent model would predict. The lentils-for-immunization result from Banerjee and Duflo's Udaipur work, the deworming cascade, the Progresa/Oportunidades evaluations: these are now anchor points in development economics for good reason.

But I want to push back on three places where the essay glides past harder problems.

First, the leap from "behavioral frictions matter at the margin" to "small changes drive big results" risks confusing point estimates with program design. Consider the chlorine dispenser. It is a beautiful example of behaviorally-informed design — visible reminder, default quantity, peer monitoring, free at point of use. But the dispenser only works because someone refills it, maintains it, and replaces broken parts. The two-dollars-per-case-of-diarrhea figure rests on the dispenser actually functioning at steady state, which in turn rests on an institutional supply chain whose costs are notoriously hard to capture in a controlled trial. The same caveat applies to deworming campaigns, which depend on teachers actually distributing pills, and to conditional cash transfers, which depend on a state capable of verifying school attendance and moving money. Mexico in 1997 had that state; Sierra Leone, the essay's other example, did not. "Small changes" is sometimes shorthand for "we held the institutional scaffolding constant and varied one input." Once you try to scale, the scaffolding becomes the binding constraint.

Second, I think the essay is too quick to dismiss the external-validity critics. It is true that hundreds of RCTs are now finding family resemblances — price-sensitivity for preventive health, present bias, peer effects. But "family resemblance" is doing a lot of work. The Malawi finding that the smallest CCT achieved the average effect, the Kenyan finding that uniforms reduced teen pregnancy, the Indian call-center result on girls' schooling — each of these depends on local elasticities that we have no theoretical reason to expect to be stable across settings. Angus Deaton's recent critique deserves more engagement than the essay gives it. A randomized trial tells you what happened in this village, with this NGO, under this implementation. The inferential step from there to "people procrastinate, so put deadlines on chlorine adoption everywhere" is not itself randomized. It is an act of theoretical interpretation, and behavioral economics is still a thin enough theoretical structure that competing interpretations are easy to construct. Is low chlorine uptake about present bias, or about (rational) skepticism that the water is actually unsafe, or about distrust of outside products, or about taste? Different stories imply different policies.

Third, the essay's treatment of user fees is one-sided in a way that should make us nervous. Yes, removing fees raises uptake dramatically; the Cohen-Dupas bednet study is convincing on that. But Glennerster and Kremer concede, almost in passing, that fees may motivate frontline workers to show up and may provide flexible funding where governments fail to deliver budgets. These are not small concessions. In much of sub-Saharan Africa, the binding constraint on health delivery is not consumer demand but absentee providers and stockouts. If abolishing fees produces a surge of patients arriving at clinics where the nurse is absent and the drug shelf is empty, we may have moved the bottleneck rather than removed it. The essay's own line — "we don't know the impact of fees on the supply side" — is, I think, the most important sentence in the piece, and it deserves to be at the center rather than the periphery of the argument.

None of this is an argument against the experimental turn in development economics. It is the most intellectually honest thing that has happened to the field in a generation, and the discipline it imposes — go look, measure, replicate — is exactly what the macro-development debates of the 1990s lacked. But I'd urge a sharper distinction between two claims that the essay sometimes runs together. One claim is methodological: randomized evaluation is a powerful tool for learning what works in specific contexts. That is now beyond serious dispute. The other claim is substantive: there exists a portable behavioral toolkit — small incentives, default options, convenience, deadlines — that yields large welfare gains across the developing world. This second claim is plausible and partly evidenced, but it is also exactly the kind of "big-think" generalization that the experimental approach was supposed to make us suspicious of. The honest answer, in 2011, is that we are at the beginning of knowing, not the end.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The success of behavioral interventions often depends on an underlying institutional 'scaffolding'—such as supply chains and state capacity—that is much harder to maintain and scale than the small changes themselves.
  2. 02The results of randomized controlled trials are difficult to generalize because behavioral responses are often driven by local elasticities and specific cultural interpretations that may not remain stable across different settings.
  3. 03Focusing on increasing consumer demand by removing user fees may be counterproductive if it ignores supply-side constraints, such as provider absenteeism and the loss of flexible funding for clinics.
  4. 04The essay risks creating a new 'big-think' generalization by assuming a universal behavioral toolkit exists, which contradicts the experimental approach’s core principle of skepticism toward broad, unproven claims.
