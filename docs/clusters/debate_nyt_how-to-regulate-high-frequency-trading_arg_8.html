"To ensure market stability and fairness, regulators should deliberately slow down high-frequency trading through financial transaction taxes, minimum order resting times, and stricter obligations for fast traders."

High-frequency trading should be slowed down deliberately. The question is not whether computers can trade faster than people; of course they can. The question is whether society gains anything from an arms race measured in microseconds. It is hard to see the social value of firms spending fortunes on co-location, microwave links and algorithms whose main purpose is to jump ahead of other orders or exploit tiny, momentary discrepancies among trading venues.

The Knight Capital fiasco is only the latest warning. The May 2010 flash crash showed that liquidity supplied by fast traders can disappear just when it is needed most. Investors watched blue-chip stocks trade at absurd prices, then were told afterward that the system mostly worked. That is not reassuring. A market that can go haywire in minutes because machines interact in unpredictable ways is not a healthy market.

Regulation should change the incentives. A small financial transaction tax, even one measured in fractions of a cent per share, would barely affect long-term investors but would make hyperactive strategies less profitable. Regulators should also consider minimum resting times for displayed orders. If a quote is posted to the public market, it should be real for more than a blink. Otherwise, displayed liquidity becomes a mirage.

Some will argue that these measures would widen spreads. Perhaps they would, slightly. But the narrowest possible spread is not the only goal of a capital market. Stability, fairness and confidence matter too. If investors believe the market is rigged for those who can afford the fastest machines, they will demand a risk premium or withdraw altogether. A penny saved on a spread is not worth a market that looks like a casino run by robots.

High-frequency traders often say they are modern market makers. Traditional market makers, however, had obligations as well as privileges. They were expected to maintain orderly markets, not merely participate when profitable and vanish when volatility rises. If fast traders want the benefits of privileged access and exchange incentives, they should accept obligations: meaningful quoting requirements, capital standards and penalties for disruptive order behavior.

The average American investor should not panic and sell a retirement portfolio because of Knight. But he should be angry. Public equity markets are a national asset. They should serve companies raising capital and investors saving for the future, not just firms racing to shave microseconds from trades. A modest tax, real quote obligations and speed limits would restore balance.
