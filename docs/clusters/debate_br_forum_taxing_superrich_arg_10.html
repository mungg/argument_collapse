"A federal wealth tax is a necessary democratic response to the durable political and social influence wielded by the superrich, which the current tax system fails to address."

Emmanuel Saez and Gabriel Zucman are right to insist that the debate over taxing the superrich is not only about revenue. It is about power. The central fact of American political economy today is not simply that some people have very large fortunes, but that those fortunes buy durable influence over law, policy, markets, universities, philanthropy, and public imagination. In that sense a wealth tax is not a strange new intrusion into private life. It is a democratic response to a form of power that our existing tax system barely touches.

The most persuasive part of their argument is their insistence that the income tax, by itself, cannot reach the richest Americans. Public debate often proceeds as if the “rich” are all high earners: surgeons, lawyers, athletes, entertainers, executives with big salaries. But the truly rich are owners. They need not receive wages. They can decide whether profits are distributed or retained. They can borrow against appreciated assets. They can defer realization for years, sometimes decades. They can die holding appreciated assets and pass them on with favorable treatment. An income tax organized around annual cash receipts will always be vulnerable to this basic asymmetry: workers are taxed when paid, while owners can often choose when, whether, and how taxable income appears.

This is why the familiar objection—“just raise the top income tax rate”—misses the target. I support higher top income tax rates. The reduction of the top federal rate from its mid-twentieth-century levels to today’s 37 percent is a central part of the story of American inequality. But if Jeff Bezos or Warren Buffett can report relatively little taxable income in a given year, the statutory rate on top ordinary income is not the whole answer. The tax base matters. A high rate on a narrow and avoidable base is not enough.

Saez and Zucman also correctly reject the notion that wealth taxation is alien to American tradition. Middle-class Americans pay wealth taxes all the time. They call them property taxes. A family whose main asset is a house pays an annual levy on the market value of that asset whether or not the house has produced cash income that year. The great exception is not that ordinary Americans’ wealth is taxed; it is that the wealth of the very rich, which is disproportionately held in financial assets, closely held businesses, partnerships, and trusts, largely escapes annual taxation.

This asymmetry is especially galling because housing wealth is often far less liquid and far less diversified than billionaire wealth. A homeowner may have a large share of her net worth in a single house and still owe property taxes every year. Yet we are told to worry that a person with hundreds of millions of dollars in stock, partnership interests, and credit access cannot possibly pay a wealth tax without intolerable hardship. Liquidity is a real design problem in some cases, but as a general objection it is unconvincing. The superrich are not helpless custodians of inert assets. They are usually the people best positioned to convert paper wealth into cash on favorable terms.

The democracy argument is still more important. Great fortunes do not sit quietly in bank vaults. They endow think tanks, fund litigation, influence universities, lobby Congress, finance campaigns and Super PACs, shape local politics, and determine which ideas are treated as “serious.” Sometimes this influence is direct and crude; often it is indirect and respectable. Either way, concentrated wealth produces concentrated voice. A constitutional democracy formally assigns each adult one vote, but a society of billionaires and precarious workers does not give each person equal political power in any meaningful sense.

Critics of Saez and Zucman often respond that wealth is not necessarily ill-gotten. Many fortunes are tied to genuine innovation, entrepreneurship, and risk-taking. That is true, but it is not decisive. The wealth tax need not rest on the claim that every billionaire is a robber baron. The problem is structural. Even fortunes initially earned through socially valuable activity can become dynastic power. A successful founder may create a useful company; his grandchildren may inherit political dominance. Moreover, the market value of a firm reflects not only individual genius but also public goods: roads, courts, schools, basic science, the Internet, patent law, financial regulation, and a stable currency. Great fortunes are co-produced by society. Asking a larger annual contribution from those fortunes is not confiscation; it is an overdue accounting.

The strongest practical objection concerns valuation. Saez and Zucman’s answer—that most top wealth is easy to value, and that the rest can be estimated using market comparables, observed transactions, and in-kind payment—deserves to be taken seriously. No tax is perfectly administered. The income tax requires valuations, classifications, timing rules, transfer-pricing rules, and anti-abuse doctrines. The estate tax already requires valuation of closely held businesses. Property taxes require assessment of real estate. The question is not whether a wealth tax can be administered with mathematical perfection. It is whether it can be administered well enough to improve on the status quo. Given modern financial reporting, third-party information, and a properly funded IRS, the answer is yes.

Still, supporters of a wealth tax should not pretend design will be easy. The United States would need strong anti-avoidance rules. It would need an exit tax for those who renounce citizenship or move assets abroad. It would need serious reporting requirements for trusts, foundations, partnerships, and offshore entities. It would need penalties large enough to deter concealment. It would need to reverse the long starvation of the IRS, whose enforcement capacity has been weakened precisely when high-end tax avoidance has become more sophisticated. A wealth tax without enforcement would become a symbolic gesture. A wealth tax with enforcement would be a serious instrument of democratic government.

There is also the constitutional question. Opponents argue that a federal wealth tax is a “direct tax” that must be apportioned among the states, which would make it unworkable. Supporters argue that the Supreme Court’s doctrine leaves room for such a tax, especially in light of the Sixteenth Amendment and later cases. I am not a constitutional lawyer, but the uncertainty is real. Yet uncertainty is not a reason to abandon the policy. Congress often legislates in contested constitutional terrain. If necessary, advocates should also be willing to pursue backup strategies: mark-to-market taxation of billionaire assets, stronger estate and gift taxes, taxation of unrealized gains at death, and surtaxes on extreme capital income. But the constitutional difficulty should not be inflated into an excuse for inaction.

Where I part slightly from Saez and Zucman is on political sequencing. Their radical wealth tax—10 percent above $1 billion—has a clear philosophical appeal if the goal is deconcentration rather than revenue. I agree that democracy should not be indifferent to centibillionaires. At some level, private wealth becomes incompatible with republican equality. But the immediate political task may be to establish the principle and machinery of annual wealth taxation first. A moderate tax with high exemption thresholds would reach only the richest fraction of households, raise substantial revenue, and generate information the government currently lacks. Once the system exists and proves workable, democratic politics can decide whether rates should rise.

The present moment makes the argument more urgent. As of mid-March 2020, the coronavirus pandemic is revealing how dependent we are on public capacity: public health systems, unemployment insurance, paid leave, hospitals, scientific agencies, and emergency fiscal action. For decades Americans have been told that government is incompetent and unaffordable, even as private fortunes have reached heights unseen in modern history. A wealth tax would not solve a pandemic. But it would help rebuild the fiscal and moral basis of a state capable of protecting its people.

The best defense of the wealth tax is therefore not punitive. It is civic. The question is what kind of republic we want to be. One model tolerates unlimited private accumulation and then asks billionaires to repair social damage through philanthropy. Another model taxes great fortunes democratically and uses the proceeds through public institutions accountable, however imperfectly, to citizens. The first model flatters the benefactor. The second respects the public.

Saez and Zucman are right that the aim should be more than making billionaires pay a bit more. The aim should be to prevent any private fortune from becoming so large that it rivals democratic government itself. A wealth tax is not a complete program for equality. We need labor law reform, universal health care, campaign finance reform, stronger antitrust enforcement, public investment, and a renewed estate tax. But an annual tax on extreme wealth belongs at the center of that agenda because it addresses the place where today’s tax system is most visibly broken: the enormous fortunes that grow almost untouched while ordinary people are taxed with every paycheck.

The United States has repeatedly reinvented its tax system in response to democratic crisis: the Civil War, the Progressive Era, the New Deal, World War II. We are again in such a moment. A republic that can tax wages, homes, and consumption can tax billionaires’ fortunes. The obstacle is not technical impossibility. It is political will.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The existing income tax system is structurally incapable of reaching the truly wealthy because they can choose when and how to realize taxable income, unlike wage earners.
  2. 02Wealth taxation is consistent with American tradition, as middle-class citizens already pay annual wealth taxes through property levies on their homes.
  3. 03Concerns regarding liquidity and valuation are manageable through modern financial reporting, market comparables, and the ability of the superrich to convert assets to cash.
  4. 04Extreme concentrations of wealth create a structural imbalance in political power that undermines the principle of one person, one vote.
  5. 05Great fortunes are co-produced by public goods and societal infrastructure, making a wealth tax a fair accounting for the social costs of protecting and enabling that wealth.
  6. 06While constitutional and administrative challenges exist, they are not insurmountable and should not serve as excuses for political inaction in the face of rising inequality.
  7. 07A wealth tax shifts the social model from one of billionaire-led philanthropy to one of public institutions funded by democratic and accountable taxation.
