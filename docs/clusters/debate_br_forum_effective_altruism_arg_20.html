"While effective altruism provides a necessary, evidence-based framework for philanthropy, it must be expanded to value systemic, long-term, and complex interventions that are difficult to quantify but essential for human flourishing."

As someone sympathetic to the demand for evidence in philanthropy, I welcome Peter Singer’s essay. For too long, charitable giving has been insulated from the standards we apply elsewhere. We would not buy medicine merely because an advertisement moved us. We would not invest retirement savings in a company that refused to disclose its performance. Yet donors routinely give to organizations with little idea whether their programs work. Singer and the effective altruists are right to insist that good intentions are not enough.

Still, the phrase “doing the most good” can create a misleading impression of precision. In some domains we have relatively strong evidence. Insecticide-treated bed nets reduce malaria transmission. Vaccination saves lives. Some deworming programs appear to improve health and school attendance, though the broader long-term effects remain debated. Cash transfers can increase consumption and give poor households flexibility. Randomized controlled trials and careful cost-effectiveness analysis have improved our understanding of such interventions.

But philanthropy is not medicine alone. Much of what people reasonably value—legal empowerment, women’s rights, investigative journalism, government accountability, institution building, peacebuilding, climate mitigation—is difficult to evaluate in the same way. A campaign to change a law may fail for ten years and then suddenly transform millions of lives. A watchdog organization may prevent abuses that never appear in statistics because they did not happen. A community organizer may build trust that later becomes political capacity. If our evidentiary standards privilege interventions with easily counted outputs, we may systematically underfund work whose impact is indirect, delayed, or collective.

This is not an argument for abandoning measurement. It is an argument for matching methods to questions. The effective altruist movement has sometimes been caricatured as believing only randomized trials count. That is unfair; many effective altruists are more sophisticated than their critics. But the public examples—bed nets, cost per life saved, dollars per disability-adjusted life year—encourage a narrow view. Donors attracted by Singer’s essay may conclude that the only responsible gifts are those blessed by a small number of evaluators. That would be unfortunate.

Even in global health, cost-effectiveness estimates are not as simple as they look. The cost “to save a life” depends on assumptions about disease prevalence, proper use of bed nets, local health systems, distribution costs, insecticide resistance, and what would have happened without the donor’s gift. Charities can absorb only so much money at a given level of effectiveness. A program that looks excellent at $5 million may look weaker at $50 million. Conversely, an organization that appears less efficient may be working in harder places or serving populations others neglect.

There is also the problem of comparability. Singer asks us to recognize that saving a life is better than granting a wish. In that case, comparison is easy. But many real choices are not. How should we compare preventing a child’s death with reducing severe depression, preserving a forest, protecting a dissident newspaper, preventing animal suffering, or reducing the risk of catastrophic climate change? Effective altruism does not eliminate moral judgment; it relocates it. The weights assigned to different outcomes are not delivered by data. They come from ethical commitments.

The best version of effective altruism is therefore not a calculator that issues final answers. It is a disciplined practice of inquiry. It asks donors to be explicit about goals, to seek the best available evidence, to consider opportunity costs, to update beliefs, and to take seriously the equal worth of distant people. This is a profound improvement over “follow your passion.” Passion can help choose a field of concern, but it cannot be the final test of whether one is helping.

Singer’s criticism of large gifts to wealthy universities and cultural institutions is especially apt. When Princeton and Yale have endowments measured in tens of billions, alumni should ask what their next dollar actually accomplishes. The same question applies to hospitals that put donors’ names on buildings while basic public health remains underfunded. The problem is not that education or art lacks value. It is that marginal donations to elite institutions often buy prestige more than impact.

Where Singer is less satisfying is in his confidence that effective altruism can tell us what a “minimally acceptable ethical life” requires. I share his view that affluent people should give substantially more than many now do. But the ethical life includes more than donation. It includes how we vote, work, consume, parent, invest, and participate in institutions. A person might give 20 percent of income to excellent charities while working for a company that pollutes, exploits, or corrupts. Another might give little money but spend her life reforming a public agency that serves millions. Measurement must not tempt us to count only what comes with receipts.

What should donors do now? First, give more than they are comfortable giving, especially if they are affluent. Second, move a significant portion of their giving toward high-confidence, high-impact interventions benefiting the very poor. Third, reserve some giving for riskier systemic work, while demanding plausible theories of change rather than sentimental appeals. Fourth, support better evaluation itself, including evaluation of advocacy and institutional reform. Finally, remain humble. Evidence does not free us from uncertainty; it teaches us to navigate uncertainty responsibly.

Singer has done philanthropy a service by challenging donors to ask whether their generosity works. The next step is to ensure that “works” is understood broadly enough to include not only what is easily measured, but also what is essential to human flourishing and justice.
