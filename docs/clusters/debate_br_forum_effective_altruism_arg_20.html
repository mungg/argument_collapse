"The effective altruism framework, as presented by Peter Singer, is philosophically inconsistent because it systematically prioritizes human welfare while neglecting the immense and preventable suffering of animals."

Peter Singer's essay treats the prevention of human suffering as the primary moral priority. Malaria, extreme poverty, premature death—these are indeed serious evils, and Singer is right that we should take effective action against them. But the framework is conspicuously silent on another form of suffering that may be even more widespread and systematically ignored: the suffering of animals.

Singer mentions briefly that effective altruists believe the suffering of animals counts too, but this acknowledgment is underdeveloped. If we take seriously the principle that all suffering matters, regardless of species, then the implications for ethical behavior are radical. Billions of animals suffer in factory farms, in laboratories, in the wild. Their interests deserve moral consideration alongside those of humans.

Consider the Batkid example. Singer calculates that $7,500 given to malaria prevention could save three lives. He contrasts this with Make-A-Wish's cost, implying that the latter is less effective. But this comparison considers only human lives. What about the animals whose suffering could be reduced by alternative allocations? Factory farming causes suffering on a scale that makes even global poverty look small. A donation that reduces animal suffering might do more total good than one that saves human lives.

Some effective altruists have recognized this. They focus on reducing animal suffering through vegan advocacy, support for alternative proteins, or funding of welfare improvements for farm animals. But this cause receives a tiny fraction of the attention and resources devoted to global health. Singer's essay exemplifies this bias. Despite the explicit acknowledgment that animal suffering counts, the essay's examples and arguments center entirely on human welfare.

This is not surprising, given human self-interest and the cognitive biases that Singer himself identifies. We are drawn to identifiable human faces, not to the suffering of factory-farmed pigs or caged chickens. But effective altruism is supposed to overcome such biases through reason and evidence. If reason tells us that animal suffering matters morally, then it should also tell us to prioritize causes that reduce such suffering.

The utilitarian calculus that underlies effective altruism actually supports this conclusion. Peter Singer is one of the most prominent contemporary utilitarians, and his work on animal ethics is foundational. He has long argued that the capacity for suffering is the basis of moral consideration, and that animals who can suffer have interests that deserve weight. There is no principled reason why this insight should not be central to effective altruism rather than a footnote.

I suspect the reason for the neglect is pragmatic: animal advocacy is harder to justify to potential donors than human-focused causes. But this pragmatic concession undermines the philosophical coherence of the movement. Effective altruism cannot claim to apply reason and evidence impartially while systematically ignoring the largest source of preventable suffering on the planet.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The scale of suffering in factory farming is so vast that it may outweigh the impact of traditional human-focused global health initiatives.
  2. 02Singer’s own utilitarian principles dictate that the capacity for suffering, regardless of species, should be the primary basis for moral consideration.
  3. 03The movement's focus on human causes reflects the very cognitive biases and emotional partiality that effective altruism claims to overcome through reason.
  4. 04Pragmatic concerns about donor appeal do not justify the movement's failure to impartially address the largest sources of suffering on the planet.
  5. 05Effective altruism cannot claim to be a truly evidence-based movement if it treats animal welfare as a secondary concern or a footnote.
