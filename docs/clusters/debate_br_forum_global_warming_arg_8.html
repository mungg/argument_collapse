"While Kerry Emanuel’s assessment of climate change is scientifically grounded, it is overly cautious and understates the severity of the risks regarding social equity, ice sheet stability, and long-term planetary transformation."

The Understated Catastrophe

Kerry Emanuel has done a great service by writing about climate science with the calm authority of someone who actually does the work. His essay is sober, fair-minded, and—I want to argue—too gentle by half. By the standards of public discourse, where commentators still treat the entire problem as somehow open to question, Emanuel sounds like an alarmist. By the standards of what the science actually says, and especially what it has been saying with increasing urgency over the past five years, his essay reads to me as a careful but understated brief. Three places where the understatement matters: the framing of winners and losers, the treatment of ice sheets, and the silence about what happens after 2100.

Take the winners-and-losers framing first. Emanuel writes that "there will be winners and losers, but will the world really suffer in the net?" He answers, eventually, that yes, the world will probably suffer in the net, but he gives the rhetorical question more breathing room than it deserves. The framing is misleading in two ways.

First, it treats the climate system as a redistributor of agricultural productivity and heating bills, when in fact the harms and benefits are radically asymmetric in character. A longer growing season in Manitoba does not feed the population of Bangladesh when their rice paddies salt up from rising seas. A reduction in winter heating costs in Boston does not compensate Bangladeshis for the cyclone that drowned 300,000 of their grandparents in 1970 and whose intensified successors Emanuel's own research predicts. The "winners" are mostly populations that are already wealthy, well-adapted, and politically powerful; the "losers" are overwhelmingly populations that are poor, tropical, and voiceless. Aggregating them in a global net erases the moral structure of the problem.

Second, the framing implicitly imagines a new equilibrium—a slightly warmer world to which we will adjust, much as we adjusted to the medieval warm period or the little ice age. But we are not heading toward an equilibrium. We are heading toward a transient, and the rate of change matters as much as the destination. Ecosystems can tolerate the climates of two degrees ago and two degrees from now; what they cannot easily tolerate is the journey between, especially when habitat is fragmented and migration corridors are blocked by farms and cities. The same is true of human societies. The question is not whether humans can survive a Philadelphia-like climate in what used to be Boston; we obviously can. The question is whether the institutions, infrastructure, and agriculture built for one climate can be re-engineered for another in fifty or a hundred years without enormous disruption. The historical record Emanuel himself cites—civilizations destroyed by smaller climate swings than what we are now committing to—suggests the answer is sometimes no.

Now the ice sheets, where I think Emanuel is most carefully understated. He notes that we do not understand the physics of ice under pressure well, that meltwater is reaching the bed of the Greenland ice sheet and may be lubricating its flow, and that "were the entire Greenland ice cap to melt, sea level would increase by around 22 feet." All true, all relevant. What he does not emphasize is how recently and how dramatically glaciologists' opinions have shifted. As recently as 2001, the IPCC treated the major ice sheets as essentially stable on century timescales, contributing little to twenty-first century sea level rise. The observations of the past five years—accelerating discharge from Jakobshavn, Helheim, and Kangerdlugssuaq glaciers, the apparent surface melt-driven speedup, the unexpected mass loss from West Antarctica—have undermined that view. The pace of revision is not what one would expect from a mature science converging on a stable estimate. It is what one would expect from a field discovering, late, that its models were missing essential physics, with the missing physics pointing in the direction of faster ice loss than predicted.

This is important because the difference between sixteen inches of sea level rise by 2100 (Emanuel's upper-end IPCC number) and, say, four or five feet—which is what some glaciologists are now whispering is possible if the West Antarctic ice sheet starts to come apart—is the difference between an expensive coastal engineering problem and the loss of major world cities. Miami, much of New Orleans, large parts of the Nile and Ganges-Brahmaputra deltas, the Maldives entirely, lower Manhattan, much of the Netherlands' coastal defenses overtopped. The probability of the high end is not high, but it is also not negligible, and it is precisely the kind of risk against which a rational society insures.

Then there is the silence about post-2100. The IPCC, by convention, projects to 2100, and Emanuel follows suit. But 2100 is not the end of history. The CO2 we put in the atmosphere this century will mostly still be there in 2200 and substantially still be there in 2500. If we are on a trajectory toward 700 or 800 ppm by 2100 (a perfectly plausible business-as-usual outcome given current growth in Chinese and Indian coal use), we will not stop at 800; we will keep going. The equilibrium warming associated with such concentrations, once the oceans finish absorbing heat over the following centuries, is in the range of 5 to 10°C above pre-industrial. That is not a Boston-to-Philadelphia shift. That is a Boston-to-Atlanta shift, with the corresponding shifts everywhere else, plus the elimination over millennia of essentially all the world's terrestrial ice. The transient response by 2100 understates the commitment we are making.

I do not raise these points to score rhetorical points against Emanuel, who has been a model of scientific responsibility in a debate disfigured by bad actors. I raise them because the public conversation continues to treat climate change as a problem on roughly the same scale as, say, air pollution or ozone depletion—serious, manageable, requiring reasonable policy adjustments. The honest assessment is that the upper portion of the projection range, which we cannot rule out, describes a planetary transformation without precedent in the history of civilization. We have very few tools for thinking about events of that magnitude. Cost-benefit analysis, as conventionally practiced, breaks down because the discount rates that make sense for thirty-year decisions produce absurdities when applied across centuries. Insurance metaphors break down because there is no underwriter for a transformed planet.

What follows? A few things. First, that the locus of policy debate should shift from "is the problem real?" (settled) and "is some action warranted?" (yes, obviously) to "what stabilization target are we actually trying to hit, and what does the energy system have to look like in 2050 for us to hit it?" The implied transformations are enormous: a global power sector that is essentially decarbonized by mid-century, an industrial sector following close behind, transportation electrified or running on synthetic fuels. We are not on that trajectory and nothing currently proposed gets us there.

Second, that adaptation, while necessary, is not a substitute for mitigation; it is a complement. The argument that we should adapt rather than mitigate makes sense only if the warming we adapt to is bounded. If we mitigate enough to bound the warming at two or three degrees, adaptation may be feasible. If we do not, adaptation eventually becomes flight. There is no Dutch engineering solution to a six-meter sea level rise sustained over centuries.

Third, that scientists like Emanuel need to keep talking, including in venues like this one, because the alternative is to cede the public conversation to the polished obfuscations of the contrarian think tanks. The signal achievement of the last several years has been the gradual conversion of the major scientific institutions—the National Academies, the Royal Society, the American Geophysical Union, even (belatedly) the American Association of Petroleum Geologists—into more or less unified voices saying that the science is in. That signal needs to keep getting through.

Emanuel has done his part with grace. My quarrel is only that the picture, accurately drawn, is darker than even his careful prose conveys.

  2. 02Climate change should be viewed as a dangerous transient process rather than a shift to a new equilibrium, as the rapid rate of change threatens the survival of rigid human infrastructure and ecosystems.

  6. 06Adaptation is not a viable alternative to mitigation because human engineering cannot compensate for unbounded warming or extreme sea level rise.
