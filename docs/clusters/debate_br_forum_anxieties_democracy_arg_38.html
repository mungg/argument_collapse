"The most effective response to the crisis of democratic anxiety is to embrace and expand local participatory governance rather than focusing solely on shoring up dysfunctional national legislatures."

Ira Katznelson’s essay ends with a call for “a robust debate about these questions,” and on that point I wholeheartedly agree. Where I diverge is in his framing of the democratic crisis as a crisis of representation at the national level, one that demands we shore up legislatures against populist and deliberative incursions. To my mind, the most promising response to democratic anxiety is not to circle the wagons around a broken national politics but to shift our attention—and our energy—to the local democracy already being rebuilt beneath the radar of Washington. The scaffolding on the Capitol may be a symbol of decay, but in city halls, neighborhood assemblies, and participatory forums, the real restoration is underway.

Across the United States and the world, a quiet revolution in governance is taking place. Participatory budgeting, which Katznelson mentions in passing, has spread from a single city in Brazil in 1989 to over 1,500 municipalities worldwide, including New York, Chicago, and Boston. In these processes, ordinary residents directly decide how to allocate a portion of the public budget, transforming citizens from spectators into architects of their own communities. Far from bypassing representative institutions, participatory budgeting strengthens them: it gives elected officials clearer signals about constituent priorities, builds trust, and often leads to more equitable distribution of resources. Studies show that participants develop greater political knowledge, higher trust in government, and a stronger sense of efficacy.

Similarly, a movement toward “collaborative governance” is reshaping how local governments tackle complex problems. In cities like Grand Rapids, Michigan, and Portsmouth, New Hampshire, city officials have partnered with civic groups to run deliberative forums on school reform, economic development, and public safety. These are not one-off listening sessions; they are sustained, structured processes that bring diverse stakeholders together to find consensus solutions. The results are not plebiscitary edicts but carefully negotiated agreements that then feed back into city council votes and mayoral decisions. This is exactly the kind of “background consensus” Katznelson values, but built from the ground up through actual experience of shared problem-solving, not through elite appeals to civic virtue.

Katznelson’s fear that such innovations will substitute for national legislatures is understandable but largely misguided. The dysfunction in Washington is so profound—and so locked in by gerrymandering, money, and the permanent campaign—that waiting for Congress to heal itself is a recipe for despair. Local democratic innovation offers a different path: it builds the civic muscles and trust that can, over time, create demand for reform at higher levels. A participant in participatory budgeting in Chicago is more likely to vote, more likely to engage in state-level politics, and more likely to see herself as a capable democratic actor. The cultural shift toward a more participatory, less spectator-oriented citizenship is a necessary precondition for any national renewal, and it is happening already.

Moreover, localism is not a retreat from big issues. Cities are increasingly the front lines for climate action, immigration integration, and economic justice. The Fight for $15 movement that began with fast-food workers in New York City has pressured Seattle, Los Angeles, and other cities to pass minimum-wage laws, creating a wave that now influences state and national politics. When national legislatures fail, local governments become laboratories not just for policy but for democratic practice itself. That is not a flight from representative democracy; it is a deepening of it, adding new layers of participation and accountability that make the whole system more resilient.

Katznelson asks whether liberal democracy can survive without a shared sense of the public interest. Perhaps the answer is that such a sense cannot be imposed from above or summoned by historical memory; it must be cultivated in the face-to-face encounters where people from different walks of life actually work out compromises. The scaffolding on the Capitol will come down eventually. The scaffolding of a renewed democracy is being erected every day in town meetings, participatory panels, and community forums. We should cheer those, not fear them.
