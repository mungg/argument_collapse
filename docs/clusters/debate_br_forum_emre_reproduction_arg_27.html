Yes, And Further

Reading Merve Emre's essay, I kept wanting to say: yes, and further. Yes, all reproduction is assisted. Yes, the natural is an ideological screen. Yes, the manifesto tradition from Firestone through Haraway through Laboria Cuboniks has been right to insist that the body is not a destiny but a substrate. The essay's diagnosis is mostly correct. Where I want to push is on the gentleness of its conclusions. Emre has written what amounts to a careful, narratively rich argument for what xenofeminists have been saying more loudly: that the universalization of reproductive technology is a feminist project, that biology is not a defense, and that the work of dismantling forced kinship structures has barely begun. I think this position deserves a louder voice than Emre gives it.

Start with the manifesto tradition itself. Emre treats it with respectful skepticism: the manifestos universalize, she says, and what gets lost in their universalizing are the particular differences of particular bodies and particular care arrangements. This is true. But manifestos are not ethnographies; they are not supposed to be. The point of Firestone's call for ectogenesis, or Laboria Cuboniks's call for gender abolition through hormonal hacking, is not to describe how reproduction is currently distributed. It is to name a horizon against which the current distribution looks absurd. The narrative method Emre prefers is good at showing how particular women navigate the existing system. It is less good at imagining a different one. To get from the first to the second, you need both — and the manifesto's universalizing impulse is the form that imagines.

What does the horizon look like? It looks like a world in which gestation is one option among many, including ectogenesis at scale, including state-funded gestational labor compensated like other forms of skilled care work, including non-biological kin structures with full legal recognition, including hormonal interventions distributed as freely as eyeglasses. It looks like a world in which the question "whose womb?" is no more politically charged than "whose liver was the transplant from?" — a question of medical sourcing rather than essential identity. It looks like a world in which the trans woman banking sperm before estrogen, the cis lesbian couple using donor gametes, the single parent by IVF, the surrogate carrying a child for a same-sex couple, the gestational parent who is not the social parent, the chosen-family commune of co-parents — all of these are normal, in the sense that they are unremarkable, supported, paid for, and legally protected.

We are not in that world. The reason is not, principally, that the technology is not ready, although in many cases it isn't. The reason is that the political and economic structures that would universalize reproductive assistance do not exist, and the ideological structures that legitimize current arrangements are very strong. The "natural" is the most visible of those ideological structures, and Emre is right to attack it. But the natural is downstream of something else: the privatization of children. The reason every woman in Emre's essay has to fight her way through expensive clinics, borrow money from siblings, hide pregnancies from employers, and stake her parenting future on her ability to time her own retrievals is that having children, in the United States, is treated as a private consumption decision. The state's role in child-rearing is minimal and increasingly punitive. The community's role is residual. The kin network has been ground down to nuclear bits.

In this structure, the natural is doing very specific ideological work. It says: this is a private choice, and the responsibilities are private. The pain is yours, the cost is yours, the failure is yours. The child you produce is yours to fuck up, in Firestone's biting phrase. By making the reproduction look private and inevitable, the natural foreclosed the political question of how children get made and supported. To dismantle the natural is therefore to open that question — to make it visible that reproduction is a social process all the way down and that the social process can be organized differently.

This is why I think Emre's closing image — "the political to catch up to the technological, our behavior to catch up to our beliefs" — is too modest. The political does not need to "catch up." It needs to lead. The technology is already, in many cases, ahead of where collective imagination has caught up. Ectogenesis is not science fiction. Recent work on artificial wombs for premature lambs has demonstrated that gestation outside a human body is now a tractable engineering problem, not a metaphysical one. The relevant question is no longer whether ectogenesis is possible but who will own it, who will pay for it, who will be allowed to use it, whose bodies it will replace, whose labor it will continue to require. These are political questions, and they will be answered by political actors. If feminists do not show up for them, the answers will be supplied by venture capitalists, by religious conservatives, by the same insurance-and-clinic apparatus that currently extracts fortunes from infertile couples.

The xenofeminist slogan — "if nature is unjust, change nature" — captures the right orientation. But it has to be paired with a corresponding insistence: not just change nature, change ownership. The technologies of reproductive assistance are not neutral instruments waiting for better distribution. They are being built inside a particular political economy, and they carry that economy's marks. Egg freezing as a corporate perk; surrogacy as a globalized labor market with brown women's wombs at the bottom of the supply chain; gamete donation as a market commodity racially stratified by price; IVF as a tens-of-thousands-of-dollars purchase priced out of reach for most. The horizon of universal assisted reproduction is not the universalization of the existing for-profit clinic. It is its supersession.

Emre's narrative method is generous, and her interviewees do not deserve to be conscripted into someone else's manifesto. But I think the lesson her essay teaches, more starkly than she lets on, is that piecemeal access to a fragmented private system will not redeem reproductive politics. What is needed is the actual horizon: socialized reproduction, demystified gestation, recognized kin pluralism, dismantled property logic in children. The natural will lose its grip not when individual women buy their way around it, but when collective practice replaces it. That is what "all reproduction is assisted" should mean. Not that we have always quietly been using technology — we have — but that we have always been making, together, the conditions under which new humans appear in the world. The question is whether we do it consciously, justly, and at scale, or whether we keep pretending it is private.
