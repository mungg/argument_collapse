"To truly achieve full employment as a moral imperative, the United States should move beyond indirect economic stimulus and establish a federal job guarantee that provides a basic wage and benefit package to anyone willing and able to work."

Robert Pollin asks whether full employment is attainable in the United States today. I would answer yes, but I would push the argument one step further than he does. If we truly believe that unemployment is a social failure rather than an individual fault, then we should stop relying exclusively on indirect stimulus, interest-rate policy, and hoped-for private hiring. We should create a public job guarantee: the federal government should stand ready to fund a job at a basic wage and benefit package for anyone willing and able to work.

This idea may sound radical, but it follows from Pollin’s own premises. He rightly criticizes a definition of employment that counts desperate street vending as adequate work. He wants an abundance of decent jobs. He argues that full employment is a moral imperative and that the main obstacle is political will. Yet his principal policy proposal—redirecting spending from military and fossil-fuel sectors to education and clean energy—would still leave employment dependent on the pace of appropriations, contracting, private investment responses, and sectoral transitions. It might create millions of jobs, but it would not establish a permanent floor under the labor market. A job guarantee would.

The United States already accepts the principle that government must act as a backstop in crises. We insure bank deposits. We provide emergency liquidity to financial institutions. We insure crops, homes in flood zones, pensions to a degree, and unemployment income. In 2008 and 2009 the federal government demonstrated that it would not allow the financial system simply to collapse. But for workers, the backstop is weaker: unemployment insurance, food stamps, Medicaid for some, and eventually exhaustion. We rescue the banking system in the name of systemic necessity. The labor system is no less systemic.

A job guarantee would differ from conventional stimulus. Instead of trying to estimate the correct size of a spending package and hoping the multiplier produces enough private jobs, government would hire up to the level needed. The program would expand automatically in downturns and shrink in recoveries as private employers recruited workers away. It would function as an automatic stabilizer more powerful than unemployment insurance because it would maintain incomes, skills, work habits, and community services. It would also anchor wages at the bottom. If the guaranteed job paid, say, a modest but decent wage with basic benefits, no private employer could fill jobs at worse terms unless the work offered some compensating advantage. That is precisely why some business interests would oppose it.

The work is not hard to imagine. Communities have unmet needs everywhere: tutoring children, staffing after-school programs, maintaining parks, weatherizing homes, assisting the elderly, restoring vacant properties, digitizing public records, supporting libraries, expanding recycling, preparing for disasters, monitoring environmental hazards, and helping nonprofit organizations deliver services. These are not substitutes for highly skilled public-sector professions; they are supplementary jobs designed for people who would otherwise be unemployed. Local governments and nonprofits could propose projects, with federal funding and standards to prevent exploitation or political patronage.

Critics will immediately raise three objections: cost, efficiency, and inflation. The cost objection should be taken seriously but not exaggerated. Unemployment is already costly. We pay unemployment benefits, lose tax revenue, increase spending on poverty-related programs, and absorb the social costs of crime, illness, family breakdown, and skill erosion. A job guarantee converts some of those costs into wages paid for useful work. Moreover, because the program is countercyclical, its net cost falls when the private economy strengthens.

The efficiency objection is that public jobs may be make-work. Some would be poorly designed; no large program is immune to waste. But the private labor market is hardly a temple of perfect efficiency. The financial sector paid fortunes to people who designed instruments that helped crash the economy. Advertising, planned obsolescence, and speculative trading all employ talented people in activities of debatable social value. Meanwhile, many plainly useful tasks go undone because no private buyer can capture the return. A job guarantee should be judged not against an imaginary ideal but against the reality of idleness. Is it better for a person to remain unemployed or to repair playgrounds, read to children, and insulate homes? The answer is obvious.

The inflation objection is more serious. Pollin discusses the Phillips Curve, Sweden, and the need for wage restraint. A job guarantee offers a different inflation-control mechanism. Instead of maintaining a reserve army of unemployed workers to discipline wages, it maintains a reserve army of employed workers at a fixed basic wage. Private firms can hire from this pool by offering better pay or conditions. The guaranteed wage becomes a nominal anchor. It does not eliminate all inflationary pressures—energy shocks, monopolistic pricing, and supply bottlenecks remain—but it reduces the cruelty of using unemployment as the primary anti-inflation tool.

This approach also addresses one weakness in Pollin’s reliance on education and clean energy. Those sectors are valuable, but not every unemployed worker can quickly become a teacher, solar installer, or engineer. A job guarantee can provide transitional employment and training while larger structural investments proceed. It can be tailored to local conditions. In a city with foreclosed properties, workers can help maintain and rehabilitate neighborhoods. In rural areas, they can support conservation and elder services. In regions hit by manufacturing decline, they can combine public work with apprenticeships and community-college training.

The political challenge is formidable. A job guarantee would be attacked as socialism, as welfare by another name, and as a threat to private employers. To survive, it would need clear rules. Jobs should be available to all who meet basic requirements, not allocated through political favoritism. The wage should be high enough to prevent poverty but not so high as to pull large numbers out of decent private employment. Projects should be publicly visible and locally accountable. Participants should have rights and responsibilities. Training and placement into private or regular public employment should be built in.

There is a long American tradition to draw upon. The New Deal’s Works Progress Administration and Civilian Conservation Corps did not solve every economic problem, but they left roads, parks, buildings, art, and memories of earned dignity. Direct public employment is not alien to American democracy. What is alien is the notion that millions must remain idle because economists cannot find the precise mix of tax cuts, interest rates, and incentives to coax employers into hiring them.

Pollin’s essay usefully revives the language of full employment. But the phrase should mean more than a hoped-for unemployment rate. It should mean a standing public commitment: if the private economy cannot provide work, democratic government will. We should still invest in clean energy, education, care, and infrastructure. We should still regulate finance and reduce carbon dependence. But these policies should be joined to an employment guarantee that makes the right to work concrete.

The deepest issue is power. Unemployment disciplines workers. It tells them to accept lower wages, worse schedules, unsafe conditions, and disrespect because someone else is waiting outside the gate. A job guarantee changes that relationship. It gives every worker an exit option. That is why it would be transformative—and why it would be resisted. If full employment is truly a moral imperative, we should build institutions that deliver it directly.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A public job guarantee creates a permanent labor market floor and automatic economic stabilizer that is more effective than Pollin’s reliance on sectoral investment and private hiring.
  2. 02The program would address unmet social needs—such as community service, environmental restoration, and elder care—that the private market fails to fund.
  3. 03A job guarantee functions as a superior anti-inflation tool by maintaining a reserve army of employed workers at a fixed nominal wage rather than using the cruelty of involuntary unemployment to discipline the economy.
  4. 04Direct public employment provides an essential transitional bridge for workers who lack the specific skills required for the high-tech education and clean-energy sectors Pollin prioritizes.
