Diversified MiniMax

"Robert Pollin’s proposal for achieving full employment is insufficient because it attempts to work within the existing capitalist framework rather than pursuing a fundamental restructuring of the economy that prioritizes human flourishing over profit."

Robert Pollin's essay arrives at the right moment but asks the wrong questions. The United States stands at a crossroads, and the choice before us is not between neoliberalism and a vaguely-defined full employment agenda, but between a managed recovery that serves the few and a fundamental restructuring that serves the many.

Pollin's diagnosis of the problem is largely correct. The Great Recession laid bare the hollowness of the neoliberal consensus, the dangers of financial deregulation, and the fragility of an economy built on consumer debt and speculative finance. His critique of the stimulus as inadequate is well-founded; $787 billion was never going to reverse decades of asset bubble inflation and wage stagnation.

But Pollin's proposed solutions are too timid and too wedded to the existing framework. He wants to redirect 25 percent of military and fossil fuel spending to education and clean energy, creating 4.8 million jobs. This is better than nothing, but it does not challenge the fundamental logic of a system that requires constant growth to maintain employment levels.

The real problem is not unemployment but the nature of work itself under capitalism. As Pollin acknowledges through his discussion of Marx and Kalecki, full employment threatens capitalist control over the workplace and the broader economy. Workers with real alternatives make demands that capitalists find inconvenient. This is not a technical problem that can be solved through policy tweaks; it is a structural feature of capitalist society.

A serious full employment agenda would require breaking with the profit imperative entirely. This means public employment as a right, not as a last resort. It means shortening the work week without loss of pay as technology productivity increases. It means worker control of investment decisions through democratic planning. It means treating the economy as a means to human flourishing, not an end in itself.

Pollin's Swedish model is instructive not because it succeeded for a time but because it eventually failed. The Swedish Social Democrats abandoned full employment when it became incompatible with European integration and globalized finance. If we want a durable full employment economy, we need to go beyond the Swedish model and build institutions that cannot be rolled back when capital threatens to relocate.

The climate crisis makes this urgency even greater. We cannot simply shift spending from fossil fuels to clean energy within the existing growth model; we need a planned transition to a stationary economy that operates within ecological limits. This requires workers and communities to be guaranteed meaningful employment and income security, not as a concession but as a right.

Full employment is achievable. What is missing is not political will but the political power of working people to demand it. Building that power is the task before us.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Pollin’s plan to redirect spending fails to address the systemic requirement for constant growth and the inherent conflict between full employment and capitalist control.
  2. 02A durable full employment agenda must establish public employment as a guaranteed right and implement worker control over investment decisions through democratic planning.
  3. 03The failure of the Swedish model demonstrates that any system compatible with globalized finance is vulnerable to being rolled back by capital interests.
  4. 04The climate crisis necessitates a transition to a stationary economy operating within ecological limits, rather than simply shifting energy investments within a growth-based model.
  5. 05Achieving true full employment requires building the political power of working people to demand structural changes rather than relying on policy tweaks or the political will of elites.
