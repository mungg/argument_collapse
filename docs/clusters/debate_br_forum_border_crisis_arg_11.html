"The current border crisis is a failure of democratic sovereignty and social trust caused by the liberal state's refusal to prioritize the interests and cultural coherence of its own citizens over global capital."

Lea Ypi’s essay is a monument to a certain kind of progressive nostalgia: the belief that if we could only reawaken the Enlightenment spirit of cosmopolitanism and unite workers of all nations, we would see that borders are a class conspiracy and migration a manufactured problem. It is an elegant argument, beautifully written, and entirely detached from the lived experience of ordinary citizens in receiving states. The real border crisis is not the one Ypi describes—a crisis of neoliberal exploitation—but a crisis of democratic sovereignty, cultural coherence, and social trust. The crisis is that the liberal state, in its arrogance, has for decades refused to enforce borders in the national interest and has instead pursued policies that benefit global capital and a mobile elite at the expense of the rooted majority. Ypi is right that class matters, but she gets the class analysis exactly backwards: the principal victims of mass migration are not the migrants themselves but the native working and lower-middle classes who must compete for housing, wages, and public services while being told that their attachment to place is a form of bigotry.

Ypi’s framing rests on a false binary. She insists that political conflicts are really about class, not culture, as if the two could be separated. But culture is the medium through which solidarity is built. The welfare states that she implicitly invokes as a model for fair redistribution—those of post-war social democracy—were not based on abstract class solidarity alone. They were national projects, rooted in shared language, history, and mutual obligation. The Scandinavian social model, for instance, was built on high levels of trust that came from homogeneity and a strong sense of collective fate. When Ypi dismisses concerns about migration “undermining the relations of trust and identification required to stabilize democratic values” as a convenient myth, she ignores a vast body of social science literature showing that ethnic diversity, when introduced rapidly and in large numbers, can reduce social trust and support for redistribution. This is not a moral argument for homogeneity, but a practical one: if you want generous welfare states, you need a population that feels a strong sense of common belonging. High immigration, particularly when it brings people with very different cultural norms and without a prior commitment to liberal values, strains that solidarity.

Ypi’s response is that the liberal democratic state has never been fully liberal or democratic anyway, and that citizenship has always been exclusionary. This is historical cherry-picking. The modern democratic state, for all its imperfections, has been the most successful vehicle for expanding human flourishing in history precisely because it bounded the political community. The civil rights movement in the United States, which Ypi mentions, was a struggle to include previously excluded citizens, not to dismantle the very idea of citizenship. The women’s suffrage movement was a fight for inclusion in the national political community, not a rejection of the nation. Anticolonial movements fought for self-determination, for the right of peoples to govern themselves within their own borders. All these struggles assumed the legitimacy of a territorial political community. Ypi wants to hollow out that community into a mere platform for global class struggle, but that is a project that has never succeeded and never will, because it ignores the deep human need for a bounded place in the world.

The migration system she criticises—points-based assessments, golden visas, the brutal treatment of irregular migrants—is indeed a racket, but the solution is not to abolish borders. It is to reclaim democratic control of borders for the benefit of the native population. The golden visa schemes are an outrage because they allow wealthy foreigners to buy what should be a precious and conditional status, while ordinary citizens have no say. The deportation of criminals and visa-overstayers is a legitimate exercise of state authority, even if it should be carried out with procedural fairness and basic humanity. Ypi chooses the most inflammatory examples—Trump’s chained deportation videos—to tar any enforcement as oppressive. But the rule of law requires consequences for those who flout it, and no state can endure if it cannot say who may enter and remain.

Ypi’s insistence that migration is involuntary because of neoliberal policies is a sophistry. People have migrated for economic reasons since time immemorial, often from places far poorer than today’s sending states. The difference now is that the West has created a perverse incentive structure: generous welfare provisions, strong labour protections, and the possibility of family reunification make migration a rational economic choice for millions. That is not to blame migrants; they are acting reasonably given the rules. But it is to say that the choice is still a choice, and the receiving state has every right to limit that inflow to protect its own social contract. The claim that rich countries “made” conditions unlivable in, say, Honduras or Albania is an imperialist narrative of the left, stripping agency from the elites and populations of those countries who have ruled themselves for decades. It is a perverse form of Western guilt that denies the rest of the world its own history.

The most telling moment in Ypi’s essay is her reading of Nathan the Wise. She celebrates Nathan’s refusal to identify with any nation and his insistence that he was “forced to travel.” But Nathan is a character in a play, an idealised Enlightenment figure. Real political communities are not groups of rootless cosmopolitans; they are made up of people who inherit a language, a landscape, a set of institutions, and a way of life. To govern a country is to take responsibility for the flourishing of that particular people in that particular place. The “tragedy” she speaks of is that Western governments have forgotten that first duty. They have pursued cheap labour for businesses, diversity for the educated class, and a moralistic foreign policy that lectures other nations while hollowing out their own.

A genuine alternative would mean a sharp reduction in immigration numbers, the abolition of golden visas, and an immigration system designed to select those who can contribute to the national economy and assimilate to the national culture. It would mean integration requirements that are actually enforced, and a welfare state that is reserved for those who have paid into it over time. It would mean abandoning the fantasy of a borderless world and embracing the nation-state as the indispensable container of democratic life. This is not xenophobia; it is the basis of any coherent idea of self-government. Ypi dismisses such views as part of the “hegemony of the right,” but that just shows how far the left has drifted from ordinary voters. The real border crisis is that people feel their countries are no longer their own. Until the left can hear that cry without sneering, it deserves its electoral losses.

  2. 02The historical success of democratic movements relied on the legitimacy of bounded territorial communities rather than the dismantling of citizenship.
  3. 03The solution to migration inequities is not to abolish borders but to reclaim democratic control of them to serve the native working class instead of mobile elites.
