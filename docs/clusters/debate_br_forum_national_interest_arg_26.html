"While offshore balancing is the correct strategic framework, the United States must implement it ruthlessly by liquidating its footprint in the Middle East to focus all military, diplomatic, and economic resources on preventing Chinese hegemony in the Asia-Pacific."

Stephen M. Walt’s case for "offshore balancing" provides a desperately needed corrective to the strategic exhaustion the United States is currently experiencing in the Middle East. Writing in early 2005, with American forces bogged down in an intractable Iraqi insurgency, Walt’s call to husband American power and avoid unnecessary wars is compelling. However, his analysis suffers from a geographical fixation that is endemic to the American foreign policy establishment: he remains obsessed with the Middle East and Europe, giving only passing mention to the single most important geopolitical development of the 21st century—the rise of China.

Walt rightly identifies three regions of vital strategic importance: Europe, industrialized Asia, and the Persian Gulf. Yet, his application of offshore balancing treats these regions with a dangerous symmetry. He argues for drawing down in Europe and returning to a balance-of-power approach in the Middle East, while maintaining "a significant military presence in Asia." This significantly understates what is required. True offshore balancing today demands a massive, asymmetric pivot away from the Middle East and entirely toward the Asia-Pacific.

The core premise of offshore balancing is to prevent a peer competitor from dominating a critical region. In Europe, as Walt notes, that threat evaporated with the Soviet Union. In the Middle East, despite the rhetoric of the Bush administration, no state is close to regional hegemony. Iran is a mid-sized power with a weak economy; Iraq is broken; Saudi Arabia relies on external security guarantees. The Middle East is a region of endemic weakness, where the balance of power maintains itself precisely because all the local actors are highly constrained. The U.S. obsession with the Persian Gulf is largely a relic of the 1970s oil shocks. Today, the global energy market is far more resilient, and the true threat to American primacy is not an Islamic caliphate or a Middle Eastern dictator, but a rising, industrialized superpower in the East.

China is the only nation on earth with the demographic weight, economic trajectory, and political will to challenge American primacy globally, starting with hegemony in East Asia. Since 2001, while the United States has spent hundreds of billions of dollars and thousands of lives chasing insurgents in the deserts of Iraq and the mountains of Afghanistan, China has been quietly executing a brilliant strategy of regional integration and military modernization. Beijing is actively securing energy deals globally, expanding its naval capabilities, and using soft power to draw Southeast Asian nations into its economic orbit.

Walt’s brief prescription for Asia is to "maintain a significant military presence... and continue to build cooperative security partnerships." This is woefully insufficient. The U.S. force posture in Asia is largely a legacy of the Korean War and the Cold War, optimized for a land war on the Korean peninsula or defending Japan from a Soviet threat. It is not currently configured to deter a Chinese anti-access/area-denial (A2/AD) strategy in the Taiwan Strait or the South China Sea.

If we are to adopt offshore balancing, we must do it ruthlessly. We should immediately begin liquidating our massive footprint in the Middle East. Walt’s suggestion that we must first solve the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, while morally appealing and logically sound regarding its impact on Arab public opinion, is a strategic trap. The U.S. has spent three decades trying to solve this conflict. Making it the "cornerstone" of our foreign policy, as Walt suggests, will only keep our diplomatic and political capital tied up in a strategically marginal region while China continues to rise. We must learn to benignly neglect the Middle East, managing terrorism through intelligence and targeted strikes, not grand diplomatic or military crusades.

The resources freed up by this withdrawal must be redirected to a comprehensive Asian strategy. This means not just maintaining air and naval forces, but drastically expanding our maritime capabilities. It means moving beyond traditional bilateral alliances (hub-and-spoke) with Japan, South Korea, and Australia, and aggressively courting new strategic partnerships with India, Vietnam, and Indonesia. These nations share our concern about Chinese hegemony and are the natural "local actors" Walt’s theory relies upon to uphold the regional balance.

Furthermore, offshore balancing in Asia cannot be purely military. As China binds the region through trade agreements (like the ASEAN-China Free Trade Area being negotiated right now), the United States must offer a compelling economic alternative. We must drive economic integration in the Pacific that embeds American standards and access.

Walt rightly criticizes the Bush administration for its immaturity and lack of strategic discipline. But strategic discipline means prioritizing threats. The global war on terror and the quagmire in Iraq are strategic distractions of historic proportions. If we follow Walt’s advice and merely tinker with our Middle East policy while trying to balance everywhere, we will wake up a decade from now to find that China has achieved exactly what offshore balancing is designed to prevent: hegemony in the world’s most dynamic economic region. The pivot to Asia cannot wait; it must be the absolute centerpiece of American grand strategy today.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Middle East is a region of endemic weakness where no state is capable of achieving regional hegemony, making continued American obsession with the region a strategic distraction.
  2. 02China is the only potential peer competitor with the capacity to challenge American global primacy, necessitating a massive and asymmetric redirection of power toward East Asia.
  3. 03Walt’s proposal to make the Israeli-Palestinian conflict a cornerstone of foreign policy is a strategic trap that would waste precious diplomatic capital on a marginal region.
  4. 04Effective offshore balancing in Asia requires moving beyond legacy Cold War postures to build new strategic partnerships with nations like India and Vietnam and offering a robust economic alternative to Chinese regional integration.
  5. 05The United States must prioritize threats by treating the war on terror and Middle Eastern stability as secondary to the urgent task of deterring Chinese anti-access/area-denial capabilities.
