"While Stephen Walt’s proposed grand strategy of offshore balancing is intellectually coherent, it is ultimately an impractical academic exercise because it fails to account for the domestic political, bureaucratic, and institutional constraints of the American governing system."

Reading Stephen Walt's essay, I had the experience that anyone with practical experience in foreign-policy implementation will recognize: the strategy is coherent, the diagnoses are mostly accurate, and yet I find myself unable to imagine how most of it would actually be done. This is not a small problem. Grand strategy that cannot be implemented is not grand strategy. It is academic exercise.

Let me illustrate with three of Walt's specific recommendations.

First, the Israeli-Palestinian leverage point. Walt proposes that the United States make support for Israel conditional on Israeli acceptance of a settlement based on the 1967 borders, with American aid cut off if Israel refuses. Treat this as an operational question: how, exactly, does an American president do this?

The president would have to persuade Congress to revise or condition Israeli aid appropriations. Walt is aware that this is politically difficult — he attributes the difficulty to "the Israel lobby" — but he understates how thoroughly bipartisan and durable American support for Israel actually is. The 2003 vote on the Syria Accountability Act passed 408-8 in the House. The 2004 emergency supplemental for Israel passed by similar margins. These margins do not reflect lobby capture of a reluctant Congress. They reflect the genuine convictions of legislators across both parties, responding to genuine constituent preferences. No American president since Eisenhower has successfully threatened to suspend aid to Israel for policy non-compliance, and Eisenhower did it in conditions (the Suez crisis, with the British and French as primary targets) that have not recurred. A president who tried to do what Walt proposes would face an immediate revolt in Congress, almost certainly bipartisan, that would consume the administration's political capital and likely fail. The result would be an empty threat — worse than the present posture, because it would advertise American impotence to apply leverage Walt thinks we possess.

This is not an argument that we should never push Israel. We should, and presidents do, but quietly, on specific issues, with sustained patience. The "use leverage" prescription Walt offers underestimates how political the leverage actually is.

Second, the nuclear grand bargain. Walt proposes that the United States dramatically reduce its arsenal, abandon new weapons programs, and offer nonaggression pacts to North Korea and Iran in exchange for verifiable abandonment of their nuclear programs. As a treaty-implementation matter, this is a multi-year negotiation involving multiple parties (the Six-Party process in Korea, the EU-3 plus the United States plus Iran, plus the IAEA, plus the Russian and Chinese governments whose cooperation is required for any meaningful pressure). Treaty ratification requires two-thirds of the Senate. The reductions Walt proposes would require modifying the Moscow Treaty framework and reopening discussions with Russia. The verification arrangements would require unprecedented IAEA access to two regimes that have made obfuscation a state art.

Each of these is doable in principle. None of them is doable on the timeline a president has. A first-term president has perhaps eighteen months of legislative bandwidth before the midterm cycle distracts Congress; a second-term president has perhaps a year before lame-duck status sets in. The grand bargain Walt proposes would require eight to ten years of sustained, focused presidential attention across at least two administrations. Show me the institutional arrangement that produces that continuity in the American system, and I will believe the bargain is feasible. Walt does not address this. He proposes the bargain as if presidential will alone could deliver it.

Third, the "buddy system" for use of force. Walt proposes that the United States should generally use force only with the buy-in of NATO, the Security Council, or other multilateral bodies, on grounds that this would legitimize American action and reduce backlash. The intent is sound. The practical question is what happens when the buddies say no.

In Kosovo, NATO acted without Security Council authorization because Russia would have vetoed. Walt presumably accepts this. In Iraq in 2003, the Security Council declined to authorize force; the Bush administration went anyway. Walt thinks this was wrong. But the principle has to cut both ways. If the buddy system means "act only with multilateral consent," then any veto from any major member of the relevant body becomes a blocker on American action. Russia and China can block at the Security Council. France can effectively block at NATO. We have just constructed a system in which other powers can prevent us from acting on threats we believe to be urgent, regardless of the merits.

The honest position is that the buddy system is a useful presumption but cannot be a binding constraint. Walt seems to want it to be more than a presumption but less than a binding constraint, and the difference matters operationally in a way his essay does not resolve.

What I find missing in the essay, more broadly, is engagement with the bureaucratic, congressional, and political constraints within which any actual grand strategy has to operate. The presidency is not a strategy-implementation machine. It is a contested political institution embedded in a separated-powers system, with limited bandwidth, short time horizons, and competing demands. Grand strategies that ignore these features become wish lists.

A grand strategy that takes implementation seriously would look something like this. First, identify the half-dozen things a president can plausibly accomplish in a four-year term given political constraints. Second, sequence them so that early wins build political capital for later, harder moves. Third, invest in the institutional capacity — language training, regional expertise, intelligence reform, public diplomacy infrastructure — that any successful strategy requires over decades, not years. Fourth, accept that on many questions, the right answer is not "execute the optimal policy" but "avoid the worst available outcomes while preserving options." Fifth, communicate constantly with the American public, because no foreign policy that lacks domestic support can be sustained beyond a single administration.

Walt's essay does some of this but not enough. His critique of the Bush administration's strategic missteps is mostly right. His proposed alternative would face implementation problems he does not adequately confront. The result is a thoughtful contribution to public debate that would, if attempted, leave any administration trying to execute it considerably less successful than it hopes to be.

The country needs better grand strategy than it is currently getting. It also needs grand strategy that takes seriously the messy institutional realities through which strategy must be made real. Walt has given us half of what we need. The other half is the harder half.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Conditioning aid to Israel is politically unfeasible because it ignores the deep-seated, bipartisan support for Israel within Congress and among the American electorate.
  2. 02The proposed 'nuclear grand bargain' is operationally impossible because it requires a level of sustained presidential focus and multi-administration continuity that the American political cycle cannot provide.
  3. 03A 'buddy system' for the use of force is strategically problematic because it would effectively grant foreign powers a veto over American national security actions.
  4. 04The presidency is a contested political institution with limited bandwidth, meaning any viable grand strategy must prioritize and sequence goals based on what is actually achievable within a four-year term.
  5. 05Effective grand strategy requires long-term investment in institutional capacity, such as language training and diplomatic infrastructure, rather than just a shift in presidential will.
