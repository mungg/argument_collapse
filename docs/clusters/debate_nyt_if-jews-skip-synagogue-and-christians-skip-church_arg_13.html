"To survive the decline of institutional loyalty, religious leaders must move beyond nostalgia and intentionally build congregations that offer clarity of purpose and institutional flexibility."

The numbers should not surprise anyone who has watched American religion over the last half-century. The old model of belonging was supported by ethnicity, neighborhood, social expectation and institutional loyalty. Catholics went to Mass because Catholics went to Mass. Mainline Protestants joined churches because respectable families joined churches. Jews affiliated with synagogues because Jewish neighborhoods and communal institutions reinforced Jewish identity. That world has weakened.

The mistake would be to assume that a few programmatic adjustments can bring it back. Longer office hours, a Facebook page or a guitar service will not by themselves reverse broad cultural change. Americans now move often, marry across boundaries, distrust institutions, and treat affiliation as voluntary. The weekend has become crowded with work, entertainment and children’s activities. Religious leaders must stop imagining that the surrounding culture will deliver people to their doors.

Yet this is not necessarily bad news. Voluntary religion can be more intentional. The people who do come may come because they mean it, not because convention requires it. The task is to build congregations for an age of choice.

That means clarity of purpose. A synagogue should be able to say why Jewish prayer, study, Hebrew, Israel, ritual, memory and peoplehood matter in a life otherwise full of options. A church should be able to say why Jesus, Scripture, baptism, communion, confession and discipleship matter beyond being a nice person. If leaders cannot articulate that without jargon or embarrassment, the unaffiliated will not do it for them.

It also means institutional flexibility. Large sanctuaries and fixed weekly schedules may not be the only form of community. Congregations might support small groups, neighborhood minyanim, weekday prayer, online teaching, service fellowships, campus-style adult education and partnerships with other organizations. But flexibility should serve substance, not replace it.

Finally, religious leaders should redefine success. A congregation with fewer members but deeper prayer, stronger learning, more mutual care and greater service may be healthier than a larger one held together by nostalgia. The goal is not to recreate 1955. It is to form faithful communities in 2013.

The Pew reports are not death notices. They are notices of disestablishment. Religious identity remains, but institutional habit has eroded. The congregations that survive will be those that understand that inherited loyalty can no longer be presumed; it must be awakened, taught and practiced.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The traditional model of religious belonging based on ethnicity, neighborhood, and social expectation has been permanently weakened by broad cultural changes.
  2. 02Religious leaders must clearly articulate the specific value and substance of their faith traditions to compete in an age where affiliation is entirely voluntary.
  3. 03Congregations should adopt flexible institutional structures, such as small groups and diverse schedules, to meet the needs of a mobile and busy population.
  4. 04Success should be redefined by the depth of a community's spiritual practice and mutual care rather than the size of its membership rolls.
  5. 05Inherited loyalty can no longer be presumed, so religious identity must be actively awakened and taught through intentional practice.
