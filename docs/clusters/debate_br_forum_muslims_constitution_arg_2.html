The Courts Were Always the Problem

Akbar and Theoharis's lead essay is a powerful indictment of the political branches and a useful corrective to the post-airport-protest mood. But I want to add a doctrinal layer that the essay touches on only briefly: the role of the federal judiciary in producing, not merely failing to check, the legal architecture of the war on terror. The authors are right that Khizr Khan's pocket Constitution did not protect Muslim Americans from fifteen years of rights violations. But the deeper question is why. The Constitution did not fail on its own. It was interpreted by federal judges, in case after case, in ways that gave the executive branch nearly everything it asked for. Any serious response to the lead essay's call to action has to reckon with judicial doctrine, because the doctrine is what made the rest possible.

Start with the cases the authors mention only obliquely. In Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project (2010), the Supreme Court held that the material support statute could constitutionally be applied to people who wanted to provide nonviolent training—in international human rights law and peaceful conflict resolution—to designated foreign terrorist organizations. The vote was 6-3. The majority, written by Chief Justice Roberts, held that the government's national security interest justified the limitation on First Amendment activity, and that courts owed substantial deference to executive judgments about which organizations were dangerous and what kinds of support might be fungible. Justice Breyer, dissenting, observed that the majority's reasoning would criminalize teaching a designated organization to file petitions with the United Nations. He was right, and the majority's logic has since been used to prosecute Americans for translating documents, sharing social media posts, and conducting religious instruction.

Holder is the keystone case for the modern terrorism prosecution apparatus. It is also a First Amendment case decided by a Supreme Court that, in other contexts, is among the most speech-protective in American history. The same court that struck down restrictions on corporate political spending in Citizens United, that protected the Westboro Baptist Church's right to picket military funerals in Snyder v. Phelps, that invalidated California's restriction on violent video games in Brown v. Entertainment Merchants—that court, in Holder, decided that translating a treatise on nonviolent conflict resolution was not protected speech if the recipient was on a list maintained by the State Department. This is not a minor doctrinal anomaly. It is a categorical exception, carved out of the First Amendment, for Muslims (in practice; the statute is technically neutral). It is the kind of exception the Court has historically made only in moments of acute panic and has later disavowed: Schenck, Korematsu, Dennis.

Now consider the standing cases. In Clapper v. Amnesty International USA (2013), the Supreme Court held that journalists, lawyers, and human rights workers who feared their international communications were being surveilled under Section 702 of FISA lacked standing to challenge the program because they could not prove their communications had actually been intercepted. The catch: the government would not tell them, the FISA Court's opinions were classified, and any specific surveillance was itself secret. The Court effectively held that the existence of a classification regime is sufficient to prevent constitutional challenge to that regime. Six months later, Edward Snowden's leaks revealed that the plaintiffs in Clapper had been precisely the kind of people the government was surveilling. The Court has not revisited the standing question.

Consider also the No Fly List litigation. In Latif v. Holder (2014), Judge Anna Brown of the District of Oregon held that the procedures for redress of No Fly designations violated due process. This was a significant win, but it has been slow to produce meaningful relief. The government's response was to create a marginally improved redress process and to litigate aggressively against further claims. The D.C. Circuit and other courts have been substantially more deferential, applying the Mathews v. Eldridge balancing test in ways that consistently favor the government's claimed security interest over the individual's interest in not being barred from flying.

The pattern across these cases is consistent. Where Muslim plaintiffs raise claims about counterterrorism programs, federal courts have applied unusually deferential standards of review, accepted government affidavits invoking the state secrets privilege with minimal scrutiny, and constructed standing and pleading doctrines that make merits review nearly impossible. This is not the same as saying the courts have endorsed the underlying programs. It is to say that the courts have erected procedural and doctrinal barriers that prevent merits review. The functional result is the same: programs proceed, harms accumulate, plaintiffs lose.

So when Akbar and Theoharis note, with cautious optimism, that the judiciary's willingness to push back against Trump's executive order is "a heartening development," I share the cautious part more than the optimism. The injunction issued by Judge Robart in Seattle and the Ninth Circuit's refusal to stay it are real. They reflect a judiciary that, at least at the district and circuit levels, retains the capacity to act when an executive action is sufficiently outrageous and sufficiently visible. But the executive order was sufficiently outrageous because it was crudely executed, transparently religious in motivation, and procedurally chaotic in implementation. It was, in other words, the easy case. The hard cases—the material support prosecution that turns on a translation, the No Fly designation that turns on an algorithm fed by classified data, the FISA Court order that no defendant will ever see—are the cases the federal judiciary has consistently declined to scrutinize seriously.

What would meaningful judicial engagement look like? Several things. First, narrowing of Holder, either by Congress (through amendment to the material support statute) or by the Court itself if a sufficiently sympathetic case reaches it. The doctrinal vehicle is available: the majority opinion in Holder expressly disclaimed application to "independent advocacy" and left room for narrowing through future cases. Second, revisiting Clapper's standing analysis in light of what we now know about Section 702 collection. Third, more aggressive application of the Due Process Clause to administrative deprivations in the security context, including watchlists, suspicious activity reports, and CVE assessments. Fourth, much greater scrutiny of the state secrets privilege, which has metastasized from a narrow evidentiary rule into a near-categorical bar on Fourth and Fifth Amendment claims involving classified programs.

None of this will happen on its own. It will happen, if at all, through sustained doctrinal advocacy by groups like the ACLU, the Center for Constitutional Rights, Muslim Advocates, and CAIR, supported by academic work that takes counterterrorism law seriously as a domain of constitutional doctrine rather than as a sui generis emergency regime. The legal academy has been better on these issues than the bench, but academic critique only translates into doctrine through cases, and cases require plaintiffs, lawyers, and a judiciary willing to engage.

Here I want to register a partial disagreement with the lead essay's framing. Akbar and Theoharis treat the courts as part of the problem and as occasional, contingent participants in the solution. That is descriptively accurate. But it understates the leverage that doctrinal change can have on the broader political economy of counterterrorism. The reason the executive branch built so much of its program around legal authorities that the Supreme Court had blessed (or that the FISA Court had blessed in secret) is that legal authority matters. Programs that lose their legal foundation become much harder to sustain politically. The Bush administration's warrantless wiretapping program was, in the end, brought into the FISA framework precisely because its legal vulnerability made it politically vulnerable. If Holder were narrowed by the Supreme Court, dozens of pending and future prosecutions would have to be reconsidered. If Clapper were revisited, the Section 702 program would face constitutional challenge on the merits for the first time.

The lead essay's call to "push the courts" is exactly right. But pushing requires a theory of what to push toward, and the theory has to be doctrinal as well as moral. Muslim Americans need lawyers who can articulate, in terms the federal judiciary will recognize, why the post-9/11 framework violates the Constitution. They have many such lawyers already. What they need from the rest of us is the kind of sustained public attention that makes courts feel they have room to rule against the government. That attention, again, is what the airport protests provided in microcosm. The question is whether we can sustain it through the longer, less televisable litigation that will determine whether the next fifteen years look like the last fifteen.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Supreme Court's decision in Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project created a categorical exception to the First Amendment that criminalizes nonviolent speech and association for Muslims.
  2. 02Procedural rulings on standing, such as in Clapper v. Amnesty International USA, have effectively shielded secret surveillance regimes from any meaningful constitutional challenge.
  3. 03Federal courts have normalized rights violations by applying unusually deferential standards of review and accepting government claims of state secrets with minimal scrutiny.
  4. 04The judiciary's recent pushback against Trump’s travel ban is an outlier involving an 'easy case' of crude implementation rather than a reversal of the deeper, systemic deference shown in harder national security cases.
  5. 05Meaningful reform requires a sustained doctrinal shift—including narrowing the material support statute and increasing scrutiny of the state secrets privilege—to dismantle the legal foundation of discriminatory counterterrorism programs.
  6. 06Legal authority is a critical lever of political power; therefore, forcing doctrinal changes in the courts is essential to making oppressive executive programs politically unsustainable.
