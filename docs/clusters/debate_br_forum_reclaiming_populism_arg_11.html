"To reclaim populism as a viable democratic force, the left must move beyond mere rhetoric and reconstruct 'the people' through multiracial solidarity, disciplined institutional organization, and a commitment to internationalism."

Adom Getachew is right to insist that “populism” cannot be surrendered to its worst contemporary examples. To treat populism as by definition authoritarian, xenophobic, or contemptuous of institutions is to accept a narrowing of the democratic imagination at precisely the moment when democratic societies need a broader one. Every democratic politics must, in some form, ask who “the people” are, what they are owed, and what powers prevent them from governing. If the left refuses that terrain, others will occupy it. They already have.

Still, the case for reclaiming populism must begin with a sober admission: the word is damaged because the historical practices associated with it have often been damaging. The appeal to “the people” has been used to democratize power, but also to purify the polity; to extend rights, but also to mark certain groups as parasitic, alien, or criminal; to contest oligarchy, but also to authorize leaders who claim to embody the nation without mediation. A left populism worthy of the name cannot merely add “multiracial” to an inherited form. It has to reconstruct the very meaning of the people around institutions, solidarities, and material demands that make domination harder rather than easier.

The present crisis makes that task both more urgent and more difficult. The COVID-19 pandemic has rendered visible the basic fact that societies are sustained not by financiers, celebrity executives, or the professional class alone, but by nurses, janitors, home health aides, grocery workers, farmworkers, warehouse workers, sanitation workers, bus drivers, teachers, delivery workers, and caregivers. Many of those workers are black, Latino, immigrant, and female. They are praised as “essential” while being denied the protections and power that would make such praise meaningful. At the same time, the crisis has provided the right with familiar opportunities: border closures recoded as public health, anti-Asian racism disguised as geopolitical realism, emergency authority used to punish migrants and prisoners, and scarcity transformed into nationalist competition.

This is the central paradox of the moment. The pandemic has discredited many of the premises of neoliberal governance, but discrediting is not the same as defeating. It has revealed the cruelty of tying health care to employment, but millions losing jobs does not automatically produce Medicare for All. It has shown that government can spend trillions when political elites deem it necessary, but public spending can rescue corporations as easily as households. It has exposed the indispensable labor of the working class, but exposure does not itself build organization. The left cannot wait for crisis to do its work. It must name a people, identify antagonists, build institutions, and contest power.

The most promising part of Getachew’s essay is its attention to experiments that join movement politics to electoral politics. The Chicago Teachers Union matters not simply because it won strikes, though it did. It matters because it helped change the public meaning of a labor struggle. Teachers were not only bargaining for wages; they were bargaining for the common good. Smaller class sizes, nurses, librarians, social workers, sanctuary schools, and housing supports were not narrow workplace demands. They were claims about what a city owes its children and what austerity has stolen from public life.

That model is crucial for a democratic populism because it avoids two failures at once. It avoids the technocratic failure of presenting politics as expert management, in which ordinary people appear mainly as clients of policy design. And it avoids the demagogic failure of reducing politics to resentment without organization. A strike is a form of popular power disciplined by collective action. It requires persuasion, risk, internal democracy, alliances, and an account of the public. It says: the people are not an abstract mass waiting to be voiced by a leader; they are organized actors capable of governing their own institutions and reshaping the polity.

The Sanders campaign tried to nationalize something like this insight. Its language of “not me, us” was not incidental. It was a direct attempt to solve the leadership problem that haunts populism. Sanders offered a sharp antagonism—working people versus billionaires and corporate power—but also insisted that no president could deliver the program without a movement. That formulation distinguished his campaign from charismatic plebiscitary politics. It also exposed the campaign’s limits. The movement was real but uneven. It was strong among younger voters, some unions, segments of the multiracial working class, and ideological leftists; it was weaker among older black voters, many suburban moderates, and voters whose primary goal was defeating Donald Trump. A populism that cannot win the confidence of decisive constituencies among the very people it claims to unite must study its defeat without condescension.

The lesson should not be that class politics failed because it was too universal, nor that racial justice should be added later as a moral supplement. The lesson is that the American working class has always been formed through race, immigration status, gender, geography, and state violence. A multiracial working-class politics must speak to wages and debt, but also to incarceration, segregation, environmental racism, reproductive labor, deportation, and unequal death. The pandemic has made this impossible to deny. When black residents of Chicago and Milwaukee die at wildly disproportionate rates, this is not a “racial” issue separate from economic policy. It is the accumulated result of housing, employment, health care, environmental exposure, policing, hospital access, and political abandonment. To build a people across race is not to avoid race; it is to show how racial hierarchy organizes the distribution of life chances.

This point also clarifies the proper target of left populism. The enemy cannot be “elites” in a vague cultural sense. That way lies confusion, and often reaction. A Wall Street executive, a public health expert, a university professor, a union lawyer, and a Chinese American scientist can all be placed under the lazy heading of “elite,” but they do not occupy the same role in social domination. The left should name oligarchy, racial capitalism, corporate power, fossil fuel interests, private health insurers, anti-union employers, carceral profiteers, and political actors who protect them. It should not encourage generalized suspicion of knowledge, institutions, or minorities coded as cosmopolitan. The question is not whether there will be antagonism in politics. There will. The question is whether the antagonism clarifies structures of power or mystifies them.

A reclaimed populism therefore needs a democratic ethic as much as a distributive program. Critics of populism are not wrong to worry about anti-pluralism. The claim to represent “the real people” can become a weapon against dissent. But the answer cannot be to abandon popular sovereignty to constitutional formalism or expert administration. The answer is to build forms of popular politics that institutionalize contestation. That means strong unions with internal democracy, tenant associations, worker centers, mutual aid networks, party organizations accountable to members, local assemblies, and campaigns that train people to act rather than merely donate or consume media. Institutions are not the enemy of democratic populism; they are its necessary condition.

The Working Families Party’s interest in a “left-led, multiracial working class alignment” points in this direction. The phrase is useful because it joins leadership, race, class, and organization. But it also raises hard questions. What does “left-led” mean when the American electoral system heavily pressures insurgent politics into the Democratic Party? How can a party-like formation maintain independence while avoiding marginality? How can it build durable local power rather than appearing only during presidential cycles? How can it negotiate differences between socialists, progressives, community organizations, unions, and voters who may share material demands but not ideological labels?

The answer will not be found in a single presidential campaign. Indeed, one of the dangers of recent left politics has been the inflation of presidential politics into the measure of all things. A presidential run can popularize demands, reveal constituencies, and polarize debate. But durable realignment happens through school boards, city councils, county commissions, state legislatures, union locals, district attorney races, ballot initiatives, strike funds, media ecosystems, and political education. The examples from Chicago matter because they show power being accumulated at multiple levels. Brandon Johnson’s election, the Socialist Caucus, Jeanette Taylor’s movement credentials, and the CTU’s wider agenda illustrate how electoral victories can be rooted in struggle rather than substituted for it.

The pandemic strengthens the case for such rooted politics. In a crisis, people need immediate material help: food distribution, rent relief, unemployment assistance, workplace protections, bail funds, immigrant support, and health care access. Mutual aid alone cannot replace the state, but it can generate trust, networks, and a sense of collective agency. A left populism that appears only as a national message will be too thin. One that helps people survive, organizes them where they work and live, and then connects that survival to structural demands can become thick enough to endure.

The programmatic content of that politics is increasingly clear. It must include universal health care, paid sick leave, unemployment protections, direct cash support, rent and mortgage relief, debt cancellation, protection for undocumented workers, decarceration, massive investment in public health, and a Green New Deal oriented toward care work as well as infrastructure. It must defend public education and public housing, strengthen labor law, and democratize workplaces. It must confront the fact that “essential” workers often lack essential rights. If a worker is necessary to the functioning of society, that worker should have protective equipment, hazard pay, paid leave, health care, bargaining power, and legal protection regardless of immigration status.

Yet a domestic program is insufficient, as Getachew rightly emphasizes. The pandemic has exposed the deadly absurdity of nationalist hoarding and sanctions regimes. Viruses do not respect borders, but ventilators, masks, credit, and medical supply chains are distributed through global hierarchies. A progressive populism confined to the nation can easily become a softer version of “our people first.” That would be both morally indefensible and practically self-defeating. The United States cannot build a humane democracy at home while treating migrants as disposable, sanctions as routine instruments of policy, and poorer countries as afterthoughts in a scramble for supplies.

Internationalism is often the weak point of left populism because the language of “the people” tends to be nationally bounded. But there are resources for another approach. The people can be constituted against transnational structures of domination: debt regimes, tax havens, pharmaceutical monopolies, extractive corporations, militarized borders, and imperial sanctions. Workers in hospitals, warehouses, fields, and homes are connected across borders by supply chains, remittances, migration, and care networks. The same system that leaves nurses in New York reusing masks leaves countries in the global South outbid for medical equipment. A serious left populism must make those connections legible.

This does not mean abandoning the nation-state as a site of struggle. The pandemic has reminded us that states matter enormously. They can mobilize resources, impose rules, distribute income, coordinate health systems, and either protect or abandon people. But democratic control of the state must be linked to obligations beyond borders. Lifting sanctions that block medical supplies, halting deportations, supporting global public health, sharing technology, and resisting anti-Chinese racism are not distractions from working-class politics. They are tests of whether “the people” is being built as an emancipatory category or a possessive one.

The danger now is that right-wing forces will offer a more emotionally satisfying story than the left: the virus came from foreigners; international institutions failed us; experts lied; borders must close; China must pay; migrants threaten health; only a strong leader can protect the nation. Against this, liberals may offer competence and restoration. Competence is necessary. Restoration is not enough. The pre-pandemic normal was already a disaster for millions. A left populism must offer a story that is both angrier and more solidaristic: we are in danger because decades of privatization, austerity, racism, union-busting, ecological destruction, and imperial arrogance left us vulnerable; we survive because ordinary people care for one another; we can govern by placing life over profit.

Reclaiming populism, then, is not a branding exercise. It is not chiefly about whether candidates should use the word. It is about whether democratic politics can again make popular power visible and effective. The left should not romanticize “the people” as naturally virtuous. People are divided, frightened, contradictory, and shaped by inherited hierarchies. But they are also capable of solidarity, courage, and collective intelligence when institutions cultivate those capacities.

The task is to build a people that does not yet fully exist. That means organizing across real differences rather than pretending they are superficial. It means confronting white supremacy not as a cultural side issue but as a governing structure. It means treating unions, parties, and movements as schools of democracy. It means rejecting both technocratic contempt for mass politics and demagogic contempt for pluralism. It means seeing the essential worker not as a temporary hero of pandemic rhetoric but as a democratic subject whose power should reorganize society.

Populism gets a bad rap because much populism has deserved one. But democracy cannot do without appeals to the people, and the people will be constituted by someone. If the left declines the work, the right will continue to define the people through fear and exclusion. The better answer is not to abandon populism, but to discipline it through multiracial solidarity, institutional democracy, and internationalism. In that sense, the project Getachew describes is not merely worth attempting. It may be the name for democratic politics under conditions of crisis.
