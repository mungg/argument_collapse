"The current opposition to Donald Trump must move beyond resisting his personal cruelty and instead commit to dismantling the bipartisan national security infrastructure that has systematically targeted Muslims for the past fifteen years."

The Apparatus, Not the Man

Akbar and Theoharis have written the essay that needs to be read right now, and the most important sentence in it may be the most easily overlooked: "Trump's easiest route to achieve his aims is therefore to amplify, extend, and revisit practices already in place." Every protest sign at JFK and LAX in the last month has been, in some sense, an indictment not just of a 90-day suspension on travel from seven countries but of fifteen years of construction. The question is whether the movement that materialized at those airports is willing to acknowledge what it has been protesting all along without knowing it.

I want to push the authors' argument further, because I think the political moment requires it. The lead essay properly catalogs the bipartisan machinery built since 2001: material support prosecutions, watchlists, CVE, sting operations dressed up as preemption, drone strikes in countries where Congress has never authorized force. But naming this machinery is not the same as committing to dismantle it. And much of the liberal opposition to Trump right now is busy trying to restore the machinery to its pre-Trump operators—to put the gun back in Obama's hand, where it supposedly belonged.

Consider the strange rehabilitation of the intelligence community happening in real time on cable news. Former Bush and Obama national security officials, several of them personally responsible for signing off on torture memos, kill lists, or the metadata dragnet, are being treated as the adults in the room because they oppose Trump. James Clapper, who lied under oath to the Senate about NSA collection in 2013, is now a folk hero in some quarters because he signed a January assessment about Russian interference. John Brennan, architect of the drone program's "disposition matrix," is invoked admiringly. Sally Yates's refusal to defend the executive order was admirable, but the same Justice Department she led had argued vociferously for the constitutionality of the No Fly List and against habeas petitions from Guantánamo detainees a few years earlier.

This is the mood the authors are warning about. If our quarrel with Trump is only that he is crude, unpredictable, and unconstrained by establishment norms, then victory looks like a return to a national security state run by competent professionals who target Muslims more efficiently. That is not victory; that is the trap. The pre-Trump status quo was deportation at record rates, the FBI sending informants into mosques to coax mentally ill young men into terrorism plots the bureau itself manufactured, and a CVE program that hired Muslim community leaders to surveil their own neighbors in exchange for grant money. The "good Muslim/bad Muslim" sorting Akbar and Theoharis describe was always the operating logic; Trump's contribution is candor about it.

The point about Khizr Khan is uncomfortable but right, and I want to extend it. The Khan moment was a brilliant tactical intervention in a campaign and a moving display of a father's grief. But politically it expressed an exchange: Muslim belonging mediated through blood sacrifice for American wars. Khan's son died in Iraq, in a war that killed hundreds of thousands of Iraqis and that the Khans' co-religionists are still fleeing—including, indeed, from one of the seven countries on Trump's list. The image of the pocket Constitution worked because it offered the audience a way to be on the right side of Trump without questioning anything about the wars, the surveillance, the prosecutions, or the bombing campaigns those Constitutions have been read to authorize. It was, in the deepest sense, a constitutional patriotism that left every existing structure intact while feeling like resistance.

The authors are too generous, I think, in their framing of "Six Misapprehensions." These are not innocent confusions. They are the load-bearing fictions of a political consensus. The misapprehension that surveillance is bad mainly because it might catch you, the white reader, in its net—rather than because of what it does to Muslim communities every day—is not an analytical error. It is a confession about whose civil liberties count. When the ACLU's most successful organizing frame around metadata in 2014 was "they're watching all of us," the implicit subject of "us" did not include the teenagers whose Facebook posts were already being read by JTTF analysts in Brooklyn.

Similarly, the misapprehension that Guantánamo is exceptional is not just a mistake but a useful one for the federal prosecutors who use Special Administrative Measures, anonymous juries, and Communications Management Units in places like Florence, Colorado and Terre Haute, Indiana. Guantánamo's symbolic awfulness has provided cover for a domestic terror prosecution system that, by some accounts, has a conviction rate north of 95 percent and routinely deploys evidence obtained under FISA that defendants cannot meaningfully challenge. Closing Guantánamo without confronting these practices was always going to be a half-measure, and Obama's failure to close it even on those terms reveals how thin the bipartisan commitment to even symbolic reform was.

What would a serious response require? At minimum: repeal of the material support statute as currently construed under Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project, an end to the FBI's informant-driven sting operations, abolition of the No Fly List in its current form, defunding of CVE entirely (not its rebranding), repeal of the 2001 AUMF, declassification of FISA Court opinions, and a real reckoning with the drone program's legal architecture. None of these are on the agenda of the Democratic Party leadership. Several were not on Obama's agenda in eight years. They will not be on Trump's. They will only get there if the people now showing up at airports demand them.

Akbar and Theoharis are right that the airport protests are an opportunity. They are also a test. The history of post-9/11 American liberalism is the history of moral energy mobilized at moments of obvious cruelty—Abu Ghraib, the family separations at the border under Trump's emerging immigration regime, the ban itself—and demobilized as soon as the obvious cruelty receded into the bureaucratic routines that produced it. The Senate hearings on torture in 2014 produced a 500-page summary and no prosecutions. The 2015 USA Freedom Act ended one narrow NSA program and left the architecture intact. If the energy of the last month dissipates the moment a court enjoins the executive order or a slightly less inflammatory version is issued, the machine will simply continue running, and Trump or his successor will use it again.

There is a temptation, in essays like this, to end on hope. I will not. The honest assessment of the political coalition assembling against Trump is that it does not yet understand what it is fighting, and large parts of it are actively invested in preserving the substructure of what it claims to oppose. The lead essay's contribution is to insist that this substructure be made visible. Whether anyone with political power is willing to look at it is a different question. Khizr Khan asked Donald Trump to read the Constitution. Akbar and Theoharis are asking the rest of us to read something harder: the fifteen years of statutes, executive orders, FISA opinions, and prosecutorial guidance that turned the Constitution into a document compatible with everything we now claim to find appalling. That reading list does not fit in a pocket.
