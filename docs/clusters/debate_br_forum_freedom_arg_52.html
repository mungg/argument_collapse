Diversified Gemini

"The American Left must prioritize an immediate and aggressive messaging strategy to reclaim the language of freedom from the right, as institutional building is impossible without first winning the elections that such rhetoric facilitates."

In "A Different Freedom," Aziz Rana provides a sweeping, erudite, and deeply historical analysis of how the concept of liberty in America has been captured by the political right. By tracing this capture back to the material realities of settler colonialism, racial exclusion, and imperial expansion, Rana makes a compelling case that the dominant American understanding of freedom is inherently individualistic, zero-sum, and exclusionary. His ultimate conclusion, however, creates a false dichotomy that threatens to paralyze the contemporary Left. Rana insists that reclaiming freedom "is not chiefly about communication strategy or political messaging," but rather about long-term "institution building" and altering the "cultural environments" through massive structural shifts like the PRO Act and a radical overhaul of U.S. foreign policy.

While Rana’s historical diagnosis is brilliant, his strategic prescription is fundamentally flawed. As a blueprint for the American Left in December 2023, it demands we win a marathon before we have even secured the right to buy running shoes. You cannot build the transformative institutions Rana envisions—democratized workplaces, reformed electoral systems, a dismantled military-industrial complex—without first winning elections. And you cannot win elections in the United States without aggressively, immediately, and effectively contesting the messaging and rhetoric of freedom.

Rana waves away "communication strategy" as if it is a superficial distraction from the "real" work of organizing. He argues that we must wait for the "unsettled reality" of our times to organically generate new cultural narratives through the revitalized labor movement. This is a fatal misreading of how political power is acquired in a hyper-mediated, modern democracy.

Consider the Right's success. The conservative movement did not capture the language of freedom simply because of the structural legacy of the 19th-century frontier. They captured it because, for the last fifty years, they built a massive, multi-billion-dollar communications infrastructure—think tanks, talk radio, cable news, and digital media—dedicated relentlessly to defining freedom as tax cuts, deregulation, and the destruction of the welfare state. They told a compelling, albeit destructive, story.

The Left cannot counter this by merely organizing Amazon warehouses and hoping the cultural resonance of "collective self-rule" trickles up to the electorate. We are in an immediate, existential fight against an authoritarian, white nationalist movement that is openly planning to dismantle democratic institutions if they regain power in 2024. In this environment, ceding the most powerful word in the American political lexicon because we haven't yet achieved perfect workplace democracy is unilateral disarmament.

Rana rightly points out that the right-wing version of freedom is currently "fragile" and unable to deliver material well-being, relying increasingly on the raw psychic wage of dominating outsiders. This is exactly why the Left must strike rhetorically *now*. We do not need to wait for a generational revival of union density to explain to voters that they are not free.

We need a relentless, populist messaging strategy that redefines freedom in material, everyday terms. The Left must articulate that true freedom is impossible when you are terrified of an unexpected medical bill. True freedom is impossible when your employer can dictate your schedule with zero notice, destroying your ability to care for your children. True freedom is impossible when a handful of billionaires can buy elections and dictate the tax code. We must aggressively brand the Right's agenda not just as "unequal" or "unjust," but as fundamentally *anti-freedom*. We must accuse corporate monopolies, price-gouging pharmaceutical companies, and union-busting CEOs of being the true enemies of American liberty.

Furthermore, Rana’s insistence on tying domestic emancipation to a radical, anti-imperialist foreign policy and open borders ("presumptive admission for all") is a messaging disaster that will actively prevent the institution building he desires. To pass the PRO Act or protect voting rights, Democrats need to hold the Senate and win the House. This requires winning in competitive, working-class districts across the Midwest and the Sunbelt. Framing the Democratic project around slashing defense spending by hundreds of billions of dollars and decriminalizing border crossings is a guaranteed way to alienate the moderate and working-class voters whose support is mathematically required to pass any pro-labor legislation.

This is not to say we should abandon internationalism or human rights. But political strategy requires prioritization and sequencing. Franklin D. Roosevelt did not build the New Deal by leading with a critique of American empire; he built it by focusing relentlessly on the immediate, material desperation of the American people and framing government action as the restoration of their liberty.

Rana writes that "the primary battle for the left is less about communications strategy and more about institution building." But how do we pass the laws to build the institutions? How do we overcome the filibuster, defeat right-wing judicial nominees, and overcome gerrymandering? We do it by winning a mandate from the public. And we win that mandate by telling a better story about what America can be.

We must embrace the rhetoric of freedom, strip it of its right-wing, exclusionary baggage, and wield it as a weapon against corporate oligarchy and right-wing authoritarianism. The Left must stop viewing messaging and structural organizing as mutually exclusive. They are symbiotic. A brilliant communication strategy that taps into the American desire for freedom is the necessary prerequisite to building the political coalition that can actually deliver the institutional transformation Aziz Rana so eloquently envisions. We do not have the luxury of time; we must reclaim the word today, so we can rebuild the world tomorrow.
