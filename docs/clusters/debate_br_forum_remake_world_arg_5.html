"While Walter Johnson correctly identifies that slavery exploited the human capacities of the enslaved, his rejection of the concept of 'dehumanization' is flawed because it fails to account for the systematic denial of normative human standing and moral recognition inherent in chattel slavery."

Walter Johnson's essay is a sophisticated intervention in the debate on slavery, capitalism, and justice, and it deserves praise for the breadth of its historical learning and the depth of its political engagement. Yet in its ambitious synthesis of the Du Boisian tradition, it may oversimplify the philosophical issues at stake in the critique of dehumanization.

Johnson's core argument is that slavery depended upon the human capacities of enslaved people, and that therefore the notion of dehumanization is misleading. Slaveholders did not treat enslaved people as less than human; they exploited their full humanity for profit. The violence of slavery was not the violence of treating people as nonhuman but the violence of claiming the right to treat human beings as things.

This argument has force, but it risks conflating two different senses of "humanity" that we need to keep apart. There is the descriptive sense: the capacities for thought, feeling, labor, and resistance that enslaved people obviously retained. And there is the normative sense: the standing in a community that entitles a person to recognition and respect.

Slavery did not destroy the first; it systematically violated the second. The enslaved person was not recognized as a legal subject, not permitted to form lasting family relationships, not able to move freely, not entitled to the fruits of her labor. She was socially dead—excluded from the community of rights-bearing persons that defined full membership in human society.

To say that slavery dehumanized enslaved people is to say that it systematically violated the second, normative sense of humanity—not that it destroyed their human capacities but that it denied them the recognition those capacities demanded. This is not a misleading claim; it is an essential one.

The problem with Johnson's argument is that it makes it harder to articulate what was distinctive and terrible about slavery. If slavery was merely the exploitation of human capacities—if it did not involve the destruction of human dignity—then it becomes harder to explain why it demands the transformative response that the Movement for Black Lives demands.

The spirituals point toward a different understanding. They drew on Christian concepts of human dignity—the God who created all humans equal, the inherent worth of every soul—to critique the conditions of slavery. They did not assert "humanity" in the abstract; they articulated a specific vision of justice grounded in a specific cultural tradition. And in doing so, they revealed the contradiction between the Christian profession of human dignity and the practice of human property.

This vision of justice is not liberal in the narrow sense that Johnson critiques. It does not reduce human dignity to the capacity for choice and self-determination. But it does insist that human beings are ends in themselves, not merely means to the ends of others—and this insistence is the moral heart of the critique of slavery.

Johnson's argument about racial capitalism is powerful, but it should be supplemented by attention to the moral and philosophical resources that the enslaved themselves developed for critiquing their condition. These resources are not only of historical interest; they remain vital for understanding what justice demands today.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A distinction must be made between descriptive humanity (biological and cognitive capacities) and normative humanity (social and legal recognition), the latter of which was indeed systematically destroyed by slavery.
  2. 02Defining slavery solely as the exploitation of human capacities, rather than the destruction of human dignity, weakens the moral foundation required to justify transformative justice and reparations.
  3. 03The cultural and spiritual traditions of the enslaved, such as spirituals, demonstrate that they themselves utilized normative concepts of inherent human dignity to critique their condition.
  4. 04The moral core of the critique of slavery—that humans are ends in themselves and not merely means—is a necessary philosophical component of justice that goes beyond Johnson's focus on racial capitalism.

"While Walter Johnson correctly identifies that slavery exploited human capacities, his dismissal of the 'dehumanization' framework is misguided because it conflates the descriptive reality of being human with the normative social and political recognition of humanity."

Walter Johnson's critique of the "dehumanization" framework in slavery scholarship is provocative but ultimately misguided. While he raises important questions about the ideological work done by certain formulations of this concept, his wholesale dismissal of "dehumanization" risks throwing out crucial analytical and moral insights.

Johnson's core argument is that slavery depended upon the human capacities of enslaved people—their labor, their reproduction, their sentience—and that therefore the notion of "dehumanization" is misleading. Slaveholders, he insists, did not treat enslaved people as less than human; they exploited their full humanity for profit. The violence of slavery was not the violence of treating people as nonhuman but the violence of claiming the right to treat human beings as things.

There is something important in this argument. Johnson is right that slavery involved the recognition and exploitation of enslaved people's capacities, and that this recognition was not merely incidental but constitutive of the system. The slaveholder who prized the intelligence of a skilled artisan or the fertility of a field hand was not operating outside the bounds of human comprehension; he was rationalizing the exploitation of human capacities for profit.

Yet this argument does not require us to abandon the concept of dehumanization. What it requires is a more precise understanding of what dehumanization means.

To say that slavery dehumanized enslaved people is not to say that they ceased to be human beings in any metaphysical sense. It is to say that slavery involved the systematic destruction of the social and political conditions of human flourishing. The enslaved person was not recognized as a legal subject, not permitted to form lasting family relationships, not able to move freely, not entitled to the fruits of her labor. She was, in Orlando Patterson's famous formulation, socially dead—excluded from the community of rights-bearing persons that defined full membership in human society.

This social death was not a matter of losing some metaphysical essence of humanity. It was a matter of being subjected to a set of social relations that denied the person the recognition and protection that full humanity requires. The enslaved person remained capable of love, of suffering, of resistance, of thought—but she was denied the social conditions in which these capacities could flourish. This is what we mean when we say that slavery was dehumanizing: not that enslaved people ceased to be human, but that they were subjected to conditions that systematically denied them the recognition of their humanity.

Johnson's argument thus proves too much. If slavery involved the exploitation of human capacities, and if this exploitation is not dehumanization, then we have no vocabulary for capturing what was distinctive and terrible about the violence of slavery. The sexual violation of enslaved women, the separation of families, the denial of legal personhood—these are not merely instances of exploitation; they are violations of the conditions of human dignity. They are, in a word, dehumanizing.

The problem with Johnson's argument is that it conflates two different senses of "humanity." There is the descriptive sense—the capacity for thought, feeling, labor, and resistance that enslaved people obviously retained. And there is the normative sense—the standing in a community that entitles a person to recognition and respect. Slavery did not destroy the first; it systematically violated the second. This is what we mean when we say that slavery was dehumanizing, and the concept retains its force despite Johnson's critique.

Johnson is right, however, that the language of dehumanization can be used in ways that are problematic. If "dehumanization" is understood to mean that enslaved people were not fully human—that they were subhuman or less than human—then we are participating in the same logic that justified slavery. This is a real danger, and we should be vigilant against it.

But the solution is not to abandon the concept but to use it correctly. Dehumanization, properly understood, does not describe the condition of enslaved people; it describes the violence to which they were subjected. It is a term of critique, not of classification. And in this sense, it remains indispensable.

There is also a political stakes to this debate. The Movement for Black Lives and its allies have drawn on the history of slavery to argue for reparations and structural transformation. This argument depends on the claim that the history of slavery continues to shape the present—that the racial wealth gap, mass incarceration, and political disempowerment of Black Americans are not separate problems but interconnected expressions of a single system of racial domination.

Johnson's argument threatens to undermine this political claim. If slavery was not dehumanizing—if it was merely the exploitation of human capacities without the destruction of human dignity—then it becomes harder to argue that its legacy requires transformative response. One might agree that slavery was an economic crime, to be remedied through economic compensation, without accepting that it was a crime against humanity requiring fundamental change.

This is not Johnson's intention, but it is a possible implication of his argument. We should be clear that slavery was a crime against humanity—not because enslaved people were not fully human, but because it systematically denied them the recognition that their humanity demanded. This is the moral basis for the demand for reparative justice, and it should not be lightly surrendered.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Dehumanization should be understood not as a loss of metaphysical essence, but as the systematic destruction of the social and political conditions required for human flourishing.
  2. 02The concept of 'social death' captures how slavery denied enslaved people the legal and communal standing that defines full membership in human society.
  3. 03Abandoning the vocabulary of dehumanization leaves scholars without a way to distinguish between general economic exploitation and the specific violations of human dignity inherent in slavery.
  4. 04Dehumanization is a term of moral critique regarding the violence of the perpetrator rather than a classification of the victim's status.
  5. 05Labeling slavery as a crime against humanity provides the necessary moral and legal foundation for contemporary demands for reparative justice and structural transformation.
