"While Walter Johnson correctly identifies the historical failures of liberal justice, his proposal lacks a sufficiently developed practical framework for adjudicating competing claims of redress and institutionalizing restorative justice."

Walter Johnson’s essay is an invitation to think historically about justice. It asks us to consider not just what justice requires in the abstract, but what justice requires in light of the specific, concrete wrongs that have shaped the world we inhabit. This is a valuable invitation, and Johnson is right to issue it. Too much contemporary philosophy of justice operates in a historical vacuum, as if the problem were simply to identify the correct principles and then apply them, as if the world were a blank slate that could be inscribed with the philosopher’s preferred design. Johnson reminds us that the world is not a blank slate. It is a palimpsest, overwritten with the traces of past injustices, and any adequate account of justice must begin by deciphering those traces.

But having issued the invitation, Johnson does not, I think, fully follow through on its implications. His essay is stronger on the historical diagnosis than on the prescriptive follow-up. He tells us, with admirable clarity, what is wrong with the dominant liberal approach to justice—its abstraction, its Eurocentrism, its blindness to the ways that past wrongs structure present inequalities. And he tells us, in broad strokes, what an alternative approach might look like: it would begin from the standpoint of the enslaved rather than the citizen, focus on restorative rather than distributive justice, and attend to the intersections of race, class, gender, and empire. But he does not give us a worked-out theory. He does not tell us, for instance, how to adjudicate between competing claims for redress from different groups who have suffered different wrongs at different times. He does not tell us how to balance the claims of the past against the needs of the present and the future. He does not tell us what institutions would be required to realize his vision, or how those institutions would be legitimated in a democratic society.

These are not quibbles. They are questions that any practical proposal for justice must answer, and the history of the black radical tradition provides resources for answering them that Johnson does not fully exploit. Let me focus on one question in particular: the question of what we owe to the dead.

Johnson’s essay is haunted by the dead. The millions of Africans who died in the Middle Passage, the enslaved people who were worked to death on plantations, the Native Americans who perished from disease and displacement, the elephants slaughtered for ivory—all of them crowd the margins of his argument, demanding justice. But what justice can we offer them? We cannot restore their lives or their freedom. We cannot undo the suffering they endured. At best, we can acknowledge it, memorialize it, and try to ensure that nothing like it happens again. But that seems, somehow, insufficient. The dead are not comforted by our memorials. They are not redeemed by our good intentions. If justice is about putting things right, then the dead are, in an important sense, beyond the reach of justice.

This is a problem for any backward-looking account of justice, not just Johnson’s. But it is especially acute for an account that, like his, insists on the centrality of slavery and genocide to the making of the modern world. If the wrongs of the past are so vast, and if they can never truly be righted, then what is the point of talking about justice at all? Are we not simply engaged in an exercise in collective guilt, a perpetual mourning that can never lead to resolution?

One answer to this question, which Johnson gestures toward but does not fully develop, is the idea of reparations. Reparations are not about paying off the dead; they are about addressing the living legacies of past wrongs. They are a way of saying: the injustice that your ancestors suffered is still affecting you today, in the form of diminished wealth, damaged health, disrupted communities, and stunted opportunities. We cannot undo the original wrong, but we can try to compensate for its ongoing effects. This is not a perfect solution—no solution that involves putting a price on human suffering can be perfect—but it is a practical one, grounded in the recognition that history matters for present distributions.

The problem with reparations, as a political project, is that they require us to trace chains of causation across centuries, and those chains are always contested. Johnson’s essay provides a masterful overview of the ways that slavery and racial capitalism shaped the modern world, but the very scale of that shaping creates difficulties for the reparations argument. If the wrongs are everywhere—if the entire global political economy is tainted by the legacy of slavery—then how do we determine who owes what to whom? The descendant of an enslaved person in the United States has a claim, but what about the descendant of a colonized person in the Congo, or an indentured laborer in Fiji, or a peasant displaced by the enclosure movement in England? Are all of these claims equally valid? If not, how do we rank them? And who gets to decide?

The black radical tradition has, at times, struggled with these questions. The Pan-Africanism of Du Bois and Garvey, for instance, tended to focus on the unique wrongs of the slave trade and colonialism, and to advocate for reparations and solidarity among people of African descent. This was a natural and necessary response to the specific history of anti-Black racism. But it risked leaving other victims of racial capitalism—indigenous peoples, colonized Asians, the white working class—out of the frame, or subordinating their struggles to the struggle for Black liberation. Robinson’s concept of racial capitalism was meant, in part, to address this problem by showing that the fates of different oppressed groups were intertwined from the beginning. But even Robinson, as Johnson reads him, is primarily concerned with the black radical tradition as a source of democratic practice and revolutionary thought. The question of how that tradition relates to other traditions—the indigenous, the anti-colonial, the socialist feminist—remains underexplored.

I do not raise these difficulties to dismiss Johnson’s project. On the contrary, I raise them because I take his project seriously. If we are going to build an account of justice rooted in the history of slavery and racial capitalism, we need to do the hard work of specifying what that account entails. And that work, I suggest, requires us to go beyond the history of slavery to consider the broader field of historical injustices and their remediation. It requires us to engage with the philosophers and legal theorists who have been working for decades on the problem of rectificatory justice—thinkers like Robert Nozick, Jeremy Waldron, and Janna Thompson—and to bring them into conversation with the black radical tradition. It requires us to think about the institutional design of reparations programs: how they would be funded, who would administer them, how eligibility would be determined, how they would interact with existing welfare and development policies. It requires us to grapple with the problem of collective responsibility: the fact that the wrongs of the past were committed not by abstract “systems” but by human beings acting through institutions, and that the responsibility for addressing their legacies falls on human beings who were not yet born when those wrongs were committed.

None of this is easy. It is much easier to critique the inadequacies of liberal justice than to construct a viable alternative. But I hope that Johnson and others who share his commitments will take up the challenge. The history of slavery and racial capitalism is too important to be left as a critique. It must become the foundation for a politics.

  3. 03The reparations model faces significant political and logistical challenges in tracing contested chains of causation and determining eligibility across a global economy where injustice is omnipresent.
  4. 04The black radical tradition must be brought into a more rigorous dialogue with legal and philosophical theories of rectificatory justice to move from a critique of the past to a viable political foundation.
  5. 05An effective account of justice rooted in racial capitalism requires specifying how to relate the unique wrongs of the black radical tradition to the struggles of other oppressed groups, such as indigenous and colonized peoples.

"While Walter Johnson’s essay provides a powerful critique of racial capitalism and a compelling vision of justice, it lacks a concrete strategic plan for how to realize that vision within the current 'propitious moment' of political crisis."

The most radical claim in Walter Johnson’s essay is not, as one might expect, about capitalism or slavery. It is about time. Buried in the final paragraph, almost as an afterthought, Johnson references Du Bois’s argument in *The Suppression of the African Slave Trade* that there are “moments that were propitious for change, moments when it was possible—with courageous and concerted action—to remake the world in its own better image.” And he adds, in his own voice, that “the cost, for Du Bois, of missing those moments could only be reckoned in the blood of the subsequent generations who paid the price for their forebears’ failures.”

This is a striking image: propitious moments, windows of possibility that open and then close, after which the blood of the innocent becomes the interest on the debt of inaction. It is a vision of historical time that is at odds with the linear, progressive temporality that Johnson elsewhere criticizes—the temporality of liberal history, in which things get better, slowly, through the accumulation of reforms. It is also at odds with the fatalistic temporality that sometimes creeps into structural analyses of racial capitalism, in which the system reproduces itself endlessly, absorbing every challenge and turning it to its own purposes. Du Bois’s propitious moment is something else: a kairos, a time of crisis and decision, a moment when the normal rules are suspended and the future hangs in the balance.

I want to take this idea seriously, because I think it illuminates both the power and the pathos of Johnson’s essay. The essay is written, unmistakably, in the voice of someone who believes we are in such a moment now. The Movement for Black Lives, the public reckoning with the history of slavery, the crisis of legitimacy afflicting American institutions—all of this, Johnson seems to suggest, adds up to a propitious moment, a chance to “remake the world.” And yet the essay is also suffused with a kind of melancholy, a sense that the moment may already be slipping away. The scholarship that Johnson criticizes—the “new history of capitalism” that abstracts enslaved people out of the story, the liberal human-rights discourse that domesticates the radical claims of the oppressed—is evidence that the window is closing, that the opportunity for genuine transformation is being lost to a combination of academic fashion and political co-optation.

If Johnson is right that we are in a propitious moment, then the most important question we can ask is: what should we do? What does courageous and concerted action look like, here and now? Johnson’s essay is surprisingly thin on this point. He tells us what kind of justice we should want—restorative, historically grounded, attentive to race and gender and empire—but he does not tell us how to get it. The six virtues he enumerates are, as he says, features of an “account of justice,” not steps toward its realization. They are criteria by which to evaluate a vision of the just society, not strategies for building one.

This is not, I think, an accident. The intellectual tradition Johnson draws on has always been stronger on critique than on strategy. Du Bois, for all his brilliance, was never a successful organizer; his various political projects—the Niagara Movement, the NAACP, the Pan-African Congresses, the Peace Information Center—had mixed results at best. C. L. R. James’s revolutionary politics were, by his own later admission, out of step with the actual conditions of the mid-twentieth century. Cedric Robinson was a theorist, not a movement-builder. This is not to diminish their contributions; it is simply to note that a theory of justice is not the same thing as a plan of action.

What would a plan of action look like, if it took Johnson’s analysis seriously? I can sketch only the barest outline here, but let me try. The starting point, as Johnson argues, is the recognition that the history of slavery and racial capitalism is not just a matter of past wrongs but of ongoing structures. This means that the struggle for justice is not primarily a struggle for recognition or apology or even monetary compensation, but for the transformation of the institutions that reproduce racial inequality: the financial system that channels capital away from black communities, the criminal justice system that incarcerates black people at staggering rates, the education system that underfunds black schools, the housing market that perpetuates segregation. Each of these institutions has a history that runs back through Jim Crow to slavery, and each requires a specific set of remedies: not just more money or better policies, but a fundamental redesign of the way they operate.

This is an overwhelming agenda, and it cannot be accomplished all at once. The question, then, is how to sequence it—where to start, what to prioritize, how to build momentum. The Movement for Black Lives platform, which Johnson cites approvingly, is one attempt to answer this question. It is a comprehensive document, covering everything from reparations to police reform to environmental justice to education. But it is also, in a sense, a wish list: a catalog of demands that the current political system is nowhere close to meeting. The distance between the platform and the possible is vast, and the question for anyone who takes Johnson’s analysis seriously is how to close that gap.

One path is the path of disruptive protest. The protests in Ferguson, Baltimore, and elsewhere in recent years have succeeded in placing police violence and racial inequality at the center of public debate in a way that decades of policy advocacy did not. This is a real achievement, and it vindicates something important in Johnson’s argument: the idea that the perspectives of the oppressed, when expressed collectively and forcefully, can reshape the political landscape. But protest is a limited tool. It can force issues onto the agenda, but it cannot, by itself, enact legislation or build institutions. The history of social movements suggests that protest must be combined with other forms of political action—electoral organizing, legal advocacy, coalition-building with labor and environmental groups—if it is to produce lasting change.

Another path is the path of electoral politics. The Movement for Black Lives has, to a significant degree, embraced this path, with activists running for office at local, state, and national levels. The idea is to take power within the existing system and use it to enact the reforms that the platform calls for. This is a plausible strategy, but it runs up against the very structures that Johnson’s analysis identifies: the entrenchment of white supremacy in the American political system, the disproportionate influence of money in politics, the gerrymandering and voter suppression that dilute black political power. It also runs up against the limits of reformism: even if black candidates are elected in larger numbers, they will still be operating within a capitalist economy and a carceral state that were built, in part, on the foundations of slavery. How much can be changed from within?

A third path is the path of building alternative institutions. This is the path most deeply rooted in the black radical tradition, from the mutual aid societies of the nineteenth century to the Black Panther Party’s survival programs to the contemporary experiments in worker cooperatives and community land trusts. The idea is to create spaces of autonomy and self-determination that are not dependent on the white-dominated institutions of the larger society. Johnson’s invocation of the “democratic practices and revolutionary thought of black people living under conditions of racial capitalism” points in this direction. But here, too, there are limits: alternative institutions can provide important forms of immediate relief and models for the future, but they cannot, on their own, transform the structures of racial capitalism that operate at the level of the global economy and the nation-state.

My own view is that all three paths are necessary, and that the most important task for the present moment is to build the kind of multiracial, cross-class coalition that can pursue them simultaneously. This is not a new insight. It was the insight of the Popular Front in the 1930s, of the civil rights movement in the 1960s, of the Rainbow Coalition in the 1980s. Each of those coalitions had its limits and its failures, but each also achieved real gains that improved the lives of millions of people. The challenge for the current moment is to build a coalition that is durable enough to survive setbacks, inclusive enough to hold together diverse interests, and radical enough to demand the systemic changes that Johnson’s analysis shows are necessary.

Johnson’s essay does not, in the end, tell us how to do this. But it does something almost as important: it reminds us of the stakes. If this is indeed a propitious moment, and if we fail to take advantage of it, then the cost, as Du Bois warned, will be reckoned in the blood of our children and grandchildren. That is a heavy burden, and Johnson has done us the service of bringing it into view.
