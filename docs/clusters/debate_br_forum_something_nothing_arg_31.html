"While Nir Rosen correctly identifies the futility of the current U.S. military strategy in Afghanistan, his analysis overlooks the potential for an Afghan-led political settlement and local peace processes to break the cycle of violence."

Nir Rosen’s long, intimate report from Helmand is a necessary corrective to the whitewashed narratives that dominate official briefings, but it is also a profoundly disempowering read. The essay leaves an impression of almost total futility: the Americans are callow or cruel, the Afghan police are incompetent or thuggish, the Taliban are always watching, and the civilians are permanently victimized, able only to complain in the morning and be slaughtered at night. This portrait has the ring of lived truth, but it is incomplete and, in its own way, ideological. It omits a crucial dimension: the agency of ordinary Afghans, and the possibility—however slim—that a political settlement, not a military victory, could break the cycle of violence. By focusing relentlessly on the hopelessness of the current strategy, Rosen risks reinforcing the very cynicism that justifies both the occupation and the insurgency. The real question is not whether the American counterinsurgency campaign can succeed on its own terms—by now it is clear it cannot—but whether there is an Afghan-led path to an imperfect peace that the United States could support rather than undermine.

Rosen’s most powerful material is the testimony of the Afghans themselves. The old farmer who says, “If the Taliban see us talking to you they will slaughter us tonight,” and the policeman who has lost nearly a hundred comrades—these voices convey the horror of being trapped between two armed camps. But what the essay largely ignores is that many Afghans are actively trying to carve out a space between those camps. The “tribal” dimension that Rosen dismisses as an American obsession is actually a real feature of Afghan social organization, and in many parts of the country, especially in the east and south, local communities have negotiated their own ceasefires with the Taliban or played the government and the insurgents off each other to minimize bloodshed. These are not grand political deals of the kind the U.S. seeks, but they represent a deep reservoir of local knowledge and pragmatism. A smarter American policy would double down on supporting these local peace processes rather than imposing a centralized, Kabul-centric model of governance that nobody trusts.

The central obstacle to such an approach is not Afghan culture but the American insistence on fighting the Taliban everywhere, all the time, while simultaneously demanding that the Karzai government be the sole legitimate authority. This two-headed strategy is self-defeating, because the Taliban are a political and social movement, not just a terrorist gang. As Rosen notes, there is no single Taliban; there are multiple networks, many of which have local grievances having little to do with global jihad. A political settlement that offers these groups a share of local power, security guarantees, and a path away from the insurgency is exactly what a genuinely population-centric strategy would pursue. Yet McChrystal’s COIN plan focuses overwhelmingly on security and development, while the Obama administration’s conditions for reconciliation remain so demanding—disarmament, acceptance of the constitution, breaking with al Qaeda—that no significant Taliban leader has yet been willing to accept them. The missing component is a good-faith negotiation supported by Afghanistan’s neighbors, including Pakistan and Iran, in which the Taliban see a realistic chance of achieving their core goal: the departure of foreign troops and a role in governing the country.

Rosen’s essay is itself evidence of the cost of failing to take the political track seriously. The police checkpoint where the men are outgunned and outsupplied is not a failure of training alone; it is a failure of politics. If local communities felt the police were their protectors rather than an alien arm of a hated central government, the insurgents would have a much harder time operating. But that shift in perception cannot happen while the police are seen as the enforcers of an American occupation. A political settlement that includes a cease-fire and a gradual withdrawal of foreign forces could, paradoxically, create the breathing room that allows the Afghan state to rebuild its legitimacy organically, with less overt foreign control. That is a long-term proposition, but it is more realistic than the expectation that a few more years of military mentoring will turn the Afghan security forces into a cohesive, legitimate institution.

The danger of Rosen’s narrative, powerful as it is, is that it encourages a flight from complexity. The reader is left with the impression that the whole enterprise is irredeemably corrupt and that the only dignified response is to wash one’s hands and leave. That instinct is understandable, but it ignores the millions of Afghans who are not armed, not Taliban, and not warlords—the teachers, farmers, and health workers who have invested their hopes in the gradual reconstruction of civil society since 2001. Abandoning them to a renewed civil war between the Taliban and the northern militias would be a moral catastrophe that Rosen, for all his sympathy with Afghan suffering, does not adequately reckon with. The choice is not between permanent occupation and total withdrawal; there is a middle ground that involves supporting an Afghan-led peace process while gradually reducing the foreign military footprint. That middle ground is not glamorous, and it may not produce something shiny from the dust of Helmand, but it is the best hope for a country that has already buried far too many of its sons and daughters. The Americans should not be trying to build something from nothing; they should be listening to the Afghans who are already building, and getting out of their way.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The U.S. should pivot from a centralized, Kabul-centric governance model to supporting local communities that have already demonstrated the agency to negotiate their own ceasefires and manage local conflicts.
  2. 02A successful strategy requires recognizing the Taliban as a diverse political and social movement and offering them a realistic path to power-sharing and foreign troop withdrawal through good-faith negotiations.
  3. 03The failure of the Afghan police is primarily a political issue, as they cannot gain legitimacy while perceived as the enforcers of a foreign occupation rather than protectors of the local community.
  4. 04Total withdrawal would be a moral catastrophe for Afghan civil society; instead, the U.S. should pursue a middle ground that supports an indigenous peace process while gradually reducing its military footprint.
