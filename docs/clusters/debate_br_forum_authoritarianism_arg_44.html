"The rejection of moderation in favor of a mobilization-only strategy is a strategic error that misinterprets voter psychology and risks permanent minority status for the Democratic Party."

The provocative title of Bonica and Grumbach’s essay promises a guide to saving democracy, but what it actually delivers is a brittle and ultimately unconvincing dismissal of moderation. For all its data and scholarly apparatus, the piece rests on a series of false choices, a misreading of the 2024 election, and a vision of politics that is psychologically unrealistic. The answer to the authoritarian threat is not to abandon moderation; it is to deepen and diversify it.

The authors set up a straw man: popularism, they say, reduces voters to fixed consumers, mirrors poll data without leadership, and produces empty pandering. But this is a cartoon. The best version of popularism—articulated by thinkers like Klein and Teixeira—is simply the insistence that Democrats should distinguish between their most popular ideas and their least popular ones, and prioritize accordingly. It does not demand that candidates have no convictions, or that they never attempt to persuade voters. It merely recognizes that you cannot persuade anyone if you start by insulting their deepest intuitions or by embracing policies that large majorities find alarming. This is not Cleon; it is basic democratic accountability.

The essay’s empirical claim—that moderation has no consistent effect on vote share in the Trump era—is vulnerable on multiple fronts. First, the authors rely on a composite ideology measure that includes campaign finance data and voting records, but as they themselves note, voters do not perceive candidate ideology in these terms. What matters is the public’s perception of extremism, which is shaped by media, national party brand, and salient cultural issues. A candidate can be moderate on tax policy but still be tarred as a radical because of the Democratic Party’s association with “defund the police” or “gender-affirming care for minors.” The real question isn’t whether a candidate’s DW-NOMINATE score predicts their vote share; it’s whether candidates who credibly signal moderation on culturally salient issues do better than those who don’t. On that question, the evidence is much more mixed, and the authors’ own examples—Manchin’s success, Golden’s eventual collapse—are better explained by the durability and then exhaustion of their cultural brands than by any irrelevance of moderation per se.

Furthermore, the focus on “moderation” conflates two distinct strategic dimensions: positional and relational. Positional moderation, staking out a midpoint between two poles, is indeed often a loser in a polarized age, as the authors demonstrate. But relational moderation—the cultivation of a temperament of reasonableness, willingness to listen, and refusal to moralize incessantly—remains a powerful electoral asset. Many voters who have drifted to the GOP in recent years are not demanding a particular ideological mix; they are repelled by what they perceive as the Democratic Party’s smug, scolding, and culturally alien sensibility. They don’t want a candidate who splits the difference on taxes; they want one who speaks like a normal person and seems to respect people who didn’t go to college. To call that “moderation” may be imprecise, but the authors’ dismissal of all moderation talk sweeps this relational dimension away with it.

The 2024 election is Exhibit A for why the authors’ alternative is insufficient. They acknowledge the turnout gap but blame it on a lack of anti-corruption messaging. This ignores the more obvious culprit: inflation. From 2021 to 2024, voters experienced the highest sustained inflation in four decades. Incumbent parties around the world were punished for it, regardless of ideology. No amount of anti-corruption framing or base mobilization could have shielded Democrats from that headwind. To pretend that a different message would have fundamentally altered the outcome is wishful thinking. Worse, it misleads Democrats into believing that they can win without addressing the economic pain that drove millions of working-class voters into the arms of a demagogue who promised to “fix it.”

The anti-corruption frame itself has serious flaws as a central electoral strategy. First, it’s an elite-centric concern. The polls the authors cite show that Americans are worried about corruption, but when forced to rank priorities, corruption falls far behind the economy, healthcare, and immigration. That’s because most people experience corruption as a background condition, not an immediate pocketbook issue. They may believe the system is rigged, but they will vote for the candidate who promises to lower gas prices and secure the border, even if that candidate is himself a walking corruption scandal. Trump’s entire career is testament to this.

Second, anti-corruption rhetoric is inherently negative and backward-looking. It tells voters what you’re against, but not what you’re for. It does not give people a positive vision of what life would look like under Democratic governance. By contrast, the most successful Democratic campaigns in recent memory—Obama 2008, even Biden 2020 to some extent—offered a forward-looking, hopeful message. An anti-corruption crusade risks sounding like a perpetual indictment, which, while emotionally satisfying to some, grows exhausting for a broad electorate.

The more fundamental weakness of the authors’ call for mobilization over persuasion is that it assumes a stable base that merely needs energizing. But the Democratic base itself is ideologically diverse and includes many voters who are moderate on cultural issues. If the party moves to a full-throated mobilization strategy centered on anti-corruption and implicitly left-wing economic populism, it risks alienating a significant chunk of its own voters, especially older Black and Latino voters who are more socially conservative. The authors’ own data on progressive candidates slightly boosting turnout among Democrats is an aggregate effect that might obscure these internal divisions. A campaign message that fires up young progressives in safe blue cities may depress turnout among older, churchgoing Black voters in North Carolina or Georgia, without whom there is no Democratic path in the South.

The essay closes with a stark warning: moderate and fail, or transform and live. But the binary misreads the moment. What is needed is not a wholesale rejection of moderation but a more sophisticated, layered approach. Democrats should be immoderate in their ambition: promise structural economic change, protect democracy boldly, and articulate a clear moral alternative to authoritarianism. But they should be moderate in their tactics: signal cultural sensibility, respect religious freedom, back strong border enforcement, and speak to the legitimate fears of people who feel their way of life is being erased. The task is not to pick one pole of a false dichotomy but to build a pluralistic coalition that can hold the tension. That is hard, but it’s the only path that can deliver the durable majorities that American democracy requires. The authors’ abandonment of the center is a recipe for permanent minority status, not a blueprint for salvation.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The authors present a straw-man version of popularism that ignores its core function as a tool for democratic accountability and prioritizing popular policies.
  2. 02The essay fails to distinguish between positional moderation and relational moderation, the latter of which remains a vital asset for appealing to voters repelled by the party's perceived cultural elitism.
  3. 03The 2024 election results were primarily driven by global economic headwinds like inflation rather than a failure of Democratic messaging or a lack of anti-corruption focus.
  4. 04An anti-corruption platform is an elite-centric concern that lacks the pocketbook urgency required to override voters' primary interests in the economy and immigration.
  5. 05A pure mobilization strategy centered on left-wing populism risks alienating socially conservative segments of the existing Democratic base, such as older Black and Latino voters.
  6. 06The most effective path forward is a layered approach that combines bold policy ambition with cultural and tactical moderation to build a broad, pluralistic coalition.
