"Amnesty should be framed and defended as essential labor reform rather than just a matter of social membership, as unauthorized status functions as a tool for employer exploitation that degrades standards for all workers."

The debate over amnesty is often framed as a conflict between immigrants and citizens. That frame misses one of the central facts of unauthorized immigration: employers are the hinge of the system. Joseph Carens rightly emphasizes the moral claims that grow from residence, family, and social membership. But the case for legalization becomes even stronger when we look at the workplace. Unauthorized status is not merely a technical legal defect; it is a labor condition. It creates a class of workers who can be threatened, cheated, injured, and discarded with unusual ease.

The United States has seen this clearly in industries that rely on low-wage immigrant labor. Meatpacking plants, construction sites, farms, restaurants, cleaning services, and domestic work do not merely “happen” to employ unauthorized workers. They often organize around the workers’ vulnerability. A worker who fears deportation is less likely to report wage theft, unsafe conditions, sexual harassment, or retaliation. He is less likely to join a union. She is less likely to challenge an employer who refuses overtime pay. Unauthorized status lowers the floor for everyone.

This is why a pro-worker immigration policy cannot be built on deportability. Some labor advocates have worried that legalization will increase labor competition. That fear is understandable, especially in the middle of the 2009 recession, when unemployment is rising and many families are frightened. But the current system already creates competition on unfair terms. The problem is not simply that unauthorized workers are present; it is that they are present without rights they can enforce. Employers gain leverage by pitting workers against one another. Legalization would not solve every labor-market problem, but it would remove one of the most powerful tools of exploitation.

Carens’s “social membership” argument captures the immigrant’s claim against the state. A labor argument adds the citizen worker’s claim against the same system. When undocumented roofers can be underpaid, documented roofers are pressured to accept less. When a poultry plant can call immigration authorities after a union drive begins, all workers lose bargaining power. When domestic workers are isolated in private homes with no realistic complaint mechanism, legal status becomes a matter of bodily safety. Amnesty is therefore not a gift to “them.” It is part of reconstructing a labor market for all of us.

The 1986 Immigration Reform and Control Act is instructive. It combined legalization with employer sanctions. The legalization was real and humane; millions became lawful residents and later citizens. But employer sanctions were poorly designed. Workers bore much of the burden through document checks, discrimination, and the growth of false papers, while employers often escaped serious accountability. A new amnesty should learn from that failure. Legalization must be paired with vigorous labor enforcement: more wage-and-hour investigators, meaningful penalties for retaliation, protection for workers who report violations, and a firewall between labor complaints and immigration enforcement.

That last point is crucial. If an unauthorized worker who reports unpaid wages is placed in removal proceedings, the law has chosen the employer’s side. Whatever one thinks about border control, labor standards should apply to every worker in the country. Otherwise minimum wage and safety laws become optional wherever the workforce is vulnerable. In practice, “enforcing immigration law” can become a way to nullify labor law.

Carens proposes that settled migrants acquire legal status after five to seven years. From a workplace perspective, I would go further and say that any broad legalization should be implemented quickly for those already here. The reason is not that time is irrelevant, but that exploitation is happening now. A worker who has been here three years may be just as vulnerable to abuse as one who has been here ten. Still, if political compromise requires a residency threshold, the threshold should be generous and combined with provisional status for those who can show employment, family ties, or community residence.

Critics will say that legalization rewards illegal hiring and illegal entry. But the status quo rewards illegal hiring far more. It allows employers to enjoy labor while externalizing the costs onto workers and communities. If we are serious about lawbreaking, we should ask why the national conversation so often focuses on the migrant with false papers and so rarely on the employer who builds a business model around fear. The worker’s violation is visible; the employer’s advantage is normalized.

The recession complicates the politics. Many citizens and lawful immigrants are losing jobs and homes. It is easy to say this is the wrong time to legalize unauthorized workers. But economic insecurity is precisely when labor standards matter most. A shadow workforce is not less dangerous in a downturn; it is more dangerous. Desperate employers cut corners. Desperate workers accept risks. Deportability magnifies the imbalance.

Legalization should be accompanied by reforms that protect low-wage workers generally: stronger enforcement of prevailing wage rules, portable visas for future migrant workers so they are not tied to a single employer, sanctions for labor recruiters who charge abusive fees, and investment in job training and unemployment support for citizens and immigrants alike. The choice should not be between immigrant legalization and native-born workers. The choice should be between a labor market governed by rights and one governed by fear.

Carens is right to say that people who live and work in a society become members of it. Work is not the only basis of membership, and we should resist valuing immigrants only for their economic contribution. But work is where the hypocrisy of current policy is most obvious. We call people illegal while eating food they picked, sleeping in hotels they cleaned, and living in houses they helped build. Then we threaten to deport them for having done the work our economy made available.

Amnesty should be defended not as charity, but as labor reform. It would bring millions of workers out of the shadows, reduce employers’ ability to divide the workforce, and make existing workplace laws more than paper promises. A democracy cannot sustain equal citizenship on top of a caste of rightless laborers. If we want the law to command respect, it must first stop licensing exploitation.
