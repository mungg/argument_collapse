"The Occupy movement and its supporters misinterpret American founding principles as egalitarian ideals when, in reality, those principles are rooted in limited government, individual liberty, and personal responsibility."

The editors of the Boston Review frame the moment in terms of a tension between principles and institutions. They invite us to see Occupy Wall Street as the latest chapter in a noble American tradition of reform. It is a generous description, but it misunderstands both the nature of our founding principles and the character of the current protest. The principles of the United States are not about equal outcomes or even about an abstract “right to be heard” that implies equal influence. The Declaration of Independence secures the pursuit of happiness, not its guaranteed capture. The Constitution erects a system of limited government designed to protect liberty, property, and the fruits of individual labor. The disjuncture we now face is not between our institutions and some egalitarian ideal; it is between the enervating logic of entitlement and the founders’ vision of a self-reliant citizenry.

Occupy Wall Street, for all its evocative symbolic power, is animated by envy, not principle. The notion that the rich have seized a disproportionate share of American prosperity ignores the essential dynamism of a market economy. The incomes of the wealthy largely reflect the creation of enormous value—companies like Apple or Google that have transformed daily life—and the global market’s voracious appetite for talent. When a financier earns a fortune, we may question the social utility, but the remedy is not street theater. It is better regulation, more competition, and a culture that honors creators rather than condemning them. There is no fixed pie of “national voice” that the rich have somehow monopolized. In a republic, the homeless man’s ballot counts as much as the billionaire’s; the problem is not political inequality but the growing sense that government can and should deliver material equality through redistribution.

That impulse is precisely what Occupy, in its inchoate way, demands. The movement’s supporters speak of bringing “our institutions into better alignment” with egalitarian principles, but the logical end of this alignment is a vast expansion of state power—higher taxes, more regimented labor markets, intrusive regulation—that would choke the very engine of prosperity that has lifted millions out of poverty. We have seen this script before. The twentieth century is littered with grand movements that claimed to speak for “the people” against a capitalist elite, only to devour liberty in the name of equality. I do not suggest Occupy is headed for the gulag, but its rhetoric of class division corrodes the social fabric. It teaches young people to see their neighbors not as fellow citizens but as oppressors in a zero-sum struggle.

Our institutions are not blameless. Government has grown cozy with big business, creating crony capitalism that favors the connected over the enterprising. But that is a failing of government overreach, not of markets. The answer is to return to first principles: limit the state’s ability to pick winners and losers, free entrepreneurs from the regulatory thicket, and restore a culture of thrift, hard work, and deferred gratification. The “dramatic reform” the editors hope for should not be a leftward lurch but a recommitment to the principles of limited government and personal responsibility that made the American experiment exceptional in the first place.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Declaration of Independence and the Constitution protect the pursuit of happiness and property rights rather than guaranteeing equal outcomes or equal political influence.
  2. 02Economic inequality is largely a reflection of value creation and talent in a dynamic market rather than a result of the wealthy monopolizing a fixed national prosperity.
  3. 03The movement's demand for institutional alignment with egalitarianism would require a dangerous expansion of state power that threatens economic prosperity and individual liberty.
  4. 04The rhetoric of class struggle promoted by the movement corrodes the social fabric by encouraging citizens to view one another as oppressors in a zero-sum game.
  5. 05The solution to current institutional failings is not redistribution, but a return to first principles that limit government overreach and end crony capitalism.
