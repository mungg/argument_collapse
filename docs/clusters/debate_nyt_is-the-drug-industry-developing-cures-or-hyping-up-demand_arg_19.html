"Reducing the influence of drug companies should focus on improving diagnostic rigor and insurance policies rather than simply demonizing medications or assuming the conditions they treat are manufactured."

The current anger at drug companies is understandable, but it risks flattening a complicated reality. A.D.H.D. was not invented by pharmaceutical marketers. Long before today’s branded extended-release stimulants, physicians were describing children with severe impulsivity, poor attention and disruptive restlessness. The condition runs in families, is associated with real academic and social impairment, and often responds to medications that have been studied for decades.

So the answer cannot be simply: push drug companies out and the problem disappears. Some of the most commonly used A.D.H.D. medicines are generic stimulants. Many parents who seek treatment are not gullible victims of advertising; they are exhausted, worried and trying to help a child who cannot function at school or at home. Adults who were never diagnosed as children may recognize, sometimes for the first time, a pattern that has damaged their education, work and relationships.

That said, no one should trust a sales force to set the boundaries of a psychiatric diagnosis. The incentives are too obvious. A company that sells a daily pill benefits when mild symptoms become medicalized, when long-term use is assumed and when medication is presented as the default rather than one option. Direct-to-consumer advertising, paid speakers and industry-funded education can all tilt judgment, even when nobody is consciously corrupt.

The right response is not to demonize useful drugs, but to make diagnosis and treatment more trustworthy. A child should not receive an A.D.H.D. prescription after a ten-minute visit and a parent’s checklist alone. Schools and teachers should provide structured observations. Clinicians should screen for sleep problems, anxiety, learning disabilities, trauma and family stress. For young children and milder cases, behavioral interventions and parent training should be available before medication becomes the path of least resistance.

Insurance policy matters as much as drug policy. If insurers pay readily for pills but not for careful evaluation, coaching, classroom consultation or therapy, they effectively vote for medication. Pediatricians also need enough reimbursement time to do more than renew prescriptions.

Drug companies should be kept out of guideline writing, gifts should remain off limits, and all payments to doctors should be public. But we should not confuse reducing influence with reducing treatment. The goal is not fewer prescriptions at any cost. The goal is prescriptions that follow from careful diagnosis, honest evidence and the patient’s actual impairment—not from a marketing plan.

  4. 04Insurance companies contribute to the over-reliance on drugs by prioritizing payment for pills over more time-intensive therapies and thorough evaluations.
