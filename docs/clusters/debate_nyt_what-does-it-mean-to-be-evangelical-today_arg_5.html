"To be an evangelical today is to be caught in a profound generational and institutional identity crisis that is forcing the movement to choose between its legacy of political power and a more holistic, gospel-centered future."

To be an evangelical in 2016 is to be trapped in a profound generational and institutional identity crisis. The current hand-wringing over the label, prompted by the chaotic Republican primary, is just a surface symptom of a much deeper earthquake reshaping American religion.

For the older generation, evangelicalism was defined by the culture wars. From the late 1970s onward, the movement mobilized primarily around issues like abortion and traditional marriage, building massive institutions and lobbying groups to fight for a specific vision of Christian America. To be an evangelical meant to be a foot soldier in this moral army.

However, younger evangelicals—the millennials who are now coming of age and stepping into leadership—view the world through a radically different lens. They are exhausted by the partisan hostility of their parents’ generation. While they largely retain the core orthodox theology of their forebears, their moral imagination is much broader. For a millennial evangelical, living out the gospel is just as likely to involve fighting human trafficking, providing clean water in developing nations, or addressing racial reconciliation in their own cities as it is to involve voting for a socially conservative politician.

This generational divide has created a schism in the meaning of the word itself. The older guard watches in confusion as their political consensus shatters, while the younger generation feels alienated by leaders who seem more concerned with maintaining cultural power than demonstrating the grace of Christ. The rise of a brash, secular populist among self-identified evangelicals is forcing the issue to a breaking point.

Ultimately, to be evangelical today means to be at a crossroads. The movement must decide whether it will cling to the fading political machinery of the 20th century or embrace a more holistic, culturally engaged, and politically independent witness for the 21st century. The label may currently be tarnished, but the movement itself is undergoing a necessary, if painful, evolution.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The older generation of evangelicals defined the movement through the lens of the culture wars and the pursuit of political influence.
  2. 02Millennial evangelicals are shifting the movement's focus away from partisan hostility toward broader social justice issues like human trafficking and racial reconciliation.
  3. 03The current political climate has created a schism where younger believers feel alienated by a leadership that prioritizes cultural power over the grace of Christ.
  4. 04The movement is undergoing a necessary but painful evolution that requires deciding whether to maintain 20th-century political machinery or embrace a more independent and culturally engaged witness.
