Diversified Gemini

"While Merve Emre correctly critiques the consumerist privilege of the 'natural' parenting industry, her essay dangerously dismisses the physiological reality of bodily autonomy and risks surrendering feminist agency to a patriarchal medical-industrial complex."

Merve Emre’s thought-provoking essay, “All Reproduction Is Assisted,” provides a necessary critique of the consumerist, privilege-laden discourse surrounding "natural" motherhood. Emre rightly points out how the Goop-ification of childbirth—with its $500 hypnosis tutorials and expensive water-birth tubs—alienates those who cannot or choose not to reproduce in this manner. However, in her zeal to dismantle the "School of Great Experience" and elevate assisted reproductive technology (ART) as a unifying political framework, Emre dangerously conflates the commodified *lifestyle* of "natural parenting" with the deeply vital, physiological reality of bodily autonomy in childbirth. In doing so, she risks erasing one of the most significant feminist victories of the late twentieth century: the demedicalization of the female body.

Emre quotes Shulamith Firestone’s mockery of childbirth as "shitting a pumpkin," using it to dismiss the desire for physiological birth as "internalized misogyny masquerading as liberation." This framing fundamentally misrepresents the history and purpose of the natural childbirth and midwifery movements. When feminists in the 1970s and 80s fought against the standard medical practices of the time, they were not doing so out of a masochistic desire for pain or a "hippie-Rousseauean" delusion. They were fighting against a deeply patriarchal obstetric system that routinely drugged women into twilight sleep, strapped them to delivery tables, performed non-consensual episiotomies, and forcefully separated them from their newborns.

The movement to reclaim the "natural" or physiological processes of birth was, at its core, a movement for informed consent and bodily integrity. It was a rejection of the idea that women’s bodies were inherently defective machines that required the management of (mostly male) surgeons to function. By collapsing this profound political struggle into a caricature of wealthy white women buying organic prenatal vitamins, Emre does a disservice to the generations of women—and particularly Black and Indigenous midwives—who fought to protect birth from being entirely subsumed by a hostile medical-industrial complex.

Emre urges us to embrace ART and recognize that "all reproduction is assisted." She is, of course, factually correct that no one gives birth or raises a child in a vacuum; social, medical, and community assistance are universal. But by flattening the distinction between the biological processes of gestation and the highly interventionist procedures of IVF, Emre obscures the very real power dynamics inherent in medicalized spaces.

When a woman undergoes IVF, as Emre’s own reporting shows, she must surrender a massive degree of bodily autonomy to the medical establishment. S’s life is dictated by the "unforgiving choreography" of the clinic; B endures agonizing hyperstimulation and invasive procedures by dismissive residents; K is repeatedly misgendered and ignored by her clinicians. Emre portrays these indignities as unfortunate hurdles on the path to the liberatory promise of technology. But what if these are not mere hurdles? What if the systemic disrespect, the commodification of reproductive tissue, and the reduction of women to "follicles doing their thing" are inherent features of a reproductive model that views the body as raw material to be engineered?

There is a profound difference between a midwife *assisting* a physiological process by providing emotional support and monitoring for complications, and a reproductive endocrinologist chemically halting a woman's cycle, surgically extracting her eggs, fertilizing them in a lab, and transferring them back. Both are forms of assistance, yes, but they represent entirely different paradigms of power. To pretend they are points on a continuous spectrum of "assistance" is to ignore who holds the power in these interactions.

Feminism has long maintained that women's bodies belong to them, not to the state, and not to the medical establishment. The push for physiological birth—stripped of its capitalist distortions—is an insistence that a person's body is capable, strong, and worthy of respect without requiring technological rescue. It is a defense against the medical gaze that views pregnancy as a disease to be managed rather than a normal human function.

Emre’s vision of using ART to "close the gap" between different classes and sexes relies on a techno-utopianism that ignores how medical technology has historically been deployed. The same medical establishment that controls IVF is the one that has systematically ignored Black women's pain, leading to the current crisis in maternal mortality where Black women die at three to four times the rate of white women. If we make heavily medicalized reproduction our preferred strategy, we are placing even more power in the hands of a system steeped in structural racism and misogyny.

We can and must fight for universal healthcare, inclusive family building, and respect for queer, trans, and disabled parents without throwing out the physiological realities of the human body. We do not need to view the body as a machine to be hacked in order to validate the experiences of those who rely on ART. A truly inclusive reproductive politics would not mock the desire to experience childbirth physiologically; rather, it would recognize that true reproductive justice means giving every person the autonomy to choose the conditions of their reproduction, free from both the coercion of corporate "naturalism" and the hubris of the medical-industrial complex.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The movement for natural childbirth was historically a vital feminist struggle for informed consent and bodily integrity against a hostile and paternalistic obstetric system.
  2. 02Emre’s framework flattens the distinction between biological gestation and interventionist technology, obscuring the reality that medicalized reproduction often requires women to surrender their autonomy to clinicians.
  3. 03Assisted reproductive technology and physiological birth represent different paradigms of power, and treating them as a single spectrum ignores the potential for medical exploitation.
  4. 04Relying on heavily medicalized reproduction as a primary political strategy grants more power to a healthcare system that has a history of structural racism and ignoring the pain of marginalized women.
  5. 05True reproductive justice should protect the right to choose the conditions of reproduction—including physiological birth—without viewing the human body as a defective machine that requires technological rescue.
