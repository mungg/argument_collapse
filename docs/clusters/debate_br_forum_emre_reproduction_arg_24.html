The Trans Reproductive Frontier

The most quietly radical moments in Merve Emre's essay are the ones featuring trans people: Lara, the trans woman in Joanne Spataro's New York Times piece, gradually tapering her estrogen to try to conceive a child with her cis partner; micha cárdenas, banking sperm before HRT, photographing her gametes through a fifty-dollar children's microscope, writing a poem-pregnancy as art. These figures sit at the edge of Emre's argument, and I think they should be at its center. The reason is not identity-political. It is that trans reproduction makes the structural argument of the essay — that the natural is an ideological screen, that all reproduction is assisted — undeniable in a way that other examples do not.

Consider what is involved, materially, in Lara and Joanne's project. Lara has been on estrogen and androgen blockers for some period before deciding to try to conceive. Her endocrine system has been intentionally restructured. To produce viable sperm again, she must reverse parts of that restructuring — taper the estrogen, accept the return of features she chose hormones partly to suppress, ride out the physical and psychological changes of partial detransition, and then conceive, and then either resume HRT or not. Every stage of this process is technologically mediated. Every stage is also chosen. There is no moment at which "the natural" can pretend to be the default. The conception is biological in the narrow sense — egg, sperm, uterus, the standard ingredients — but no part of it is unmediated, automatic, or pre-political.

This is what is so radical about Lara and Joanne's situation, and why it sparked the angry online reaction Emre describes. The "old-fashioned way" framing in the Times tweet was not an accident; it was an attempt to defuse what the story actually reveals. Trans reproduction makes visible the technological mediation of all sexed bodies. It makes visible the fact that endocrinology is not destiny, that gametes can be banked, that fertility can be paused and resumed, that the boundary between "natural" and "assisted" reproduction is not a biological line but a cultural one, drawn at certain culturally legible interventions and not others. Hormonal contraception, which restructures fertility in cis women for decades at a stretch, has been domesticated; HRT, which restructures fertility in trans women, has not. The difference is political, not biological. To watch the conversation rage about Lara and Joanne in 2018 is to watch the policing of that political line in real time.

cárdenas's bioart makes the same point with a different texture. She had been told she would be sterile. She was not. She bought a child's microscope and filmed her own sperm and put the video in a Facebook group for trans women banking gametes — a small underground network of self-knowledge that the medical establishment had refused them. The poem Emre quotes — "they told me I would be sterile, / the doctors and brochures, / that I couldn't do this, / what I'm doing" — names something specific. The medical apparatus that purports to manage trans bodies has consistently lied to trans people about what those bodies can do. The pre-transition counseling about fertility preservation has historically been inadequate or absent. The information about post-transition reproductive capacity has been wrong as often as it has been right. Trans people have built their own knowledge networks, against this apparatus, to find out what their bodies are. This is technologically assisted reproduction in the deepest sense: the technology of shared knowledge, accumulated against institutional indifference, that allows a person to know what reproductive options she has at all.

This frame — trans reproduction as paradigmatic rather than peripheral — has implications for how we read the rest of Emre's essay. It means that S, B, and N and K are not the central cases with cárdenas as a poetic afterthought; cárdenas is the central case and the others are recognizable variants. Each of these women is navigating a reproductive system that requires technological mediation to deliver what cultural ideology pretends is natural. Each is doing so in the context of an information environment partially structured by misinformation, neglect, or hostility. Each is leaning on networks — friends, partners, online communities, Facebook groups, queer TTC sisters, employer-sponsored LGBT associations — to compensate for the failures of mainstream medicine. The trans case is not exceptional. It is, in a sense, the unmasked version of everyone's case.

This has political implications. If trans reproduction is the paradigm rather than the periphery, then reproductive politics needs to be reorganized around what trans reproduction needs. That includes: routine fertility counseling and preservation as part of standard transition care, fully funded, without gatekeeping; legal recognition of all forms of parenthood that emerge from trans reproductive paths, including non-genetic and non-gestational parents; protection of trans people from family-court attempts to use transition as evidence of unfitness; expansion of insurance coverage for assisted reproductive technology to all sexed configurations, not just those that fit the infertility-diagnosis template; protection of trans youth from interventions, including hormonal interventions, that proceed without informed consent about fertility implications; and, not least, the basic safety conditions cárdenas names when she observes that for trans women of color the reproductive horizon includes the possibility of being killed before having children.

The last point deserves more weight than Emre gives it. cárdenas's line — "I could be dead anytime. There is a real sense of urgency" — names a reproductive issue that virtually no mainstream feminist literature treats as such. The murder rate of trans women, particularly Black and Latina trans women, in the United States in 2018 is a reproductive issue because it forecloses reproductive futures. The asylum cases at the border, where trans women are routinely detained in male facilities and assaulted, are reproductive issues. The healthcare denials that shorten trans lives are reproductive issues. To treat these as separate from reproductive politics — as belonging to "trans rights" or "anti-violence" but not to "reproductive justice" — is to misunderstand the categories. Reproductive justice in its founding articulation has always included the right to live long enough to parent, in conditions safe enough to parent. Trans women of color make that demand particularly legible.

What I am asking, then, is for the trans reproductive frontier to be read not as one more interesting case in the pluralism of reproductive arrangements, but as the case that exposes the underlying truth of the others. All reproduction is assisted; trans reproduction makes that fact unhideable. All reproductive bodies are technologically mediated; trans bodies have always been described as such, while cis bodies have been allowed to pretend otherwise. All reproductive desire takes place against structural conditions that variously enable or foreclose it; for trans people, especially trans women of color, those conditions include the elementary condition of staying alive.

Emre's essay does serious justice to this material. I want to take it one step further. The future of reproductive politics, if it is going to be honest, will be a trans future. Not in the sense that everyone will be trans, but in the sense that the reproductive imagination organized around fixed sex, given fertility, and unmediated biology will have been abandoned. Cárdenas is not at the margin of this picture. She is the picture.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The experiences of trans individuals like Lara demonstrate that the boundary between 'natural' and 'assisted' reproduction is a political and cultural distinction rather than a biological one.
  2. 02Trans reproductive networks illustrate that assisted reproduction includes the technology of shared community knowledge developed in response to institutional medical neglect.
  3. 03Centering the trans experience reveals that all reproductive bodies are technologically mediated, stripping away the 'natural' facade that cisgender bodies are permitted to maintain.
  4. 04A truly honest reproductive politics must be reorganized around trans needs, such as routine fertility preservation and legal recognition of non-normative parenthood.
  5. 05The high rates of violence against trans women of color must be recognized as a core reproductive justice issue, as safety is a prerequisite for the right to parent.
  6. 06The future of reproductive imagination requires abandoning the concepts of fixed sex and unmediated biology in favor of a model that acknowledges all reproduction as assisted.
