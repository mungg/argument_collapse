"Feminism must move beyond merely acknowledging assisted reproduction to actively denaturalizing the process, ultimately aiming to dissolve the normative distinction between natural and artificial reproduction to achieve true liberation."

The essay “All Reproduction Is Assisted” performs an extraordinary act of political defamiliarization. By tracing the continuities between infant incubators, egg freezing, IVF, and the humble pregnancy test, it makes visible what the ideology of the natural tirelessly obscures: that there is no such thing as unmediated reproduction. Every birth, from the Paleolithic era to the present, has been assisted—by midwives, by knowledge systems, by tools, and by social arrangements. The essay’s masterstroke is to take Shulamith Firestone’s radical feminism and show, through intimate reporting, that her vision was never a techno‑utopian pipe dream but a brutally realistic assessment of what it would take to free human beings from the tyranny of reproductive biology. I want to push the argument one step further. The project is not just to make assisted reproduction universal; it is to so thoroughly denaturalize reproduction that the very distinction between the natural and the artificial ceases to carry any normative weight whatsoever.

Firestone, in *The Dialectic of Sex*, imagined a world in which gestation could be externalized, liberating women from the “barbaric” experience of pregnancy. The Boston Review essay implicitly softens this vision, focusing on incremental technologies—egg freezing, IUI, IVF—that still, in the end, rely on a uterus and a birth. But the logic of its argument points toward the full‑blown artificial womb, a technology that remains scientifically distant but philosophically explosive. We should not shy away from that horizon. If we take seriously the claim that all reproduction is assisted, then the uterus is just one more piece of equipment—remarkably elegant, but also prone to failure, capable of causing pain and death, and distributed by the lottery of birth. There is no moral reason to prefer it over a well‑designed artificial placenta except a lingering attachment to the romance of “the maternal body.” That romance, as the essay notes, has been weaponized for millennia to convince women that their suffering is sacred. The woman who prefers a traditional gestation is entitled to that choice, but she is no more a “real” mother than the parent whose embryo develops outside the body.

The essay’s narratives show people caught in a painful gap between their bodily realities and their inherited ideas about normal reproduction. S, surrounded by silent pressure to “just have the baby,” internalizes the stigma that taints her frozen eggs. B, after a miscarriage, wonders how much grief she is permitted to voice when the pregnancy was so technologically mediated. Their distress is a direct consequence of the nature/culture binary: they have absorbed the lie that true motherhood begins in a spontaneous, unsullied act of heterosexual love, and so their own technologically brokered pregnancies feel like counterfeit. The feminist task is not to reassure them that their pregnancies are just as valid “in their own way”; it is to annihilate the category of validity itself. When all reproduction is assisted, no one gets to stand on the high ground of the natural. There are only more or less effective, more or less desired, more or less justly distributed ways of bringing new people into existence.

This insight has profound implications for how we think about sex and gender. Donna Haraway’s cyborg manifesto declared that we are all chimeras, theorized and fabricated hybrids. In a cyborg world, the fact that some of us gestate and some do not becomes a fact about our technological assemblages rather than an essence. The dream of Firestone and Haraway is not gender equality inside the existing system; it is the dissolution of the sex/gender system itself. We should be aiming for a society in which the biological division of reproductive labor matters no more to life outcomes than hair color. That requires not only technological innovation, but a massive cultural shift, one that the essay’s focus on personal narratives can only partially accomplish. Stories humanize, but they can also sentimentalize. We need a language of bold, speculative ambition.

Of course, technological access without structural transformation is a cruel joke, as another response in this forum likely notes. The point is not to celebrate corporate egg‑freezing perks or to pretend that current ART is a democratic good. But the structural argument cuts both ways. If we wait to demand full ex‑utero gestation until after we have achieved universal healthcare and paid family leave, we will be waiting forever. In the meantime, the work of pregnancy will continue to fall disproportionately on people who are already disadvantaged. Radical feminism’s insight was that the biological family is the primary engine of patriarchy, and that tinkering with its economic supports will never be enough to stop the machine. We need to imagine technologies that don’t just assist reproduction inside the existing family form, but that make new forms of kinship thinkable. What would it mean to gestate a child in a community‑owned artificial womb, shared among a collective of co‑parents of all genders? What if ectogenesis made it possible for an elderly grandparent and a teenager to generate a sibling without any single person bearing the bodily cost? These questions sound outlandish only because we are still trapped by the natural.

The fierce responses to Joanne Spataro’s essay, described in the lead piece, are telling. Commentators were outraged not simply by the trans‑ness of the couple but by the deep ordinariness of their reproductive desire: they wanted a biological child, the “mixture of us two in human form.” That desire, the essay suggests, is a “fantasy,” but it is a fantasy that many share. The political task is not to shame people for wanting genetic connection; it is to so proliferate the ways of making that connection—and of making family without it—that the genetic link loses its talismanic power. When everyone has access to some form of assisted reproduction, the pride of the natural withers into a quaint personal aesthetic, like growing your own vegetables when the grocery store is just around the corner. Some will still do it, and good for them. But it will no longer be a source of moral hierarchy.

Firestone’s mistake was not her techno‑utopianism but her impatience. She wanted the artificial womb in 1970, and it didn’t come. It still hasn’t. But every major technological leap—from the Pill to IVF—has been met with the same chorus of natural‑law arguments, the same predictions of dystopian dehumanization. And each time, the technology has been absorbed into everyday life, creating new possibilities for human flourishing while generating new inequities. The task for feminism now is not to slow down the technological future in the name of caution, but to seize hold of it, collectively and demandingly, so that it serves the project of liberation rather than corporate profit. The natural is a seductive retreat, but it is, as the lead essay shows, a house built on sand. Let’s be brave enough to leave it behind.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The uterus should be viewed as a piece of biological equipment with no inherent moral or maternal superiority over artificial technologies like ectogenesis.
  2. 02The psychological distress experienced by those using reproductive technologies is a direct result of a harmful 'nature/culture' binary that stigmatizes non-traditional paths to parenthood.
  3. 03True reproductive justice requires the dissolution of the sex/gender system so that biological reproductive labor no longer dictates social roles or life outcomes.
  4. 04Technological advancement must be pursued alongside structural social change to prevent reproductive tools from merely serving corporate interests or reinforcing patriarchal family forms.
  5. 05Proliferating diverse methods of assisted reproduction will eventually strip genetic and 'natural' connections of their talismanic power and moral hierarchy.
