"The responding essay argues that the lead essay's intersectional critique of the War on Terror is intellectually flawed because it falsely equates specific, threat-based national security measures with domestic racial prejudice."

In “Islam on Trial,” Amna A. Akbar and Jeanne Theoharis attempt to weave a grand, intersectional tapestry of oppression, linking the fight against radical Islamic terrorism to everything from Black Lives Matter to mass incarceration to the War on Drugs. This approach is highly representative of a contemporary academic leftism that seeks to flatten complex, distinct geopolitical and social challenges into a single, uniform narrative of structural white supremacy and state violence. While their critique of the Trump administration’s travel ban is timely, their broader attempt to equate the ideological drivers of global jihadism with domestic American racial disparities is intellectually bankrupt and dangerously minimizes the specific, theocratic nature of the threat the West faces.

The authors’ third point—that we must stop “separating national security policy from criminal and immigration law, deportation, and mass incarceration”—is the most glaring example of this ideological overreach. They argue that the tools in service of the War on Drugs and the War on Terror coincide, suggesting that the conflation of “Muslims and terrorism” is exactly like the conflation of “blackness and criminality.” This is a profound category error.

The disparities in the domestic criminal justice system are rooted in centuries of specific American history: slavery, Jim Crow, redlining, and the systemic economic disenfranchisement of African Americans. The War on Drugs, for all its catastrophic failures, was primarily a misguided domestic policy aimed at eradicating narcotics.

In stark contrast, the national security state’s focus on Muslim communities post-9/11 is not a product of historical American racial animus. It is a direct, threat-based response to a specific, globally organized, and explicitly stated theocratic ideology. Al Qaeda and the Islamic State did not attack New York, Paris, San Bernardino, Orlando, and Nice because of American structural racism. They committed these atrocities because they adhere to a fundamentalist, totalitarian interpretation of Islam that mandates the subjugation of infidels, the execution of homosexuals, and the establishment of a global caliphate. To pretend that law enforcement’s focus on the demographics susceptible to this specific recruitment is akin to racist policing in Ferguson is to willfully ignore the reality of global jihadism.

When the authors complain that the state mobilizes a "dichotomy between acceptable and radical Islam," one must ask: what is the alternative? Should the state *not* distinguish between the millions of peaceful, law-abiding Muslim Americans and the radicalized individuals who wish to detonate pressure-cooker bombs at the Boston Marathon? The ability to discern this difference is not a form of prejudice; it is the fundamental requirement of an effective intelligence apparatus. The authors seem to suggest that any scrutiny of ideology is inherently illegitimate. But ideology matters. Belief systems drive actions. When a specific global movement is actively recruiting individuals to commit mass murder, it is the height of absurdity to demand that the government ignore the ideological and demographic contours of that movement in the name of intersectional equity.

Furthermore, Akbar and Theoharis attack the “material support ban,” arguing that it criminalizes association and charitable giving. They fail to acknowledge the fungible nature of terrorism financing. Organizations like Hamas and Hezbollah deliberately obscure their military wings behind networks of social services and charities. If the United States cannot prosecute the financial networks that keep these organizations solvent, it cannot effectively combat them. The material support statute is not a tool of random harassment; it is a legally tested mechanism designed to starve terrorist organizations of the resources they need to operate. The fact that it occasionally ensnares individuals whose intentions may have been naive rather than malicious is an unfortunate byproduct of a necessary law, but it does not render the law inherently unjust.

The authors also fall back on the tired trope that American counterterrorism is entirely a product of the United States’ own making—that if we simply stopped our military engagements abroad, the threat of terrorism would vanish. They cite the 23,000 bombs dropped by the U.S. in 2015 as evidence of American malfeasance. But context matters. The vast majority of those munitions were dropped in Iraq and Syria to halt the advance of ISIS, a genocidal death cult that was engaged in the mass slaughter of Yazidis, Christians, and fellow Muslims. To frame the military campaign against ISIS as an act of arbitrary American aggression that unjustly pressures American Muslims is to engage in profound moral inversion. The United States military, for all its flaws and historical errors, was acting in concert with a global coalition to stop a horrific humanitarian catastrophe.

None of this is to defend the crude, demagogic rhetoric of Donald Trump. His campaign promises regarding Muslims were inflammatory, and his initial execution of the travel ban was chaotic and seemingly driven by animus rather than strategic logic. The authors are correct to point out that the ban harms innocent refugees. But opposing Trump’s excesses does not require us to adopt the radical left’s premise that the entire War on Terror is an illegitimate, racist enterprise.

We can fiercely protect the civil rights of Muslim Americans and speak out against genuine bigotry without blinding ourselves to the distinct and specific nature of radical Islamic terrorism. The attempt by Akbar and Theoharis to merge the fight against terrorism into a broader crusade against American law enforcement, border enforcement, and foreign policy is a rhetorical sleight of hand. It obscures the real challenges of national security and asks us to dismantle the very tools that have prevented another 9/11 in the name of an ill-fitting, academic theory of intersectionality. We must reject this ideological flattening and maintain a clear-eyed view of the unique threats of the twenty-first century.
