Diversified Claude

"The United States should return to the strategy of selective engagement rather than adopting offshore balancing, as maintaining a forward military presence is essential for credible alliances and a stable international order."

Stephen Walt has written a stimulating essay and I am grateful to have the chance to respond to it. We agree on much; we disagree on framework. The framework I want to defend is what Walt calls "selective engagement" and dismisses as having been "not selective enough." I think the strategy is broadly correct, and that Walt's preferred alternative — offshore balancing — would surrender real benefits that selective engagement has secured.

Walt's case against selective engagement, narrowly, comes down to four charges: it kept too many troops in Europe after the Soviet collapse; it adopted "dual containment" in the Gulf, which contributed to the rise of al Qaeda; it failed to settle the Israeli-Palestinian conflict; and it bred resentment by trumpeting American indispensability. These are real complaints, but I think Walt overstates them and understates what selective engagement accomplished.

What did the 1990s selective-engagement strategy actually do? It managed German reunification without provoking Russian backlash or French sabotage. It enlarged NATO to include Poland, Hungary, and the Czech Republic, locking in the democratic transitions of Central Europe at modest cost. It assembled and held together a thirty-four-nation coalition to evict Iraq from Kuwait, restoring the principle that aggression does not pay. It built APEC and the WTO as architectures for managing economic globalization. It ended the war in Bosnia after the European Union conspicuously failed to do so. It conducted the Kosovo intervention without a single American combat fatality. It expanded ties with India after fifty years of frost. It managed the 1997 Asian financial crisis, with errors but without systemic collapse. It oversaw the longest economic expansion in American history, in part by maintaining the open international economic order on which that expansion depended.

This is a record of historic accomplishment. It is the record produced by exactly the strategy Walt finds insufficiently selective. The American troops in Europe were not a strategic burden; they were the visible commitment that made NATO enlargement credible, that reassured Russia that the alliance was not directed at it (a reassurance that has eroded in recent years, regrettably), and that allowed the Bosnia and Kosovo interventions to be possible. The cost was real but modest — and the benefit was the consolidation of a peaceful, democratic, prosperous Europe, an outcome American statesmen labored toward for half a century.

Walt's preferred alternative — drastically reducing the European presence, leaving regional balancing to local actors — would have made none of this possible. NATO enlargement, in particular, required sustained American leadership and the credibility of American commitment. Without it, the Central European democracies would have remained in a geopolitical gray zone, more vulnerable to internal anti-democratic backsliding and external pressure from a recovering Russia. The Balkan wars would have continued. Russian behavior toward its near abroad would have been more aggressive earlier. These are not small things. They are the actual successes of an actual American grand strategy, and Walt does not engage them.

The Gulf case is harder, and I will grant Walt more here. "Dual containment" was a flawed policy, and the basing of American forces in Saudi Arabia did contribute to bin Laden's radicalization. But the alternatives at the time were not obvious. Removing Saddam in 1991 would have produced approximately the situation we now face after 2003, with arguably less international support. Letting Saddam roll over Saudi Arabia or reabsorb Kuwait was not an option. The dual containment policy was a holding pattern that bought time at real costs, and the costs included al Qaeda. Walt is right that the costs were higher than recognized. He has not shown that available alternatives would have been better.

On Israel-Palestine, Walt's critique of the 1990s is largely directed at the second-term Clinton administration's energetic but ultimately unsuccessful efforts to broker a final settlement. He says these efforts were insufficient. But it is worth recalling that Clinton brought the parties closer to a deal than any American president before or since, that he laid down a set of parameters that remain the rough outline of any future settlement, and that he did so against the determined opposition of Likud, Hamas, and significant portions of his own foreign-policy bureaucracy. The failure of Camp David was not a failure of insufficient American effort. It was a failure of will and timing on the part of the actual parties — and primarily, in my view, on the Palestinian side, though Israel bears responsibility too. Walt's framework does not adequately reckon with the structural difficulty of imposing peace from outside on parties not yet ready to make peace.

The deeper disagreement between Walt's framework and selective engagement is over the value of forward presence. Walt sees presence as costly, provocative, and largely substitutable by reliance on local balancing. I see presence as the foundation of credible commitment, which in turn is the foundation of stable alliances, which in turn is the foundation of the international order that has been broadly favorable to American interests for sixty years. The strongest evidence for the value of presence is its absence: in regions where the United States has not been forward-deployed, we have repeatedly faced more costly crises later. The Korean War broke out in part because the United States had publicly excluded Korea from its defense perimeter. The Falklands War occurred in part because Britain had withdrawn its visible commitment to South Atlantic defense. Kuwait was invaded in part because Saddam read American signals as indifference. Forward presence is preventive in a way that "we will balance from offshore if things get bad enough" never quite achieves.

I agree with Walt on much of his policy program. The preemption doctrine should be retired. Nuclear hypocrisy should be addressed. Public diplomacy should be revived. Israeli-Palestinian engagement should be intensified. The "axis of evil" rhetoric was a mistake. But none of these reforms requires us to abandon the strategic posture that has served us well. They require us to execute that posture more wisely.

Walt's offshore balancing is presented as the prudent alternative to current administration recklessness. It is actually a more dramatic departure from American practice over the past sixty years than the current administration represents. Selective engagement, properly understood, is not the failure that produced the current moment; it is the success on which the current moment depends. Returning to its disciplines, rather than abandoning them for a new framework, is the right response to the present discontent.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The selective engagement strategy of the 1990s produced a record of historic accomplishments, including the peaceful reunification of Germany, the successful enlargement of NATO, and the management of global economic crises.
  2. 02A visible military presence in Europe was not a strategic burden but the necessary foundation for consolidating a democratic and prosperous continent.
  3. 03Offshore balancing fails to account for the fact that American leadership and credible commitments are required to prevent regional conflicts and anti-democratic backsliding in places like Central Europe.
  4. 04The failure to achieve an Israeli-Palestinian peace settlement in the 1990s was due to the lack of will among the local parties rather than a lack of American effort or a flawed strategic framework.
  5. 05Forward presence acts as a preventive measure against aggression, whereas the promise of offshore balancing often sends signals of indifference that can invite conflict.
  6. 06The current failures in American foreign policy are not inherent to selective engagement but are the result of poor execution, which can be remedied without abandoning a proven strategic posture.
