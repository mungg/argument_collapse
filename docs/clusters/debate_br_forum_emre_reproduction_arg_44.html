Diversified Gemini

"While Merve Emre correctly identifies that all reproduction is technologically and socially assisted, her focus on democratizing access to assisted reproductive technologies inadvertently reinforces a conservative, property-based obsession with genetic kinship at the expense of more radical, communal forms of family-making."

Merve Emre’s “All Reproduction Is Assisted” is a vital intervention in the contemporary feminist discourse surrounding childbirth, family planning, and technology. By systematically dismantling the myth of "natural" reproduction, Emre exposes the ideological scaffolding that elevates certain forms of motherhood while pathologizing others. However, in her effort to validate the profound biological desires of those excluded from the "natural" paradigm, Emre unintentionally reinforces a different kind of essentialism: the primacy of genetic kinship. If we are to radically reimagine reproductive politics, as Emre urges, we must ask why the drive to produce biological replicas of ourselves remains so sacrosanct, and why alternative modes of family-making—specifically adoption and communal care—are so quickly dismissed.

Emre’s essay is populated by individuals undergoing immense physical, emotional, and financial suffering to create biological children. S injects herself with hormones to freeze her eggs; B endures agonizing treatments and financial debt as a single woman; N and K navigate systemic homophobia and medical trauma; Spataro and cárdenas negotiate the complexities of gender transition and fertility preservation. Emre approaches their desires with deep empathy, explicitly refusing to judge the "irrational" fantasy of a biological child. But empathy should not preclude critique. We must interrogate the cultural forces that make the biological fantasy so powerful that individuals will endure profound suffering to achieve it.

When B considers adoption, she quickly abandons the idea after attending a foster parent training. She learns that the goal of foster care is family reunification, meaning she must guard against attachment. "B did not want her inaugural experience of parenting to be a year-long rehearsal for losing and letting go," Emre writes. This is entirely understandable on an individual level. The foster system in the United States is deeply broken, often weaponized against poor families and families of color. But by dropping the thread of adoption here, the essay tacitly accepts the premise that non-biological parenting is merely a substandard, emotionally risky backup plan to the "real" thing.

Similarly, N and K face friends asking, “Why don’t you just adopt?” N responds with understandable frustration: “People do not run up to pregnant women and ask, ‘Why didn’t you just adopt?’” N’s point is sharp—queer couples shouldn't be expected to shoulder the moral burden of adopting while straight couples reproduce biologically without question. Yet, the underlying question remains unanswered for *everyone*: why don't more people, straight or queer, adopt?

The answer lies in the very structures of hetero-patriarchy and capitalism that Shulamith Firestone sought to dismantle. The obsession with biological lineage is historically rooted in the transmission of private property and the patriarchal need to ensure genetic paternity. While IVF and donor sperm allow queer and single people to participate in this biological continuity, they do not inherently subvert the underlying logic that values genetic offspring over social kin. When Spataro dreams of a child that is "a mixture of us two in human form," she is expressing a deeply romanticized, culturally conditioned view of family that marginalizes the millions of existing children who need care.

Emre quotes Firestone’s vision of a future where artificial wombs liberate women, but Firestone's true radicalism lay not just in ectogenesis, but in her call to abolish the nuclear family entirely. Firestone argued that the biological family unit was inherently oppressive, and that children should be raised communally, free from the ownership models of biological parenting. Emre’s essay, conversely, focuses on democratizing *access* to the nuclear, biological family through technology. It seeks to bring marginalized people into the fold of biological reproduction, rather than questioning whether the fold itself is where we want to be.

We are writing in August 2018, at a moment of unprecedented global crisis. Climate change threatens the habitability of the planet, and tens of millions of refugees—many of them children—are displaced by war, poverty, and ecological collapse. In this context, the vast expenditure of medical resources, capital, and emotional labor to create *new* genetic life through ART, while millions of existing children suffer from a lack of care and resources, presents a profound ethical tension. This is not to say that regulating individual reproduction is the answer—coercive population control is historically rooted in racism and eugenics. Rather, it is to ask what a truly emancipatory feminist politics of care would look like.

A robust reproductive politics must encompass more than the right to not have children and the right to have biological children via technology. It must also encompass a radical restructuring of kinship. What if the massive investments currently funneled into the for-profit fertility industry were instead directed toward dismantling the punitive foster care system, supporting biological families torn apart by poverty and the carceral state, and normalizing expansive, non-biological forms of adoption and community care?

Emre writes, "The 'unnaturalness' of your endeavor becomes a proxy for your subjecthood." She is right that society unfairly punishes those who rely on ART. But we must push further. We must challenge the supremacy of the biological imperative itself. True solidarity will not be found merely in shared access to the clinic, but in a collective realization that family is not defined by shared DNA, but by shared commitment, labor, and love. Until we elevate social reproduction to the same sacred status as biological reproduction, our reproductive politics will remain fundamentally constrained.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02By dismissing adoption as an emotionally risky or substandard alternative, the response tacitly accepts the hetero-patriarchal premise that non-biological parenting is secondary to 'real' motherhood.
  3. 03The focus on expanding access to the biological nuclear family through technology ignores Shulamith Firestone’s more radical call to abolish the oppressive biological family unit entirely.
  4. 04In an era of global crisis and displacement, the massive expenditure of resources on creating new genetic life through the for-profit fertility industry presents an ethical tension that necessitates a shift toward supporting existing children and communal care.
  5. 05A truly emancipatory feminist politics must elevate social reproduction and non-biological kinship to the same sacred status as shared DNA.
