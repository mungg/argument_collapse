"The essay argues that choosing full-time motherhood is a noble and morally competent decision that should be respected as a pursuit of a meaningful life rather than dismissed as a regressive capitulation to patriarchy."

Reading Nancy Hirschmann’s essay, I felt a creeping sadness, not because her argument is wrong in every particular but because it so perfectly captures a certain kind of feminist despair. She looks at her young students, who say they want to be mothers above all else, and sees a regression, a failure to enlist in the march toward equality. She hears of mothers who cover for their children’s misconduct and sees only the corruption of care. What she cannot see, or will not credit, is that these women might be making a competent, even noble, judgment about how to live a meaningful life.

The impulse to protect one’s child from the full force of institutional punishment is not a perversion of care; it is the most elemental expression of it. When the mother of the plagiarizing student called Hirschmann to protest, she was doing what most good mothers would do: advocating for her son, however misguidedly. That she didn’t grasp the larger civic lesson about cheating matters, but Hirschmann’s contempt for her displays a failure of empathy much greater than the one she attributes to the care feminists. Love is particular; it is not the same as justice, and it never will be. The fact that it sometimes conflicts with abstract principles of fairness does not make it a lower form of morality. It may make it a higher one, for it demands the constant, grueling work of balancing the claims of those closest to us with the claims of the wider world.

Hirschmann’s students, who overwhelmingly plan to opt out of paid work to raise children, are not the dupes of a regressive culture. They are responding to something real: the experience of having been raised, in many cases, by mothers who worked long hours, and the sense that something was lost in that arrangement. Is it so irrational for them to want to give their own children a different kind of presence? The empirical evidence on the effects of maternal employment is mixed, but there is a solid body of work, from attachment theory onward, suggesting that a consistent, loving caregiver in the early years provides a foundation for psychological security that no high-quality daycare can replicate with the same intensity. This is not to say that women must be those caregivers, but Hirschmann herself concedes that men are not stepping up. Given that reality, a woman who decides to stay home is not necessarily capitulating to patriarchy; she may be making the best of a world that offers her no good solution, and choosing what she, and many reasonable observers, regard as the less damaging option.

To dismiss this as a false choice imposed by systemic inequality is to insult the moral agency of millions of women who find genuine fulfillment in mothering. Yes, the tax code and workplace structures punish dual earners, and yes, that shapes choices. But Hirschmann’s alternative—that women should enter the workforce as a kind of political obligation, to gain power and force men to change—reduces human life to a calculus of leverage. It treats women’s love for their children, and the joy they take in being with them, as something to be strategically rationed. That is a strikingly joyless vision. One can accept every one of Hirschmann’s structural critiques—the sexual division of labor must change, men must do more, public policy should be less hostile to dual-income families—and still believe that a society that makes room for a parent to stay home with young children, without economic penalty, is a better society than one that pushes every adult into the market.

The old feminist slogan “the personal is political” was meant to illuminate how individual lives are shaped by power, not to argue that every personal decision must be made with an eye to the political consequences. Hirschmann wants women to choose employment to send a message, to avoid being free riders on the feminist movement. But the women she criticizes are not free riders; they are living their lives, caring for the actual human beings in front of them. If that care is sometimes narrow or defensive, it is also the daily work of creating a decent life. The cure for its excesses is not to care less, but to care more wisely, to teach one’s children empathy and integrity alongside a robust sense of their own worth. That is a task a stay‑at‑home mother can do as well as anyone.

Hirschmann’s essay purports to be a tough-minded corrective, but in its eagerness to expose the dark side of care, it forgets why the care tradition arose in the first place: because the world of abstraction, justice, and market competition is not enough. Human beings need nurture, and someone has to provide it. Until feminists can honor that need without making women its exclusive servants, a feminism that asks women to care less will sound like a demand that they become less human.
