"Zittrain’s proposal to save the generative internet through mass user participation and community-based security is an idealistic vision that ignores the reality of human nature and the market's natural shift toward specialized, secure devices."

Jonathan Zittrain has written a deeply learned and sophisticated brief for saving the generative PC and Internet. He vividly describes the creeping abandonment of openness in favor of tethered, sterile appliances, and he proposes an array of ingenious patches—virtual dual-boot machines, community-based code vetting, legal nudges—to keep the patient alive. The diagnosis is acute, but the proposed cure asks too much of ordinary human nature and places too little faith in the messy, workable equilibrium that the market is already forging. Zittrain’s “third way” between anarchy and lockdown, however inspiring, is built on a sandbar of wishful thinking about mass participation.

The central problem is his reliance on community-generated security at the code layer. Zittrain invokes Wikipedia as the great success story of a generative system that policed its own vandalism through social norms and simple technical tools. But encyclopedia editing and software vetting are fundamentally different activities. Writing an article is a creative act that yields immediate, visible satisfaction and a sense of authorship. Judging whether a piece of code is malicious or merely a poorly written widget demands expertise that is far rarer and, crucially, far more tedious. I might happily correct a typo on the entry for “Pythagorean theorem”; I am not going to sift through a network capture to decide if a new tax-preparation add-on is a Trojan. Zittrain’s Herdict envisions “self-described experts” vouching for code, but sustaining a critical mass of expert volunteers in the face of a professionalized criminal adversary is a historical fluke, not a blueprint. Wikipedia succeeded because most contributions are small, positive edits; security requires constant, adversarial attention. The dynamics are closer to a neighborhood watch in a neighborhood where the burglars have university degrees in lock-picking.

Even the Red-Green virtual machine idea, elegant as it is, stumbles on the “Checkpoint Charlie” problem that Zittrain himself acknowledges. The Red PC is supposed to be the experimental sandbox, but users will inevitably generate valuable data there—the half-finished novel, the photos of a new grandchild—and will need to move it to the safe Green side. That transfer is precisely the moment attackers will target, and the “checkpoint” inevitably becomes a complex piece of vetting software. Worse, the user who cannot be trusted to choose safe software in the first place is not going to be able to adjudicate whether a particular file movement from Red to Green is harmless. The Red-Green split solves only the easy part: containing damage that never needs to be recovered.

Zittrain speaks movingly of “netizens” who will take ownership of the infrastructure. But the evidence of the past decade points in the opposite direction. The average home user, by his own admission in study after study, does not keep even first-order protections up to date. Asking that same user to participate in a distributed community-sourced antivirus collective is like asking a drowning man to join a swimming instruction committee. When faced with a confusing security alert, the universal reflex is to click “OK” or “Allow” to make the nuisance go away. Tools that rely on user judgment have already been exploited by malware authors who simply label their malicious executables “New Virus Sweeper” and watch the clicks roll in.

Zittrain’s implicit narrative is that we are on a precipice, that without his suite of interventions the generative internet will die. But the market is already sorting through the dilemma in a way that is not as catastrophic as he suggests. Consumers are indeed choosing iPhones, BlackBerrys, and Xbox 360s—not because they have been duped into forfeiting freedom, but because those devices do what they want reliably. And, crucially, the PC is not vanishing; it is becoming a distinct product for a distinct audience. Enthusiasts, programmers, and businesses that need genuine flexibility will continue to buy general-purpose computers and run open-source operating systems. The fact that my father’s next computer will likely be an iPad-like slab that does not let him tinker with the registry is not a tragedy; it is a specialization that frees him from the tyranny of system maintenance. Meanwhile, the experimentalist ethos survives in the labs, the gaming rigs, the Linux boxes, and the server rooms that actually drive the innovation Zittrain cherishes.

His proposed legal interventions betray an anxiety that the tethered appliance is the enemy. But portability policies and appliance neutrality, however well-meaning, are attempts to legislate a single open model onto devices that consumers freely choose for their closure. If I buy a TiVo knowing I cannot extract the video files, and I value its ease of use over that limitation, is it the law’s place to mandate that TiVo give me an export button? That is not consumer protection; it is an ideological preference for generativity masquerading as regulation.

None of this is to dismiss Zittrain’s profound historical account of the generative miracle. The Apple II was a wonder, and the open internet has liberated human creativity on an unprecedented scale. But the security crisis is real, and its solution lies not in persuading millions of amateurs to become amateur malware hunters, but in accepting a more modest, segmented digital ecology. A thriving generative subculture will persist at the edges, nurtured by expert communities and open-source foundations, while the broad consumer market flocks to safer, simpler, and yes, less generative devices. That is not the death of the Internet; it is the healthy differentiation of a maturing technology. The challenge is not to turn every Uber driver into a mechanic, but to ensure the roads stay open for those who still want to build and race their own cars.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Software vetting and security maintenance are too technically demanding and tedious to be successfully crowdsourced in the same way as creative content like Wikipedia.
  2. 02Technical solutions like the Red-Green virtual machine fail because users lack the judgment to safely manage the transfer of data between experimental and secure environments.
  3. 03The average user has consistently demonstrated a preference for convenience and automated safety over the responsibility of active system maintenance and 'netizenship.'
  4. 04The rise of locked-down appliances is a positive market differentiation that provides reliability for general consumers while leaving generative platforms available for the enthusiasts and experts who actually drive innovation.
  5. 05Proposed legal mandates for appliance neutrality and data portability represent an ideological preference for openness that overrides the valid consumer choice for closed, stable systems.
