"The purpose of Lent is to provide a collective, time-bound, and embodied structure for discipline that satisfies a deep human need for shared ritual and restraint."

What I find most interesting about Lent is how stubbornly it has survived in a culture that has supposedly outgrown such things. Most of the old liturgical calendar has faded from public consciousness — Ember Days, Rogation, Septuagesima — but Lent persists, and not only among the devout. Why?

My hypothesis is that Lent answers a need that secular modernity has not figured out how to meet on its own: the need for collective, time-bound, embodied discipline. We are very good at telling individuals to improve themselves, but very bad at offering them a shared framework in which to do it. Lent provides what the self-help industry cannot: a fixed start (Ash Wednesday), a fixed end (Easter), a community of people doing it with you, and a set of practices handed down rather than invented. Émile Durkheim would have recognized exactly what was going on.

This is why I think the question "is there a point in giving something up?" slightly misses the mark. The point is not really the thing given up. The point is the structure — the fact that on a specific Wednesday, millions of people simultaneously agree to take on a small, deliberate constraint, and that on a specific Sunday they will collectively release it. That rhythm of restraint and feast, lack and abundance, is something older than Christianity and probably older than agriculture. Christianity baptized it; secular culture has nothing comparable.

For believers, of course, the structure carries the substance: Lent prepares the heart for the death and resurrection of Christ. But even for the nonbeliever who participates, the form does its quiet work. You learn that your wants are not your needs. You learn that you can wait. You learn that there are seasons. In a culture organized around the instantaneous and the individual, this may be the most countercultural thing Lent has left to offer.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Lent offers a communal framework for self-improvement that the individualistic self-help industry cannot replicate.
  2. 02The value of Lent lies in its fixed temporal structure and the rhythm of restraint and feast rather than the specific item being sacrificed.
  3. 03Lent serves as a countercultural tool that teaches individuals to distinguish between wants and needs in a society driven by instant gratification.
  4. 04The tradition persists because it provides a sense of shared discipline and inherited practice that secular modernity has failed to replace.
