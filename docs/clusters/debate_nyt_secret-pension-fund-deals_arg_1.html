"Public pension funds must be subject to rigorous transparency and disclosure requirements because they manage taxpayer-guaranteed funds and deferred wages that are vulnerable to excessive fees and political corruption."

Yes. Public pension funds are not private endowments gambling with donated money; they are stewards of deferred wages and taxpayer guarantees. When a state promises teachers, firefighters and clerks a retirement benefit, the public has a right to know whether the people managing that promise are paying Wall Street handsomely for genuine value or simply buying complexity, opacity and political access.

That does not mean every trade in a hedge fund portfolio must be posted in real time. There are legitimate reasons to protect specific trading strategies, pending transactions or proprietary models. But those limited concerns have too often been stretched into blanket secrecy over contracts, fee arrangements, placement agents, side letters and performance benchmarks. Those are not trade secrets in any meaningful civic sense. They are the basic facts needed to judge whether public officials are doing their jobs.

The move into alternative investments has made this especially urgent. Hedge funds, private equity funds and real estate partnerships typically charge much higher fees than index funds or traditional bond managers. Their reported returns can be difficult to compare, their risks can be hidden by illiquidity, and their contracts often give managers discretion that ordinary investors would never accept without careful scrutiny. If a pension board cannot explain, in public, what it is paying, what risks it is taking and why the expected reward justifies both, it probably should not be making the investment.

The corruption risk is also real. Public pension systems control enormous pools of capital, and that attracts politically connected placement agents, campaign donors and former officials. The pay-to-play scandals involving pension investments in New York and elsewhere showed how easily retirement money can become a prize in a patronage system. Disclosure is not a cure-all, but secrecy is an invitation.

A sensible transparency regime would require publication of all investment contracts with narrow redactions, full disclosure of management fees, carried interest and other expenses, identification of placement agents and political contributions, regular performance reporting against appropriate benchmarks, and strong conflict-of-interest rules for trustees and consultants. Pension boards should also have independent expertise, not merely rely on consultants who may have their own industry ties.

Public workers deserve secure pensions. Taxpayers deserve honest accounting. Neither interest is served by letting complex, expensive deals disappear behind confidentiality clauses written by the very firms seeking public money.

Vanilla Claude

"Public pension funds must adopt a policy of radical transparency regarding their alternative investments to ensure accountability to beneficiaries and taxpayers."

Public pension funds manage trillions of dollars on behalf of teachers, firefighters, and other public servants. Beneficiaries and the taxpayers who backstop these plans have an unambiguous right to know how that money is invested, what it costs, and who is profiting from the arrangements. The case for radical transparency is overwhelming, and the standard objections to it do not survive scrutiny.

The usual defense of secrecy is that hedge funds, private equity firms, and other "alternative" managers demand confidentiality to protect proprietary strategies, and that pensions will be excluded from the best deals if they disclose terms. But the recent reporting by Pando and others, building on earlier work by the SEC's Andrew Bowden — who in a notable May speech described widespread fee abuse in private equity — suggests the opposite problem. Secrecy is not protecting trade secrets; it is concealing 2-and-20 fee structures, undisclosed monitoring fees, side letters favoring connected investors, and performance that, net of fees, often trails a plain index. CalPERS' own research has questioned whether hedge fund allocations earn their keep. If a manager truly cannot bear sunlight on basic terms, a fiduciary should ask why.

There is also a democratic dimension. State treasurers and pension trustees are often elected or politically appointed, and the flow of campaign contributions from placement agents and fund principals to those officials — documented in the New York and New Mexico pay-to-play scandals — shows how confidentiality clauses can shade into corruption. SEC pay-to-play Rule 206(4)-5 was a start, but enforcement remains thin.

A reasonable disclosure regime would require publication of every fund's fees, carried interest terms, net-of-fee returns, and the identity of any placement agents and their compensation. Genuinely proprietary positions can be reported on a lag. None of this prevents pensions from investing in alternatives; it merely forces them to do so on terms they can defend in daylight. Public money demands public accounting.

Real accountability requires that investment contracts—including the true cost of fees and the identities of all intermediaries—be open records, not proprietary secrets.

Transparency is not merely a safeguard against corruption; it is the foundation for preserving the social compact that underpins defined-benefit pensions in the first place. The multibillion-dollar state pension industry must choose: honor its public mission with open books, or continue to erode confidence one secret deal at a time.

The case for transparency in public pension management is compelling, and the tension between legitimate confidentiality and democratic accountability must resolve firmly on the side of openness. The shift toward alternative investments like hedge funds reflects genuine pressures: traditional fixed-income returns proved insufficient as pension obligations ballooned. But this shift created new vulnerabilities. Confidentiality agreements, while perhaps necessary to access certain investment opportunities, simultaneously shield arrangements that taxpayers and beneficiaries have every right to scrutinize. High fees erode returns quietly. Politically connected managers may receive preferential treatment regardless of performance. Without sunlight, governance failures fester.

Meaningful reform does not require abandoning alternative investments entirely. It does require standardizing fee disclosure, requiring explanation of material conflicts, and establishing clear criteria for why certain investments demand confidentiality. Even where legitimate competitive concerns exist—trading strategies that would be undercut if public—regulators can impose tiered disclosure that protects genuine intellectual property while still enabling oversight.

State pension systems manage assets that will determine whether teachers, firefighters, and clerical workers retire with dignity or poverty. That consequence demands transparency, not as an administrative preference but as a constitutional imperative. Secrecy may be comfortable for fund managers; it is dangerous for everyone else.

Diversified GPT

"Public pension funds should adopt a smart transparency regime that balances the need for public accountability and political integrity with the protection of legitimate investment strategies."

The demand for transparency in public pension investing is justified, but it needs to be aimed carefully. A rule that simply says “disclose everything” could make pension funds worse investors without making them cleaner institutions. The task is to separate secrecy that protects legitimate investment activity from secrecy that conceals fees, conflicts and bad judgment.

Public pension systems increasingly invest in alternatives because their liabilities are long term and their return targets are ambitious. In a world of low bond yields, a portfolio made only of Treasury bonds and index funds would force either much higher contributions or lower promised benefits. Private equity, real estate, infrastructure and some hedge fund strategies can play a role in diversification. The problem is not the existence of alternatives. The problem is that their complexity can overwhelm public oversight.

A pension board may be part-time. Trustees may include political appointees, union representatives and public officials who are not investment specialists. Consultants may recommend products while maintaining relationships with managers. Fund documents may be written in ways that obscure the true economic cost. A “2 and 20” fee structure is already expensive; hidden transaction fees, monitoring fees and fund-of-funds layers can make it worse. If the reported return is not clearly net of all costs, the public cannot judge whether the investment worked.

Still, there are limits to useful disclosure. Releasing detailed portfolio company information in private equity, or current hedge fund positions, may harm the fund by allowing competitors to trade against it or by causing high-quality managers to exclude public plans. The point of transparency is not voyeurism. It is accountability.

A sensible transparency regime would focus on decision-making, economics and conflicts. Every public pension plan should disclose its asset allocation, manager names, amounts committed, total fees paid, net performance, benchmarks used and the identity of consultants and placement agents. Contracts should be public except for narrowly defined proprietary information, and redactions should be reviewed independently rather than left to the manager’s preference. Trustees should receive full fee and risk reports, and those reports should be summarized for the public in plain language.

Political safeguards are equally important. Campaign contributions by firms seeking pension business should be disclosed and, in some cases, restricted. Placement agents should be registered and their compensation reported. Revolving-door rules should prevent public officials from steering money to firms and then joining them. Pension boards should adopt written investment beliefs and should explain why illiquid, high-fee products are appropriate for their liabilities.

The answer, then, is yes: the industry should be more transparent and less prone to influence. But the goal should be smart transparency, not performative transparency. Public funds must be able to invest effectively. They must also remember whose money they are investing. When those two principles conflict, the burden should be on secrecy to justify itself.

The current debate risks confusing two different questions: whether public pension funds should be honest, and whether hedge funds and private equity funds should be forced to operate like public agencies. The answer to the first is plainly yes. The answer to the second is no.

Public pensions are in trouble mainly because of political choices, not because investment contracts contain confidentiality clauses. Governments skipped contributions, enhanced benefits without fully funding them, and relied on optimistic return assumptions. Then, after the financial crisis, many plans faced enormous gaps. It is tempting to blame “Wall Street secrecy” for this mess, but that explanation is too convenient. The biggest drivers of pension stress are liabilities and contributions. Even a perfectly transparent investment book would not make an underfunded plan solvent.

Alternative investments also should not be caricatured. A large pension fund has a long time horizon and needs exposure to more than ordinary bonds. Private equity, real estate partnerships, distressed debt and hedge funds can provide diversification, inflation protection or access to opportunities unavailable in public markets. Some strategies are expensive and disappointing; others have delivered strong net returns for institutional investors. The right question is not whether an investment sounds exotic, but whether it improves the portfolio after fees and risk.

Confidentiality is part of that market. Managers may not want competitors to see their holdings, valuations, acquisition targets or financing terms. Private companies may not want sensitive operating information released because a public pension investor is in the fund. If disclosure rules become too sweeping, the best managers may simply refuse public money. That would not punish Wall Street; it would punish retirees by limiting their investment options.

That said, confidentiality should not become a cloak for corruption. The public should know who is being hired, how much money is committed, what broad strategy is being pursued, what fees are paid, and whether political intermediaries are involved. Placement agents, campaign contributions and side payments deserve aggressive scrutiny. Trustees should be held responsible for understanding net performance and risk. If a board cannot explain why it invested in a fund, that is a governance failure.

But transparency alone is not a pension policy. It can even become a distraction if it lets legislatures avoid harder decisions. States and cities must make actuarially required contributions, use realistic assumptions, and negotiate benefits honestly. They must professionalize pension boards and reduce political meddling. A public website full of contracts will not substitute for funding discipline.

So yes, there should be more disclosure of fees, conflicts and performance. But we should be careful about treating secrecy as the central villain. Public pension funds need both accountability and access to sophisticated investments. The challenge is to police corruption without imposing rules that make these funds less capable of meeting their promises.

"Mandating total transparency for public pension funds is counterproductive because it drives away top-tier investment managers and undermines the fiduciary duty to maximize returns for beneficiaries."

Transparency Is a Slogan, Not a Strategy

Everyone is in favor of transparency in the abstract, in the same way everyone is in favor of efficiency and good government. But the current campaign against "secret pension deals," driven largely by activists who oppose alternative investments on ideological grounds, conflates several distinct issues and proposes a cure that may be worse than the disease.

Start with the basic economics. Most large public pension funds are underfunded, and they carry assumed rates of return — typically 7.5 to 8 percent — that cannot plausibly be hit with a traditional 60/40 portfolio in an era when investment-grade bonds yield 2 to 3 percent. Trustees have a fiduciary duty to try to close that gap. That means moving up the risk curve, and it means accessing asset classes — private equity, real estate, distressed credit, certain hedge fund strategies — where the best performers do not need or want public capital and will simply refuse to take it on terms that broadcast their positions to competitors.

The confidentiality demanded by these managers is not a sinister cover-up; it is a commercial reality. A private equity firm that publishes its quarterly portfolio company valuations hands a roadmap to competitors and counterparties. A hedge fund that discloses its positions in real time invites front-running. When state legislatures have tried to mandate granular disclosure — Texas and California have wrestled with this — top-quartile managers have responded by capping or excluding public investors. The result: state pensions end up stuck with the second-tier funds that will accept the disclosure terms, which is precisely the opposite of what beneficiaries need.

This does not mean anything goes. There is a meaningful distinction between disclosure to the public and disclosure to the right oversight bodies. Fees paid, aggregate returns, the identity of managers, conflicts of interest involving trustees, and the use of placement agents should all be public. Position-level holdings and proprietary terms in side letters generally should not be. Pay-to-play abuses, where they occur, are already illegal under SEC Rule 206(4)-5 and state ethics laws; the answer is enforcement, not blanket disclosure regimes that punish legitimate managers along with the bad actors.

The Pando reporting has done a service by raising questions, but it has also tended to paint with too broad a brush, treating any alternative investment as suspect by definition. The real debate is not transparency versus secrecy. It is how to design oversight regimes that catch corruption without crippling the ability of pension trustees to do the job their beneficiaries are counting on them to do.

  4. 04Effective oversight should focus on targeted disclosures to regulatory bodies and the enforcement of existing pay-to-play laws rather than blanket public disclosure of sensitive investment data.

The Problem Isn't Secrecy. It's the Promises.

The transparency debate around public pension investments, while not unimportant, is a sideshow that lets the real culprits off the hook. State and local pension funds did not start chasing hedge funds and private equity because trustees suddenly developed exotic tastes. They did it because legislators and governors, over decades, promised retirement benefits they had no intention of funding honestly, and assumed rates of return high enough to paper over the gap on paper.

Look at the arithmetic. Across the fifty states, the unfunded liability is estimated at somewhere between $1 trillion (using the funds' own assumptions) and $3-plus trillion (using risk-free discount rates favored by financial economists). New Jersey has skipped or shorted its pension contributions almost every year since the Whitman administration. Illinois has effectively run its pension system as a credit card for general operating expenses. Kentucky, Connecticut, and Rhode Island are not far behind. When you are this far underwater and you cannot raise taxes or cut benefits politically, the only remaining lever is to bet the portfolio. Hence the rush into "alternatives."

Once you understand that, the transparency conversation looks different. Yes, the secrecy around alternative investment terms hides excessive fees, mediocre returns, and sometimes outright corruption. All of those should be exposed. But even if every fee were posted on a public website tomorrow, the underlying problem — that pensions are structurally insolvent and management has been pushed into Hail Mary investing — would remain. You can disclose your way to a fairer pricing of hedge fund services. You cannot disclose your way out of a $1 trillion funding hole.

There is also a perverse dynamic at work. Greater transparency, by exposing how lousy a deal alternatives often are, will likely accelerate calls to abandon those strategies. Fine. But then trustees still have to hit 7.5 percent somehow. If they retreat to bonds, the funding gap widens immediately on an actuarial basis, forcing larger contributions from strapped state budgets or, more likely, benefit cuts for retirees. The political class would much rather have an opaque hedge fund to blame than admit the math.

So by all means, end the gag orders, ban placement agent payola, publish the fees. But understand that transparency is a hygiene measure, not a solution. The real reform agenda is honest discount rates, ironclad funding requirements that politicians cannot skip, and an adult conversation with public workers about what their plans can actually deliver. Until that happens, we will keep arguing about the wrapping paper while the package rots.

The answer is tiered transparency. Trustees, internal staff, outside auditors and regulators should have full access to contracts, side letters, valuation methods and fee schedules.
