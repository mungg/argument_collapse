"Universities must reconcile the right to protest with civil rights obligations by replacing subjective, donor-driven 'safety' standards with objective, conduct-based criteria for discriminatory harassment."

The most urgent question raised by Alex Gourevitch’s essay is not whether the First Amendment protects offensive protest. In many public university contexts, it does. Nor is it whether private universities should honor similar commitments. They should. The more difficult question is how to reconcile protest rights with civil rights obligations without turning either into a weapon against the other.

Gourevitch’s answer is to exclude protest, or nearly exclude it, from the hostile environment framework. He argues that protest is a distinctive form of political expression: public, hostile, accusatory, and often uncomfortable. If subjective feelings of fear or hurt are enough to trigger antidiscrimination intervention, protest becomes impossible. This is a powerful warning, especially in light of federal pressure on universities and the use of antisemitism allegations to suppress pro-Palestinian advocacy.

But the relationship between hostile environment law and protest cannot be resolved quite so cleanly. A hostile environment is not established merely because someone feels unsafe. On that Gourevitch is plainly right. But neither is it limited to imminent threats or individualized harassment. Civil rights law has long recognized that discriminatory exclusion can arise from a pattern of conduct that is severe or pervasive enough to deny equal access. The conduct may include words, symbols, and repeated public acts. Context matters.

That means two errors must be avoided.

The first error, now widespread, is to treat political protest against Israel as presumptively discriminatory against Jews or Israelis. That move is legally and morally disastrous. It chills debate about a foreign state, U.S. policy, war, occupation, human rights, and university investments. It also ignores the diversity of Jewish opinion. Many Jews oppose Israel’s war in Gaza; some oppose Zionism; many support protest demands; many others find those views painful or wrong. Universities cannot define Jewish identity through the politics of the most institutionally powerful Jewish organizations.

The second error is to imagine that protest can never create or contribute to a discriminatory environment. Suppose a campus protest movement repeatedly follows individual Jewish students, calls them “baby killers,” blocks them from entering buildings unless they denounce Israel, vandalizes Jewish spaces, and circulates lists of “Zionists” to be shunned. The fact that the movement frames its conduct as antiwar protest would not immunize it. Similarly, pro-Israel students who target Palestinian classmates as terrorists, celebrate mass death in Gaza, or harass Muslim student groups cannot hide behind political expression.

The distinction must turn on conduct, targeting, repetition, and access—not on the emotional valence of the political message.

This distinction is what many universities failed to maintain. Under pressure from donors, lawsuits, Congress, and now the Trump administration, they have often substituted institutional risk management for legal judgment. Rather than asking whether particular students were denied equal access through severe, pervasive, objectively offensive conduct, they asked whether influential constituencies perceived the campus as unsafe. That is not civil rights enforcement. It is reputational triage.

The examples Gourevitch quotes from the Harvard Jewish Alumni Alliance report illustrate the problem. A student cannot focus because people in keffiyehs are nearby writing an op-ed. Another uses a side entrance to avoid a protest. Another wakes up afraid on hearing chants. These are real experiences of distress. They are not, without more, evidence of unlawful harassment. If they became sufficient to discipline protesters, then almost any passionate demonstration by or against any identity-linked cause could be suppressed.

The asymmetry is striking. Muslim and Palestinian students have also reported high levels of fear, self-censorship, and alienation. Many have watched universities swiftly condemn antisemitism while speaking evasively about anti-Palestinian racism and Islamophobia. Some have been doxxed or threatened for advocacy. If subjective fear is the measure, their claims should have generated comparable institutional intervention. They generally have not. This selectivity reveals the danger of abandoning objective standards: power fills the vacuum.

Yet Gourevitch’s proposed threshold—likely and imminent threat, or direct and individualized harassment and intimidation—may be too narrow for the educational civil rights context. A student need not wait until danger is imminent to seek protection from discriminatory harassment. A hostile environment can be cumulative. A university should be able to respond before a pattern escalates into violence. The key is that the response must be evidence-based, targeted, and compatible with free expression.

What would that look like in practice?

First, universities should explicitly protect political speech about Israel, Palestine, Zionism, Hamas, war, occupation, genocide, apartheid, terrorism, and U.S. foreign policy, including speech that is harsh, one-sided, or offensive. This protection should include slogans unless they are used in context to threaten or target individuals.

Second, universities should define discriminatory harassment narrowly: conduct directed at persons or groups based on protected status that is severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive enough to deny equal access to educational programs or activities. Feelings matter as evidence of impact, but they cannot substitute for an objective inquiry.

Third, universities should separate protest rule violations from discrimination violations. An overnight encampment without a permit may violate a time, place, and manner rule. That does not make it antisemitic, Islamophobic, or racist. Conversely, a student can engage in discriminatory harassment even while complying with sound limits and lawn-use policies. Conflating these categories invites abuse.

Fourth, sanctions should be individualized. If a protest includes both protected speech and unprotected conduct, punish the unprotected conduct. Do not dissolve the whole protest unless there is no less restrictive way to address a serious, ongoing problem. This principle is especially important because large demonstrations inevitably include varied participants and messages.

Fifth, universities should create independent review processes insulated from trustees, donors, political officials, and public relations staff. The credibility of civil rights enforcement depends on independence. When discipline appears to follow a congressional hearing or a donor revolt, trust collapses.

Sixth, the federal government should enforce Title VI through regular, transparent procedures, not through threats that resemble ideological extortion. The Trump administration’s suspension of funds to Columbia and threats to other universities are ominous not because antisemitism is unworthy of federal concern, but because civil rights law is being used as a lever to reshape universities politically. A government may require compliance with law. It may not demand submission to a partisan “counterrevolution blueprint.”

Finally, universities should stop promising emotional safety. They cannot deliver it. Political life includes grief, anger, offense, and fear. What universities can promise is equal access, physical safety, fair procedures, and protection from targeted harassment. That more modest promise is also more defensible.

Gourevitch is right that democracy requires discomfort. But civil rights require more than telling students to develop thicker skins. The real task is to protect both: the ability of protesters to confront power and the ability of students from vulnerable groups to participate in campus life without being targeted or excluded. That task is difficult because speech can wound, identity and politics overlap, and institutions are tempted by shortcuts.

The shortcuts have failed. Police raids have not made campuses safer. Expulsions have not clarified principles. Congressional theater has not protected Jews. Donor panic has not protected Palestinians. Broad speech codes have not produced equality. They have produced fear, resentment, and opportunism.

A better settlement is possible: strong protest rights, narrow harassment rules, equal concern for all affected groups, due process, and resistance to state coercion. That settlement will disappoint maximalists on every side. It will not satisfy those who want anti-Zionism banned as antisemitism. It will not satisfy those who want all protest conduct immunized by moral urgency. But it offers the best chance of preserving both academic freedom and civil rights in a moment when each is being invoked to destroy the other.

  3. 03Universities should separate the adjudication of protest rule violations, such as encampment permits, from the adjudication of civil rights violations like antisemitism or Islamophobia.
  4. 04Disciplinary actions must be individualized to address specific unprotected conduct rather than used as collective punishment to dissolve entire protest movements.

  6. 06Universities should stop promising students emotional safety from political discomfort and instead focus on guaranteeing physical safety, equal access, and fair procedures.

"The right to protest must be expanded from a defense of expressive hostility into a right to organize that challenges the undemocratic power structures and managerial sovereignty of modern universities."

Alex Gourevitch’s essay is a defense of protest, but its deeper subject is power. That is why it is more convincing than many campus free speech arguments. He does not treat the university as a neutral marketplace of ideas temporarily disturbed by unruly students. He treats it as an institution structured by trustees, donors, police, federal money, real estate, administrative bureaucracy, and labor hierarchy. The crackdown on Gaza protests did not fall from the sky. It was the predictable response of institutions that have learned to manage dissent as a reputational risk.

Still, if the right to protest is to be rebuilt, we should go further than Gourevitch does. The right to be hostile must become the right to organize.

Campus protest is not merely expressive. It is a form of collective power by people who usually have very little. Students do not control endowments. Graduate workers often have little security. Adjuncts are disposable. Staff are expected to keep the university running while having minimal voice in its governance. Faculty governance has been weakened for decades. The people most invested in the intellectual and social life of the university are often least empowered to decide its direction.

That imbalance explains why administrators reached so quickly for police. They experienced encampments not only as speech but as a challenge to managerial sovereignty. Students built temporary communities outside official channels. They debated, cooked, prayed, studied, held teach-ins, and issued demands. They interrupted the smooth functioning of the university-brand. That is precisely what made the encampments politically meaningful.

The language of safety then became a way to restore hierarchy. Administrators did not say, “We are clearing this encampment because trustees and donors want it gone.” They said, “We are protecting students.” They did not say, “We cannot tolerate a challenge to our investment policies.” They said, “The community is being disrupted.” They did not say, “We fear congressional Republicans and federal funding threats.” They said, “We must enforce rules.”

This is not to say that safety concerns were invented. Jewish students have faced real antisemitism. Muslim, Arab, and Palestinian students have faced real racism and repression. Students have been doxxed, harassed, and threatened. But the institution’s response has not been to build collective safety. It has been to monopolize the definition of safety and attach it to policing.

A labor movement would recognize the pattern. Employers routinely invoke “order,” “access,” and “respect” to discipline pickets. They describe disruption as coercion when it is workers doing the disrupting, but as efficiency when management restructures lives. They claim neutrality while hiring union-busting consultants. Universities are not exempt from this logic. They are employers, landlords, investors, contractors, and political actors. When students demand divestment or workers demand a contract, they are confronting power, not merely expressing views.

This is why Gourevitch’s comparison to labor pickets deserves more attention. A picket does not simply communicate disagreement. It pressures. It asks others not to cross. It makes normal operations morally and practically harder. Yet labor law and democratic tradition have recognized that such disruption can be legitimate because without it workers have little leverage. Campus protest likewise often seeks leverage: divestment, amnesty, disclosure, bargaining, an end to institutional complicity. If the only acceptable protest is one that cannot pressure anyone, then the university has protected speech while disabling politics.

The challenge is to defend disruptive pressure without romanticizing every tactic. Movements can reproduce domination. They can marginalize dissenters, tolerate bigotry, or mistake intensity for strategy. Encampments can become insular. Chants can become cruel. Activists can evade accountability by saying everything is justified by the cause. A serious left cannot accept that. But accountability should come from democratic movement culture and fair institutional processes, not from police raids or donor-driven discipline.

What would a pro-protest university look like?

First, it would democratize governance. Students, faculty, graduate workers, staff, and contingent instructors should have real representation in decisions about protest rules, investment policy, policing, and discipline. Boards should not be insulated clubs of wealth. Presidents should not govern as crisis managers for donors. If the campus is a community, its members should govern it.

Second, universities should abolish or radically shrink their dependence on armed police for protest management. The UCLA failure, where counterprotesters attacked a pro-Palestinian encampment while authorities delayed intervention, exposed the hollowness of official safety talk. Police are often deployed against protesters, not for them. Universities need trained de-escalation teams, clear protective protocols, and a strong presumption against calling municipal police unless there is serious violence or imminent danger.

Third, discipline should be restorative and proportionate. If students violate protest rules by camping overnight or refusing to disperse, the default should not be suspension, expulsion, arrest, or degree withholding. Universities should use warnings, mediated agreements, community repair, and modest sanctions. Severe penalties should be reserved for violence, threats, serious harassment, or major property destruction.

Fourth, universities should protect political boycotts and divestment campaigns as core forms of campus democracy. It is absurd to celebrate student activism in brochures while treating actual demands on institutional investments as illegitimate. Students are right to ask where tuition, endowment income, and research partnerships go. Even those who disagree with divestment should defend the right to organize for it.

Fifth, equal protection must be real. If Jewish students report antisemitic harassment, it should be investigated. If Palestinian students report anti-Palestinian racism, it should be investigated. If Muslim students are surveilled or doxxed, the institution should respond. But none of these protections should become a veto over political protest. The standard must be conduct: threats, exclusion, targeted harassment, discrimination—not the mere fact that a movement’s message is painful.

Gourevitch is right that the subjective safety standard has backfired. Many progressives once believed that expanding administrative definitions of harm would protect the vulnerable. But administrators are not agents of liberation. They are agents of institutions. They will use whatever language is available—civility, safety, inclusion, antisemitism, professionalism, disruption—to protect institutional authority. When the right learned to speak that language, the trap snapped shut.

The answer is not indifference to harm. It is collective power over the conditions that produce vulnerability. A Palestinian student whose family is under bombardment does not need an administrator’s bland wellness email; she needs political space to grieve and organize. A Jewish student afraid of antisemitism does not need the state to deport activists; he needs a campus culture that can distinguish antiwar politics from hatred of Jews and confront the latter without criminalizing the former. A graduate worker threatened for protesting does not need a listening session; she needs a union, due process, and protection from retaliation.

The right to be hostile, then, is not the right to be careless. It is the right to confront power in public, to accuse, to assemble, to disrupt, and to demand. Hostility is sometimes the voice of moral clarity in a world of euphemism. Universities that train students to avoid it train them for obedience.

But protest rights will remain fragile if they depend on enlightened administrators. They must be embedded in democratic governance and backed by organized constituencies. Faculty senates, unions, student governments, civil liberties groups, alumni, and community organizations should negotiate binding protest protections before the next crisis. Amnesty for punished student protesters should be part of that agenda. So should limits on donor influence, transparency in investments, and restraints on police.

The campus is one of the few remaining places where large numbers of young people live in proximity, argue about the world, and can act collectively. That is why it is contested. The crackdown on Gaza protests is not only about Gaza, though Gaza is reason enough. It is about whether universities will be governed as democratic commons or as fortified brands.

Gourevitch has named the freedom at stake. The next step is to build the power capable of defending it.

"A durable defense of campus liberty must protect the right to offensive protest while maintaining viewpoint-neutral rules of order that prevent disruption from swallowing the university's educational mission."

Alex Gourevitch has written the free speech essay many conservatives used to write and too few now seem willing to endorse. His central point is correct: a right to protest that disappears when protest becomes offensive, hostile, or emotionally unsettling is not a right. Universities that call police on peaceful demonstrators because donors, politicians, or administrators dislike the message have betrayed their mission.

Yet Gourevitch’s argument also exposes a problem he does not fully confront. The campus left helped build the machinery now being used against it. For years, many students and administrators treated speech as harm, demanded disinvitations, shouted down speakers, expanded bias-reporting systems, and insisted that members of marginalized groups should have broad authority to define what counts as violence. Now the same logic is being applied to pro-Palestinian protest by people with more political power. The result is ugly, but it is not surprising.

This does not justify the crackdown. Hypocrisy is not a civil liberties principle. If a student once favored deplatforming a conservative speaker, that student does not thereby forfeit protection against expulsion for peaceful protest. Rights are not rewards for consistency. Still, any durable defense of campus liberty must admit that the old progressive speech regime was wrong before it was turned against progressives.

Gourevitch says as much, and he deserves credit for it. But his remedy risks replacing one special pleading with another. He wants an “absolutist” defense of protest, including disruptive protest, and he suggests that encampments on campus commons should be broadly tolerated. I agree up to a point. But conservatives, libertarians, and old-fashioned liberals should be wary of granting protest a privileged status that swallows ordinary rules.

The university commons is not a public park in the full sense, especially at a private university. It is a shared institutional space devoted to education. Students should be able to rally there, leaflet there, hold vigils there, argue there, and display messages others hate. But they should not acquire the right to indefinitely occupy it, control access, establish checkpoints, or make other students run a gauntlet of political confrontation as the price of attending class. Property rules are not always pretexts for repression. Sometimes they are the rules that allow people with competing commitments to share a space.

That is where some defenders of the Gaza encampments have been evasive. It is not enough to say most people could still get where they needed to go, or that obstacles were erected for self-defense. The question is whether a university may impose generally applicable rules against camping, structures, amplified sound, and disruption. Of course it may. The issue is whether those rules are clear, viewpoint-neutral, and enforced proportionately.

This is why police raids and expulsions were often indefensible. If a university had long tolerated encampments for other causes and suddenly discovered a no-camping rule only when the cause was Gaza, that is viewpoint discrimination in spirit even if lawyers can dress it up otherwise. If students were suspended or denied degrees for nonviolent conduct that would previously have received a warning, that is punitive overreach. If the Trump administration is threatening funds to force universities into ideological conformity, conservatives should oppose it as an abuse of federal power.

Unfortunately, many conservatives have abandoned their own stated principles. After years of denouncing “cancel culture,” some now cheer the cancellation of anti-Israel protesters. They once argued that students must hear ideas they find hateful; now they argue that Jewish students must be shielded from anti-Zionist slogans. They once mocked “safe spaces”; now they demand them, only for different constituencies. They once warned that DEI bureaucracies would become ideological police; now they want to redirect the police rather than dismantle the ideology.

This is not conservatism. It is factional opportunism.

A principled conservative response would say three things.

First, antisemitism should be punished when it takes the form of threats, vandalism, assault, discriminatory exclusion, or targeted harassment. Jewish students have the same right as anyone else to attend school without being menaced. Universities that ignored swastikas, slurs, or intimidation would be failing in their duties.

Second, criticism of Israel, including harsh criticism and anti-Zionism, must be protected. The state should not define the boundaries of acceptable opinion on foreign policy. Nor should federal agencies adopt expansive definitions of antisemitism that effectively make one side of a geopolitical debate legally hazardous.

Third, universities may enforce neutral rules of order. They can ban camping on quads, limit amplification, keep entrances clear, and remove occupiers from buildings. But penalties should be modest unless there is violence or serious obstruction, and rules should apply to everyone: pro-Palestinian activists, pro-Israel activists, climate protesters, anti-abortion demonstrators, and anyone else.

Gourevitch is right that subjective fear is a poor guide. The examples he quotes—students frightened by keffiyehs, chants, or the need to walk near a protest—cannot justify suppression. If conservatives allow such claims to control, they will have conceded every premise they used to oppose progressive speech codes. The answer to offensive protest is counterprotest, argument, social criticism, and, where necessary, narrow discipline for actual misconduct.

But Gourevitch should be more candid about the limits of disruption. Civil disobedience has consequences. If students deliberately violate a neutral rule, they may accept a proportionate penalty as part of the protest. That is different from persecution. Expulsion for peaceful trespass may be excessive; a citation or probation may not be. The moral seriousness of a cause does not give activists unilateral authority to decide which rules bind them.

The best campus speech regime is neither therapeutic nor anarchic. It protects speech and assembly. It rejects heckler’s vetoes. It allows offensive displays, whether anti-abortion images, anti-Israel slogans, anti-affirmative-action stunts, or radical critiques of America. It punishes threats and violence. It enforces neutral rules about space and noise. It does not ask administrators to decide which political emotions deserve validation.

The Trump administration’s campaign against universities should make defenders of limited government uneasy. Federal research money should not become a tool for forcing ideological reforms through executive threat. Universities have earned criticism for ideological conformity, administrative bloat, and double standards. But the cure for campus illiberalism is not federal illiberalism.

Gourevitch’s essay is valuable because it calls everyone’s bluff. If you defended free speech only when conservatives were unpopular, now is the test. If you defended student safety only when progressives invoked it, now is the test. If you said universities should be places of fearless inquiry, then you must tolerate students saying things about Israel, Palestine, America, policing, abortion, race, and religion that you find repugnant.

The right to protest is real. It is not unlimited. It should not depend on whether I like the protesters. That simple proposition has become rare enough to sound radical.

"While the right to be hostile and engage in disruptive protest must be protected against administrative repression, a healthy university community also requires the development of democratic relationships and civic infrastructure to address the genuine pain and alienation that rights alone cannot resolve."

Alex Gourevitch is right that universities have confused discomfort with danger, and that this confusion has licensed repression. But a campus cannot live by rights alone. The right to be hostile may be necessary; it is not sufficient.

The recent protest crisis has revealed two failures at once. The first is institutional: administrators, donors, trustees, and politicians have used safety, civility, property, and civil rights language to suppress dissent, especially dissent over Israel’s war in Gaza. The second is communal: many students no longer trust that those on the other side of a political conflict recognize their humanity. The first failure requires civil liberties. The second requires practices of democratic relationship that civil liberties cannot by themselves provide.

Gourevitch’s essay is most convincing when he argues that subjective feelings of unsafety cannot determine the limits of protest. If students can shut down demonstrations by saying they feel harmed, every movement will be vulnerable to the most anxious or powerful audience. Pro-Palestinian protests can be suppressed because Jewish students feel threatened. Pro-Israel events can be suppressed because Palestinian students feel threatened. Anti-abortion displays can be suppressed because women feel attacked. Police abolition events can be suppressed because officers feel demonized. There is no stable freedom down that road.

A university must therefore draw a line between distress and danger. It should punish threats, violence, vandalism, targeted harassment, and discriminatory exclusion. It should not punish the mere expression of political hostility. It should not call police because chants are upsetting or because a protest makes a quad unpleasant to cross. Gourevitch’s warning is indispensable.

Yet his frame leaves an important question underdeveloped: what should campus communities do with the pain that does not justify suppression but is real nonetheless?

Consider a Jewish student who hears classmates celebrate “resistance” immediately after the murder of Israeli civilians. Consider a Palestinian student who hears classmates defend the bombing of Gaza as necessary or dismiss thousands of dead children as human shields. Consider an Israeli student whose family members were killed or kidnapped. Consider a Muslim student who is treated as a terrorist sympathizer for wearing a keffiyeh. Consider a student who walks through an encampment and sees not a democratic assembly but a crowd in which she believes her grief has no place.

Not all of these experiences are legally actionable. Many should not be. But a university that responds only, “You are not objectively threatened, so endure it,” will not build a healthy democratic culture. It may protect liberty while allowing alienation to harden into hatred.

This is where both administrators and activists have failed. Administrators have offered bureaucracy: bias forms, disciplinary codes, police, task forces, statements, and compliance procedures. Activists have often offered certainty: our side is liberation, your side is oppression; our grief is political, yours is propaganda. Neither response helps people inhabit a shared institution amid moral catastrophe.

The alternative is not civility in the shallow sense. Calls for civility often function as demands that the aggrieved speak softly enough for the comfortable to ignore them. The Gaza protests were not wrong because they were angry. Anger is an appropriate response to mass death, occupation, terrorism, bombardment, dispossession, antisemitism, Islamophobia, and state violence. A university that cannot hear anger cannot hear the world.

But democratic hostility should still be answerable to democratic responsibility. Protesters have a right to use slogans others find offensive. They also have a responsibility to ask what their slogans communicate to those who hear them through histories of persecution. Jewish students who seek protection from antisemitism have a right to be taken seriously. They also have a responsibility not to equate Palestinian advocacy with hatred of Jews. Administrators have a responsibility to protect access and safety. They also have a responsibility not to transform political pressure into disciplinary panic.

A better campus response would have three layers.

The first layer is rights. Universities should adopt strong protest protections: access to commons, clear rules, no viewpoint discrimination, no police except in genuine emergencies, no severe discipline for nonviolent protest, and individualized due process. They should resist federal coercion, whether it comes in the language of antisemitism, anti-DEI politics, or national security.

The second layer is equality. Universities should investigate actual harassment and discrimination against Jews, Israelis, Palestinians, Arabs, Muslims, and others under the same standards. They should protect students from doxxing, threats, exclusion, and retaliation. They should be explicit that neither Zionist nor anti-Zionist identity, neither Palestinian nationalism nor Jewish peoplehood, places a student outside the community.

The third layer is encounter. This is the missing one. Universities should create spaces that are not disciplinary hearings or public relations events but sustained forums for difficult speech: facilitated dialogues, jointly taught courses, historical workshops, mourning spaces, and structured debates where students must listen as well as accuse. Participation should not be a precondition for protest rights; compelled dialogue is usually bad dialogue. But institutions should invest in the civic infrastructure that makes conflict more intelligible.

Some will dismiss this as naïve. Dialogue did not stop bombs from falling on Gaza. Dialogue did not free hostages. Dialogue did not prevent antisemitic attacks or anti-Palestinian racism. True. But universities cannot directly solve war. What they can do is form people capable of political judgment under conditions of anguish. That requires more than punishing misconduct and more than defending abstract liberty. It requires teaching students how to remain in the presence of those whose convictions they abhor.

Gourevitch worries that appeals to vulnerability empower authorities. He is right. But vulnerability is not only a tool of repression. It is also a fact of human life, especially in a campus community containing people with intimate ties to the violence being debated. The task is to acknowledge vulnerability without making it sovereign. Feelings should matter morally without ruling legally.

This distinction could change the tone of campus conflict. A student might say: “Your protest does not violate my rights, but it wounds me, and I want you to understand why.” Another might answer: “Your wound does not give you a veto, but I will listen, and I ask you to listen to mine.” That exchange will not always happen. Sometimes anger will overwhelm listening. Sometimes one side will exploit the language of pain. Sometimes the only appropriate response to speech is counter-speech. But universities should make such exchanges more possible, not less.

The crackdown has done the opposite. Police raids teach students that force settles conflict. Congressional hearings teach them that political theater matters more than truth. Donor pressure teaches them that wealth governs speech. Expulsions teach them that dissent is risky when it embarrasses the institution. Deportation threats teach noncitizens that their rights are conditional. These are anti-educational lessons.

A university worthy of the name would teach different lessons: that freedom includes the freedom to offend; that safety means protection from violence and exclusion, not insulation from anguish; that political opponents are not automatically enemies; that some speech can be lawful and still morally wrong; that the answer to bad protest is often better protest; and that shared life requires both courage and restraint.

Gourevitch gives us the courage. We should add the restraint—not the restraint of administrative censorship, but the restraint of people who understand that winning a political argument is not the same as building a livable world. The right to be hostile must be defended. So must the possibility that, after the chanting stops, members of a university can still face one another as human beings.

"Universities must maintain a strict distinction between subjective feelings of discomfort and objective threats of harm to preserve the right to protest and the integrity of academic freedom."

Alex Gourevitch is right to insist on a distinction that American institutions have spent years blurring: feeling unsafe is not the same thing as being threatened. A university that cannot maintain that distinction cannot sustain protest, academic freedom, or even ordinary intellectual disagreement.

The most persuasive part of Gourevitch’s essay is not his defense of any particular campus encampment, slogan, or political demand. It is his insistence that a right to protest is not meaningful if it survives only when protest is quiet, brief, tasteful, and emotionally agreeable to its audience. Protest is not a seminar paper. It is not a panel discussion. It is not an op-ed with footnotes and a civil tone. Protest is often accusatory, repetitive, loud, morally charged, and inconvenient. Its purpose is not merely to communicate a proposition but to dramatize a conflict.

That is why universities’ recent behavior has been so alarming. Many administrators have spoken as if they support free expression in principle while treating the ordinary features of protest as evidence that protest has become impermissible. Encampments were disruptive. Chants were upsetting. Students had to walk around tents. Some people saw symbols or heard slogans they found frightening. But if those facts are enough to justify police raids, suspensions, expulsions, degree denials, and criminal prosecution, then “the right to protest” has been reduced to a managed expressive privilege granted at administrative convenience.

Gourevitch is especially strong on collective punishment. Where a protester threatens someone, assaults someone, vandalizes property, or blocks access to a building, universities should act. But they should act against the person who did the thing, with evidence, due process, and proportionate sanctions. Instead, many institutions treated whole encampments as presumptively guilty. They used the existence of some ugly rhetoric, some alleged threats, and some rule violations to justify suppressing an entire political mobilization.

That is not safety. It is governance by panic.

The panic has been intensified by the instrumentalization of antisemitism. Antisemitism is real. It has surged in frightening ways. Jewish students have faced slurs, conspiracy theories, vandalism, and sometimes intimidation. No serious defense of protest can dismiss that. But the moral and legal seriousness of antisemitism is precisely why it should not be diluted into a general claim that anti-Zionist protest, criticism of Israel, or the presence of keffiyehs creates a hostile environment. When universities collapse the difference between antisemitic harassment and political protest against Israel’s war in Gaza, they do two things at once: they chill legitimate speech, and they weaken the credibility of efforts to identify actual antisemitism.

The Title VI frame has proved particularly dangerous. Properly understood, civil rights law is meant to secure equal access to education. It should protect students from targeted harassment, exclusion, threats, and discriminatory treatment. It should not be converted into a tool for suppressing political movements that make some members of a protected group feel distressed. A Jewish student’s fear may be sincere; so may a Muslim, Arab, or Palestinian student’s fear in the face of pro-Israel advocacy, donor pressure, doxxing campaigns, or official statements treating Palestinian grief as suspect. The university cannot adjudicate political expression by asking whose fear has more institutional backing.

A regime based on subjective injury will not protect the vulnerable. It will protect those whose vulnerability is legible to power.

This is the hard lesson of the last year. For decades, many progressives became too comfortable with administrative management of speech. They often assumed that speech regulation would be used against racists, sexists, homophobes, and reactionaries. Conservatives, meanwhile, spent years denouncing “cancel culture” only to embrace state coercion when the speech at issue was anti-Israel. The result is a bipartisan safety bureaucracy: the left helped legitimate the language of harm, and the right seized the machinery.

The Trump administration’s threats against universities show where this leads. Federal funding can be made conditional on ideological compliance. Departments can be targeted. Faculty can be purged. Immigrants can be made examples. Students can be disciplined not because they pose a threat but because they embarrass institutions and offend powerful constituencies. Once universities accept that the government may dictate campus rules in the name of protecting students from political discomfort, academic freedom becomes a hollow phrase.

Gourevitch’s critics will say that he romanticizes disruption. But his argument does not require universities to allow anything and everything. A serious protest regime can include rules against blocking entrances, occupying laboratories, preventing classes from meeting, targeted harassment, property destruction, and violence. It can regulate amplified sound during exams or late at night. It can require fire lanes and emergency access. It can impose modest discipline for civil disobedience. But such rules must be clear, viewpoint-neutral, and proportionate. They must not be rewritten under donor pressure or enforced selectively against one movement.

The university commons matters here. A campus is not merely a collection of classrooms and offices. It is a community where students learn democratic habits by exercising them. If the central quad is treated exactly like a corporate lobby, then the university has already conceded that its educational mission is subordinate to property management. Students should not have unlimited control over campus space, but they should have meaningful access to it for collective dissent.

The most important word in Gourevitch’s essay is “hostile.” He does not defend cruelty for its own sake. He defends the right to express hostility toward political positions, institutions, states, wars, ideologies, and movements. That right is indispensable because politics often concerns matters of life and death. To demand that protest avoid hostility is to demand that the powerless address power in the language power prefers.

A democratic university should teach students to argue, organize, endure disagreement, answer speech with speech, and distinguish danger from discomfort. It should not teach them that the first response to moral shock is administrative suppression. The current crackdown teaches exactly that lesson. It tells students that rights are conditional, that property beats conscience, that donors outrank faculty, that police are the final arbiters of campus conflict, and that the state may decide which forms of grief count as discrimination.

That lesson is far more dangerous than a noisy encampment.

The best way to destroy the right to protest is to pretend that protest presents no hard administrative problems. Alex Gourevitch does not quite do that, but he comes close. His essay powerfully indicts the overreaction of universities and the opportunism of the Trump administration. Yet it gives too little attention to the mundane obligations that make a university different from a town square: classes must meet, laboratories must operate, libraries must remain usable, dormitories must be livable, and students who want no part in a demonstration must still be able to go about their lives.

The issue is not whether students have a right to protest. They do. Nor is it whether universities should have called armed police on peaceful encampments as readily as many did. They should not have. The issue is whether Gourevitch’s near-absolutist defense of disruptive protest gives institutions enough guidance to distinguish tolerable disruption from coercive occupation.

Universities failed this test in both directions. Before the Gaza protests, many had cultivated expansive, selectively enforced speech norms. Administrators routinely sent moral pronouncements on public events, policed interpersonal conduct through broad harassment rules, and encouraged students to understand emotional injury as a matter for bureaucratic resolution. Then, when protests over Gaza erupted, the same institutions abruptly rediscovered property rights, order, and neutrality. That hypocrisy helped create the crisis.

But the remedy cannot be simply to say that encampments belong in the campus commons until they become violent or directly threatening. Space is scarce. Time matters. Scale matters. Duration matters. A twelve-hour rally is not a three-week encampment. A symbolic sit-in is not the indefinite seizure of a building. A march through campus is not a blockade. A protest that creates ambient noise on the quad is not one that makes a classroom unusable. Rules have to account for those differences.

Gourevitch argues that campus commons are special, not like classrooms or libraries. That is right. But “less governed” cannot mean “governed by whoever can mobilize enough people to hold the space.” A commons exists for the whole community. If one group may convert it into a semi-permanent political camp, others must have the same right. In theory, Gourevitch accepts that symmetry. In practice, simultaneous encampments by mutually hostile groups can make the commons unusable for everyone and potentially dangerous. Administrators cannot avoid responsibility by invoking protest rights in the abstract.

The answer is not repression but constitutionalism for campus life: clear, public, viewpoint-neutral rules adopted through legitimate processes and enforced with restraint.

A reasonable protest code would protect rallies, marches, pickets, vigils, symbolic displays, and short-term encampments in designated public areas. It would permit noise but regulate amplification during class hours and quiet hours. It would allow signs, chants, and offensive messages but prohibit targeted threats, discriminatory exclusion from campus facilities, harassment directed at specific individuals, and obstruction of entrances. It would distinguish occupying an administrative building from sitting on a lawn. It would provide escalating consequences: warning, citation, modest disciplinary sanction, and only in the most serious cases suspension or police referral.

The most important feature would be due process. Students should not face suspension, expulsion, degree withholding, or arrest through improvised procedures created under donor, congressional, or executive pressure. Evidence should be individualized. Charges should be specific. Hearings should be timely and fair. Sanctions should be proportionate. Collective punishment should be forbidden.

On this point Gourevitch’s critique is devastating. The extraordinary punishments imposed on many Gaza protesters look less like discipline than deterrence. Universities were not simply enforcing rules; they were signaling to politicians, trustees, and donors that they had regained control. That is an institutional failure of the first order.

Yet defenders of protest should also be honest about the concept of civil disobedience. Civil disobedience often involves breaking rules to expose injustice. Its moral power does not depend on pretending no rules were broken. If students occupy a building, refuse lawful instructions, or establish an encampment in violation of a clear time limit, they may be disciplined. The question is whether discipline is modest and whether the institution recognizes the conscientious nature of the act. A warning or probation may be appropriate where expulsion is outrageous. A trespass arrest may be disproportionate where an internal sanction would suffice. But “protest” cannot be a universal exemption from rules.

The hostile environment question is harder. Gourevitch is correct that subjective fear cannot be enough. If every group can shut down protest by saying it feels unsafe, no protest will survive. At the same time, administrators cannot ignore patterns of intimidation simply because they are politically framed. If students are followed, filmed, surrounded, spat on, denied access, or targeted by slurs, the university has an obligation to intervene. If Jewish, Israeli, Palestinian, Arab, or Muslim students are treated as legitimate objects of contempt because of identity, that is not merely robust politics.

Here too the answer is specificity. “I felt afraid walking past a protest” is not, by itself, evidence of a hostile environment. “A group blocked me from entering a building while calling me a terrorist” is different. “I dislike that slogan” is not the same as “students surrounded me and shouted it in my face.” “This movement’s demands make me feel unwelcome” is different from “this organization excludes people of my national origin from events.” Universities should investigate conduct, not adjudicate metaphysical debates about whether anti-Zionism is always, sometimes, or never antisemitism.

The Trump administration’s intervention makes this disciplined approach harder but more necessary. The federal government has legitimate authority to enforce civil rights laws. It does not have legitimate authority to use Title VI as a pretext for ideological takeover. Conditioning hundreds of millions of dollars on sweeping political demands threatens the independence of universities and the integrity of civil rights enforcement. Administrators should resist such coercion. But they will resist more credibly if their own procedures are fair, transparent, and serious.

Gourevitch’s broader diagnosis of the university is also right: boards are too powerful, faculty governance too weak, donor influence too pervasive, and student life too bureaucratized. The crisis over protest has exposed institutional rot. Presidents who spent years issuing statements on every public controversy found themselves unable to articulate principled neutrality. Offices built to manage harm lacked the conceptual tools to defend liberty. Trustees treated universities as brands to protect rather than communities to govern.

Still, a university cannot be governed by maximalist protest rights alone. It must protect dissent and continuity at once. It must allow students to disrupt ordinary complacency without allowing them to make ordinary education impossible. It must take fear seriously without letting fear rule. It must punish misconduct without persecuting movements.

The right standard is not hostility without limits. It is liberty under fair rules. Universities need fewer emergency decrees, fewer police raids, fewer donor-driven investigations, and fewer therapeutic abstractions. They need stable norms that everyone can understand before the next crisis arrives.

If they do not build those norms now, the next wave of protest—whether over Gaza, climate change, abortion, immigration, policing, or something not yet visible—will produce the same cycle: administrative improvisation, accusations of selective enforcement, outside political pressure, excessive discipline, and deeper distrust. Gourevitch is right that democracy requires discomfort. But democracy also requires institutions capable of channeling conflict without either smothering it or surrendering to it. That is the work universities have avoided for too long.
