"While Kate Soper’s "alternative hedonism" correctly identifies the miseries of consumer culture, it can only be realized through a labor-centered political program that redistributes power and time rather than just moralizing about individual consumer choices."

Kate Soper’s essay names a truth that many people feel but rarely hear in politics: affluent consumer society is not very pleasurable. It is frantic, indebted, surveilled, polluted, lonely, and full of bad bargains. Yet the people most likely to agree with that diagnosis are also likely to ask a harder question: who gets to leave it? For workers living paycheck to paycheck, for tenants facing rising rents, for parents coordinating shifts and school pickups, for immigrants sending remittances, the problem is not an excess of consumer pleasure. It is the absence of power.

That is why alternative hedonism must be rebuilt from the standpoint of labor. Soper criticizes the left for focusing on distribution while neglecting consumer culture. There is something to this. Socialist politics has often assumed that the goods of affluent society need only be more equally shared. But the emphasis on distribution did not come from a failure of imagination. It came from the fact that unequal access to income, security, housing, health care, transport, and leisure remains the main way millions experience unfreedom. Before people can be persuaded that they should want differently, they need the power to refuse what is currently imposed on them.

Take the fast food meal. It is easy to describe it as a symptom of time-scarce, junk-ridden affluence. It is also dinner after a double shift. It is calories for a delivery driver whose route is timed by an app. It is a treat for a child when the parent cannot afford a holiday. It is warmth, a toilet, and a place to sit in a city where public space has disappeared. To criticize the culture that makes such food ubiquitous is necessary. To imply that people rely on it because they are trapped in consumerist desire is incomplete. They are trapped in labor markets, housing markets, and care arrangements that make other choices costly.

The same applies to Amazon. Many people know perfectly well that the convenience of next-day delivery rests on brutal warehouse discipline, anti-union practices, packaging waste, and a logistics system that clogs roads with vans. They use it anyway because local retail has been hollowed out, because prices are lower, because time is scarce, because disability or caregiving makes shopping difficult, because returning an item online is easier than navigating an underfunded bus system. Consumer choices are downstream from collective defeats.

Soper’s most promising proposal is the reduction of working time. Here the politics of pleasure and the politics of class meet. A shorter workweek would do more than reduce emissions or improve well-being; it would challenge the domination of life by wage labor. The demand has a long history in labor movements: eight hours for work, eight hours for rest, eight hours for what we will. The tragedy is that productivity gains in recent decades have so often been captured as profits, executive compensation, or cheaper goods rather than as free time. If alternative hedonism means taking productivity back in the form of life, then unions should embrace it.

But this cannot be a polite lifestyle reform. Reduced hours without loss of pay will require struggle. Employers will not grant it because cycling and supper clubs sound delightful. They will resist because time is power. Control over schedules is one of the central mechanisms by which workers are disciplined. Unpredictable shifts, mandatory overtime, split shifts, speedup, on-call expectations, and algorithmic management all make it harder to organize, parent, rest, or participate in public life. A politics of alternative hedonism must therefore be a politics of collective bargaining, sectoral standards, stronger labor law, and worker control over scheduling.

It must also include the workers whose labor makes low-carbon pleasure possible. Calls for walkable cities, fresh food, repaired goods, public transit, and care-rich communities can conceal a labor question. Who drives the buses, grows the vegetables, repairs the shoes, maintains the parks, staffs the libraries, watches the children, cleans the community centers, and cares for the elderly? If these jobs are underpaid and feminized, racialized, or precarious, then the pleasant life of some will again rest on the invisible labor of others. The goal cannot simply be to shift from commodity consumption to service-rich conviviality. It must be to dignify, democratize, and fairly distribute the necessary work of sustaining life.

Soper gestures toward this when she calls for less gendered, racialized, and class-based divisions of labor. But the point deserves more centrality. The history of “simple living” is often haunted by women’s unpaid work. Cooking from scratch, mending clothes, growing food, caring for neighbors, organizing local exchanges—all can be satisfying. They can also become obligations loaded onto those already expected to serve. A shorter workweek for men that results in more leisure for men and more domestic labor for women would not be liberation. Alternative hedonism must be feminist in its institutional design: universal childcare, paid family leave, eldercare, shorter hours for all, and cultural expectations that men do care work.

There is another class problem: the aesthetics of postconsumerism. Farmers’ markets, repair cafés, cargo bikes, train holidays, handmade goods, and local cooperatives may be admirable. They can also become markers of status, available mainly to those with money, education, flexible time, and safe neighborhoods. Meanwhile the poor are judged for buying cheap clothes, driving old cars, eating processed food, or wanting a package holiday. Nothing would discredit ecological politics faster than a new moral hierarchy in which affluent people perform virtue through tasteful restraint while working-class people are told their pleasures are vulgar.

The answer is not to abandon Soper’s critique of consumer culture, but to aim it upward. The first targets should be luxury emissions and elite waste: private jets, frequent business flights, yachts, multiple large homes, speculative real estate, planned obsolescence, advertising to children, and the political power of fossil capital. Working people will not accept climate restraint if they see the rich exempted from it. Nor should they. Climate justice in affluent countries begins with the principle that the more one has benefited from the carbon economy, the more one must change and pay.

Public provision is the bridge between ecological limits and working-class freedom. If people are to consume fewer private goods, they need better shared goods. A household with access to excellent transit can own fewer cars. A neighborhood with safe parks and youth centers needs fewer commercial entertainments. A family with secure housing, health care, childcare, and good schools needs less compensatory spending. A worker with paid vacation and predictable hours can travel more slowly. A tenant with the right to repair, efficient appliances, and protection from eviction can live more sustainably. Without such provision, “consume less” means “make do with insecurity.”

The left should therefore translate alternative hedonism into a program: decarbonize energy; build social housing near transit; guarantee health care and childcare; shorten the workweek; raise wages; unionize care, logistics, retail, and food service; ban the worst forms of planned obsolescence; create public repair and reuse systems; tax wealth and carbon-intensive luxuries; bring utilities and transit under democratic control; restrict manipulative advertising; and expand parks, libraries, and cultural institutions. This is not anti-consumer moralism. It is a material basis for different desires.

Soper is right that we cannot leave the idea of pleasure to advertisers. But neither can we leave the remaking of pleasure to consumers. The subject capable of changing daily life is not the ethical shopper but the organized public: workers, tenants, parents, students, caregivers, and communities acting together. Desire changes when institutions change. People discover the pleasure of cycling when streets are safe. They discover the pleasure of cooking when they have time and decent kitchens. They discover the pleasure of public space when it is clean, welcoming, and unpoliced. They discover the pleasure of repair when goods are made repairable and skills are shared. They discover the pleasure of solidarity when collective action wins.

Alternative hedonism is most valuable as a refusal of the grim story that climate politics must be all loss. But it should not be framed as a correction to a left too concerned with distribution. Distribution is the precondition of freedom from consumerism. If the good life is to be less dependent on buying things, then people need more control over time, space, and the conditions of survival. The route to a postconsumerist society runs through the old questions of class power: who owns, who works, who decides, who pays, and who has time to enjoy the world we are trying to save.

"To be politically viable and effective, the climate movement must move beyond the moralism of sacrifice and green technology by institutionalizing 'alternative hedonism' through structural changes that prioritize time, public luxury, and the commons over consumerist compulsion."

Kate Soper’s most important claim is not that we must consume less. It is that “less” is the wrong word for what we need. A politics that asks people to exchange pleasure for virtue will fail, and perhaps deserves to fail. The moralism of sacrifice has always been too thin a foundation for democratic transformation. What Soper calls alternative hedonism is a stronger proposition: much of what affluent societies call prosperity is already experienced as stress, ugliness, waste, fatigue, loneliness, and dependence. The climate crisis does not merely require us to give up satisfactions; it gives us reason to ask whether many of those satisfactions were ever as satisfying as advertised.

This is exactly the terrain on which climate politics has been weakest. For decades the public has been told either that the energy transition will be painless or that it will be a grim necessity. The first promise is increasingly unbelievable; the second is politically disastrous. We know enough by 2022 to say that solar panels, wind turbines, heat pumps, batteries, and electrified transport can do a great deal. We also know they do not abolish the problem of scale. A world of ever-rising extraction, ever-faster turnover of goods, and ever-expanding infrastructure cannot be made ecologically harmless by swapping the fuel source. The atmosphere is not the only boundary we are crossing. Land, water, minerals, soils, forests, and species are also under pressure. If climate politics means only replacing every gasoline SUV with an electric SUV, every airport expansion with “sustainable aviation fuel,” and every disposable gadget with a slightly more recyclable one, then it will have left the cultural logic of the crisis untouched.

Soper is right, then, to turn our attention to the organization of daily life. The case against consumerism is strongest when it begins not with the polar bear or the future generation, important as they are, but with the commuter stuck in traffic, the parent eating dinner from a plastic tray at 9 p.m., the child whose neighborhood has no safe place to play, the warehouse worker whose body is organized around next-day delivery, the office employee spending life on tasks no one can justify. These are not incidental costs of affluence; they are constitutive of it. The system produces time scarcity and then sells compensations for it. It produces sedentary lives and then sells gym memberships. It produces social comparison and then sells self-improvement. It produces exhaustion and then sells convenience.

The great virtue of alternative hedonism is that it makes visible the double deprivation at the heart of affluent consumer society. First, we are deprived of ecological security. Second, we are deprived of forms of pleasure that require time, trust, public space, and noncommercial relations. A walkable neighborhood is not merely lower-carbon than a car-dependent suburb. It is a different sensory and social world. A shorter workweek is not merely a way to reduce output. It is a way to make care, friendship, cooking, repair, political participation, and idleness possible. A culture of repair and sharing is not merely a waste-reduction strategy. It can restore skills, pride, and local interdependence.

Still, the argument needs a sharper politics. “Concerned consumers” may help initiate a cultural shift, but they cannot carry it. The barriers Soper describes are not simply habits of desire; they are built into housing markets, transport systems, work contracts, school schedules, credit relations, and corporate power. Millions of people do not choose fast food because they have mistaken it for the good life. They choose it because wages are low, hours are long, kitchens are inadequate, commutes are punishing, and children must be fed now. Many do not buy from Amazon because they are dupes of advertising. They do so because local shops have disappeared, prices matter, and time has been organized against them. If alternative hedonism is to be more than a countercultural preference, it must become institutional.

That means starting with time. The reduction of working hours should be treated as climate policy, public health policy, family policy, and democratic policy at once. A four-day week, where feasible, is not a boutique perk for professionals; it should be a demand of the labor movement. But reduced hours must not mean reduced pay for those already struggling. Nor can it apply only to salaried workers while care workers, cleaners, delivery drivers, and nurses absorb the inconvenience. The goal should be a general redistribution of necessary labor, supported by stronger unions, living wages, public services, and income guarantees. Soper mentions universal basic income; whether one favors UBI, a job guarantee, expanded social security, or some combination, the point is that freedom from consumerist compulsion requires material security.

Second, we need public luxury, not private austerity. The language of reduced consumption often fails because people imagine losing the few comforts they possess while the rich continue flying private jets and buying second homes. A politics of alternative hedonism must be ruthlessly egalitarian. It should tax and regulate luxury emissions first: frequent flying, oversized vehicles, large inefficient homes, fossil-intensive leisure, and speculative overbuilding. But it should simultaneously expand the shared goods that make low-carbon life attractive: excellent public transit, libraries open late, parks, playgrounds, public pools, community kitchens, repair workshops, safe cycling networks, clean public toilets, cultural centers, and universal broadband. People will give up some private consumption more readily if the commons become richer.

Third, the built environment must change. Appeals to individual virtue are absurd in places where not driving is dangerous, where rents force people far from work, where schools and shops are unreachable by foot, where public transit is infrequent or humiliating. Car culture is not just a taste; it is a land-use regime. To move beyond it requires zoning reform, social housing, transit investment, traffic calming, and the conversion of streets from conduits for vehicles into places for people. Soper’s evocation of children reclaiming urban space is not nostalgic. It is one of the simplest tests of a civilized society: can children move safely and freely through their neighborhood? In much of the United States, the answer is no. That is not prosperity.

Fourth, alternative hedonism must confront advertising. It is strange to call consumer demand “free choice” in a society that permits vast resources to be spent manufacturing insecurity and desire, including among children. Restrictions on advertising to children, limits on outdoor advertising, transparency rules for digital targeting, and public-interest media are not puritanical intrusions. They are defenses of mental autonomy. A society that bans certain pollutants from rivers can also restrict pollutants in the attention economy.

The hardest question is whether Soper underestimates the pleasures of modern consumer life. Many people genuinely enjoy fast travel, novelty, fashion, automobiles, and digital devices. Some of these pleasures are not false; they are real. A credible postconsumerist politics must admit this. It should not pretend that every sacrifice is secretly a gain. There will be losses: less spontaneity in long-distance travel, fewer cheap goods, slower delivery, smaller wardrobes, perhaps less private space. But politics is always a choice among losses. The current path also imposes losses: burned landscapes, flooded homes, asthma, anxiety, debt, noise, deadening work, and the narrowing of imagination to what can be purchased.

Alternative hedonism is most persuasive when it does not deny ambivalence. We are attached to the very systems that harm us. The car is a polluter, but it is also a refuge and a symbol of autonomy in societies that have failed to provide other forms of freedom. The smartphone is a device of surveillance and distraction, but also a tool of connection, navigation, memory, and mutual aid. Shopping can be compulsive, but it can also be aesthetic play in a world where other forms of creativity have been stripped from work. The answer is not scolding but substitution: build better freedoms, richer connections, more democratic aesthetics.

The climate movement needs this language because fear alone cannot sustain transformation. Fear can mobilize, but it also paralyzes. Justice can guide, but it can feel abstract. Pleasure, rightly understood, can attract. Soper asks us to imagine climate action not as a narrowing of life but as an escape from a bad bargain: we trade our time, health, attention, streets, and planet for goods that cannot finally satisfy. The promise of alternative hedonism is that another bargain is possible. It will not emerge automatically from individual lifestyle changes, and it will not be delivered by green technology alone. It requires conflict with corporate power, redistribution from the rich, and rebuilding the commons. But it begins with a simple and radical question: what if the good life sold to us is not good enough?
