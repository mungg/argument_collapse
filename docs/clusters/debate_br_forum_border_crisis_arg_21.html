Diversified DeepSeek

"While Lea Ypi correctly identifies migration as a class crisis, her solution of reforming citizenship is insufficient; true justice requires the radical abolition of border coercion and the recognition of freedom of movement as a fundamental universal right."

Lea Ypi has given us a bracing, uncompromising diagnosis: the migration crisis is a class crisis, a symptom of a liberal democratic order that serves capital and immiserates workers across the globe. She is right that the contemporary border regime is a machine for sorting people by wealth, that the selling of golden visas and the chaining of poor migrants are two sides of the same coin, and that the progressive retreat into nativist rhetoric is a moral and strategic disaster. But if we take her argument seriously, we must take it further than she does. Ypi stops at a call for expanded political representation and transnational labour solidarity, yet the logic of her own analysis points toward a more radical conclusion: the only just response to a world economic order that makes migration involuntary is to refuse the very principle of border coercion that sustains it.

Ypi’s own hero, Nathan the Wise, is not merely a cosmopolitan who sees all cultures as equally valid in some distant future. He is a man who, having lost his family to one pogrom, refuses to let the identity of migrant be stripped from him. He insists that he was “forced to travel.” That word—forced—is the key. If the movement of people is a consequence of power relations that liberal states themselves have created and sustain, then the border is not a neutral administrative line but an instrument of class war. Ypi shows how migrants from the Global South and post-socialist states have been displaced by the very neoliberal policies advanced by Western governments and international financial institutions. Her evidence is devastating: austerity, agricultural dumping, debt traps, and coercive military interventions have turned entire regions into zones of unlivelihood. In this light, border enforcement is not a defence of a national “we” but the violent policing of the victims of your own economic policies.

Yet Ypi, like many on the contemporary left, still frames the solution in terms of democratising the state and expanding citizenship. She wants to decouple citizenship from wealth and to open political spaces for migrant contestation. These are worthy reforms. But they invite a question: if the state, as she herself argues, “has rarely been either liberal or democratic with regard to all those subjected to the power of its institutions,” why do we imagine that tinkering with representation will suddenly transform it into an instrument of justice for foreign workers? The history Ypi herself recounts—of workers’ movements, feminist struggles, anticolonial campaigns—was a history of forcing the state to concede rights through mass disruption, not of politely asking for a seat at the table. And those victories were always partial, always reversible, always limited by the territorial logic of the state itself. The very structure of citizenship, as a status that assigns rights on the basis of national membership, creates an inside and an outside. Even the most expansive citizenship regime will exclude someone.

The alternative is to treat freedom of movement not as a derivative claim to be balanced against the interests of native workers, but as a fundamental right that flows from the universal values Ypi invokes. If “freedom, equality, solidarity” are to mean anything, they must mean that no person should be imprisoned in a territory made unlivable by the economic decisions of another country’s elites. The right to exit, so celebrated during the Cold War as a check on tyranny, is a hollow right if there is no corresponding right to enter somewhere else. A genuine internationalism would demand not just “joint bargaining” between nationally based unions but the abolition of the entire system of work permits, visas, and deportations that gives employers a captive workforce. It would demand that people be free to move and to stay, on their own terms, without the constant threat of state violence.

Sceptics will say this is politically impossible, that it would destroy welfare states and provoke a nativist backlash. But this objection mistakes a transitional challenge for a permanent constraint. Social democracy did not wait for full public support before extending the franchise; it built that support through struggle. The same is true for migration. The current backlash is partly the product of a deliberate political strategy by the right, but it is also the product of a left that has failed to articulate a vision of solidarity across borders. When progressive politicians talk about “tough words and tough policy,” they validate the premise that migration is a problem to be managed rather than a consequence of injustice to be remedied. They lose the argument before it starts. Ypi rightly notes that the left cannot outcompete the right on xenophobia. But she stops short of saying what must be said: that the left’s credibility depends on its willingness to fight openly for the rights of all workers, regardless of passport, including their right to move.

A movement that takes this stance would not simply “shift the costs of economic decisions from workers to capital,” as Ypi proposes, important as that is. It would also challenge the very legitimacy of a state system that holds poor people hostage to rich countries’ migration policies. In practical terms, that means opposing not only the worst excesses—detention centres, deportations, the sale of citizenship—but the mundane apparatuses of border control: the visa requirements, the carrier sanctions, the digital tracking systems that turn the Mediterranean into a mass grave. It means insisting that climate displacement, economic displacement, and war displacement are all forms of forced migration that states have an obligation to accommodate, not out of charity, but out of reparative justice. It means making a positive case for the social and cultural benefits of mixed, mobile societies, rather than simply assuring voters that migrants are not a burden.

Ypi’s conclusion is powerful: “the commitment to a world in which nobody is forced to leave their home” is the ultimate test of liberal values. But there is a tension here. A world in which nobody is forced to leave would require such a radical redistribution of global resources and such a dismantling of extractive capitalism that borders would become largely irrelevant. People would still migrate, but they would do so as part of a normal human life, not as a desperate gamble. In the meantime, while we fight for that world, the refusal to open borders now consigns millions to continued exploitation and death. The cosmopolitan spirit of Lessing’s Nathan is not a promise for some distant future when all cultures have proven themselves; it is a demand on the present, to treat every person as a person, not as a member of a nation. To take that demand seriously is to say: no more borders built on the bones of the poor. That is the real Enlightenment courage we need.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The state's historical failure to be truly liberal or democratic suggests that merely expanding representation within the existing state structure will not transform it into an instrument of justice for foreign workers.
  2. 02The right to exit is a hollow concept unless it is accompanied by a corresponding universal right to enter, ensuring that individuals are not trapped in territories made unlivable by global economic forces.
  3. 03A genuine internationalist left must move beyond joint bargaining between national unions to demand the total dismantling of the visa and deportation systems that provide capital with a captive, exploitable workforce.
  4. 04The left's political credibility depends on articulating a vision of reparative justice that treats climate, economic, and war displacement as obligations for host states rather than acts of charity.
  5. 05The ultimate test of Enlightenment values is the immediate refusal to enforce borders that police the victims of neoliberalism, rather than waiting for a distant future of global redistribution.
