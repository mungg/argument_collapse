The Debt of Anger

Agnes Callard’s essay moved me, not because I agree with her conclusions, but because her story of the grandmother and the German girl forced me to think about my own inheritance of anger. I am the great-grandson of Armenian genocide survivors. My great-grandmother watched her parents be killed; she was marched through the desert as a child, and she survived to raise children in a diaspora shaped by loss. When I read Callard’s description of her grandmother’s fury and the forbidden friendship across the fence, I recognized the same burden I have carried: a moral debt that demands we remain angry forever, and a quiet suspicion that this debt, once accepted, can crush those it is meant to protect.

Callard argues that the reasons for anger are eternal, that apologetic gestures do nothing to change the fact of the original crime, and therefore the rationality of the grudge is intact. As an abstract argument, this has a terrifying elegance. But the lived experience of intergenerational anger teaches something else: that holding the grudge as a rational policy often reproduces the very logic of the oppressor inside the victim family. It becomes a form of emotional self-policing, a test of loyalty that demands we sacrifice ordinary human connection on the altar of memory.

I grew up hearing the stories of the genocide. My great-grandmother, before she died, would weep when she recalled the taste of bread made from grass, the bodies by the roadside. My grandmother, her daughter, spoke little about it but taught me to be wary of Turks and to distrust anyone who denied the genocide. This was not presented as personal animosity but as a moral duty: to forget would be to let the killers win. I absorbed this as a child. I remember at age ten, in a schoolyard, a classmate casually mentioned that his family had Turkish friends. I felt a stab of something like betrayal, though he had done nothing wrong. I avoided him for weeks. My mind had already been colonized by a logic that saw any positive connection to “the other side” as a mark of disloyalty.

Callard’s story of her grandmother catching her marching with a German girl is a perfect illustration of how the moral impulse can become a fence. Her grandmother, shaped by the Holocaust, could not separate the child from the nation that had murdered her world. And Callard, the child, was forced to internalize that her natural desire to play and laugh was a kind of treason. I know that feeling. I know the confusion of being told that my instinct for friendship was morally suspect. I know the moral weight that comes from being a descendant of victims, where every gesture of reconciliation with the descendants of perpetrators feels like a betrayal of the dead.

And yet, Callard seems to accept this as inevitable. She says that innocence was not a possibility, that both grandmother and granddaughter were caught in a world where moral corruption is unavoidable. I want to push back, not with philosophical arguments but with another kind of knowledge: the knowledge that comes from deciding to let some part of the grudge go. Not to forget, not to forgive the unrepentant, but to refuse to visit the sins of the past onto every person who, by accident of birth, stands on the other side of a historical line.

In my twenties, I met a Turkish student at my university. We became friends, tentatively. One evening, over tea, the subject of 1915 came up. He said something that I have replayed in my mind a thousand times: “My grandfather told me terrible things happened, but I was taught that Armenians were traitors. I don’t believe that now, but I can’t escape what I inherited either.” In that moment, I recognized that he, too, was a prisoner of history, carrying a different but parallel moral damage. And I realized that my anger, my eternal grudge as Callard would have it, had been directed not just at the perpetrators but at an entire category of people stretching into the present. It was an anger that would never run out of targets, because the original sin would never be undone. That is exactly what Callard describes as rational. But I saw, standing there, that if I stayed in that logic, I would lose the chance for a genuine human bond that might, in some small way, heal the very rupture the genocide had created.

This is not a story of pure forgiveness. I have not forgotten what was done to my family. I still believe that the Turkish state should acknowledge the genocide, that historical justice is owed. But I have also learned to distinguish between the eternal reason for historical anger—which I can hold as a collective truth—and the ways that anger plays out in my personal relationships. I have decided that the child of perpetrators is not the perpetrator, and that my accountability to my great-grandmother does not require me to cut myself off from living human beings who had no part in the crime. Callard might say that this decision is, in her terms, a “nonrational” one because the moral fact remains. But I think it is deeply rational, because it is based on a truer picture of moral reality: that persons are not simply representatives of historical groups, but individuals capable of relationship and change.

What Callard’s argument misses is the difference between reasons for anger and obligations to act on that anger in perpetuity. The fact of the genocide is unchangeable. The suffering it caused is infinite in its particularity. Those are reasons for anger. But they are not reasons to treat every German, or every Turk, or every descendant of oppressors as an emissary of evil. To do so is to adopt the essentializing logic that made the genocide possible in the first place. It is to say, in effect, that blood determines guilt, that identity is destiny. That may be an emotionally satisfying form of revenge, but it is politically and morally catastrophic.

Callard’s grandmother was not wrong to be angry; she was wrong to forbid a childhood game that had no political meaning. Her anger, however justified, became a barrier that punished the innocent and deprived her granddaughter of a friend. That is not an indictment of anger itself but of an unexamined permission to let anger dictate all boundaries. There is a middle path between purifying anger and surrendering to it: it is the path of discriminating wisely among the objects of our anger, and of recognizing that the moral fact of the past does not mandate an unyielding present hostility.

I imagine that Callard, as an adult, might look back on that fence with some sorrow. Perhaps she understands her grandmother’s rage but also mourns the lost playmate. In my own life, the most important moral lesson I have learned is that honoring the dead does not mean killing the living. It means building a world where those deaths are not repeated. And that world is built, at least in part, by the very connections that anger, left unchecked, would sever.
