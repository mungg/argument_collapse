Diversified GPT

"For Africa’s economic and political progress to be sustainable and broad-based, the continent must prioritize an agricultural transformation that supports the rural smallholders who make up the majority of the population."

The debate over Africa’s future too often begins in capitals—with elections, aid flows, commodity prices, and foreign investors. Those things matter. But for most Africans, development still begins in the field. If Africa is to have a real turn, it will have to be an agricultural turn.

Edward Miguel’s essay gives agriculture significant attention through coffee, cotton, drought, and climate change, but the implications are even larger than he suggests. Sub-Saharan Africa remains overwhelmingly rural. A majority of people depend directly or indirectly on farming and herding. Urban growth is rapid, but many cities are growing without sufficient industrial employment. Under these conditions, broad-based development cannot bypass smallholder agriculture.

The comparison with Asia is instructive. Before China became the workshop of the world, it raised agricultural productivity and rural incomes. India’s Green Revolution was uneven and had environmental costs, but it helped avert famine and supported later growth. In much of Africa, yields remain low, fertilizer use is limited, irrigation is scarce, improved seeds are underused, extension systems are weak, and rural roads are inadequate. This is not because African farmers are irrational or tradition-bound. It is because they face high risks and poor incentives.

A farmer who cannot be sure rain will come, fertilizer will be genuine, roads will be passable, prices will hold, or land rights will be respected may rationally avoid risky investments. A bag of fertilizer is not a development slogan; it is a gamble. If rains fail, the farmer may lose both the crop and the money borrowed to buy inputs. If the road collapses, a surplus may not reach the market. If a local official or relative can claim the land after it becomes valuable, investment becomes dangerous.

This is why Miguel’s proposed rapid conflict prevention support should be linked to a wider system of rural risk reduction. Drought relief after harvest failure is necessary but insufficient. Farmers need predictable tools before disaster: weather-indexed insurance, grain storage, seasonal credit, water harvesting, veterinary services, public works during lean seasons, and safety nets that prevent distress sales of livestock and tools. Botswana’s Drought Relief Program is valuable not only as conflict prevention, but as an example of a state making a credible promise to rural citizens: bad weather will not mean abandonment.

Climate change makes this agenda urgent. The Sahel may become hotter and drier; rainfall may become more variable elsewhere. Even where total rainfall does not decline, timing matters. Seeds fail when rains arrive late or stop early. Livestock die when pasture patterns shift. Conflict between farmers and herders can intensify when water points shrink. Climate adaptation is not a future environmental luxury. It is central to African development now.

Research into drought-resistant crop varieties is therefore crucial, but it must be locally grounded. Africa’s agro-ecological diversity is enormous. Maize, sorghum, millet, cassava, yams, teff, rice, bananas, cowpeas, and livestock systems all matter in different places. A single Green Revolution package will not work everywhere. Agricultural research institutions need stable funding, African scientists, and close relationships with farmers, including women farmers who often cultivate food crops with fewer resources and weaker land claims.

Rich-country policy can help or harm. Miguel’s criticism of U.S. cotton subsidies is exactly right. It is indefensible for wealthy governments to preach market discipline while protecting their own farmers. West African cotton producers are efficient, but they compete against treasuries, not just farmers. Ending such subsidies would not solve all rural poverty, but it would remove a major injustice. European and Japanese protections deserve similar scrutiny.

At the same time, African governments must not use Western hypocrisy as an excuse for their own neglect of agriculture. For years, many states taxed farmers implicitly through marketing boards, overvalued exchange rates, poor roads, and urban-biased spending. Governments often prefer visible urban projects to rural extension. Ministries of agriculture can become patronage machines rather than service providers. If Africa’s current growth produces more revenue, a substantial share should go to rural public goods: feeder roads, research, soil conservation, irrigation, market information, and rural schools.

The food-price situation in 2008 adds urgency. Around the world, prices for staples have risen sharply. For African farmers who produce a surplus, this may be an opportunity. For net food buyers, including many small farmers and urban poor people, it is a crisis. High food prices can provoke unrest and malnutrition. They also remind us that food security cannot be reduced to import capacity. African countries need more productive domestic and regional food systems.

Regional trade is particularly important. Drought in one area need not cause famine if food can move from surplus regions to deficit regions. Too often borders, roadblocks, bribes, export bans, and poor transport prevent this. Busia’s paved highway is thus not merely a symbol of commerce; it is part of a food-security system. When Kenya’s post-election violence shut transport routes, Uganda felt the consequences. A region’s stomach depends on its roads and politics.

Agriculture is also connected to democracy. Rural citizens are often numerous but politically fragmented. Competitive elections can make governments more attentive to them, but only if rural votes are not merely mobilized through ethnic brokers. Farmer associations, cooperatives, women’s groups, and local media can help translate rural needs into policy. Otherwise agricultural budgets may be announced in capitals and captured before reaching villages.

What about manufacturing? Africa needs it. But manufacturing will not absorb enough workers soon if agriculture stagnates. Higher rural incomes create demand for local goods and services. They finance schooling. They reduce desperate migration. They provide raw materials for agro-processing. In many countries, the path to industrialization may begin with processing food, fibers, leather, and timber more efficiently.

Miguel asks whether Africa can become the next development miracle. Miracles are the wrong metaphor for farming. Farmers understand seasons, risk, labor, and patience. A continent-wide agricultural transformation would look less dramatic than skyscrapers in Shanghai, but it would be no less revolutionary: fewer hungry seasons, higher yields, healthier children, more girls in school, more local trade, fewer conflicts over scarce resources, and stronger domestic markets.

If Africa’s turn is to last, it must reach the village before the village is emptied by desperation. Roads, democracy, Chinese investment, aid, and climate policy should be judged partly by this standard: do they make it easier for rural Africans to produce, sell, save, insure, and educate? If not, the boom will remain shallow. If so, Africa’s future may be rooted where most Africans still live—in the soil, and in the institutions that help people cultivate it securely.

Diversified GPT

"Africa’s recent economic growth represents a cyclical recovery driven by high commodity prices rather than a structural transformation toward a sustainable, industrialized development miracle."

The phrase “Africa’s turn” is seductive, but it risks mistaking a cyclical upswing for a structural break. Edward Miguel’s essay is admirable in its caution, yet even his cautious optimism may grant too much significance to the growth numbers of 2000–2007. Africa has had good years before. Commodity exporters always look better when commodity prices rise. Postwar economies often rebound rapidly from very low bases. Debt relief can temporarily improve fiscal space. None of this necessarily means that the continent is on the verge of an Asian-style development miracle.

The central question is not whether African per capita income has grown recently. It has. The question is whether the sources of growth are capable of transforming productivity, employment, and state capacity over a generation. On that test, the evidence is thinner.

Miguel’s own account points to the problem: oil, copper, coffee, Chinese demand, and revived infrastructure. These are welcome developments, but they do not amount to industrialization. Indeed, he notes that manufacturing remains as small as it was in 2000 in most African countries. That fact should dominate the discussion. China’s transformation was not driven mainly by selling raw materials at higher prices. Nor was India’s recent acceleration simply a matter of favorable terms of trade. East Asia’s miracle economies moved workers from low-productivity agriculture into higher-productivity manufacturing and services, while building export capacity and learning technologically. Africa’s recent boom has not yet done that.

Natural-resource growth can even postpone the hard work of reform. Oil revenues in Nigeria, Angola, Sudan, Chad, and Gabon may raise GDP, but they can also strengthen patronage states, appreciate exchange rates, and reduce pressure to tax citizens. Governments that do not need to tax broadly often do not need to bargain broadly. They can buy off opponents, fund security services, and neglect the institutions that ordinary businesses require. A copper boom in Zambia or an oil boom in Angola can raise national income while leaving most citizens with little more than inflation and promises.

Even agricultural commodity gains are ambiguous. Higher coffee or cotton prices can help smallholders, but price spikes do not solve low yields, poor storage, limited irrigation, weak extension services, or the lack of rural roads. If the price of coffee falls again, what remains? A development miracle must rest on productivity growth, not simply better prices for the same products.

Democratization, too, should be treated carefully. Miguel is right that Africa is far more politically open than it was in the 1970s and 1980s. This matters profoundly. A free press, opposition parties, and civic activism can expose theft and force governments to respond to citizens. But the record of electoral democracy in poor, divided, institutionally weak states is mixed. Elections can remove bad rulers, but they can also intensify ethnic mobilization and encourage short-term spending. Kenya’s crisis is not an incidental exception to the democratic story. It is a warning that formal political competition can outrun the development of impartial institutions.

The Kenyan case is especially sobering because Kenya was supposed to be one of the safer bets: a diversified economy, an educated population, an energetic press, a strong private sector, and years of relative stability. If Kenya can be pushed to the brink by a disputed tally, what of countries with weaker courts, poorer media, and more militarized politics? It is not enough to count elections. We must ask whether losers believe they can safely lose.

Miguel’s discussion of foreign aid is sensible but incomplete. He argues that aid cannot explain the recent growth revival, and the broad timing supports him. Yet the aid debate often distracts from more important constraints. Whether aid is doubled or halved, African economies will not transform without electricity, ports, roads, enforceable contracts, capable bureaucracies, and healthy workers. Some of these can be supported by aid; many require domestic political settlements. The problem with aid is not merely that it may be wasted, but that it can encourage donors and governments to pretend that project success equals national development.

Consider infrastructure. A paved road to Busia changes commerce immediately. But roads must be maintained; otherwise today’s miracle becomes tomorrow’s pothole. Maintenance is a political problem as much as an engineering one. It requires budgets, procurement discipline, limits on corruption, and citizen oversight. Donors like ribbon-cutting; governments like contracts; nobody likes maintenance. Yet development depends on maintenance.

The China factor is similarly double-edged. Chinese firms are building infrastructure Western donors talked about for years. They are willing to invest in difficult environments. They bring competition, speed, and a practical knowledge of low-cost construction. But Chinese demand also reinforces Africa’s commodity dependence. If Africa exports oil and copper to China and imports manufactured goods from China, that is not a new development model. It is an updated version of the old colonial pattern: raw materials out, finished goods in.

Nor should we assume that global conditions will remain favorable. By May 2008, signs of financial stress in the United States and Europe are already serious. Food and fuel prices are high. A global slowdown could reduce demand for African exports, cut remittances, lower investment, and squeeze budgets. The very openness that helps African economies grow can transmit shocks. A miracle built on world prices can vanish when those prices change.

What, then, would justify optimism? First, sustained agricultural productivity growth. Most Africans still live in rural areas, and improvements in seeds, fertilizer access, water management, land tenure, and roads could raise incomes broadly. Second, regional integration. Many African national markets are too small and landlocked economies face enormous transport disadvantages. Lowering border costs and improving corridors may matter more than another round of donor conferences. Third, power generation. Without reliable electricity, manufacturing will remain a slogan. Fourth, institutionalized political competition, especially credible electoral administration and courts.

Africa may indeed be better positioned today than it was in 1995 or 1985. The end of some wars, the spread of mobile phones, more open politics, and better macroeconomic management are real achievements. But a “turn” implies a durable change in direction. The evidence so far shows recovery, not transformation.

The danger of excessive optimism is not merely analytical. It can encourage complacency. If policymakers believe Africa is already on the runway, they may neglect the structural barriers that have kept planes grounded for decades. Africa’s citizens do not need another mood swing from despair to euphoria. They need the unglamorous foundations of development: farms that produce more, firms that employ more, states that steal less, courts that decide fairly, and roads that stay paved after the donors leave.

Vanilla GPT

"While Africa has experienced significant growth since 2000, this progress remains fragile because it is driven by volatile commodity booms rather than the deep institutional and industrial transformations required for long-term stability."

Edward Miguel is right to resist both Afro-pessimism and easy celebration. The Busia he describes—new roads, supermarkets, mobile phones, and cross-border trade—is real, and it matters. Too many discussions of Africa still proceed as if nothing has changed since the worst years of famine, structural adjustment, and civil war. Yet the central question is not whether Africa has improved since 2000. It has. The question is whether the improvement rests on foundations strong enough to survive bad politics, bad weather, and bad luck.

On that point, I would put more emphasis than Miguel does on the character of growth. A continent can grow rapidly for several years without undergoing a development transformation. Higher oil, copper, coffee, and other commodity prices have filled government coffers and raised some rural incomes. Debt relief and better macroeconomic management have also helped. But commodity booms are not the same as industrialization, and they can even postpone it. They strengthen currencies, draw talent toward government and extractive sectors, and tempt rulers to distribute rents rather than build states. Nigeria and Angola may record impressive growth rates, but if oil revenue does not generate jobs, power, roads, courts, and accountable public finance, then the boom will remain politically dangerous.

The missing link is employment. Africa’s demographic future will be decided not by GDP averages but by whether young people can find work outside subsistence farming and informal petty trade. Miguel notes the disappointing performance of manufacturing, and this deserves to be at the center of the debate. East Asia’s miracle was not simply export growth; it was labor-intensive export growth, accompanied by learning, discipline, and rising productivity. In many African countries, the factory floor remains too expensive because electricity is unreliable, transport costs are high, customs procedures are slow, credit is scarce, and contract enforcement is weak. A paved road to Busia is not a symbol only of progress; it is a reminder of how much of the continent’s potential is still trapped by logistics.

Democracy is part of the answer, but here too caution is necessary. Elections can discipline leaders, expose corruption, and give citizens a peaceful way to remove failed governments. Ghana, Benin, Botswana, Mali, Senegal, South Africa, and others have shown that African constitutional politics is not an illusion. But Kenya’s crisis reveals a harder truth: elections in societies with winner-take-all presidencies can become censuses of ethnic fear. When control of the state means access to land, contracts, jobs, police protection, and impunity, losing an election can look existential. The remedy is not less democracy, but deeper democracy: independent electoral commissions, credible courts, devolved authority, professional police, public broadcasting rules, and constitutional limits on presidential power.

Miguel’s proposal for rapid conflict-prevention support is imaginative and deserves attention. If droughts and commodity collapses predict violence, then aid should arrive before militias form and refugees flee. Botswana’s drought relief program is a useful example of social insurance reinforcing citizenship. Still, outside donors should be humble about their ability to identify and stabilize fragile states. Money sent quickly to governments under stress can be captured quickly as well. Conflict-prevention aid should therefore be transparent, rule-bound, and as automatic as possible: triggered by rainfall, food prices, or export shocks, and delivered through public works, school feeding, local infrastructure maintenance, or cash support where administrative capacity exists. Insurance should strengthen the social contract, not become another discretionary fund for incumbents.

The discussion of China also needs a sharper distinction. China is not simply a new imperialist or a benevolent partner. It is a commercial and strategic actor pursuing oil, minerals, markets, and diplomatic support. African governments can benefit enormously from Chinese roads, telecoms, and construction capacity, especially after decades in which Western investors saw only risk. But “no strings attached” finance is not neutral when the missing strings concern labor rights, environmental damage, procurement transparency, and repression. The answer is not to demand that Africa choose between Beijing and Washington. It is for African parliaments, unions, courts, journalists, and regional bodies to raise the price of bad deals.

Finally, rich countries have obligations that go beyond aid. Miguel is especially persuasive on cotton subsidies and agricultural protection. It is absurd to preach markets to Mali or Burkina Faso while protecting American and European farmers from competition. Trade reform, peacekeeping support, climate adaptation, and serious action on greenhouse-gas emissions may matter more than another round of grand aid pledges.

So is it Africa’s turn? Perhaps—but not in the sense of an inevitable continental takeoff. There is no single African trajectory. Some countries are consolidating peace and growth; others remain trapped by predation and war. The hopeful fact is that the distance between success and failure is increasingly political, not civilizational or geographical. The danger is that recent growth will be mistaken for transformation before institutions, jobs, and resilience have caught up. Africa’s turn will come not when the world discovers its promise, but when African citizens can reliably hold power to account and turn booms into broad-based opportunity.

"Africa’s recent economic growth is a temporary cyclical upturn driven by a global commodity boom rather than the fundamental structural and industrial transformation required for long-term development."

**The Commodity Mirage**

Edward Miguel's essay paints an inviting picture of African renewal, but I worry it mistakes a commodity windfall for structural transformation. The data he himself cites should give us pause: oil prices tripled since 2000, copper soared five-fold, coffee nearly tripled. Of course African growth rates picked up. The harder question is whether anything beneath the price boom has actually changed—and on the evidence Miguel marshals, the answer is mostly no.

Consider what Miguel concedes almost in passing near the end of his essay: "in most African countries, the manufacturing sector remains as small today as it was in 2000." This is an extraordinary admission buried in an otherwise hopeful narrative. Every previous development success story—Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, Malaysia, now China and Vietnam—has been driven by industrial transformation: moving labor out of low-productivity agriculture into higher-productivity manufacturing, and then climbing the technological ladder. Africa has done none of this. It has simply ridden a wave of Chinese demand for the raw materials it happened to be sitting on.

This matters enormously because commodity booms end. The history of resource-dependent economies is a history of boom-and-bust cycles that leave nothing durable behind. Nigeria has been pumping oil since the 1960s, and what does it have to show? Decaying infrastructure, mass poverty, and a kleptocratic political class. Zambia has been mining copper for nearly as long, with similar results. The "resource curse" literature pioneered by Sachs, Warner, Collier, and others suggests that natural-resource booms can actually undermine long-term development by encouraging rent-seeking, distorting exchange rates (Dutch disease), and weakening incentives for governments to build broad-based tax systems accountable to citizens.

Miguel acknowledges the Sudan and Angola problems, but he treats them as exceptions rather than as warnings about the underlying logic. When Chinese state-owned enterprises pay African governments directly for resource extraction rights, they are essentially funding regimes without requiring them to develop productive economies or accountable institutions. The fiscal independence this provides authoritarian elites is precisely the opposite of what produced democratic accountability in Western history, where rulers needed to bargain with productive citizens for tax revenue.

Compare this with the East Asian model Miguel implicitly invokes when he talks about China and India "transforming." Those countries did not grow rich because the world wanted their commodities. They grew rich by making things—first cheap things, then better things—and selling them on global markets. They invested obsessively in education, infrastructure, and technology absorption. South Korea's per capita income was lower than Ghana's in 1960. Today it is around twenty times higher, not because the world started wanting more Korean iron ore, but because Korea built shipyards, steel mills, semiconductor fabs, and Hyundai.

What would it take for Africa to follow that path? At minimum, it would require the kind of industrial transformation that the textile data Miguel cites shows isn't happening. The expiration of the Multi-Fiber Agreement gutted nascent African textile production almost as soon as it began. Chinese competition is now ferocious in exactly the labor-intensive manufacturing sectors that historically have served as the entry point for poor countries. Africa missed the window.

This is not Africa's fault—it is partly an accident of timing, partly a consequence of geography (landlocked countries face brutal transport costs), and partly a result of weak institutions that make manufacturing investment risky. But it means the optimistic story Miguel tells is built on sand. If Chinese growth slows—and no economy grows at 10 percent forever—commodity prices will tumble, African budgets will collapse, and the construction cranes in Nairobi will go silent.

I also think Miguel is too sanguine about the democratization story. Yes, Freedom House scores improved. But the African democratization wave has produced a long list of disappointments: Kenya's stolen 2007 election, Nigeria's fraud-riddled 2007 polls, Ethiopia's crackdown after 2005, Zimbabwe's catastrophe, and the routine extension of presidential term limits across the continent. The institutional substance of democracy—an independent judiciary, professional civil service, neutral security forces—has not kept pace with the procedural form of elections. When elections become merely the periodic means by which one ethnic coalition captures the state's rents, they can deepen conflict rather than channel it.

What I would emphasize, against Miguel's narrative, is that we have been here before. In the 1960s and early 1970s, African growth rates were also impressive. African democratization had its first wave. Optimistic essays were written. Then commodity prices crashed in the late 1970s and 1980s, debt mounted, structural adjustment slashed public services, and a quarter-century of decline ensued. The structural features that made Africa vulnerable then—commodity dependence, weak institutions, ethnic fragmentation, limited human capital—have not fundamentally changed.

I don't want to be entirely dismissive. Cell phone penetration is genuinely transformative, and Miguel is right that it has improved market functioning, especially for smallholders. The end of several civil wars is real progress. Anti-retroviral rollout has saved lives. And the post-Cold War political opening, however fragile, gave space to a vibrant civil society in many countries that did not exist before.

But "is it Africa's turn?" implies a structural break, and I see only a cyclical upturn. The real test will come when commodity prices fall—as they must eventually—and we discover whether anything has been built that can survive the downturn. My fear is that on present evidence, not enough has. Africa's leaders, with help from international partners, should be using the current windfall to invest in education, infrastructure, and institutional reform that could enable genuine industrial transformation. That is happening too rarely. The boom is being consumed, not invested. When it ends, we will likely be writing the same essays we wrote in the 1980s, wondering what went wrong this time.

"The recent economic upturn in Africa is not a durable development miracle but rather a misleading veneer of commodity-driven growth that masks persistent structural inequalities and political fragility."

It is tempting, as Edward Miguel does, to frame Africa’s recent economic upturn as a Lazarus story interrupted by occasional political setbacks. But this narrative, however carefully hedged, is misleading. Africa is not experiencing a miracle, and talk of “Africa’s turn” obscures more than it reveals. What we are seeing is a continent still largely stuck in the same structural constraints that produced the lost decades, with a thin veneer of commodity-driven growth that has enriched a few and left the vast majority untouched. Rather than evidence of a durable transformation, the data point to the persistence of a deeply inequitable economic model that has failed to deliver development for ordinary Africans.

First, celebrate the GDP numbers carefully. Yes, per capita income growth has averaged around 3 percent since 2000, but that average masks enormous variation and is heavily skewed by a handful of oil exporters and mineral-rich states. Remove Nigeria, Angola, and Sudan, and the picture for the remaining forty-plus countries is far less buoyant. More importantly, income gains are concentrated at the top. The United Nations’ own data indicate that the number of Africans living in extreme poverty has actually increased over the same period, once population growth is accounted for. In rural areas where most Africans still live, subsistence agriculture remains barely productive, and land grabs by foreign investors have intensified. The smallholder farmer in Western Kenya or rural Tanzania has seen little of the coffee price boom Miguel mentions, partly because global price increases are swallowed by middlemen and because the terms of trade remain stacked against producers.

Second, the celebration of democratization is premature. The Freedom House index, which Miguel deploys, registers procedural changes — elections, a measure of press freedom — but not the substantive quality of democracy. Across the continent, elections are increasingly held, but the space between elections is often filled with executive dominance, corruption, and the manipulation of ethnic divisions. Kenya’s crisis is not a deviation from some democratic norm; it is a feature of a system in which political power remains the main pathway to economic resources. The power-sharing deal brokered by Kofi Annan may have stopped the killing, but it also institutionalized a system of ethnic arithmetic that postpones the harder work of institutional reform. One need only look at Zimbabwe’s descent from breadbasket to catastrophe to see how electoral farce can coexist with economic implosion, even as international donors praise “democratic” openings.

Third, the China factor, which Miguel treats as a mixed blessing, is in fact a central part of the problem. Chinese investment has undeniably built roads and revived some mines, but it operates on an extractive logic that reinforces Africa’s role as a raw-material appendage. Chinese firms bring their own labor, import inputs, and repatriate profits, creating few backward linkages. The labor conditions at Chambishi are not an anomaly; they reflect a model that depends on cheap, pliable labor and minimal regulation. Worse, Beijing’s non-interference policy directly undermines the very democratic norms that Miguel wants to champion, by propping up abusive regimes in Sudan, Angola, and Zimbabwe. Africa is not getting an Asian-style developmental state; it is getting a new form of dependency, dressed up in the language of South-South solidarity.

The real story, then, is one of continuity more than change. The neoliberal structural adjustment programs of the 1980s and 1990s dismantled state capacity and opened economies to foreign extraction. Today’s commodity boom is enriching the same transnational capital, with African states serving as junior partners. Until there is a fundamental renegotiation of the continent’s position in the global political economy — including debt cancellation, an end to western agricultural subsidies, fairer trade rules, and genuine industrial policy space — talk of an “African turn” will remain a comforting fiction for northern liberals, while the continent’s majority scrapes by as before.

Miguel's essay is a model of cautious optimism, and the caution is well-placed. The risk in this kind of essay is that we mistake favorable tailwinds for structural transformation, and then design policy as if the transformation were real.

Consider the growth story. Miguel rightly notes that sub-Saharan Africa is averaging roughly 3 percent per capita growth since 2000—respectable, but, as he concedes, achieved without the industrial transformation that distinguished the East Asian miracles. Strip out the resource exporters—Angola, Nigeria, Sudan, Equatorial Guinea, Chad—and the picture looks considerably less impressive. The continent's terms of trade have improved dramatically because Chinese demand has bid up oil, copper, and (to a lesser extent) coffee and cotton. This is a price effect, not a productivity effect. Dani Rodrik and others have argued for years that sustained growth requires moving labor from low-productivity agriculture into tradable manufacturing. By Miguel's own admission, that is not happening. African manufacturing as a share of GDP has, if anything, drifted downward since 1990. A commodity boom that ends—and they always end—could expose how little of this growth was structural.

Second, the democratization-causes-growth link deserves more scrutiny than Miguel gives it. The Freedom House aggregate has improved, but much of that improvement reflects the easy transition from outright tyranny to competitive-authoritarian regimes—what Levitsky and Way have described as hybrid systems that hold elections without delivering accountability. Kenya's December 2007 debacle is exhibit A, but it is hardly unique: Ethiopia's 2005 elections, Nigeria's 2007 vote, Uganda's manipulated term limits, Zimbabwe's ongoing horror show. The Sen argument about famines and free press is powerful, but it concerns the prevention of catastrophic policy failure, not the engineering of sustained growth. Botswana and Mauritius—Africa's genuine success stories—were democratic from the start. The question is whether the new democracies will consolidate or hollow out, and on present evidence the latter is at least as likely.

Third, I think Miguel is too sanguine about Chinese engagement, even as he flags the Chambishi mine and Sudan. The deeper problem is not labor practices but the political economy of resource-for-infrastructure deals. When Beijing finances a railway in exchange for mineral concessions, it does so on terms negotiated with incumbent governments that are not obliged to disclose them. This deprives African legislatures and civil society—the very institutions Miguel celebrates—of leverage over their own resource endowments. The "resource curse" literature (Collier, Humphreys, Sachs and Warner) suggests that opaque resource rents tend to corrode institutional development, fund patronage networks, and foster the kind of winner-take-all politics that produced Kenya's electoral crisis in the first place.

Fourth, Miguel's RCPS proposal is intriguing, and I'd endorse expanding it. But the binding constraint on conflict prevention is rarely money; it's the absence of credible commitment devices among rival elites. Botswana's Drought Relief Program works because Botswana already had a functioning state. Exporting the program without exporting the institutions may not yield the same dividend.

None of this is to deny that things are better than they were in 1995. They clearly are. But "Africa's turn" is the wrong frame. The right question is whether the current window—high commodity prices, Chinese capital, debt relief, modest democratization—is being used to build the productive and institutional foundations that will outlast it.

Historical state-building in Europe and North America was tightly linked to the struggle over taxation and representation. Africa’s resource-rich and aid-receiving countries are, perversely, able to govern without building that compact.

If those battles are lost, the current “turn” may be remembered as just another commodity cycle, another false dawn.

He is appropriately cautious about causal claims, acknowledging that "it is impossible to prove a causal link" between democracy and economic growth. But in attempting to explain the turnaround, I think he underweights the role of external macroeconomic forces—specifically, Chinese demand for raw materials—and overweights internal African agency. This matters because if external factors are driving the boom, its durability depends on conditions beyond African control; if internal factors are key, then African reformers deserve more credit and more support.

Consider the timeline. African per capita income growth accelerated dramatically after 2000, precisely when Chinese demand for petroleum and metals began its meteoric rise. Copper prices increased fivefold between 2001 and 2007; oil prices more than tripled. These are not coincidences. Countries like Angola, Nigeria, Sudan, and Zambia—Africa's major resource exporters—benefited enormously from the commodity supercycle. Kenya's recovery, as Miguel describes it, reflects not just domestic reforms but rising coffee prices, new cut-flower exports to Europe, and increased trade with Uganda stimulated by regional economic conditions.

The democratization story is similarly dependent on external factors. Miguel correctly notes that the wave of political opening beginning in 1991 was "inspired by the freedom wave then sweeping the Soviet bloc and the demise of Apartheid in South Africa." African democrats were not acting in isolation; they were responding to global norm cascades and, importantly, to Western pressure on ruling autocrats. The Cold War's end removed the geopolitical cover that had protected dictators from accountability. This is not to diminish the courage of African dissidents like Raila Odinga, but rather to situate their achievements within a broader international context.

If external factors explain much of Africa's recent recovery, what are the implications? First, we should expect volatility. Commodity prices are notoriously cyclical, driven by global demand fluctuations and speculative capital flows. When Chinese growth slows—as it eventually must—African exporters will feel the pain. The 3 percent per capita growth rates Miguel celebrates look less impressive when we consider that they depend on conditions that may not persist. Second, African countries have less control over their destinies than the narrative of democratic reform and good governance would suggest. Structural power in the international economy remains concentrated in wealthy nations and emerging giants like China. African policymakers can improve their domestic environments, but they cannot reconfigure the global trading system or dictate Chinese investment decisions.

I do not mean to dismiss the importance of internal African agency. Miguel's observations about the Busia supermarket, the internet cafés, and the paved road are evidence of genuine entrepreneurial energy and improving infrastructure. The new generation of Kenyan civil society leaders is real and valuable. Democratic institutions, where they have taken root, create possibilities for better governance that did not exist under one-party rule. These gains matter and deserve support.

But support should be calibrated to realistic expectations. The Latin American historical parallel Miguel invokes is apt but sobering. It took Latin American republics most of the nineteenth century to establish the state institutions necessary for sustained development, and even then, many countries remained trapped in oligarchic rule and primary commodity dependency until the twentieth century. Africa has had only fifty years since independence. The comparison suggests that African development will be measured in generations, not years or decades, and that the trajectory will include reversals alongside advances. Kenya's 2007-2008 crisis is not an anomaly; it is a preview of the kind of setback that will recur as Africa struggles toward stability.

The most important implication of recognizing external factors' role is that wealthy nations bear responsibility for creating conditions that allow African development to succeed. This means reducing agricultural subsidies that distort markets, providing aid that strengthens rather than bypasses African states, and engaging with China on the terms of Chinese investment in Africa. Rich-country policies are not just hypocritical; they are consequential. If Africa's turn requires global economic conditions to remain favorable, then the international community has an obligation to work toward that outcome.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The timing of Africa's economic turnaround correlates almost exactly with the meteoric rise in Chinese demand for raw materials and the resulting commodity price boom.
  2. 02Internal democratization was largely a response to global norm cascades and the removal of geopolitical cover for dictators following the end of the Cold War.
  3. 03The dependence on external factors like commodity cycles and Chinese growth means that African nations have less control over their economic destinies than the narrative of domestic reform suggests.
  4. 04Historical parallels with Latin America suggest that building stable state institutions is a multi-generational process that will inevitably involve frequent and severe reversals.
  5. 05Because external factors are so influential, the responsibility for Africa's success lies heavily with wealthy nations to reform their own trade and aid policies.
Diversified MiniMax

"Africa’s recent economic progress is a fragile, extractive boom rather than a sustainable structural transformation, leaving the continent vulnerable to political instability and economic reversal."

Let me offer a dissent from Edward Miguel's largely positive assessment of Africa's trajectory. I want to argue that the 2000-2007 economic boom, however welcome, has obscured deeper structural problems that make sustained development unlikely without fundamental changes in African political economy—and that the Kenya crisis of early 2008 is a symptom of these deeper problems, not an aberration.

The core of my argument is that Africa's recent growth has been extractive rather than transformative. Countries like Angola, Nigeria, and Sudan have benefited enormously from oil revenues; Zambia from copper; Kenya and Uganda from coffee and horticultural exports. But extractive growth follows a predictable pattern: foreign exchange earnings increase, consumption rises, infrastructure improves in boom sectors, and a narrow class connected to resource extraction accumulates wealth. The rest of the economy—manufacturing, services, agriculture for domestic markets—does not transform. Workers do not acquire industrial skills; entrepreneurs do not build competitive firms; states do not develop the tax capacity to fund public goods. When commodity prices fall or demand declines, the boom ends and countries are left with little to show for it except depleted resources and environmental damage.

Miguel acknowledges this pattern when he notes that "industrial transformation is not driving Africa's growth." But he does not fully reckon with its implications. Sustained development requires structural change: the movement of labor from low-productivity agriculture to higher-productivity manufacturing and services, the accumulation of physical and human capital, and the development of institutional capacity to enforce contracts, regulate markets, and provide public goods. None of this is occurring at scale in Africa. The supermarket in Busia is a pleasant sight, but it does not represent industrial transformation; it represents import substitution by foreign retail chains and rising consumption by a narrow urban middle class.

Why has structural transformation not occurred? The reasons are multiple, but I want to highlight one that Miguel touches on but does not emphasize: the persistence of predatory political economies in which ruling elites extract wealth from natural resources and state power rather than building broadly shared prosperity. Angola's ruling MPLA has been described by analysts as a classic extractive regime, using oil revenues to enrich insiders while the majority lives in poverty. Nigeria's oil wealth has generated corruption, conflict, and underdevelopment for decades. Sudan's regime uses oil revenues to fund military campaigns against its own citizens. These are not accidental outcomes; they reflect political choices by elites who benefit from the status quo.

The Kenya crisis reveals how fragile even relatively better-performing countries remain. Kenya in 2007 had experienced a decade of democratic transition, economic growth, and institutional reform. It had a vibrant civil society, an independent press, and an engaged opposition. Yet when election results were disputed, ethnic violence erupted with stunning speed and intensity. Hundreds of thousands were displaced. The economic gains of the previous decade evaporated as investors fled and tourism collapsed. This is what extractive political economy looks like when the thin veneer of institutional stability is scratched.

Miguel places considerable faith in democracy as a driver of better governance and economic performance. The Amartya Sen argument—that democratic accountability improves policy responsiveness—is theoretically compelling. But the Kenyan experience suggests that democracy without complementary institutions and without economic inclusion can be destabilizing rather than stabilizing. Competitive elections in deeply unequal societies with ethnic political mobilization can produce zero-sum conflicts where the stakes of winning and losing are existential. Electoral democracy in such contexts can deepen polarization rather than build consensus.

What would genuine transformation require? First, African countries need to diversify their economies away from primary commodity dependence, but this is extremely difficult in the current international context where wealthy nations protect their markets and China outcompetes Africa in labor-intensive manufacturing. Second, African countries need to build state capacity to regulate markets, collect taxes, and provide public goods, but state-building is itself a long and difficult process that cannot be accelerated by foreign aid alone. Third, African countries need to address the political economy of redistribution to ensure that growth benefits broadly rather than enriching narrow elites. None of these challenges has easy solutions.

I share Miguel's hope that Africa may be turning a corner, but hope is not analysis. The evidence from May 2008 suggests that extractive growth, fragile democracies, and predatory political economies remain the norm rather than the exception. Until these structural problems are addressed, Africa's turns will continue to be followed by setbacks, and the continent's vast human potential will remain tragically underutilized.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current growth is driven by primary commodity exports and resource extraction, which fails to develop the manufacturing sectors or industrial skills necessary for long-term development.
  2. 02The persistence of predatory political economies allows ruling elites to enrich themselves through state power and natural resources rather than building broadly shared prosperity.
  3. 03The 2008 Kenya crisis demonstrates that democratic gains are superficial and can easily collapse into ethnic violence when economic inclusion and robust institutions are lacking.
  4. 04Electoral democracy in deeply unequal and ethnically polarized societies can act as a destabilizing force by creating zero-sum political conflicts.
  5. 05Genuine transformation is hindered by a global economic environment where wealthy nations protect their markets and China outcompetes Africa in labor-intensive manufacturing.
  6. 06State-building and the development of institutional capacity to provide public goods are long-term processes that cannot be bypassed or accelerated simply through foreign aid.
Vanilla MiniMax

"While Africa has made genuine progress, its current recovery is dangerously fragile because it relies on external economic factors and lacks the deep institutional reforms necessary for long-term sustainability."

Miguel's essay presents a carefully balanced assessment of Africa's prospects, acknowledging both the genuine progress of recent years and the profound vulnerabilities that remain. His optimism about democratization and economic growth is warranted, but I would push back on several of his conclusions and emphasize factors he underweights.

First, while the 3 percent annual per capita growth since 2000 is encouraging, we should be cautious about attributing it primarily to internal African reforms. Miguel himself notes that commodity prices and Chinese demand are driving much of this growth. This creates a troubling dependency: Africa's current recovery is partly exogenous, dependent on factors beyond African control. If Chinese growth slows or commodity prices fall, as they did repeatedly during Africa's lost decades, the structural weaknesses Miguel identifies could resurface quickly.

Second, on democratization, the Kenyan crisis deserves more weight than Miguel accords it. While he rightly notes that opposition media and civil society have strengthened, the December 2007 election revealed something fundamental: African elections remain deeply susceptible to ethnic manipulation and institutional capture. The ability of Kibaki's government to steal an election and survive, even with power-sharing, suggests that democratic norms are still thin. Other "success" countries—Côte d'Ivoire, Zimbabwe—have shown similar fragilities. Democratization without strong independent institutions is election cycles without substance.

Third, I am more skeptical than Miguel about Chinese investment's long-term benefits. He acknowledges the Chambishi labor abuses and Sudan's Darfur complicity, but frames these as unfortunate exceptions. I would argue they represent the logical endpoint of Chinese engagement: Beijing explicitly prioritizes resource access over governance and human rights. African governments receiving Chinese money face less pressure to reform, and citizens have fewer levers to demand accountability. The short-term infrastructure gains may come at the cost of longer-term institutional development.

Fourth, Miguel's argument about foreign aid's limited role is compelling, but it understates aid's potential when well-targeted. Botswana's drought insurance worked because it addressed a specific, predictable risk through capable institutions. The key insight is not that aid fails but that aid fails when it ignores institutional context. Simply withdrawing aid because it hasn't worked in corrupt environments concedes the problem rather than addressing it.

Finally, climate change deserves greater urgency. Miguel treats it as a distant threat, but for Sahelian farmers, changing rainfall patterns may be affecting livelihoods right now. The eighty-year horizon in climate models masks immediate adaptations already underway—migration, changed planting patterns, conflict over shrinking arable land.

Africa's progress is real, but sustainability requires addressing the structural weaknesses that caused the long decline: weak institutions, over-reliance on commodities, and poor governance. Growth without these reforms is a foundation built on sand.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Recent economic growth is overly dependent on exogenous factors like Chinese demand and high commodity prices rather than internal structural improvements.
  2. 02The Kenyan crisis demonstrates that democratization remains superficial and highly susceptible to ethnic manipulation and institutional capture.
  3. 03Chinese investment prioritizes resource extraction over human rights and governance, potentially undermining long-term institutional development in exchange for short-term infrastructure gains.
  4. 04The failure of foreign aid is not a result of the medium itself but rather a failure to account for specific institutional contexts and predictable risks.
  5. 05Climate change is an immediate threat rather than a distant one, as changing rainfall patterns are already destabilizing livelihoods and fueling conflict in vulnerable regions.
Vanilla Gemini

"While Africa’s recent economic growth and democratic gains are promising, the continent's reliance on commodity exports and lack of industrialization create a fragile foundation that threatens the sustainability of its development."

Edward Miguel provides a sobering yet cautiously optimistic assessment of sub-Saharan Africa’s recent trajectory. By grounding his analysis in the tangible, albeit fragile, economic renaissance of towns like Busia, he captures the duality of the continent in 2008: unprecedented growth rates and democratic gains, shadowed by the persistent threats of political violence and climate vulnerability. However, while Miguel rightly identifies the structural weaknesses in Africa's current boom—most notably its reliance on commodity exports and a stagnant manufacturing sector—he perhaps understates just how deeply these factors threaten the sustainability of this "development miracle."

Miguel correctly identifies the voracious appetite of a rising China as a primary engine of Africa’s recent economic growth. Surging prices for copper, oil, and agricultural goods have undoubtedly enriched nations like Zambia and Angola. Yet, history is littered with developing regions that mistook a cyclical commodity boom for permanent structural transformation. By Miguel’s own admission, industrial transformation is not driving Africa’s growth, and the manufacturing sector remains as small today as it was in 2000. This is not merely a footnote to the continent's success; it is the central vulnerability of the current narrative.

The nature of Sino-African trade actively reinforces this vulnerability. While Miguel astutely points out the human rights and labor abuses associated with Chinese foreign direct investment, the broader macroeconomic dynamic is equally troubling. China is effectively replicating the colonial economic model: extracting raw, unrefined resources while flooding African markets with cheap manufactured goods. As Miguel notes, the 2005 expiration of the Multi-Fiber Agreement allowed low-cost Chinese factories to outcompete African textile producers. Without the ability to compete in global manufacturing, African economies cannot climb the value-added ladder necessary for long-term, self-sustaining development. When the current commodity super-cycle inevitably cools, economies built solely on resource extraction will face severe contractions.

Given this precarious economic foundation, Miguel’s skepticism toward the "big push" foreign aid models championed by Jeffrey Sachs is well-founded. The historical disconnect between massive aid influxes and sustained economic growth in the 1980s and 1990s suggests that simply pouring more money into weak state institutions is not a panacea.

Instead, Miguel’s proposal for Rapid Conflict Prevention Support (RCPS) stands out as a highly pragmatic, evidence-based policy alternative. By treating aid as an insurance mechanism against the specific economic shocks—such as droughts or commodity price crashes—that precipitate civil violence, the international community could protect fragile states during their most vulnerable moments. The Botswana model of drought relief proves that targeted social safety nets can prevent the desperation that fuels conflict. As climate models predict increasingly severe conditions for the Sahel, RCPS offers a proactive, rather than reactive, approach to regional stability.

Yet, an insurance mechanism, no matter how intelligently designed, is not an engine for growth; it is a shock absorber. For Africa’s current progress to solidify into a true development miracle, the continent must transition away from an over-reliance on primary resource extraction. The international community can assist this transition, as Miguel suggests, by dismantling the hypocritical agricultural subsidies in the United States and Europe that depress global prices and punish African farmers.

Ultimately, the energy and optimism witnessed in Kenya before the tragic 2007 election violence demonstrate the profound resilience of the African people. But unless African nations can leverage this current commodity windfall to build diversified, industrialized economies, this decade's growth may ultimately be remembered as a fleeting boom rather than the dawn of Africa’s turn.

  1. 01The current economic boom is driven by a cyclical commodity super-cycle rather than the permanent structural transformation required for long-term stability.
  2. 02Sino-African trade dynamics risk trapping African nations in a colonial-style economic model that extracts raw resources while stifling local manufacturing through cheap imports.
  3. 03Foreign aid is more effective as a pragmatic insurance mechanism against economic shocks, such as Rapid Conflict Prevention Support, than as a 'big push' for growth.
  4. 04The international community must dismantle hypocritical agricultural subsidies in wealthy nations to allow African farmers to compete fairly and help diversify African economies.
  5. 05Without a transition from resource extraction to diversified, value-added industrialization, the current progress may result in a fleeting boom rather than a lasting development miracle.
