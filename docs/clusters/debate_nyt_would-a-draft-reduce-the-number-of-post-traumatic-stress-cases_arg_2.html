"Reinstating a military draft would reduce cases of post-traumatic stress by spreading the burden of service across a larger population and increasing political accountability for entering and sustaining conflicts."

Yes, a draft would probably reduce some post-traumatic stress cases — not because conscripts are magically more resilient, but because the present system asks too few people to absorb too much war. The most obvious risk factor here is not simply “combat,” but repeated exposure to combat, separation from family, ambiguous missions and the knowledge that after one tour another may be waiting. When a soldier is sent to Iraq, then Afghanistan, then back again, the odds of psychological injury rise. A larger pool of available troops would make it easier to lengthen time at home, reduce stop-loss pressures and avoid sending the same units into the same strain again and again.

But the more important effect of a draft would be political. A war fought by a narrow slice of the country is easier to sustain than a war whose costs are felt in every congressional district and at every elite college. The all-volunteer force has performed with extraordinary skill, but it has also allowed the rest of us to treat war as a kind of background activity. We praise “the troops,” buy them a beer in airports and then return to lives untouched by the decisions made in Washington. That distance makes long wars possible.

If the children of bankers, editors, professors and members of Congress were meaningfully at risk of serving in Afghanistan, the country would debate the war differently. We might still fight when vital interests are at stake. But we would be less likely to drift into open-ended counterinsurgency campaigns with unclear endpoints and modest public attention. In that sense, a draft could reduce PTSD by reducing the wars and deployments that produce it.

There are caveats. A badly designed draft, full of deferments and loopholes, would not spread the burden fairly. Nor would conscription substitute for better mental-health care, better screening, less stigma, and serious attention to traumatic brain injury and moral injury. And some drafted soldiers would suffer terribly.

Still, the current arrangement is not morally neutral. It concentrates risk in military families and communities while permitting the broader public to remain spectators. A draft would not eliminate trauma. It might, however, force the country to ask sooner and more honestly whether a mission is worth the minds and lives it consumes.

"A universal draft would reduce post-traumatic stress only if it forces the American public and political leaders to confront the true costs of war, thereby making unnecessary and prolonged deployments politically unsustainable."

A draft would reduce post-traumatic stress only if it forced the United States to abandon the fantasy of cost-free war. That is why many people oppose it and why it deserves consideration. The all-volunteer force has become a pressure valve for the political system. Presidents can deploy it, Congress can fund it, citizens can revere it, and very few households must reorganize their lives because of it. The result is not merely unfairness. It is strategic laziness.

America’s post-9/11 wars have been fought by a narrow military community: active-duty troops, reservists, National Guard members, contractors and their families. Some have volunteered with clear eyes and pride. That does not absolve the rest of the country. We have effectively created a system in which repeated deployments are the price paid by those who raised their hands first. When the wars last longer than expected, the same people are asked to pay again.

Conscription would make that arrangement harder to ignore. If young Americans across class lines faced the possibility of service, the debate over Afghanistan would become more concrete. What is the objective? Denying Al Qaeda safe haven? Building an Afghan state? Preventing Taliban return? Training local forces? How many years, how many lives, how many minds? These questions are asked now, but not with the urgency they would have if the burden were broader.

Still, I would not defend a draft as a mental-health policy in the narrow sense. Draftees can suffer PTSD. Volunteers can be resilient. A larger army can be misused just as a smaller one can. The military might even become more tempting to policymakers if manpower appeared abundant. Without strict political limits, a draft could feed the very overreach it is meant to restrain.

Therefore the best argument is conditional: a truly universal draft, with no class-based escape routes and with women included, might reduce trauma by making unnecessary war politically unsustainable. A fake draft riddled with exemptions would merely reproduce inequality under a patriotic banner. And no draft should substitute for immediate steps to reduce repeat deployments and improve care.

The country owes soldiers more than applause. It owes them a foreign policy that does not spend their sanity casually. If a draft is what it takes to make civilians understand that, the fault lies not with the soldiers but with the democracy that forgot them.

"Implementing a military draft would reduce the prevalence of post-traumatic stress by spreading combat exposure across a larger population and decreasing the frequency of repeat deployments."

A draft would almost certainly reduce the rate of post-traumatic stress in the U.S. military, for a simple reason: it would dilute combat exposure. The PTSD epidemic emerging from Iraq and Afghanistan is not just about war; it is about repetition. Researchers at Walter Reed and the RAND Corporation have shown that the risk of PTSD rises sharply with each successive deployment, and that troops who serve three or four tours show dramatically elevated rates of depression, suicidal ideation, and stress disorders compared with those who deploy once.

Our current all-volunteer force, with roughly 1.4 million active personnel, simply cannot sustain two simultaneous long wars without grinding the same soldiers through tour after tour. Stop-loss orders, shortened dwell times, and the chronic deployment of National Guard units that were never designed for sustained combat have all become routine. A conscript army of even modestly larger size would allow a single 12-month combat tour to be a soldier's entire wartime experience, rather than the first installment of four. That alone would push PTSD rates down.

There is a secondary mechanism as well. Conscription forces a society to confront the human cost of its wars. When the burden of fighting falls on a narrow professional caste—disproportionately drawn from rural areas, the South, and working-class families—political elites can wage long wars indefinitely without public consequence. A draft tied to middle-class and upper-middle-class households would generate the political pressure that ends wars sooner. Shorter wars mean fewer cumulative trauma exposures, and fewer veterans returning home with shattered nervous systems.

None of this is to romanticize conscription. Vietnam should chasten anyone who imagines a draft as a panacea; conscripts there suffered grievously from PTSD, and a poorly designed draft can produce its own injustices, particularly if deferments favor the privileged. But the specific question here is narrower: would spreading combat across more shoulders reduce trauma per shoulder? The math is unforgiving and the answer is yes. The Bales case is a symptom of a force that has been asked to do too much with too few. The honest remedies are either fewer wars or more soldiers—and a draft is the only politically credible path to the latter.

  3. 03Conscription would create political pressure to end wars sooner by forcing the broader public and political elites to share the human cost of conflict.
  4. 04Shorter wars resulting from increased public scrutiny would lead to fewer cumulative trauma exposures for service members.

"Reinstating a military draft would reduce cases of PTSD by creating the political pressure necessary to shorten wars and prevent the excessive redeployments that cause psychological trauma."

The most important effect of a draft would not be clinical but political, and that political effect would, in turn, save countless soldiers from psychological injury.

Consider the asymmetry of our current arrangement. Fewer than one percent of Americans have served in Iraq or Afghanistan. Congress has not declared war, taxes have not been raised to fund these conflicts, and most citizens have been asked to sacrifice nothing more than the occasional yellow ribbon on a bumper. This is, historically speaking, a bizarre way to run a decade of warfare. It is also why the wars have lasted so long. When the costs of war are externalized onto a small professional caste, the natural democratic check on military adventurism—public outrage—never engages.

A draft would change that calculus instantly. The Vietnam War ended in part because middle-class parents began receiving telegrams. If members of Congress had to imagine their own children deploying to Kandahar, the strategic patience that has kept us in Afghanistan for more than ten years would evaporate within months. Reduced deployments mean reduced trauma. That is the mechanism through which conscription would lower PTSD rates—not by spreading combat across more bodies, but by shortening the wars in the first place.

Critics will say this is undemocratic, that no one should be forced to fight. But there is something equally undemocratic about the current system, in which the burdens of empire are quietly outsourced to those without other options. Recruiting offices in working-class neighborhoods and rural towns hum along while elite universities haven't seen a uniformed undergraduate in a generation. This is not a sustainable model for a republic.

The Bales tragedy is the predictable product of a system that asks too few to do too much for too long. A draft would not eliminate the horror of combat or the wounds it inflicts on the mind. But by making war politically expensive again, it would force the country to decide, openly and honestly, whether the missions it sends soldiers on are worth the human price. That decision, made seriously, is the only real cure for the PTSD epidemic.

The tragic unraveling of Staff Sgt. Robert Bales, accused of murdering 16 Afghan civilians, is not an isolated incident but a symptom of a system grinding its warriors to dust. The question is whether a military draft would reduce such cases of post-traumatic stress. The answer is an emphatic yes, and the mechanism is straightforward: it would break the cycle of perpetual redeployment. Our current reliance on a small, all-volunteer force, coupled with the relentless operational tempo in Iraq and Afghanistan, has meant that the burden of combat falls on an ever-shrinking cohort. Research from the Walter Reed Army Institute of Research has consistently shown that the single greatest predictor of PTSD and other mental health injuries is the frequency and cumulative duration of combat tours. A soldier on his fourth deployment is not just tired; his psychological reserves are depleted, his moral compass frayed, and his sense of connection to a “normal” world severed.

A universal draft would immediately expand the pool of combat-eligible personnel. This would allow the military to impose humane deployment limits—perhaps one combat tour per person, with years of non-deployable duty in between—without sacrificing operational capacity. It would end the cruel necessity of "stop-loss" and involuntary extensions that rip soldiers from their families and treatment cycles. Beyond the operational mechanics, a draft would fundamentally alter the political calculus of war. When the children of CEOs and senators are in the line of fire, the decision to extend a foreign occupation becomes a matter of immediate, visceral national consequence. The public pressure to reduce military exposure and accelerate withdrawals would be immense, ultimately leading to fewer combat environments, and thus fewer traumatized individuals. The draft is the only systemic safeguard against burning out our warriors by forcing us all to share the heat. Furthermore, the social solidarity created by shared national service could help destigmatize the psychological wounds of war, making it easier for returning veterans to seek help. Today, a deep civilian-military divide means that post-traumatic stress is often seen as a strange, individual failure rather than a predictable, widespread consequence of war.

A draft would not be a treatment for PTSD. It would be a democratic alarm bell. And in that sense, yes, it might reduce post-traumatic stress cases—because it would make long wars harder to begin, harder to expand and harder to ignore.

Since 2001, the United States has asked an astonishing amount of a small military population. Many Americans express admiration for the troops, but admiration is not sacrifice. Most citizens have not paid higher taxes for the wars, have not faced the possibility that their children will be called, and have not had to learn the difference between a first deployment and a fourth. The all-volunteer force has performed with skill and courage, but it has also made war politically comfortable for the rest of us.

Afghanistan is now in its second decade. Its rationale has shifted over time: destroying Al Qaeda, denying sanctuary, building Afghan institutions, protecting gains, leaving responsibly. Those may be debated on their merits. A truly fair draft would force that conversation. If college students, bankers’ children, farm kids, immigrants’ children and the children of elected officials all faced the same lottery, the public would demand clearer answers. What is the objective? How long will it take? Why are repeated tours necessary? What will we owe the wounded for the next 40 years? Of course, a corrupt draft would be worse than no draft. Vietnam taught us that deferments and connections can turn “shared sacrifice” into class privilege. A draft that sends the poor while the well-connected find exemptions would not reduce injustice or trauma.

But a universal, honest system would likely shrink our appetite for indefinite war. It would not make combat less frightening, and it would not prevent a single-tour soldier from suffering PTSD. Its effect would be political: fewer casual commitments, fewer repeated deployments, fewer wars treated as background noise. If Americans are unwilling to share the burden, they should be unwilling to impose it indefinitely on the same few.
