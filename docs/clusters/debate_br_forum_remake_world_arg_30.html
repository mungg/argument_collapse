Some Conservative Doubts About Racial Capitalism

I am writing this response from a perspective that does not, I suspect, share much ground with most of Walter Johnson's other respondents. I am a conservative — by which I mean a person who is skeptical of large-scale schemes for social reconstruction, who values inherited institutions, and who is suspicious of historical narratives constructed to support comprehensive political demands. I am also a person who takes the history of slavery seriously and who believes that the moral weight of that history places real obligations on the American polity. I want to engage Johnson's essay seriously, from this perspective, because I think the conservative tradition has something to say about it that the broader conversation is not hearing.

Let me start by conceding what is conceded. The history of African slavery in the Americas is among the great moral failures of human civilization. The political economy of the antebellum United States was built on enslaved labor and on the dispossession of Indigenous peoples. The wealth that the American nation has enjoyed since emancipation was built in significant part on the unpaid labor of generations of African Americans, and the wealth disparities we observe today have real continuities with that history. None of this is in dispute, and a serious conservative response to Johnson cannot evade it.

What I want to dispute is the move from these historical premises to the political and analytical conclusions Johnson draws. Several moves in the essay strike me as problematic, and I will take them in turn.

First, the move from history to total system. Johnson's framework, following Robinson, treats racial capitalism as a comprehensive system that integrates plantation, factory, counting house, and colonial extraction into a single object of analysis. The analytical impulse here is totalizing — it wants to bring everything under a single conceptual umbrella. The conservative impulse is the opposite: to insist on the irreducibility of particulars, the multiplicity of institutions, the resistance of historical reality to schematization. I do not think Johnson is wrong that the various elements of the nineteenth-century world economy were related. But I think he is wrong to suppose that the relationships are best understood through a single integrating concept. The relationships were specific, contingent, often contradictory, and frequently the elements of the system worked against each other (the abolitionist movement in Britain, for instance, was not simply the ideological extension of British capitalism; it operated, in important respects, against the immediate interests of British capital). A more modest framework, one that recognized the connections without insisting on their unity, would do better justice to the historical complexity.

Second, the move from system to standpoint. Johnson argues that the appropriate standpoint of justice is that of the Global South and the "dark and dispossessed." This is a standpoint-epistemological claim, and it bears scrutiny. The claim that the experience of the oppressed gives privileged access to social truth has a long history in radical political theory, but the conservative tradition has reasons to be skeptical of it. Different people, even among the oppressed, draw different conclusions from their experience. The standpoint of the dispossessed in the early twentieth century gave us Du Bois but also Marcus Garvey, Booker T. Washington but also Ida B. Wells. Among the contemporary descendants of the enslaved are conservatives, libertarians, religious traditionalists, and political moderates whose political conclusions diverge significantly from those Johnson's framework would license. Privileging a particular interpretive frame and calling it "the" standpoint of the dispossessed is, at the very least, a contestable move.

Third, the move from standpoint to comprehensive program. The six principal virtues Johnson lists at the end of his essay constitute, in effect, a political program: a politics of redistribution rooted in the experience of the Global South, oriented toward reparations and restorative justice, organized around the entanglement of race and class, attentive to gender and ecology, hostile to the categories of liberal individualism. Each of these elements has been contested in mainstream American politics for as long as such politics has existed, and the framework Johnson articulates does not so much resolve the contestation as decide it by analytical fiat. If you accept the racial capitalism framework, the political program follows. But that is a large "if," and the program does not become more defensible because the framework has been adopted.

Fourth, the move from program to political imperative. Johnson invokes Du Bois on propitious moments and on the cost of failing to act when they arrive. The implication is that we are at or near such a moment and that the costs of failure will be paid in the blood of future generations. This is rhetorically powerful but politically dangerous. The history of comprehensive political programs justified by appeal to historical urgency is not a happy one. The conservative instinct here — to be suspicious of political imperatives that present themselves as beyond the ordinary processes of deliberation, accommodation, and compromise — is, I think, a sound one. Democratic societies do not get better by being told that the time for deliberation is past; they get better by working through their disagreements within institutions designed for that purpose.

This is not to say that nothing should be done. The conservative tradition is capable of acknowledging real wrongs and supporting real redress; the question is the form that redress should take. Edmund Burke, after all, was a critic of British colonial misrule and a defender of the rights of subject peoples; he was not an opponent of redress as such but a critic of the particular form of redress (the wholesale reconstruction of society) that revolutionary politics preferred. A similar distinction applies here. One can take the history of slavery seriously, support targeted policies that address its continuing consequences, and still resist the comprehensive program Johnson's framework licenses.

What would such a moderate position look like? Several elements come to mind.

It would acknowledge the historical wrongs in clear terms and support official measures of acknowledgment — a formal apology, the inclusion of the history in public commemoration, the preservation of historical sites, the funding of museums and educational programs. These are not trivial measures; the absence of clear official acknowledgment continues to shape American racial politics in damaging ways.

It would support targeted policies that address specific continuing harms — discrimination in housing, education, and employment; the disparate operation of the criminal justice system; the persistent underinvestment in historically black communities. These policies have a moral basis that does not require subscribing to the racial capitalism framework; they can be defended on grounds available to any serious participant in American political life.

It would be more cautious about the broader reparations program, not because the underlying claim lacks moral force but because the program's design raises difficulties (about recipients, amounts, mechanisms, political coalition-building) that have not been adequately resolved. A study commission of the sort H.R. 40 has proposed for nearly thirty years would be a useful next step, and the resistance to even that modest measure tells us something about the current political moment.

It would maintain a robust commitment to the universalist tradition of American politics — the tradition of equal citizenship, equal protection, equal opportunity. This tradition has been imperfectly realized, but its commitments are the basis on which civil-rights advances have been made, and abandoning them in favor of a framework that emphasizes group-based historical claims risks losing more than it gains. The conservative defense of universalism is not a denial of group-based history; it is an insistence that the political response to that history should operate through institutions and principles that command broad assent.

It would, finally, take seriously the conservative case for institutional preservation. The American political order, for all its failures, has been the framework within which significant racial-justice progress has been made — emancipation, Reconstruction (incomplete though it was), the civil-rights revolution, the long if uneven development of equal protection doctrine. The framework Johnson articulates is, in its more ambitious moments, suspicious of these institutions and the universalist tradition they embody. A conservative response would urge that progress is more likely through the institutions we have than against them — that the institutions are works in progress that have produced real if inadequate results, and that dismantling them in favor of an alternative framework is more likely to produce political chaos than substantive justice.

I want to register one more thing, which is the worry I have about the political dynamics of the racial capitalism framework in the current moment. The framework, in its more polemical forms, treats the white working class principally as beneficiary of racial capitalism — as a constituency that has been bought off by the "wages of whiteness" and whose interests are aligned against those of black and brown Americans. Whatever the analytical merits of this view, its political consequences in a moment of rising white nationalist politics are not encouraging. The white working class is, by other measures, in the midst of a serious economic and social crisis. Treating that crisis as either irrelevant or as a deserved consequence of racial privilege is not, I think, a politically productive stance. A more capacious framework would recognize that the white working class, too, has been damaged by the broader processes Johnson describes, and that any politics adequate to the moment must speak to its situation as well as to the situation of black and brown Americans.

Let me close with a concession. The argument I am making is a moderate one, and the moderate position in politics is often the position that ends up being run over by events. Du Bois's argument about propitious moments has real force; sometimes incremental change is inadequate, and the conservative instinct toward institutional preservation can be a way of preserving real injustices. I take this seriously. But I would suggest that the test of whether a moment is propitious in Du Bois's sense is not whether a comprehensive analytical framework licenses comprehensive demands; it is whether the political conditions actually support transformative change. The conditions of February 2017 do not, by my reading, support the transformations Johnson's framework envisions. They support, perhaps, the patient work of building toward a future moment when more becomes possible — and that work is best done within institutions and traditions that can sustain it across the long haul.

Whether Johnson's framework or something like the more modest one I have sketched is the right basis for that patient work is the question on which thoughtful people disagree. I have tried to give my own answer with the respect that Johnson's serious essay deserves. The disagreement, I hope, is itself a sign of the kind of public discourse that the moment requires.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The concept of 'racial capitalism' reduces complex, contingent, and often contradictory historical relationships into a single, totalizing analytical system.
  2. 02Claiming a single 'standpoint of the dispossessed' ignores the ideological diversity among oppressed groups and privileges one specific radical interpretation over others.
  3. 03Johnson’s framework functions as a closed political program that decides contested issues of redistribution and reparations by analytical fiat rather than democratic debate.
  4. 04The appeal to historical urgency and 'propitious moments' is a rhetorically dangerous tactic that bypasses the necessary processes of compromise and institutional deliberation.
  5. 05Meaningful redress for historical wrongs is better achieved through targeted policies and the preservation of universalist liberal institutions than through wholesale social reconstruction.
  6. 06The 'racial capitalism' framework politically alienates the white working class by treating them solely as beneficiaries of privilege rather than as a group also damaged by modern economic processes.
