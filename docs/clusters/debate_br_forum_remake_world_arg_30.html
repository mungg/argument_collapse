"The current ecological crisis is not a product of a generalized humanity but is the direct result of the historical logic of racial capitalism, which links the instrumentalization of human beings to the exploitation of nature."

In his essay, Walter Johnson issues a powerful warning against the current trend in the environmental humanities to use the “Anthropocene” as a totalizing framework. By arguing that the history of racial capitalism reveals “the intimate and dialectical relationship between domination and dominion,” Johnson suggests that we cannot understand the instrumentalization of nature without first understanding the instrumentalization of human beings through slavery.

This is a crucial intervention in environmental history, a field that too often dates the start of our current ecological crisis to the invention of the steam engine in Britain, treating the accumulation of atmospheric carbon as the byproduct of a generalized “humanity” (the *anthropos*). Johnson, building on the Black radical tradition, rightly demands we look earlier and elsewhere: to the plantations of the Americas and the expropriation of indigenous lands.

The environment was not a passive backdrop to racial capitalism; it was completely terraformed by it. The plantation system was an ecological catastrophe before it was an industrial one. It involved the clearing of ancient forests, the destruction of indigenous subsistence ecologies, the depletion of soil nutrients through forced monocrop cultivation (first sugar, then cotton), and the massive transfer of biological matter across oceans.

When Johnson quotes W. E. B. Du Bois’s harrowing description of the slaughter of elephants for billiard balls alongside the enslavement of Africans, he is highlighting what we might call the "extractivist gaze." The logic that rendered a human being into "capital" and "labor" is the exact same logic that reduced complex ecosystems into "natural resources." Therefore, the climate crisis we face in 2017 is not the result of human nature, but of racial capitalism.

Any climate justice movement that seeks merely to replace fossil fuels with renewable energy while leaving the global inequalities of the North-South divide intact is bound to fail. A truly emancipatory environmentalism must heed Johnson’s sixth virtue of justice: it must relate the critique of human instrumentalization to environmental extraction. Decolonization and reparations are, ultimately, climate policies.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The environmental humanities must reject the 'Anthropocene' as a totalizing framework because it obscures the specific roles of slavery and indigenous expropriation in driving ecological collapse.
  2. 02The plantation system functioned as an early ecological catastrophe that terraformed the Americas through deforestation, soil depletion, and the destruction of indigenous subsistence ecologies.
  3. 03The 'extractivist gaze' that commodified enslaved people as capital is the same logic that reduces complex ecosystems to mere natural resources.
  4. 04Effective climate justice requires more than a transition to renewable energy; it must incorporate decolonization and reparations to address the global inequalities created by racial capitalism.
