"While Elaine Scarry correctly identifies the democratic and moral failures of 'nuclear monarchy' and the cult of speed, her call for an egalitarian, conventional-only defense is strategically unrealistic and should be replaced by a disciplined, step-by-step reform of existing nuclear and military structures."

Elaine Scarry’s essay is a powerful indictment of nuclear monarchy and executive war-making. It is less successful as a strategic argument about how the United States can safely move from the world we inhabit to the democratic defense she desires. The distinction matters. Moral clarity is indispensable in nuclear policy, but if it is not joined to strategic prudence, it may produce either irrelevance or danger.

Scarry is surely right that nuclear weapons sit uneasily with democracy. They compress decision time, concentrate authority, and threaten destruction on a scale no ordinary political mandate can comprehend. The president’s ability to order nuclear use, especially first use, is a constitutional and moral scandal that has been normalized by familiarity. Citizens have lived for decades under arrangements that would be intolerable if freshly proposed today: launch procedures designed around minutes, war plans capable of killing millions, and public debate limited by secrecy and technical intimidation.

She is also right that “speed” has excused too much. The Cold War trained Americans to think of survival in terms of rapidity: warning, launch, retaliation. That mentality leaked into conventional war powers. Presidents found it easier to act first and consult later. Congress often preferred evasion to responsibility. Now the Bush administration’s doctrine of preemption risks extending emergency logic to preventive war. If Iraq is to be attacked, Congress must authorize it explicitly and on the basis of evidence, not mood. On these points Scarry’s warning is timely.

But her long-term prescription—an egalitarian model of defense, conventional weapons only, perhaps a restored draft—underestimates the strategic dilemmas that made nuclear weapons politically durable. The United States cannot abolish nuclear danger by abolishing its own arsenal. Russia still possesses thousands of nuclear weapons, many in a state of uncertain security amid economic strain. China has a smaller arsenal but is modernizing. India and Pakistan tested nuclear weapons in 1998 and have already confronted each other in crises. Israel is widely believed to possess nuclear weapons, though it does not acknowledge them. Iraq has sought weapons of mass destruction; North Korea remains opaque and troubling. In such a world, unilateral American nuclear disarmament would not necessarily produce democratic safety. It might produce vulnerability, allied panic, proliferation, or conventional wars fought under another power’s nuclear shadow.

This does not mean the status quo is acceptable. It means reform must proceed through a realistic arms-control agenda. The first goal should be to reduce the role of nuclear weapons in American strategy. A no-first-use policy deserves serious consideration. If the sole legitimate purpose of nuclear weapons is to deter nuclear attack, then American doctrine should say so. Threatening nuclear use against chemical, biological, or conventional threats blurs categories and encourages others to seek nuclear deterrents of their own. No-first-use would not abolish nuclear danger, but it would narrow presidential discretion and strengthen nonproliferation norms.

Second, the United States and Russia should take weapons off hair-trigger alert. The gravest democratic defect in nuclear policy is not only destructive scale but decision compression. If leaders believe they have minutes to decide whether warning data are real, constitutional deliberation is impossible and accidental war becomes more plausible. De-alerting measures—removing warheads from some missiles, changing launch procedures, lengthening readiness times, improving transparency—would reduce the tyranny of speed Scarry rightly condemns.

Third, command authority should be broadened without making retaliation impossible. Requiring concurrence by another constitutional officer, perhaps the secretary of defense and congressional leaders in non-immediate circumstances, could reduce the danger of a solitary decision. For first use, Congress should legislate strict limits. The president must retain the capacity to respond to an actual nuclear attack, but initiating nuclear use should not be treated as an ordinary commander-in-chief power.

Fourth, deep bilateral reductions should continue, but with verification. The Moscow Treaty signed this year points toward lower deployed strategic warheads, but numbers alone are not enough if warheads are stored rather than dismantled and verification is weak. Arms control must address tactical nuclear weapons, fissile material security, and transparency. Cooperative threat reduction programs that help secure Russian materials may do more to prevent nuclear terrorism than new nuclear doctrines or missile defenses.

Fifth, the United States should strengthen global nonproliferation by reducing hypocrisy. Scarry is right that other countries notice when Washington treats nuclear weapons as essential for itself and intolerable for others. But the answer is not immediate abolition; it is visible movement toward reduced reliance, ratification and observance of test-ban norms, support for inspections, fuel-cycle controls, and security assurances to states that remain nonnuclear. Nonproliferation is a bargain. If nuclear states do not move toward disarmament, the bargain frays.

Where Scarry’s argument falters most is in the analogy between September 11 air defense failures and missile defense or nuclear command. The failure to intercept Flight 77 should humble claims about perfect defense, especially against fast-moving threats and ambiguous data. It should make us skeptical of extravagant promises for national missile defense, particularly given decoys and countermeasures. But it does not prove that all rapid military systems are illusions. A domestic airliner with its transponder off inside American airspace posed a different detection and command problem from a ballistic missile launch observed by satellites and radars—though missile warning has its own terrible risks of error. The better conclusion is not that speed is always fake, but that systems built around speed tend to evade democratic judgment and may fail in unanticipated scenarios.

Flight 93, similarly, should inspire but not govern nuclear strategy. The passengers could deliberate because the threat was physically present and the relevant lives were chiefly their own. Nuclear policy concerns threats across continents, alliance commitments, adversary perceptions, and catastrophic uncertainty. Citizen deliberation is essential at the level of doctrine and authorization; it cannot occur at the moment of launch. Therefore the democratic task is to structure policy in advance so that no president faces—or claims to face—a civilization-ending decision in minutes. That supports de-alerting and no-first-use more directly than it supports abolition by unilateral renunciation.

The draft question is also more complicated than Scarry allows. A draft might make citizens more attentive to war by spreading risk beyond volunteers and military families. It might also create a larger military instrument for presidents to use and coerce young people in the name of civic equality. The Vietnam experience suggests that conscription can generate democratic resistance, but at enormous human cost and with inequities of its own. A better reform would be to require Congress to vote clearly on wars, to pay for wars honestly rather than through fiscal evasion, and to support veterans visibly. Shared burden need not always mean compulsory service.

On Iraq, Scarry’s framework counsels skepticism. Preventive war against a regime suspected of weapons programs raises precisely the problem of concentrated judgment. Intelligence will be uncertain; intentions are inferred; postwar consequences are unpredictable. If the administration believes force is necessary, it must persuade Congress and the public with evidence. A broad resolution allowing the president to decide later would be an abdication. Moreover, the United States should exhaust inspections and multilateral pressure if they can realistically contain the threat. War may be justified in some circumstances, but it should not be smuggled in under the emotional authority of September 11.

The strategic realist and the democratic critic need not be enemies. In fact, they need each other. Realism without democratic accountability becomes managerial fatalism: experts assuring citizens that terrible powers are necessary and too complex for public debate. Democratic moralism without strategic discipline risks gestures that do not make people safer. The right course is a disciplined reduction of nuclear danger: no-first-use, de-alerting, verified reductions, stronger nonproliferation, secured materials, constrained presidential authority, and congressional responsibility for war.

Scarry has opened the right conversation. We should not answer it with either complacency or utopian impatience. Nuclear weapons are indeed monarchic; the task is to dismantle the monarchy step by step, without inviting the chaos that monarchs always claim only they can prevent.
