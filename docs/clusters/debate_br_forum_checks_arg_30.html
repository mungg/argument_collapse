Diversified Gemini

"While the American constitutional system's veto points often hinder progress, progressive federalism remains a vital and necessary tool for protecting democratic norms and civil liberties against an authoritarian federal government."

In "The Dead End of Checks and Balances," Lisa L. Miller delivers a blistering and largely persuasive critique of the national American constitutional order. It is hard to dispute her assertion that our federal system is choked by veto points that disproportionately favor wealthy interests and reactionary minorities. However, in her zeal to advocate for a national, majoritarian democracy, Miller tragically dismisses one of the most vital and effective tools currently available to safeguard democratic norms and protect vulnerable populations: progressive federalism.

Writing in May 2025, with the Trump administration actively deploying the executive branch to assault environmental regulations, immigrant communities, and political opponents, Miller's dismissal of state-level resistance is not just theoretical—it borders on political malpractice. She rightly points out the ugly history of "states’ rights" as a shield for slavery and Jim Crow segregation. But to allow the ghosts of John C. Calhoun and Strom Thurmond to dictate our strategic choices today is to surrender a crucial battlefield to an increasingly authoritarian federal government.

Miller waves away the efforts of governors like California’s Gavin Newsom, arguing that embracing the constitutional authority of state governments is "itself an obstacle to effective governance" and perpetuates inequality. She argues that federalism "has long served as a veto of the national public interest." But this assumes that the national government is currently the engine of the public interest. What do we do when the national government has been captured by an administration committed to kleptocracy and the rollback of fundamental rights?

In 2025, progressive federalism is not an abstract theory; it is the frontline of democratic defense. When the federal judiciary overturned *Roe v. Wade*, it was state constitutions and state legislatures that immediately became the sole protectors of reproductive autonomy for millions of women. When the current administration’s EPA guts emissions standards to reward fossil fuel executives, it is states like California, New York, and Washington that utilize their market power and regulatory authority to keep green energy transitions alive. When the federal government weaponizes ICE to terrorize communities, it is sanctuary cities and state attorneys general who throw sand in the gears of the deportation machine.

Miller laments that relying on federalism creates "state-level variation (and hence inequality) in government standards." She is entirely correct. A woman in Texas does not have the same rights as a woman in Massachusetts; a transgender youth in Florida faces a radically different legal landscape than one in Minnesota. This inequality is abhorrent. But Miller's implicit alternative—waiting for a mass national movement to achieve unified control of the federal government, abolish the filibuster, reform the Supreme Court, and enact sweeping national standards—is a generational project. We cannot sacrifice the rights and safety of millions of people residing in blue and purple states today on the altar of a utopian, centralized, majoritarian tomorrow.

Furthermore, Miller profoundly misunderstands the dynamic relationship between state action and national policy. State-level reforms are rarely a "veto" on the national public interest; historically, they are the incubator for it. The majoritarian national policies Miller champions—such as minimum wage increases, environmental protections, and LGBTQ+ rights—did not spring fully formed from the minds of a national electorate. They were fought for, tested, and normalized at the state level first. States like Massachusetts proved that near-universal healthcare was viable, laying the groundwork for the Affordable Care Act. State-level action on marriage equality forced a national reckoning. In a system paralyzed by federal veto points, states are the only functioning laboratories of democracy we have left.

Miller's proposed solution involves massive constitutional reforms: eliminating the Electoral College, ending lifetime judicial appointments, and expanding the House. These are laudable goals, but they require amending the Constitution, which demands supermajorities in Congress and ratification by three-fourths of the states. How does Miller propose we achieve these national reforms while simultaneously abandoning the levers of state power? The right wing understood decades ago that capturing state legislatures was the key to dominating national politics, which is why they invested heavily in gerrymandering and local elections. Surrendering the power of federalism to the right out of a misplaced historical guilt regarding "states' rights" is a recipe for permanent minority rule.

Miller is correct that a decentralized system allows for local tyrannies. But in 2025, the threat of a national tyranny is far more acute. The Trump administration and its allies at the Department of Government Efficiency are not trying to decentralize power; they are actively trying to centralize it in the executive branch, overriding state policies that displease them. The true defense against this creeping authoritarianism is not to preemptively disarm ourselves by rejecting federalism.

We must embrace a muscular, unapologetic progressive federalism. This means utilizing state attorneys general to tie up federal overreach in litigation. It means blue states forming interstate compacts to purchase essential medications, regulate tech monopolies, and protect data privacy when the federal government refuses to do so. It means utilizing the fiscal power of state economies to pressure corporations that capitulate to federal authoritarianism.

Checks and balances may well be a "dead end" for passing the ideal progressive agenda at the national level. But when the national government has become an active threat to civil liberties and the rule of law, the friction of federalism is not a bug; it is a vital survival mechanism. We must use the tools we have, rather than the tools we wish we had. State power is a fortress; in dark times, it is foolish to leave the gates open merely because the fortress was built by flawed architects.
