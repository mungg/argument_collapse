"The Democratic Party must abandon its reliance on ideological moderation and technocratic caution in favor of a bold, anti-establishment strategy centered on anti-corruption and genuine mobilization."

Bonica and Grumbach are right about the central strategic mistake: Democrats have treated moderation as if it were a machine that converts caution into votes. The machine is broken. It may never have worked quite as well as its admirers claimed, but even if it did in the age of split-ticket voting, local newspapers, and weaker party identities, that is not the world Democrats now inhabit. The party cannot spreadsheet its way out of authoritarianism by shaving down every sentence until it offends no one and inspires almost no one.

The most important contribution of the lead essay is not the claim that progressives always win. Bonica and Grumbach explicitly avoid that claim. Their point is more modest and more devastating: the confident case for moderation rests on an outdated model of voters and elections. In that model, citizens possess stable preferences, compare candidate platforms, and reward the candidate nearest to them ideologically. In today’s politics, many citizens are instead sorting through identity, distrust, resentment, media narratives, leader attachments, and a pervasive sense that the system is rigged. A candidate who says, in effect, “I am exactly 7 percent less alarming than my coalition’s activists,” is not obviously solving any of those problems.

The old moderation story also mistakes absence for appeal. When a Democrat avoids talking about climate, immigration, policing, or trans rights, commentators often describe that as a move toward the center. But voters do not experience politics as a candidate’s internal memo. They experience it as a noisy field of signals. If Republicans spend hundreds of millions defining Democrats as dangerous radicals, and Democrats respond by whispering about bipartisan tax credits, the electorate does not necessarily conclude that Democrats are moderate. Many conclude that Democrats are evasive, weak, or guilty as charged.

That is why the essay’s emphasis on authenticity matters. A candidate’s position is not merely a dot on an ideological line. It is a claim about who they are, whom they fight for, and what kind of risks they are willing to take. Voters may not know the details of a health care plan, but they often have a strong sense of whether a politician sounds like a real person or a consultant-generated product. Strategic retreat can easily read as contempt: “We know what we believe, but we have been advised not to say it around you.”

The Harris campaign’s moderation cannot bear all the explanatory weight some left critics place on it. Incumbent-party fatigue, inflation, international turmoil, media fragmentation, sexism, racism, and the strange dynamics of a late nomination all mattered. But Bonica and Grumbach are not saying moderation caused the defeat. They are saying it failed to prevent it, and that matters because moderation had been sold as the safe harbor. If democracy was on the ballot, and if Democrats adopted the safer, more popular, less ideologically threatening style recommended by the popularists, then the result should at least make the theory’s proponents less certain.

The anti-corruption frame they propose is powerful because it cuts beneath the left-center argument. Americans may disagree sharply about taxes, guns, immigration, and schools, but they overwhelmingly suspect that the powerful play by different rules. This is not a fringe intuition. It is reinforced every time a Supreme Court justice accepts lavish gifts from billionaires, every time members of Congress trade stocks while receiving sensitive briefings, every time the wealthy seem to buy access and then call the resulting system “freedom.” Anti-corruption politics can connect the material and the moral: high prices, corporate concentration, tax avoidance, regulatory capture, and democratic decay are not separate problems but symptoms of government serving insiders.

Yet anti-corruption cannot be only a slogan. Here Bonica and Grumbach are especially persuasive. Democrats cannot credibly attack a rigged system while relying on the same lobbyists, consultants, dark money, and donor courtship that make politics feel rigged. The party does not need purity in the adolescent sense; politics is coalition-building, not monastic life. But it does need visible sacrifices. Ban congressional stock trading. Refuse the most corrupting forms of fundraising. End manipulative recurring-donation schemes. Make donor access less central to advancement. Recruit candidates who have lived the consequences of policy rather than merely studied them at elite institutions. If Democrats want to be believed as an anti-establishment party, they must behave in ways that cost the establishment something.

The turnout argument also deserves emphasis. Democrats have spent years speaking as though the marginal voter is always a white suburban moderate who reads both parties’ websites and chooses the less extreme plan. That voter exists, but the fixation on this archetype has warped the party’s imagination. The missing voter is often younger, poorer, less trusting, less likely to see politics as responsive, and more likely to think both parties are compromised. Such citizens are not mobilized by being told that Democrats have adopted the least controversial available position. They are mobilized, if at all, by a sense of stakes, solidarity, and possibility.

This is where the democracy message needs repair. “Democracy is on the ballot” was true, but it was too abstract when separated from everyday corruption and domination. For many citizens, democracy does not mean a civics-textbook procedure; it means whether anyone in power can be forced to listen. A democracy message that sounds like “protect institutions” will fail if people experience those institutions as indifferent or predatory. A democracy message that says “the billionaires, judges, lobbyists, and authoritarian politicians are trying to take away your power, and we will stop them” has a better chance.

Still, I would add one caution to Bonica and Grumbach’s argument. Mobilization without persuasion is not enough. The electorate Democrats need includes irregular voters, but it also includes citizens who voted Republican, citizens who distrust both parties, and citizens whose cultural instincts are not progressive. The anti-corruption frame works precisely because it can speak across those lines. But it will fail if it becomes merely progressive politics with a new label. To redraw the battlefield, Democrats must make room for people who are angry at concentrated power but not fluent in liberal cultural language. That means a politics less controlled by professional-class norms and more grounded in workplaces, churches, unions, local associations, veterans’ groups, tenant organizations, and community institutions.

The point is not to replace moderation with maximalism. It is to replace defensiveness with purpose. A party facing authoritarianism cannot afford to be reckless, but neither can it afford to be bloodless. The question should not be, “How do we move to the center?” It should be, “What conflict can we make clear enough that people know which side they are on?” Authoritarians thrive by turning politics into spectacle, grievance, and loyalty. They are not defeated by technocratic caution. They are defeated when democratic forces build a broader, deeper loyalty: to one another, to equal citizenship, and to the belief that public power should serve the many rather than the protected few.

Bonica and Grumbach’s essay is valuable because it asks Democrats to stop confusing risk avoidance with strategy. The risk of saying too much is real. But the risk of saying too little has already been tested. It produced a politics unable to match the emotional force of the authoritarian right. If Democrats want to defend democracy, they must offer more than competent management of a distrusted status quo. They must become the vehicle for unrigging it.

"To defeat authoritarianism, Democrats must move beyond the false choice of ideological moderation and instead embrace a robust economic populism that confronts concentrated wealth and corporate power."

The argument over moderation often begins in the wrong place. It asks whether Democrats should be more centrist or more progressive, as if voters were waiting to reward the party for finding the right ideological coordinate. Bonica and Grumbach rightly reject that picture. But the deeper problem is not merely that moderation fails to inspire. It is that the kind of moderation usually recommended to Democrats is a class project. It protects the comfort of donors, consultants, lobbyists, and professional elites while asking working people to accept a politics of small adjustments to an economy that has treated them brutally.

If Democrats want to defeat authoritarianism, they need to understand why authoritarianism has become attractive. It is not only racism, misogyny, Christian nationalism, or disinformation, though all of these matter. It is also the collapse of faith that democratic government can discipline capital and improve ordinary life. For decades, large parts of the country have experienced plant closures, wage stagnation, opioid addiction, hospital consolidation, private equity predation, housing insecurity, debt, and the slow disappearance of local institutions. The Democratic answer has too often been a tax credit, a credential, a pilot program, or a lecture about the future economy. The Republican answer has been scapegoating and domination. One answer is inadequate; the other is dangerous. But danger can beat inadequacy.

That is why anti-corruption is promising, but only if it becomes economic populism with teeth. Corruption should not be defined narrowly as bribery, gifts, and insider trading. Those scandals matter because they are visible signs of a deeper order: government captured by wealth. A billionaire giving luxury vacations to a justice is corrupt. So is a tax code that lets billionaires borrow against appreciating assets while workers pay every paycheck. So is a health care system in which insurers profit by denying care. So is a housing system that lets corporate landlords and speculative capital squeeze tenants. So is a labor law regime that allows employers to violate organizing rights with minimal consequences. If Democrats make anti-corruption only about bad apples, they will miss the orchard.

The lead essay’s critique of popularism is especially important here. Polling can tell a campaign which words voters currently like, but it cannot supply a theory of power. Voters may say they dislike “socialism,” support “cutting waste,” oppose “big government,” and favor “Medicare negotiating drug prices.” These answers are not a coherent program; they are sedimented fragments of decades of political conflict. Leadership means organizing those fragments into a story. The right has done this relentlessly: your life is hard because immigrants, liberals, feminists, bureaucrats, and globalists are taking what is yours. Democrats need an equally clear and more truthful story: your life is hard because concentrated wealth has rigged the rules, and authoritarian politicians are using hate to keep you from noticing who profits.

This story can unite the material and the democratic. Authoritarianism is not only a threat to norms; it is a tool of plunder. When an administration attacks civil servants, intimidates prosecutors, weakens independent agencies, and demands loyalty from law enforcement, it is not simply indulging one man’s ego. It is removing obstacles to impunity. The same movement that rails against “elites” empowers billionaires, crypto speculators, anti-union corporations, polluters, and monopolists. Democracy is the mechanism by which ordinary people can fight back. That should be the message.

Would such a message be “left”? In policy terms, yes. It implies stronger unions, higher taxes on wealth, aggressive antitrust, public investment, universal social benefits, stricter ethics rules, and a government willing to confront corporate power. But politically it need not be a boutique ideology. Many working-class voters who would never call themselves progressive already believe the economy is rigged. They are not waiting for a seminar on democratic socialism. They are waiting for someone who will name the villains they can see: employers who steal wages, hospitals that sue patients, landlords who raise rent, corporations that raise prices while buying back stock, politicians who trade on inside information, and judges who accept billionaire favors.

The difficulty is credibility. Democrats cannot run as tribunes of the working class while appearing to be managed by affluent professionals and financed by the winners of the current economy. The party’s cultural style often communicates distance from the people it claims to represent. Its campaign apparatus is expensive, consultant-heavy, and donor-oriented. Its leaders celebrate unions but still treat working-class candidates as novelties. Its policy language is frequently incomprehensible to anyone without a graduate degree. This is not a problem that can be solved by a better slogan. It requires a different party infrastructure.

That infrastructure should begin with labor. The most effective anti-authoritarian institution in American life is not a think tank, a super PAC, or a viral influencer. It is a union. Unions build cross-racial solidarity, increase political participation, train leaders, and give people a direct experience of collective power. They also create a language of conflict that is neither technocratic nor nihilistic: boss versus worker, solidarity versus exploitation, dignity versus domination. If Democrats are serious about turnout and anti-establishment credibility, they should make labor organizing central to their democracy strategy.

This means more than praising unions at rallies. It means passing labor law reform when power allows, using executive authority to support organizing, putting union members in office, refusing to reward union-busting donors, and treating strikes as major democratic events. It also means learning from the new labor militancy of teachers, nurses, autoworkers, warehouse workers, graduate employees, baristas, and service workers. These campaigns often succeed because they connect workplace issues to community concerns: safe staffing, good schools, reliable services, dignity for caregivers. That is the kind of politics Democrats need.

The authors are right that turnout is central. But people do not turn out merely because a campaign asks. They turn out when they are embedded in networks that make politics meaningful. The decline of unions, local parties, churches, newspapers, and civic associations has left many citizens isolated before the algorithmic rage machine. A presidential ad cannot compensate for the disappearance of year-round organization. Democrats need precinct captains, shop stewards, tenant organizers, immigrant-rights groups, parent networks, and local candidates who remain after the election ends. Mobilization is not a message; it is a social relation.

The moderation debate obscures this because it focuses on the candidate as product. Should the product be more centrist or more progressive? Should the packaging emphasize this issue or that one? But working-class politics is not branding. It is taking sides in conflicts that structure people’s lives. When Democrats refuse to take sides clearly, the right fills the vacuum with cultural enemies. A party that will not confront landlords, insurers, monopolists, and union-busting employers will find itself arguing endlessly about whether it has moved far enough away from activists.

There are risks. Economic populism can be reduced to empty rhetoric. It can ignore race, gender, and immigration. It can romanticize a narrow image of the working class. It can become protectionist nostalgia. Democrats must avoid all of that. A multiracial working-class politics must include care workers, migrants, public employees, gig workers, disabled people, and people criminalized by the state. It must defend civil rights not as side issues but as protections against domination. The boss, the landlord, the abusive police officer, the predatory lender, and the authoritarian president are not identical, but they all exercise power without accountability.

Bonica and Grumbach end by saying Democrats need to fight hard and smart. Fighting smart means recognizing that authoritarianism feeds on despair and status loss, but also that it governs for the powerful. The answer is not a more moderate ideology. It is a more grounded democracy: one that gives people institutions through which they can act, not merely candidates for whom they can vote.

The party should stop asking whether to stand five or fifteen degrees from the center. It should ask whether it is willing to stand with workers against concentrated power. If the answer is no, no amount of message discipline will save it. If the answer is yes, anti-corruption can become more than a frame. It can become the beginning of a democratic reconstruction.

"To defeat authoritarianism, Democrats must move beyond simple anti-corruption rhetoric and directly confront the alliance between capital and hierarchy by democratizing economic power."

Bonica and Grumbach rightly argue that Democrats cannot defeat authoritarianism by moderating their way into respectability. But the lead essay could go further in naming the social force behind the crisis: authoritarianism in the United States is not merely a style of politics or a threat to institutions. It is a project for protecting hierarchy and capital from democratic accountability. Its corruption is not incidental. It is constitutive.

The temptation in liberal analysis is to separate “democracy issues” from “economic issues.” On one side are elections, courts, norms, and the rule of law. On the other are wages, prices, health care, housing, taxes, and unions. Bonica and Grumbach reject this division by proposing anti-corruption as a bridge. That is a good start. But the bridge should be stronger: democracy is the means by which ordinary people can contest economic domination. When economic power becomes too concentrated, it seeks to insulate itself from democracy. Authoritarian politics is one method of insulation.

This is visible in the alliance between Trumpism and plutocracy. Trump’s rhetoric is anti-elite; his governing coalitions empower the rich, deregulators, fossil fuel interests, union busters, private equity, tax cutters, and ideological judges hostile to the administrative state. The movement’s mass appeal comes from resentment and identity, but its policy payoff flows upward. That combination is not accidental. Scapegoating supplies emotional compensation while the wealthy receive material compensation. The people are given enemies; the donors are given law.

Moderation fails because it refuses to confront this structure. A moderate Democrat may promise competent management, bipartisan gestures, and incremental relief. But if voters believe the economy is rigged, managerial liberalism sounds like maintenance of the rigging. Worse, when Democrats court corporate donors and celebrate fiscal restraint while inequality persists, they appear to be junior partners in the same order. Authoritarians then pose as the only alternative to a corrupt consensus.

The answer is not simply “move left” on a one-dimensional spectrum. It is to democratize power. That means labor power, tenant power, consumer power, public power, and political power. It means making the economy less dependent on the preferences of billionaires and less vulnerable to blackmail by mobile capital. It means treating unions as democratic institutions, not interest groups. It means public options where private gatekeepers exploit necessity. It means antitrust enforcement that understands monopoly as political power. It means taxes that reduce oligarchy, not merely raise revenue. It means campaign finance and lobbying reforms that prevent wealth from converting too easily into rule.

Bonica and Grumbach’s anti-corruption frame should therefore be class-conscious. Congressional stock trading is outrageous not only because it looks bad, but because it symbolizes a legislature more attuned to asset owners than workers. Supreme Court gifts are outrageous not only because of ethics forms, but because they reveal a judiciary socially embedded with the billionaire class. Corporate PACs are a problem not only because money influences votes, but because they structure politicians’ sense of whose approval matters. Corruption is the everyday merging of public authority and private wealth.

A class-conscious democracy strategy could also address the right’s appeal to working-class voters. Many voters drawn to Trump are not yearning for free-market orthodoxy. They want protection, recognition, and revenge against forces they cannot control. The right gives them cultural revenge while leaving economic domination intact. Democrats should offer democratic protection instead: strong unions, industrial policy tied to labor standards, universal benefits, rural investment, debt relief, affordable housing, and public enforcement against corporate abuse. The message should be: you do not need a strongman to fight for you; you need power of your own.

This requires breaking with donor liberalism. The Democratic Party cannot convincingly run against oligarchy while treating billionaires as enlightened partners whose interests must be delicately managed. There will always be wealthy individuals who support democracy, and in a crisis one should not reject allies foolishly. But there is a difference between accepting support and allowing donor comfort to define the agenda. If the party’s economic program is constrained by what private equity, Silicon Valley, Wall Street, crypto investors, or real estate interests will tolerate, anti-corruption rhetoric will ring hollow.

The authors call for rejecting corporate PAC money and deceptive fundraising practices. Good. Add to that: refuse union-busting consultants and vendors; bar party leaders from becoming lobbyists immediately after leaving office; deny committee influence to members who trade individual stocks; democratize convention and committee processes; support public financing; and make labor and community organizations, not donor networks, the core of candidate recruitment. These reforms would not purify politics, but they would signal a shift in power.

Critics will warn that this agenda is too left-wing. But many of its elements are popular because they address obvious unfairness. Voters may distrust “big government,” but they also hate being cheated by insurers, landlords, banks, employers, and monopolies. The question is whether Democrats can speak in a language of freedom rather than bureaucracy. Freedom is not only freedom from government; it is freedom from domination by bosses, creditors, monopolists, abusive spouses, bigoted officials, and presidents who place themselves above law. A democratic left should reclaim freedom.

Race and gender must be central to this class politics, not appended awkwardly. Economic domination in America has always been racialized and gendered. Care work is underpaid because women’s labor is devalued. Black wealth has been suppressed through exclusion, predation, and state violence. Immigrant workers are exploited through legal precarity. Trans people and disabled people face employment and housing discrimination. A class politics that ignores these realities will become nostalgic and exclusionary. A rights politics that ignores material power will become thin and elite. The task is to join them.

Bonica and Grumbach are also right that turnout depends on belief. People participate when they think politics can change the balance of power. Incremental policy lists do not create that belief. Conflict does. Democrats must be willing to identify opponents: not ordinary Republicans as irredeemable enemies, but the powerful actors who profit from minority rule and social division. Name the corporations, donors, judges, lobbyists, media owners, and politicians who benefit. Then offer people a way to fight them collectively.

There is danger in this. Class politics can be demagogic if it abandons truth or pluralism. But liberal fear of conflict has left the field to worse demagogues. Democracy is not the absence of conflict; it is the organization of conflict under equal rules. Authoritarianism arises when conflict is channeled into obedience to a leader and hatred of vulnerable groups. Democratic class politics channels conflict upward, toward accountable power.

The lead essay ends with a call to fight hard and smart. Fighting smart means recognizing that authoritarianism is not defeated by defending the pre-Trump status quo. That status quo produced too much inequality, too much impunity, and too little democratic control. If democracy means a return to a system where donors write the menu and voters choose between small variations, many people will shrug as it erodes. If democracy means the chance to govern the economy in the public interest, it can inspire.

Democrats should stop asking how to appear moderate enough to be trusted by a rigged system. They should ask how to become trustworthy enough to unrig it. That requires a confrontation with oligarchy. Anything less will leave authoritarianism’s material foundations intact.

"While anti-corruption politics is a necessary and powerful alternative to failed moderation, it must be framed as a specific institutional reconstruction rather than a nihilistic attack on all government to avoid inadvertently fueling authoritarian distrust."

Bonica and Grumbach are right that anti-corruption politics has unusual promise. They are also right that moderation, understood as cautious movement toward an imagined center, cannot meet the authoritarian moment. But anti-corruption is a volatile instrument. It can renew democracy, or it can deepen the conspiratorial mood that authoritarians exploit. If Democrats make “the system is rigged” their central message, they must be very clear about what is rigged, who rigged it, and how democratic government can repair it.

The appeal is obvious. Americans across ideology believe the powerful play by different rules. They see Supreme Court justices accepting undisclosed gifts, members of Congress trading stocks, lobbyists writing legislation, billionaires funding super PACs, corporations raising prices while blaming workers, and presidents treating public office as a private brand. This disgust cuts through partisan language. It gives democracy a concrete meaning: public power should not be for sale.

But the same disgust can travel in darker directions. “Corruption” is one of the most common words in authoritarian rhetoric. Strongmen claim that courts, journalists, civil servants, universities, scientists, election administrators, and opposition parties are corrupt. They promise to cleanse the state. They use public anger not to create accountability but to justify loyalty tests and purges. Trumpism itself is an anti-corruption narrative of a perverse kind: the “deep state,” the “fake news,” the “Biden crime family,” the stolen election, the globalist cabal. The fact that these claims are often false does not diminish their political power.

Democrats therefore need an anti-corruption politics that is institutional rather than nihilistic. It should not say, “Everything is corrupt.” That message confirms despair. It should say, “Specific people and interests have corrupted specific institutions, and here are the rules that will stop them.” Ban congressional stock trading. Require binding Supreme Court ethics. Disclose dark money. Strengthen inspectors general. Protect whistleblowers. Limit the revolving door. Enforce anti-bribery laws. Make emergency powers reviewable. Protect election workers. Reform campaign finance. These demands channel anger into reconstruction.

The distinction matters because many Americans already distrust government too much. Low trust is not automatically democratic. It can produce apathy, privatization, conspiracy, and appetite for strongman rule. If voters conclude that all politicians are crooks and all institutions are fake, then the leader who says “Only I can fix it” gains strength. Anti-corruption must restore the idea of public trust, not merely attack the untrustworthy.

Bonica and Grumbach understand this when they call for costly reform inside the Democratic Party. The party cannot credibly accuse Republicans of corruption while tolerating its own insider privileges. Yet self-purification has limits. Rejecting corporate PAC money may signal independence, but money will still flow through other channels. Small donors can be ideological and volatile. Wealthy individual donors can be as influential as PACs. Nonprofits and super PACs can obscure accountability. The party should pursue reforms because they are right, not because they will magically create purity.

Moreover, corruption is not the only thing voters care about. The lead essay cites polling showing high concern about corruption, even above cost of living in one survey. That is important but not dispositive. Polls about “corruption” capture broad frustration; they do not tell us whether voters will prioritize ethics reform over prices, immigration, crime, or cultural identity when choosing a president or member of Congress. Anti-corruption works best when it explains those issues. If it sits beside them as another plank, it will fade.

For example, high grocery prices can be framed as the result of corporate concentration and political capture. Housing costs can be linked to real estate interests, zoning politics, private equity, and weak tenant power. Health care frustration can be connected to insurer lobbying and pharmaceutical influence. Immigration dysfunction can be tied to employers who profit from exploitable labor while politicians prefer the issue unresolved. Climate inaction can be linked to fossil fuel money. In each case, corruption is not an abstract moral failing; it is why government does not solve problems.

State capacity failures, local veto points, bureaucratic complexity, professional guilds, public-sector dysfunction, and cultural conflict also matter. If anti-corruption becomes a universal solvent explaining everything, it will lose credibility. Voters can tell when a message is too convenient.

The essay’s critique of popularism is relevant here. Polling may show that anti-corruption language resonates. That does not mean repeating “corruption” will build a movement. The frame must be embodied by candidates who seem trustworthy and by organizations that give people a role. Otherwise, anti-corruption becomes one more campaign theme competing for attention. The most credible anti-corruption messengers may be local: a mayor who cleaned up procurement, a state legislator who exposed utility capture, a veteran who refuses contractor money, a nurse fighting hospital monopolies, a teacher opposing school privatization scams.

There is also a moral hazard in anti-establishment politics. Bonica and Grumbach argue that Democratic leaders lack anti-establishment credibility. True. But some of that deficit comes from being the party that still believes in government. Democrats should not pretend to be outsiders to the state in the way Trump pretends to be an outsider while occupying power. They should be anti-oligarchic, anti-crony, anti-impunity—not anti-public. A civil servant administering clean water rules is not the enemy. A scientist at NIH is not the enemy. An election worker counting ballots is not the enemy. A judge enforcing due process is not the enemy. The enemy is the capture or intimidation of such roles by private wealth and partisan loyalty.

This distinction can help Democrats avoid feeding the right’s attack on expertise. During the pandemic and after, many institutions damaged their credibility through mistakes, overconfidence, or politicization. A democratic anti-corruption politics should allow criticism of experts without endorsing know-nothingism. It should demand transparency, accountability, and humility from institutions precisely because they are necessary.

The authors are right that moderation will not defeat authoritarianism. But a reckless anti-corruption politics could also fail by intensifying the very distrust that makes authoritarianism plausible. The task is to convert anger into standards. Not “burn it all down,” but “no one is above the law.” Not “all elites are evil,” but “public office is a public trust.” Not “the deep state controls everything,” but “independent institutions must be protected from billionaires and presidents alike.” Not “politics is hopelessly rigged,” but “we can unrig it with rules, power, and participation.”

This requires discipline from Democrats and from movements to their left. Conspiratorial shortcuts should be rejected even when they seem useful. Corrupt Democrats should be named, not hidden. Reforms should be specific enough to verify. Claims should be factual. Anger should be moral, not paranoid. The line can be hard to hold in a media environment that rewards exaggeration, but holding it is part of defending democracy.

Bonica and Grumbach’s central insight remains: voters want purpose, not mere positioning. Anti-corruption can provide that purpose if it joins outrage to reconstruction. It can show citizens that democracy is not a lecture about norms but a system for making power answerable. But it must resist the authoritarian counterfeit of anti-corruption—the fantasy that one leader can purify the nation by destroying constraints.

Democrats should run against corruption, loudly and relentlessly. They should also run for clean government, equal law, competent administration, and public trust. The difference between those two emphases may determine whether anti-corruption becomes a democratic renewal or just another current in the river of distrust.

At the same time, coalition politics must not become hostage politics, where the most vulnerable are traded away to reassure the most anxious. The rights of trans people, immigrants, racial minorities, women, religious minorities, and dissenters are not optional accessories to democracy. Anti-corruption should lead to binding Supreme Court ethics, stock-trading bans, disclosure rules, protection for inspectors general and whistleblowers, voting rights, anti-gerrymandering reforms, campaign finance changes, election-worker protection, and constraints on executive abuse. Democrats have too often treated democracy reform as procedural housekeeping. It is the architecture of every other policy goal.

National committees should support different kinds of candidates and messages, then evaluate them transparently. Safe blue districts can test bold agendas; red and purple districts can test culturally rooted populism; state parties can experiment with anti-corruption ballot measures and local democracy campaigns. Failure should produce learning, not factional purges.

The rhetorical posture should be neither “return to normal” nor “burn it down.” Many Americans distrust the status quo, but they also fear chaos. Democrats should offer disciplined disruption: a promise to break the power of corrupt insiders in order to make government work for ordinary people. That phrase contains both conflict and competence. The party must be willing to fight, but the fight must have an end beyond catharsis.

Never Trump conservatives, democratic socialists, suburban moderates, union households, civil libertarians, faith leaders, young activists, and disillusioned nonvoters all have a role. It will judge whether they defended self-government when it was threatened. But defending self-government requires both passion and patience, both moral clarity and tactical flexibility, both mobilization and persuasion, both institutional defense and institutional reform.

In its place Democrats need a harder rule: choose the conflicts that reveal whose side you are on, speak about them in language people can understand, build organizations that outlast campaigns, protect every member of the democratic coalition, and reform the institutions that make minority rule possible. That is not moderation. It is not radicalism for its own sake. It is democratic politics under emergency conditions.

In Defense of the Despised Median Voter

Bonica and Grumbach have produced a vigorous, data-rich argument that I find ultimately unconvincing, and I want to explain why someone who has read the same political science literature can reach the opposite conclusion. The essay's central empirical claim—that the moderation advantage has disappeared in the Trump era—rests on a methodological move that deserves more scrutiny than it has received.

Consider their closely-contested-primaries design. The logic is sound: in primaries decided by tiny margins, the winner is essentially randomly assigned, so we can compare the general-election performance of moderates versus progressives who won "coin-flip" primaries and isolate the causal effect of candidate ideology. They find no significant general-election difference. Therefore, moderation provides no benefit. QED.

But notice what this design measures. It compares moderates and progressives who survived a competitive primary in the same district. By construction, both candidates are viable enough to win a Democratic primary. Both have run effective campaigns. Both have raised money. The design is excellent for isolating ideology while holding district and viability constant, but it tells us almost nothing about the cases that worry moderates most: districts where progressives never make it through the primary because they cannot raise money, mobilize voters, or attract endorsements in the first place. The design conditions on selection into the competitive set, which is precisely where the moderation advantage is most likely to operate.

A related problem haunts their twenty-one-out-of-twenty-two statistic. Of course twenty-one Democratic incumbents who lost competitive seats from 2016 to 2024 were moderates. Almost every Democrat representing a competitive seat is a moderate. The denominator matters. If 95 percent of Democrats in competitive districts are moderates and 95 percent of losses are moderates, that is a finding of zero ideological signal, not a finding against moderation. The authors know this. But the way the statistic is deployed in the essay invites the reader to draw a stronger inference than the data support.

There is a more fundamental issue. The authors' argument implicitly treats "moderation" as a single variable—a position on a left-right axis. But the popularist critique of progressive Democrats has rarely been about left-right placement in the abstract. It has been about a specific cluster of culturally coded positions: "defund the police," opposition to enforcement at the border, "Latinx," the language and posture associated with the post-2020 left. Whether one approves or disapproves of these positions, the empirical question is whether they specifically harm Democratic candidates. There is substantial evidence that they do—not because voters carefully evaluate policy platforms, but because these positions become symbolic markers that travel through national media and attach to the party brand regardless of what local candidates say.

David Shor's analyses, whatever their methodological imperfections, consistently identify a specific subset of progressive positions that perform poorly. The authors largely ignore this granularity, treating "moderation" as a monolithic variable they can statistically dispatch. But the popularist argument that they need to refute is not "candidates should be more centrist on every issue." It is "candidates and the party should stop letting a small number of culturally unpopular positions define them." On that narrower claim, the evidence is much stronger than the authors let on.

I also want to push back on their treatment of turnout. They argue that Democrats' problem is not persuading moderates but mobilizing the base, and that progressive candidates are "slightly more effective" at mobilizing registered Democrats. Two problems. First, "slightly" is doing a lot of work. The base-mobilization effect they identify, even taken at face value, is small relative to the swings produced by national conditions and partisan tides. Second, the 2024 election presents an awkward problem for the mobilization theory: Harris lost in part because nonvoters in key states leaned Trump. The disengaged were not progressive young people waiting for a clarion call. Many were working-class voters of all races who had drifted right on cultural and economic issues. A more aggressively progressive nominee in 2024 would likely have lost by more, not less, in the Sun Belt and the industrial Midwest. The base-mobilization story works for some elections and fails for others. It is not the general theory the authors imply.

Finally, the anti-corruption pivot. I am sympathetic in principle. Democrats should ban congressional stock trading; they should reject corporate PAC money where possible; they should recruit working-class candidates. But the strategic theory behind the prescription is weaker than it looks. Anti-corruption framing has been Trump's frame, not the Democrats', for a decade. Voters who are persuadable on corruption have already chosen their corruption-fighter. The Democratic anti-corruption pitch has the disadvantage of being directed at an opponent whose voters have spent ten years deciding that "corrupt" means "the establishment that the establishment media defends," which they read as the Democrats.

What would I recommend instead? Roughly what the popularists recommend, with corrections. Democrats should run candidates who match their districts. They should de-emphasize the small set of culturally polarizing positions that have become unforced errors. They should defend tangible material achievements when they have them and propose tangible material improvements when they do not. They should mobilize the base where mobilization is the binding constraint and persuade swing voters where persuasion is. This is unglamorous, varies by context, and lacks the moral grandeur of the authors' prescription. But it is closer to how elections are actually won.

The authors close by warning that "history won't judge Democrats on whether they were moderate enough." Perhaps not. But history will judge them on whether they won. And the path to winning, in the absence of any reliable evidence that aggressive populism produces consistent gains, runs through the boring discipline of meeting voters where they are.

What the Authors Get Right

Most of the responses to Bonica and Grumbach's essay will, I expect, focus on what it gets wrong. I want to focus on what it gets right, because I think the central argument has been more dismissed than refuted in the early reactions, and the dismissals do not engage with what the authors have actually shown.

The central argument is this: the empirical foundation on which popularism rests—that strategic moderation produces consistent electoral gains—has eroded in the Trump era, to the point where its boosters are increasingly relying on opaque proprietary metrics and selection-biased comparisons to sustain a claim that the more transparent academic literature does not support.

This is a significant claim, and the authors marshal substantial evidence for it. The closely-contested-primaries design is among the cleanest causal designs available in observational political science, and its null finding for ideology effects in general elections is hard to wave away. The breakdown of the Split Ticket WAR metric is a real contribution; the demonstration that less than a third of WAR scores reflect actual candidate overperformance is methodologically devastating to the most-cited popularist data source. The replication failures of the Split Ticket model across multiple reasonable alternative specifications confirm that the headline number is fragile.

These findings deserve to be confronted directly by anyone defending popularism. The honest popularist response would be: yes, the magnitude of the moderation effect has been overstated; yes, the proprietary models have not been adequately scrutinized; yes, the academic literature now finds smaller and more context-dependent effects than the consultants have claimed; but here is why we still think moderate positioning has a meaningful effect in the specific cases that matter. I have not seen this response made carefully. What I have seen instead is dismissal of the academic findings as "too complex" or assertions that the case for moderation is intuitive enough not to require empirical support. Neither response is satisfying.

The authors are also right about Manchin. The Manchin example has been so overworked in popularist commentary that it deserves the specific deflation the authors give it. Manchin won because Manchin was a uniquely embedded political brand in West Virginia, built over decades of state-level office and personal relationships. He is not a generalizable case for moderation; he is a generalizable case for incumbency, personal brand, and biographical fit. The Elliott loss in 2024 confirms this directly: a generic moderate replacement in the same state, in the same year, lost by forty points. The lesson is not "moderation does not work in West Virginia"; it is "Manchin does not generalize."

The authors are also right that the median voter theorem, as commonly invoked in political strategy commentary, no longer fits American political reality. The theorem assumes single-dimensional ideological space, voters who evaluate candidates on policy positions, low partisan attachment, and elections that respond elastically to candidate positioning. None of these assumptions describes contemporary American electoral politics. The theorem has not been definitively refuted in formal political theory; it remains useful in some applications. But its application as a strategic-political prescription—"move to the center, win the median voter"—is obsolete.

The authors are also right that the turnout question deserves more weight than popularist commentary has given it. In a nationalized, polarized electoral environment where vote choice is largely determined by partisan identification, the binding constraint on electoral success is often turnout rather than persuasion. Democrats have a documented turnout deficit, and the magnitude of this deficit in 2024 was decisive in key states. Closing it requires strategies that popularist commentary has tended to under-emphasize: investing in field organizations, year-round mobilization in low-engagement communities, and substantive promises that give marginal voters reason to participate.

The authors are also right about the corruption opportunity, though I disagreed with elements of their treatment in another response I drafted. The general point—that anti-corruption framing has been a powerful axis of political mobilization throughout democratic history, that the current American public is unusually receptive to it, and that the Democratic Party has not fully exploited the available terrain—is correct, even if the implementation is more complicated than the essay allows.

What follows from accepting these points? Several things.

First, the popularist intellectual coalition needs to update. The most-cited popularist sources—Yglesias, Klein, Teixeira, the Split Ticket analysts—have been working from analyses whose empirical foundations are weaker than they have publicly acknowledged. Their prescriptions may still be partially right (I think some of them are), but the certainty with which they have been delivered is unjustified. A more humble popularist program would acknowledge that the moderation premium has shrunk substantially, that the cases where it remains operative are narrower than previously thought, and that complementary strategies—including base mobilization—are necessary even within a popularist framework.

Second, the popularist political coalition—the Democratic consultants, donors, and elected officials who have organized around this strategy—needs to confront the gap between the public confidence of their intellectual leadership and the actual state of the empirical case. This is uncomfortable. The 2024 election was, in many respects, a clean test of the popularist program; the Harris campaign implemented something close to a textbook popularist strategy, and the result was a Democratic loss across the board. The popularist response to this has mostly been to argue that the moderation was insufficient, not to consider that the framework itself is failing. This is the move that I find least defensible in current commentary.

Third, alternatives deserve more serious consideration than they have received. This does not mean the authors' anti-corruption populism is the right alternative. It means that experimentation is warranted, that different candidates should be running different strategies in different districts, and that no single approach should be presented as the demonstrated path forward.

I have my own disagreements with the authors, which I have aired elsewhere. But I want to say plainly that the empirical core of their argument is stronger than its critics have generally allowed, and that the popularist response has so far been more rhetorical than substantive. The next phase of this debate should engage with the evidence rather than asserting authority over it. If the popularist case can be reformulated to take the evidence seriously, it would be a stronger position. If it cannot, then it deserves to lose the argument.

A small, context-dependent moderation premium is exactly what we would expect, and a small premium is not nothing when elections are decided by tens of thousands of votes across three states.

A second problem is that the essay's positive program is itself a form of popularism, just one its authors like. The argument for an anti-corruption frame is supported by—of all things—polling: a Yale/George Mason survey showing Americans are "very worried" about corruption, a YouGov survey on bribery, the popularity of a congressional stock-trading ban. This is precisely the move Ezra Klein recommends: find what voters tell pollsters they care about and talk about that. There is nothing wrong with the move; in fact it suggests that popularism, properly understood, is not the cartoon of pandering Bonica and Grumbach paint. The disagreement is really about which popular things to emphasize, and whether "corruption" is a more mobilizing frame than "cost of living." That is a real and interesting debate, but it is not a debate between empiricists and idealists. It is a debate within empiricism.

Third, the anti-establishment-populism prescription understates how hard it is to wield from inside the institutions voters distrust. Trump's authority to denounce "the swamp" rests on his being personally external to the political class, however implausibly. Democratic candidates running on anti-corruption in 2026 will be running as members of the party that controls many of the institutions—universities, prosecutors' offices, regulatory agencies, blue-state governments—that the right has spent a decade coding as "the establishment." The European cases Bonica and Grumbach cite cut against them here, too: where left parties have successfully run anti-corruption campaigns against incumbent authoritarians (Poland's Civic Coalition, for instance), they did so as a coalition that included plenty of moderates and conservatives, organized around defending institutions, not attacking them. The model is closer to "anti-Trump united front" than to "left populist insurgency," and the former requires exactly the kind of broad-tent coalition-building that progressive purity tests tend to obstruct. Sherrod Brown and Jon Tester—populist, working-class-coded Democrats with reform credentials—both lost their 2024 Senate races. If economic populism plus authentic biography plus anti-establishment branding were a winning combination, they should be senators today.

Fourth, the turnout-versus-persuasion framing is too clean. The authors emphasize the partisan turnout gap in 2024, and they are correct that it matters. But the 2024 election was not solely or even mostly a turnout story; it was also a story of voters who showed up and switched. Trump improved his margins among Latino, young, and working-class voters who voted. Catalist and Pew validation data both point to real persuasion, not just differential demobilization. A strategy built entirely around mobilizing the base will leave on the table exactly the voters Democrats need to keep from defecting. The honest position is that mobilization and persuasion are both necessary, that neither is sufficient, and that there is no algorithm—popularist or progressive—that tells you the right mix in advance.

Where I think Bonica and Grumbach are most valuable is in pressing the deeper point that the strategic frame matters more than any particular issue position. If you concede that elections are nationalized, that voters follow leaders rather than the reverse, and that authenticity dominates triangulation, then the question of whether a candidate is two clicks left or right of the median is the wrong question. The right question is whether the party offers a coherent story about who it fights for and against. On that, "the system is rigged and we will un-rig it" is at least a story. The Harris campaign's careful avoidance of any such story—its retreat into joy, its embrace of Liz Cheney, its silence on Gaza, its refusal to break with Biden on anything—was not really moderation in the substantive sense. It was the absence of a frame. That absence is what cost the campaign, more than any specific position.

So the most useful reading of this essay is not as a vindication of progressive strategy over moderate strategy, but as a call for any strategy with a backbone. Their three lessons—reframe the battlefield, build anti-establishment credibility, boost turnout—are reasonable, and the first two are largely independent of left-right positioning. A moderate Democrat could run on banning congressional stock trading, refusing corporate PAC money, and breaking up monopolies; an economic progressive could run on hawkish border enforcement. The point is to have something to say that isn't downstream of a focus group. Where I'd push the authors is to admit that their own prescription is also a bet, also poll-informed, and also unproven against an opponent who has spent a decade convincing half the country that he is the anti-corruption candidate. Humility is warranted on all sides. The "old rules are dead" is a useful slogan; "we know what the new rules are" is not yet a defensible claim.

A View from the Districts the Authors Are Talking About

I have worked on House races in the kinds of districts Bonica and Grumbach are theorizing about—the rural Pennsylvania seats, the Central Valley California seats, the upstate New York seats. I want to push back, gently but firmly, on the gap between their theoretical argument and the operational reality of those campaigns.

The first thing to know about a campaign in a swing rural or exurban district is that the candidate is not running a national strategy. They are running against a specific opponent in a specific media market with specific local issues that voters care about. The "anti-corruption" frame the authors recommend is invisible at the local level. Voters in a swing district care about whether their local Democrat is going to make their lives noticeably worse on the small number of issues that have salience for them: typically taxes, the cost of living, a specific industry that matters in the district (farming, manufacturing, energy), and—increasingly—immigration and cultural issues that get nationalized through right-wing media.

The most successful Democratic House candidates in these districts—people like Mary Peltola, who won and then lost in Alaska, like Pat Ryan in upstate New York, like Marcy Kaptur in Ohio—do not win by adopting a national populist anti-corruption frame. They win by being credibly local, by demonstrating specific competence on issues that matter in their district, by visibly representing the cultural identity of the district, and by carefully avoiding association with the most polarizing national-Democratic positions. This is not "moderation" in the abstract sense the authors critique; it is something more like cultural and biographical fit.

The authors lump this into the "being Manchin wins" category and treat it as not generalizable. But it is generalizable, in the sense that what successful swing-district Democrats share is precisely the willingness to position themselves on local terms rather than national terms. This is what popularism, at its best, has been advocating: not "be more moderate on a left-right axis," but "be more attentive to local political conditions and less captured by national-progressive cultural posture."

The authors' anti-corruption frame would be a disaster in many of these districts. Why? Because the corruption the right-wing media ecosystem talks about—the Biden family's financial dealings, the FBI's alleged political bias, the deep state—is not the corruption the authors mean. In the lived informational environment of a swing-district voter, "anti-corruption" cues right-wing framings, not progressive framings. Democrats running on anti-corruption would find themselves immediately on the defensive about their own party's actual or alleged scandals, and they would lose the framing battle in the local media.

This points to a deeper problem with the authors' argument. They write as if there is a single national strategic conversation in which Democrats can collectively choose a frame and deploy it nationally. But the national-strategic conversation is precisely what the authors elsewhere argue has nationalized politics in destructive ways. If candidates must rise and fall with national tides, then the question is: what national posture should the party project? And the answer to that is not the same as the answer to the question: what should a candidate in PA-7 actually do?

The authors collapse these two questions in a way that obscures the operational dilemma. A national Democratic posture of populist anti-corruption mobilization may, plausibly, energize some segments of the base and produce mobilization gains. It may also produce a national party brand that swing-district candidates find unworkable. The right-wing media will translate the populist frame into "Democrats want to defund the police, dismantle the border, and confiscate your guns," and the swing-district candidate has to run against that translation. A more careful, less ideologically distinctive national posture—which is what the popularists have been advocating—gives swing-district candidates more room to operate locally.

This is the part of the popularist case the authors do not adequately rebut. The popularists are not arguing that moderation as an individual candidate strategy is a silver bullet. They are arguing that the national party brand has costs for swing-district candidates that the activist base does not adequately appreciate, and that a more disciplined national posture would give those candidates more room. This is closer to a true claim than the authors allow.

I will say something about turnout. The authors emphasize the turnout gap and argue that Democrats should focus on mobilizing the base. But the mobilization theory is much weaker in the kinds of swing districts I have worked in than it is in deep-blue cities. Democratic primary electorates in swing districts are small, frequently more moderate than the national party, and dominated by older voters. The "base" the authors imagine—young, diverse, mobilizable through populist messaging—is largely absent from these districts. The marginal voter in a swing-district House race is more likely to be a registered independent in their fifties who voted for both Obama and Trump than to be a 25-year-old non-voter waiting for inspiration.

The authors are theorizing about national strategy from a perspective that overweights the dynamics of national-presidential and major-urban races and underweights the operational reality of the swing districts that determine which party governs. The kinds of districts that swung in 2024—the suburban and exurban districts that decide House majorities—are not won by progressive populist mobilization. They are won, when they are won, by candidates who can credibly represent the cultural identity of the district while delivering material wins. That is what popularism actually means in practice, and it is much closer to right than the authors allow.

I conclude with one concession. The popularist case has been overstated in some places—the Split Ticket WAR analysis the authors critique is genuinely flawed, and the certainty with which some popularists have presented their conclusions has been undeserved. But the operational program—run candidates who fit their districts, avoid the cultural posture of the national progressive movement when it conflicts with local conditions, be disciplined about national messaging—remains the only program that has any track record in the kinds of districts Democrats actually need to win to govern. The authors' alternative is theoretically interesting and operationally undertested. Until I see it work in PA-7, I will keep running the playbook that has at least sometimes worked there.

Methodological Notes from a Skeptic

I want to take Bonica and Grumbach's empirical claims seriously enough to disagree with them on the methods. The essay leans heavily on the authority of "research designs that can distinguish causal effects from correlations" and "comprehensive measures of ideology" to claim that the moderation effect has disappeared. As someone who works in this corner of political science, I think both the popularist analyses they critique and their own counter-analyses are weaker than presented, and that the resulting debate has more heat than light.

Begin with measurement. The authors say they built ideology measures combining "over a dozen distinct metrics" including voting records, campaign finance data (DW-NOMINATE and CFscores), candidate websites, and voter evaluations. This is a sophisticated approach, but it conceals a real problem: these measures correlate imperfectly with each other and produce different rankings of candidates. CFscores in particular—which Bonica himself has done foundational work developing—measure something specific: the ideological composition of a candidate's donor network. A candidate who raises money from progressive donors will score progressive on CFscores even if their voting record is moderate. The reverse is also true. Combining these measures via principal components or similar techniques can mask substantial cross-measure disagreement about who is "moderate."

This matters because the empirical claim that "moderation does not help" is sensitive to how moderation is defined. Define it via DW-NOMINATE (roll-call votes), and you are measuring something downstream of the legislator's actual behavior. Define it via CFscores, and you are measuring donor composition. Define it via candidate websites, and you are measuring strategic self-presentation. These can diverge sharply, and analyses that combine them may produce stable averages with high internal variance that disappears in the aggregate.

The closely-contested-primary design has its own problems. It is, as I noted, an excellent design for isolating ideology in a specific subpopulation, but the inferential reach is limited. Close primary contests between viable moderates and viable progressives are themselves selected events. They occur in districts where the Democratic primary electorate is plausibly divided, where both candidates can raise money, and where the institutional pressures from national groups have not foreclosed one side. The estimates from this design tell us about ideology's marginal effect in this specific population, not about the general effect of running progressives in arbitrary districts.

The authors' headline claim about the twenty-one out of twenty-two competitive losses being moderates is also methodologically weaker than presented. As critics have noted, the denominator is overwhelmingly moderate; the ratio is approximately what you would expect from a null effect of ideology on outcomes. The authors know this; the essay's framing does not.

I am being hard on the authors not because I disagree with their conclusion that moderation's premium has shrunk—I think this is probably true—but because the certainty with which they present it is overstated, in a way that mirrors the popularist certainty they criticize. The honest scholarly position is that the moderation premium has likely declined, that the magnitude of decline is genuinely uncertain, and that context-dependent effects probably remain in specific races. This is not the rhetorical posture of the essay.

The same caution applies to their turnout claims. The finding that progressive candidates are "slightly more effective at mobilizing their base" is presented as the central empirical alternative to the popularist focus on swing voters. But the magnitude of "slightly" is doing significant work. The authors do not, in this essay, give a clear point estimate. If the base-mobilization effect of running progressives is one percentage point and the swing-voter persuasion effect is zero, then their argument holds. If the base-mobilization effect is one percentage point and the swing-voter effect is one percentage point in the opposite direction, then it does not. Without the magnitudes, the policy implication is indeterminate.

Their description of the Manchin/Elliott contrast is also worth scrutinizing. Manchin won; Elliott, also a moderate, lost by 40 points. The authors conclude that "being Manchin wins, not being moderate." This is too clean. The 2024 environment in West Virginia was vastly different from any previous Manchin election. Trump was on the ballot, the state had continued its rightward trend, and the open seat was always going to be much harder than any Manchin contest had been. Attributing the entire 40-point margin to "not being Manchin" overlooks the political environment in which Elliott ran. A Manchin clone in 2024 might have lost by twenty-five points instead of forty—a substantial difference that still falls short of a win, but a difference nonetheless. The contrast does not establish what the authors want it to establish.

What is my own view? I think the moderation premium has declined significantly, particularly in high-information races where partisan tides dominate. I think there are still races—lower-profile state legislative races, races involving incumbent personal brands, races in states with strong split-ticket traditions like Maine or Alaska—where it matters. I think the popularist program has overpromised on the basis of analyses that have not adequately addressed selection effects and have over-relied on opaque proprietary metrics. I also think the authors' counter-program overpromises in the opposite direction, on the basis of analyses that have their own limitations.

The honest message is one the authors articulate at one point and then walk back: "nobody knows for certain." Their preferred prescriptions—anti-corruption framing, anti-establishment credibility, turnout maximization—deserve consideration as plausible directions, but they should be tested with the same skepticism the authors apply to popularist claims. There is no demonstrated alternative strategy that reliably outperforms the moderation strategy whose decline they document. The next decade of American politics will probably involve a lot of experimentation, much of which will fail, and the strategists who insist they have already figured it out are the ones to be most suspicious of, on either side of this debate.

The authors of “How Not to Defeat Authoritarianism” are right to bury the corpse of triangulating centrism and to urge Democrats toward a politics of mobilization rather than persuasion. But their prescription—reframing the battlefield around corruption—risks repeating the very mistakes they diagnose. Not because it’s too bold, but because, in practice, it is likely to become just another flavor of anemic, process-oriented messaging that fails to address the material deprivation and powerlessness driving democratic despair.

The anti-corruption frame appears seductive. It sidesteps left-right ideological labels, taps into a deep well of cross-partisan disgust, and squares neatly with the authors’ data showing that many nonvoters are not moderates waiting to be courted but disillusioned citizens who think the system is rigged. It also mirrors tactics used by anti-authoritarian movements abroad, from Brazil’s *Lava Jato* to Ukraine’s Maidan, where framing the struggle as clean versus corrupt temporarily bridged deep divisions. But there’s a reason those moments proved fragile: corruption is a diagnosis, not a program. Once the street protests end and a new administration takes office, the energy dissipates unless the movement delivers tangible improvements in people’s lives—wages, housing, healthcare, dignity. Otherwise, corruption talk becomes the wallpaper of yet another professional-class reform effort, one that investigative journalists and good-government NGOs applaud while working-class voters wonder why their rent still went up.

The authors’ own cited polls hint at this fragility. Yes, a May 2025 survey found more Americans “very worried” about government corruption than about the cost of living or the economy. But that doesn’t mean corruption is their first priority; it may simply reflect a pervasive sense that elites are self-serving, which is a standing suspicion rather than an actionable demand. When voters are asked what they most want government to do, even in 2025, the answers remain stubbornly material: lower prices, better jobs, affordable healthcare. The risk is that an anti-corruption platform without a strong economic core will sound to many like one more elite promise—Democrats wagging their fingers about Supreme Court ethics while the party’s own ties to Wall Street and Silicon Valley remain intact. After all, one of the most effective attack lines Trump used against Hillary Clinton wasn’t about policy but about the Clinton Foundation and paid speeches to Goldman Sachs. The Democrats’ anti-corruption credibility is brittle precisely because the party as an institution is deeply intertwined with the same donor class it now wants to decry.

This is not to deny the power of a clean-government message. But history suggests it works best as a complement to, not a substitute for, a transformative economic agenda. The last time Democrats built a durable governing coalition, under Franklin Roosevelt, they didn’t run primarily against corruption—though they certainly took on the “economic royalists.” They ran on concrete material gains: Social Security, the Wagner Act, jobs programs. Corruption was a subtext, not the headline. Similarly, the populist movements of the late nineteenth century fused anti-monopoly fury with demands for a graduated income tax, the direct election of senators, and an eight-hour day. Anti-corruption was a means to structural economic change, not an end in itself.

The authors might reply that their anti-corruption frame is intended as precisely that: a crisp moral narrative that can house a broader economic program. But in calling for Democrats to “position themselves as the party that will take on the real elites—billionaires buying Supreme Court justices, corporations price-gouging families, insiders trading stocks on classified briefings,” they correctly identify the symptoms while sidestepping the question of what, substantively, Democrats should do about corporate power and inequality beyond procedural reforms. Banning congressional stock trading and rejecting corporate PAC money are worthwhile steps, but they are small-ball responses to a systemic crisis. Voters who are hurting economically don’t just want to see insiders punished; they want to see their own circumstances improve. A platform centered on rooting out graft can easily become fixated on process rather than outcomes, and process is famously poor at generating mass enthusiasm.

The deeper challenge is that the very concept of “corruption” is contested. For many working-class voters, corruption isn’t just bribery or insider trading; it’s the entire structure by which economic and political power are concentrated in a few hands. A nurse forced to work mandatory overtime while a hospital CEO takes home millions sees a corrupted system. An autoworker watching their plant close while the company buys back stock and the CEO gets a golden parachute sees a corrupted system. If the Democratic anti-corruption message focuses narrowly on campaign finance and Supreme Court ethics—the stuff of editorial boards—it will largely miss the lived corruption that fuels right-wing populism. Trump, after all, has successfully painted himself as an enemy of a corrupt system precisely by lying about his own economic populism and vilifying coastal elites. Democrats cannot hope to outbid him by offering tighter lobbying regulations. They must articulate a vision of an economy restored to serve the many, not the few, and then back it with policies that are felt in paychecks and rent bills.

That means the party’s primary challenge is not simply to adopt an anti-corruption frame but to rebuild its credibility as the party of working people. This requires a far more radical break with its own donor class than the authors acknowledge. It means not just “authentic” candidates—though nurses and teachers in Congress would be a welcome change—but a genuine commitment to policies that challenge corporate power directly: a federal job guarantee, public investment in housing and green energy at a scale that 2022’s Inflation Reduction Act only hinted at, antitrust enforcement that breaks up monopolies, and a labor law overhaul that makes unionizing trivial. Such an agenda would not be “moderate” in the old sense, but it would be popular in the sense of commanding majority support, particularly among the working-class voters the party has hemorrhaged.

The authors’ own electoral analysis helps to explain why. If candidate ideology has little effect on vote share in general elections, then Democrats have nothing to lose by running on a bold economic program that actual voters want. The constraint is not the median voter of a swing district; it’s the median donor of a party still addicted to high-dollar fundraising. The anti-corruption frame could be a useful tool to break that addiction, but only if it is married to an unambiguous economic populism that demonstrates, in concrete terms, whose side you’re on.

Ultimately, the problem with the anti-corruption prescription is not that it is too confrontational but that it is too comfortable. It allows Democrats to sound fierce while staying safely within the boundaries of neoliberal respectability. It replicates the very dynamic the authors criticize: a party searching for a message that polls well, rather than leading with a clear moral and material vision. The American electorate isn’t waiting for a cleaner government; it’s waiting for a different country. Democrats must offer that, or they will continue to lose to the authoritarians whose rise they rightly fear.

That’s like saying that because an umbrella doesn’t work well in a hurricane, you should throw it away and pray for sunshine.

They demonstrate that the measurable effect of a candidate’s ideology on vote share, controlling for other factors, is negligible in the Trump era. But this finding is entirely consistent with a world in which moderation is still essential—it just means that moderation must happen at the party brand level, not the individual candidate level. Because elections are nationalized, no single candidate can easily outrun the party label. A moderate Democrat in a reddish district will be tied to Nancy Pelosi, AOC, and whatever the latest far-left social media outrage is, regardless of their personal voting record. The appropriate response is not to give up on appealing to the middle; it is to aggressively shape the party’s national image so that it no longer repels these middle voters. That requires party leaders, presidential nominees, and prominent voices to enforce a disciplined message that foregrounds popular economic policies and marginalizes unpopular cultural positions. When the authors say that moderates have been “exhausted” because they already dominate competitive districts, they miss the point: it’s not enough to have a moderate in the seat; the party must stop constantly supplying the opposition with ammunition that incinerates that moderate’s brand.

The proposed anti-corruption frame is an interesting idea, but it’s a message, not a strategy. An anti-corruption message can cut either way, as we saw in 2016 when Trump notoriously outflanked Clinton on the “rigged system” narrative. Without deep, visible changes in the party’s own behavior—not just rejecting PAC money but severing ties with the entire donor advisory apparatus that shapes policy—an anti-corruption frame will ring hollow. And even if it were credible, it’s not clear it would motivate the marginal voters Democrats need. The voters who stayed home in 2024 were disproportionately low-income, young, and people of color. Their failure to turn out likely had more to do with economic precarity and a sense that politics doesn’t deliver for them than with a burning concern for congressional insider trading. An anti-corruption message that doesn’t immediately and credibly promise a material improvement in their lives is just more noise.

They argue that progressive candidates slightly boost base turnout, and they imply that a mobilization strategy around anti-corruption could replicate that effect at scale. But the data they cite from tracking 120 million voters is fragile: it shows a small, aggregate effect that might be driven by specific sub-populations or contexts. Meanwhile, the real-world counterexamples are glaring. The 2020 Democratic primary, which featured a historically progressive field, produced a nominee in Joe Biden who explicitly ran as a moderate and a unifier—and he won, defeating an incumbent president. Yes, Biden benefited from extraordinary circumstances, but his victory was built on significant gains among suburban moderates and older voters, groups that a more aggressively progressive candidate might have alienated. The 2024 race, by contrast, saw Harris attempt a half-hearted pivot to the center after spending much of her political career being perceived (fairly or not) as a San Francisco progressive. In districts where a moderate can credibly hold a seat—like the ones that flipped in 2018 and sustained the Democratic majority—it makes sense to run candidates who reflect their district’s cultural values. The national party should focus on what unites: a robust economic agenda that raises wages, lowers costs, and invests in public goods, while leaving cultural debates to be fought at the local level where nuance is possible. The authors’ invocation of anti-authoritarian movements abroad actually undercuts their own argument. When democratic forces rallied against Viktor Orbán in Hungary, they didn’t abandon centrist voters to chase anti-corruption mobilizations. They built a broad coalition—including conservatives, liberals, and socialists—that was united by a commitment to democratic norms and European integration. In Poland, the Civic Coalition succeeded by mobilizing a massive turnout, yes, but that mobilization was fueled by a clear, positive alternative to PiS, not just by a negative anti-corruption message. They appealed to voters’ desire for a normal, competent, decent government. That is a form of moderation in temperament if not in policy. The lesson is not to discard the center but to build a coalition that makes the center possible again.

Ultimately, the Democrats’ problem is not that they have been too moderate; it’s that they have been perceived as extreme on cultural issues while being insufficiently bold on economic ones. That’s a branding problem, not an ideological one. The solution is to rebalance the public’s perception without abandoning the moral commitments that define the party. A candidate can support transgender rights while also believing that women’s sports should be protected. A candidate can call for an assault weapons ban while also respecting the rights of responsible gun owners. These aren’t contradictions; they are the balance of a pluralistic society. Politics is the art of the possible, and in a country as diverse as the United States, the possible necessarily lies somewhere near the middle. To reject that is to reject democracy itself.
