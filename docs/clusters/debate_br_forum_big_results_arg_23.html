"The rise of behavioral economics and randomized controlled trials (RCTs) in development economics risks marginalizing the critical macroeconomic and structural questions that actually determine a nation's path out of poverty."

**The Behavioral Turn and the Macroeconomic Vacuum**

Glennerster and Kremer have produced an elegant summary of what randomized evaluations have taught us about individual decision-making among the global poor. The findings they describe — that small fees deter, that small incentives mobilize, that convenience matters more than the rational model predicts — are valuable contributions to the microeconomic understanding of poverty. I want to use this response to argue, however, that the very prominence of these findings is symptomatic of a worrying drift in development economics: away from the macroeconomic questions that actually determine the trajectory of nations and toward the microeconomic puzzles that lend themselves to clean identification.

Consider what we know about the past sixty years of development. The countries that have most dramatically reduced poverty — South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, more recently China, and to varying degrees Vietnam, Indonesia, Malaysia, and others — did so primarily through sustained economic growth driven by structural transformation: the movement of labor from low-productivity agriculture into higher-productivity manufacturing and services. The policies that supported this transformation were not designed by behavioral economists. They involved exchange rate management, industrial policy, infrastructure investment, land reform, education systems geared toward an industrializing economy, and the painful construction of effective state institutions. None of these were tested in randomized trials. Many of them violated the contemporary best practice as understood by Western economists at the time.

By contrast, the countries that have remained mired in poverty — much of sub-Saharan Africa, parts of Latin America, Haiti, Yemen — have generally failed at this macroeconomic transformation. They have remained dependent on commodity exports, have not built domestic manufacturing capacity, have suffered from chronic state weakness, and have seen their educated youth emigrate. Behavioral interventions can do little against these structural constraints. A chlorine dispenser does not industrialize a country.

This is the elephant in the room of the randomista program. Its central findings are about how individuals in poor countries can be helped to make marginally better decisions about specific health and education investments. They tell us little about why some countries grow rich and others do not. They tell us nothing about the speed of structural transformation. They cannot evaluate trade policy, exchange rate regimes, financial sector reform, or industrial strategy. And yet these are the policies that, historically, have determined whether nations enter modernity or remain trapped at its margin.

Glennerster and Kremer might respond that no one is claiming RCTs answer every question, and that the program is properly modest about its scope. Fair enough. But the discipline does not always behave with the modesty the authors recommend. Behavioral and experimental findings are increasingly setting the agenda for development funding, for graduate training, for the choice of dissertation topics, and for the policy advice donor agencies provide. A young economist today, choosing a research path, faces strong incentives to work on questions amenable to randomization and strong disincentives to work on questions of macroeconomic policy or political economy where causal identification is harder. Over time, the discipline becomes shaped by what its methods can study, and the questions its methods cannot study become marginalized.

There is also a quieter intellectual cost. The randomista literature has produced a stylized image of the poor — present-biased, sensitive to small incentives, easily mobilized by clever choice architecture — that has begun to displace earlier images, including the image of the poor as economic actors making rational decisions under harsh constraints. Both images contain truth. The earlier image was important because it pushed back against patronizing assumptions of cultural deficiency. The newer image is important because it pushes back against simplistic assumptions of perfect rationality. The question is which image is more useful for which purpose. For the design of small projects, the behavioral image works well. For the design of national strategy, the rational-actor image, with its emphasis on aggregate incentives and structural constraints, is often more illuminating. The current intellectual climate, in which behavioral findings are everywhere and macroeconomic analysis is recessed, suggests we have lost the balance.

Consider, finally, the user-fee debate that Glennerster and Kremer treat as a behavioral question. From a macroeconomic perspective, the question is different: what are the implications of free public health and education provision for the size of the state, for tax capacity, for the relationship between citizens and the fiscal contract? Free provision requires either taxation or external aid. Taxation requires state capacity, which is built over decades of political development. External aid creates dependencies and distorts political accountability. The behavioral finding — that user fees deter use — is one input into this larger question, not its answer. A country that abolishes fees on paper but cannot fund free provision adequately may end up with worse services than one that charged small fees and used the revenue to maintain quality. The trial does not settle this.

I do not want to be misunderstood. The randomista program has produced genuine knowledge, and the specific findings Glennerster and Kremer summarize are useful. I would happily endorse most of the policy implications they draw. But the development field needs to maintain a sense of proportion. Curing one child of worms is good. Building a state that no longer has worm epidemics is better. The first is achievable by a project; the second is the work of a generation. The current emphasis on the first risks teaching us how to manage poverty more effectively rather than how to end it.

The Stellaluna analogy with which the authors close — "How can we feel so different and be so much alike?" — is touching but misleading as a guide to policy. The relevant question for development is not whether Kenyans and Americans share psychological dispositions. They do. The relevant question is why one country has the institutions to make those dispositions productive and the other does not. The answer is not behavioral. It is structural, political, and historical, and the experimental program has, by design, almost nothing to say about it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Historical evidence shows that dramatic poverty reduction in successful nations resulted from structural transformations like industrialization and state-building rather than microeconomic behavioral interventions.
  2. 02The focus on RCTs creates an intellectual bias where research agendas and funding are dictated by what is easily measurable rather than what is most impactful for national development.
  3. 03The behavioral image of the poor as present-biased actors is useful for small project design but less effective for national strategy than models emphasizing aggregate incentives and structural constraints.
  4. 04Issues like the abolition of user fees involve complex macroeconomic trade-offs regarding state capacity and fiscal contracts that cannot be settled by behavioral trials alone.
  5. 05An overreliance on experimental micro-interventions risks teaching the world how to manage the symptoms of poverty more effectively instead of how to end it through institutional and political change.
