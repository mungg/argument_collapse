"While Lawrence Lessig correctly identifies the problem of 'dependency corruption,' his argument for restricting political speech relies on unproven empirical claims about public trust and legislative behavior that do not justify broad judicial deference to campaign finance regulations."

Lawrence Lessig’s essay rests on a proposition that sounds obvious but deserves more scrutiny: that campaign money has weakened public trust in Congress and impaired Congress’s ability to do its work. I share much of his distaste for the fundraising culture of Washington, and I do not doubt that money buys access. But before building a constitutional theory around “institutional corruption,” we should be more careful about the empirical claims doing the work. Low trust in government may have many causes, and campaign finance may be less central than reformers assume.

Start with trust. Americans’ confidence in Congress is indeed dismal. But it has been shaped by Vietnam, Watergate, stagflation, the Iran hostage crisis, recession, deficits, impeachment, the Iraq War, Hurricane Katrina, the financial crisis, bailouts, unemployment, and partisan polarization. In 2010, citizens are angry for concrete reasons: the economy is weak, the unemployment rate is high, the housing market remains damaged, and both parties have supported unpopular rescue measures. It is not surprising that trust is low. To attribute this primarily to campaign finance risks mistaking a background condition for the main cause.

Moreover, Americans often distrust Congress while liking their own representatives. They condemn “special interests” in the abstract but ask their members to bring home benefits, protect local industries, and attend to organized constituencies. A representative who supports dairy programs, sugar protections, defense contracts, or used-car dealer exemptions may be responding not only to campaign contributors but to employers, workers, local businesses, and voters who care intensely. What looks like corruption from a national policy perspective may look like representation from a district perspective.

This does not mean that money is irrelevant. It means causation is complicated. Lessig cites examples in which members appear to betray ideological commitments under pressure from contributors. But anecdotes cannot establish the magnitude of the effect. Legislators’ votes are strongly predicted by party, ideology, constituency, region, and interest-group alignment. Contributions often follow expected votes rather than cause them. A pro-business PAC gives to a pro-business candidate; a labor union gives to a pro-labor candidate. When the candidate later votes accordingly, is that dependence or agreement? The answer varies by case, and constitutional doctrine should not rest on assuming the worst.

Independent expenditures are even more difficult to assess. Campaign consultants believe advertising matters, and it surely can matter at the margins. But American voters are not blank slates. Party identification, economic conditions, candidate quality, incumbency, scandals, and national mood often dominate advertising effects. A flood of outside spending may help define an unknown candidate, but it can also backfire if voters perceive manipulation. The relationship between money and electoral outcomes is real but not linear. Wealthy self-financed candidates often lose. Better-funded candidates often win partly because donors can identify viable candidates, not because money mechanically purchases votes.

Lessig’s “iceberg theory” of threatened expenditures is intriguing, but it too needs evidence. A credible threat to spend against a candidate may influence behavior, but how often does this happen, and how decisive is it compared with other threats—party opposition, activist anger, editorial criticism, primary challenges, presidential pressure? Politics is filled with potential retaliation. Environmental groups threaten scorecards; anti-tax groups threaten pledges; unions threaten mobilization; newspapers threaten hostile coverage; parties threaten committee assignments. The fact that threats exist does not tell us which ones dominate or how law should respond.

This is where I find the Lochner analogy strained. Lessig argues that Justice Kennedy made an unsupported factual assertion when he wrote that the appearance of influence or access will not cause the electorate to lose faith in democracy. Perhaps so. But reformers also make broad factual assertions: that independent corporate expenditures will meaningfully increase corruption, that disclosure cannot work, that public financing will restore trust, that dependency on contributors explains legislative dysfunction. These claims may be plausible, but they are not self-evident. If courts should not rely on armchair sociology, neither should legislatures be granted unlimited deference when restricting core political speech.

A prudent First Amendment doctrine must account for uncertainty. Political speech is not like bakery hours. When government restricts electoral advocacy, the danger of self-dealing is acute. Legislators may sincerely believe they are protecting democracy while also protecting themselves. Incumbents know which speakers are troublesome. They can write rules that burden challengers, outsider groups, and last-minute criticism. The fact that Congress held hearings and compiled evidence does not eliminate the conflict of interest. Judicial skepticism is not Lochnerism in the pejorative sense; it is a recognition that the regulated activity is criticism of the regulators.

Still, Citizens United may have gone too far in declaring independent expenditures incapable of corruption. The world is not so clean. Independence is a legal category that can be manipulated, and gratitude toward outside spenders is predictable. The Court could have acknowledged a broader anti-corruption interest while requiring narrow tailoring and strong evidence. Instead it adopted language that may disable sensible regulation. Lessig is right to object to that rigidity.

But the practical response should be experimental rather than absolutist. We should test reforms that do not suppress speech: real-time disclosure, better databases, clear identification in advertisements, stronger enforcement of coordination rules, shareholder approval for corporate political spending, and voluntary public financing. States can serve as laboratories. If small-donor matching systems increase competition, reduce fundraising time, and improve trust, that evidence will strengthen the case for broader adoption. If they mainly subsidize candidates who would have run anyway or empower ideological extremes, we should know that too.

Public financing deserves particular scrutiny. Reformers often assume that small donors are more representative than large donors. Sometimes they are; often they are not. Small donors may be highly ideological, affluent enough to give, and nationally mobilized by polarizing appeals. Internet fundraising can reward outrage and purity tests. A candidate dependent on thousands of small donors outside the district may still be dependent on non-voters. Democracy vouchers might broaden participation, but only if citizens actually use them. Otherwise politically attentive groups will harvest vouchers and reproduce existing inequalities in another form.

Trust may also fail to improve even after reform. The public’s belief that “money buys results” is part of a broader suspicion of institutions—banks, parties, media, corporations, unions, and government. If Congress adopted public financing tomorrow, citizens might still see lobbying, revolving-door employment, think-tank influence, bureaucratic capture, and partisan gamesmanship. They might conclude that corruption has merely changed channels. Reformers should not promise civic renewal from campaign-finance reform alone.

The better case for reform is more modest: private fundraising consumes legislators’ time, creates access inequalities, and sometimes distorts priorities. These are real costs even if they are not the master explanation for democratic decline. A system that gives candidates incentives to seek many small contributions from constituents is preferable to one that rewards dialing for large checks. But we should pursue it as institutional improvement, not as a constitutional crusade grounded in uncertain claims about public trust.

Citizens United has provoked a panic that may cloud judgment. Corporate independent expenditures may increase; they may also be tempered by reputational risk. Many corporations will hesitate to alienate customers, employees, or shareholders by overtly backing candidates. Trade associations and nonprofits may become more active, but they were already influential. Unions, wealthy individuals, ideological groups, and media corporations remain major players. The post-decision world may be worse, but it will not be wholly new.

Lessig has done an important service by naming dependency corruption. But naming a phenomenon is not measuring it. Before we ask courts to permit restrictions on political speech in the name of institutional integrity, we need clearer evidence about mechanisms, magnitudes, and alternatives. Democracy is harmed by money, but it is also harmed by overconfident reform. The challenge after Citizens United is to improve the campaign-finance system without exaggerating what we know or empowering government to regulate political advocacy on the basis of intuitions no firmer than those we criticize in Justice Kennedy.

"While Lawrence Lessig correctly identifies 'dependency corruption' as a systemic threat to democracy, the constitutional solution should not involve empowering the government to suppress independent political speech, but rather focusing on architectural reforms that incentivize broader participation."

Lawrence Lessig is right to insist that the central problem in American campaign finance is not bags of cash changing hands. The more serious problem is the daily, lawful dependence of elected officials on a tiny class of funders whose priorities are not identical with those of the public. Any account of corruption that begins and ends with bribery will miss most of what citizens actually experience as corrupt: the phone calls returned, the meetings granted, the amendments quietly inserted, the issues never brought to a vote. A Congress may be composed mostly of honorable people and still operate within a dishonorable structure.

But I am less persuaded that Citizens United is the best place to locate the constitutional cure for that disease. The decision is troubling, and Justice Kennedy’s confidence that independent expenditures cannot corrupt is too breezy. Still, the First Amendment problem is not an afterthought. When government prohibits a corporation, union, or nonprofit association from spending money to distribute a film about a presidential candidate, it is regulating political advocacy at the core of democratic debate. That should make even campaign-finance reformers uneasy.

The awkward fact in Citizens United is that the plaintiff was not General Motors or ExxonMobil but a nonprofit ideological corporation that wanted to distribute a documentary attacking Hillary Clinton. The statute did not merely regulate contributions to candidates; it prohibited certain election-season communications funded from a corporate treasury. To say that Citizens United could have spoken through a PAC is not a complete answer. PACs are burdensome, regulated vehicles, and the right to speak often includes the right not to speak through a government-designed substitute. If a conservative nonprofit can be silenced near an election, so can the Sierra Club, the ACLU, the NRA, or a small incorporated publisher of political documentaries.

Lessig’s answer is to distinguish suppression of speech from regulation aimed at “dependency corruption.” That distinction is morally powerful, but constitutionally slippery. If Congress may restrict independent political spending because it produces public distrust or creates a dependency on funders, then the question becomes: which speakers may Congress silence, and when? Corporations? Unions? Wealthy individuals? Media corporations? Advocacy nonprofits funded by wealthy donors? A great many speakers participate in “economies of influence.” The danger of a broad anti-corruption rationale is that it can become a license for incumbents to decide which concentrations of political power are too “distorting” to be heard.

That is why the Court’s narrowness, though unsatisfying, has a democratic logic. Quid pro quo corruption is not the only morally relevant corruption, but it is a relatively administrable constitutional boundary. Bribery and coordinated contributions can be regulated because they are tied directly to candidates and officeholders. Independent advocacy is different, not because it is harmless, but because prohibiting it requires the state to ration political persuasion. Once we move from “this payment bought that official act” to “this spending creates an unhealthy political dependency,” courts must judge the legitimacy of political influence itself. That judgment is hard to make without favoring some speakers over others.

Lessig’s Lochner analogy therefore cuts both ways. He is right that the Court should not simply announce, without evidence, that access and influence will not damage public faith in democracy. The assertion is implausible. Americans plainly believe that money matters too much in politics, and Congress’s miserable approval ratings cannot be separated from the perception that organized money receives privileged treatment. But Lochner also warns against judges constitutionalizing their own theory of proper political economy. If the Court should not impose laissez-faire economics on legislatures, neither should it too readily bless legislative attempts to equalize political influence by suppressing disfavored speakers.

The deeper problem is that both sides are tempted by an impossible aspiration: a politics cleansed of unequal influence. Representative government has always involved organized interests, wealthy patrons, partisan newspapers, labor unions, churches, trade associations, and ideological movements. Madison did not think factions could be eliminated; he thought their effects could be controlled through institutional design. The question is not whether some citizens will have more influence than others. They will. The question is whether our institutions can prevent that inequality from hardening into dependence.

On that point Lessig’s proposed remedies are far more promising than his constitutional critique. Small-donor public financing, matching funds, and democracy vouchers attack dependency without banning speech. They change the incentives of candidates rather than silence independent speakers. If members of Congress could raise competitive sums by appealing to large numbers of ordinary citizens, they would spend less time cultivating lobbyists, PACs, and wealthy contributors. The New York City matching-funds model, whatever its imperfections, suggests that public matching can diversify the donor pool and make small contributions politically meaningful. A national system along those lines would do more to restore trust than another round of prohibitions vulnerable to judicial invalidation.

Disclosure also deserves more defense than Lessig gives it. He is right that disclosure cannot reveal every threat, wink, or implied promise. The “iceberg” problem is real: a corporation or union need not spend millions if candidates know it credibly could. But secrecy makes the problem far worse. Voters, journalists, shareholders, and competitors should be able to know who is financing political messages. Disclosure will not eliminate dependency corruption, but it can discipline it. The Court in Citizens United upheld disclosure requirements by a lopsided vote, and reformers should exploit that opening aggressively.

There are also corporate-governance reforms that do not require banning speech. If corporations are to spend treasury funds in elections, shareholders should have meaningful notice and perhaps approval rights. Political spending by managers may not reflect the views of investors, employees, or consumers. Requiring internal accountability would respect the First Amendment interest in political association while reducing the agency problem created when corporate officers deploy other people’s money for political ends.

Finally, Congress can tighten rules on coordination. The formal line between independent and coordinated spending is too easy to manipulate. Lessig is convincing when he describes a world of nods and winks. If “independent” expenditures are functionally planned with campaigns, they should be treated as contributions and limited accordingly. That is not censorship; it is enforcement of the very distinction on which Citizens United relies.

Lessig’s most important contribution is to shift attention from villainy to architecture. We do not need to believe that members of Congress are crooks to see that their dependence on funders deforms representation. Yet the remedy should be architectural as well. Build alternative funding channels. Require sunlight. Police coordination. Empower shareholders and members of associations. Reduce the amount of time candidates spend dialing for dollars.

Citizens United may make reform harder, and the Court’s complacency about corruption is unwarranted. But a democracy worth defending must be cautious about empowering Congress to prohibit political advocacy, even advocacy financed by corporations. The better path is not to make some speakers quieter. It is to make representatives less dependent on the few who can afford to speak the loudest.

"While the Supreme Court’s legal reasoning in Citizens United is detached from political reality, the most effective response to institutional corruption is to build a robust public financing system that makes dependence on ordinary citizens more viable than dependence on wealthy donors."

The most convincing part of Lawrence Lessig’s essay is not the constitutional theory but the description of how Washington feels to anyone who has spent time near campaigns. Candidates do not need to be bribed to be influenced. They need to raise money. That fact structures calendars, conversations, staffing, committee assignments, issue priorities, and courage. It determines who gets called back quickly and who gets a form letter. Citizens United matters because it adds another set of actors to an already crowded room of people whom candidates must anticipate, placate, or fear.

The public often imagines campaign fundraising as a series of dramatic encounters: a donor offers a check, a politician offers a vote. That is mostly not how it works. The routine is more banal and more corrosive. A member has call time for two hours. A finance director hands over lists. The member asks for money from people who have given before, people who care about pending legislation, people who attended an industry event, people who know other people. There is no explicit promise. There usually does not need to be. Everyone understands the relationship. The donor wants access and sympathy; the candidate wants resources and future support. Both may sincerely believe in the same policies. The dependency is no less real for being sincere.

Lessig’s phrase “institutional corruption” gives moral vocabulary to that routine. It is not fair to describe most members of Congress as crooks. Many work punishing hours, master difficult issues, and make sacrifices that cynics ignore. But they operate in a system that constantly asks them to consider the fundraising implications of political judgment. A member who says, “I don’t think about the money,” is either unusually saintly or not being candid. Even if money does not change final votes, it changes agendas. It affects which bills get energy, which amendments are worth angering people over, which oversight fights are pursued, and which issues are quietly left for another day.

Citizens United’s defenders are right about one thing: independent expenditures are not contributions. A candidate cannot deposit an outside group’s money into her campaign account. Sometimes outside spending is even unwelcome. A badly made ad can distract from the campaign’s message or create backlash. But in competitive races, helpful outside spending is valuable. Campaigns know who is helping them. They know which groups can come in late with mail, television, radio, phone calls, or now increasingly online ads. They know which groups can punish them in a primary. The legal wall against coordination is important, but only a lawyer would think it eliminates influence.

There are many ways to signal without coordinating. A candidate gives a public speech emphasizing an opponent’s vote. An outside group sees the clip and turns it into an ad. A party committee releases polling themes. Consultants move between campaigns and independent groups in different cycles. Donors attend conferences where everyone discusses priorities in general terms. No one says, “Please spend $500,000 in the third week of October on suburban women.” They do not have to. Modern politics is professionalized enough that people can read the same map.

Lessig’s “iceberg” theory also matches campaign reality. The threat of spending often matters as much as the spending. If a group has a reputation for attacking candidates who cross it, members notice. They do not need to receive a threat letter. They remember what happened to someone else. In Congress, caution spreads by example. The first expensive defeat teaches the lesson; the next ten members learn it for free. That is why disclosure of actual expenditures, while necessary, misses part of the influence. The most effective political money may be the money that never has to be spent.

Where I part company with some reformers is on the ease of solutions. Public financing is essential, but it must be designed by people who understand campaigns. If the grant is too small, serious candidates will opt out. If spending limits are too rigid, participants will be defenseless against outside attacks. If qualifying thresholds are too high, challengers will not qualify; if too low, funds will be wasted on vanity campaigns. If the system is slow, money will arrive after the race is already defined. Reform that looks elegant in a seminar can fail in October.

The Fair Elections model of small contributions plus public matching is promising because it preserves a test of political support. Candidates must find real people willing to give small amounts. That process is healthy. It forces campaigns to organize. But matching funds should be generous enough to let participants communicate through the media voters actually use. Reformers sometimes speak as if paid advertising is inherently corrupt. It is not. Advertising is how many voters learn anything about down-ballot races. A publicly financed candidate who cannot afford television in an expensive media market, or cannot respond to a barrage of mail, is not empowered.

A voucher system may be even more democratic, but it raises administrative questions. How are vouchers distributed? Can they be assigned online without fraud? What happens to unused vouchers? Can parties collect them? Can outside organizations bundle them? Bundling may be good if it helps citizens organize, but it may also create new brokers. Churches, unions, advocacy groups, and local machines could become voucher harvesters. That may still be better than dependence on wealthy donors, but we should be honest that every funding system creates intermediaries.

Public financing must also be paired with aggressive disclosure. Voters should know, quickly and clearly, who is paying for outside messages. “Paid for by Americans for a Better Tomorrow” tells them almost nothing. The true major funders should be listed in a database that reporters, opponents, and citizens can search immediately. Advertisements should identify top funders in plain language. If a trade association pays, voters should know which companies supplied the money for that political effort. Sunlight will not eliminate influence, but darkness guarantees abuse.

Coordination rules need updating too. The formal tests often lag behind campaign practice. Common vendors, former staff, strategic information posted for outside groups, and fundraising by candidates for nominally independent entities all deserve scrutiny. Enforcement must be faster. A penalty imposed two years after an election is just a cost of doing business. The Federal Election Commission, divided by design and often paralyzed, is not up to the task. If we are serious about independence, we need an enforcement body capable of acting during campaigns without becoming a partisan weapon. That is difficult, but the current system invites evasion.

Lessig’s constitutional criticism of Justice Kennedy is largely right. The Court’s confidence that independent expenditures do not create corruption or its appearance is detached from political practice. But reformers should not put all their energy into denouncing the Court. For the foreseeable future, expenditure limits face a hostile judiciary. The more immediate path is to make participation in a better system attractive enough that candidates choose it and voters reward it. If a candidate opts into public financing, refuses large checks, and builds a campaign on small donors, that can itself become a political asset. Reform must be not only legal architecture but campaign message.

Citizens can help by changing what they honor. Too often, fundraising prowess is treated as proof of seriousness. Reporters write about quarterly totals as if they were batting averages. Parties recruit candidates who can self-finance or tap wealthy networks. Donors become kingmakers because everyone agrees to treat money as viability. Public financing can change incentives, but political culture must change too. A candidate with broad small-dollar support should be considered more legitimate than one with a few max-out events in Washington.

The danger after Citizens United is not that corporations alone will own elections. Many corporations will be cautious; some spending will come through associations; unions and ideological groups will spend too. The danger is that candidates will operate under an expanded field of anticipated attack and reward, making them even more risk-averse and funder-conscious. That is dependency corruption in practical terms. It narrows the space for judgment.

The solution is to give candidates another way to survive. A member who can fund a campaign through constituents, small donors, vouchers, and public matching can say no more often. Not always; politics will never be pure. But more often is enough to matter. Democracy after Citizens United will not be saved by pretending money is not speech or by trusting disclosure alone. It will be improved by building campaigns that make dependence on ordinary citizens the easiest, most reliable, and most honorable path to office.

**The Framers Did Not Authorize the Speech Police**

Lawrence Lessig's invocation of the framers is rhetorically masterful and, on examination, mostly anachronistic. He builds his case for a broad regulatory power over political speech on the foundation of a single phrase—"dependent upon the People alone"—as if Madison and Hamilton would have nodded approvingly at the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act. They would not have. They would have been astonished and probably appalled.

Begin with what the framers actually did about money in politics: nothing. There is no provision in the Constitution regulating campaign expenditures, contributions, or political advertising, because in the eighteenth-century imagination these activities were precisely the activities that the First Amendment, and the broader culture of republican liberty, were meant to protect. Federalist 57, which Lessig quotes for the proposition that members of the House should feel "an habitual recollection of their dependence on the people," is concerned with the *structural* features of representation—frequent elections, broad suffrage, the absence of property qualifications—not with regulating the content or financing of political communication. To leap from "dependence on the people" to a congressional power to suppress corporate political films during election season is to leap across two centuries of constitutional development.

Indeed, the dependencies the framers actually feared, which Lessig himself catalogs with some charm, were dependencies on *foreign* powers and on the executive—on monarchs who might buy off legislators through patronage, on hereditary aristocracies that might capture republican institutions through wealth and pedigree. The Constitution's emoluments clauses, its prohibitions on titles of nobility, and its separation of legislative and executive power all aim at these specific dangers. None of these provisions is plausibly extended to cover voluntary contributions from American citizens to American campaigns. Lessig wishes "the framers had said 'princes, foreign or domestic.'" Well, they didn't. And the reason they didn't is that they understood the difference between a political system corrupted by entrenched aristocratic power and a political system in which free citizens organize, persuade, and yes, fund advocacy on the issues they care about.

Lessig's framework runs into a deeper problem when it tries to specify which "dependencies" count as corrupting. He concedes that a representative's dependence on voters is appropriate—it is the very point of representative democracy. He claims that a representative's dependence on contributors is corrupting, because contributors are not the same set as voters. But what about the dependencies in between? A senator from Idaho is more "dependent" on potato farmers than on tech workers because potato farmers vote in larger numbers in Idaho. Is that corruption? A representative from a swing district is more "dependent" on the median voter than on her party's base. Corruption? A national party leader is "dependent" on donors in New York and California to fund races in marginal districts elsewhere. Corruption? Once you start labeling structural features of pluralist politics as "dependency corruption," you have given the regulator a license to declare any disfavored pattern of influence illegitimate. That is not a constitutional theory. It is an invitation to incumbent self-dealing.

Notice, too, the specific outcome Lessig wants regulators to be able to achieve. He wants Congress to be able to forbid Citizens United, a nonprofit corporation organized by ordinary citizens, from showing a film critical of Hillary Clinton in the run-up to a primary. Read that sentence twice. The most basic activity of self-government—organizing with fellow citizens to criticize a candidate for high office—was, under the McCain-Feingold regime that Lessig defends, a federal crime carrying potential prison time. Lessig is not wrong that political speech can have lopsided effects depending on who has more money to spend. But the response that the First Amendment authorizes to that fact is more speech, not government licensing of political films. Justice Kennedy's opinion got the basic structure right. The default is liberty. The exception is narrow.

The Lochner analogy that Lessig deploys deserves a closer look, because it cuts in the opposite direction from the one he intends. *Lochner* was wrong because it invented an unenumerated right—liberty of contract—and used that right to strike down ordinary economic regulation. The First Amendment is the precise opposite of an unenumerated right. It is the most explicit, textually rooted, historically grounded protection in the Bill of Rights. When the Court strikes down a federal speech restriction, it is enforcing the text of the Constitution, not inventing a doctrine to override it. Lessig's complaint that Kennedy made "factual speculations" rather than gathering social-science data inverts the proper allocation of burden. The First Amendment does not say "Congress shall make no law abridging the freedom of speech, unless social scientists report that the public would tolerate the abridgment." It says no law. The presumption runs against the regulator. That is not Lochnerism; that is constitutionalism.

There is also something quietly elitist about the entire "institutional corruption" framework, despite Lessig's populist gloss. The framework assumes that ordinary voters cannot evaluate political advertising, cannot discount it for source bias, cannot decide for themselves whether a candidate who took money from Wall Street is therefore unworthy of their vote. The voucher schemes Lessig endorses essentially say: the political conversation will be sanitized in advance by a public-financing apparatus, so that voters do not have to do the difficult work of discriminating among speakers. Lessig is surely right that the framers intended Congress to be dependent upon the People alone, and that our current system of private campaign finance has created a second, conflicting dependency. The statistics he cites about out-of-state donations to candidates are striking and suggest a fundamental disconnect between those who fund campaigns and those who elect representatives. When 67 percent of a Massachusetts senator's contributions come from outside the state, and 73 percent of his opponent's funding follows the same pattern, the claim that representatives are responsive to their constituents becomes difficult to sustain. This is institutional corruption in the truest sense: not the bad acts of evil individuals, but the systematic distortion of democratic accountability.

Where I find myself more skeptical is in Lessig's proposed solutions. The democracy voucher system he describes with Ackerman and Ayres is elegant in its logic—if every citizen can allocate a small amount to candidates, and candidates can receive money only from citizens in their districts, then the dependency on voters and the dependency on contributors might indeed converge. But this proposal faces a daunting political hurdle that Lessig does not adequately address: its enactment would require dismantling the very system that benefits those with the power to prevent it. Incumbent legislators, who benefit disproportionately from the current regime of private fundraising, would have to vote themselves out of their primary source of competitive advantage. The history of campaign finance reform suggests that we should be humble about the prospects for such transformational change.

The Fair Elections Now Act, with its matching small-dollar contributions, faces somewhat less daunting obstacles, but still confronts the basic problem of getting incumbents to embrace a system that might disadvantage them. Lessig notes that sponsors estimate it could raise more money for campaigns than the current system, but this estimate requires faith that small-dollar donors would materialize in sufficient numbers to replace bundled large contributions. The record of public financing regimes at the state level is mixed at best—some have shown genuine promise, but others have struggled to attract participants.

I also want to push back somewhat on Lessig's treatment of the Court's decision in Citizens United. His critique of Justice Kennedy's majority opinion is often incisive, particularly his observation that Kennedy's dismissal of the appearance-of-corruption concern lacks any empirical grounding. The Lochnerism charge is apt in a narrow sense: just as the Lochner Court substituted its own judgment about economic regulation for that of legislatures, the Citizens United Court substituted its intuitions about the effects of independent expenditures for any serious engagement with evidence. Lessig is right that courts should not be entitled to a "free pass" when invalidating legislation.

But Lessig goes too far when he suggests that the solution is simply to recognize "dependency corruption" as a constitutionally cognizable category that justifies speech restrictions. The difficulty is that any such recognition requires courts to draw lines that may be no less contestable than the lines they refused to draw in Citizens United. What counts as an "improper dependency"? If large independent expenditures create such dependencies, does that mean the Court was wrong to protect them, or merely that Congress could regulate them? The distinction matters because Lessig's own analysis acknowledges that independent expenditures are "not benign in every case"—they may matter when campaigns are aware of them and when tacit coordination is possible. But acknowledging that they sometimes matter does not tell us where to draw the line, and courts would have to draw it.

This is not a small difficulty. The history of campaign finance regulation is littered with well-intentioned laws that courts struck down, sometimes correctly and sometimes not, but almost always in ways that reflected deep disagreement about the proper relationship between money and speech. Lessig wants us to believe that if we just adopt his framework of institutional corruption, we can solve these problems. I am not so sure. The framework is valuable as a diagnostic tool—it helps us see what has gone wrong—but it does not itself provide a therapeutic prescription.

What does seem clear to me, and what Lessig's essay establishes beyond reasonable dispute, is that the current system is unsustainable. Public trust in Congress at historic lows, combined with the Citizens United decision removing limits on corporate independent expenditures, has created conditions that may produce the "great cynicism" about democratic governance that Lessig rightly warns against. Whether the answer is democracy vouchers, small-dollar matching, or something else entirely, some structural reform is necessary if we are to restore even a plausible fiction of representative government. Lessig has given us better language to describe the problem. Converting that description into durable reform will require political will that has so far been conspicuously absent.

As a matter of constitutional doctrine, Citizens United is a significant but not unprecedented decision. The doctrinal question is whether this extension was correct. Lessig argues that it was wrong because the Court should have recognized a broader concept of corruption that goes beyond quid pro quo exchange. But this argument faces serious obstacles.

The constitutional text is unhelpful. The First Amendment prohibits Congress from abridging speech, and the Fourteenth Amendment extends this prohibition to state governments. Neither text distinguishes between individual and corporate speakers or between direct and indirect political influence.

The Court's precedent is more tractable. The Court has recognized that some regulation of political speech is permissible, particularly to prevent quid pro quo corruption and the appearance thereof. Lessig is right that Austin v. Michigan Chamber of Commerce recognized a broader concept of corruption, but Citizens United effectively overruled Austin. The question is whether that overruling was justified.

This is an innovative argument, but it is not clearly supported by constitutional text or precedent.

A wealthy individual who contributes to a campaign may influence the representative, but we do not restrict such contributions on corruption grounds. Lawrence Lessig’s diagnosis of “institutional corruption” offers a remarkably lucid vocabulary for the unease that most Americans feel when they look at Washington today. By separating the venal, *quid pro quo* bribery of a Rod Blagojevich from the systemic, legal dependencies that shape congressional behavior, Lessig accurately captures why a body composed largely of well-intentioned public servants routinely produces policies that the public actively dislikes. The dependency on funders has indeed eclipsed the intended dependency on voters.

Lessig laments that the Court in *Citizens United* rejected “dependency corruption” as a justification for banning independent corporate political expenditures. He argues that if Congress can ban bribery, it should be able to ban systemic dependency. But this leaps over a massive constitutional chasm, conflating the regulation of a transaction with the regulation of political speech.

The First Amendment sets an intentionally high bar—*quid pro quo* corruption—because any broader definition, such as the “distorting effects” rationale from the now-overruled *Austin* decision, inevitably allows the government to decide whose voices are legitimate and whose are improperly loud.

But this analogy misrepresents the architecture of constitutional law. *Lochner* involved the Court inventing unenumerated economic rights to strike down labor regulations under a basic rational-basis standard. *Citizens United*, by contrast, deals with the explicitly enumerated right to free speech, which necessarily triggers strict scrutiny. When a legislature bans political speech—even speech funded by a corporate treasury—the burden is entirely on the government to prove that the ban is narrowly tailored to a compelling interest.

Kennedy did not need to prove that the public would retain faith in democracy; rather, the government failed to prove that banning independent political documentaries was the only constitutional way to save that faith. Strict scrutiny places a heavy thumb on the scale of free expression. If we abandon that standard to defer to congressional fact-finding on what constitutes “corruption,” we invite majorities to perpetually rewrite the rules of political discourse to their own advantage.

Lessig is certainly correct about the "iceberg theory"—the credible threat of a massive independent expenditure by a corporation or union will absolutely influence candidate behavior. The implicit threat of a million-dollar ad campaign against a climate-change bill is a powerful deterrent. But in a free society, threatening to vigorously and publicly criticize a legislator’s record is not a loophole; it is the essence of democratic accountability, even if we find the wealth behind the megaphone distasteful. We cannot make it illegal to threaten to criticize a politician.

Where Lessig and I find absolute agreement is in his concluding prescription. Because the First Amendment rightly prevents us from artificially lowering the ceiling of political speech, our only constitutional recourse is to raise the floor. The structural alternative to censorship is subsidization.

If we wish to break the dependency on wealthy, out-of-district funders, we must create a counter-economy of influence that makes candidates dependent on the voters they actually represent. A system that matches small-dollar donations or grants voters democracy vouchers changes the incentive structure of campaigning without requiring a single censorious ban on independent expenditures. It forces politicians to spend their time courting thousands of constituents rather than a handful of corporate executives.

As we navigate this first election cycle after *Citizens United*, the surge of outside spending is undeniably alarming. But the path out of this crisis does not run through a reversal of First Amendment protections or a nostalgic return to the patchwork regulations of McCain-Feingold. It requires us to stop trying to silence the few and start financing the voices of the many. Lessig’s "dependency corruption" is the defining political crisis of our time, but we must cure it with more democracy, not less speech.

The practical question after Citizens United is what can be done to address the legitimate concerns about money in politics. Lessig offers several proposals, and they deserve serious consideration.

Full disclosure of independent expenditures, including the identity of contributors to organizations that fund such expenditures, would give voters information they need to evaluate political messages. The Court has generally upheld disclosure requirements, and they impose less burden on speech than outright prohibitions.

Coordination rules should be clarified and strengthened. The Court's assumption that independent expenditures are truly independent may be unrealistic given the informal coordination that Lessig rightly identifies. Tighter rules about communication between campaigns and outside groups would help.

The deeper challenge is political. The people who would lose from reform—incumbents who benefit from the current system—have strong incentives to block change.

Breaking this deadlock will require building coalitions across partisan lines. The concerns Lessig identifies are not partisan concerns. Democrats and Republicans alike are dependent on wealthy contributors. Both parties should be interested in a system that reduces their collective dependency on money.

Lessig's essay provides a valuable service by framing the issue in terms that transcend partisan politics. The problem is not that Republicans or Democrats are corrupt; it is that our political system is corrupted by a structure of incentives that draws representatives away from the interests of ordinary voters.
