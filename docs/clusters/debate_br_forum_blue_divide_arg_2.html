Diversified DeepSeek

"The Democratic Party's shift toward a robust, redistributionist economic agenda was driven less by domestic coalition-building and more by the geopolitical necessity of competing with a rising China."

The lead essay by Hacker and Pierson is a landmark in a vital debate, and its central reframing is both accurate and overdue. Against simplistic laments about a party lost in the wilderness of “wokeness,” the authors marshal overwhelming evidence for a Democratic Party that has, in policy substance, made a historic U-turn back toward robust, redistributionist economic governance. Their political economy of the “density divide” is indispensable. Yet, for all its strengths, the essay contains a structural blind spot so vast it undermines its explanatory power: the near-total absence of the global economy from its analysis. The authors recount a domestic political drama of coalitions, elites, and institutional veto points, but they never ask what it is, exactly, that funded the entire progressive experiment and gave elites the confidence to attempt it. The answer is a new geopolitical rivalry that rendered the old neoliberal consensus not just politically thorny, but a material and strategic impossibility. The Democrats did not just choose to embrace industrial policy and massive domestic investment; they were forced to by the specter of a rising China.

The neoliberal economic order that Hacker and Pierson trace from Clinton’s Rubinomics through Obama’s presidency was not just a set of domestic preferences. It was the internal expression of a bipartisan foreign policy consensus: that a hyper-globalized, free-trading world, underwritten by American military hegemony, would produce peace and prosperity. China’s entry into the World Trade Organization in 2000 was the ultimate bet on that vision, predicated on the assumption that economic integration would inevitably liberalize and pacify Beijing. By the late 2010s, it was devastatingly clear that this bet had failed. China had not liberalized. It had instead used access to American markets to supercharge its own state-directed capitalism, steal intellectual property, build the world’s most formidable manufacturing base, and emerge as a genuine peer competitor and systemic rival to the United States.

This geopolitical earthquake is the missing independent variable in Hacker and Pierson’s account. It fundamentally reshuffled the deck for Democratic economic policymaking in at least three ways. First, it shattered the libertarian logic of free-market capitalism. One could not be a serious national leader and argue that the market alone should dictate the location of critical supply chains, the pace of green energy transition, or the makeup of the semiconductor industry, when China was using every tool of state power to dominate those exact sectors. Industrial policy, from the CHIPS Act to the green manufacturing credits in the Inflation Reduction Act, was not an ideological choice born of progressive organizing. It was a national security imperative that brought together hawks and doves, labor and capital. This convergence provided political cover and critical legislative votes well beyond the progressive faction the authors celebrate.

Second, the rise of China recast the politics of the working class. The authors correctly note that Democrats’ economic agenda was designed to reach white working-class voters in deindustrialized regions. What they miss is that this “reaching out” was not just through checks and care-economy jobs, but through the promise of reindustrialization itself—a promise explicitly framed in geo-strategic terms. The argument for a million new manufacturing jobs in electric vehicles and batteries is not just a social welfare appeal; it is a nationalist appeal to rebuild America’s productive might in a time of great power competition. This was a way to speak about class and dignity that transcended the cultural divides of the Trump era, a rhetorical and material pivot that the Brahmin left of the 1990s, with its celebration of the frictionless global economy, could never have authentically made.

Third, it justified the price tag. Hacker and Pierson mention Manchin’s concerns about inflation as a veto point. But the broader context was the sheer scale of spending needed to compete with China’s state-subsidized green and tech industries. When the world is framed as a zero-sum contest for the industries of the future, the trillions in spending proposed in Build Back Better stop looking like mere redistribution aimed at bridging a domestic “Blue Divide” and start looking like a down payment on national survival. The fear of falling behind China dwarfed traditional deficit concerns and allowed a Democratic president to cast a massive spending package in the language of national strength, not just social justice. The story of the new Democratic economics is not one of a party finally listening to its progressive base in a vacuum. It is the story of a superpower whose political elite, across much of the spectrum, woke up to the end of history being replaced by the clash of competing systems, and who were forced, by the harsh light of geopolitical dawn, to re-learn how to run a mixed economy.

"The Democratic Party's recent economic shift was driven primarily by a bipartisan national security consensus regarding competition with China rather than purely domestic coalitional dynamics."

The Missing Country in the Story Is China

Hacker and Pierson have produced a careful and largely persuasive account of the Democratic Party's recent leftward turn on economics, anchored almost entirely in domestic coalitional dynamics: the rise of the progressive wing, the influence of think tanks like the Roosevelt Institute, the activism that followed George Floyd, the shock of COVID. These are real factors. But there is a startling omission at the heart of their analysis, one that becomes more conspicuous the longer one reads. The word "China" does not appear. The word "Russia" does not appear. The phrase "supply chain" does not appear. The international context within which the Biden administration's industrial policy was actually conceived and sold is simply absent.

This matters because the most distinctive features of Bidenomics — the CHIPS Act, the Inflation Reduction Act's domestic-content requirements, the export controls on advanced semiconductors, the willingness to subsidize manufacturing on a scale not seen in generations — were not primarily products of the Sanders-Warren wing of the party. They were products of a bipartisan elite consensus that the United States had become dangerously dependent on Chinese manufacturing and that great-power competition required reshoring strategic industries. Jake Sullivan's "foreign policy for the middle class" was the conceptual hinge: a recognition that the geopolitical and the distributional could be welded together, with industrial subsidies serving both ends.

Read the actual political speeches surrounding the passage of CHIPS and the IRA. Read the floor statements of the Republicans who voted for CHIPS. The framing is overwhelmingly about Beijing, not about inequality. Senators who would not vote a dime for child care found tens of billions for semiconductor fabs because Taiwan looks fragile and TSMC sits at the choke point of the global economy. This is industrial policy, yes, and it has redistributive consequences, yes. But its political logic does not run through the U-shaped coalition Hacker and Pierson describe. It runs through Lockheed Martin, Intel, Micron, and the Pentagon.

Once you see this, several puzzles in the Hacker-Pierson account begin to resolve. Why did Democrats manage to pass the most ambitious industrial legislation in decades while failing to pass paid family leave and the Child Tax Credit extension? Not because Manchin was capricious. Because industrial policy aligned with a bipartisan national-security consensus and a powerful set of corporate interests, while social policy did not. Why did the party's affluent suburban wing tolerate massive subsidies to manufacturing? Because those subsidies enrich firms in which the professional-managerial class holds stock and provide a respectable patriotic frame for spending that would otherwise look like industrial favoritism. Why did the bipartisan elements of the Biden agenda survive while the partisan ones did not? Because the bipartisan elements were the ones with a China rationale.

This is not a small point. It changes the political economy of what we are seeing. Hacker and Pierson present a story in which a left-leaning party, pressured from below by progressives and activists, embraced a redistributive agenda and ran into the wall of the Senate. The actual story is more layered: a national-security-driven shift in elite consensus opened space for a partial redistributive agenda that progressives then tried to ride. When the geopolitical justification ran out — when there was no Beijing-flavored reason to make the CTC permanent — the agenda collapsed. The progressive wing did not have the autonomous power to carry the rest. It had power only insofar as its preferences overlapped with the China hawks' priorities.

There is a parallel here to a much older story. The American welfare state was largely built under the impetus of two world wars and a cold war. The GI Bill, the interstate highway system, the National Defense Education Act, even substantial parts of the postwar housing finance system, were sold as national-security imperatives. Sweden and Britain did not need this framing; the United States, with its more individualist political culture and its powerful business class, did. The Biden agenda continues this pattern. Where it could be framed as competition with China — chips, batteries, electric vehicles, critical minerals, rare earths — it advanced. Where it could not — care work, child poverty, paid leave — it stalled.

This frame also illuminates the international dimension of the leftward turn that Hacker and Pierson treat as exceptional. They note that other center-left parties have lost working-class voters and gained the highly educated. They might also have noted that those same parties — the German SPD, the French Socialists insofar as they still exist, even British Labour under Starmer — are also moving toward industrial policy, energy security, and reshoring. The synchronization is not coincidence. It is the response of advanced-economy political systems to the breakdown of the post-Cold War globalization consensus and the recognition that strategic dependence on geopolitical rivals is a vulnerability. Bidenomics is the American instantiation of a wider pattern.

The omission of China also leads Hacker and Pierson to misjudge the durability of the shift. They suggest, hopefully, that with a slightly stronger congressional hand, the 2021 agenda could be revived. But the durable parts of that agenda were durable because they had a national-security backstop. The vulnerable parts were vulnerable because they did not. Unless the Democratic coalition can find a way to give care policy, child poverty, and housing the same geopolitical valence as semiconductors — which is genuinely difficult, though demographic decline and labor force participation arguments offer some purchase — the next window will see the same pattern: industrial spending advances, social spending stalls.

There is a critical political lesson here for the left. If you want to expand the welfare state in the United States, you almost certainly have to find a way to frame your preferred policies in terms that the national-security state can endorse. This is uncomfortable. It involves alliances with people whose other commitments — to defense spending, to confrontation with China, to military deployments — many progressives find odious. But the historical record is clear: in the American context, the welfare state grows when it can plausibly claim to be making the country stronger against rivals, and stalls when it cannot. The Hacker-Pierson account treats the international as a kind of stage backdrop. It is in fact the engine of much of what they describe.

I do not mean to dismiss the domestic story they tell. The Sanders campaigns mattered. The Roosevelt Institute mattered. Sabeel Rahman and Lina Khan mattered. The energy of post-Floyd organizing mattered. These were necessary conditions. But they were not sufficient, and treating them as the whole story produces an account that cannot explain why some parts of the 2021 agenda survived and others did not, why the synchronization with other rich democracies is so striking, or why the next administration — Democratic or Republican — will continue to do industrial policy regardless of which coalition wins. The new blue divide cannot be understood without the new Cold War.

"The Democratic Party's economic shift under Biden was driven less by internal progressive evolution than by a post-neoliberal turn necessitated by global security imperatives and the collapse of the globalization consensus."

Hacker and Pierson have written a powerful account of the Democratic Party’s economic turn, but their explanation is too domesticated. They treat the Biden agenda mainly as a product of intra-party evolution: progressive pressure, the lessons of the Great Recession, the shock of COVID-19, the rise of policy entrepreneurs, and the need to hold together a U-shaped metropolitan coalition. All of that matters. But it leaves out one of the central facts of the period: the return of great-power competition and the collapse of the old globalization consensus.

The Democrats did not rediscover industrial policy in a vacuum. They rediscovered it in a world in which the United States had become anxious about China’s technological rise, semiconductor dependence, fragile supply chains, deindustrialization, energy security, and the geopolitical consequences of letting strategic production move offshore. The political economy of the Biden years cannot be understood without that shift. The CHIPS and Science Act, the domestic content rules, the battery supply chain provisions, the emphasis on manufacturing, the more skeptical approach to trade, and the fusion of climate policy with industrial strategy all reflect a world in which national security and economic policy have merged.

This does not refute Hacker and Pierson’s thesis, but it changes its meaning. The Biden agenda was not simply a leftward economic turn. It was also a nationalist turn, or at least a post-neoliberal turn shaped by security imperatives. That distinction matters because security politics can make policies possible that egalitarian politics alone cannot.

For decades, many Democrats accepted the broad premises of liberal globalization: free trade, financial integration, efficiency, consumer welfare, and the idea that distributional harms could be compensated after the fact. The compensation rarely arrived, and the political backlash did. But the decisive break with that model did not come only because progressives won arguments inside the party. It came because elites across the political spectrum began to see the old model as strategically dangerous. The pandemic exposed supply-chain fragility. Russia’s invasion of Ukraine underscored the geopolitical importance of energy and industrial capacity. China’s rise made semiconductors, artificial intelligence, telecommunications, batteries, and critical minerals matters of national power.

In that context, “industrial policy” stopped sounding like a left-wing fantasy and started sounding like prudence. The same suburban professionals who might have balked at old-style redistribution could support public investment in chips, clean energy, infrastructure, and scientific research because these programs could be framed as competitiveness, resilience, and national renewal. The politics of “make stuff in America again” are not identical to the politics of “tax the rich to fund social rights.” The former can attract support from business, labor, security hawks, climate advocates, and regional development interests. The latter depends more directly on class conflict.

This distinction helps explain why the surviving pieces of the Biden agenda look the way they do. The expanded child tax credit expired. Paid leave died. Free community college died. Much of the care agenda died. But climate industrial policy, infrastructure, semiconductor subsidies, and research spending survived. That pattern is not random. Programs that could be integrated into investment, competitiveness, and supply-chain resilience had a better chance than programs that more straightforwardly expanded the welfare state. The Inflation Reduction Act is an enormous climate law, but it is also an industrial policy law. Its structure relies heavily on tax credits, private investment incentives, domestic production, and place-based manufacturing. It is more Hamilton than Beveridge.

Hacker and Pierson acknowledge that much of Build Back Better failed, but they still treat the near miss as evidence of a broad Democratic transformation. I would put more weight on the distinction between what Democrats proposed and what the governing system accepted. The party’s aspirations may have moved left, but the enacted settlement was more selective: green investment, infrastructure, chips, modest health care subsidies, some drug pricing reforms, and limited corporate taxation. That is significant, but it is not the same as a durable social-democratic breakthrough.

The difference matters for the party’s future. If the Democrats’ economic turn depends heavily on national security and climate investment, it may be more durable in some areas and weaker in others. The politics of clean energy manufacturing in Georgia, Arizona, Michigan, and Ohio may outlast a narrow Democratic trifecta because those investments create local stakeholders, business constituencies, and jobs. Republican officials may denounce the laws while welcoming the factories. That is how industrial policy can build power: by creating material facts on the ground.

But the politics of care, child poverty, and income support remain far more vulnerable. They lack the same alliance with national security. They are easier to attack as spending. They are more dependent on federal transfers. They also require a more direct confrontation with gender and class hierarchies. The United States has long tolerated underpaid care work because the costs are hidden inside families and imposed on women, immigrants, and workers of color. Rebuilding the care economy would require not merely a subsidy but a moral and political revaluation of labor that the market has treated as cheap. That is a harder coalition to assemble than one built around semiconductor fabs and electric vehicle batteries.

The China omission also matters because it complicates the authors’ account of affluent suburban voters. Many high-income Democratic voters work in sectors deeply tied to global integration: technology, finance, consulting, higher education, medicine, law, and multinational business services. Yet many are also national-security liberals, climate liberals, or institutionalists who now accept a more active state. Their support for Bidenomics may rest less on redistributive solidarity than on a belief that the United States needs competent public investment to compete and survive. That is still a meaningful change from the Clinton era, but it is not necessarily a left-populist one.

Indeed, the Biden administration’s economic program contains several strands that sit uneasily together. There is a Sanders-Warren strand focused on inequality, labor power, antitrust, and social rights. There is a climate strand focused on decarbonization. There is a national-development strand focused on infrastructure, manufacturing, and regional revival. There is a China-competition strand focused on strategic industries. There is also a technocratic growth strand that sees public investment as a way to crowd in private capital. These factions overlapped in 2021 and 2022, but they are not the same. Their alliance may be powerful; it may also fracture.

Consider trade. Biden has largely maintained Trump’s break with the old free-trade consensus, though with different rhetoric and alliances. That shift appeals to some union voters and industrial communities. It also reflects concern about China. But protection and domestic content rules can raise costs, anger trading partners, and complicate climate goals if they slow deployment. A purely egalitarian analysis might celebrate the return of manufacturing jobs; a climate analysis might worry about bottlenecks; a geopolitical analysis might prioritize secure supply chains; a consumer-welfare analysis might object to higher prices. The “new blue” agenda is not one thing.

Inflation exposed another fault line. Hacker and Pierson note that Manchin invoked inflation to shrink the agenda. They are right that inflation became a convenient weapon against social spending, and many Build Back Better provisions would not have been major near-term inflation drivers. But inflation was not imaginary. By 2021 and 2022, voters were experiencing sharp price increases in food, energy, housing, and other essentials. The politics of abundance collided with the politics of scarcity. A party promising activist government had to show not only that it could distribute money, but that it could deliver capacity: homes, power lines, child care slots, heat pumps, chips, ports, workers, and permits.

This is where the domestic and geopolitical stories converge. The United States has spent decades underinvesting in public capacity while relying on global markets and private actors to deliver abundance. Reversing that is not just a matter of passing spending bills. It requires state competence. It requires procurement reform, permitting capacity, trained workforces, regional planning, and institutions that can coordinate investment. If Democrats promise industrial transformation and then cannot build transmission lines, housing, transit, or clean energy projects quickly enough, the new economic agenda will disappoint.

The authors are right to resist the lazy claim that Democrats care only about culture. The Biden years plainly disprove that. But to understand the party’s transformation, we need a broader frame: not just redistribution within metro America, but the reorganization of American capitalism under conditions of climate crisis, geopolitical rivalry, and supply-chain insecurity. The Democratic Party moved left partly because progressives gained influence. It moved toward industrial policy because the world changed.

The future of the new blue divide will depend on which strand dominates. If the agenda becomes primarily a subsidy program for favored industries, it may produce jobs and emissions reductions but leave inequality and care work largely untouched. If it becomes a serious social-democratic project, it must find a way to revive the child tax credit, paid leave, child care, housing reform, and labor organization. If it becomes a national-security project, it may attract bipartisan rhetorical support while narrowing the moral horizon.

Biden’s economic turn is real. But its deepest source may be less the triumph of the Brahmin left over neoliberalism than the end of the world in which neoliberalism seemed plausible. China, COVID-19, climate change, and war have forced American elites to rediscover the state. The question is whether that rediscovered state will be used merely to compete—or also to care.

"The Democratic Party’s shift toward muscular industrial policy and state intervention is primarily driven by the geopolitical and technological challenge posed by China rather than domestic electoral dynamics alone."

Jacob S. Hacker and Paul Pierson’s account of the Democratic Party’s surprising economic pivot is an insightful look at domestic coalition dynamics, but it suffers from a glaring omission: it entirely ignores the rest of the world. In reading "The New Blue Divide," one would think the Biden administration’s embrace of industrial policy, massive infrastructure spending, and the revitalization of domestic manufacturing were solely the products of progressive pressure, Covid-19 disruptions, and a desire to win back white working-class voters. The authors manage to dissect the shift in American political economy without ever mentioning the primary catalyst driving the bipartisan consensus for state intervention: the geopolitical and technological challenge of the People's Republic of China.

The shift from the neoliberal "Rubinomics" of the Clinton and Obama eras to the muscular industrial policy of the Biden years cannot be understood merely as a victory of the left over the center. It is, more accurately, a profound reassessment of national security in an era of renewed great power competition.

For three decades, the core assumption of both Democratic and Republican economic establishments was that global economic integration—facilitated by free trade, off-shoring, and frictionless supply chains—would inherently serve American interests, ultimately liberalizing authoritarian regimes like China. By the time Biden took office, that assumption had collapsed. The COVID-19 pandemic laid bare the perilous fragility of America's supply chains, revealing that the U.S. was critically dependent on a strategic rival for everything from basic personal protective equipment to the active pharmaceutical ingredients in crucial medications.

Simultaneously, China’s aggressive state-backed investments in strategic sectors—artificial intelligence, electric vehicles, quantum computing, and advanced manufacturing—demonstrated that the laissez-faire approach of the West was losing ground to state capitalism. When Hacker and Pierson point to the "unprecedented investments in green and high tech manufacturing" and the "requirements or incentives for U.S.-made materials" in Biden's agenda, they frame these purely as redistributive measures or sops to organized labor.

But look at the CHIPS and Science Act, a cornerstone of this new paradigm that the authors scarcely analyze. The push to subsidize domestic semiconductor manufacturing was not born out of a desire to appease the Congressional Progressive Caucus or to manage the "U-shaped" coalition. It was driven by the terrifying realization that over 90% of the world's most advanced semiconductors are manufactured in Taiwan, sitting squarely in the crosshairs of the People's Liberation Army. If that supply chain were severed, the U.S. economy would face an immediate, catastrophic depression, and the U.S. military would lose access to the components necessary for modern defense systems.

The Biden administration’s economic agenda is an attempt to achieve economic security and strategic autonomy. The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA), while undeniably a historic climate bill, is also a profound piece of geoeconomic legislation. Its stringent domestic content requirements for electric vehicle tax credits are explicitly designed to freeze China out of the American clean energy supply chain and force companies to on-shore or "friend-shore" the processing of critical minerals.

Hacker and Pierson wonder how the Democratic establishment broke so sharply with the fiscal restraint and free-trade dogmas of the past. The answer is that the national security establishment and the economic establishment merged. The "Bigger, Bolder, Broader" agenda was politically viable because it appealed not just to climate activists and labor unions, but to defense hawks and foreign policy realists. This is precisely why pieces of this agenda, like the CHIPS Act and the infrastructure bill, garnered bipartisan support in a way that the purely social-welfare components of Build Back Better did not.

The failure of the authors to acknowledge the China factor leads to a misinterpretation of the durability of this economic shift. They worry that the moment has passed and that the "Brahmin Left" might revert to the mean. However, because the structural driver of this new industrial policy is the inescapable reality of Sino-American competition, the shift is likely far more durable than they assume. Even if Republicans take total control of government, the drive to subsidize domestic manufacturing in strategic sectors and restrict technology transfers to China will continue.

By viewing the Democratic Party's transformation purely through the lens of domestic electoral geography and factional disputes, Hacker and Pierson miss the defining global dynamic of our time. The "New Blue Divide" is certainly a fascinating domestic political phenomenon, but the new American political economy is being forged in the crucible of a new Cold War. The return of the active state is not just a choice made by Democratic elites to placate their progressive base; it is an absolute necessity for American survival in a multipolar world.

"The Democratic Party's shift toward bold state intervention and industrial policy was primarily driven by the existential necessity of the climate crisis, which served as the essential structural scaffolding for their new economic agenda."

In "The New Blue Divide," Jacob S. Hacker and Paul Pierson identify a remarkable shift in the Democratic Party's economic approach, highlighting the ambitious domestic agenda pursued in the first two years of the Biden administration. They skillfully map the tension within the Democrats’ "U-shaped" coalition—an alliance of affluent, highly educated suburbanites and a diverse, struggling urban working class. However, in their search for the political mechanisms that allowed this coalition to coalesce around massive state intervention, they underplay the singular, existential forcing mechanism that has fundamentally rewritten the rules of American political economy: the climate crisis.

Hacker and Pierson mention climate initiatives as part of the broader Build Back Better (BBB) package, grouping them alongside childcare, infrastructure, and healthcare as examples of a revived left-wing economic agenda. But climate change is not merely one issue among many on a laundry list of progressive demands; it is the structural scaffolding upon which the new Democratic economic agenda has been built. The transition to a decarbonized economy is the specific catalyst that forced the Democratic Party to abandon the neoliberal incrementalism of the Clinton and Obama eras and embrace the tools of industrial policy, massive public investment, and market intervention.

For decades, the tension Hacker and Pierson describe—between a professional-managerial "Brahmin Left" and a working-class base—seemed insurmountable, particularly on environmental issues. In the Obama era, as the authors briefly note, efforts like cap-and-trade were perceived as elitist projects that would inflict economic pain on working-class communities and industrial unions. Environmentalism was often framed in terms of sacrifice, austerity, and carbon pricing, which inherently alienated the lower end of the U-shaped coalition.

The breakthrough of the 2020s was a paradigm shift in climate politics, driven by the urgency of the crisis and the advocacy of groups surrounding the Green New Deal framework. The realization was profound: decarbonizing the American economy cannot be achieved through punitive taxes or regulatory tweaks alone. It requires building things—solar arrays, wind farms, advanced electrical grids, electric vehicles, and new transit systems—on a scale not seen since World War II.

This requirement for massive physical mobilization transformed climate policy from a vector of austerity into a vehicle for economic expansion and job creation. Suddenly, the interests of affluent, climate-anxious suburbanites aligned with the material needs of the working class and organized labor. The affluent voters in the U-shaped coalition, deeply alarmed by apocalyptic wildfires and superstorms, were willing to tolerate—and even demand—unprecedented levels of federal spending to address the crisis. Simultaneously, the working-class base and labor unions saw the promise of millions of high-paying, non-exportable jobs in construction, manufacturing, and infrastructure maintenance.

This climate-driven synthesis is what made the "Bigger, Bolder, Broader" agenda possible. When Joe Manchin ultimately killed the social welfare components of Build Back Better—the expanded child tax credit, universal pre-K, paid leave—what survived? The climate provisions. The resulting Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) was the largest investment in clean energy in world history. This wasn't an accident. The climate investments survived because they represented the true, durable point of intersection within the Democratic coalition.

Furthermore, Hacker and Pierson note that Democrats wove "environmental justice" into their plans, using it to address racial inequality without provoking a backlash. This is a critical insight, but it deserves deeper exploration. Frontline communities—disproportionately Black, Latino, and Indigenous—bear the brunt of fossil fuel pollution and climate disasters. By directing a significant percentage of federal climate investments specifically to these disadvantaged communities (as mandated by the Biden administration's Justice40 initiative), the Democratic Party found a mechanism to deliver targeted, race-conscious material benefits under the universalizing banner of climate action.

The authors wonder if the Democratic Party's pivot is sustainable or merely a "near miss" artifact of 2021. If viewed purely as a traditional welfare-state expansion, its future is indeed precarious, given the constraints of the density tax and a hostile Supreme Court. But when viewed as a climate-driven industrial policy, the agenda possesses a unique durability. The physical realities of a warming planet will continue to exert relentless pressure on the political system. The economic logic of the energy transition—driven now by the plummeting costs of renewables and global competition for clean tech dominance—has an inertia that transcends short-term electoral cycles.

Hacker and Pierson have correctly identified that the Democratic Party has undergone a profound transformation, breaking with half a century of economic orthodoxy despite a coalition that theorists predicted would demand moderation. But to fully understand why this happened, we must look beyond the mechanics of negative polarization, progressive primary challenges, or COVID-19 stimulus. The Democratic Party became the party of massive state intervention because the atmospheric physics of the 21st century left them with no other choice. The U-shaped coalition holds together not just because of shared cultural antipathies toward the right, but because both the affluent suburbs and the diverse working class share a material interest in securing a habitable future.
