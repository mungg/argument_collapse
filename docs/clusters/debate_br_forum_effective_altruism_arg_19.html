"While effective altruism provides a necessary correction to moral provincialism, it risks devaluing the essential role that personal attachments, local obligations, and emotional connections play in a meaningful ethical life."

Peter Singer is right that our moral instincts are unreliable. We are moved by the face on the screen more than by the statistical child abroad. We overvalue the familiar and undervalue the distant. We give to institutions that have touched our own lives while strangers die of preventable causes. Effective altruism is a needed rebuke to moral provincialism. But Singer’s essay also risks replacing one distortion with another: a picture of ethical life too thinly governed by calculation, too suspicious of partiality, and too quick to treat emotional attachment as an obstacle rather than a source of moral knowledge.

The phrase “warm glow giving” does a great deal of work in Singer’s argument. It names a real failing: donations made mainly to purchase the feeling of being generous. Anyone who has attended a gala for an institution with a large endowment, watched donors bid lavishly for recognition, or received glossy mailings that say almost nothing about outcomes knows the problem. Still, warmth is not the enemy of goodness. Moral life begins in attachment. Most of us learn to care for strangers by first being cared for, and by caring for particular people whose needs are not abstractions. The ability to see a child as a child, rather than as a unit in a cost-effectiveness table, is not a primitive impulse to be overcome. It is the seed from which wider concern grows.

Singer acknowledges that parents may give priority to their own children. But he treats this as a concession to psychological reality and social usefulness. We cannot realistically love everyone equally; children thrive in close families; therefore some partiality may be permitted. This is too grudging. Special obligations are not merely tolerable deviations from impartial benevolence. They are constitutive of a human life. A daughter has reasons to care for her aging mother that I do not have in the same way. A teacher has duties to her students. Neighbors owe one another forms of attention that cannot be discharged by sending money overseas. These obligations are not always trumped by greater measurable need elsewhere.

Consider the parent who spends money on a modest birthday party rather than donating every available dollar to malaria prevention. Singer is right that lavishness is morally troubling in a world of extreme poverty. But not every expenditure on family or community is indulgence. Rituals, celebrations, visits, meals, and gifts sustain relationships. They tell people: you are known, remembered, and loved. These goods do not become unreal because they are difficult to compare with bed nets.

The Make-A-Wish example is therefore more complicated than Singer allows. I agree that, as a matter of philanthropic priority, saving lives is more urgent than granting wishes. If I were deciding where to send a large donation, I would choose the malaria charity. But the Batkid story was not only a transfer of resources to one child. It was an act of civic imagination. Thousands of people gave time, attention, and playfulness to a family that had endured fear and illness. That does not make it more important than preventing death. But it does mean that the value was not captured by dividing dollars by wishes. Communities need occasions on which people practice public tenderness. A society with efficient charities but few such practices would be morally impoverished.

Effective altruists sometimes answer that no one is against tenderness; they are merely asking what we should do with our marginal charitable dollars. That narrower claim is more persuasive. But movements are shaped by their rhetoric, and Singer’s rhetoric can suggest that the highest moral intelligence is detached, comparative, and impersonal. Many people will hear that as an accusation that their local loyalties are childish. The risk is not only alienating potential donors; it is misunderstanding how moral motivation works.

Singer celebrates reason’s capacity to expand our horizons. Good. But reason rarely acts alone. The civil rights movement, anti-apartheid struggle, abolitionism, and campaigns against cruelty to animals have all depended on images, songs, friendships, churches, unions, grief, anger, and hope. They relied on disciplined emotion, not emotion’s defeat. Even effective altruism itself depends on a moral picture: the image of the drowning child, the burning building, the saved life. Its champions are not pure calculators. They are moved by a vision of impartial rescue.

The question, then, is how to educate emotion rather than bypass it. We should learn to feel the force of distant suffering, not merely acknowledge it in spreadsheets. We should tell stories about children saved from malaria as vividly as stories about Batkid. We should connect donors to global health work without turning recipients into props. We should build habits of giving that combine evidence with compassion, humility, and gratitude.

A care-centered response to Singer would say: yes, give more, and give better. Stop pretending that a gift to an already wealthy alma mater is morally equivalent to a gift that prevents disease among the poorest. Stop allowing national borders to determine whose pain counts. But do not imagine that the ethical life consists primarily in maximizing an impersonal quantity called “the good.” It also consists in fidelity, presence, responsibility, and love.

Effective altruism is most attractive when it disciplines our self-indulgence. It is least attractive when it appears to discipline our humanity. The challenge is to let evidence correct our biases without letting calculation flatten our moral world. We need fewer self-congratulatory donations. We also need more forms of attachment that train us to care beyond ourselves. The heart can mislead, but without it reason has nowhere to begin.
