"The authors' attempt to rescue economics from neoliberalism fails because it relies on the discipline's existing methods and market-centric frameworks rather than addressing the structural and methodological flaws that created current inequalities."

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman have written a thoughtful essay, but it suffers from a fundamental limitation: it takes the current structure of economics as given and attempts to work within it rather than questioning whether that structure is adequate to the challenges we face. The authors want to rescue economics from its association with neoliberalism, but they do not confront the possibility that the discipline's core assumptions and methods are themselves part of the problem.

Consider the claim that "economics has become even richer in the intervening four decades" and that contemporary graduate students "need not even be that bright" to produce models that yield predetermined policy conclusions. This is presented as a cause for optimism—if the models are flexible enough to support any conclusion, then they can equally well support progressive policies. But this reasoning misses the point. The problem is not that economics can be used to justify any conclusion; the problem is that the discipline's reliance on mathematical modeling systematically channels research toward questions that can be answered with formal analysis and away from questions that resist such treatment.

Power asymmetries, institutional contexts, historical path dependencies, and the distribution of political influence are not easily captured in the frameworks that dominate academic economics. When Rodrik writes that "the competitive model is rarely the right benchmark for understanding the problems and suggesting solutions," he is right—but he does not explain why economists continue to use the competitive model as the starting point, or what would be required to displace it. The behavioral revolution has challenged homo economicus, and economic history has reintroduced time and context, but these remain subfields rather than transformations of the discipline's core.

The authors propose a series of policy reforms—wage boards, financial regulation, taxation of multinationals, social insurance, technological stewardship—that are individually reasonable but collectively insufficient. They do not address the structural drivers of inequality, including the concentration of ownership in capital markets, the role of lobbying and campaign finance in shaping policy, and the relationship between economic and political power. Their proposal to "rebalance power" through unions and regulation is sensible as far as it goes, but it does not engage with the deeper question of why such imbalances arose in the first place or what would be required to prevent them from reasserting themselves.

The essay's treatment of globalization is particularly problematic. The authors support "hyper-globalization" but argue that trade agreements should include "social safeguards" to protect domestic social contracts. This is a reasonable position, but it underestimates the difficulty of implementing such safeguards within the framework of existing trade agreements. The deals negotiated over the past three decades have been designed explicitly to limit the policy space available to national governments, and reversing that dynamic will require more than clever drafting. It will require a fundamental renegotiation of the terms on which global economic integration has been organized.

The authors write that "nothing in laissez-faire guarantees that growth will be equitable or globalization sustainable." This is true, but it sidesteps the question of what kind of economic system would actually deliver inclusive prosperity. The authors assume that markets, properly regulated, can serve this purpose. But the historical record suggests otherwise. The periods of relatively equitable growth in the mid-twentieth century were characterized not by market-friendly regulation but by extensive public ownership, strong unions, capital controls, and industrial policy. These arrangements were not aberrations from the logic of capitalism; they were responses to the structural pressures generated by capitalism itself, pressures that have not disappeared.

What is needed is not a better set of policy proposals but a more fundamental rethinking of economic organization. This does not mean abandoning the insights of economics, which include valuable tools for analyzing incentives, allocation, and trade-offs. But it does mean recognizing that those tools are embedded in a broader framework of social and political institutions that must itself be subject to democratic deliberation and control.

  2. 02The proposed policy reforms are individually reasonable but collectively insufficient because they do not address the deeper structural drivers of inequality and political influence.

  4. 04The essay incorrectly assumes that properly regulated markets can deliver inclusive prosperity, ignoring historical evidence that equitable growth required extensive non-market interventions like public ownership and capital controls.
  5. 05True inclusive prosperity requires a fundamental rethinking of economic organization and democratic control over social institutions rather than just a better set of mainstream economic policy proposals.
