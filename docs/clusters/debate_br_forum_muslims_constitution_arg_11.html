"The domestic counterterrorism apparatus and Islamophobia are not merely legal or cultural pathologies, but are functional ideologies meticulously designed to justify and sustain U.S. imperialist domination and resource extraction in the Muslim world."

Akbar and Theoharis have rendered an unflinching account of the domestic counterterrorism apparatus, and for that they deserve gratitude. Yet their analysis remains, in a crucial sense, incomplete. They trace the web of surveillance, prosecution, and border policy back to a bipartisan consensus and a history of colonialism. What they do not do is name the engine that drives the entire machine: the U.S. empire’s relentless need to dominate the oil-rich, strategically vital lands of the Muslim world, and to crush any form of self-determination that challenges its hegemony. Without this imperial dimension at the very center of the frame, the domestic persecution of American Muslims appears as a kind of cultural pathology—a “prejudice” that can be educated away, or a set of “legal paradigms” that can be reformed. In truth, Islamophobia is not an irrational bias; it is a functional ideology, meticulously produced to justify permanent war and the extraction of wealth from the Global South. To dismantle it, we must dismantle the empire that requires it.

The lead essay cites the 23,000 bombs the United States dropped in 2015 on six Muslim-majority countries. That staggering number should be the starting point, not a closing illustration. Why does the U.S. bomb these nations? The official answer is to fight terrorism, but the historical record tells a different story. From the 1953 CIA-orchestrated coup against Mossadegh in Iran, to the arming of the mujahideen in Afghanistan, to the destruction of Libya after 2011, U.S. policy has consistently sought to install client regimes, open markets, control oil flows, and eliminate independent nationalist or Islamist movements that refuse subordination. The rise of groups like al-Qaeda and ISIS is not a cosmic eruption of evil; it is a direct, predictable consequence of decades of intervention, support for dictatorships, and the brutal enforcement of a neoliberal order. The United States created the very “terrorism” it now purports to fight, and it uses the specter of the Muslim terrorist to discipline populations both at home and abroad.

Viewed through this lens, the domestic War on Terror is not an unfortunate spillover from foreign wars but an integral component of empire management. The surveillance of American Muslims, the FBI’s infiltration of mosques, the CVE programs that monitor political speech—all of these serve to neutralize the one constituency that could build a politically effective antiwar movement rooted in diasporic connections. When the state labels criticism of U.S. foreign policy as a “risk factor” for radicalization, it is not merely being overzealous; it is engaged in a conscious project of suppressing dissent against its imperial project. The material support statute functions as a direct criminalization of cross-border solidarity. Sending money to a charity in Palestine, providing humanitarian aid in Somalia, even tweeting support for a self-determination movement—all become “terrorism” because they represent a material challenge to the U.S.-led order.

The airport protests, for all their energy, exposed the tragic limits of liberal dissent. The chants of “No Ban, No Wall” and the defense of refugees as “innocent” victims left wholly untouched the reason there are millions of refugees in the first place: the systematic destruction of their homelands by U.S. bombs and economic sanctions. How many of the protesters who waved signs calling for compassion toward a Syrian family would have been willing to also demand an immediate end to the CIA’s covert arming of rebel groups, or to call for reparations for the Iraqi people? The liberal framework is one that wants to care for the wounded without ever questioning who wields the knife. That is not solidarity; it is a sanitized paternalism.

The essay’s critique of the Khizr Khan moment is incisive but does not go far enough. The Constitution that Khan brandished is the same document that has been interpreted to permit the imperial presidency, the drone assassination program, and the indefinite detention at Guantánamo. To invoke the Constitution as a shield while ignoring how it has served as a sword for endless war is to place a naive faith in parchment barriers. The Khan family’s sacrifice was real, but the state’s demand for Muslim blood as the price of conditional belonging is a classic colonial technique, familiar from the French empire’s recruitment of Senegalese tirailleurs and the British use of Indian sepoys. The good Muslim is the one who dies for the empire, and even then his family’s loyalty is always subject to review.

A genuinely anti-imperialist politics begins with the recognition that freedom for Muslims at home is impossible without freedom for the peoples of the Muslim world abroad. That means demanding not merely the end of the travel ban but the total cessation of U.S. military operations across the Middle East, Africa, and South Asia—the closure of all bases, the grounding of all drones, the termination of arms sales to Israel, Saudi Arabia, and Egypt. It means repudiating the entire bipartisan consensus on American exceptionalism and military intervention, a consensus that was best articulated by Obama himself when he spoke of “the world’s oldest constitutional democracy” bearing its inevitable burdens. It means seeing the movement for Black lives not as an analogous struggle, but as an overlapping front in the same war against imperial domination. The police who kill Black people in U.S. cities are trained by Israeli occupation forces; the tear gas canisters fired at Standing Rock are the same as those dropped on Gaza. The empire is a single system, and it must be opposed as one.

I am often told that this analysis is too radical, that it asks for too much, that we must work within the system. But the system is the crisis. The lead essay’s call to “contest these practices” on the terms of legal reform and public debate implicitly reaffirms the legitimacy of the very state that is bombing children. What we need is not contestation but abolition—the abolition of the national security state, of the imperial military apparatus, and of the economic structure that assigns Muslim lives, whether in Baghdad or Dearborn, a lesser value. That will require building a different kind of power, one rooted in international solidarity, mutual aid, and direct action. It will require that we stop asking for a seat at the imperial table and start overturning it.

The Trump presidency is a symptom of imperial decline, a fascistic turn that has stripped away the liberal veneer of the Obama years. This is a frightening moment, but also an instructive one. The liberal middle is collapsing. We now face a stark choice: retreat into a nostalgic defense of the pre-Trump order, with all its violence and hypocrisy, or push forward toward a world in which no community is required to prove its humanity by dying for empire. I choose the latter. The only Muslim ban that will ever be just is a ban on empire itself.
