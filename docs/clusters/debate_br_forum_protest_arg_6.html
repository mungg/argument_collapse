Diversified DeepSeek

"An absolutist defense of the right to protest is incompatible with the university's core function as a community of learning, which requires clear and enforceable regulations to maintain academic life."

It is painful to read Alex Gourevitch’s essay as someone who has spent decades in university leadership and who deeply believes in the value of protest. I agree with much of his diagnosis of the structural vulnerabilities that made universities so susceptible to the Trump administration’s bullying: the overreliance on federal research dollars, the donor-driven model of prestige, the administrative bloat, and the erosion of shared governance. These are all real, and they have produced a leadership class that is often more committed to institutional preservation than to institutional integrity. And I agree fully that the administration’s demand to gut departments and blacklist certain speech in exchange for federal funds is an outrageous assault on academic autonomy that must be fought with every tool available.

But Gourevitch’s solution—an absolutist defense of the right to protest, with only the most minimal restrictions allowed—is simply not realistic for any university that hopes to function as a community of learning. His narrative of the past year elides the genuine governance crisis that large-scale, open-ended encampments create. He writes that “in the vast majority of cases, everyone could find their way to where they needed to be,” and that disruptions were “inconvenient, but only that.” That is not the testimony I heard on the ground, from faculty whose courses were impossible to conduct in buildings near the encampments, from staff whose workplaces became inaccessible, and from students who did not feel unsafe because of the content of chants but because of the sheer physical congestion and the presence of large, often aggressive crowds. A university is not a city park; it is a residential community with thousands of inhabitants who have legitimate expectations of quiet study, predictable access, and a secure environment. To label all those expectations as mere signs of a “therapeutic consumerism” is to dismiss the ordinary conditions necessary for academic life.

Moreover, the idea that universities should adopt a viewpoint-neutral posture is, in principle, correct. The problem is that neutrality in these circumstances is nearly impossible to achieve. When a large, ideologically committed group occupies the symbolic center of campus for weeks, it changes the political climate for everyone. It may not explicitly bar anyone from entering, but it creates an atmosphere of implicit pressure: students who disagree with the protesters may avoid the area, self-censor in class, or feel that the university has tacitly endorsed the protest. That, too, is a form of exclusion, and it is one that administrators cannot simply ignore. The difficult task is to balance the right of protesters to make their case with the right of others not to have their educational environment dominated by a single, all-consuming political message. Gourevitch’s essay offers no guidance on how to strike that balance. He seems to think that any regulation beyond the most minimal is per se illegitimate.

This is not an abstract problem. During the height of the protests, many of my colleagues in administration spent their days in triage—trying to ensure that exams could proceed, that commencement could be planned, that students on both sides of the divide did not come to blows. We failed, sometimes spectacularly, as at UCLA. But the lesson of those failures is not that we should stop trying to manage protest; it is that management requires clear, consistently enforced rules that are decided through legitimate governance processes, not imposed under the threat of a federal funding cut. Those rules must include reasonable limits on amplified sound, on the erection of structures, on overnight camping, and on blocking access to buildings. They must also ensure that disciplinary responses are proportionate and not vindictive. Expelling students for nonviolent occupation of a building is extreme; a one-semester suspension may be appropriate.

The deepest difficulty is that we are living through a moment in which the very concept of institutional neutrality has been weaponized. The right demands neutrality when it comes to climate change or racial justice but insists on active protection of a particular definition of antisemitism. The left demands institutional statements on every social issue but cries censorship when the statements turn against it. The result is a chaotic, politically charged environment where every decision about protest is read as taking sides. Gourevitch wants universities to be forums for unbridled hostility; I suspect he would not like the result if that norm were applied consistently and the Genocide Awareness Project erected its display every month or Turning Point USA brought in a speaker who denied the Holocaust. The campus would become a perpetual warzone, and the intellectual life he claims to defend would evaporate.

What we need is not absolute rights but a recommitment to a set of institutional practices that allow multiple, competing claims to coexist. That means restoring faculty governance, so that decisions about protest policies are made by those closest to the educational mission, not by administrators responding to trustees and donors. It means rebuilding a culture of civil disagreement in which protest is one mode among many, not a permanent occupation. And it means resisting, with every legal and political means, the federal government’s attempt to dictate the content of campus speech. But it also means accepting that a university has a right to protect its core functions and that the exercise of this right is not, in itself, a betrayal of its principles. Gourevitch’s absolutism, I fear, would make that impossible.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Large-scale, open-ended encampments create a genuine governance crisis by physically obstructing the ordinary work of faculty, staff, and students.
  2. 02A viewpoint-neutral posture is insufficient because a permanent, all-consuming political occupation of campus space creates an atmosphere of implicit pressure and exclusion for those who disagree.
  3. 03Universities must have the authority to enforce reasonable time, place, and manner restrictions—such as limits on amplified sound and overnight camping—to protect the educational environment.
  4. 04The solution to current campus tensions lies in restoring shared faculty governance and proportionate discipline rather than adopting an absolute right to unbridled hostility.
  5. 05If universities allowed unrestricted protest and hostility, the resulting perpetual conflict would cause the intellectual life of the institution to evaporate.
Diversified DeepSeek

"While the recent crackdowns on campus protests are authoritarian and based on flawed justifications, an absolutist defense of the right to protest is unsustainable because it undermines the university's core educational mission and institutional order."

Alex Gourevitch has written a powerful and, in many respects, persuasive essay. His central claim—that the recent crackdowns on campus protests represent a dangerous repudiation of the right to protest, and that the justifications offered for them are deeply flawed—is undeniable. The violent clearing of peaceful encampments, the mass arrests, the politically motivated threats to federal funding, and the extraordinary punishment of individual students all mark a severe authoritarian turn. And his careful dissection of the “hostile environment” rationale is particularly important: the conflation of subjective feelings of discomfort with actionable harm is a mistake that progressives have made for years, and we are now seeing it exploited with devastating effectiveness by the right. Nevertheless, Gourevitch’s essay pushes too far in the direction of an absolutist defense of protest, one that risks undermining the very institutional conditions that make a university possible. It is not enough to wave away concerns about disruption, trespass, and hostile environments; we need a more nuanced account of how protest can flourish within a university community, not against it.

To begin with, Gourevitch’s treatment of disruption is too dismissive. He acknowledges that protests can be noisy and can interfere with foot traffic, but he insists that these are trivial inconveniences that a healthy university must tolerate. Yet the distinction between minor disturbance and serious disruption is not always clear, and it is not solely a matter of physical obstruction. A protest that lasts for weeks in a central quad, with amplified chants during class hours, can genuinely impede the academic work of students who are not involved. That does not mean such protests should be banned, but it does mean that universities have a legitimate interest in regulating them through reasonable time, place, and manner restrictions. A commons is a shared resource, and its use for one purpose necessarily limits its availability for others. The challenge is to design rules that accommodate robust expression without sacrificing the university’s core educational mission.

Gourevitch’s argument about trespass similarly overreaches. He is correct that universities have historically tolerated encampments and that the campus commons should function as a limited public forum, even at private institutions. But the right to occupy space indefinitely is not a natural corollary of the right to protest. Allowing a group to set up a semi-permanent structure on a quad is a discretionary decision that should be made in light of the overall demands on that space, the safety of the occupants, and the impact on the rest of the community. When protesters refuse to leave after being asked to do so by authorities who have not established any alternative space or time for protest, they are not simply exercising a right; they are asserting a unilateral claim to control university property. That may sometimes be justified as an act of civil disobedience, but it cannot be elevated to a general rule. If any group can occupy any space for any duration, the result is not a vibrant commons but a lawless scramble for territory.

On the question of hostile environment, Gourevitch is on stronger ground, and his insistence that subjective fear cannot be the sole basis for suppressing speech is vital. But he goes too far in suggesting that the hostile environment standard should never apply to protest. There is a meaningful difference between a chanted slogan denouncing Israeli policy and a targeted campaign of harassment that follows a Jewish student to their dormitory or posts their picture online with threats. Administrators must be able to act against the latter without being accused of suppressing viewpoint. The difficulty, of course, is that in practice these lines are often blurry, and the recent record shows that universities have been far too quick to treat the former as the latter. But the cure is not to abandon the hostile environment framework altogether; it is to demand that its application be grounded in specific, objective evidence of targeted abuse, not in ambient feelings of discomfort.

The upshot is a call for proportionality and evenhandedness, not absolutism. Universities should write clear rules that define protest activities—including noise levels, locations, durations, and the prohibition on targeted harassment—and enforce those rules neutrally across all viewpoints. They should also adopt a presumption of tolerance for offensive speech, with discipline reserved for the most serious violations and calibrated to the severity of the offense. Students who quietly occupy a lawn for a day should not face the same sanctions as those who vandalize property or assault a fellow student. And outside political pressure, whether from the federal government or wealthy donors, should be resisted with all the institutional strength the university can muster.

The path forward is not to insist on an unlimited right to protest, but to rebuild a campus culture in which protest is seen as a normal, regulated part of university life, with clear expectations on all sides. That requires administrators to stop treating protesters as criminals, and protesters to accept that a university has other purposes besides being a stage for political expression. Both sides have lost sight of that balance, and Gourevitch’s essay, for all its virtues, leans too far toward one pole. We need not a right to be hostile, but a right to be heard within a shared institutional framework that remains dedicated to learning, research, and the protection of all its members.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Protest disruptions, such as prolonged noise during class hours, are not trivial inconveniences but genuine impediments to academic work that justify reasonable time, place, and manner restrictions.
  2. 02The right to protest does not grant a unilateral claim to occupy university property indefinitely, as such actions infringe upon the shared use of the commons and institutional safety.
  3. 03The 'hostile environment' standard should not be abandoned entirely, but rather refined to distinguish between protected political slogans and objective, targeted harassment of individuals.
  4. 04Universities should replace absolutism with a framework of proportionality and evenhandedness, enforcing neutral rules that balance robust expression with the protection of all community members.
  5. 05A sustainable campus culture requires both administrators to stop criminalizing dissent and protesters to acknowledge that the university has essential purposes beyond political expression.
