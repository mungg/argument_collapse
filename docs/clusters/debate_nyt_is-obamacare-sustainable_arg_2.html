"The Affordable Care Act is only sustainable in the long term if the government shifts focus from insurance market regulations to aggressive control of the underlying costs of medical care."

The debate over Aetna’s withdrawal risks mistaking an insurance-market symptom for the entire disease. Obamacare can be made sustainable only if the United States gets more serious about the underlying price of medical care. Premiums rise because hospitals, specialists, drug companies and device makers charge more, and because Americans receive a great deal of expensive care. Rearranging insurance rules can spread those costs more fairly, but it cannot make them disappear.

The Affordable Care Act did more on cost control than critics admit. It encouraged experiments in accountable care organizations, bundled payments, penalties for hospital readmissions and a shift away from pure fee-for-service medicine. Medicare spending growth has slowed in recent years, though economists debate how much credit the ACA deserves versus the recession and broader trends. But the exchange drama shows that delivery reform has not yet translated into a stable retail market for individual insurance.

Insurers leaving exchanges may reflect bad pricing, adverse selection and political uncertainty. It also reflects local medical markets where one dominant hospital system can dictate rates. In such places, adding more insurers will not magically create affordability. If every carrier must pay high provider prices, premiums will remain high, and consumers will blame the exchange.

Long-term viability requires moving beyond the narrow question of how many insurers sell plans in a county. Policymakers should strengthen subsidies and risk adjustment, yes. But they should also push harder on provider prices, drug costs, payment reform, transparency and antitrust enforcement in health care. Some states may need all-payer rate setting or other tools to prevent providers from simply absorbing public subsidies through higher prices.

Obamacare is sustainable as a coverage platform only if it becomes part of a broader cost-control project. Otherwise the political cycle will repeat: premiums rise, insurers exit, critics declare collapse, and lawmakers debate who should pay more. The real test is not whether every private insurer stays in every exchange. It is whether the health system can deliver care at prices families and taxpayers can bear.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Rising premiums are primarily driven by the high prices charged by hospitals, specialists, and pharmaceutical companies rather than insurance market structure.
  2. 02The lack of competition in local medical markets allows dominant hospital systems to dictate high rates that insurers cannot offset.
  3. 03Existing delivery reforms and experiments in payment models have not yet been sufficient to stabilize the retail market for individual insurance.
  4. 04Long-term viability requires policy interventions such as drug cost regulation, increased price transparency, and antitrust enforcement against healthcare providers.
  5. 05Without broader cost-control measures, public subsidies will simply be absorbed by providers through higher prices, leading to a cycle of rising premiums and insurer exits.
