"The Democratic Party must shift its focus from electoral messaging and positioning to a whole-of-society mobilization aimed at protecting the institutional machinery of democracy from active authoritarian dismantling."

Reading Bonica and Grumbach’s essay, one might be forgiven for thinking that the major obstacle to defeating authoritarianism is a tactical disagreement among Democrats about the best way to win elections. But this framing is itself a symptom of the larger crisis: the American left and center-left continue to treat politics as a contest of messages and electoral positioning, when what we are witnessing is the product of a decades-long, massively funded, and deeply ideological counter-revolution that has systematically captured state and media power. The authoritarians aren’t winning because Democrats are too moderate or too progressive; they’re winning because they have built a parallel epistemic and institutional universe that uses raw power, disinformation, and voter suppression to negate democratic majorities. Fighting that requires something far beyond a new slogan.

The essay’s data-driven case is compelling on its own terms: moderation doesn’t deliver clear electoral rewards. But the unspoken premise is that if Democrats change their strategy, they can win elections under the current rules and then enact reforms to protect democracy. This drastically underestimates the extent to which those rules are already being manipulated by the authoritarian right. Since 2021, Republican-controlled states have passed wave after wave of voter suppression laws, gutted election administration, and, in several cases, created mechanisms for partisan legislators to overturn election results. In 2024, we saw this play out in real time, with rogue county officials in multiple states refusing to certify results, and a heavily partisan Supreme Court continuing to greenlight gerrymandering and weaken voting rights protections. The playing field is not merely tilted; it is being actively bulldozed by one side while the other side debates message framing.

In such an environment, the question isn’t what message will energize Democratic voters; it’s whether Democratic voters can cast a ballot that will be counted and whether the institutions exist to enforce the outcome. The anti-corruption frame is particularly ill-suited to this reality. Corruption is a second-order problem when the first-order problem is that the machinery of democracy is being dismantled. Voters concerned about insider trading or Clarence Thomas’s gifts are likely already part of the politically engaged base. The millions of people who have stopped voting, or who will be purged from the rolls, or who will face hour-long lines in minority precincts, need not a more compelling pitch but a functioning pathway to political participation. That requires a relentless, federally-backed push to protect voting rights, re-enfranchise the millions of people with felony convictions who have served their time, and establish non-partisan redistricting everywhere. These are not “good government” reforms; they are existential bulwarks against minority authoritarian rule.

The essay rightly notes that “playing defense forfeits the moral clarity and collective purpose” needed to confront authoritarian threats. But it then prescribes an anti-corruption campaign that is, in essence, still a defensive maneuver: it’s about cleaning up the system so that people trust it again. Yet trust cannot be restored while the system is actively being gutted by those who wield power. What’s needed is an offensive strategy that names, without euphemism, the Republican Party as the vehicle of democratic destruction. That doesn’t mean abandoning persuasion entirely, but it means that the central mission of the Democratic Party must become the defense of democracy itself, not as one issue among many but as the overriding organizing principle.

That requires a dramatic shift in priorities. Rather than pouring resources into closing messages and ad buys, the party should invest in long-term election protection infrastructure, year-round voter registration in marginalized communities, and legal defense funds to challenge suppressive laws before they take effect. It should build a bench of candidates who can credibly run on a democracy-first platform, including ex-prosecutors, civil rights attorneys, and election officials who have stood up to intimidation. And it should make common cause with every civic organization—from the League of Women Voters to the Chamber of Commerce factions that still believe in the rule of law—to create a broad pro-democracy alliance capable of protecting the 2026 and 2028 elections from sabotage.

The international analogy is not anti-corruption movements per se, but pro-democracy movements in places like Ukraine’s Orange Revolution or South Korea’s Candlelight Revolution, where the rallying cry was not “end corruption” alone but “protect our right to choose our leaders.” In those contexts, massive civic mobilization, combined with clear demands for institutional safeguards (independent election commissions, constitutional courts with teeth), ultimately held the line. The Democratic Party can and should be a part of such a movement, but it must understand that its own electoral fortunes are secondary. If the GOP succeeds in rigging the electoral infrastructure sufficiently, no message, no matter how authentic or bold, will matter.

Moreover, the essay’s critique of moderation implicitly assumes a world in which electoral competition is the primary mode of resistance. But authoritarian movements don’t always come to power by winning elections; they often destroy the electoral process from within and then entrench themselves. The response must therefore include robust plans for non-electoral resistance: mass civil disobedience, strikes, and economic boycotts, should the worst come to pass. The party should be engaging with labor unions, community organizations, and student groups to build the capacity for such resistance now, while there is still space. That is not a traditional Democratic role, but these are not traditional times.

In the end, the essay’s analytical rigor is welcome, but its horizon is too narrow. It asks how Democrats can win elections; we need to ask how democracy can survive. The answer involves a whole-of-society mobilization, not a party rebranding. The anti-corruption frame may help at the margins, but the beating heart of the anti-authoritarian struggle must be the defense of free and fair elections, the expansion of the franchise, and the building of a cross-ideological movement that refuses to accept the legitimacy of a system that no longer represents the people. That is the real “reframing” that history demands.
