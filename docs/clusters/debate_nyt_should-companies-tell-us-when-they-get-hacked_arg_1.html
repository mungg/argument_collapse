"Companies should be legally required to disclose security breaches when there is a meaningful risk to stakeholders, provided the rules allow for investigative discretion and avoid unnecessary noise."

Yes, but the requirement should be smarter than a simple rule that every intrusion must be announced immediately to the world.

The strongest case for disclosure is that the harm from a breach often falls on people who cannot see it. If credit-card numbers, passwords, Social Security numbers, medical files or e-mail accounts have been exposed, customers need to change passwords, watch accounts, place fraud alerts or demand new cards. Keeping quiet protects a company’s reputation at the expense of the people whose information it was trusted to hold. That is why many states, beginning with California, adopted breach-notification laws; the basic principle is sound.

Disclosure also has a public-health value. Computer attacks spread through reused techniques: malicious attachments, stolen administrator credentials, unpatched software, compromised vendors. If every victim treats an attack as a private embarrassment, other firms remain ignorant and vulnerable. Recent reports about attacks on newspapers, technology companies and government contractors suggest that many incidents are not isolated crimes but part of broader campaigns. Secrecy helps the attackers.

Still, disclosure rules must avoid creating noise or making investigations harder. A company should not have to issue a press release every time a firewall blocks a probe or an employee receives a phishing e-mail. Nor should it be forced to reveal technical details while law enforcement is tracking intruders or while systems remain exposed. The trigger should be meaningful risk: unauthorized access to sensitive personal data, material financial impact, major service disruption, or theft of information important enough to affect investors, customers or national security.

The rule should require prompt notice, with limited delays approved by regulators or law enforcement, and should include useful facts: what happened, what data may be affected, what customers should do, and what the company is doing now. Silence should not be the default. Companies that collect and profit from data owe the public candor when they fail to protect it.

"The United States should implement a uniform federal mandate for security breach disclosure to replace the current incoherent patchwork of state laws and improve collective defense."

Yes — and the case for mandatory disclosure has only grown stronger as breaches have moved from credit-card theft to industrial espionage and journalist surveillance. The recent Mandiant report on "APT1," the revelations from The New York Times and Wall Street Journal about Chinese intrusions, and the steady drumbeat of incidents at companies like Zappos, LinkedIn, and Global Payments all point to the same lesson: silence serves the breached company at the expense of nearly everyone else.

Forty-six states already require notification when personally identifiable information is exposed, and California's pioneering 2003 law showed that disclosure regimes can work without sinking the firms they cover. But the patchwork is incoherent — definitions of "breach" vary, triggers differ, and many serious intrusions (intellectual property theft, network compromises that don't clearly touch consumer data) fall through the cracks. A uniform federal standard, of the sort proposed in successive versions of the Data Security and Breach Notification Act, would give companies clarity and customers consistent protection.

Disclosure also has positive externalities. When firms share indicators of compromise, defenders across an industry can patch faster; this is why the FBI's outreach programs and information-sharing groups like the FS-ISAC matter. Mandatory reporting — even confidentially to a body like US-CERT, with public notice when consumers are affected — would convert private pain into collective defense.

The counterarguments (tipping off attackers, harming reputations, encouraging frivolous litigation) are real but manageable through reasonable delay provisions and safe harbors for good-faith security practices. What is not defensible is the status quo, in which whether you learn your data was stolen depends on which state you live in and which company lost it.

"Companies should be legally required to disclose security breaches to ensure consumer protection, corporate accountability, and collective digital security."

The notion that companies should have a choice about disclosing security breaches rests on an outdated understanding of corporate responsibility. When a business loses control of customer data—credit card numbers, Social Security records, email addresses—it is not merely suffering a private misfortune. It is exposing every affected individual to identity theft, fraud, and phishing attacks that can take years to untangle. Mandatory disclosure is not an unreasonable burden; it is the bare minimum of accountability.

At present, disclosure in the United States is a patchwork of state laws, most notably California’s pioneering 2003 statute. The result is that a breach affecting residents of forty-six states may be disclosed in a letter, while residents of the remaining four are left in the dark. A federal standard would end this lottery, ensuring that a citizen’s right to know does not depend on geography. It would also force companies to internalize the true cost of lax security, which today is often passed silently to consumers.

Opponents argue that mandatory reporting harms reputations and invites lawsuits. Yet the alternative is a market in which customers cannot distinguish firms that invest in security from those that cut corners. In a world of asymmetric information, voluntary disclosure is a mirage: companies that come forward, as The New York Times did, are penalized precisely because the norm of silence makes them appear uniquely vulnerable. A universal requirement levels the playing field, so that no single firm is punished for honesty while others hide identical lapses.

Beyond consumer protection, mandatory reporting generates a public good. The technical details of an intrusion—the malware used, the server misconfiguration exploited—are intelligence that help other organizations patch their own defenses. When a breach is buried, that knowledge is lost, and an adversary can reuse the same tool dozens of times without detection. In this sense, silence is not just a betrayal of customers; it is a gift to the next attacker. For a truly resilient digital society, sunlight is not optional.

"Companies should be legally required to disclose security breaches because individuals have a right to know when their data is at risk and transparency fosters better security practices."

The case for mandatory breach disclosure rests on a simple principle: those who bear the risk of harm deserve to know about it. When hackers compromise customer data — whether financial information, medical records, or login credentials — the individuals affected are the ones who must change passwords, monitor accounts, or guard against identity theft. They cannot take these precautions unless they know a breach has occurred.

Critics argue that disclosure requirements harm companies economically and might even alert future attackers to vulnerabilities. These concerns have merit, but they do not outweigh the public interest in transparency. Economic harm from disclosure is itself a form of accountability — it creates market pressure for better security practices. Companies that invest adequately in protecting customer data have less to fear from disclosure than those that do not.

California's breach notification law, enacted in 2003, demonstrated that such requirements can function without crippling industry or creating chaos. Today, nearly all states have similar laws, suggesting that a workable framework exists. A federal standard would bring consistency and ensure that customers in less vigilant states receive the same protections as those in California or New York.

The objection that disclosure tips off future attackers deserves skepticism. Sophisticated hackers already share intelligence through underground networks; the security community does as well through organizations like the Internet Storm Center. The asymmetry is not between informed attackers and uninformed ones, but between informed companies and ignorant consumers.

Companies often resist disclosure not from genuine security concerns but from reputational and legal risk. That self-interest, left unchecked, has produced the culture of silence that makes mandatory disclosure necessary.

Yes. Companies should be required to disclose security breaches, because secrecy shifts the cost of bad security onto the very people least able to protect themselves: customers, employees, sources, patients and investors.

The usual corporate argument against disclosure is that not every intrusion matters. That is true. But that distinction argues for sensible disclosure rules, not for silence. If personal data, payment information, confidential communications or operational systems have been compromised, the affected public deserves timely notice.

Breach notification laws already exist in many states, led by California’s 2002 law, and they have changed corporate behavior for the better. Before such laws, companies often had little incentive to admit that they had lost laptops, exposed databases or stored customer data carelessly. Disclosure creates accountability. It lets consumers cancel cards, change passwords, place fraud alerts and watch for identity theft. It also lets competitors and partners recognize common attack patterns. The recent reports of intrusions at media companies are useful not because they embarrass the victims, but because they show others what to look for.

The fear that disclosure will “help the hackers” is often overstated. A company need not publish its firewall rules or forensic details in the first announcement. A responsible requirement can say: disclose what happened, when it was discovered, what kinds of information may have been affected, what customers should do, and whom to contact. Technical indicators can be shared with trusted industry groups or law enforcement while still giving the public meaningful warning.

Nor is reputational harm a good reason for secrecy. If disclosure causes embarrassment, that is part of the market’s discipline. Security is now a core part of doing business, not an obscure matter for the server room. A bank, hospital, retailer or newspaper that cannot protect sensitive information should expect questions from customers and shareholders.

The law should include safe harbors for prompt, good-faith disclosure and should allow short delays when law enforcement can show that immediate notice would seriously compromise an investigation. But the default must change. Hacking is not merely a private inconvenience suffered by companies. It is a public risk created by the way companies collect, store and monetize data. When that risk materializes, silence should not be an option.

Sunshine is uncomfortable for the exposed, but it is the only reliable disinfectant we have.

The Real Audience Isn't Customers—It's Other Defenders

Most arguments for breach disclosure focus on protecting consumers. That's understandable but incomplete. The bigger payoff from telling the truth about hacks is collective: every defender learns from every attack. Right now, that learning barely happens, and the country pays the price.

Consider what the Mandiant report on APT1, released this week, actually accomplished. By publicly naming Chinese PLA Unit 61398 and detailing its tradecraft—the malware families, the command-and-control infrastructure, the operational patterns—Mandiant gave every American security team a checklist to use tomorrow morning. Logs can be searched. Indicators can be blocked. Patterns can be recognized. That report is more useful to the average CISO than a thousand generic warnings about "advanced persistent threats."

Now imagine that intelligence multiplied across every significant intrusion in the U.S. economy. Imagine that when one defense contractor finds a novel phishing lure, every other contractor sees it within hours. Imagine that when a bank detects a new wire-fraud technique, peer banks are alerted before they are hit. This is not a fantasy; it is roughly what the Financial Services ISAC tries to do, and what other sectors do far less well. The bottleneck is not technology. The bottleneck is the legal and reputational risk attached to admitting a breach.

That is why disclosure rules matter, but they need to be designed with sharing—not shaming—in mind. A useful regime would require timely confidential reporting of serious intrusions to a federal coordinator, with mandatory de-identified sharing of technical indicators across affected sectors. Liability protections should accompany the mandate, so general counsels stop treating threat data like radioactive waste. Public disclosure to customers should remain mandatory when their data is exposed, but the broader reporting regime should be oriented toward defenders, not journalists.

The legislation now circulating in Washington, including CISPA, gets some of this right and some of it wrong. Civil-liberties concerns about handing private data to intelligence agencies are legitimate and need real guardrails. But the underlying insight—that we cannot defend a networked economy if every defender fights alone—is correct.

Disclosure, properly designed, is less about transparency for its own sake than about turning isolated victims into a learning system. That is what the country is missing.

Those laws are imperfect, but they have done something important: they changed data breaches from invisible corporate mishaps into events that customers, journalists, regulators and competitors can evaluate. That transparency has exposed weak security practices that otherwise would have remained hidden.

The current patchwork is not enough. A national rule should require prompt notice when sensitive information has been accessed or is reasonably likely to have been accessed. The notice should be written in plain English, identify the categories of information involved, say what the company is doing, and tell affected people what they can do. Companies should also provide technical indicators to a trusted clearinghouse so that other businesses can defend themselves, without publishing a how-to guide for attackers.

There should be narrow exceptions. A company should be allowed to delay public notice briefly if law enforcement certifies that disclosure would compromise an investigation or if immediate disclosure would worsen an ongoing attack. But “we are still investigating” cannot become a months-long excuse. Nor should a company be allowed to avoid notice simply because it cannot prove exactly what was taken; attackers rarely leave notarized receipts.

Cybersecurity suffers from a classic market failure. Customers cannot easily judge which companies protect data well, and companies have every incentive to minimize bad news. Mandatory disclosure does not solve computer security. But it aligns incentives: collect less data, encrypt what you keep, monitor networks, and tell people when your failure becomes their risk. That is the least a digital custodian owes the public.

  3. 03A national rule would replace the current insufficient patchwork of state laws with a standardized requirement for clear, prompt, and actionable communication.
  4. 04Sharing technical indicators of a breach with a trusted clearinghouse allows other businesses to defend themselves against similar threats.
  5. 05Mandatory disclosure corrects a market failure by aligning corporate incentives toward better data protection and transparency rather than minimizing bad news to protect a brand.

Companies should tell us when they are hacked because secrecy has become part of the problem. The argument for disclosure is often framed narrowly: customers need to know whether to cancel a credit card. That is true, but too limited. Breach disclosure also teaches the public how insecure our shared infrastructure really is. It helps researchers and competitors see patterns. It allows journalists to ask why the same mistakes keep recurring. It gives boards of directors a reason to treat cybersecurity as governance rather than plumbing.

Other industries have learned this lesson. We investigate plane crashes not because airlines enjoy embarrassment, but because the next crash may be prevented. Hospitals track infections not because every infection is scandalous, but because hidden infections spread. Cybersecurity should move in the same direction. A breach is not just a private misfortune; it is evidence about the health of a system on which others rely.

Recent disclosures by the New York Times and other organizations have been valuable precisely because they point beyond a single victim. They suggest coordinated attacks, sometimes apparently tied to state interests, against classes of targets. If each victim stays silent, every company fights alone and the public never sees the campaign. When one speaks, others can compare notes.

There are legitimate limits. A company should not be forced to publish exploit code, name unpatched systems or announce an intrusion while attackers are still being expelled. Law enforcement may sometimes need a short delay. But these concerns justify careful disclosure, not nondisclosure. A public notice can state the fact of the breach, the type of information affected, the broad method of attack and the protective steps being taken without providing a manual for criminals.

We should also create a public breach registry with standardized information, updated as investigations mature. The first notice can be preliminary; later reports can correct the record. That is better than the current system, in which many incidents surface only through rumor, lawsuits or stolen data appearing in criminal markets.

The loss of trust comes less from admitting a breach than from hiding one. If companies want the benefits of collecting and monetizing personal information, they must accept the duty to tell the public when that information, or the systems protecting it, has been compromised.
