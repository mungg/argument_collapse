"While Nir Rosen’s essay accurately documents the systemic failures of American strategy in Afghanistan, its overarching pessimism fails to provide a constructive policy alternative or distinguish between surmountable difficulties and impossible obstacles."

Nir Rosen's essay presents American strategy in Afghanistan as fundamentally misguided, and while the specific failures he documents deserve attention, his overall framework suffers from a failure to distinguish between difficulties that can be overcome and obstacles that cannot. The result is an essay that captures much about what is wrong with current policy while offering inadequate guidance about what should replace it.

The essay's most valuable contribution lies in its documentation of Afghan security force failures. Rosen provides specific, credible evidence of corruption, incompetence, and popular alienation that official reports consistently minimize. The anecdote about police refusing body armor because "it was hot and heavy" captures a fundamental problem that strategic planning documents cannot adequately address. These failures are not marginal concerns; they strike at the heart of American exit strategy, which depends on developing Afghan forces capable of maintaining security after American departure.

The comparison with Iraq produces genuine insight. Rosen correctly identifies that the Surge succeeded partly because conditions had already exhausted the insurgency's momentum before American reinforcements arrived. The Sunni Awakening reflected Sunni recognition that they had been defeated in civil war and saw American alliance as their best remaining option. This analysis explains why the same COIN methods that succeeded in Iraq might fail in Afghanistan, where no such exhaustion has occurred and where the Taliban retain popular support among significant populations.

Rosen's institutional critique of the American military deserves serious attention. The gap between COIN doctrine and field practice reflects not individual failures but systemic dysfunction. The military's culture, training, and operational rhythms remain oriented toward conventional warfare despite years of counterinsurgency operations. Rosen's observation that soldiers joke about "doing that COIN shit" while privately focusing on survival captures the distance between official doctrine and actual behavior that no amount of new manuals can bridge.

However, the essay's conclusion that American efforts are attempting "something from nothing" overstates the case. Afghanistan has never possessed strong centralized institutions, true, but neither had many countries that subsequently developed them. The assumption that Afghan society is somehow more resistant to institutional development than other societies reflects assumptions that historical evidence does not support. The more defensible claim is that current American approaches are inadequate to the challenge, not that the challenge itself is impossible.

The treatment of al-Qaeda's relationship with the Taliban deserves particular attention. Rosen argues that the Taliban's alliance with al-Qaeda reflects "convenience, not ideology," and that the Taliban are primarily concerned with expelling foreign occupiers rather than global jihad. These observations have significant implications for strategy. If correct, they suggest that a credible American drawdown timeline might create space for political accommodation that continued presence prevents. The Taliban's nationalist goals might be achievable through negotiation rather than military victory.

Yet Rosen never develops this argument explicitly. His analysis points toward withdrawal but stops short of recommending it, perhaps recognizing that such a position remains politically impossible in early 2010. The result is an essay that implies conclusions it does not state and recommendations it does not defend.

The author's passing suggestion that "addressing these problems at their roots"—meaning American imperial excess and support for Israel and Middle Eastern dictators—would do more for everyone than military intervention deserves scrutiny. This argument conflates distinct issues and assumes policy changes in one domain would automatically affect outcomes in another. Reducing American support for allies or retrenching from global commitments would not eliminate terrorist grievances or terrorist capabilities. The assumption that terrorist movements are purely reactive to American policy ignores their own ideological commitments and strategic objectives.

For policymakers, Rosen's essay provides valuable perspective on the gap between official narratives and ground realities. But its sweeping pessimism offers equally poor guidance as naive optimism. The path forward requires honest assessment of what American intervention can and cannot accomplish, clarity about objectives, and willingness to adjust strategy based on evidence rather than predetermined commitments.

The essay's lasting contribution may be its documentation of failure rather than its policy recommendations. By revealing the specific ways in which American strategy has fallen short, Rosen creates space for honest reassessment that official discourse forecloses. Whatever policy direction ultimately prevails, it must engage seriously with the failures he documents.
