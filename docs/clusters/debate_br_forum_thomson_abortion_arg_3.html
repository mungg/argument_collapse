"A formal legal right to abortion is insufficient if it is not supported by the material resources, safety, and social conditions necessary for all women to exercise that right equally."

Judith Jarvis Thomson’s essay is a defense of liberty, but it is also, perhaps unintentionally, an indictment of how thin liberty becomes when it is reduced to a formal legal right. Casey may have preserved the constitutional right to abortion, but Thomson begins by reminding us that rights can be hollowed out by fear, geography, money, and stigma. A woman has little meaningful liberty if the nearest provider is hundreds of miles away, if the clinic is surrounded by screaming demonstrators, if the doctor has quit after threats to his children, or if Medicaid will not pay for the procedure she cannot otherwise afford.

This is why the abortion question cannot be separated from equality. Thomson says denial of abortion impedes women’s “political, social, and economic equality.” That is true, but we should sharpen it: the burdens fall unequally among women. A professional woman in Boston or New York may have privacy, transportation, money, and a sympathetic physician. A poor woman in rural Mississippi, a teenager in a violent household, an immigrant afraid of institutions, or a woman dependent on public assistance may experience the “same” right very differently. The constitutional right is national; the ability to use it is local and class-bound.

The Hyde Amendment has for years made this plain. The state may not ban abortion outright, but it may refuse to fund most abortions for poor women, while funding childbirth. That is not neutrality. It is a thumb on the scale, and a heavy one. It says to poor women: wealthier women may exercise constitutional choice; you may choose only if you can pay. Those who speak reverently about unborn life often ignore the born woman’s poverty, health, housing, and existing children. Those who speak reverently about “choice” sometimes ignore that choice without resources is a cruel abstraction.

Thomson’s public-reason argument is therefore indispensable but incomplete. She argues that the state may not impose a severe constraint on women’s liberty on grounds women are reasonable in rejecting. Yes. But the state also has affirmative obligations if it is to make liberty real. Protecting clinic entrances through the FACE Act and injunctions is one part of that obligation. Training physicians is another. So is ensuring that abortion is included in ordinary medical education rather than exiled to besieged freestanding clinics. So is making contraception widely available. So is providing accurate sex education instead of pretending that ignorance produces chastity.

The violence Thomson describes should be understood not as an aberration from the abortion debate but as an extreme version of its disciplinary logic. Most opponents of abortion are not violent; many are decent people moved by sincere moral concern. But clinic blockades, harassment at doctors’ homes, publication of providers’ names, and the ritual shaming of patients all seek to do through private intimidation what the state has not fully done through law: make abortion unavailable. In that sense, the formal line between legal prohibition and practical obstruction is less clear for women than it is for lawyers.

This is also why the rhetoric of “murder” matters. Of course moral language must be free. A democracy cannot forbid citizens from saying that abortion is evil. But leaders who call doctors murderers while disclaiming any connection to violence are engaged in a dangerous game. If abortion providers are really equivalent to serial killers of children, why would one not stop them? The surprise is not that a few fanatics take the next step; the surprise is that more do not. Moral absolutism combined with personal identification of targets creates an atmosphere in which murder can present itself as rescue.

Still, abortion-rights advocates should not respond by denying all moral seriousness to pregnancy. Women do not need to pretend that embryos are meaningless in order to claim authority over their bodies. Many women who have abortions mourn, pray, deliberate, hesitate, or feel relief and sadness together. Others feel only relief. The point is not that abortion has one correct emotional meaning. The point is that the meaning belongs first to the woman whose body and future are at stake, not to legislators, bishops, judges, or protesters.

The encyclical Thomson discusses is especially troubling because it presents women’s moral agency as nearly invisible. The pregnant woman appears mainly as the location of another being’s vulnerability. But a woman is not a location. She is a person with memory, commitments, health, dependents, fear, work, sexuality, and conscience. Any moral argument that treats her as a vessel has already failed to recognize the central fact of pregnancy: two forms of human significance are entangled in one body, and only one is a rights-bearing citizen already living in the social world.

The equality argument also requires us to speak about men. Abortion restrictions regulate women’s bodies after men have often escaped responsibility for sex, contraception, child support, and care. A politics of “life” that does not demand male accountability is morally unserious. If Congress wishes fewer abortions, it can require better child support enforcement, fund prenatal and pediatric care, support battered women, and make contraception inexpensive and available. Punishing women is not family policy.

Thomson is right that the law cannot justifiably force women to accept the claim that personhood begins at conception. But the reproductive freedom worth defending is broader than freedom from criminal prohibition. It includes the material capacity to avoid unwanted pregnancy, to end a pregnancy safely, or to bear and raise children decently. In this sense, abortion rights and social welfare rights are not enemies. They are part of the same struggle over whether women, especially poor women, will be treated as full persons.

The question “Abortion: whose right?” has a simple first answer: it is the woman’s right because it is the woman’s body. But the fuller answer is social. It is a right that requires clinics, doctors, money, transportation, safety, honesty, and respect. Without those, the Constitution speaks in one room while women are turned away in another.
