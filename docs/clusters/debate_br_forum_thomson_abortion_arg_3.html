Diversified Gemini

"The defense of abortion rights should not depend on the moral status of the fetus, but rather on the absolute primacy of a woman's bodily autonomy and her right to be treated as an equal, autonomous citizen."

It is profoundly disappointing to see Judith Jarvis Thomson, who revolutionized the philosophical debate on abortion in 1971, retreat to the exhausting and unwinnable terrain of fetal personhood. In her famous earlier work, Thomson powerfully demonstrated that *even if* a fetus is granted full moral personhood from the moment of conception, it does not follow that the fetus has a right to use a woman’s body to sustain its life against her will. Yet in this essay, she inexplicably abandons her most potent feminist insight, choosing instead to argue with the Pope over whether a fertilized egg has interests.

By centering her defense of abortion access on the assertion that embryos and fetuses lack a right to life, Thomson implicitly concedes a dangerous premise: that if the fetus *did* have a right to life, abortion would indeed be impermissible, and women would be morally and legally obligated to endure forced pregnancy. This concession is fatal to the cause of women's equality.

If we accept that a woman’s right to an abortion hinges on the biological or psychological status of the fetus, women’s bodily autonomy becomes entirely contingent. As medical technology advances, pushing the threshold of viability earlier and earlier, or revealing more about fetal neural development, the window for women's freedom shrinks. Why should a woman’s status as a free and equal citizen depend on whether a fetus has developed the capacity for "wants, hopes, fears, likes and dislikes"?

The abortion debate is fundamentally not about the moral status of the fetus; it is about the moral status of women. It is about whether women are to be treated as autonomous individuals or as conscripted biological incubators. No person in our legal system has the right to use another person’s body without their continuous consent. A father cannot be legally compelled to donate a kidney or even a pint of blood to save his dying child. Why, then, should a woman be legally compelled to surrender her organs, her metabolism, and her health for nine months?

Thomson mentions the importance of abortion to women’s equality and liberty, but she subordinates these concerns to a sterile debate over fetal rights. When anti-abortion activists blockade clinics and shoot doctors, they are not merely expressing a philosophical disagreement about the point at which human rights begin; they are violently enforcing the patriarchal subjugation of women. They are demanding that women's bodies be treated as public property.

Instead of parsing whether a fertilized egg has "interests," we must unequivocally assert that women have interests, women have rights, and women are the sole moral arbiters of what happens inside their own bodies. Until the abortion rights movement stops apologizing, stops arguing about fetal development, and reclaims the absolute primacy of bodily autonomy, our rights will remain precarious.

  3. 03The legal system does not compel any other person to sacrifice their body or organs to sustain another's life, and women should not be an exception to this principle of consent.
  4. 04The abortion debate is fundamentally about the moral status of women and whether they are viewed as autonomous individuals or conscripted biological incubators.
  5. 05Anti-abortion violence and political pressure are tools used to enforce patriarchal subjugation by treating women's bodies as public property rather than private domain.
