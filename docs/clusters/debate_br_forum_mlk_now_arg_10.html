"Martin Luther King Jr.’s radical political vision cannot be fully understood or replicated if it is stripped of its essential theological foundations and the communal religious practices of the black church."

Brandon Terry’s essay rightly insists that Martin Luther King, Jr. cannot be understood apart from his radical critique of racism, poverty, and militarism. It also rightly insists that King’s ethic of love was not a decorative feature of his politics but one of its organizing principles. Yet Terry’s most provocative question may be the one he leaves partly open: can King’s theological vision survive in a secular age and in a weakened black church? I am not sure it can be translated as easily as many admirers suppose. Nor am I sure we should want a translation that leaves the theology behind.

King’s political thought was Christian all the way down. This does not mean it was sectarian, or that only Christians can learn from him. King drew on Gandhi, liberal Protestantism, personalism, democratic theory, the black freedom tradition, and the Constitution. But the grammar of his public life—sin, redemption, beloved community, agape, sacrifice, witness, prophetic judgment—was theological. Remove that grammar and something essential disappears.

This matters because contemporary readers often want King’s courage, discipline, and universalism without his God. We want agape to mean empathy, beloved community to mean inclusion, redemption to mean progress, and sin to mean social dysfunction. These secular equivalents are not worthless. They make parts of King available to people outside the church. But they also thin his thought. For King, love was not merely a generous attitude toward opponents. It was participation in the divine ordering of reality. The beloved community was not simply a diverse democracy with better policies. It was a social expression of God’s will for human beings. Sin was not only ignorance or bias. It was a deep deformation of persons and institutions, requiring conversion as well as reform.

Terry is right that King should not be reduced to moral suasion. King came to understand power, coercion, and structure. But neither should King be reduced to strategy. His nonviolence was effective in part because it was embedded in communities that prayed, sang, testified, mourned, forgave, and hoped together. The mass meeting was not a policy seminar. It was a liturgy of courage. People who had been insulted all week gathered to hear that they were children of God, that Pharaoh would not rule forever, that jail could be a place of witness, and that suffering endured for justice could disclose dignity rather than destroy it. This cannot be replicated by a social media campaign or a nonprofit training manual.

The weakening of the black church therefore poses a serious problem for any Kingian politics. Terry lists familiar reasons for that weakening: integration, prosperity theology, partisan clientelism, social conservatism, scandal, and the alienation of the unchurched. One could add generational distrust of institutions, the rise of therapeutic individualism, the pressures of work schedules, and the moral failures of churches around gender and sexuality. Many younger activists, especially queer activists and feminists, have good reasons to regard the church with suspicion. The church has often wounded those it should have sheltered.

But if the church is discarded, what forms people for sacrifice? What teaches courage without cruelty, discipline without domination, and hope without denial? Universities? They can sharpen critique, but they often train status competition as much as solidarity. Political parties? They mobilize voters, but they are organized around winning office, not cultivating souls. Nonprofits? They do important work, but they depend on grants, metrics, and professional staff. Social media? It can publicize injustice, but it is almost perfectly designed to inflame vanity and contempt. Labor unions? At their best, they teach solidarity across difference, but they too have been weakened, and they do not by themselves supply a metaphysics of human dignity.

King’s religious inheritance gave him resources our politics lacks. One was a language of sin capacious enough to include both personal and structural evil. Today we often split the difference badly. Some speak as if injustice is entirely systemic, leaving no one morally responsible. Others speak as if it is entirely personal, a matter of prejudice in individual hearts. King could say both that people are caught in evil systems and that they remain answerable for how they live within them. He could attack the system without denying the soul.

Another resource was repentance. Our public culture has almost no serious practice of repentance. We have exposure, apology, punishment, denial, and public relations management. But repentance is different. It requires truth-telling, contrition, repair, and transformation. King’s insistence that opponents must not be humiliated rested partly on the belief that people can repent. This belief is not naïve. It is demanding. It refuses to let the wrongdoer escape into defensiveness, but it also refuses to freeze him forever at the moment of wrongdoing.

A third resource was eschatological hope. King’s hope was not optimism. Optimism predicts that things will improve; hope remains faithful when improvement is not visible. In 2018, optimism is in short supply for good reasons. Democratic norms are strained, racism is emboldened, inequality is grotesque, and the planet is warming. A purely empirical politics may conclude that despair is the most rational posture. King’s hope did not deny evidence; it defied despair as a spiritual temptation. That defiance is difficult to justify without some account of reality deeper than the news cycle.

Still, any theological retrieval of King must reckon with the failures Terry names, especially sexism. It is not enough to say King was a man of his time. The black church’s patriarchal patterns shaped who spoke, who served, who was protected, and who was sacrificed. Women sustained the movement as organizers, strategists, fundraisers, teachers, and local leaders, while men often received the title of prophet. King’s own views of gender and family were limited and sometimes harmful. A church that wants to claim King’s legacy must repent of this, not merely acknowledge it.

That repentance must include queer people. In many black churches, the language of beloved community has stopped at the boundary of sexuality. The result is a cruel contradiction: churches proclaim liberation while asking some of their own members to hide, split themselves, or accept second-class belonging. If King’s “triple evils” must become four by adding sexism, they must also be confronted in relation to homophobia and transphobia. The theological claim that every person bears sacred worth cannot remain abstract.

Here King’s own method may help. He held together judgment and transformation. He condemned America not because he hated it but because he believed it could become something else. The same posture can be directed toward the church. Those wounded by the church owe it no sentimental loyalty. But those who remain, or who return, should not confuse preservation with faithfulness. A Kingian church in 2018 would have to be more democratic, more feminist, more honest about sexuality, more accountable in leadership, and less captive to celebrity pastors and partisan access. It would have to recover the mass meeting not as nostalgia but as a school for freedom.

What about those outside the church? King can still speak to them, but not if his theology is hidden out of embarrassment. A secular activist can admire agape as a discipline of recognizing the humanity of opponents. A democratic socialist can learn from King’s fusion of economic justice and moral vision. A Black Lives Matter organizer can adapt nonviolent direct action without accepting Christian doctrine. But these appropriations should be honest that they are adaptations. King’s original power came from a community that believed freedom struggle was bound up with God’s purposes.

Terry’s essay is especially useful because it refuses to make King easy. King’s love was not politeness. His nonviolence was not passivity. His hope was not complacency. His Christianity was not respectability draped over liberal reform. It was a radical source of courage and a constraint on hatred. That combination is rare. Our politics often offers courage without constraint or constraint without courage. King offered both.

The task, then, is not simply to revive the black church as it was. That church produced saints and scandals, freedom songs and silences, prophetic fire and patriarchal injury. The task is to ask what kind of moral communities can form people capable of sustained democratic sacrifice. If churches cannot do it, something else must. But we should not pretend that outrage, analysis, or policy expertise alone will suffice.

King’s question was ultimately spiritual as well as political: what must human beings become in order to live together justly? That question cannot be answered by legislation alone, though it requires legislation; nor by protest alone, though it requires protest. It requires conversion of habits, institutions, and desires. Terry is right that King remains alive where there is “divine dissatisfaction with injustice.” The word “divine” should not be passed over too quickly. In King’s politics, dissatisfaction became transformative because it was rooted in a love stronger than defeat. Whether we can recover such love without the communities that taught it may be one of the decisive questions of our time.
