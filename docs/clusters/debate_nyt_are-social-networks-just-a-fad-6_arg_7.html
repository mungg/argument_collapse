"Social networks are not a passing fad but rather enduring tools for maintaining weak ties that will eventually settle into a permanent, domesticated role alongside other communication technologies."

The debate about social media often swings between utopian and dismissive claims. Both miss the central point: social networks are tools for weak ties, and weak ties are genuinely important but easily overvalued.

Weak ties help people find jobs, hear news, join events, donate money, discover music, reconnect with classmates and maintain a sense of belonging across distance. Facebook excels at this. Twitter, in a different way, allows strangers with shared interests to form temporary publics. These are not minor achievements. They change the texture of daily life.

But weak ties do not do everything. They do not automatically produce trust, discipline, courage or long-term obligation. That is why Friedman’s distinction between ignition and “brick and mortar” is useful. Social networks can spread outrage faster than organizations can channel it. They can produce a crowd before they produce a program. In democratic politics, that can energize campaigns and expose abuses. In revolutionary politics, it can create dangerous illusions about what mobilization alone can accomplish.

So are social networks a fad? As a supplement to real relationships and institutions, no. As a substitute for them, yes. The fad is the inflated claim that a retweet is a movement, a friend count is a community, or a viral video is political power.

Facebook’s I.P.O. may mark the end of innocence. Investors are asking whether attention can be monetized; users are asking whether their attention is being exploited; societies are asking what happens when private companies host public conversation. These are mature questions, not signs of disappearance.

The most likely future is not abandonment but domestication. Social networks will settle into a place alongside email, texting, phones, television and face-to-face life. They will be indispensable for some tasks and harmful for others. We should neither sneer at them as toys nor worship them as engines of history. They are powerful connectors, and connection is only the beginning of social change.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Social media platforms excel at facilitating weak ties, which are essential for tasks like networking, sharing information, and maintaining a sense of belonging over distances.
  2. 02While effective for spreading information and outrage, social networks lack the capacity to generate the trust, discipline, and long-term obligation required for sustained political change.
  3. 03The current skepticism surrounding social media's economic and social impact reflects a transition into a mature phase of integration rather than a sign of the technology's disappearance.
  4. 04The true fad is not the technology itself, but the inflated belief that digital connections can serve as a complete substitute for physical institutions and deep personal relationships.
