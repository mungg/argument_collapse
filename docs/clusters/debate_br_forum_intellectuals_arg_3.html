Vanilla Claude

"While Robin D. G. Kelley correctly identifies the need for intellectual activism and institutional resistance, he errs in suggesting that Noam Chomsky’s mandate to tell the truth has been diminished or rendered incomplete by a 'post-truth' era."

Robin D. G. Kelley's essay is a powerful indictment of the present and a needed corrective to a sanitized memory of Chomsky's 1967 intervention. The genealogy he draws — from Macdonald and Chomsky through Gramsci, Du Bois, Drake, Cayton, Claudia Jones, William Patterson, Césaire — is not a detour but the main road. The Black antifascist tradition recognized decades before our current commentariat that fascism is not an importation from Europe but a domestic possibility nourished by colonial violence, lynch law, and the suppression of labor. Kelley is right that any account of "the responsibility of intellectuals" written today that does not begin from this archive will be provincial. And he is right that the conditions for dissident speech inside the U.S. university have sharply deteriorated, that the post–October 7 wave of firings, suspensions, and police actions amounts to the harshest repression of academic freedom since the McCarthy years, and that university administrators who capitulated first to donors and then to the Trump administration cannot now plead surprise at the consequences.

Where I want to push back is on the essay's central rhetorical move: the claim that Chomsky's injunction to "speak the truth and expose lies" has been rendered "incomplete" or its power "radically diminished" because we are in a "post-truth" age. I think this concedes too much to the right and, more importantly, misreads the very tradition Kelley wants to recover.

Consider what the organic intellectuals he celebrates actually did. *Black Metropolis* is a 800-page work of empirical sociology. *We Charge Genocide* is, as Kelley notes, a meticulous documentation of "hundreds of incidents of anti-Black violence" framed as a legal petition. Du Bois's *Black Reconstruction* is an evidentiary demolition of the Dunning School. Patterson and Robeson did not march into the UN with a mood; they marched in with footnotes. C. L. R. James wrote *The Black Jacobins* against an archive Europeans had built to deny that Haitians had made a revolution. These figures were partisans, yes — they had "chosen a side" — but their partisanship was inseparable from a ferocious commitment to documentary truth. They knew that the lies of empire were not merely rhetorical preferences but specific, falsifiable claims, and that exposing them mattered both to the morale of the oppressed and to the conscience of potential allies. To say truth-telling is now "incomplete" risks suggesting that the partisans Kelley admires were doing something other than truth-telling. They weren't. They were doing it harder, and in worse conditions, than most of us are willing to.

The "post-truth" diagnosis is also analytically suspect. The right's narratives — that immigrants depress wages, that the 2020 election was stolen, that anti-Zionism is antisemitism — do not succeed because audiences have become indifferent to evidence. They succeed because the institutional architecture that once translated evidence into shared belief has fractured: local newspapers, broadcast monopolies, faculties insulated from donor caprice, professional norms in mainstream journalism. Audiences have been pre-sorted into epistemic communities by platform incentives and partisan media, and the right has invested far more than the left in building counter-institutions to circulate its claims. The conclusion is not that we should abandon Chomsky's injunction but that truth-telling now requires institutional reconstruction in addition to individual courage. Kelley gestures at this when he praises the Institute on Inequality and Democracy, the Hampton Institute, the Tricontinental, and unionization drives. These are exactly the right targets. But they are infrastructure for truth-telling, not alternatives to it.

A second concern is with the Gramscian framework on its own terms. Kelley admits, candidly, that MAGA has its own organic intellectuals: Yarvin, Anton, Rufo, Bannon, the Project 2025 contributors. They too are "embedded" in a class fraction, articulating its interests and its imagined future. They too refuse the pose of detachment. They too see themselves as choosing sides and fighting. If "organic intellectual" describes anyone who consciously works on behalf of a social bloc, the category cannot, by itself, distinguish liberatory from reactionary intellectual practice. Something else has to do that work — and what does it, I would argue, is precisely the commitment to truthful description of the world that Kelley risks treating as quaint. Yarvin's work is bad not because he is partisan but because his historical claims about democracy, race, and political order are largely false. Anton's "nation of settlers" argument is bad not because it advances a class project but because it misrepresents American demographic and political history. The criterion of evaluation is, in the last instance, the criterion Chomsky named.

A third worry is more practical. The exhortation to "choose sides" is unobjectionable in the abstract; in concrete movement contexts it can underwrite its own intolerance. The Palestine solidarity movement that Kelley rightly defends from doxxing campaigns and university repression has, in places, also enforced its own loyalty tests, with consequences for Jewish, Israeli, and Arab scholars who articulate positions deemed insufficiently aligned. Atalia Omer, whom Kelley names as a casualty of Harvard's capitulation, is a useful example of what the "choose sides" framing can flatten: she is a scholar of peacebuilding whose work resists exactly the demand that one's commitments collapse into the slogans of any single bloc. A genuinely Gramscian sensibility — Gramsci was, after all, the great theorist of the *war of position* — should be more patient with that complexity than the current discourse often allows. Solidarity does not require unanimity, and movements that punish internal heterodoxy tend to reproduce, in miniature, the institutional pathologies they oppose.

None of this contests Kelley's core claim that intellectuals today must be more than commentators; that the university is contested terrain rather than a sanctuary; that academic freedom is meaningless if it does not extend to those whose speech the powerful most want suppressed; or that the Palestinian case is the live test of whether American academic institutions have any moral seriousness left. On all of this he is correct, and his catalog of fired and harassed colleagues should be read as a roll of honor and a warning. Tiffany Willoughby-Herard's question — "What job do I have if the students don't have a future?" — is the right question, and Kelley is right to end there.

But the answer to that question, I'd argue, still includes the older Chomskyan duty alongside the newer Gramscian one. The responsibility of intellectuals in the age of fascism and genocide is to organize, yes — to build counter-institutions, to unionize, to plug into movements, to refuse the comforts of detached commentary. And it is still to tell the truth: about Gaza, about the longue durée of American racial violence, about the lies that justify deportation, about the structural conditions that produce billionaire-class authoritarianism. The Black radicals Kelley honors did both at once and would not have recognized the distinction. Our task is not to choose between truth and partisanship but to rebuild the institutions in which truth, told from a position, can again move people to act.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Black antifascist tradition Kelley celebrates was defined by a ferocious commitment to empirical, documentary truth-telling rather than a departure from it.
  2. 02The 'post-truth' condition is a failure of institutional infrastructure rather than an audience indifference to evidence, necessitating the reconstruction of truth-telling institutions rather than the abandonment of truth as a primary tool.
  3. 03The commitment to truthful description is the only objective criterion that distinguishes liberatory intellectual work from the partisan 'organic' intellectualism of the far right.
  4. 04A rigid 'choose sides' framework risks flattening the necessary complexity of scholarly work and enforcing a movement unanimity that can reproduce the same institutional pathologies it seeks to oppose.
  5. 05The responsibility of the modern intellectual is not to choose between truth and partisanship, but to rebuild the institutional capacity for truth to move people to action.
Vanilla Gemini

"While Robin D. G. Kelley correctly identifies the need for intellectuals to engage in direct social struggle, his dismissal of truth-telling and the liberal university as obsolete tools is a tactical error that risks surrendering vital epistemological and institutional ground to the fascist right."

In his piercing and timely essay, Robin D. G. Kelley offers a necessary corrective to Noam Chomsky’s celebrated 1967 manifesto, “The Responsibility of Intellectuals.” Writing from the vantage point of September 2025—a moment defined by the consolidation of a second Trump administration, the aggressive implementation of the Heritage Foundation’s right-wing agenda, and the relentless, catastrophic violence in Gaza—Kelley rightly identifies the limits of Chomsky’s liberal framework. For Chomsky, the highest calling of the intellectual was to “speak the truth and to expose lies.” This injunction rested on an unspoken premise: that exposing the machinations of the state to a purportedly democratic public would shock the conscience of the nation and compel a change in policy.

Kelley shatters this premise. He points out that in an era of post-truth politics, where the American right has openly embraced fascism and unapologetically constructed its own alternative epistemologies, the mere exposure of a lie is politically impotent. When power no longer requires the illusion of moral coherence, truth-telling alone cannot stop it. To remedy this, Kelley reaches for Antonio Gramsci’s concept of the “organic intellectual” and the rich history of the Black radical tradition, arguing that intellectuals must abandon the myth of objective detachment, cast their lot with the insurgent class, and physically enter the struggle. For Kelley, the responsibility of the intellectual today is not merely to speak truth to power, but to help build the power necessary to defeat fascism.

Kelley’s diagnosis of our political reality is flawless, and his call for solidarity is morally urgent. However, in his eagerness to bury Chomsky’s framework and dismantle the "myth of the liberal university," Kelley risks discarding the specific tactical advantages that intellectual labor and academic institutions provide to any resistance movement. While truth-telling may no longer shock the state into changing course, it remains the essential scaffolding upon which any durable counter-hegemonic movement must be built. Furthermore, by framing the university primarily as a compromised terrain to be moved beyond, Kelley inadvertently risks ceding the very institutional fortresses that the fascist right is currently fighting so ruthlessly to capture. The responsibility of the intellectual in 2025 is not to abandon truth-telling or the institutions of inquiry for the picket line, but to weaponize truth and rigorously defend the university as vital fronts in the antifascist war.

First, we must critically examine Kelley’s assertion that "whatever power truth-telling might have had against fascism has been radically diminished." It is true that pointing out the hypocrisy of the state does not stop the bombs from falling or ICE from conducting mass deportations. But to declare truth-telling diminished is to misunderstand how the right wing operates. If truth were truly powerless, the MAGA political bloc would not expend such immense political capital and institutional energy to suppress it.

The coordinated campaign to ban books, dismantle diversity, equity, and inclusion (DEI) programs, rewrite history curricula, and fire dissenting professors is not the behavior of a regime that views truth as irrelevant. The Heritage Foundation’s Project Esther and the broader Project 2025 agenda are explicitly designed to criminalize and silence critics because the right understands that historical and material truth—when rigorously documented and widely disseminated—remains a potent threat to their ideological hegemony. When intellectuals stop insisting on the factual record, they surrender the epistemological battlefield to the right’s organic intellectuals—the Stephen Millers, Christopher Rufos, and Michael Antons whom Kelley correctly identifies as the architects of this new fascist bloc.

Kelley grounds his argument in the heroic examples of Black radical intellectuals from the 1930s and 1940s, such as St. Clair Drake, Horace Cayton, William L. Patterson, and Claudia Jones. He rightly notes that these figures did not pretend to be detached; they were explicit antifascists embedded in social movements. Yet, it is crucial to recognize *how* these organic intellectuals fought fascism. They did not abandon the rigorous pursuit of truth; they weaponized it.

When Patterson, W. E. B. Du Bois, and the Civil Rights Congress submitted *We Charge Genocide!* to the United Nations in 1951, they were engaged in a monumental act of truth-telling. The document was a meticulously researched, evidentiary indictment of American state-sanctioned racial violence. Its power derived precisely from its unassailable documentation of facts. Similarly, Drake and Cayton’s *Black Metropolis* was a landmark sociological study. These intellectuals did not view rigorous academic inquiry and movement building as mutually exclusive; they understood that the movement required the unique labor that only intellectuals could provide—the labor of research, synthesis, historical contextualization, and systemic analysis.

If we accept Kelley’s implication that Chomsky’s injunction to "speak the truth" is obsolete, we risk cultivating an anti-intellectualism on the left that flattens the division of labor required in any successful mass movement. Gramsci’s concept of the organic intellectual is instructive here. Gramsci did not simply want intellectuals to join the workers on the barricades. He understood that the bourgeoisie maintained dominance not just through force, but through cultural hegemony—controlling the common sense of society through schools, media, and the church. The task of the working-class organic intellectual was to build a counter-hegemony. This requires deep, often quiet, and sustained intellectual labor. It requires writing the books, compiling the data, and teaching the classes that will shape the consciousness of the next generation. If the intellectual's only valid function is physical confrontation or organizing, then the movement loses its strategists, its historians, and its theorists.

This brings us to the question of the university itself. Kelley paints a bleak but accurate picture of higher education in 2025. It is thoroughly corporatized, heavily reliant on finance capital, and entirely subservient to conservative state legislatures, hostile donors, and the Trump administration's aggressive funding mandates. In the wake of the October 7 attacks and the ensuing student encampments, administrators have capitulated to right-wing pressure, resulting in the mass firing, doxxing, and arrest of scholars advocating for Palestinian liberation. Consequently, Kelley suggests that the myth of the liberal university is shattered and that our efforts must be directed toward "moving beyond the ivory tower" and creating a "new university."

But we must be incredibly cautious about abandoning the defense of the existing university, however compromised it may be. The fascist right is currently engaged in a highly organized, well-funded campaign to conquer American higher education. They are not abandoning the university; they are capturing it. From the hostile takeover of the New College of Florida, which served as a blueprint, to the systematic purging of left-leaning faculty and the installation of right-wing ideologues in university presidencies across the country, MAGA organic intellectuals recognize that universities are engines of cultural reproduction and institutional power.

If progressive, antifascist intellectuals view the university merely as "contested terrain" where they occasionally stage disruptions before inevitably being fired—or worse, if they view academic freedom and tenure as outdated liberal myths not worth defending—they will effectively hand the keys of these vital institutions to the fascists. Defending the traditional tenets of the liberal university—academic freedom, tenure, and the right to free inquiry—is ironically a radical necessity in 2025. These mechanisms were designed, however imperfectly, to protect dissenting voices from the exact kind of state and corporate repression we are witnessing today. To dismiss these protections because they are currently failing is akin to abandoning a fortress because its walls are being breached. We do not need to abandon the fortress; we need to fight to repair and hold it.

We must look no further than the catastrophic situation in Gaza, which Kelley profoundly highlights, to understand the value of academic institutions. The concept of "scholasticide," coined by Karma Nabulsi, describes the intentional destruction of Palestinian intellectual life and academic infrastructure by the Israeli military. The annihilation of every college and university in Gaza is not merely collateral damage; it is a targeted campaign to erase the history, culture, and future of the Palestinian people. Why does the Israeli state target universities with such ferocity? Because institutions of learning are the lifeblood of national identity and resistance. Palestinian scholars and students have consistently fought for their universities, not just as staging grounds for political protest, but as sacred centers of learning, research, and truth-telling.

By recognizing the profound tragedy of scholasticide in Gaza, American intellectuals must also recognize the inherent value of their own academic institutions. If universities are critical enough to the survival of a people that an occupying power will systematically bomb them into rubble, then they are critical enough for us to fiercely defend from administrative rot and fascist takeover at home. We cannot effectively stand in solidarity with Palestinian scholars whose institutions have been physically destroyed if we voluntarily surrender our own institutions to the ideological destruction wrought by the Trump administration and its allies.

Kelley concludes his essay with a moving tribute to his colleague, Professor Tiffany Willoughby-Herard, who was arrested by riot police while trying to protect her students at a Palestine solidarity encampment at UC Irvine. Her words, "What job do I have if the students don’t have a future?" are haunting and profound. Her willingness to put her body on the line is an undeniable act of moral courage.

But we must also ask the inverse question: How can we secure a future for our students if we abandon our jobs, our research, and our institutions? The physical bravery of standing between students and riot police is deeply commendable, but it cannot be the sole metric of intellectual responsibility. A movement against fascism needs martyrs and frontline activists, but it also desperately needs tenured radicals who can leverage the resources of the university to fund insurgent research. It needs sociologists who can document the mechanisms of mass deportation; it needs historians who can expose the long, ugly roots of Christian nationalism; it needs legal scholars who can dismantle the jurisprudence of the radical right; and it needs educators who can teach students how to parse the difference between fascist propaganda and material reality.

Chomsky’s 1967 framework was indeed a product of its time—a time when there was still a functioning, albeit deeply flawed, liberal consensus that could be shamed by its own contradictions. Kelley is absolutely correct that this consensus is dead, murdered by a right-wing movement that views hypocrisy as a virtue and cruelty as a political strategy. But the death of the liberal consensus does not render truth-telling obsolete; it renders it more vital, more dangerous, and more necessary than ever before.

The responsibility of intellectuals in the age of fascism and genocide is a dual mandate. We must, as Kelley demands, cast our lot with the oppressed, organize our workplaces, and integrate our intellectual labor with the material struggles of our communities. But we must do so without losing sight of our specific function within that struggle. We are not just activists who happen to hold degrees; we are the custodians of history, the analysts of the present, and the architects of the theoretical frameworks necessary for the future.

We must not cede the concept of truth to the post-truth fascists, nor should we cede the institution of the university to the right-wing operatives seeking to dismantle it. We must speak the truth, not because it will magically change the minds of those in power, but because it arms the powerless. We must fight for our institutions, not because they are pure, but because they are necessary weapons in a war we cannot afford to lose. Defeating the fascism of 2025 will require all the organizing power, all the solidarity, and all the physical courage we can muster, but it will also require the cold, hard, unrelenting force of the truth.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The right wing’s aggressive efforts to suppress dissent and rewrite curricula prove that rigorous truth-telling remains a potent threat to their ideological hegemony rather than a diminished force.
  2. 02Historical examples of organic intellectuals, such as those in the Black radical tradition, demonstrate that the pursuit of academic truth and active movement building are mutually reinforcing rather than mutually exclusive.
  3. 03Abandoning the defense of the university and its protections, like tenure and academic freedom, effectively hands the keys of these essential cultural and institutional engines to the fascist movement currently seeking to capture them.
  4. 04The intellectual's specific responsibility in a mass movement is to provide the specialized labor of research, historical contextualization, and systemic analysis that physical activism alone cannot replace.
  5. 05The targeted destruction of universities in Gaza, or 'scholasticide,' underscores that academic institutions are indispensable centers of national identity and resistance that must be fiercely protected at home.
