"The essay argues that justice for the legacies of slavery requires moving beyond the limited framework of liberal human rights toward a politics of 'racial capitalism' and structural reparations."

Walter Johnson’s essay is at its strongest when it asks us to stop treating slavery as a moral exception to modern history. The phrase “dehumanization” often functions as a ritual of absolution: it allows us to say, with appropriate horror, that slavery was so terrible that it must have depended on some departure from humanity. Johnson’s counterclaim is more disturbing and more useful. Slavery did not require slaveholders to cease recognizing the humanity of enslaved people. It required them to exploit that humanity with precision.

This is not a small semantic point. The plantation depended on human intelligence, human memory, human fear, human kinship, human grief, human endurance, human sexuality, and human creativity. The enslaved were not treated as rocks or machines, though they were often compared to both. They were treated as people whose capacities could be disciplined, priced, mortgaged, bred, coerced, punished, and made productive. The whip was not applied to an object that could not feel. It was applied to a person because he or she could feel. The auction block did not merely misrecognize kinship; it monetized it by threatening to destroy it. Rape under slavery was not an incidental cruelty appended to labor exploitation; it was a form of domination made possible by law and by the political economy of human property.

Johnson is right, then, to worry that “dehumanization” can smuggle in a dubious premise: that humanity is something that can be lost by the victim rather than violated by the perpetrator. We can say, of course, that slaveholders denied the humanity of enslaved people. But even this needs qualification. They denied equality, dignity, autonomy, rights, and moral claims; they did not deny, in any stable or comprehensive way, the human capacities on which slavery rested. Indeed, the slave codes, patrols, passes, literacy prohibitions, marriage restrictions, and punishments for rebellion all testify that slaveholders knew perfectly well they were ruling human beings. No one writes a law against a plow learning to read.

The more provocative part of Johnson’s essay is his move from this critique of language to a critique of liberal human rights. Here, too, his argument deserves serious attention. The conventional moral architecture of rights has often been too thin to reckon with slavery’s afterlives. Rights can prohibit formal enslavement, formal disfranchisement, and formal segregation. They can recognize persons before the law. These are monumental achievements, and no one who studies slavery should sneer at them. But rights alone do not restore stolen land, return stolen wages, repair broken lineages, or dismantle inherited structures of wealth and exposure. A freed person with a right to contract but no land, no protection from terror, no access to credit, and no claim on the wealth created by generations of forced labor is politically emancipated but materially cornered.

Johnson’s invocation of Marx on political emancipation is therefore apt, even if one need not be a Marxist to see the problem. Legal equality can coexist with economic dependency. The formal subject of rights can be hungry, indebted, evicted, incarcerated, undereducated, overpoliced, and still told that justice has already been done because the law no longer names him property. The Thirteenth, Fourteenth, and Fifteenth Amendments remade the constitutional order, but Reconstruction’s overthrow demonstrated that rights without power can be strangled by organized counterrevolution. The later victories of the civil rights movement were likewise indispensable, but the persistence of racialized poverty, residential segregation, policing disparities, and wealth inequality suggests that juridical inclusion is not the same as repair.

Johnson’s term “racial capitalism” helps describe this deeper continuity. The point is not that every institution in a capitalist society is identical to a plantation. Nor is it that class disappears into race, or race into class. It is that modern capitalism developed through racial differentiation: through slavery, conquest, colonial extraction, immigration exclusion, segregated labor markets, and uneven distributions of vulnerability and entitlement. The plantation and the factory were not the same workplace, but neither were they separate worlds. Cotton moved from enslaved hands in the Mississippi Valley to merchants, insurers, shippers, bankers, manufacturers, and consumers across the Atlantic. The abstraction of “the market” hides a series of concrete relationships: credit secured by enslaved bodies; land made available by Native dispossession; metropolitan industry fed by colonial and plantation raw materials; white labor politics shaped by proximity to and distance from racialized unfreedom.

One of the virtues of Johnson’s essay is that it refuses the comforting division between “economic” and “racial” history. Too often public debate proceeds as if class injustice is real and material, while racial injustice is symbolic and attitudinal. Or, conversely, as if racism is merely a prejudice floating above a neutral economy. Slavery exposes the absurdity of both views. Race was made in law, labor, property, sexuality, policing, accounting, and empire. It was not just an idea in someone’s head. It organized who could be owned, who could own, who could inherit, who could testify, who could move, who could be raped with impunity, who could claim land, who could borrow, and who could be believed.

That insight matters urgently in 2017. We are living through a political moment in which some commentators insist that economic anxiety among white voters must be understood apart from race, while others reduce white grievance to irrational bigotry. Johnson’s essay helps explain why the separation is false. White working-class politics in the United States has long been shaped by both vulnerability to capital and claims on racial entitlement. The white worker could be exploited by employers and still receive public recognition, legal protection, or psychological wages denied to black workers. Sometimes those wages were meager; sometimes they were substantial. But they mattered politically. They helped make cross-racial labor solidarity difficult, and they helped translate economic fear into racial backlash.

Still, Johnson’s argument should not be read as a counsel of despair. If racial capitalism was made, it can be unmade. But unmaking it requires a more ambitious politics than nondiscrimination. Reparations are central, not peripheral, to such a politics. The word is often caricatured as a demand for individual checks, as if the only question were whether a living white person owes money to a living black person for something neither personally did. That framing is designed to trivialize the issue. Reparations should begin from institutions and structures: the federal government that protected slavery and segregation; states and municipalities that enforced exclusion; universities, banks, insurers, churches, and corporations that profited from slavery or Jim Crow; property regimes built on indigenous dispossession; public policies that subsidized white wealth accumulation while denying the same to black citizens.

A reparative politics could include direct payments, but it should not end there. It should include land claims and tribal sovereignty; massive investment in black communities; debt relief; baby bonds or wealth-building accounts; housing restitution; school funding equalization; criminal justice transformation; health care access; environmental remediation; and democratic control over institutions that have extracted from the poor and racialized. The test is not whether a policy makes liberal observers feel innocent. The test is whether it changes the distribution of life chances produced by centuries of expropriation.

Johnson’s critique of “humanity” also pushes us to think differently about dignity. The task is not to prove that enslaved people were human by finding moments when they acted like liberal individuals choosing among options. The task is to understand the worlds they made under conditions they did not choose: families formed under threat of sale, worship under surveillance, knowledge transmitted despite bans, work slowed or redirected, rebellions planned, escapes attempted, children loved, dead mourned, futures imagined. These histories do not demonstrate humanity; they begin from it.

There is a danger, of course, that “racial capitalism” becomes a master phrase used to explain everything and therefore nothing. Johnson’s essay sometimes risks that. The concept must be disciplined by historical specificity. The cotton plantation, the Caribbean sugar estate, convict leasing, colonial rubber extraction, domestic service, migrant farm labor, prison labor, and global sweatshops are related but not identical. To call them all racial capitalism should sharpen our questions, not replace investigation. How exactly did law, credit, violence, kinship, gender, ecology, and labor combine in each case? Who profited? Who resisted? What changed? What persisted?

But this is a caution about use, not a rejection of the framework. Johnson’s central demand is that we change the standpoint from which we think about modern justice. If we begin from Europe after genocide, we may imagine human rights primarily as protections against state atrocity and violations of conscience. If we begin from slavery, colonialism, and indigenous dispossession, we will ask different questions: Who owns the land? Who accumulated wealth from forced labor? Whose reproduction was controlled? Whose communities were made disposable? Whose exposure to premature death was treated as the cost of prosperity? These are not questions that the language of universal rights can easily answer by itself.

The essay ends with Du Bois’s warning that there are moments when the world can be remade and that failure leaves a blood debt to future generations. That warning feels acute today. The United States has just inaugurated a president who rose in politics through birtherism, campaigned on walls and bans, and entered office amid open appeals to white grievance. But the deeper crisis did not begin with him. It lies in the long refusal to confront how our wealth, institutions, borders, police powers, and common sense were made.

To remake the world, we must first name it accurately. Johnson’s essay does not ask us to abandon the moral language of humanity because the enslaved were less than human. It asks us to abandon it because they were always fully human, and because their humanity was the very substance slavery exploited. That recognition should not make us more comfortable. It should make us less able to live with the arrangements that have descended from it.
