"While Walter Johnson correctly identifies the need to center the history of slavery in our understanding of capitalism and justice, his reliance on a simplified 'standpoint of the enslaved' fails to account for the internal contestations of the black radical tradition and the ethical complexities of navigating a biased archive."

I want to begin by thanking Walter Johnson for writing this essay. It is not often that a piece of historical scholarship manages to be at once a methodological intervention, a political argument, a memorial tribute, and a stirring call to action. Johnson’s essay is all of these things, and it is written with a moral urgency that is rare in academic prose. I learned a great deal from it, and I expect to be thinking about it for a long time.

And yet I find myself, at the end, feeling something like disappointment. Not because the essay is wrong—on most of the major points I think Johnson is right—but because it does not go far enough. It raises questions it does not answer, follows lines of argument it does not complete, and gestures toward a politics it does not specify. In what follows, I want to press Johnson on one particular set of questions that I think he leaves hanging: the questions about epistemology, about how we know what we know about slavery and its legacies, and about who is authorized to speak about them.

Johnson is deeply critical of the way that mainstream historians have written about slavery. He objects to the language of “dehumanization,” the liberal framing of enslaved people as proto-citizens, and the persistent need that historians seem to feel to “prove” the humanity of enslaved people. He objects to the “increasingly being written without enslaved people”—to the shift in the field from bottom-up social history to top-down economic history, from the experiences of the enslaved to the calculations of slaveholders and their business partners. And he objects to the way that the “capitalism-slavery debate” has proceeded without a full engagement with the black radical tradition: the work of Du Bois, Williams, Robinson, Davis, and others who, he argues, long ago established the foundational insights that the “new history of capitalism” claims to be discovering.

These are important objections, and they raise questions that go beyond historiography. They are questions about what it means to produce knowledge about an injustice as profound as slavery. Who has the standing to write this history? What methods are appropriate to the task? What is the relationship between the historian and the community whose history is being written? These are not new questions, but they have been given new urgency by the debates of the past few years: the controversy over the *1619 Project*, the protests against Confederate monuments, the demands for curriculum reform. In each of these debates, the question of who gets to tell the story of slavery—and how—has been central.

Johnson’s answer, as I understand it, is that the story should be told from the “standpoint” of the enslaved. He borrows this term from the tradition of standpoint epistemology associated with feminist theory and critical race studies. The idea, roughly, is that the perspectives of the oppressed are epistemically privileged: they see things that the powerful miss, because their survival depends on understanding both their own condition and the condition of their oppressors. The enslaved, in this view, had a clearer understanding of slavery than the slaveholders did, because the slaveholders were blinded by ideology and self-interest. And the black radical tradition, which carries forward the insights of the enslaved, is therefore a more reliable guide to the history of racial capitalism than the white Marxist tradition, which has historically been blind to questions of race.

There is a great deal of truth in this. The slave narratives, the folk tales, the spirituals, the oral histories collected by the WPA—these are extraordinary sources of insight into the world the enslaved made and the world that made them. And Johnson is right to chide historians who treat these sources as merely illustrative or supplemental, rather than as central to their analytic frameworks. But I worry that Johnson’s version of standpoint epistemology is too simple. It treats “the standpoint of the enslaved” as if it were a single, unified thing—as if all enslaved people had the same perspective, and as if that perspective could be transmitted intact across the generations to the present. This is not how standpoint epistemology is supposed to work, at least in its more sophisticated versions. Standpoints are achieved, not given. They are the product of collective struggle and critical reflection, not the automatic consequence of occupying a particular social location. And they are always partial, always situated, always open to contestation.

The black radical tradition, in other words, is a tradition because it is contested. There have been sharp debates within it—between integrationists and separatists, between Marxists and cultural nationalists, between feminists and male-centered organizers, between respectability politics and radical self-assertion. To invoke “the standpoint of the enslaved” as an authority is to gloss over these debates and to foreclose the possibility that there might be multiple, conflicting standpoints that are all rooted in the experience of slavery. It is also to foreclose the possibility that the enslaved themselves might have disagreed with each other, and with the uses to which their descendants have put their memory.

I want to be careful here. I am not saying that Johnson is wrong to center the perspectives of the oppressed. I am saying that centering those perspectives requires more than an act of will or a citation of Du Bois. It requires a methodology: a systematic effort to recover the voices of the enslaved, to read them critically rather than hagiographically, and to place them in conversation with the other voices—the slaveholders, the abolitionists, the imperial administrators, the workers in Manchester—who also populate the history of racial capitalism. It requires, in short, exactly the kind of social history that Johnson laments is disappearing, but with a self-reflexive awareness of the limits and biases of the archive.

The archive is the elephant in the room here. Johnson is writing about the history of slavery, but he is remarkably silent about the sources. Most of what we know about the experiences of enslaved people comes from records created by their oppressors: plantation records, court documents, runaway advertisements, the occasional interview or memoir. These sources are profoundly skewed. They record what slaveholders thought was important, not what enslaved people thought was important. They are shot through with the distortions of racism and the self-justifications of power. To extract the “standpoint of the enslaved” from such sources is not a simple matter of looking; it is a demanding interpretive labor, one that requires not only historical skill but also a kind of ethical attunement to the traces of resistance, resilience, and world-making that survive in the cracks of the archive.

Johnson knows this, of course. He is a skilled practitioner of the historian’s craft, and his previous work on the slave markets of New Orleans and the world of the Mississippi Valley slaveholders demonstrates his ability to read sources against the grain. But his essay does not reflect on the challenges of that reading, or on the ways that the archive itself is a product of the very processes he is describing. The plantation ledgers that record the value of enslaved people are not neutral documents; they are instruments of racial capitalism, designed to track human beings as assets. When we use them as sources for the history of slavery, we are, in a sense, participating in that instrumentalization—even as we try to turn the tools of the master against him. I wish Johnson had said more about this, and about what it means for his larger argument about justice.

Let me try to bring this back to the question of justice. If the history of slavery is a standpoint from which to rethink justice, as Johnson argues, then we need to be clear about how that standpoint is constructed. It is not simply given by the fact of oppression; it is produced through a process of historical recovery, political struggle, and theoretical reflection. And that process is itself a site of justice claims. Who gets to do the recovering? Who gets to tell the story? Who gets to decide what the story means for the present? These are questions of power, and they are not answered by appealing to “the standpoint of the enslaved” as if it were a settled thing. They are answered, if they are answered at all, by the often messy and contentious work of building movements, institutions, and solidarities.

Johnson’s essay is a powerful contribution to that work. But it is not the last word, and I do not think it means to be. It is an opening, a provocation, a starting point for a conversation that is long overdue. For that, I am grateful, even as I find myself wanting more.

  2. 02Centering the perspectives of the oppressed requires a rigorous social history methodology that reads voices hagiographically and critically rather than just an act of political will.
  3. 03The essay overlooks the epistemic challenges posed by the archive, which is itself an instrument of racial capitalism that requires demanding interpretive labor to read against the grain.
  4. 04A truly just historical recovery must address the power dynamics of who is authorized to speak for the enslaved and how their stories are translated into contemporary political claims.
