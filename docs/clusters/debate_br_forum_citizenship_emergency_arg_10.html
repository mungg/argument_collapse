"While some of Scarry’s operational comparisons are overstated, her essay serves as an urgent constitutional warning against the executive branch's long-standing habit of using the 'argument from speed' to bypass congressional war-making authority."

Elaine Scarry’s essay is most compelling when read not as an aviation-security memorandum but as a constitutional warning. Her central subject is not really the failure to intercept Flight 77 or even the heroism aboard Flight 93. It is the long transfer of war-making authority from the people’s representatives to the executive, justified again and again by the claim that modern danger moves too quickly for deliberation. On that point she is right, and the point is urgent.

The Constitution does not give Congress the power to declare war because the framers imagined war to be slow, tidy, or convenient. They gave Congress that power because they feared executive war. They knew emergencies would arise. They knew foreign dangers could be sudden. They also knew that executives are tempted by glory, secrecy, and unilateral action. The requirement of legislative authorization was not an anachronistic procedural ornament. It was a structural safeguard against the most dangerous power government possesses: the power to move a society from peace to organized killing.

For half a century, that safeguard has weakened. Korea was called a police action. Vietnam grew by increments under resolutions and executive claims. Presidents have ordered force in Grenada, Panama, Libya, the Persian Gulf, Somalia, Haiti, Bosnia, and Kosovo under varying mixtures of statutory authorization, United Nations resolutions, NATO commitments, and Article II assertions. Some of these actions were defensible on the merits; some were not. But the pattern is unmistakable: Congress often complains, sometimes funds, occasionally gestures, and rarely decides. The War Powers Resolution of 1973 was meant to restore balance, but presidents have treated it as either unconstitutional or permissive. The people’s branch has become a commentator on wars it was supposed to authorize.

Now, in October 2002, we face the possibility of war with Iraq. The administration argues that Saddam Hussein possesses or seeks weapons of mass destruction, has defied the United Nations, and may someday provide terrible weapons to terrorists. These are grave claims. They may justify coercive action; they certainly justify inspection, containment, and debate. But they do not justify executive war without Congress. If anything, the absence of immediate attack makes constitutional deliberation more, not less, necessary. A preventive war—one fought not to repel an attack underway but to forestall a possible future danger—requires the clearest public authorization.

Scarry’s discussion of “speed” bears directly on this moment. The case for executive unilateralism is strongest when time is shortest: an invasion force crossing a border, missiles inbound, an attack unfolding. But Iraq is not such a case. The administration has been making its argument for months. Members of Congress have time to examine evidence, propose limits, ask whether inspections can work, debate regional consequences, and decide whether military force is necessary. To rush such a decision while invoking emergency is to counterfeit emergency. It converts the atmosphere of September 11 into a standing permission slip.

Yet I would separate this constitutional argument from some of Scarry’s broader claims. The passengers of Flight 93 offer an example of democratic action under extreme duress, but they were not a legislature in any ordinary sense. They were a trapped community deciding whether to resist murderers. Their phone calls and vote are profoundly moving, but they cannot bear all the weight Scarry places on them. We should be careful not to turn a desperate act of courage into a constitutional parable too neat for the realities of government.

The Constitution does not require that all defensive action be decentralized. The president is commander in chief for a reason. Once war is authorized, tactical decisions cannot be made by Congress. Even before authorization, the president must be able to repel sudden attacks. If more hijacked planes had been approaching Washington on September 11, no one would have expected the House and Senate to convene before fighters took off. A constitutional order can contain emergency powers without surrendering to emergency government.

The problem is not action in genuine emergency. The problem is the migration of emergency reasoning into non-emergency decisions. Nuclear first use is the most extreme example. If one person can initiate nuclear war, then constitutional democracy has a hole at its center. Perhaps during the Cold War, when officials feared a Soviet first strike, some rapid-response capacity seemed unavoidable. But even then citizens were entitled to ask whether launch-on-warning policies, hair-trigger alerts, and sole presidential authority were compatible with republican government. Today, with the Soviet Union gone, the argument for such compressed authority is weaker. At minimum, nuclear use should require more than one person’s decision, and first use should be renounced or subjected to explicit congressional policy. No constitutional structure should make civilization depend on the solitary judgment of a single official in minutes.

Scarry is also right to connect war powers with civil liberties. Emergency centralization rarely confines itself to battlefields. It changes the relation between citizen and state. Since September 11, the government has detained noncitizens in secrecy, expanded surveillance powers, and blurred lines between immigration enforcement and anti-terror investigation. Some legal changes may be justified by real dangers. But secrecy about who has been detained and why is corrosive. The government may not defend constitutional democracy by making people disappear from public accountability. Courts and Congress must insist that even noncitizens possess basic legal protections, and that executive claims of necessity be tested.

Still, constitutionalism must not become nostalgia. The framers did not design a detailed system for nuclear weapons, global terrorism, commercial aviation, cyber vulnerability, or biological attack. We cannot simply “return” to 1789. The question is how to translate constitutional principles into modern conditions. That translation may include standing armed forces, intelligence agencies, rapid-response units, and classified operations. The founders distrusted standing armies, but the United States cannot defend itself today with only a militia. The constitutional principle is not that all military capacity must be physically distributed among citizens. It is that military capacity must remain politically subordinate to civilian law, legislative authorization, and public judgment.

A better constitutional settlement would include several elements. First, Congress should reclaim war authorization. It should not pass vague resolutions allowing the president to determine whether war is appropriate whenever he thinks best. If it authorizes force against Iraq, it should specify purposes, reporting requirements, relation to United Nations efforts, and limits on escalation. Second, Congress should require regular public accounting of emergency powers used domestically: detentions, surveillance, military tribunals, and communications controls. Third, nuclear command authority should be reviewed, with serious consideration given to no-first-use, de-alerting, and shared authorization procedures. Fourth, citizens should have access to information sufficient for democratic judgment. Classified details may remain classified; the basic case for war cannot.

The deepest lesson of Flight 93 is not that passengers should replace pilots, soldiers, or officials. It is that people treated as responsible moral agents can act responsibly. The passengers were not protected by ignorance. They were empowered by knowledge. They did not need soothing assurances; they needed facts. That is a lesson our government should take to heart. A republic cannot be defended by a public kept permanently in the dark.

In the coming days Congress may vote on Iraq. That vote should not be a ritual of deference wrapped in patriotic language. It should be an act of constitutional responsibility. Members should ask whether the threat is imminent, whether inspections are possible, whether war will reduce or increase danger, whether allies are with us, what postwar obligations would follow, and what authority they are actually granting. They should remember that the Constitution assigns them not the power to applaud the commander in chief but the power to decide on war.

Scarry has perhaps overstated the operational meaning of September 11. But she has not overstated the democratic danger of our response. The American emergency is real. So is the temptation to let emergency become a form of government. Citizenship in emergency means refusing that temptation. It means insisting that fear may quicken our attention but must not cancel our Constitution.
