"To effectively dismantle Islamophobia, we must reject the 'good Muslim' innocence narrative and instead challenge the broader, interconnected machinery of racial control that links the War on Terror to anti-Blackness, mass incarceration, and imperial warfare."

Akbar and Theoharis have done a vital service by insisting that the Muslim Ban did not spring fully formed from the brow of Donald Trump. They are right: the apparatus of suspicion, surveillance, and preemptive prosecution that now threatens millions of American Muslims was built by both parties over two decades, with the quiet assent of the courts and the public. But to understand why this apparatus has been so resilient—and how we might finally dismantle it—we must situate it within a broader set of interlocking systems of racial control that our country has long refused to confront. Islamophobia is not a stand-alone bigotry; it is the latest mutation of a centuries-old project that binds together anti-Blackness, immigrant scapegoating, and imperial war. Until we see these as a single machinery, we will be left defending Muslims only as the “good” ones, all the while leaving the machinery itself intact.

The airport protests that followed Trump’s first travel ban were electrifying. Tens of thousands of people, many of them non-Muslim, chanted “Let them in!” and held signs declaring “Refugees welcome.” It was a beautiful display of solidarity. Yet the terms of that solidarity often troubled me. Much of the outrage focused on the cruelty of detaining grandmothers and toddlers, or the injustice of barring interpreters who had risked their lives for U.S. soldiers. Rarely did the crowd’s empathy extend to the young man whose social media posts had been judged “questionable,” or to the family whose charitable giving had ended up on a watchlist. The message was clear: these specific Muslims are innocent, so they deserve entry. But the underlying premise—that there exists a category of “dangerous” Muslims who might deserve exclusion—remained untouched. The lead essay is absolutely right to call out this “innocence” framing as a dangerous trap. It is a trap because it leaves the door wide open for the state to define who is suspect, and because it mirrors the logic that has devastated Black communities for generations. We are told that if you are not a criminal, you have nothing to fear from the police; if you are not a terrorist, you have nothing to fear from the NSA. And yet we know that Black and brown people are systematically criminalized, that whole neighborhoods are surveilled, that minor infractions become pretexts for life-altering violence. The “innocent” Muslim and the “nonviolent” Black citizen are both concessions the state grants—temporarily, revocably—while reserving the right to come for the rest.

The material support statute, which Akbar and Theoharis rightly identify as a cornerstone of the domestic War on Terror, functions exactly like the conspiracy and racketeering laws that have been used for decades to destroy Black political organizations. Under the guise of fighting gangs or drug cartels, prosecutors have criminalized association itself: attending a meeting, offering a ride, wearing a certain color. Similarly, the broad “material support” ban turns charitable donations, travel, even speech into terrorism offenses. In both contexts, the law does not simply punish actions; it polices community. It compels informants, sows distrust, and makes collective organizing near-suicidal. The FBI’s infamous COINTELPRO operations against Black leaders and the post-9/11 infiltration of mosques are not anomalies; they are standard procedure.

The War on Drugs and the War on Terror are not merely analogous but structurally fused. Surveillance technologies developed for foreign battlefields have been transferred to domestic police departments under the 1033 program that funnels military equipment to local law enforcement. The tanks that rolled into Ferguson after Michael Brown’s killing came from the same Pentagon surplus that arms the endless wars in Somalia, Yemen, and Syria. The no-knock raids, the informant networks, the fusion centers that share intelligence between local cops and federal agencies—all of these blur the line between criminal law and national security. American Muslims experience this blurring directly when they are stopped at the border not for any crime but because the CBP’s algorithms flag them, or when an undercover agent tries to entrap a vulnerable teenager into a fake terrorism plot while the FBI simultaneously runs nearly identical stings against Black activists. If we only fight Islamophobia as a discrete problem, we cede the ground to a national security apparatus that will simply find new populations to label as threats.

The lead essay gestures toward this connection but pulls back. It rightly notes that “vibrant, growing movements for black liberation and immigrant rights” have shifted public discourse, but it stops short of demanding a unified front against the entire architecture of surveillance, policing, and preemptive detention. That is a mistake. The same forces that have kept Guantánamo open for fifteen years are the forces that sustain mass incarceration in supermax prisons on U.S. soil. When we exceptionalize Guantánamo—treating it as a lawless aberration while our federal prisons are somehow legitimate—we obscure the fact that the “ordinary” criminal system also deploys secret evidence (classified information in terrorism trials, but also confidential informants whose identities are hidden in drug cases), inhumane conditions (solitary confinement is routine in both), and grossly disproportionate sentences. The demand to close Guantánamo must be part of a broader demand to dismantle the carceral state, not to relocate its practices to an already brutal domestic system.

A genuinely liberatory politics cannot rely on the state’s certification of who is a “good” Muslim, any more than it can rely on the state’s recognition of “respectable” Blackness. The Khans’ story is heartbreaking, but its political uptake—that Muslims can belong only if they prove their patriotism through military sacrifice—is a poison pill. It implies that the millions of Muslims who oppose U.S. foreign policy, who criticize drone strikes, who practice a conservative piety, or who simply refuse to perform gratitude are less entitled to equal protection. This is the logic of conditional citizenship, and it is as old as the Republic. It was deployed against Japanese Americans who had to demonstrate “loyalty” through a humiliating questionnaire while in internment camps. It is deployed today against Black protesters told to stop being “divisive” and instead “work within the system.” The response to Trump cannot be a plea for a kinder, gentler version of this bargain.

So what is to be done? First, we must refuse to trade in the currency of innocence. The demand should never be “Protect these peaceful Muslims,” but rather “End the entire regime of surveillance, watchlisting, and preemptive prosecution.” No one should be on a secret No Fly List, regardless of their politics. Material support laws must be repealed or radically narrowed so that they no longer criminalize association, speech, or humanitarian aid. Countering Violent Extremism programs, which treat Muslim religiosity as a potential precursor to violence, must be defunded entirely—and alongside them, the gang databases and “bias-free” predictive policing tools that similarly profile Black and Latino youth as proto-criminals.

Second, we must build coalitions that are not merely defensive. It is not enough to show up at the airport for a weekend when a ban is announced. We need sustained organizing that links mosques with Black Lives Matter chapters, immigrant rights groups with privacy advocates, antiwar veterans with Palestinian solidarity activists. The state thrives on dividing us into silos: the “worthy” refugee and the “suspicious” asylum seeker, the “peaceful” protester and the “rioter.” Our solidarity must be as stubborn as their categorization is fluid.

Third, we must confront the imperial dimension head-on. The lead essay mentions the 23,000 bombs dropped in 2015 on majority-Muslim countries. That number should haunt every American. We cannot meaningfully oppose Islamophobia at home while our tax dollars fuel destruction abroad. The wars create the very refugees we then bar; the drone strikes produce the grievances that the government then cites as evidence of extremism. Any movement that seeks to end the targeting of Muslims must also demand an end to the bipartisan consensus on militarism, from Obama’s “kinetic strikes” to Trump’s escalations.

The moment is terrifying, but it is also clarifying. The mask has slipped, and the ugly continuity is now visible to millions who had not seen it before. Our task is to ensure that the outrage does not settle into a comfortable nostalgia for the Obama years, when the mechanisms of the security state were wrapped in more elegant language. Instead, we must seize this crisis to dismantle not just the ban, not just the “Muslim registry” talk, but the whole edifice. That means embracing a vision of justice in which no community is asked to prove its worth by dying for empire, and in which safety is defined not by the number of informants we plant in mosques, but by the depth of care we offer one another. The airport protests showed us what solidarity looks like in an emergency. Now we have to build the permanent infrastructure to dismantle what the emergency revealed.
