"Reclaiming freedom for the left requires more than better messaging; it necessitates building democratic institutions that allow people to experience collective agency and self-rule in their daily lives."

Aziz Rana is right to insist that the left’s problem with freedom is not primarily semantic. The word has not drifted rightward because Democrats forgot to hire better consultants or because progressive candidates failed to say “freedom” enough times in stump speeches. It has drifted because many Americans experience the institutions around them—workplaces, schools, courts, hospitals, landlords, police departments, immigration offices—not as sites of democratic agency but as places where decisions are made elsewhere. Under those conditions, the right’s promise of freedom as release from constraint, from taxes, from regulation, from unfamiliar neighbors, has an intuitive appeal. It may not deliver self-rule, but it gives a name to resentment.

The strongest part of Rana’s essay is his insistence that a democratic meaning of freedom must be lived before it can be believed. That claim should become a starting point for left politics. Too often progressive freedom talk has the cadence of a moral lecture: you are not really free unless others are free too; you are not really free unless institutions are democratic; you are not really free unless your private choices are embedded in public obligations. All true. But truth is not the same as political force. If people have no ordinary experience of collective power, solidarity can sound like another demand placed on already burdened lives.

The old labor movement understood this better than many contemporary progressives do. A union was not merely a redistributive instrument, though it was that. It was also a school of freedom. In a shop where workers could elect stewards, file grievances, bargain over schedules, and strike, freedom meant something more than being left alone. It meant having a say. It meant that the foreman’s whim could be contested. It meant that wage workers, who were formally free to quit but practically dependent on a paycheck, could exercise power together. No messaging operation can substitute for that kind of experience.

This is why recent labor activity matters beyond the contract gains themselves. The strikes and organizing drives of the last few years—among teachers, nurses, autoworkers, graduate workers, warehouse workers, baristas, hotel workers, and others—have been important not only because they sought higher wages or better staffing ratios. They have reopened a basic question: who should govern the workplace? When autoworkers challenge a two-tier wage system, they are not just asking for money. They are rejecting a structure that tells younger workers they are permanently less entitled to security. When nurses demand safe staffing, they are not merely bargaining for working conditions. They are asserting that those who care for patients should have authority over the conditions of care. These are freedom claims, whether or not every striker uses that word.

But Rana’s account also asks us to see why such claims have been difficult to sustain in the United States. His history of settler freedom is especially useful because it explains a durable emotional pattern: the belief that government coercion is tyranny when applied to insiders but legitimate when directed against outsiders. One can see this pattern in nineteenth-century Indian removal, in slavery and Jim Crow, in anti-Mexican violence, in the policing of Black neighborhoods, in immigration raids, and in today’s fantasies of mass deportation. The same people who denounce public health rules as despotism often celebrate the state when it cages migrants or clears homeless encampments. That is not a contradiction. It is a theory of freedom organized around membership and domination.

Still, if the left is to build a more democratic freedom, it must avoid two temptations. The first is to speak as if ordinary Americans who are drawn to right-wing freedom are simply dupes of empire and racism. Some are deeply invested in racial hierarchy, and that must be said plainly. But many also respond to a real loss of control: plant closures, medical debt, unaffordable housing, algorithmic management, decaying public services, and an electoral system that often feels immune to popular pressure. A left freedom politics should be unsparing about the reactionary channeling of that loss, but it should not deny the loss itself.

The second temptation is to treat institution building as a slow substitute for immediate political conflict. Rana rightly says there is no shortcut around unions, voting rights, and democratic workplaces. Yet institution building requires state power. Workers cannot simply will a new labor regime into existence while employers enjoy weak penalties for union busting. Tenants cannot build durable associations when eviction is swift and housing is scarce. Parents cannot participate meaningfully in schools that are starved of resources. The left needs both bottom-up organizing and legislative confrontation: labor law reform, sectoral bargaining experiments, just-cause eviction protections, public housing, universal health care, child care, and voting rights. These are not technocratic add-ons. They are the scaffolding of self-rule.

Rana’s foreign policy argument is also essential. The United States cannot indefinitely practice domination abroad and democracy at home without the former contaminating the latter. Militarized habits travel. So do racial stories about dangerous populations needing discipline. So do budgetary priorities. When public money flows easily to weapons but grudgingly to schools, hospitals, transit, and climate adaptation, citizens learn what kind of security counts. The wars in Afghanistan and Iraq did not make Americans freer; they helped normalize secrecy, surveillance, executive unilateralism, anti-Muslim suspicion, and a politics in which sacrifice was demanded of soldiers and civilians abroad while the public at home was asked mainly to shop.

The hard question is how to make anti-imperial freedom tangible to people who do not experience foreign policy as part of their daily lives. One answer is budgetary: every unnecessary weapons system is a school not repaired, a lead pipe not replaced, a clinic not funded. But the deeper answer is moral and institutional. A country that accepts limits abroad may become more capable of democracy at home because it must give up the fantasy that security comes from command. It must learn negotiation, interdependence, and restraint. Those are democratic virtues.

The left should reclaim freedom, but not by pretending the word is innocent. Rana’s essay shows that American freedom has often been built through unfreedom. The point is not to purify the term by assertion. It is to change the social relations that give it content. Freedom should mean not fearing a boss’s retaliation, not rationing insulin, not being trapped in an abusive relationship by poverty, not watching police treat your neighborhood as occupied territory, not having your family separated by an immigration bureaucracy, not seeing your future consumed by climate disaster, not being told that democracy ends at the factory gate or the border.

Such a freedom will not be won by slogans. But slogans may matter once backed by institutions. “Freedom” on the right has power because it condenses a world of practices: guns, property, policing, borders, markets, and suspicion of the state. A democratic freedom must condense another world: unions, public goods, equal citizenship, demilitarization, racial justice, reproductive autonomy, and shared power. The task is not merely to say that this freedom is better. It is to make enough people live it that the word begins to sound different in their mouths.
