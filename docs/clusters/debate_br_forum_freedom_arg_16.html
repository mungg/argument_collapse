"To reclaim freedom as a practice of collective self-rule, the United States must undergo a fundamental constitutional reconstruction that dismantles the structural veto points and countermajoritarian institutions currently obstructing democratic majorities."

Aziz Rana’s essay returns freedom to an older republican question: who rules? That is the right question. A person subject to arbitrary power is not free merely because no one interferes at a given moment. A worker who can be fired at will for no good reason, a tenant dependent on a negligent landlord, a citizen whose vote is diluted by gerrymandering, a migrant vulnerable to deportation, a community governed by distant capital—all live under forms of domination. Rana is right that freedom must mean self-rule, not simply noninterference.

But the institutional implications of that insight should lead us not only to labor organizing and anti-imperialism, important as both are, but also to constitutional reconstruction. American freedom has been stunted because American democracy has been stunted. The United States is full of veto points, unequal representation, countermajoritarian courts, money-driven elections, and administrative structures that separate public decision from popular control. If people do not experience collective agency, one reason is that the constitutional order repeatedly teaches them that majorities can vote and still not govern.

Consider the Senate. Wyoming and California have equal representation despite vast population differences. This is not a minor procedural oddity. It means citizens do not have equal voice in national lawmaking. It also means that a coalition representing a minority of the population can block labor law reform, climate legislation, voting rights, gun regulation, reproductive rights, and social provision. The malapportionment of the Senate then shapes the Electoral College, the federal judiciary, and the amendment process. A freedom politics that does not confront this structure leaves the machinery of minority rule intact.

The Electoral College likewise distorts democratic freedom. Twice in recent decades, presidents have taken office after losing the popular vote. Even when that does not happen, campaigns concentrate attention on a handful of swing states while most citizens are spectators. The presidency is the only nationally elected office, yet it is not nationally elected in any straightforward democratic sense. This weakens the relationship between citizenship and equal political power.

The Supreme Court compounds the problem. Judicial review can protect rights, and any serious left must acknowledge that courts have sometimes been indispensable for minorities. But the present Court’s power is extraordinary: life-tenured justices selected through a skewed Senate and Electoral College can invalidate economic regulation, weaken unions, expand gun rights, restrict affirmative action, and eliminate abortion protections. When constitutional meaning is so insulated from democratic correction, citizens learn that freedom is something granted or withdrawn by a legal priesthood.

Rana emphasizes institution building, and rightly so. Yet the basic political institution—the constitutional system itself—requires democratization. This should include statehood for the District of Columbia and, if its people choose, Puerto Rico; a path to a national popular vote for president; independent redistricting; automatic voter registration; robust protection against voter suppression; campaign finance reform; limits on partisan gerrymandering; expansion or reform of the federal courts; and, ultimately, a serious conversation about constitutional amendment. These are not “process” issues separate from freedom. They determine whether Americans can govern themselves.

Rana’s history of settler freedom helps explain why these reforms meet such resistance. The American system has often treated democracy as dangerous when it threatens entrenched hierarchies. Enslaved people, women, Indigenous nations, propertyless men, immigrants, Black citizens in the South, residents of U.S. territories, and people with felony convictions have all been excluded or partially included. Freedom was repeatedly defined as the security of some against the political claims of others. Today, restrictions on voting, purges, felony disenfranchisement, and attacks on election administration continue that tradition in updated form.

Yet I would place more weight than Rana does on the democratic potential within the constitutional tradition. The Reconstruction Amendments, for example, were not mere additions to a settler constitution. They were a second founding, born of war, emancipation, and Black political struggle. They redefined citizenship, equal protection, due process, and voting rights. Their promise was betrayed by Jim Crow and narrowed by courts, but not extinguished. The labor movement, women’s suffrage movement, civil rights movement, and later rights movements all appealed to constitutional principles as well as to moral ones. The left should not abandon constitutional patriotism to conservatives. It should contest what the Constitution is for.

A democratic constitutionalism would define freedom as non-domination secured by equal citizenship. That means rights, but it also means power. Free speech is essential, but if money dominates political communication, speech becomes unequal in practice. Voting is essential, but if districts are rigged or offices malapportioned, votes do not translate into rule. Property rights matter, but if property allows employers and landlords to exercise arbitrary authority, law must intervene. Federalism can protect pluralism, but if states use it to deny basic rights, national citizenship must prevail.

This framework also clarifies the relationship between individual and collective freedom. Some critics of left freedom worry that democratic self-rule will trample personal liberty. They are right to worry. Majorities can oppress. That is why a democratic constitution must protect bodily autonomy, religious liberty, due process, equal protection, and dissent. But minorities can oppress too, especially when constitutional structures give them durable veto power. The current danger is not an excess of majoritarian democracy. It is a system in which unpopular positions can become entrenched law because institutions overrepresent some citizens and underrepresent others.

Take abortion after Dobbs. The Court returned the issue to “the people,” but not to an actually equal democratic process. In many states, gerrymandered legislatures enacted or maintained bans despite divided or hostile public opinion. Some officials then tried to make ballot initiatives harder. Here the issue of bodily freedom merged with the issue of democratic freedom. A person denied reproductive autonomy by a minority-entrenched state is not living under self-rule.

Or take labor law. Polls show high public approval of unions, and organizing interest has risen. Yet national labor reform remains blocked by Senate rules and partisan structures. Workers are told they are free to organize while the legal system leaves them exposed. Political democracy and workplace democracy are mutually reinforcing; their obstruction is also mutually reinforcing.

Rana is right to connect domestic democracy to empire. A constitutional order that tolerates domination abroad will struggle to sustain equal freedom at home. But the reverse is also true: a more democratic constitutional system would make imperial adventures harder. Congress has ceded war powers to presidents. The public has limited influence over security policy. Defense spending is spread across districts to manufacture dependence. Secret law and classified rationales weaken accountability. Anti-imperial freedom requires constitutional checks on the national security state: stronger war powers enforcement, transparency, limits on emergency authority, and democratic oversight of arms sales and military commitments.

The language of freedom can be reclaimed if Americans experience themselves as citizens rather than subjects. That requires unions, social movements, and local institutions. But it also requires a constitutional order that does not convert popular majorities into frustrated spectators. The old republican fear was domination by kings. Our problem is domination by a more complex regime: employers, courts, donors, gerrymanders, police, agencies, presidents, and inherited constitutional compromises.

A different freedom will require a different democracy. Not a democracy without rights, not a democracy of momentary passions, but a democracy in which equal citizens can actually shape the laws under which they live. Freedom as self-rule cannot flourish in institutions designed to distrust the people. It is time to trust them more—and to build the constitutional forms that make such trust real.
