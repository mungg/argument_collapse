In Defense of the Silicon Valley That Actually Exists

Dan Breznitz's essay is an elegant exercise in taxonomy, and I will concede much of its descriptive content. Of course there are multiple stages of innovation. Of course Taiwanese foundries and Italian shoe designers add enormous value. Of course it is silly for every mid-sized American city to imagine itself as the next Mountain View. But I want to push back hard against the implicit framing that "Silicon Valley" is the villain of a morality play about inequality and lost prosperity, and that the alternative is some virtuous focus on incremental innovation. This framing distorts both what Silicon Valley actually does and what the costs of moving away from it would be.

Start with the empirical case. Breznitz leans heavily on Israel to argue that VC-led, exit-oriented stage one innovation widens inequality. But the inequality of contemporary Israel has many fathers, including the demographic explosion of the Haredi and Israeli Arab populations whose labor force participation lags the rest of the country, the political economy of settlement subsidies, the breakdown of the Histadrut-era corporatist wage compression, and the chronic underfunding of Arab-Israeli education. To pin "second most egalitarian to most unequal" on the high-tech sector requires ignoring most of what social scientists actually know about the Israeli economy. The high-tech sector is in fact one of the few parts of that economy generating high-productivity employment and tax revenue; without it, Israel's distributional picture would almost certainly be worse, not better.

The same elision happens with Silicon Valley itself. Yes, the Bay Area has nightmarish housing costs and shameful homelessness. Yes, the gulf between a Google L7 and the contractor who cleans the cafeteria is grotesque. But the principal cause of Bay Area inequality is not the venture capital business model. It is local zoning policy that has refused to permit anything resembling adequate housing construction for forty years. San Francisco's homelessness crisis is reproduced almost as severely in Los Angeles and Seattle, which have very different industrial bases. The pathology Breznitz attributes to stage one innovation is in significant part a pathology of land-use politics. Fixing zoning would do more for inclusive prosperity in the Bay Area than redirecting capital to Taiwanese-style component manufacturing.

Now consider what stage one innovation actually buys us. mRNA vaccines were not the product of stage three incrementalism. They were the product of decades of high-risk basic and translational research, much of it funded through exactly the venture-backed biotech model Breznitz disparages. The COVID-19 response depended on Moderna and BioNTech—both of them VC-backed exit-oriented enterprises that looked, to outside observers in 2018, like the sort of speculative bets that "channel money back to foreign investors." Without that ecosystem, hundreds of thousands more people would be dead. Similarly, the foundational work on lithium-ion batteries, solar photovoltaics, and the machine-learning revolution all came out of research environments and financing structures that include American stage one innovation as a central node.

Breznitz is right that incremental innovation matters. He is wrong to imply that one can substitute for the other. Taiwan's brilliance at semiconductor fabrication is real, but those fabs produce chips designed in California, on architectures developed in California, running software written in Cambridge and Seattle. The global innovation system is genuinely interdependent. Cheerleading for stages two through four while denigrating stage one is like praising the chef while complaining about the farmer.

There is also a question about job quality that Breznitz handles too quickly. He celebrates the Shenzhen production model for creating "jobs to millions of people, many of them immigrants from other parts of China." This is true, and those jobs are an enormous improvement on rural poverty. But anyone who has read accounts of Foxconn dormitories should hesitate before holding up Pearl River Delta employment as a model of inclusive prosperity. The stage four employment Breznitz lauds is built on labor regimes—internal migration controls, dormitory labor, suppressed collective bargaining—that no American or European reader would tolerate at home. The comparison flatters the Chinese model in a way that obscures what would actually be required to reproduce it.

Finally, a word on what Breznitz calls "techno-fetishism." I share his irritation with breathless coverage of yet another DTC mattress startup. But the deeper claim—that we mistakenly equate innovation with new products—undersells how much human welfare actually depends on those new products. The smartphone, for all its discontents, has done more to extend the reach of basic information services to poor people globally than any government program in history. The gene-editing revolution is poised to transform agriculture and medicine. The fact that most people working on these technologies will not get rich and that some of their employers will fail does not change the social return. We need both the Taiwanese gear factory and the Cambridge biotech lab.

The right response to Silicon Valley's excesses, in other words, is not to dismiss the model but to fix its housing markets, tax its windfalls more aggressively, and complement it with serious manufacturing policy. We can chew gum and walk at the same time. What we should not do is conclude from a stylized contrast between Israel and Taiwan that the entire stage one project is misguided. That is throwing out a great deal of baby with not all that much bathwater.

Breznitz's most valuable contribution is the reminder that there are dignified industrial strategies beyond Sand Hill Road. Cities that cannot plausibly become hubs of frontier biotech can still aspire to be Brenta or Sakai. That is a real and useful insight. But the framing that pits inclusive prosperity against high-tech entrepreneurship gets the politics wrong and gets the economics half wrong. The frontier still matters. Pretending otherwise is its own form of fetishism.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The rise in inequality in places like Israel and the San Francisco Bay Area is primarily driven by demographic shifts, political factors, and restrictive land-use policies rather than the venture capital business model.
  2. 02High-risk, stage-one innovation is indispensable for global welfare, as evidenced by the life-saving development of mRNA vaccines and foundational advancements in green technology.
  3. 03The global innovation ecosystem is interdependent, meaning that the incremental improvements in manufacturing and design celebrated by Breznitz rely fundamentally on the frontier architectures developed in stage-one hubs.
  4. 04The labor models underpinning stage-four production in regions like the Pearl River Delta involve harsh conditions and suppressed rights that should not be held up as a desirable standard for inclusive prosperity in democratic societies.
  5. 05The social returns of new products, such as smartphones and gene-editing, provide immense human welfare benefits that justify the risks and financial exits associated with high-tech entrepreneurship.
  6. 06Policymakers should focus on fixing the specific externalities of the Silicon Valley model, such as housing and taxation, rather than dismissing the model itself in favor of a stylized preference for incrementalism.
