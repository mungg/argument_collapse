"While Lawrence Lessig’s concept of institutional corruption is a useful tool for reform, the argument against Citizens United must be rooted more fundamentally in the principle of political equality and the fair capacity of all citizens to participate in collective self-rule."

The most troubling feature of Citizens United is not that it will make Americans trust Congress less, though it probably will. Nor is it simply that it will make members of Congress more dependent on funders, though it may. The deepest problem is that it entrenches a vision of democracy in which political liberty is measured by the absence of restraints on spending rather than by the fair capacity of citizens to participate as equals. Lawrence Lessig’s concept of institutional corruption is powerful, but the argument against Citizens United should be rooted even more fundamentally in political equality.

American constitutional law has long struggled to say whether equality has any place in the First Amendment. Buckley v. Valeo famously rejected the idea that government may restrict the speech of some to enhance the relative voice of others. That rejection has shaped campaign-finance doctrine ever since. The Court treats equality as a forbidden rationale, while corruption remains the only permissible door through which reform may pass. Lessig’s essay tries to widen that door by redefining corruption as improper dependence. This is a clever and historically serious move. But it also reveals the poverty of the doctrine. Why must we translate a concern about equal citizenship into the language of corruption before it can be constitutionally heard?

A democracy is not merely a mechanism for aggregating votes after a privately financed competition for attention. It is a system of collective self-rule among political equals. Voting is the formal expression of that equality, but the campaign preceding the vote matters too. Citizens form opinions, hear arguments, test claims, and decide what issues deserve priority. If some actors can dominate that process because they control immense economic resources, then the equality of the ballot is diminished even if not formally denied. One person still has one vote, but some persons have vastly greater power to shape the choices among which votes are cast.

Justice Kennedy’s opinion assumes that independent expenditures simply provide voters with more information. Sometimes they do. But political communication is not an abstract seminar in which arguments arrive weightless and listeners calmly evaluate them. Repetition matters. Agenda-setting matters. Emotional framing matters. The ability to define an opponent before she defines herself matters. A million-dollar advertising campaign is not just another pamphlet. It can alter the conditions under which citizens deliberate.

Lessig is right that disclosure cannot fully answer this problem. Knowing who paid for a message helps, but it does not undo the effects of saturation. Nor does it address the absence of voices that never obtain comparable reach. The poor, the young, immigrants who cannot vote but are affected by policy, future generations, and diffuse consumers lack concentrated funds. Corporations, unions, and wealthy individuals can speak loudly because they are organized and resourced. The resulting debate may contain many voices, but it is not for that reason democratic.

The difficulty, of course, is that any effort to equalize political voice risks censorship. A government empowered to decide when one speaker has too much influence may abuse that power. This is the strongest argument for Citizens United, and it should not be dismissed. Free political criticism, including criticism by associations, is indispensable. McCain-Feingold’s restriction on electioneering communications was broad enough to create hard cases, and Citizens United itself involved a film about a candidate for president. A democracy that bans such speech has reason to worry about itself.

But the choice is not between censorship and plutocracy. We can design institutions that expand the effective speech of ordinary citizens without suppressing the speech of others. Lessig’s discussion of democracy vouchers points in the right direction. Imagine every eligible voter receiving a publicly funded voucher that can be assigned to candidates, parties, or qualified political organizations. Suddenly candidates would have a reason to seek political support from people who cannot write large checks. Neighborhood meetings, local debates, and citizen networks would become financially meaningful. Fundraising would not disappear; it would be democratized.

Small-donor matching systems serve a similar function. If a $25 contribution from a constituent is matched several times by public funds, candidates can build viable campaigns through broad support. This changes not only who gives money but how candidates spend time. Instead of calling wealthy donors, they must persuade ordinary citizens to invest a little. That is not merely a financing mechanism; it is a deliberative practice. It brings representatives into conversation with the represented.

Such systems should be designed with equality in mind. Matching funds should privilege in-district or in-state donors, because representation is relational. National small-donor enthusiasm can be valuable, but it can also make candidates dependent on ideological publics outside the electorate they seek to represent. Vouchers should be easy to use, available in multiple languages, and accompanied by civic information. Otherwise the already organized will capture them. Public financing must not become a boutique reform for the politically attentive; it must be a tool of mass participation.

We also need institutions that improve the quality of political information. Public debates, nonpartisan voter guides, free or reduced-cost broadcast time for candidates, and stronger public media could reduce the dependence of campaigns on paid advertising. Broadcasters use public airwaves; it is not unreasonable to ask them to serve democratic purposes during elections. The First Amendment protects the press, but it need not prevent thoughtful obligations tied to licenses and public resources. If campaigns are expensive because television time is expensive, then media policy is campaign-finance policy.

Lessig emphasizes trust, and trust matters. But trust can be an ambiguous measure. Citizens may distrust Congress because they are misinformed, because they dislike compromise, because the economy is bad, or because they have been encouraged to despise government. A more democratic system might not immediately produce higher trust. Indeed, empowered citizens may become more demanding and critical. The goal should not be trust as passive confidence. It should be justified trust: the belief that institutions are structured so citizens have a fair chance to be heard and to hold officials accountable.

The corruption frame also risks romanticizing an uncorrupted baseline. The framers’ “People” excluded many people. American democracy has always been a struggle over who counts and whose voice matters. Campaign-finance reform should be understood in continuity with that struggle. Just as the abolition of property requirements, the Reconstruction amendments, women’s suffrage, direct election of senators, and the Voting Rights Act expanded political equality, so too must modern reforms address the unequal conversion of wealth into political power. The ballot was not enough when formal barriers excluded citizens; it is not enough when economic barriers drown them out.

What, then, should courts do? They need not abandon free-speech vigilance. But they should stop treating equality as alien to the First Amendment. The amendment protects democratic self-government, not merely private expressive autonomy. That purpose can sometimes justify carefully drawn limits on electoral spending, especially by entities whose resources derive from economic activity rather than political support. At the very least, courts should permit legislatures to experiment where the evidence shows that concentrated spending undermines equal citizenship and representative responsiveness. Strict scrutiny need not be fatal scrutiny.

Still, given the current Court, the immediate work is political. Congress should pass public financing. States and cities should innovate. Citizens should demand disclosure and shareholder accountability. Reformers should build coalitions across party lines by emphasizing that ordinary conservatives, liberals, and independents alike are marginalized by a donor-driven system. The Tea Party anger now visible on the right and the anti-bank anger on the left share a suspicion that government is responsive to insiders. That suspicion can curdle into nihilism, or it can be organized into democratic reform.

Citizens United is a setback not because it lets more people speak, but because it confuses the spending power of artificial entities with the democratic voice of citizens. Lessig is right that dependence on funders corrupts Congress. But even a perfectly independent Congress would face a problem if public debate were structured by vast inequalities of wealth. Democracy requires more than representatives who are not bribed and not dependent. It requires citizens who can appear in public life as equals. The task after Citizens United is to build institutions worthy of that ideal.
