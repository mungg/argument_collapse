"While Kate Soper’s 'alternative hedonism' correctly identifies the spiritual poverty of affluent consumerism, a truly global post-consumerist politics must move beyond Northern lifestyle philosophy to prioritize global justice, climate reparations, and the diverse material needs of the Global South."

Kate Soper’s call for alternative hedonism is a welcome interruption in a climate politics too often divided between technocratic optimism and apocalyptic warning. She asks a question that must be asked in every rich society: what if the high-consumption life we are defending is not only ecologically impossible but spiritually and sensually impoverished? Yet if the argument is to become genuinely global, it must move even further from the habits of the affluent world. The point is not only that rich societies should consume less differently. It is that they must surrender the authority to define development, pleasure, and progress for everyone else.

Soper recognizes this. She cites the vast inequality of carbon emissions between the richest and poorest people on earth and invokes Indigenous critics of “development” imposed from outside. These are not decorative additions to the argument; they are central. The climate crisis is not humanity in general colliding with nature in general. It is the result of particular histories: colonial extraction, fossil capitalism, racialized labor, land dispossession, militarized trade routes, and debt. A postconsumerist politics that does not confront these histories risks becoming another Northern lifestyle philosophy.

The phrase “alternative hedonism” has real power in London, New York, Paris, Berlin, or other centers of exhausted affluence. It speaks to people who have too much stuff and too little time, too many choices and too little meaning. But in much of the world the central experience is different. It is not the burden of abundance but the violence of deprivation combined with the seduction of an unattainable consumer ideal. Billions of people are told, through media, migration networks, tourism, and development institutions, that the good life means the mall, the car, the air-conditioned apartment, the foreign degree, the meat-heavy diet, the branded phone, the flight. Many also know that this model is destructive and exclusionary. The task is not simply to persuade them to desire less. It is to create conditions under which other aspirations are viable, respected, and materially secure.

This requires climate reparations and debt justice, not only cultural critique. Poor countries cannot be expected to protect forests, leapfrog to clean energy, build climate-resilient infrastructure, and expand health and education while servicing debts and absorbing climate damages they did little to cause. The Pakistan floods of 2022, like droughts and cyclones elsewhere, reveal the cruelty of a world in which vulnerability is treated as misfortune rather than as the outcome of unequal power. If rich countries want to speak of post-growth, they must first ask what they owe: finance for loss and damage, grants rather than loans, technology sharing, fair trade rules, and an end to extractive arrangements that move wealth outward while leaving ecological damage behind.

Soper’s emphasis on “concerned consumers” leading the way should therefore be handled carefully. Consumers in affluent societies do have responsibilities. They can support debt cancellation, climate finance, supply-chain regulation, fossil fuel phaseout, and reduced luxury consumption. But the agent of global transformation cannot be the Northern consumer discovering the pleasures of less. It must include Southern workers, peasants, Indigenous nations, women’s movements, climate justice activists, and states seeking policy space. Otherwise the politics of alternative hedonism remains asymmetrical: the rich rethink their desires while the poor are asked to wait for the benefits.

The question of work illustrates the problem. A shorter workweek is a humane and necessary demand in many affluent economies. It may also be relevant to urban middle classes elsewhere. But as Jayati Ghosh’s comment in the forum preview suggests, peasants cannot simply decide that nature will honor a four-day week. Subsistence farmers, informal workers, street vendors, domestic workers, and migrant laborers face rhythms of work shaped by seasons, survival, and employers who often evade regulation. For many women, the workday never ends because paid work is followed by unpaid cooking, cleaning, water collection, fuel gathering, childcare, and eldercare. A politics of pleasure must begin here: with the redistribution and recognition of care and subsistence labor.

Indeed, any alternative hedonism adequate to the global crisis must be feminist. Consumer capitalism has offered some women forms of autonomy: wages, mobility, contraception, education, public life, escape from patriarchal households. It has also commodified liberation, exploited feminized labor, and intensified beauty discipline, body shame, and time poverty. The answer cannot be a romantic return to household production or community life. Communities can nurture, but they can also police. Self-provisioning can empower, but it can also increase unpaid labor. Slow food is pleasurable when chosen; it is oppressive when women are expected to provide it without help. Repair, reuse, gardening, and local care must be organized as shared, supported practices, not as a new moral burden on women.

This is where Soper’s argument would benefit from a broader vocabulary of pleasure. Pleasure is not only sensory delight or relief from consumer stress. It is also security, dignity, reciprocity, freedom from humiliation, and the capacity to refuse domination. For an Indigenous community, pleasure may include the defense of land, language, ceremony, and intergenerational knowledge. For a garment worker, it may include a living wage and freedom from factory abuse. For an urban migrant, it may include remittances sent home, a smartphone connection to family, and the anonymity of the city. For a queer young person, it may include leaving a “traditional” community that was not safe. No single image of the good life—whether consumerist or postconsumerist—can do justice to these differences.

The critique of travel also looks different from a global perspective. It is true that frequent leisure flying by the affluent is indefensible in a warming world. It is also true that mobility has been one of the great dreams of the colonized and the poor. The right to move has never been equally distributed. Some passports open the world; others trap their holders behind consulates, detention centers, and deadly borders. Calls for slower, less frequent travel must not become a defense of immobility for those already confined. Climate politics should restrict luxury mobility while defending migrant rights and family reunification. The problem is not that people move; it is that a small fraction move constantly and comfortably while others are immobilized or forced into dangerous movement by poverty, war, and climate disruption.

Technology, too, should be approached from the South with ambivalence rather than either celebration or rejection. Rich countries cannot tell poorer ones to avoid industrialization after becoming wealthy through it. At the same time, the fossil path is no longer viable. The just alternative is not low-energy poverty but democratic access to clean energy and appropriate technology. Solar microgrids, public transit, efficient cooking, agroecology, digital communication, vaccines, water systems, and climate-resilient housing can all expand capabilities without reproducing the full wastefulness of Northern consumerism. Some growth is necessary; some growth is destructive. The distinction should be made politically, not abstractly.

Soper’s invocation of Indigenous knowledge is important but also risky. Indigenous peoples should not be valued only because they model sustainability for the rest of us. They have rights whether or not they are ecologically exemplary. Nor should “traditional” lifeways be frozen in place. Indigenous communities, like all communities, contain debate, innovation, conflict, and changing desires. The deeper lesson is not that modern people should imitate Indigenous simplicity. It is that there are many ways to inhabit the earth, and capitalist modernity has violently narrowed them. Protecting plurality is itself an ecological act.

What would a global alternative hedonism look like? It would begin with contraction of excess in rich societies: less meat, fewer flights, smaller and more efficient homes, fewer cars, less fast fashion, less waste, less advertising, less militarism. But it would pair contraction with expansion where expansion is just: clean electricity, public health, education, sanitation, care, climate adaptation, democratic media, and women’s freedom. It would support agroecology and food sovereignty rather than agribusiness dependence. It would defend land rights and forests by empowering the people who live there, not by creating fortress conservation schemes that expel them. It would measure prosperity by capabilities and relationships, not by aggregate output. It would treat time as wealth, but also ask whose time has been stolen.

The most compelling part of Soper’s essay is her insistence that the current order is not pleasurable enough. That claim should be radicalized. The global consumer dream is cruel because it invites everyone to pursue a form of life that only a minority can enjoy and that the planet cannot sustain. But a just alternative cannot be a Northern pastoral fantasy. It must be a world in which many pleasures flourish: the pleasure of a safe home, a restored river, a free afternoon, a dignified wage, a language kept alive, a child breathing clean air, a grandmother cooled during a heat wave, a migrant crossing a border safely, a farmer no longer trapped by debt, a city street reclaimed from traffic, a forest defended by its people.

The good life after consumerism will not be singular. Its plurality is its promise. The rich must consume less so that others may live more fully; but “more fully” will be defined by those whose lives are at stake. Alternative hedonism can help rich societies loosen their grip on a destructive ideal. Global justice requires that they loosen their grip on the future as well.
