Diversified DeepSeek

"While Kate Soper’s critique of consumerism is valuable, her vision of 'alternative hedonism' risks becoming a moralistic and exclusionary framework by assuming a universal, rational subject and failing to account for the diverse, unruly, and identity-driven nature of human desire."

I share Kate Soper’s desire for a world beyond the exhausting, environmentally ruinous cycles of overwork and overconsumption. Her diagnosis of a sick consumer culture is acute, and her call to reimagine pleasure is timely. Yet her vision of “alternative hedonism” has an unexamined underbelly: it presumes a universal subject of pleasure whose desires, once liberated from the advertising industry, will naturally converge on the wholesome, convivial, ecologically sound enjoyments she describes. As Lida Maxwell implies in her comment, if we don’t challenge the very idea of desire as a void to be filled — and I would add, if we don’t reckon with the way pleasures are shaped by gender, sexuality, and race — we risk advocating a politics that remains implicitly white, heteronormative, and ultimately moralistic in its promise of the good life.

Soper’s alternative hedonism rests on a kind of desire that is corrigible, that can be steered by rational persuasion toward better objects. The marketing industry, in her telling, tricks us into wanting the wrong things; once the veil is lifted, we will see that slow travel, bike lanes, and potlucks are genuinely more enjoyable. But queer and feminist theory has long insisted that desire is not so transparently manageable. Desires are contradictory, anachronistic, and sometimes bound up with pain, risk, and that which is not “good for us.” The fantasy of the fast car, the glittering dress, the excessive vacation — these are not simply false wants implanted by an external power. They can also be sites of escape, gender play, or a refusal of the dutiful, productive self. For a teenager in a stifling suburb, the car might be a lifeline of freedom; for a drag performer, fashion’s dizzying churn is not just conformity but the raw material for self-invention. Soper’s framework risks flattening these pleasures into mere error.

This becomes especially problematic when she equates sustainable consumption with virtue and consumerist pleasure with a kind of spiritual poverty. The language of “spiritual poverty” that she approvingly quotes from Nemonte Nenquimo is powerful when used against colonial extraction. But when it becomes a general indictment of affluence, it shades into a secularized puritanism that can stigmatize pleasures that fall outside a narrow conception of the good life. Who decides which pleasures are “false” and which are authentic? Historically, such judgments have been wielded against the leisure of the poor, the indulgences of women, and the sexual cultures of queer people. The 19th-century campaign against drinking, for example, was often a middle-class assault on working-class conviviality dressed in the language of moral improvement.

I am not suggesting Soper intends any of this. But her model of reformed hedonism inadvertently restores a normative hierarchy of pleasures in which the quiet, the local, the handmade, and the child-centric are elevated, while the loud, the commercial, the technologically mediated, and the sexually ambiguous are implicitly disparaged. The alternative city she imagines — with its cycle rickshaws, colonnaded bike lanes, and community gardens — is lovely, but it is also a highly ordered, family-friendly space. Where, one wonders, are the nightclubs, the cruising grounds, the carnivalesque gatherings that have always been part of urban pleasure and that often resist the kind of “community” that Soper idealizes? A genuinely plural hedonism would make room for these, not just as pragmatic concession but as valuable expressions of desire’s unruly diversity.

My point is not that we should defend the ecocidal status quo because drag brunches require glitter and air travel. It is that any credible alternative must grapple with the fact that desire is not simply a matter of choosing the right object. It is bound up with identity, history, and the unconscious in ways that are not easily retrained by green manifestos. A post-consumerist world will have to offer spaces for pleasure that are as wild, experimental, and excessive as those currently provided — often destructively — by capitalism. The degrowth movement has been justly criticized for being too earnest, too ready to substitute the satisfaction of making jam for the thrill of a night out. To avoid that trap, we need a vision of the good life that is not a single, cohesive answer but a field of contestation in which multiple, sometimes incompatible pleasures can coexist.

This suggests a different political strategy. Rather than try to convince everyone that their current pleasures are fake and that true enjoyment lies in biking and knitting, we might ask how to expand the range of pleasures available to people across lines of class, gender, and race, while collectively bearing down on the most destructive forms of consumption. The pleasure of a queer youth in a fast-fashion outfit that finally makes them feel visible does not have to be shamed; it can be honored even as we build systems — libraries of shared clothing, publicly funded queer fashion collectives — that reduce the material throughput. The goal is not to define the good life but to democratize the means of living well, leaving people free to discover what that means for them.

Soper is right that the structure of feeling might be shifting. But that shift must include a recognition that the “we” of alternative hedonism is fractured by different experiences of pleasure, different histories of surveillance and moral regulation. A hedonism worthy of the name must be capacious enough to hold all our messy, beautiful, and sometimes environmentally awkward desires. Otherwise, it risks simply replacing one orthodoxy of the good life with another.
