"The AI revolution will likely follow historical patterns of increasing human prosperity through market-driven innovation, making efforts to centrally redirect the technology unnecessary and potentially harmful."

Daron Acemoglu’s essay is an erudite warning, but it gets the larger story wrong. The AI revolution, like the industrial revolutions before it, will elevate human prosperity overall, even if the transition is painful. The doom-laden narrative of runaway automation and democratic collapse misses the self-correcting mechanisms of market economies and the tremendous creative potential of new technology. We should be cautiously optimistic, not terrified, and we should focus our energies on smoothing the transition rather than attempting to redirect the technology itself in ways that will almost certainly backfire.

Acemoglu’s history lesson is selective. He rightly notes that the mechanization of agriculture displaced nearly half the U.S. workforce without causing permanent mass unemployment, because new tasks in manufacturing and services emerged. But he then claims that the recent decades are different: more automation and fewer new tasks. This narrative fails to account for the actual composition of today’s labor market. The occupations that have grown most rapidly — healthcare practitioners, software developers, data analysts, personal care aides, renewable-energy technicians — are, in large part, products of digital technology and the very information economy that AI extends. It is simply too early to declare that AI will be a net destroyer of human-relevant tasks. Historically, every wave of automation induces predictions of permanent technological unemployment, and every time those predictions have been proven wrong by the emergence of entirely new categories of work. There is no strong reason to believe this time is different.

Moreover, Acemoglu’s focus on labor share as a metric of technological health is misleading. The labor share of value added can decline for reasons that have nothing to do with dystopian automation. The global integration of labor markets, the entry of billions of workers into the global economy, and shifting consumption patterns all affect the relative returns to capital and labor. If AI enables massive productivity gains that lower the prices of goods and services — including healthcare, education, and housing — then a declining labor share could coincide with rising real living standards for ordinary people. The real challenge is not the ratio itself but whether the gains are broadly distributed through lower costs and new opportunities.

On democracy, Acemoglu paints a frightening picture of AI-powered manipulation, but he overstates the technology’s power and understates human agency. Social media polarization certainly poses real problems, but the evidence that AI-driven microtargeting actually flips elections or profoundly changes attitudes is thin and contested. Cambridge Analytica became a sensational story, yet subsequent research suggests that its methods were far less effective than claimed. Meanwhile, the same algorithmic tools that can spread disinformation also empower fact-checkers, investigative journalists, and grassroots movements. The internet and AI have given voice to marginalized communities and enabled mass mobilization in ways that would have been impossible in the buttoned-up media environment of the 1950s. The real threats to democracy are not technological but political: the erosion of voting rights, gerrymandering, the influence of big money in politics, and the decay of civic education. Blaming AI for those deeper woes is a distraction.

Acemoglu’s proposed solutions rest on a dirigiste confidence in government’s ability to steer technological innovation toward the public good. But government is not a wise and impartial planner. The same administrative state that gave us the Vietnam War and the Tuskegee syphilis experiment is now supposed to determine which AI applications are “socially beneficial” and channel funding accordingly. Even with the best intentions, such top-down steering is vulnerable to capture by established interests, bureaucratic inertia, and a persistent lag behind private-sector dynamism. The clean-energy analogy cuts both ways: government subsidies and mandates have indeed supported renewables, but they have also produced spectacular boondoggles and have been heavily shaped by political lobbying rather than dispassionate analysis. Do we really want the same apparatus deciding which AI research is acceptable?

The better path is not to redirect AI but to accelerate it, while simultaneously strengthening the social safety net and investing massively in education and retraining. A universal basic income or a negative income tax, combined with portable benefits, would decouple basic security from employment and give workers the freedom to adapt. Improving the quality and accessibility of lifelong learning would allow people to transition into the new tasks that AI will inevitably create. This approach respects the knowledge problem that no central planner can solve, while still addressing the legitimate worries about inequality.

Acemoglu’s vision, for all its sophistication, ultimately seeks to discipline a technological revolution into a predetermined set of human-defined channels. That impulse has failed every time it has been attempted with epochal transformations. We do not know what the most valuable uses of AI will be in twenty years, just as nobody in 1920 could have predicted the rise of software engineering or digital marketing. The wiser course is to let the technology evolve in response to market demand, while building resilient institutions that can absorb the shocks. Fear should not lead us to stifle the most powerful engine of human betterment ever created. AI’s future does not have to be dystopian, but neither should it be forced into a straitjacket designed by the anxieties of today.

  3. 03The perceived threats to democracy attributed to AI are overstated and distract from deeper, non-technological political issues like gerrymandering and the erosion of voting rights.
