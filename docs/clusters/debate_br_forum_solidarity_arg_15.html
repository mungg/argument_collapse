Material Interests Still Matter

Mie Inouye's essay is generous and humane, but I want to push back on what I read as its central drift: away from material interest as the basis of solidarity and toward something thinner — ideology in her preferred formulation, social endurance as her bottom-line prescription. I worry that this drift reflects, and reinforces, a broader pattern in left intellectual life that has weakened our capacity to build the coalitions we say we want.

Inouye begins with the question Henry asks her: why do you organize? Henry's answer is straightforward: because his family needs material changes to survive. Inouye's answer is more reflexive: because she needs meetings to go to. She admits feeling chastened by the contrast, but the essay then proceeds to recuperate her own answer, treating Henry's material-needs framing as one option among several and ultimately preferring an ideology-based account in which "a shared vision of a just society that aligns with but exceeds our material interests" does the binding work.

I think Henry is right and Inouye is wrong, and I think the essay's discomfort with this conclusion is symptomatic. The most durable solidarities in modern history — the trade union movement, the Black freedom struggle in its mass phases, the welfare rights movement, the farmworkers' organizations, the recent renaissance in graduate-worker unionism — have been built on shared material interest first, with morality and ideology following. People show up, repeatedly, over years, when something concrete is at stake for them. They do not show up, repeatedly, over years, because they have been persuaded of an attractive vision in the abstract.

This is not a reductive economism. Material interest, properly understood, is not just wages. It is housing, healthcare, education, safety from state violence, the capacity to live near one's family and community, time, dignity at work. Black workers in the South had material interests in ending Jim Crow that ran far beyond pay scales. Queer workers have material interests in nondiscrimination that include the protection of their family lives. Disabled people have material interests in accessible workplaces that the able-bodied left has too often failed to register. To say that solidarity rests on material interest is not to say that it rests on the narrow self-interest of the homo economicus model. It is to say that solidarity rests on the recognition of shared stakes in concrete conditions of life.

Inouye, following Jared Clemons, acknowledges that material interest is important and even essential — but then pivots, on the grounds that under contemporary conditions, full interest convergence between the white PMC and the multiracial working class is unlikely. From this she concludes that we need a different basis for coalition, located in shared ideology or shared "visceral stakes" of some kind. I want to suggest this pivot is premature.

First, the claim that interest convergence is impossible under neoliberal conditions is overstated. The PMC's material situation has been deteriorating for years. Adjunctification has destroyed the academic labor market. Junior lawyers, doctors, and journalists face working conditions and debt loads that earlier PMC generations did not. The recent unionization waves among museum workers, journalists, grad students, and even nonprofit staff suggest that significant portions of the PMC have material interests far more aligned with the broader working class than the older sociological category implies. The Hollywood writers' strike currently in progress is a PMC labor struggle, and it has clear material objectives that converge with broader anti-monopoly and anti-AI concerns across the working class.

Second, the empirical record of "ideology-first" coalitions is mixed at best. The Popular Fronts of the 1930s held together because they were also material coalitions — unions, mutual aid societies, ethnic federations — with shared ideological commitments laid on top. When the material infrastructure withered, the ideology alone proved insufficient to hold the coalition. The same is true of the New Left at its peak. SDS could mobilize huge crowds, but it could not sustain itself once the material infrastructure of student life and draft resistance shifted.

Third, the appeal to "ideology" as the binder risks asking people to commit to abstractions before they have committed to each other through concrete struggle. This inverts the actual psychology of political development. People typically arrive at shared ideology through shared experience of fighting for material things. The Memphis sanitation workers did not strike in 1968 because they had been converted to a vision of multiracial socialist democracy. They struck because two of their co-workers had been crushed in a garbage truck and their wages were starvation wages. The ideology came later, and in many of them, it came through the fight itself.

Inouye herself, I think, half-recognizes this. The Bob Lee footage she analyzes so well is not a story of ideology converting white Appalachians to multiracial solidarity. It is a story of an organizer asking, repeatedly, "What do you want?" — that is, eliciting material concerns and then helping participants see the structural sources of those concerns and the strategic value of cross-racial alliance. Lee's work was material-interest organizing of the highest order. The Rainbow Coalition's program was a program of material demands: against police brutality, against urban renewal, for housing, for jobs.

What would it look like to take material interest more seriously than Inouye's essay does? It would mean, first, much more attention to the empirical question of how interests actually align and diverge in particular conjunctures. The PMC is not a monolith; neither is the working class. The interests of an unhoused person in a rapidly gentrifying city are not identical to the interests of a stably housed nurse, and both are different again from the interests of a tech worker whose stock options just vested. Effective solidarity work begins with these particulars, not with general claims about which classes can or cannot ally.

It would mean, second, organizing strategies that lead with concrete fights and let solidarity develop through the fight rather than asking participants to commit to solidarity in advance. The strongest multiracial coalitions in recent labor history — Smithfield, the LA hotel workers, the Chicago teachers — were built by leading with grievances that mattered to people in their daily lives and gradually expanding the political horizon as the struggle developed. None of these was a "solidarity organization" first; they were organizations fighting for specific things, which became solidarity organizations through the fighting.

It would mean, third, more skepticism about the kind of mobilization that asks privileged participants to show up out of moral conviction. Inouye is right that "allyship" turned out to be a thin foundation in 2020. But the answer is not to thicken allyship into ideology; it is to redirect privileged participants toward fights where they have actual stakes — climate, healthcare, housing, working conditions in their own industries — and to build sustained connections from those fights to the broader struggle.

Henry was right at the fundraiser. The most durable reason to organize is because something material is at stake for you and yours. Inouye's reasons — meetings, the joy of association, the generative possibilities — are real, and I don't begrudge them. But they will not, by themselves, build the kind of movement we need. The materialist intuition that Inouye half-resists is the intuition we should be reclaiming, not retreating from. Solidarity follows interest more often than interest follows solidarity, and the left's recent forgetting of this fact has cost us more than we have yet reckoned with.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Historical and contemporary movements demonstrate that people sustain long-term commitment when concrete conditions of life are at stake, whereas ideology-first coalitions are fragile and difficult to maintain.
  2. 02Material interest should be understood broadly to include housing, healthcare, and safety from state violence, rather than being reduced to narrow economic self-interest.
  3. 03The material interests of the professional-managerial class (PMC) and the working class are increasingly converging due to deteriorating labor conditions, making materialist coalitions more viable than the lead essay suggests.
  4. 04Political ideology and shared visions are typically the result of engaging in concrete material struggles together, rather than a prerequisite for joining them.
  5. 05Effective organizing should prioritize eliciting specific grievances and 'what people want' to build solidarity through the process of fighting for those needs, rather than asking for a commitment to abstract solidarity in advance.
