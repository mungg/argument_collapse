The crisis in STEM education isn’t a shortage of intelligence—it’s a failure of imagination. For too many students, math and science remain a joyless grind of rote formulas and disconnected facts, taught by teachers who often lack deep content knowledge and receive little support to inspire. If we want to fill the pipeline with a diverse generation of innovators, we must stop treating these subjects as filter courses designed to weed out all but the “geeks” and start showing every student how science and math are tools to solve problems they actually care about.

The single most powerful lever is relevance. Young people are passionate about climate change, clean water, video games, medical breakthroughs, and building things—yet they rarely see the math and physics behind these endeavors. Project-based learning that ties directly to real-world challenges makes the abstract concrete. When a student writes code to model an epidemic or designs a wind turbine blade in geometry class, the equations stop being a punishment and become a power. Schools need the flexibility and resources to adopt this approach, moving beyond textbooks that still pretend the internet doesn’t exist.

Equally critical is smashing the stale image of the solitary lab-coated genius. Role models matter enormously, especially for women and minorities who remain dramatically underrepresented. Programs that bring engineers, game designers, environmental scientists, and medical professionals into classrooms—particularly those who reflect the students’ own backgrounds—can shatter stereotypes. Mentorship and after-school clubs like robotics, coding, or science Olympiads offer additional entry points where students discover that technical skill is collaborative and creative, not just an endurance test.

Teacher preparation must be overhauled. A shocking number of middle and high school math and science instructors never majored in their subject. Deep content knowledge paired with immersive training in inquiry-based pedagogy is essential. We should reward expert teachers with better pay and time to collaborate, not leave them isolated with outdated materials.

Finally, we must normalize struggle. Too many students, especially high achievers, are taught that if math isn’t effortless, they lack “talent.” A culture that celebrates persistence, welcomes mistakes as part of learning, and praises effort over raw speed can keep students engaged long after the first stumbling block. The goal isn’t to force everyone into a lab coat, but to give every young person the confidence and curiosity to see that science and math are not a secret language for a chosen few—they are the common language of our future.

Teachers are the linchpin. Professional development that models inquiry lessons, trains culturally responsive pedagogy, and pairs educators with industry mentors bridges the gap between textbook content and practice. Programs like the National Science Teachers Association’s lab residencies give teachers stories and kits that re‑energize classrooms, turning confidence into contagious enthusiasm for STEM.

Community and industry partnerships demystify STEM careers. School “career days” that bring engineers, data scientists, and health researchers to work on mini‑projects show students that STEM work is collaborative, socially relevant, and open to all. Robotics leagues and math‑modeling contests provide success markers, countering the notion that “geeks” belong on stage, and give peers and parents reason to celebrate achievement. One way to make STEM more attractive is to stop isolating it from the humanities and the arts. The phrase “math and science for more than just geeks” suggests that the problem is image, and image matters. But the deeper problem is that students are often asked to choose between being technical and being creative, between being analytical and being humane. That division is false.

A student interested in music is already encountering mathematics in rhythm, acoustics and digital production. A student interested in fashion is encountering materials science, geometry and manufacturing. A student interested in history is encountering demography, climate, mapping and statistical evidence. A student interested in film is encountering optics, algorithms, animation and sound engineering. The world does not divide itself into school departments.

Schools should exploit those connections. A unit on geometry might involve architecture and design. A physics course might analyze musical instruments or sports motion. A computer science class might build interactive stories, data visualizations or digital art. Biology might be taught alongside ethics and public policy when students discuss genetics or vaccination. Students who would never join a traditional science club may become deeply engaged when the entry point is something they already care about.

This is not a call to dilute STEM into vague “creativity.” The standards should remain high. In fact, interdisciplinary work can demand more precision, because students must make something that works and explain why it works. A bridge model that collapses, a simulation that gives nonsense results, or a data graphic that misleads cannot be rescued by enthusiasm.

The arts also offer a lesson in practice. No one expects a young violinist to become accomplished by listening to lectures about the violin. Students perform, receive feedback and try again. STEM classes should be more like studios: places where students make conjectures, build models, debug code, design experiments and revise their work. The teacher is not merely a dispenser of answers but a coach in disciplined creativity.

If we want to expand the STEM pipeline, we should widen the door. Many young people are curious, imaginative and ambitious, but they do not identify with the stereotype of the math-science kid. Schools should show them that STEM is not an alternative to creativity. It is one of creativity’s most powerful languages.

The surest way to make STEM attractive is to stop presenting it as a long audition for a narrow club. Too often math and science classes imply that the reward for enduring years of abstraction is, someday, permission to do something interesting. That order should be reversed. Put problems first and formalism second. A ninth grader can ask why a cell phone battery dies, whether a bridge is safe, how a flu virus spreads, why a neighborhood floods, or how Netflix guesses what a viewer might like. A physics class might build and test solar cookers. A biology class might analyze local water quality. A statistics class might examine stop-and-frisk data, public health trends or school lunch nutrition. A computer science class might make simple applications for real users in the community. Students should see that STEM is not merely about gadgets or equations; it is about evidence, design, uncertainty and consequences.

This approach is especially important for students who have been implicitly told that science and engineering are not for them. Girls and minority students do not need a condescending “pink” or “urban” version of STEM. They need rigorous work connected to questions that matter, teachers who expect success, and visible examples of people like them using technical knowledge in medicine, energy, environmental justice, media, agriculture, finance and public service.

Curricula also need to make room for iteration. In many classrooms, a wrong answer is a verdict. In real science and engineering, it is often a clue. Students should test, fail, revise and explain. That requires time, better teacher preparation and assessments that value reasoning over speed.

STEM will become more attractive when it becomes more honest: less a sequence of dull prerequisites, more an invitation to investigate, build and improve the world students already inhabit.

"To increase student interest in STEM, education must shift from rote memorization to active, project-based learning led by well-supported, expert teachers."

Anyone who has sat through a typical American high school chemistry class knows exactly why 90 percent of students walk away uninterested. The class is a forced march through a textbook written by committee, taught by someone who may or may not have a chemistry degree, assessed by multiple-choice tests aligned to a state standard, and divorced from any question a teenager would actually ask. The wonder is not that nine out of ten students are bored. The wonder is that one in ten survives.

If we want more young people drawn to STEM, the single most important change is to let them do science rather than memorize about it. Real scientists ask questions, design experiments, fail, argue, and revise. Real engineers build things that don't work and then figure out why. Almost none of this happens in a typical classroom, where "lab" means following a recipe to confirm a result the teacher already knows.

Project-based learning is not a new idea — John Dewey was making this argument a century ago — but the evidence for it has grown stronger. Schools like High Tech High in San Diego have shown that you can teach rigorous content through extended, open-ended projects and get students who actually care about what they're doing. The recent Next Generation Science Standards are a step in the right direction, emphasizing scientific practices over content recall, but they will be hollow if classrooms don't change.

Two other things matter. One is the teacher. We will not solve this problem until we can recruit and retain people who actually love math and science, which means paying them, respecting them, and giving them time to plan. Finland and Singapore figured this out; we have not.

The other is the early grades. By the time a kid hits high school, attitudes toward math are largely set. Elementary teachers, most of whom were not math majors and many of whom are themselves math-anxious, transmit that anxiety to their students with disturbing reliability. Investing in math content training for K-5 teachers might do more than any high school reform.

The technology will come and go. Khan Academy and MOOCs are interesting, but they are not the answer. The answer is older and harder: better teachers doing better teaching, with the freedom to let kids actually think.

"To make STEM education more attractive and effective, schools must prioritize intellectual rigor and deep problem-solving over superficial engagement and constant assessment."

The question asks how to make STEM more "interesting and attractive," but I want to flip the framing. The goal of math and science education isn't to be attractive. It's to develop the habits of mind that allow people to think clearly about the natural world and quantitative problems. Sometimes those habits are exhilarating to develop. Often they're hard work. Pretending otherwise — promising students that learning calculus will be like playing Angry Birds — sets up disappointments and trivializes the subject.

That doesn't mean classes need to be boring. There's a difference between rigorous and tedious, and most American math and science classes are tedious without being particularly rigorous. The tedium comes from drill divorced from context; the lack of rigor comes from coverage-driven curricula that move too fast to allow real understanding.

Here's a concrete proposal: teach less, deeper. The "mile wide, inch deep" criticism of American math curricula has been around since the TIMSS results of the 1990s. The Common Core standards, whatever their flaws, do try to address this by emphasizing focus and depth in the early grades. Whether implementation lives up to the design is an open question, and the political backlash is intensifying, but the underlying instinct is correct.

A second proposal: take problem-solving seriously as the central activity. The Putnam-style or Olympiad-style approach, where students wrestle with a small number of hard problems over an extended period, is closer to what mathematicians actually do than the worksheet model. Singapore's textbooks, much imitated and rarely understood, do something like this. The point is not to train future olympiad winners; it is that struggling productively with a real problem is the experience that hooks people.

Third: separate the assessment from the learning. American math classes are constantly assessing — quiz Friday, test next week, midterm, final, standardized exam in the spring. Students learn to optimize for short-term recall and forget afterward. If we want them to develop deep interest, we have to give them time to be confused, to play, to try and fail without it counting.

Make math and science demanding, intellectually honest, and given enough time, and a much larger fraction of students will find them attractive. Soften them into edutainment and we lose both the rigor and, eventually, the interest.

  3. 03Frequent high-stakes assessments should be reduced to allow students the time to explore, fail, and develop a genuine understanding of the material.

"To make STEM more attractive and effective, schools must systematically integrate the arts and humanities into science and math curricula to create a cross-disciplinary "STEAM" approach."

We have a false choice baked into our educational culture: you are either a math-science person or an arts-humanities person. This schism is a modern invention, and it does immense damage. Historically, the greatest scientists—from Leonardo da Vinci to Albert Einstein—were deeply engaged with art, music, and philosophy. The division we enforce in schools by eighth grade, where students are sorted into tracks and told that creativity belongs in art class while precision belongs in calculus, drives away countless young people who don’t see their whole selves reflected in a narrow STEM identity.

The solution is not to make science “fun” with superficial gimmicks, but to systematically integrate the arts and humanities into STEM, transforming it into STEAM. This is not about dumbing down content; it’s about teaching that the design of a bridge is as much an aesthetic and ethical act as a calculation of load. Students who love to write can produce scientific journalism about local environmental issues. Those passionate about visual art can learn anatomy through detailed drawing or understand geometry by designing tessellations. The engineering design process—identify a problem, brainstorm, prototype, test, iterate—is essentially a creative act identical in spirit to composing music or choreographing a dance. When we present it that way, we invite a whole new population into the field.

Pilot programs around the country have shown that integrating art into science classes increases engagement, especially among girls and students who previously labeled themselves “not good at math.” It also produces better scientists, because the messy, open-ended problems of the real world demand exactly the kind of flexible, cross-disciplinary thinking that arts training cultivates. Instead of asking how to make STEM more interesting to young people, we should ask why we severed it from the other half of human genius in the first place. The wall must come down.
