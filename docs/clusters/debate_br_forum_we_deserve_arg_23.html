What a Builder Hears in Morozov's Essay

I work as an engineer at a mid-sized company that uses generative AI as a core part of our product. I am not a "frontier lab" person—I don't train foundation models—but I deploy them, evaluate them, write the scaffolding around them, and spend a lot of time thinking about how they fit into actual workflows. I read Morozov's essay carefully and want to offer a builder's perspective, which is different from both the philosopher's and the activist's.

The first thing I want to say is that much of Morozov's essay describes my experience accurately. He writes about discovering a feature in one AI service and wondering whether he could combine it with others; about ChatGPT functioning as "an experimental workshop where the eolithic flâneur takes his discovery to see what it's really good for"; about building something useful in an afternoon that would have taken weeks before. This is what daily work with these tools is like. Anyone who has spent serious time with GPT-4 or Claude knows the particular pleasure of the unexpected connection, the rapid prototype, the "huh, that worked" moment. Morozov is right that this is a genuinely different mode of work than the rigid, rules-based interaction with classical software. He has named something real.

The second thing I want to say is that the political-economic constraints he identifies are also real, and they shape my work in ways that are easier to feel than to articulate. The fact that I am paying OpenAI per token does change what I build. There are queries I don't run because they cost too much. There are experiments I don't try because the inference budget would be unjustifiable. There are features I don't ship because I cannot guarantee they won't hallucinate in costly ways. The "subscription model" is not an incidental feature of the technology; it shapes the design space of what gets built. A non-commercial alternative—local models running on my laptop, or shared community infrastructure—would produce different products. I have built small things with Llama and Mistral models on my own hardware, and the experience is genuinely different. Cheaper experimentation, more willingness to throw things away, more eolithic in Morozov's sense.

But here is where my builder's perspective starts to diverge from Morozov's framing. The reason these local models work for some of my projects is that the underlying open-weights ecosystem is itself a product of corporate decision-making (Meta releasing Llama, Mistral being VC-funded), not of any "public" or "solidarity-based" alternative. There is genuine open-source AI work happening, but the compute economics mean that the frontier of capability is set by a small number of well-funded labs. Even the most committed open-source contributors are using infrastructure—pre-trained weights, evaluation benchmarks, datasets—that came out of corporate or quasi-corporate efforts. This is not a moral indictment; it is a structural fact. A "public AI" that wants to be at the frontier of capability would need budgets and talent comparable to those at OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google. That is a political project of enormous scale, and I do not see it coming from the kind of philosophical reorientation Morozov proposes.

The third thing is about what these systems actually are, technically. Morozov writes that LLMs "resemble the flexibility of the market more than the rigidity of bureaucracy" and that "their outputs are driven by changing data, not fixed protocols." This is approximately right but obscures something important. A trained model is, once trained, a fixed artifact. Inference from GPT-4 in December 2024 produces output from the same weights it produced in October. What changes is the prompt, the context, the scaffolding around the model. The "market-like" flexibility Morozov describes is really the flexibility of the prompting layer, not of the model itself. This matters because the prompting layer is precisely where the eolithic experimentation happens—and where, in principle, alternative institutional arrangements could intervene. Even leaving the foundation models in corporate hands, there is enormous room for non-commercial, public-interest, or community-controlled work at the prompting and tooling layer. This is happening, in scattered ways, through projects like Hugging Face's open ecosystem, through academic NLP groups, through civic-tech communities. It is not glamorous, but it is real.

The fourth thing I want to say is that Morozov's eolithic ideal, while phenomenologically accurate to a part of AI-assisted work, misses a part that matters more for outcomes. A lot of what makes generative AI actually useful, in production settings, is teleological in exactly the way Morozov criticizes. We define an evaluation set, we measure performance against it, we iterate on prompts and scaffolding until performance improves. This is means-ends analysis on a model. Without it, the "experimentation" Morozov describes produces a lot of fun demos and few reliable tools. The genuine engineering work of making AI useful is, currently, much closer to Newell and Simon than to Storm's flâneur. I do not think this is a problem; I think it is what useful engineering has always looked like.

What follows? I think the right way to read Morozov, from a builder's perspective, is as proposing a corrective rather than a replacement. The dominant culture of AI development is over-indexed on benchmarks, on user-engagement metrics, on shipping features quickly to capture market share. It is under-indexed on the kind of open-ended exploration that produces genuinely new capabilities and on the kind of public-interest deliberation that decides what to build in the first place. Building in more eolithic time and space—both at the individual level and at the institutional level—would probably make the technology better, in both technical and social senses. Public-option AI infrastructure would expand the design space. Better tools for non-commercial experimentation would democratize who gets to build.

But the framing of "instrumental versus ecological reason" feels, from the inside of the work, less like a fundamental dichotomy and more like a choice of emphasis within a single activity that necessarily involves both. The hard questions are not philosophical but practical: how do we fund public compute? how do we structure access? how do we evaluate models for public-interest applications? how do we build the technical talent pipeline outside the FAANG salary regime? These are the questions builders care about, and I would love to read Morozov's next essay engaging them in detail.

In the meantime, I will say that his essay has changed how I think about my own work in at least one specific way. I have started, in the last few weeks, to spend an hour or two each week building things with no defined goal—just exploring what the models can do, with no product roadmap in mind. It is the most productive hour of my week, by every metric I care about. Whether that vindicates Morozov's eolithism or just describes good engineering practice, I leave to readers to decide.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The flexible, experimental mode of work Morozov describes is a genuine experience for builders, but it is currently enabled by a corporate-funded ecosystem rather than public alternatives.
  2. 02Creating a 'public AI' at the frontier of capability is a political and economic project of such enormous scale that it cannot be achieved through philosophical reorientation alone.
  3. 03The flexibility Morozov attributes to the models themselves actually resides in the prompting and scaffolding layers, which offers a more feasible space for non-commercial and community-controlled intervention.
  4. 04The 'teleological' or goal-oriented approach Morozov criticizes is actually essential for transforming interesting AI demos into reliable, useful engineering tools.
  5. 05The tension between instrumental and ecological reason is not a fundamental dichotomy but rather a choice of emphasis within the unified practice of building technology.
  6. 06The most urgent task for democratizing AI is addressing practical hurdles like public compute funding and technical talent pipelines rather than focusing on abstract philosophical shifts.
