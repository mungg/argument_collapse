Diversified Gemini

"While Robert Chapman’s vision for 'Neurodivergent Power' is ambitious, dismissing 'Liberal Neurodiversity' is a mistake because rights-based, reformist frameworks provide the only practical and effective tools for protecting the dignity and livelihoods of neurodivergent individuals."

Robert Chapman’s critique of what they term “Liberal Neurodiversity” offers a provocative and sweeping re-evaluation of the movement’s trajectory. By framing the hard-won victories of the past three decades as mere “elite capture” that ultimately reinforces a harmful capitalist status quo, Chapman attempts to pivot the neurodiversity movement toward a radical, anti-capitalist, and anti-imperialist project of “Neurodivergent Power.” While the ambition of this vision is certainly compelling, Chapman’s dismissal of rights-based, reformist achievements overlooks the tangible, life-saving impacts these legal and cultural shifts have had on millions of neurodivergent individuals. In their eagerness to tear down the master’s house, Chapman risks discarding the only tools that have successfully provided shelter for those living on the neurological margins.

To understand the stakes of this debate, we must first look at what “Liberal Neurodiversity” has actually achieved. The shift from a purely medical model—which viewed autism, ADHD, dyslexia, and other conditions strictly as deficits to be cured—to a social model emphasizing accommodation and acceptance is arguably one of the most profound cultural transformations of our time. Chapman acknowledges this shift but characterizes it as fundamentally limited, arguing that it primarily benefits the privileged and functions to extract profit from a select few.

However, this characterization deeply undervalues the protective power of civil rights frameworks like the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) in the United States, or the Equality Act in the UK. These legal instruments are not mere abstract ideals; they are the mechanisms by which a neurodivergent person keeps their job, an autistic child receives an individualized education plan (IEP) rather than being segregated, and a person with severe sensory processing issues can access public spaces. When Burger King was forced to pay damages for denying an autistic applicant a job coach, as Chapman notes, this was not just a symbol of “neoliberal” compromise. It was a concrete assertion of human dignity backed by the power of the state.

Chapman argues that these rights are often unevenly distributed, disproportionately leaving behind multiply marginalized individuals—those who are racialized, living in poverty, or caught in the carceral system. This is an undeniable and tragic reality. The school-to-prison pipeline for neurodivergent youth of color is a crisis that demands urgent, systemic intervention. But the failure to fully realize the promise of disability rights does not mean the framework itself is flawed; rather, it indicates that our work in enforcing and expanding these rights is incomplete. The solution to unequal access to legal protection is not to abandon the legal framework in favor of a nebulous Marxist revolution, but to strengthen enforcement, expand legal aid, and tackle the intersecting racial and economic disparities that prevent equitable access to justice.

Furthermore, Chapman is highly critical of the “superpower” narrative and the increasing corporate embrace of neurodiversity. It is true that framing neurodivergence purely in terms of its utility to capitalist production—such as tech companies actively recruiting autistic coders for their attention to detail—can be dehumanizing. It creates a hierarchy of value where only “productive” neurodivergent people are welcomed. Yet, we must also recognize the psychological and cultural necessity of this shift. For generations, neurodivergent people were told they were broken, disordered, and useless. The emergence of a narrative that highlights strengths, creativity, and unique problem-solving abilities has been a vital corrective. It has fostered the very "Neurodivergent Pride" that Chapman acknowledges as beneficial. To wholesale reject the concept that neurodivergent minds offer unique, competitive advantages in the workplace is to risk regressing to a narrative where neurodivergence is once again viewed solely through the lens of impairment and burden.

Chapman’s proposed alternative, “Neurodivergent Power,” seeks to ally neurodivergent liberation with global anti-capitalist, anti-imperialist, and abolitionist movements. Drawing explicit parallels to the Black Power movement and the Black Panther Party, Chapman advocates for mass organization, unionizing against “neuronormativity,” and fundamentally changing economic relations.

There are two major problems with this approach. First, it assumes a unified political consciousness among neurodivergent people that simply does not exist. The neurodivergent population is incredibly diverse, encompassing individuals across the entire political spectrum. Tying the advocacy for neurodivergent accommodations to a specific, radical left-wing political agenda risks alienating the vast majority of neurodivergent people and their families who do not identify as revolutionary socialists. A parent fighting for their dyslexic child’s right to extra time on a test should not have to subscribe to anti-imperialist theory to find a home in the neurodiversity movement.

Second, the structural revolution Chapman calls for is a distant, utopian goal. While we can and should debate the merits of our economic system, the immediate, pressing needs of neurodivergent people cannot wait for the collapse of global capitalism. When someone is experiencing an autistic burnout, facing eviction because they cannot manage their executive functioning well enough to pay bills on time, or languishing in a psychiatric facility, they need robust social safety nets, accessible healthcare, and legal protections *now*. They need the very “liberal reforms” that Chapman finds insufficient.

Chapman suggests that neurodivergent people are inherently oppressed by capitalism because the system demands a specific, rigid type of productivity. Yet, it is precisely within market economies that we have seen the greatest leaps in assistive technologies, specialized educational resources, and the proliferation of flexible, remote work arrangements that benefit many neurodivergent individuals. The transition to a post-capitalist society, historically and practically, does not guarantee a sudden, enlightened embrace of neurological diversity. In many historical instances, centrally planned economies have been ruthlessly standardizing and deeply intolerant of individual difference.

The future of neurodiversity must indeed be intersectional and inclusive of those who have been marginalized by current systems. But it must also remain grounded in pragmatic, achievable reality. We can push corporations to be genuinely inclusive without demanding they cease to seek profit. We can advocate for the abolition of the school-to-prison pipeline while still utilizing the courts to enforce the ADA. “Liberal Neurodiversity” is not a dead end; it is the vital, ongoing project of building a society that bends toward inclusion, one reasonable accommodation, one policy change, and one shifted attitude at a time. To discard this in favor of a revolutionary gamble is to abandon the most effective tools we have for changing neurodivergent lives for the better.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Civil rights frameworks like the ADA provide concrete, state-backed protections for employment, education, and public access that are life-saving rather than merely abstract or neoliberal compromises.
  2. 02The failure of current laws to protect multiply marginalized neurodivergent people is a reason to strengthen and expand legal enforcement rather than a reason to abandon the rights-based model entirely.
  3. 03The 'superpower' narrative and corporate inclusion efforts serve as a vital psychological corrective to generations of pathologization, fostering necessary pride and self-worth.
  4. 04Tying neurodiversity advocacy to a radical anti-capitalist agenda risks alienating the diverse majority of neurodivergent people who do not share a unified revolutionary consciousness.
  5. 05The immediate, pressing needs of neurodivergent people—such as healthcare and housing—require robust social safety nets and pragmatic reforms that cannot wait for a distant utopian revolution.
  6. 06Market economies have historically driven the development of assistive technologies and flexible work arrangements that benefit neurodivergent people more effectively than centrally planned systems.
