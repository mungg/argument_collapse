"The trial of Dzhokhar Tsarnaev should be moved outside of Massachusetts because the local community's shared trauma and specific legal culture regarding the death penalty compromise the jury's ability to make an impartial moral judgment during the sentencing phase."

The hardest part of the Tsarnaev venue question is not publicity alone. It is publicity joined to the death penalty. A capital trial asks jurors to do more than decide historical facts. It asks them to make a moral judgment about whether the defendant should live or die. That judgment is precisely where local trauma may matter most.

Massachusetts has rejected the death penalty as a matter of state law, but this is a federal prosecution, so capital punishment is available. That creates an unusual tension. The jury will be drawn from a community that has no state death penalty, yet was also the site of a spectacular attack that killed and maimed civilians. Some jurors may oppose execution in ordinary cases but feel that this case is different because it was “our” Marathon, “our” city, “our” dead. Others may resent the federal government’s decision to seek death in a state that has chosen otherwise. Both reactions are deeply local.

Courts usually ask whether jurors can be impartial about guilt. In a capital case, they must ask with equal seriousness whether jurors can be impartial about punishment. Can they consider mitigation? Can they distinguish the younger brother’s culpability from that of the older brother, if the evidence warrants it? Can they resist the idea that a death sentence is necessary to vindicate Boston? These questions are not abstract. They go to the heart of the sentencing phase.

A change of venue would not guarantee a better jury, and no American jury pool will be ignorant of the bombing. But moving the trial outside Massachusetts would reduce the pressure on jurors to speak as representatives of an injured community. It would also make any death verdict, if one is returned, less vulnerable to the charge that it was produced by local grief rather than legal judgment.

Capital punishment requires special caution. If the government seeks the ultimate penalty, it should accept the inconvenience of trying the case where the moral atmosphere is less saturated by the crime.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A capital trial requires jurors to make a moral judgment on life or death, a process uniquely vulnerable to the influence of local grief and trauma.
  2. 02The tension between Massachusetts' state-level rejection of the death penalty and the federal government's pursuit of it creates local biases that could unfairly sway jurors in either direction.
  3. 03Moving the trial would alleviate the social pressure on jurors to act as representatives of an injured community rather than impartial arbiters of justice.
  4. 04A change of venue would protect the legitimacy of a potential death verdict by ensuring it is based on legal judgment rather than local emotional saturation.
