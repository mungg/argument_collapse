"While amnesty for long-settled migrants is a necessary step toward justice, it is a minimalist moral half-measure that fails to challenge the fundamentally unjust premise that states have a right to restrict human movement."

In his compelling essay, Joseph Carens mounts a powerful case that long-settled irregular migrants acquire a moral right to stay. His core insight—that social membership is not something the state grants but something human beings build simply by living—is one that should reshape how we think about immigration. Yet for all its force, Carens’s argument remains trapped within a framework I believe is fundamentally unjust: the assumption that the state has an unqualified right to control its borders in the first place. By accepting that premise, Carens ends up offering a minimalist argument that is both too generous to the state and too stingy with human freedom. A genuinely liberal approach would go further: it would recognize that freedom of international movement is a basic human right, and that amnesty is merely a remedial step on the way to open borders.

Carens explicitly sets aside the deeper question of whether states should have the power to restrict entry at all. He says, “Let us accept that conventional view about states and borders as a premise.” But why should we accept it? The conventional view is hardly self-evident, and its acceptance has produced a world of profound inequality and suffering. People born in wealthy, stable countries enjoy, through no merit of their own, the freedom to move, work, and thrive almost anywhere on earth, while those born in poverty face walls of exclusion backed by force. This is a version of feudal birthright privilege, and it sits uneasily with liberal commitments to equal moral worth and opportunity.

Carens’s own examples underscore the point. Miguel Sanchez had to make his way across the desert to find work that allowed him to support his family. He was denied a visa not because he was dangerous or unneeded—indeed, his labor was eagerly sought—but because immigration quotas were designed to exclude people like him. The state’s decision to exclude him was not a morally neutral act of self-governance; it was a coercive restriction that severely harmed his interests for the benefit of insiders. Once we see that, the question shifts from “When should we grant amnesty?” to “What justifies the initial exclusion?”

Carens’s answer relies on a kind of quietism: he accepts the conventional view because he thinks it is too politically unrealistic to challenge, and he focuses on the minimal moral constraints that even conventional thinking must respect. But this approach has a paradoxical effect. By insisting that the state’s right to control entry is legitimate, he implicitly endorses the very legal categories that make irregular migrants’ lives so precarious. His amnesty proposal, even if adopted, would leave future irregular migrants in the same position: they, too, would have to endure years of vulnerability before gaining a secure status. The underlying injustice of border controls remains untouched.

The complicity argument Carens discusses provides a partial corrective, but it, too, is too narrow. He is reluctant to infer state complicity from the mere presence of irregular migrants because structural factors may be at work. But in a global economy shaped by centuries of colonial exploitation, unequal trade treaties, and state-backed corporate power, the conditions that drive migration are far from natural. Rich states actively create and maintain the poverty that pushes people to move, then build walls to keep them out. This is not a failure of enforcement; it is a system of global apartheid. To then say that those who manage to breach the walls “deserve” amnesty only after a period of settlement, while those who wait outside are left with no legal recourse, is a moral half-measure that dignifies the apartheid itself.

Of course, I recognize that open borders are a distant goal. But political feasibility should not limit our moral vision. Even if we cannot abolish border controls tomorrow, we can refuse to legitimate them. My critique is not that Carens’s amnesty proposal is too radical but that it is too conservative—it treats the cage as a given and only asks that the prisoners be kept in somewhat more humane conditions. A more radical view would hold that every person has a right to move and settle anywhere on the planet, and that the burden of proof lies entirely on those who would restrict that right. From that perspective, the “irregular migrant” is not a lawbreaker but a rights-bearer asserting a fundamental liberty. The state’s attempt to deport her is the real violation.

I do not oppose amnesty; I enthusiastically support it. But I support it as a first step, not as a permanent settlement. Granting legal status to the millions already here is a matter of urgent justice. Yet we should not sanctify the border regime that made them irregular in the first place. The deep lesson of Carens’s essay is that social ties trump legal categories. That lesson should push us beyond amnesty to the recognition that all persons, wherever born, are members of the human community, entitled to live and work wherever they can flourish. Until that day, let us have amnesty—and let us also have the honesty to say that what we are really doing is repairing the damage caused by unjust laws.
