"A marriage is over when it involves non-negotiable abuse or when a partner's persistent unwillingness to repair the relationship forces the other person to compromise their fundamental self."

There is no universal breaking point in a marriage, and anyone who claims otherwise is selling a fantasy of moral clarity that real relationships rarely provide. But there are warning signs serious enough that I would call them non-negotiable: physical violence, sexual coercion, sustained emotional abuse, and the kind of compulsive deception (around money, infidelity, addiction) that makes partnership structurally impossible. In these cases, the question isn't whether to leave but how to leave safely.

Beyond those bright lines, the harder cases require something the "stay or go" framing tends to obscure: an honest accounting of whether both people are still trying. John Gottman's research on what he calls the Four Horsemen — criticism, contempt, defensiveness, stonewalling — is useful here precisely because it doesn't measure conflict (all marriages have conflict) but measures the quality of repair. A marriage in which both partners still attempt to repair after rupture is salvageable, even when it feels unbearable. A marriage in which contempt has become the default register is, in most cases, already over; the legal dissolution is just paperwork catching up.

The Alain de Botton essay that prompted this debate is right that we all marry the wrong person, in the sense that no spouse can fulfill the fantasy we project onto them. But that insight cuts two ways. Yes, it should make us patient with ordinary disappointment. It should also make us suspicious of the opposite move — using "everyone is flawed" as a reason to tolerate genuine cruelty or chronic neglect.

The honest answer to "when is it time?" is: when staying requires you to become a smaller, more frightened, or more dishonest version of yourself, and when your partner is unwilling to engage with that fact. That isn't a single moment. It's usually a long, sad recognition that arrives in pieces.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Physical violence, sexual coercion, emotional abuse, and compulsive deception are non-negotiable breaking points that make a partnership structurally impossible.
  2. 02The viability of a marriage depends more on the quality of repair after conflict than on the presence of conflict itself.
  3. 03A relationship is effectively over when contempt becomes the default mode of communication and one partner is no longer willing to engage in the work of improvement.
  4. 04While ordinary disappointment is a natural part of marriage, the inherent flaws of spouses should never be used to justify staying in a relationship characterized by chronic neglect or cruelty.
  5. 05The definitive time to leave is when staying requires you to become a diminished, frightened, or dishonest version of yourself.
