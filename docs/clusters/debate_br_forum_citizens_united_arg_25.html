The Pragmatic Path: Small Donors, Big Change

Lawrence Lessig’s essay is an intellectually bracing call to arms, but I suspect it will leave many readers with a familiar sense of despair. If the Supreme Court is in the grip of First Amendment Lochnerism, and if the only cure is to overturn Citizens United by constitutional amendment or judicial epiphany, what are we to do in the meantime? Lessig gestures toward voluntary small-donor matching and democracy vouchers as “less-direct” ways to address dependency corruption. I want to take that gesture seriously and argue that, properly understood, the small-donor revolution is not a second-best compromise but the most promising strategy available — one that can change the political calculus without waiting for the Court to come to its senses.

The core insight of Lessig’s “dependency corruption” is that representatives become distracted from their proper constituency because they must spend extraordinary amounts of time and attention chasing large checks from a tiny, unrepresentative slice of the population. The behavioral and cognitive forces he describes — the unconscious tendency to reward those upon whom we depend — are real. But these forces are not activated solely by the amount of money raised; they are activated by the act of fundraising and the social networks it embeds. A candidate who raises her campaign budget entirely from small contributions raised in face-to-face meetings with constituents is not only financially independent of wealthy donors; she is also psychologically and socially anchored in her district. Her dependency, in a meaningful sense, has been transferred back to the voters.

This is why the Fair Elections Now Act, which provides a multiple match for small donations, deserves much more emphasis than Lessig gives it. Under that proposal, a candidate who agrees to limit donations to $100 per person receives a public match, turning each $100 into $500 or more. The system makes it possible to win elections without a single large check. Just as important, it changes the daily rhythm of a campaign’s life: instead of traveling to Wall Street or K Street, candidates spend their time at town halls, community centers, and neighborhood gatherings, building the very relationships that sustain genuine representation. Early evidence from states like Connecticut and Arizona, which have adopted similar “clean elections” systems, suggests that participating legislators do indeed spend less time fundraising and more time on legislative work and constituent service.

Skeptics will rightly point out that voluntary public financing does nothing to stop the flood of independent expenditures that Citizens United has unleashed. A small-donor candidate might still face a barrage of attack ads funded by a corporate-backed Super PAC. But here, the political dynamics are more complex than a simple arms race. Voters are not blank slates; they can distinguish between a candidate who has built a grassroots funding base and one who is propped up by anonymous corporate money. When a candidate can say, “I haven’t taken a dime from corporate PACs, and my average donation is $40 from people in this district,” that narrative becomes a powerful shield. It does not neutralize the independent spending, but it reframes the election as a contest between the people and the special interests. And in a time of deep public cynicism, that framing can be electorally decisive.

Moreover, a successful small-donor system creates its own virtuous cycle. The more candidates who opt into such a system and win, the stronger the political constituency for expanding it. A critical mass of public-financed lawmakers — especially those who owe their seats to the program — will be motivated to protect and increase its funding. Over time, this can shift norms within the institution, making it embarrassing rather than normal to rely on large corporate and out-of-district contributions. That sort of norm change may ultimately do more to reduce dependency corruption than any judicial ruling.

Lessig is right that we need a better lens. The small-donor movement provides that lens in a practical, politically feasible form. It does not require a constitutional amendment or a reversal of Citizens United. It requires only that Congress pass a law — and that candidates have the courage to use it. That, I believe, is the most realistic and transformative path out of the corruption trap.
