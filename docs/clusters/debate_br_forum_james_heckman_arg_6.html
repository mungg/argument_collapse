"While early childhood intervention is a valuable tool for social mobility, public policy must prioritize strengthening civil society and the family unit itself rather than expanding bureaucratic state control over parenting."

James Heckman deserves credit for saying something that many social scientists and politicians prefer to avoid: family life matters. Not just income, not just school funding, not just discrimination, not just macroeconomic trends, but the daily habits, attachments, disciplines, and expectations of the home. Children do not arrive at school as blank slates. By the time the state begins formal education, many of the most important differences among them have already taken root.

For conservatives, there is much to welcome in Heckman’s essay. He rejects the fantasy that the solution to inequality is simply to write larger checks. He treats character as real. He recognizes that self-control, perseverance, reliability, and the ability to cooperate are not bourgeois decorations but foundations of adult freedom. He is also refreshingly skeptical of the GED illusion, the idea that a credential can substitute for the habits formed by actually completing high school. In a society addicted to paper qualifications, that is an important warning.

But Heckman’s argument also creates a temptation: if families are failing, let government move earlier and more deeply into the family. That temptation should be resisted. Early childhood intervention may be necessary in some cases, and programs such as Nurse-Family Partnership have a plausible record precisely because they work with mothers in practical ways. But the more ambitious dream of a large public apparatus to improve parenting should make us uneasy. The family is not merely a delivery system for human capital. It is an institution with its own dignity, authority, and moral purposes. When the state begins to define “quality parenting,” the risks are not theoretical.

One risk is bureaucratic arrogance. The kinds of families most likely to be targeted—poor, unmarried, minority, immigrant, religiously unconventional—are also least able to resist official judgment. A program that begins as assistance can become inspection. A home visitor may be a nurse and ally; she may also be perceived as a representative of child protective services, the school district, or the welfare office. Trust is fragile. We should not assume that because an intervention is labeled “evidence-based,” it will be experienced as respectful.

A second risk is scaling. Heckman’s strongest evidence comes from small, intensive programs. Perry Preschool and Abecedarian were not ordinary government services delivered by a sprawling bureaucracy under fiscal pressure. They were carefully designed demonstrations. It is one thing to produce impressive results with a small group of children, high-quality staff, and close supervision. It is another to reproduce those results across fifty states, thousands of providers, union rules, political mandates, and uneven local capacity. Head Start itself is a cautionary example: beloved, sometimes helpful, but hardly a consistent engine of transformation. The federal government is much better at expanding eligibility than ensuring excellence.

A third risk is that early childhood policy will become a way to avoid the more difficult conversation about marriage and family formation. Heckman notes the rise in never-married motherhood and the diverging destinies of children born to more and less educated women. But he does not dwell on what follows. The collapse of stable two-parent family life among the less educated is one of the central social facts of our time. It affects not only income but supervision, authority, kin networks, male role models, and the emotional security of children. To say this is not to denounce single mothers, many of whom perform heroically. It is to recognize that heroism is not a social policy.

If we care about mobility, we must rebuild norms and institutions that support marriage before childbearing. This cannot be done by government alone, and it certainly cannot be done by lecturing the poor while elites quietly practice the bourgeois virtues they publicly refuse to defend. The educated classes marry later, have children within marriage, read to them, monitor their schools, and transmit discipline. Then they describe family structure as a private lifestyle choice. That hypocrisy harms the poor most of all.

Public policy can help at the margins. Welfare programs should not penalize marriage. The tax code should reduce marriage penalties for low-income couples. Child support enforcement should be fair but not so punitive that it drives marginal fathers into the underground economy. Criminal justice policy should distinguish between dangerous offenders and men who could remain connected to work and family. Schools and community organizations should involve fathers, not treat them as optional visitors. But the deeper work belongs to civil society: churches, neighborhood associations, mentoring groups, extended families, and local charities that can speak morally without becoming bureaucratic.

Heckman’s emphasis on non-cognitive skills also points toward institutions outside the state. Character is formed through repeated practice in settings of authority and affection. Families do this best, but when families struggle, the next best supports are often local and relational: a coach, a pastor, a scout leader, an employer, an uncle, a demanding teacher. We should be wary of professionalizing every form of adult guidance. A society that pays specialists to teach perseverance while its ordinary institutions dissolve will not get very far.

That said, conservatives should not use these cautions as an excuse for inaction. If the evidence shows that targeted home visiting can reduce abuse, improve maternal health, and help children develop, we should support it. If high-quality preschool for the most disadvantaged children reduces later crime and dependency, it is a legitimate public investment. Preventing social breakdown is a proper function of government, and it is cheaper and more humane than prisons.

But the design matters. Programs should be targeted rather than universal, locally administered where possible, open to faith-based and community providers, and rigorously evaluated. Funding should follow children and families, not bureaucracies. Parents should have choices among providers. Programs that fail should be closed. We should not create a new entitlement to mediocre care and call it equality.

Heckman’s essay is most valuable when it reminds us that opportunity is formed in the nursery, the kitchen, the church basement, the playground, and the preschool classroom long before it is measured by an admissions office or an employer. The conservative response should be: yes, start early—but start by strengthening the institutions that make early life human. Government can assist. It cannot love a child, marry a mother, discipline a son, or substitute for a father. A mobility agenda that forgets that will spend more and more while repairing less and less.
