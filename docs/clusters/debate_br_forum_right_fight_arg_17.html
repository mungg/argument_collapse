"Daniel Richman’s proposal to integrate local police into a national intelligence network is dangerously naive because it ignores the reality that such cooperation destroys community trust and targets immigrant populations as a suspect class."

I am a community organizer in a large urban county that is home to one of the most concentrated Arab-American populations in the country. Since September 11, my work has consisted largely of helping families navigate the aftermath of arbitrary detentions, special registration call-ins, and FBI interviews. I have watched as the trust between immigrant communities and law enforcement, painstakingly built over a decade, has been systematically dismantled—not entirely by malice, but by the logic of the domestic intelligence network that Daniel Richman’s essay seeks to civilize. From where I sit, his proposal seems both dangerously naive and deeply disheartening.

Richman argues that local police, precisely because they need the trust of immigrant communities to fight street crime, will temper federal counterterrorism demands and serve as a “buffer.” The argument sounds reasonable. The problem is that it bears little resemblance to the lived experience of people in our neighborhoods. The beat cop may be a familiar face, but he is also an agent of a city government that, when push comes to shove, aligns itself with Washington. When the FBI came with the list of “absconders” to be interviewed, our local police didn’t push back; they picked up the phone and helped coordinate the visits. When a man who had cooperated with police on a robbery investigation was later picked up in an ICE raid and deported, the local liaison officer shrugged and said, “That’s federal, we can’t control it.” That answer may be legally accurate, but it tells the community that the police are either powerless or complicit—and in either case, they are not to be trusted.

The chilling effect Richman acknowledges in passing is not a minor side-effect; it is the core dynamic. We have documented case after case in which victims of domestic violence, witnesses to assaults, and people with information about drug dealers refused to contact the police because they feared—reasonably—that any interaction would lead to questions about immigration status or be entered into some intelligence database. I am not talking about actual terrorists; I am talking about mothers, shopkeepers, students. When we tell them that the local police are now part of a “national intelligence network,” they hear what Richman apparently does not: that the state has declared them a suspect class. The result is less safety, not more.

Richman’s response is that we need to “give local police more of a voice” and let them negotiate the terms of cooperation. But who exactly is this “voice” representing? In most big cities, immigrant communities are politically disenfranchised. Many of our neighbors are not citizens; they cannot vote. The local community organizations that could represent their interests are often stretched thin and lack access to the corridors of power. Meanwhile, the mayor and the police chief are under enormous pressure, after the 2004 election, to appear “tough on terror.” That pressure does not translate into defending immigrant rights; it translates into photo-ops with the FBI director and press releases about the number of terrorism investigations opened. The political economy that Richman describes—federal carrots and sticks—works to suppress local resistance, not to encourage it.

I do not dispute that some police chiefs, and some officers, mean well. There are individuals who have stood up to unreasonable federal requests, like the city attorney in Portland. But institutional goodwill is a reed to lean on. What we need are hard legal walls that define what local police can and cannot do with immigration status and political intelligence. The “firewall” must be mandatory, not negotiable. Local police should be required by law to refrain from inquiring into immigration status during routine contacts, and they should be prohibited from sharing personal information with federal intelligence agencies except where there is a specific, documented criminal predicate and a judicial warrant. The sanctuary-city ordinances that some places have enacted embody this principle, but they are under assault. Congressmen are proposing to strip funding from cities that refuse to cooperate. So the “voice” that Richman wants to amplify is being threatened with a gag order.

The tragedy of Richman’s essay is that it mistakes a structural problem for a managerial one. He thinks that if we just get the incentives right—if we let the locals have a say—the intelligence network will become acceptable. But no amount of local consultation can legitimize a system whose very premise is that millions of people are, because of their national origin or religion, subjects of suspicion and surveillance. The right response to this premise is not to make the spying more polite; it is to reject it. We should be demanding that counterterrorism investigations be conducted like any other criminal probe—on the basis of evidence, not ethnicity, and with all the protections of due process. That is the fight worth having, and Richman’s vision of a federated spying apparatus, however elegantly defended, is an obstacle to it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'chilling effect' of police-federal cooperation is a core dynamic that prevents immigrants from reporting crimes, thereby decreasing overall public safety.
  2. 02Local police lack the institutional power or political will to act as a 'buffer' against federal overreach, often becoming complicit in federal enforcement actions like deportations.
  3. 03The political disenfranchisement of immigrant communities means that the 'local voice' Richman champions represents the interests of city officials rather than the vulnerable populations being surveilled.
  4. 04Instead of flexible managerial cooperation, the law must establish hard 'firewalls' that strictly prohibit local police from inquiring into immigration status or sharing data without a judicial warrant.
  5. 05A domestic intelligence system based on national origin or religion is fundamentally illegitimate and cannot be redeemed through local consultation or better incentives.
