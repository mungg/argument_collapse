"The disproportionate influence of the affluent on public policy is not a threat to democracy but rather a beneficial feature of a representative system that filters out the economically biased or uninformed preferences of the general public."

Martin Gilens’s essay operates on a foundational, unquestioned premise: that the ideal democracy is one in which government policy perfectly mirrors the public opinion polls of the day, regardless of the income, education, or expertise of the respondents. By demonstrating that policy outcomes correlate more closely with the preferences of the affluent than with those of the poor, Gilens concludes that "democracy itself is threatened." But before we despair over the state of the American republic, we ought to critically examine whether perfectly equal responsiveness to public opinion is actually a desirable goal.

The Founders of the American republic certainly did not think so. They deliberately designed a representative system—complete with a bicameral legislature, an independent judiciary, and an Electoral College—specifically to filter and refine the public will, preventing the immediate passions of the majority from dictating national policy. They feared populism as much as they feared monarchy.

When we look at the specific issues where the preferences of the affluent diverge from those of the poor, the "representational inequality" Gilens decries often looks less like a subversion of democracy and more like a necessary buffer against economic illiteracy. Gilens points out that over the past few decades, American trade policy has become less protectionist, aligning with the views of the affluent but opposing those of the poor, who broadly opposed free trade agreements like NAFTA.

Should we mourn the fact that the poor did not get the protectionism they wanted? As economists across the political spectrum will attest, free trade creates aggregate economic growth, lowers prices for consumer goods (which disproportionately benefits the poor), and opens global markets to American exports. Protectionism, while intuitively appealing to workers anxious about manufacturing competition, inevitably leads to trade wars, higher inflation, and economic stagnation.

In his 2007 book *The Myth of the Rational Voter*, economist Bryan Caplan demonstrated that the general public holds systematically biased views on economics, largely because they lack formal economic education. The public consistently underestimates the benefits of markets, overestimates the dangers of foreign trade, and fundamentally misunderstands how job creation works. However, Caplan’s data also shows that as voters become more educated—and education is inextricably linked with income—their views begin to align much more closely with the consensus of economic experts.

The affluent in Gilens’s study are not merely a class of wealthy plutocrats acting entirely out of greedy self-interest. They are, by and large, the most highly educated segment of the American populace. When they support looser corporate regulations or free trade, they are often supporting policies that mainstream economic science indicates will grow the national economy. If Congress were to become perfectly responsive to the poorest Americans, as Gilens seems to wish, we would likely see a resurgence of disastrous economic policies: strict price controls, punishing tariffs, and regulatory strangulation.

Furthermore, Gilens admits that on moral and religious issues, the affluent are actually more socially liberal than the poor, favoring abortion access and opposing school prayer. If we implemented Gilens's "greater representational equality," the result would be a more socially conservative, economically protectionist America. It is highly doubtful that the progressive advocates who champion Gilens’s work would actually prefer the policy outcomes generated by the median American voter.

Representative government requires lawmakers to exercise their own judgment, weighing evidence and long-term consequences, rather than acting as unthinking seismographs for public polling. The fact that policy more often aligns with the demographic that possesses higher levels of education and civic engagement is not a sign that our system is fundamentally broken. It is a sign that the Madisonian filter is working exactly as intended.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The American Founders intentionally designed a representative system to refine the public will and prevent the immediate passions of the majority from dictating national policy.
  2. 02The policy preferences of the affluent often align with expert economic consensus, such as the benefits of free trade, whereas the preferences of the poor are frequently based on economic illiteracy.
  3. 03Higher income is closely linked to higher education, meaning the affluent are more likely to support policies that promote aggregate economic growth rather than populist but damaging measures.
  4. 04Perfectly equal representation would lead to socially conservative and economically protectionist outcomes that even the proponents of Gilens's study would likely find undesirable.
  5. 05Lawmakers should act as independent judges of the long-term national interest rather than as passive mirrors of public opinion polls.
