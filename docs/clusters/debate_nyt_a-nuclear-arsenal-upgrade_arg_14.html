"The United States should modernize its nuclear arsenal by prioritizing a submarine-centered force while eliminating or delaying vulnerable land-based missiles to reduce the risk of accidental nuclear war."

The United States should use this modernization debate to ask a question Washington usually avoids: why does it still need all three legs of the nuclear triad? The standard answer is habit disguised as strategy. Submarines, land-based missiles and bombers made sense in the early Cold War, when accuracy was poor, communications were fragile and surprise attack scenarios dominated planning. In 2016, survivable ballistic missile submarines provide the essence of deterrence by themselves.

A nuclear force must do one thing above all: survive a first strike and retaliate with enough force that no adversary could regard nuclear attack as rational. The submarine force can do that. A smaller number of ballistic missile submarines at sea, hidden in the oceans, is far harder to target than silo-based missiles in the American West. Those silos may complicate an enemy’s attack plan, but they also create pressure in a crisis. Because fixed missiles are vulnerable, a president warned of incoming attack might face an awful incentive to launch before confirmation is complete.

Modernization should therefore include the next generation of ballistic missile submarines, secure command-and-control networks, warhead life-extension programs that do not add new military capabilities, and improved early-warning systems designed to reduce false alarms. It should exclude a new intercontinental ballistic missile fleet, or at least delay it while the country debates whether land-based missiles add more danger than security. It should also exclude new low-yield warheads whose main political effect is to make nuclear use easier to imagine.

Bombers occupy a middle ground. They are useful as signals because they can be dispersed, flown and recalled. But they should not become the justification for a large suite of new nuclear cruise missiles. The bomber leg should be sized for deterrence and signaling, not for fighting a prolonged nuclear campaign.

This approach would not be unilateral disarmament. The United States would retain a devastating retaliatory capability, one that no adversary could eliminate. It would also save money and reduce the number of launch-on-warning weapons. Critics will say that removing one leg weakens deterrence. But deterrence is not a museum of inherited systems. It is a relationship between capability, survivability and credibility.

A safer modernization would be submarine-centered, command-and-control-heavy and warhead-conservative. It would preserve the ability to deter nuclear war while reducing the machinery that could turn a false alarm or political panic into irreversible catastrophe.

  3. 03Modernization should focus on secure command-and-control networks and early-warning systems rather than developing new military capabilities or low-yield warheads that make nuclear use more likely.
  4. 04The bomber leg of the triad should be maintained only for signaling and deterrence purposes rather than for conducting prolonged nuclear campaigns.
  5. 05A streamlined nuclear force maintains a devastating retaliatory capability while saving money and increasing global stability.
