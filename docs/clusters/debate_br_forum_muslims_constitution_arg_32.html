"The 'continuity thesis'—the claim that Trump’s anti-Muslim policies are merely an extension of previous administrations—is analytically flawed and politically counterproductive because it ignores meaningful differences in policy intent and undermines incremental political mobilization."

The Limits of Continuity

Akbar and Theoharis present a clear and morally serious case, and on many specifics they are right. The material support statute is overbroad. CVE was a poorly designed program built on contested premises. The No Fly List has glaring due process problems that the Ninth Circuit has only begun to address. The federal terrorism prosecution apparatus has produced disturbing cases—the Newburgh Four, the Liberty City Seven—where the line between detection and entrapment looks vanishingly thin. The brunt of post-9/11 surveillance has indeed fallen on American Muslim communities, and the legal architecture built by Bush and continued by Obama deserves the scrutiny the authors demand. Where I part company with them is in the central rhetorical move of the essay: the insistence that Trump's executive order is essentially continuous with prior policy, and that liberal opposition to the ban which does not also repudiate the entire counterterrorism apparatus is hypocritical or naive.

This continuity thesis is analytically attractive and politically counterproductive, and I think it gets the moral structure of the situation wrong.

Begin with the executive order itself. The authors note, correctly, that the seven countries on Trump's list were drawn from a 2015 Obama-era law restricting the Visa Waiver Program for travelers who had visited those countries. But the Obama policy did not bar entry to nationals of those countries. It required them—and dual nationals of waiver-program countries who had traveled there—to go through the regular visa process rather than the streamlined waiver. That is a very different policy. It is the difference between additional screening and categorical exclusion, between treating travel history as a risk factor and treating national origin as a disqualification. To call the ban a mere "amplification" of Obama policy is to elide a categorical change in kind.

Similarly, while it is true that the Obama administration deported large numbers of people and built much of the surveillance architecture the authors decry, the orientation of that administration toward Muslim Americans differed in ways that matter. Obama personally and repeatedly affirmed that the United States was not at war with Islam. He visited mosques. He spoke at Muslim community events. His Justice Department prosecuted hate crimes against Muslims. His CVE program, whatever its real flaws, was at least notionally framed as a partnership with Muslim communities rather than a confrontation with them. Trump's first month has been categorically different: the appointment of advisors who have called Islam a "cancer," the reported plan to rename CVE "Countering Radical Islamic Extremism," the explicit campaign promises of a Muslim registry and a ban. To treat these as equivalent because both administrations operate within the same legal architecture is to ignore that the same architecture, in different hands, produces different harms.

The authors' answer to this would be: yes, but the architecture exists, and Trump can now wield it. That is true and it is the strongest version of their argument. But it is not the same as saying that Obama and Trump are interchangeable, or that the airport protestors are dupes for failing to demand abolition of the entire national security state. Most political reform happens incrementally, by mobilizing public concern around specific abuses, and the implicit contempt in the lead essay for "particularized notions of Muslim innocence" misreads how movements actually build.

Take the No Fly List. The authors criticize liberals for celebrating John Lewis's gun-control sit-in, which proposed using the list to bar firearm purchases. The criticism has merit—if you think the list is a due process disaster, you should not want to extend it to a new domain. But the appropriate response is to argue, as the ACLU did, that the list itself needs reform, not to dismiss the gun control effort as Islamophobic. The civil libertarian and the gun control proponent can be in conversation; they need not be enemies, and the authors' framing makes that conversation harder, not easier.

The same is true of the "innocence" framing. The lead essay treats public outrage focused on detained children and elderly travelers as a defect, because it implicitly cedes the legitimacy of harsher treatment for some "dangerous" Muslims. But this is how moral persuasion works. People become invested in a category of victims through specific, sympathetic cases; the category then expands as the underlying analysis develops. The civil rights movement did not begin by demanding the abolition of all racial classifications in law; it began with lunch counters and bus seats and incremental claims that brought ordinary white Americans into a moral relationship with Black plaintiffs. To insist that protestors at JFK must, on day one, articulate a fully developed critique of the AUMF or they are part of the problem is to set a purity test that no real political movement can meet.

There are also matters of fact and emphasis where the lead essay reads too tendentiously. The claim that CVE focuses "exclusively on Muslim extremism" under Trump (which is plausible) is paired with criticism of Obama's CVE for emphasizing Muslim communities and devoting "little emphasis" to white power or neo-Nazi extremism. This is partly true and partly unfair: the Obama DHS in fact funded several non-Muslim CVE grantees including programs targeting white supremacist recruitment, and the intelligence community produced reports on right-wing extremism (notably the 2009 DHS report that was rescinded after conservative pushback). The disproportion was real, but it was contested within the bureaucracy, not unanimous. Trump's reported decision to formally exclude white extremism is a real and meaningful change that the continuity thesis obscures.

On material support, the authors do not mention that the statute has also been used—rightly, in my view—to prosecute Americans who provided money, fighters, or logistical aid to groups like ISIS and al-Shabaab that have engaged in mass atrocities against Muslims. The case for narrowing the statute (particularly after Humanitarian Law Project, which extended liability to nonviolent advocacy training) is strong. The case for repealing it entirely is much weaker and would require ignoring real harms.

Finally, on the global picture: the authors are right that domestic and foreign policies are connected, and they are right that the bombing campaigns in Muslim-majority countries are part of the context. But the suggestion that opposing the ban requires confronting "the role of the United States around the world" can flatten important distinctions. The U.S. has bombed Syria—on behalf of, among others, the Syrian civilians being slaughtered by the Assad regime, which has killed more Muslims than any actor in the conflict. The U.S. has fought al-Shabaab in Somalia, which has killed thousands of Somali Muslims. The honest position acknowledges that American military power has done grave harm and also that withdrawing it entirely is not a costless choice for the populations involved.

What the moment calls for, I would suggest, is something subtler than the lead essay allows: a defense of liberal-constitutional values that is honest about how those values have failed Muslim Americans, committed to specific reforms (narrowing material support, due process for the No Fly List, ending entrapment-style stings, ending the CVE program in its current form), and unwilling to be paralyzed by the demand that the only authentic opposition to Trump is a comprehensive rejection of the modern American state. The airport protestors were not naive. They saw something specific and outrageous, and they showed up. That is how political mobilization starts. It is not how it ends, and Akbar and Theoharis are right that more is needed. But it is also not nothing, and it is not the same as the apparatus they protested.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Trump’s executive order represents a categorical shift from additional screening to total exclusion, rather than a mere amplification of Obama-era travel restrictions.
  2. 02The orientation and rhetoric of an administration matter, as the Obama administration's attempts at community partnership and mosque visits differ fundamentally from the Trump administration's open hostility toward Islam.
  3. 03Dismissing public outrage over 'innocent' victims like children and the elderly ignores the reality that moral persuasion and political movements often begin with specific, sympathetic cases before expanding to broader systemic critiques.
  4. 04The continuity thesis obscures meaningful changes in government focus, such as the Trump administration's reported decision to formally exclude white supremacist extremism from counter-extremism programs.
  5. 05Demanding a total repudiation of the national security state as a prerequisite for authentic opposition creates an impossible purity test that alienates potential allies and hinders practical reform.
  6. 06The lead essay's critique of U.S. foreign policy flattens the moral complexity of military interventions that, while often harmful, are sometimes directed against actors who are themselves committing mass atrocities against Muslims.
