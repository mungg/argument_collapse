The Body in the Room

I want to dwell on a part of Mie Inouye's essay that has lingered with me longest: Bob Lee's hands. The hand in Roger's hair. The hands on the shoulders of the middle-aged man with his arms crossed. The hands grasping the young mother's hands and lifting her to her feet. Inouye notices that her students cannot imagine touching strangers this way, and reads this as evidence of how thoroughly neoliberalism has stripped us of the embodied repertoire of organizing. I think she is right, and I want to develop this thread further, because I believe the body — the actually felt, breathing, touching body — is where solidarity is made and unmade, and where most contemporary theorizing about solidarity does not go.

Let me start with a confession. I have been in left organizing for two decades, and the meetings I remember are not the ones with the most sophisticated political analysis. They are the ones with the meals, the singing, the physical exhaustion of canvassing for ten hours and coming back to a house where someone had cooked. They are the moments at picket lines when a stranger handed me a coffee and our fingers touched. They are the funerals of comrades, where bodies held each other up. They are the long bus rides to protests, the shared cigarettes outside meetings, the late-night kitchen conversations after the formal session ended. These were not auxiliary to the organizing; they were the organizing. The political education happened in the body, before it happened in the head.

This is not a sentimental claim. It is a claim grounded in what we know about how human cognition and affiliation actually work. Decades of research on embodied cognition, on the social neuroscience of trust, on the role of shared physical experience in group formation, all converge on the point that human solidarity is built largely below the level of conscious deliberation, through synchronized bodies, shared rhythms, mutual gaze, touch, and the rituals of co-presence. Émile Durkheim called the resulting state "collective effervescence." Randall Collins, working in his tradition, has spent a career documenting how "interaction ritual chains" produce solidarity through embodied co-presence, shared mood, and mutual focus. The strongest organizations — religious, military, political — are those that have understood this and built ritual infrastructure to harness it.

Now consider the contemporary left's relationship to embodied co-presence. It is, to put it gently, attenuated. The pandemic dealt a brutal blow to organizational forms that depended on bodies in rooms; many groups have not recovered. The displacement of meetings to Zoom — efficient, accessible, inclusive in important ways — has come at a cost that we are only beginning to register. Touch has become politically fraught for reasons that have very good origins (the necessary critique of harassment, the consent revolution) but that have left us without a comfortable repertoire for the kind of solidarity-making touch that Bob Lee deployed so effectively. The standardization of nonprofit professional culture has imported HR norms into organizing spaces, attenuating the embodied informality that older organizing traditions took for granted.

Inouye's students cannot imagine touching a stranger because they have grown up in a culture that has, for good and ill, taught them not to. They also cannot imagine the sustained co-presence that earlier generations of organizers took for granted, because they have not lived in institutions that produced it. The Mormon ward Inouye describes is, among many other things, an embodied co-presence machine: three hours every Sunday, two hours Wednesday night, an hour every morning. The Black church traditions out of which the civil rights movement emerged were similar machines. So were the labor halls, the social clubs, the immigrant societies. These were not just spaces where political work happened; they were factories of bodily solidarity.

What does it mean to take this seriously now? First, I think it means that any theory of solidarity that does not center the body is going to miss most of what actually matters. Inouye's essay, to its credit, gestures repeatedly at the embodied: the streets in 2020, the bodies pressed against each other, Lee's hands, the awkwardness of the young white people in the room. But these gestures are descriptions, not analytic centerpieces. I would want to push the body more firmly to the center of the analysis. Solidarity is not first an idea, a moral commitment, a material interest, or even an ideology. It is first a fact of bodies in proximity, and everything else is a description of what those bodies are doing or what we hope they will do.

Second, it means that the practices of solidarity-building have to be reverse-engineered from what we know about how embodied affiliation actually forms. This is not mysterious. Eat together, frequently. Sing together. Walk together, including in the literal sense of marching but also in the everyday sense of going somewhere on foot together. Cook for each other. Show up at funerals and weddings and births. Sit in waiting rooms together. Watch each other's children. Touch each other in the small ways that are still socially licensed — handshakes, hugs, hands on shoulders, the casual contact of dense co-presence. These are not auxiliary practices that supplement the real organizing. They are the real organizing, or at least the substrate on which everything else depends.

Third, it means that we have to take seriously the ways that the built environment, the schedule, the format, and the materiality of organizing shape what becomes possible. A meeting in a fluorescent-lit room with folding chairs in rows is a different political event than a meeting around a table with food. A protest that ends in dispersal is a different event than one that ends with everyone going to a common space to debrief. A campaign that asks volunteers to phonebank from home is a different event than one that asks them to phonebank from a common office where they meet each other and the candidate. These are not aesthetic preferences. They are infrastructural decisions that determine what kinds of solidarity can form.

Fourth — and here I am offering an addition to Inouye's framework rather than a critique — the embodied frame helps us understand why "social endurance" matters in the way that it does. Endurance is not just a virtue we cultivate by gritting our teeth through hard meetings. It is the byproduct of accumulated embodied co-presence. After enough meals together, enough Sundays, enough funerals, enough picket lines, our bodies recognize each other and want to be in proximity to each other, even when our minds are in disagreement. This is why thick institutions produce endurance and thin ones do not. The body knows things the mind has forgotten.

Inouye's own family story is, in this light, an embodied story. The sugar cookie her father received at his first ward social did the political work it did because it was eaten, by a small body, in a room with other bodies who had baked it and who would soon know his name. The mutual aid network that helped her family survive postwar racism was a network of bodies showing up, repeatedly, in person, to do things together. Mormonism's power — for good and ill — is not finally doctrinal. It is bodily. The doctrine is the rationalization of practices that work on the body before they work on the mind.

If we want what the Mormon ward has, we have to build what the Mormon ward has: dense, repeated, embodied co-presence around shared practices, in physical spaces that we own or hold or steward. There is no shortcut through better ideology or thicker theory. The body in the room is the basic unit, and we have spent forty years failing to build the spaces where bodies can gather. That is the project, and Bob Lee's hands are the curriculum.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Human affiliation and trust are built through subconscious biological processes like synchronized movement, touch, and shared rhythms that occur below the level of conscious deliberation.
  2. 02The contemporary left's reliance on digital spaces and professionalized nonprofit norms has stripped organizing of the informal, physical interactions necessary to forge deep bonds.
  3. 03Historical movements and institutions like the Mormon Church or the Black Panther Party succeeded because they functioned as 'factories of bodily solidarity' through dense, repeated physical gathering.
  4. 04Social endurance is not merely a mental virtue but a byproduct of accumulated physical experiences, such as eating and singing together, which keep people in the room despite ideological disagreements.
  5. 05Organizing strategy must prioritize the 'infrastructural' elements of gathering—such as shared meals and physical proximity—as the primary work of building a movement rather than as auxiliary activities.
