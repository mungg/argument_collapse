"While social endurance and epistemic humility are vital for political life, they should be oriented toward a pluralistic democratic politics of compromise and civic obligation rather than a radical, totalizing critique of racial capitalism."

Mie Inouye’s essay offers an eloquent account of why political solidarity is difficult in a fragmented society. Her emphasis on patience, humility, and the need to remain in relationship with people one dislikes is welcome. American public life would be healthier if more activists, intellectuals, and citizens accepted that politics is not therapy and that disagreement is not contamination. But the essay is less convincing in its assumption that solidarity should be oriented toward a radical critique of “racial capitalism” and perhaps even revolution. The habits Inouye praises may be better suited to a more modest, pluralist democratic politics than to the transformative project she envisions.

The central problem is that the essay treats the United States largely as a system of domination to be overcome. Police violence, Japanese internment, COVID deaths, racial inequality, neoliberal capitalism, Mormon assimilation, and Uncle Gerald’s Trumpism are all drawn into a single explanatory frame. There is truth in parts of this frame. The United States has committed grave injustices. Racial hierarchy has shaped its institutions. Market ideology has often produced cruelty. The pandemic exposed incompetence, distrust, and inequality. But when every tragedy is interpreted as an expression of the same underlying system, political judgment can become too easy. The world divides into those who see the system and those who do not.

This risks undermining the very solidarity Inouye wants. Many Americans will not recognize themselves in the claim that “the same shit that killed George Floyd” killed a rural doctor who refused hospital care and experimented with hydroxychloroquine. They may agree that George Floyd was murdered and that the pandemic was mishandled. They may support police reform, public health investment, and a stronger safety net. But they will resist a politics that explains their lives primarily through racial capitalism or asks them to join a revolutionary project. If solidarity requires that level of ideological convergence, it will remain narrow.

Inouye explicitly rejects perfect agreement, but her examples tend to privilege organizations with radical analyses: the Black Panthers, Young Lords, Rainbow Coalition, abolitionist movements, critiques of the PMC, and theorists of capitalism and domination. These traditions deserve study. Yet democratic solidarity in a large, diverse country cannot be built only among those prepared to adopt left theory. It must include people who are religious, patriotic, moderate, skeptical of academic language, attached to family and local community, worried about crime, supportive of markets but critical of corporate abuse, and unwilling to see their lives as false consciousness.

Uncle Gerald is the most interesting figure in the essay because he does not fit neatly into the categories used to judge him. He was reactionary in some respects, intolerant in others, and reckless about COVID. But he also provided affordable medical care, helped build a school, mentored young people, and served his community. He sounds, in many ways, like a citizen. Not a perfect citizen, not an enlightened one, but a person whose life was thick with obligation. A politics that can only understand him as someone who needed a better analysis of racial capitalism may miss what he already knew: that one has duties to neighbors.

That neighborly ethic is not enough. It can be parochial, exclusionary, and blind to structural injustice. But it is also indispensable. Many Americans who will never attend a socialist meeting will coach Little League, volunteer at church, bring meals after a death, serve on a school board, join a veterans’ group, or help a neighbor repair a roof. These practices cultivate the “social endurance” Inouye rightly values. They also form the basis of democratic life. Rather than asking how to move such people toward revolutionary solidarity, we might ask how to connect their existing civic commitments to practical reforms.

The critique of deference politics is useful here. Deference has often made progressive politics seem less like a common democratic enterprise than a hierarchy of moral authority. People are asked to accept claims not because they are persuaded but because of who is making them. This breeds resentment and dishonesty. Inouye’s alternative, epistemic humility, is better. Yet humility should apply not only to privileged progressives listening to the marginalized. It should also apply to radicals listening to ordinary citizens who worry that some left proposals are impractical, coercive, or dismissive of real goods.

Take policing. The summer of 2020 rightly forced national attention to police violence and racial injustice. But many communities also faced rising gun violence in 2020 and 2021, and residents of high-crime neighborhoods often held complex views: anger at abusive policing alongside desire for protection from violence. A politics of solidarity must be able to hear both. If “deference” means listening only to activists who endorse abolition, it fails even on its own terms, because affected communities are not unanimous. If “solidarity” means insisting that concern about crime is merely reactionary, it will alienate people with urgent needs.

Or consider public health during COVID. Inouye describes Uncle Gerald’s death as an avoidable consequence of capitalism and state failure, but also of his ideology and personality. That seems fair. Yet millions of Americans who resisted mandates or mistrusted authorities were not simply libertarian fools. Some were misled by partisan media; others had reasonable distrust of institutions that had failed them; still others faced economic pressures that made isolation impossible. A solidaristic public health politics would have required not only condemning misinformation but building trust through local institutions, primary care, churches, employers, and schools. It would have needed persuasion, not just structural critique.

The most valuable part of Inouye’s essay is her insistence that people must remain in contact across difference. This insight points toward democratic pluralism. In a pluralist society, we do not need to become comrades in a thick ideological sense to cooperate. We can build overlapping solidarities around concrete goods: safer streets and accountable police; higher wages and successful small businesses; religious liberty and equal citizenship for LGBTQ people; economic growth and environmental stewardship; national belonging and immigrant inclusion. Such coalitions are imperfect and unstable, but they may be more realistic than the search for a unified left.

This does not mean settling for shallow centrism. The United States needs serious reforms: stronger labor rights, child allowances or other family supports, universal health coverage in some form, housing construction and tenant protections, criminal justice reform, voting rights, climate policy, and better taxation of inherited wealth. These are not minor matters. They would materially improve lives. But they can be argued for in the language of democratic fairness, civic obligation, and national repair, not only in the language of anti-capitalist transformation.

Institutions capable of sustaining such politics already exist, though they are weakened: unions, congregations, schools, libraries, local newspapers, civic associations, and political parties. Reviving them may do more for solidarity than creating new ideological formations. The decline of these mediating institutions has left people alone with nationalized media narratives and algorithmic outrage. In that environment, both right and left become more performative. People lose the habit of working with neighbors who vote differently. They become skilled at denunciation and unskilled at compromise.

Compromise is not betrayal. It is how free and equal citizens govern together when they disagree about ultimate things. Movements need moral clarity, but democracies need bargaining. A politics that treats bargaining as contamination will either remain powerless or become authoritarian if it gains power. Inouye warns against permanent unity because it suppresses conflict. The same warning should apply to revolutionary visions that imagine society reorganized around a single emancipatory analysis.

The better lesson of Mormonism may be that people are formed by concrete obligations more than abstract theories. That formation can go wrong, as Inouye’s family history shows. But it can also produce generosity, resilience, and service. The task is not to dissolve such local loyalties into a totalizing critique but to broaden them. The neighbor must come to include the stranger, the immigrant, the political opponent, the person harmed by institutions we trust, and the person afraid of reforms we support.

Solidarity now should therefore mean democratic patience: the willingness to argue, serve, compromise, and reform alongside people who will never share all our premises. Inouye’s social endurance is a necessary virtue for that project. But it should be detached from the expectation that endurance culminates in radical agreement. Sometimes the point of staying in the room is not to become comrades. It is to remain fellow citizens.
