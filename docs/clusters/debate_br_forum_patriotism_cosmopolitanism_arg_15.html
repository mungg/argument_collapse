What Cosmopolitanism Forgets About Power

There is a kind of essay that gets written every generation by humane and learned people who would like the world to be more just, and who suppose that the path to justice lies through the broadening of moral imagination. Professor Nussbaum's essay is a distinguished example of the genre. Its premises are decent; its tone is admirable; its references are well chosen. And yet, reading it, one cannot escape the suspicion that it is addressed to a world that does not exist, and that its prescriptions, if adopted, would leave the actual world more or less as it is.

The world that does not exist is one in which moral persuasion does the heavy lifting of politics. In that world, if American students were taught more about Bolivia, they would feel more solidarity with Bolivians, and this feeling would translate into policy choices more favorable to Bolivia. In that world, if the cosmopolitan Stoic image of humanity as a single body could be imparted in the schools, the trade regime, the pattern of capital flows, the structure of military alliances, and the distribution of ecological harms would all begin to shift. In that world, in short, the explanation for the suffering of the global poor is that the global rich do not yet sufficiently understand them. Better curricula will fix it.

The actual world is otherwise. The relation between the United States and Bolivia is not principally constituted by the sentiments of American students. It is constituted by the operations of mining companies, by the conditions attached to loans from international financial institutions, by the policies of the United States Trade Representative, by the demands of the war on drugs. American ignorance of Bolivia is a real problem, but it is downstream of, not upstream of, the structural arrangements that determine how Bolivia is treated. A maximally cosmopolitan curriculum, taught by saints, would do little to disturb those arrangements unless it were accompanied by something Nussbaum's essay almost entirely lacks: an analysis of who benefits from them and how their power is to be confronted.

This is, I think, the deepest difference between the cosmopolitan tradition Nussbaum invokes and the internationalist tradition of the labor and socialist movements. Both traditions speak of humanity, but they speak of it differently. The Stoic cosmopolitan says: recognize humanity in the foreigner, attend to her dignity, deliberate with her. The socialist internationalist says: the workers of Bolivia and the workers of Pittsburgh share a common antagonist, and only by acting together can either be free. The first is an ethical stance; the second is a political strategy. The first can be adopted by a humanities professor at the University of Chicago without disturbing anyone's interests; the second cannot be adopted by anyone without disturbing the interests of a great many powerful people. That is not an accident.

I do not mean to suggest that Nussbaum is unaware of power. Her work in development economics has obviously brought her into contact with it. But the essay before us treats the obstacle to a more just world as essentially educational and imaginative, and it locates the cosmopolitan tradition almost entirely in literature, philosophy, and the curriculum. The political vehicles by which a more cosmopolitan order might actually be brought into being—international labor solidarity, the non-aligned movement, the various campaigns against the new debt regime, the indigenous mobilizations now stirring across Latin America—do not appear. Nor does the question of why those vehicles have been so systematically obstructed, and by whom.

There is, additionally, a class question internal to the cosmopolitan ideal as Nussbaum presents it. To be a citizen of the world, in the relevant sense, requires a certain detachment from one's local circumstances. Diogenes could afford it because he had renounced property. Marcus Aurelius could afford it because he owned an empire. The university professor can afford it because her labor is portable and her income secure. The auto worker in Flint cannot afford it, and when she is told that her attachment to her nation, her neighborhood, her union local is a parochialism to be overcome in favor of allegiance to humanity as such, she correctly suspects that the proposal is being made on behalf of someone who would like her to stop making expensive claims on her national polity. Cosmopolitanism has too often been the ideology of those for whom borders are an inconvenience, addressed to those for whom borders are a protection.

None of this is to defend American chauvinism, which is a sickness and which Rorty's essay underestimates. Nor is it to deny that internationalism—real internationalism, the kind that organizes across borders rather than dissolving them—is desperately needed. It is to insist that internationalism is a political project, with allies and enemies, and that it cannot be conducted as a seminar on the Stoics. Tagore's Nikhil, whom Nussbaum admires, is in the novel a landlord; his cosmopolitan virtue is, among other things, a function of his position. Sandip is a demagogue, and Nussbaum is right to deplore him, but the appeal of demagogues to people who have been condescended to by cosmopolitan landlords is a phenomenon she might have addressed more directly. The defeat of cosmopolitanism in the novel is not merely a tragedy of mistaken passion; it is a tragedy of a class that mistook its own decency for politics.

What is wanted, then, is not less cosmopolitanism but a cosmopolitanism with teeth: one that names the structures by which the global rich extract wealth from the global poor, one that allies itself with the movements of the poor rather than improving their image among the rich, one that understands that the question "to what community do I owe allegiance?" is less important than the question "with whom must I act, and against whom?" Nussbaum's essay gives us most of the materials for such a position. It just does not, in the end, build it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The suffering of the global poor is caused by structural economic and military arrangements rather than a lack of empathy or knowledge among the global rich.
  2. 02Cosmopolitanism functions as an ethical stance for the privileged, whereas true internationalism requires a political strategy based on shared interests and collective action against common antagonists.
  3. 03The essay ignores the specific political vehicles and movements, such as labor solidarity and indigenous mobilizations, that are necessary to actually challenge the status quo.
  4. 04Cosmopolitanism often serves as an ideology for the mobile elite while dismissing the national and local borders that provide essential protections for the working class.
  5. 05A viable cosmopolitanism must move beyond moral decency to identify the specific structures of wealth extraction and determine who must be acted against to achieve justice.
