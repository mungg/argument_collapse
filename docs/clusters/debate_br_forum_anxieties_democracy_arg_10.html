"The perceived crisis of American democracy is largely an exaggeration by intellectuals who mistake the constitutional design of slow, deliberative disagreement for institutional failure."

The anxieties of democracy are real, but they are also recurrently exaggerated by intellectuals who dislike the outcomes of democratic politics. Ira Katznelson sees paralysis, moneyed influence, and civic decay. Much of the public sees something less dramatic: a large, diverse country disagreeing about hard questions and a constitutional system preventing temporary majorities from doing too much too quickly. That is not always pretty. It is also not necessarily a crisis.

Gridlock is often treated as evidence of democratic failure. Sometimes it is. But in a republic with separated powers, bicameralism, federalism, judicial review, and staggered elections, difficulty in making law is a design feature. The Constitution does not assume that action is the highest political good. It assumes that power is dangerous, factions are permanent, and durable law should require broad consent. When that consent is absent, inaction may be more democratic than forced resolution.

Consider the issues Katznelson lists: distribution, racial equity, immigration, and the balance between liberty and security. These are not technical problems awaiting enlightened legislative craftsmanship. They involve deep moral disagreement, conflicting interests, and uncertainty about consequences. On immigration, citizens disagree about labor markets, national identity, law enforcement, humanitarian obligation, and cultural change. On distribution, they disagree about fairness, incentives, debt, and the proper scope of government. On security, they disagree about risk. A legislature that fails to settle these questions quickly is not necessarily broken. It may be accurately reflecting the country.

The essay’s standard of democratic health sometimes seems too demanding. Katznelson wants equal access, informed participation, deliberative lawmaking, civic virtue, effective policy, toleration, and insulation from wealth. These are worthy aspirations, but no large democracy has ever fully met them. Politics is conducted by imperfect citizens with unequal resources, limited information, and mixed motives. The relevant comparison is not ideal democracy but available alternatives. On that scale, American democracy looks troubled but hardly moribund.

The concern about money in politics, for example, deserves perspective. Wealthy individuals and groups spend large sums, but money does not simply buy elections. Well-funded candidates lose; poorly funded insurgents sometimes win; parties and donors often chase public opinion rather than create it. In 2012, vast outside spending did not prevent President Obama’s reelection. The Tea Party, whatever one thinks of it, showed that motivated voters could defeat establishment Republicans with more money. Bernie Sanders’s current presidential campaign, powered by small donors and large crowds, is another reminder that American politics is not closed.

Nor is it obvious that political spending is uniquely corrupting compared with other forms of influence. Newspapers, universities, unions, churches, celebrities, foundations, and advocacy organizations all shape opinion. A democracy committed to free speech cannot eliminate unequal persuasive capacity. It can require disclosure and punish bribery, but it should be cautious about empowering government to decide which associations may speak too much.

Katznelson is more persuasive when he criticizes the temptation to bypass legislatures. The growth of executive unilateralism should worry liberals and conservatives alike. Presidents increasingly govern through waivers, enforcement discretion, military initiatives, and administrative rules. Courts resolve disputes that legislatures avoid. Agencies make policy with limited accountability. Yet this tendency is often encouraged by the same reformers who lament congressional obstruction. They want Congress restored when it will enact their priorities and circumvented when it will not.

A healthier democratic temperament would accept that losing is normal. If Congress declines to enact cap-and-trade, immigration reform, gun control, or entitlement cuts, the answer should usually be persuasion and elections, not executive workaround or judicial creativity. Democracy includes the right of citizens to be wrong, shortsighted, or stubborn. The alternative is rule by those who claim to know better.

The most promising reforms are therefore modest. Make voting easy and secure. Draw districts more fairly where possible. Increase transparency in campaign finance. Require Congress to vote on major military commitments. Simplify laws so citizens can understand them. Sunset regulations and programs that no longer command support. Return more questions to states and localities, where disagreement can be managed through diversity rather than national compulsion.

But we should resist the romance of democratic “capacity.” The twentieth century’s most energetic states were not always admirable. Speed and coherence are not the same as justice. The New Deal did many necessary things, but it also excluded many black workers from key protections, cartelized parts of the economy, and expanded federal power in ways still debated. A political system that moves slowly can frustrate reformers; it can also protect liberty from reformers who are too sure of themselves.

There is another source of anxiety Katznelson underplays: citizens increasingly expect politics to provide meaning, identity, and moral vindication. That expectation makes compromise feel like betrayal. If we ask less of national politics, we may be less enraged by its imperfections. Families, markets, religious communities, voluntary associations, local governments, and private enterprise are not substitutes for citizenship, but they are buffers against political absolutism.

Representative democracy deserves defense, but not because it can solve every consequential public problem. It deserves defense because it allows a free people to argue, experiment, resist, and sometimes refuse collective action. The scaffolding around the Capitol should remind us of maintenance, not reconstruction. The American system needs repairs. It does not need to be remade in the image of those who mistake disagreement for decay.
