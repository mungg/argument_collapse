"While Daniel Richman argues that involving local police in counterterrorism will naturally increase accountability through federalism, this optimistic view ignores the systemic history of local law enforcement abuses and the inherent lack of effective civilian oversight."

Daniel Richman's argument rests on an optimistic assumption that deserves scrutiny: that involving local police in counterterrorism intelligence will make the system more accountable. The history of local law enforcement suggests otherwise.

Consider the track record. Local police departments have been subjects of civil rights litigation for decades. The pattern is depressingly familiar: aggressive enforcement in minority communities, discriminatory targeting, and resistance to accountability. The consent decree in New York City that Richman mentions arose from systematic abuse by the police intelligence bureau. Denver was still negotiating settlements with the ACLU in 2002. These are not isolated incidents; they reflect systemic tendencies that community policing has moderated but not eliminated.

Richman argues that local accountability— mayors facing voters, police chiefs reporting to elected officials—will check abuses. But this accountability is weak in practice. Police departments operate with considerable autonomy from civilian oversight. Civilian review boards often lack authority or access. Elected officials have strong incentives to support police departments, particularly after terrorist incidents or spikes in violent crime. The accountability Richman describes exists in theory but is often absent in fact.

The federalist argument also underestimates the capacity of local police to resist accountability. Richman notes that many cities passed resolutions criticizing federal counterterrorism efforts. Some of these were symbolic, but they reflected genuine local resistance to federal priorities. If local departments can resist federal priorities they disagree with, they can also pursue local priorities that may conflict with community interests or civil liberties.

The deeper problem is that counterterrorism intelligence is fundamentally different from ordinary law enforcement intelligence. Street crime is visible and measurable; its targets are identifiable; its investigation can be reviewed against established standards. Terrorism intelligence involves speculation about future threats based on fragmentary information, and its targets are often defined by ethnicity, religion, or political viewpoint. The standards for acceptable investigation are much harder to specify, and the potential for abuse is much greater.

Richman correctly notes that courts alone cannot constrain executive discretion in this area. But he errs in concluding that institutional structure among enforcement agencies will fill the gap. Structural constraints are only as strong as the institutions that implement them. Federalism disperses authority, but it does not create accountability unless the dispersed authorities are subject to genuine oversight.

The answer is not to abandon local involvement but to combine it with stronger external oversight. State legislatures should fund independent civil liberties monitoring for police counterterrorism activities. Congress should establish robust reporting requirements for any federal-local intelligence sharing. And the Justice Department's Civil Rights Division should be empowered to investigate patterns of discriminatory enforcement, not just individual incidents.

Richman's federalism offers a partial solution. But partial solutions require supplementation by other mechanisms. We cannot rely on the goodwill of law enforcement agencies at any level to protect rights that those agencies have both the incentive and the opportunity to violate.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Local police departments have a long, documented history of discriminatory targeting and civil rights violations that community policing models have failed to fully eliminate.
  2. 02The theoretical accountability of police to local elected officials is weak in practice because departments maintain high autonomy and politicians are incentivized to support aggressive policing.
  3. 03Counterterrorism intelligence is uniquely prone to abuse compared to ordinary law enforcement because it relies on speculative, fragmentary information often tied to ethnicity or religion rather than visible crimes.
  4. 04Dispersing authority through federalism does not create genuine accountability unless it is supplemented by strong external oversight, such as independent civil liberties monitoring and robust congressional reporting requirements.
  5. 05The ability of local departments to resist federal priorities suggests they can just as easily resist community interests or civil liberties protections when pursuing their own local agendas.
Diversified MiniMax

"The essay argues that relying on federalism and local police participation is an insufficient safeguard against the inherent risks of domestic surveillance, necessitating instead robust structural constraints and oversight to prevent the historical pattern of intelligence abuses."

Daniel Richman's invocation of COINTELPRO is revealing, though not in the way he intends. He uses the historical parallel to argue that current concerns about federal-local cooperation are "not ancient history," but he quickly moves past it to argue that modern community policing has changed the landscape. The lesson he draws is that we should be cautious but not fearful. But a more careful reading of the COINTELPRO era suggests that we should be more fearful than Richman admits.

The Church Committee report that Richman cites documented how local police intelligence units, originally created to address local disorder, became instruments of political surveillance at federal behest. Local police had "common interest" with federal authorities in investigating the sources of urban unrest, just as Richman argues that local police now have common interest with federal authorities in investigating terrorism. The problem was not that local police were unwilling participants—it was that their participation seemed legitimate at the time. The FBI wanted information about "subversive masterminds"; local police wanted to protect their jurisdictions. Cooperation seemed reasonable. Only later did it become clear that the "subversives" included civil rights activists, antiwar protesters, and ordinary citizens exercising their First Amendment rights.

The parallel to today's counterterrorism efforts is not exact, but the structural similarity is disturbing. Terrorist threats are real, and the need for intelligence gathering is genuine. But intelligence gathering requires definitions of what constitutes a threat—and those definitions are politically contingent. In the 1960s, civil rights organizing was defined as a threat. Today, ordinary religious practice by Muslims can be defined as indicators of radicalization. The categories shift, but the dynamic of escalating surveillance in response to perceived threats remains constant.

Richman argues that modern community policing has changed local police culture, making them more sensitive to civil liberties concerns. This is partially true but overstated. The community policing movement emerged in response to concerns about aggressive enforcement in minority communities—it was a response to police abuse, not a guarantee against future abuse. Many departments adopted community policing rhetoric without operational change. Others embraced it selectively, maintaining aggressive enforcement in minority neighborhoods while presenting a gentler face in more politically powerful communities.

More fundamentally, the question is not whether local police are trustworthy but whether any police force, local or federal, should be trusted with broad surveillance authority without meaningful oversight. Richman's argument that "none has a monopoly on virtue" is correct, but his conclusion—that we should therefore distribute authority among multiple levels—does not follow. The solution to untrustworthy surveillance is better oversight, not more surveillance at more levels.

The history of domestic intelligence in this country is a history of abuse followed by reform followed by new abuse. COINTELPRO gave way to guidelines that restricted FBI surveillance, but those guidelines were relaxed in the 1980s and 1990s, and new abuses emerged. The lesson is not that we should trust federalism to protect us but that we need structural constraints—judicial oversight, meaningful legislative supervision, robust civil remedies—that transcend the preferences of any particular administration or enforcement level.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The COINTELPRO era demonstrates that local police cooperation with federal authorities often appears legitimate at the time while facilitating the systemic surveillance of law-abiding citizens.
  2. 02The definition of a 'threat' is politically contingent, meaning that modern counterterrorism efforts risk targeting ordinary religious practices just as past efforts targeted civil rights activism.
  3. 03The impact of the community policing movement is overstated, as many departments adopted the rhetoric without making the operational changes necessary to protect civil liberties.
  4. 04Distributing surveillance authority across different levels of government is not a substitute for meaningful judicial oversight, legislative supervision, and civil remedies.
  5. 05History shows that domestic intelligence follows a cycle of abuse and reform, suggesting that administrative discretion cannot be trusted to self-regulate without external constraints.
