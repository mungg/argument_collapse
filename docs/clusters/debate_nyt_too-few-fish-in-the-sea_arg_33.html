"The most effective way to protect marine life and ensure a sustainable seafood supply is to eliminate government subsidies that fund destructive industrial fishing fleets."

The most effective lever we have to protect marine life and meet global food needs has nothing to do with consumer choices at the supermarket; it requires ending the massive government subsidies that keep destructive industrial fleets afloat.

Globally, governments hand out an estimated $30 billion in fishing subsidies every year. The vast majority of these are "capacity-enhancing" subsidies—money for fuel, vessel construction, and gear. This creates a perverse economic reality: it makes it profitable to fish even when there are practically no fish left. Without taxpayer money paying for the fuel, the massive distant-water fleets from Europe, China, and elsewhere could not afford to travel thousands of miles to scour the coasts of developing nations.

These subsidies are not only an ecological disaster; they are a profound injustice. Highly subsidized, high-tech fleets vacuum up marine protein off the coasts of West Africa and the Pacific Islands, outcompeting local artisanal fishers and threatening the food security of some of the world's most vulnerable populations.

If we eliminate harmful fishing subsidies through the World Trade Organization, the global fishing fleet will naturally shrink to a scale that aligns with the ocean's biological limits. The most destructive, fuel-intensive methods—like deep-sea bottom trawling—would immediately become economically unviable. If we want a sustainable seafood market, we have to let the market actually function. Stop subsidizing the destruction of the oceans, and the recovery of threatened marine life will follow, ensuring a sustainable, equitable supply of seafood for the future.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Capacity-enhancing subsidies make fishing profitable even when fish stocks are depleted, encouraging overfishing beyond biological limits.
  2. 02Government-funded fuel and vessel support allow distant-water fleets to travel thousands of miles to exploit the waters of developing nations.
  3. 03Subsidized industrial fleets create social injustice by outcompeting local artisanal fishers and threatening the food security of vulnerable populations.
  4. 04Removing subsidies would make the most ecologically destructive fishing methods, such as deep-sea bottom trawling, economically unviable.
