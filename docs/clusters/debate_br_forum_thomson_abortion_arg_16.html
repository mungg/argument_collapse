"The law must protect prenatal human life because the biological and moral evidence demonstrates that a fertilized egg is a human being with an inherent right to life from the moment of conception."

Judith Jarvis Thomson’s essay is a formidable defense of the abortion right, but it rests on a crucial misstep: the claim that there is no reason to believe a fertilized egg has a right to life, and thus that restricting abortion on that ground is illegitimate. I intend to show that this claim is not only false but that the biological and moral evidence points precisely the other way, and that the law must therefore protect prenatal human life.

Thomson’s starting point is that human beings have a right to life. She then challenges the anti-abortion argument that says a human being’s life begins at conception. She insists that having rights presupposes having interests, which in turn require psychological states. Since a fertilized egg lacks such states, she concludes, no reason compels us to believe it has rights. But this line of reasoning smuggles in a controversial theory of personhood. It assumes that the capacity for mental states is what grounds the right to life, rather than membership in the human species.

Consider: a newborn infant has only rudimentary wants and fears, far less sophisticated than many animals. A sleeping adult momentarily lacks any occurrent psychological states. If the right to life truly depends on the actual exercise of consciousness, we would have no ground to condemn infanticide or the killing of people in deep sleep. Yet both are universally acknowledged as murder. The common-sense view, which the encyclical reflects, is that every living member of the human family is a person with a right to life, not because of some temporary attribute, but because of what it is: a human being with an inherent capacity for rational thought and moral choice. The fertilized egg is not just a piece of tissue; it is an organism of the human species, genetically distinct from its mother and father, and actively directing its own development. It is a human being at an early stage of life.

Thomson might reply that something can be a human organism without having a right to life, but that move requires a defensible criterion for moral standing. She offers none, merely asserting that the pro-life view is “not unreasonable” to reject. But is the claim that every human being has a right to life itself unreasonable? That claim is the foundation of human equality. If we can arbitrarily exclude some humans—those at very early developmental stages—from the moral community, we undermine that foundation. The burden of proof is on those who wish to draw a line. Thomson’s line (psychological interests) would also exclude some severely disabled adults, a conclusion she surely would not welcome. The encyclical’s argument from natural law is, at a minimum, as rationally defensible as her own.

Once we grant that a fetus from conception is a human being with a right to life, the balance shifts. Thomson acknowledges that rights can be trumped in some circumstances, but she insists that abortion cannot be restricted on a ground that the constrained are not unreasonable in rejecting. This is a novel principle: that any severe constraint on liberty is impermissible unless the constrained party must rationally accept the reason. But by this logic, could we ever prohibit murder, if the murderer finds the prohibition unreasonable? A society must be able to enforce moral norms even when some citizens disagree, as long as those norms can be rationally defended in the public square. The claim that it is always wrong to intentionally kill an innocent human being is rationally defensible. The fact that some women reject it does not make it an illegitimate basis for law.

Finally, Thomson’s attempt to separate abortion—even early abortion—from the killing of a baby is belied by the biological reality. The placard she derides showed a developed infant, but the point is that abortion ends the life of a developing human at whatever stage. Calling that “fraud” is a rhetorical escape, not an argument. Violence is deplorable, but the moral outrage at the killing of millions of innocent human beings is not a product of misleading rhetoric; it springs from a reasoned conviction that these are our fellow humans. The law should protect them.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The right to life is grounded in membership in the human species rather than the temporary exercise of psychological states or consciousness.
  2. 02A fertilized egg is a genetically distinct human organism actively directing its own development, making it a human being at an early stage of life.
  3. 03Defining personhood based on psychological interests is a flawed criterion that would logically exclude infants and the severely disabled from moral protection.
  4. 04The principle of human equality is undermined if we arbitrarily exclude certain developmental stages of human life from the moral community.
  5. 05A society is justified in enforcing moral norms, such as the prohibition of killing innocent human beings, even when some citizens disagree with the underlying rationale.
  6. 06The moral outrage against abortion is based on the reasoned conviction that the procedure ends the life of a fellow human being at a specific stage of development.
