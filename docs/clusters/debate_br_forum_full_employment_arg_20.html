The Forgotten Tool: A Federal Job Guarantee

Robert Pollin's essay does many things well. It restores full employment to its proper place as a moral and economic priority. It demolishes the lazy story that immigrants or trade deficits are responsible for the jobs crisis. It correctly identifies neoliberalism, not globalization per se, as the policy regime that broke the postwar bargain. But for an essay titled "Back to Full Employment," there is a glaring omission: Pollin never seriously discusses the policy that would most directly deliver full employment, which is a federal job guarantee.

The idea is straightforward. The federal government commits to hiring, at a fixed wage with benefits, any adult who wants to work and cannot find a private-sector job. The work is useful — care for the elderly, environmental restoration, tutoring, infrastructure maintenance, public art, community gardens, the kinds of things market provision systematically underprovides. The wage acts as an effective minimum for the entire economy, since no private employer can pay less than the guarantee and still attract workers. When the private economy is booming, the guarantee program shrinks because workers are pulled into private jobs; when the private economy slumps, it expands automatically, providing a powerful counter-cyclical buffer.

The intellectual lineage here is venerable. Hyman Minsky proposed it for decades. William Vickrey, the Nobel laureate, endorsed it. The Argentine Jefes program in 2002–03 implemented something very much like it in response to that country's economic collapse, with measurable success in reducing extreme poverty and stabilizing communities. Pavlina Tcherneva, Randall Wray, and Stephanie Kelton have developed the modern American version in considerable detail. None of these names appear in Pollin's essay.

Why does this matter? Because Pollin's proposed mechanism — reallocating $330 billion from the Pentagon and fossil fuels to education and clean energy — has two weaknesses that a job guarantee does not.

First, it is indirect. Pollin estimates 4.8 million new jobs from his reallocation, which would bring unemployment from a hypothetical 7 percent down to about 3.9 percent. That's a great outcome, but it depends on a long chain of assumptions: that Congress will pass the reallocation, that the spending will flow into the predicted sectors with the predicted multipliers, that the newly created jobs will be matched to currently unemployed workers, that skills will transfer, that geographic mismatches will sort themselves out. Each link in the chain can break. A job guarantee, by contrast, hires people directly, where they live, at a wage they can count on, with no middleman.

Second, it does not solve the problem at the margin. Even if Pollin's plan worked exactly as advertised, you would still have several million people involuntarily unemployed and several million more underemployed. Their fate would still depend on whether the macroeconomy ran hot enough to absorb them. A job guarantee, by definition, leaves no involuntary unemployment. The "reserve army" that Marx, Kalecki, and Pollin all rightly identify as the source of capital's bargaining advantage is dissolved at the root, because no worker is forced to compete for survival.

Pollin worries — correctly — about Kalecki's insight that full employment threatens capital's political control. A job guarantee is the most direct realization of Kalecki's nightmare: workers would always have an outside option. Capital would lose much of its disciplinary power. This is exactly why a job guarantee will be politically difficult, but it is also why it would actually work, where weaker measures fail.

The standard objection is inflation. If everyone is guaranteed a job at, say, $12 an hour with benefits, won't this drive up wages throughout the economy and ignite an inflationary spiral? Here Pollin's Swedish example is actually instructive in ways he does not develop. The Swedish model achieved 2.1 percent unemployment with manageable inflation precisely because the job-creation mechanism included an active labor market policy that funneled workers into productive uses, combined with wage coordination. A U.S. job guarantee, by setting a fixed wage rather than chasing private-sector wages upward, functions as an inflation anchor, not an accelerant. The wage floor is set; it does not bid against itself.

A second objection is fiscal: where will the money come from? But this is precisely backward. The United States issues its own currency. The constraint on a job guarantee is not finance but real resources — workers, materials, energy. At 16.5 percent broad unemployment, real resources are precisely what we have in surplus. Funding the program is the easy part. The deficit hawks Pollin (rightly) criticizes will of course object, but they would object to anything.

I should be clear that the job guarantee is complementary to Pollin's proposals, not a replacement. The clean-energy transition is essential for reasons that go far beyond employment. Better-funded schools are essential. Cutting the Pentagon's bloat is essential. But the through-line that ties an actual full-employment policy together — that says "no one who wants to work will be denied a job" — is the guarantee. Without it, "full employment" remains a target we aim at, rather than a right we deliver.

Pollin closes by asking whether there is "the political will, in the United States, to fight for something as basic as the right to a decent job." It is a good question. But fighting for "something" is much easier when that something is concretely specified. A job guarantee — a real, administrable, federal-budget-line program with a wage and benefits package and an enrollment office — is the kind of thing people can rally for, vote for, and demand. "Reallocate Pentagon spending toward education and clean energy with a Swedish-style corporatist bargaining structure" is, by comparison, a graduate seminar.

If we want full employment, let's name the policy that delivers it.
