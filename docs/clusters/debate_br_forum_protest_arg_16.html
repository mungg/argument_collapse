**Yes, And: A Labor Perspective**

Reading Gourevitch's essay as someone who has spent a career organizing campus workers, I want to say two things. First: he is exactly right, and the analysis cannot stop where his does. Second: the people most likely to be crushed by the new repressive apparatus on campus are not the tenured professors or even the protesting students, but the workers who hold campuses together—custodians, food service workers, graduate employees, postdocs, adjuncts.

On the first point. Gourevitch's defense of an absolutist right to protest, including the right to be hostile, including the right to make people uncomfortable, is the only defense compatible with anything we would recognize as labor's traditional understanding of collective action. Every major workplace gain in American history was achieved by people who broke rules, made noise, refused to disperse, and generally inflicted on bosses and bystanders the precise sort of "subjective discomfort" that today's administrators treat as a Title VI violation. The sit-down strikes at Flint, the Memphis sanitation strike, the UC Berkeley clerical workers' strike in 1980—these were "disruptive." That was the point.

The collapse of the distinction between hurt and harm is therefore not just a campus problem or even a free-speech problem. It is the legal architecture that, if extended, makes labor militancy impossible. Already we are seeing employers argue that union-organizing speech creates a "hostile work environment" for anti-union workers; that picket lines amount to harassment; that disruptive collective action violates the rights of customers and clients to access services without distress. The same logic Gourevitch dissects on campus is being deployed to neuter the NLRA. If feelings of vulnerability suffice to shut down protest, then the strike weapon is dead.

On the second point. Gourevitch's framing tends to treat "students and faculty" as the campus constituency whose rights matter. This is understandable—they are the most visible protesters—but it is incomplete. Campus workers are the canaries in this coal mine. When Columbia called in the NYPD, the students who got arrested were eventually released and went back to class. The graduate workers and the contingent faculty who joined them face firings that have no due process at all. The food service workers who refused to cross picket lines have already been disciplined. The undocumented workers in campus dining halls live in a state of perpetual exposure that no Title VI argument will ever reach.

This is why I think Gourevitch's prescription, while correct, is too narrow. He wants administrators to apply proportionate, individualized discipline and to distinguish discomfort from harm. Fine. But the deeper problem is that the disciplinary apparatus itself—the deans of students, the campus police, the surveillance cameras, the keycard systems, the conduct codes—was built over the past forty years for the express purpose of producing a docile workforce and a consumerist student body. Reforming its standards will not change what it is for.

The labor answer is collective: campus unions, student-worker solidarity, and concrete material demands that connect the right to protest to the right to organize. The University of California academic workers' strike of 2022, which the UC system tried to suppress in part through "hostile environment" arguments against picketers, is a model. The Rutgers AAUP-AFT strike in 2023 is another. These struggles do not just defend speech; they build the kind of countervailing power without which speech rights are a paper guarantee.

So I would say to Gourevitch: take your argument further. The right to be hostile is the right to inflict the costs of disagreement on those who would otherwise ignore us. That right has always been most fully realized in labor, and labor's institutions—unions, councils, picket lines—are the only mechanism robust enough to defend it against an administrative state, public or private, that would prefer we be polite. Campus protesters who built encampments instinctively understood this. They borrowed forms from Occupy, from the South African divestment movement, and from the long history of working-class self-organization. The next step is to make the connection explicit and organizational. Otherwise, we will spend the next decade arguing about civility while the actual machinery of repression grows.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The conflation of subjective discomfort with objective harm creates a legal precedent that threatens the fundamental viability of labor strikes and union militancy.
  2. 02Campus workers, including graduate employees and contingent faculty, are more vulnerable to the university's disciplinary apparatus than tenured professors or students.
  3. 03The existing administrative and surveillance infrastructure on campuses was specifically designed to enforce docility among workers and students, making simple policy reform insufficient.
  4. 04True protection of speech and protest rights requires the building of countervailing power through unions and student-worker solidarity rather than relying on administrative benevolence.
  5. 05The right to be hostile is essentially the right to impose the costs of disagreement on those in power, a principle historically rooted in working-class self-organization.
