"The responsibility of intellectuals is not merely to choose a side in a political struggle, but to maintain independent judgment and a commitment to truth-seeking that transcends factional loyalty."

There is something unsettling in the ease with which Kelley moves from Chomsky's argument about truth-telling to a celebration of organic intellectuals who "choose sides" and "enter the struggle." The problem is not that this conclusion is wrong but that it sidesteps a harder question: what happens when the struggle itself requires lying? What happens when the movements we are called to support operate with their own silences, their own evasions, their own relationship to truth-telling that sits uncomfortably with the tradition Kelley wants to claim?

Let me be specific. The lead essay builds toward an examination of Palestinian solidarity activism, documenting with admirable precision the repression that critics of Israel face on American campuses. This is essential work, and I do not question the moral seriousness of those who engage in it. But the essay treats the Palestinian cause as simply one among many struggles requiring our solidarity, without examining the epistemological questions that activism raises. How do we determine that what is happening in Gaza constitutes genocide? What evidence would count against this characterization? What counts as a legitimate debate about Israeli policy versus the kind of speech that deserves suppression?

These questions matter because the tradition of truth-telling that Chomsky articulated—and that Kelley says feels "incomplete"—is not merely about speaking truth in the face ofpower. It is about a method: the patient gathering of evidence, the willingness to follow arguments wherever they lead, the acceptance that truth is not determined by political convenience. This tradition has not failed because it was insufficiently political. It has been undermined by the capture of politics by identity, the subordination of inquiry to advocacy, the transformation of intellectuals from truth-seekers into faction fighters.

The organic intellectual framework Kelley employs is particularly problematic in this regard. Gramsci developed this concept in the context of Italian Marxism, where the question was whether intellectuals could overcome their class background to serve the proletarian cause. The answer was that they could not do so as individuals but only as members of a collective project—the Communist Party—that would bind them to the working class and correct theirbourgeois tendencies through internal critique. This is not a reassuring precedent. The communist parties that claimed to represent organic intellectuals frequently became instruments of Stalinist terror, suppressing dissent in the name of proletarian discipline. The history of "organic" intellectuals who chose sides is not merely a history of heroism but also one of complicity in some of the twentieth century's worst atrocities.

I am not suggesting that Kelley endorses such developments, nor that the movements he celebrates are comparable to Stalinism. I am arguing that the framework of "choosing sides" and "casting our lot with the insurgent class" does not by itself guarantee moral correctness. Sides can be chosen for bad reasons, on the basis of incomplete analysis, in service of projects that harm those they claim to help. The history of leftist movements is littered with intellectuals who chose sides enthusiastically and were subsequently proven catastrophically wrong—about the Soviet Union, about the Chinese Cultural Revolution, about the virtues of various liberation movements that turned out to be neither liberating nor movements in any meaningful sense.

This is where Chomsky's original emphasis on truth-telling remains essential, not merely as a complement to political commitment but as its necessary condition. The intellectual who surrenders the capacity for independent judgment—who accepts that truth is determined by factional loyalty—has not become an organic intellectual but has instead become an ideologue, a propagandist, a servant of power in ideological clothing. The damage done by such intellectuals is often greater than that done by those who simply serve the state, precisely because their self-righteousness blinds them to their own complicity.

The current moment demands this kind of intellectual humility more than ever. We face genuine fascism in the Trump administration and its allies. We face genuine genocide, or something close enough to warrant the word, in Gaza. We face a global turn toward authoritarianism that threatens not only human rights but the possibility of reasoned discourse itself. But responding to these threats requires that we maintain our capacity for critical analysis, including analysis of the movements we support. It is not enough to stand with students, to join encampments, to denounce repression. We must also ask hard questions: about the strategies being employed, about the coalitions being built, about the longer-term goals that animate current activism. Otherwise, we risk becoming the organic intellectuals of a project that fails, that harms those it claims to help, that repeats the errors of previous movements while congratulating itself on its unprecedented radicalism.

The answer to fascism is not organic intellectualism but democratic intellectualism: the insistence that all claims to truth are subject to scrutiny, that no movement or faction has a monopoly on correct analysis, that intellectuals must maintain their independence not as a privilege but as a necessity for the kind of collective learning that genuine struggle requires. This does not mean abstaining from politics or failing to take stands. It means taking stands while maintaining the capacity to revise them, to acknowledge error, to learn from those who disagree. It means refusing the comfort of factional loyalty in favor of the harder work of genuine inquiry.

The lead essay concludes with a gesture toward solidarity: "we need to always stand in solidarity and fight for others as if our lives depended on it." This is moving but insufficient. Solidarity is not enough; what is required is solidarity combined with truth-telling, political commitment combined with intellectual humility, the willingness to choose sides combined with the recognition that we might choose wrong. This is the responsibility that the age of fascism actually demands.
