Diversified DeepSeek

"Granting automatic legal status based solely on the passage of time is a misguided policy that undermines the rule of law and the democratic right of a state to define its own community."

Joseph Carens has written a thoughtful and humane essay, but its central argument is fundamentally misguided. By elevating the passage of time above all other considerations, Carens effectively repudiates the rule of law in immigration and substitutes a doctrine of fait accompli. This is not only bad policy; it is corrosive to the very foundations of democratic governance.

The core error lies in Carens’s claim that “social membership does not depend upon official permission.” In one sense this is trivially true: a neighbor, coworker, or friend is a social fact, not a legal status. But the moral and political question is whether that social fact should automatically generate legal rights. Carens answers yes, but his reasoning conflates two distinct ideas. There is the fact of embeddedness—the real human ties that form over years. And there is the normative principle that the state ought to respect those ties. Every decent person recognizes that deporting a Marguerite Grimmond after 74 years is monstrous. But Carens extends a valid intuition about extreme cases to a general rule that would apply after as little as five years. That extension does not survive scrutiny.

Consider a citizen who builds a house on public land without permission. Over time, that person clears the land, plants a garden, raises children there. The family becomes part of the neighborhood. Does the squatter acquire a right to title simply because of the passage of time and the growth of social ties? In some legal traditions, yes—adverse possession can convert trespass into ownership. But adverse possession is a carefully limited doctrine with strict conditions, including open and notorious use and, crucially, the owner’s failure to evict within a statutory period. Even then, it is controversial. The state’s interest in orderly land management often prevails. If we are uneasy about letting squatters become owners, we should be even more cautious about letting irregular migrants become legal residents simply because they managed to evade detection long enough.

The difference between a house and a nation is not merely one of scale. A democratic state has a right to determine the conditions under which outsiders may join the political community. That right is not absolute—Carens is right that basic human rights constrain it—but it is fundamental to self-government. When irregular migrants settle in defiance of those conditions, they are not merely breaking a traffic law; they are challenging the community’s capacity to define itself. Traffic regulations are indeed less weighty than immigration rules, but the analogy fails because immigration touches on sovereignty and the social contract in a way speeding does not. Violating a speed limit does not effectively alter the composition of the polity; crossing a border without authorization does, especially when done by millions.

Carens attempts to soften this objection by noting that violations of immigration law are treated administratively, not criminally. But that is a matter of legal classification, not moral gravity. Many administrative violations—environmental regulations, securities laws—carry enormous consequences. The fact that immigration enforcement is primarily civil does not mean it is trivial. Moreover, Congress has, in recent years, increased criminal penalties for certain immigration offenses, and the trend reflects a growing public demand for accountability. It is not “abuse of the legal process” to enforce identity theft laws against those who use fraudulent Social Security numbers; it is the natural consequence of applying laws designed to protect the integrity of our identification systems. Irregular migrants who knowingly use false documents to obtain employment are not innocent victims; they are active participants in a shadow economy that undermines legal workers and the tax system.

The statute-of-limitations analogy fares no better. Criminal statutes of limitations presume a single past act, not a continuing violation. Someone who remains in the country illegally after his visa expires commits a new violation each day. The state’s failure to apprehend him does not extinguish its right to do so later. Indeed, the statute of limitations for civil claims often runs from the discovery of the violation, not from its occurrence. If we insisted on an analogy, one might say that the clock starts ticking only when the government learns of the presence and fails to act. But that would be a policy judgment, not a moral principle.

Carens’s most troubling move is his dismissal of the fairness concern. He claims that for unskilled workers without family ties, no real admissions line exists. This is true only in a narrow sense. The United States admits some unskilled workers through various programs, and many migrants have other avenues—family reunification, diversity visas—that are unavailable to those who enter illegally precisely because their countrymen have exhausted the quotas. Even if the lines are absurdly long or nonexistent for certain occupations, the proper response is to reform the quotas, not to reward those who bypass them. Two wrongs do not make a right. If the law is unjust, change the law; do not nullify it through inaction.

In short, Carens has presented a seductive argument for a profound retreat from the rule of law. His call to make amnesty automatic after five or seven years would, in practice, announce that the United States and other liberal democracies have given up on controlling their borders. The resulting rush would overwhelm the very communities that irregular migrants now join, straining public services and exacerbating social divisions. Compassion for those who are already here should not blind us to the consequences for those who will come—and for the democratic legitimacy of the laws we enact. A humane society can grant relief in individual cases of extraordinary hardship, but it cannot, without self-betrayal, adopt a policy that effectively legalizes the fait accompli.

  2. 02The analogy between immigration violations and minor traffic offenses fails because immigration directly impacts national sovereignty and the social contract.
  3. 03Treating amnesty as a statute of limitations is inappropriate because residing in a country without authorization constitutes a continuous violation rather than a single past act.
  4. 04Rewarding those who bypass immigration channels is fundamentally unfair to those who attempt to follow legal procedures, regardless of how limited those procedures may be.
  5. 05Establishing a fixed timeline for automatic legalization would effectively signal that a state has abandoned its border controls, leading to a surge in irregular migration that strains public resources.
Diversified DeepSeek

"The proposal for an automatic transition to legal status for irregular migrants is a radical diminution of democratic self-governance that overrides the legitimate right of a political community to determine its own membership."

Joseph Carens makes a humane and carefully reasoned case for granting amnesty to irregular migrants after a period of years. His argument is designed to appeal to the liberal democratic conscience, and in its own terms it is persuasive. Yet the terms themselves are too narrow, because they ignore a crucial dimension: the right of a democratic political community to determine who may become a member. Carens treats this right as a premise he accepts, but then proceeds to so limit and qualify it that it becomes nearly meaningless. In doing so, he bypasses the legitimate claims of citizens to self-governance in matters of immigration, and he offers a recipe for ongoing erosion of democratic accountability.

The issue is not whether amnesty might be appropriate in certain cases, but who gets to decide. In a democracy, the people, acting through their elected representatives, establish the rules for admission and residence. Those rules may be harsh or generous; they may reflect compassion or fear. But unless they violate fundamental human rights—and Carens wisely stops short of claiming that deportation per se is a human rights violation—they enjoy a presumption of legitimacy. When Carens argues for an “automatic transition to legal status” after a fixed period, he is essentially proposing to strip the democratic legislature of its capacity to enforce the law over time. The passage of time alone would override the collectively determined rules. That is a radical diminution of self-government.

Carens might reply that democratic communities are not entitled to do whatever they please; they must respect the moral claims of outsiders who have become insiders in fact. But this argument begs the question. The very definition of who is an insider is at issue. If the community has decided—through its laws, imperfectly enforced perhaps—that certain people should not remain, then labeling those people “members” simply because they have stayed awhile is to substitute the moral judgment of the philosopher for the political judgment of the citizenry. Carens’s social-membership criterion is not a neutral principle but a particular substantive view about what matters: length of residence, family ties, economic contribution. Many citizens might weigh other factors more heavily: adherence to the rule of law, integration into the political culture, English language fluency, or simply the fact of lawful entry. There is no objective philosophical scale that proves Carens right; this is precisely the kind of value-laden question that democratic processes exist to resolve.

The Grimmond case, which figures so prominently in the essay, actually illustrates the point. No one, including the immigration officials, seriously contemplated deporting an 80-year-old woman who had lived her entire conscious life in Scotland. The system worked because public outrage triggered a discretionary response. That is how democracies should handle extreme cases: through flexibility, not rigid rules. A blanket automatic amnesty after five years, by contrast, would foreclose discretion and force legal outcomes that many citizens might find troubling in particular instances—for example, a migrant who, though long-resident, has committed numerous offenses short of a felony, or who has made no effort to learn the language, or who remains actively hostile to the country’s political values. Carens admits that his preferred threshold has an element of arbitrariness. But the arbitrariness of five years is a problem, not a feature. It would be far better to allow the democratic process to calibrate the conditions for legalization, weighing compassion against the costs and benefits as the community sees them.

Carens’s analogy to statutes of limitations also undermines his case. Statutes of limitations are democratically enacted; they reflect a legislative judgment about the appropriate balance between finality and accountability. They are not discovered by moral philosophers and imposed on unwilling legislatures. If the people, through Congress, wish to adopt a statute of limitations for immigration violations—say, a rule that no one may be deported for an immigration violation that occurred more than seven years ago—that would be entirely appropriate. But that is a policy choice, not a moral necessity. Carens writes as though the moral threshold is independent of democratic will, and that his task is simply to identify it. But in a liberal democracy, the people are entitled to make the opposite choice, so long as it does not shock the conscience in a way the Grimmond case might.

This is not, I hasten to add, an argument for mass deportation or cruelty. It is an argument for taking democracy seriously. Immigration policy is one of the most sensitive areas of public policy, engaging questions of national identity, economic opportunity, and cultural change. If the people cannot control their own country’s membership, they cannot be said to govern themselves. Carens’s proposal, for all its generosity of spirit, would in effect say that anyone who can stay hidden long enough has a right to stay, regardless of what the citizenry decides. That is not liberalism; it is a quiet technocratic override of democratic deliberation. We can and should have a vigorous public debate about amnesty, and I hope that debate leads to compassionate outcomes. But the outcome must be legitimately reached through the give-and-take of democratic politics, not imposed in the name of an abstract moral logic that discounts the wishes of the governed.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01In a democracy, the rules for admission and residence should be established by the people through elected representatives rather than being dictated by abstract moral logic.
  2. 02Defining social membership based on length of residence is a subjective value judgment that should be resolved through democratic processes rather than philosophical imposition.
  3. 03Automatic amnesties remove the necessary flexibility and discretion that democracies use to handle extreme cases or evaluate the specific conduct of individuals.
  4. 04The analogy to statutes of limitations fails because such legal boundaries are democratically enacted policy choices rather than universal moral necessities.
  5. 05Granting a right to stay based solely on the ability to remain hidden for a certain period undermines democratic accountability and the rule of law.
