"The federal government should withdraw from prosecuting local drug cases to end the disproportionate harm of mass incarceration on disadvantaged communities, focusing instead on civil rights and large-scale interstate crimes."

Whether drug enforcement should be left to the states depends on whose experience we put at the center. For many poor black and Latino communities, federal drug prosecution has not meant protection; it has meant devastating sentences, aggressive policing partnerships, and families permanently altered by incarceration. From that perspective, the federal government should sharply withdraw from local drug cases and use its power instead to reduce the damage its own policies helped create.

The war on drugs has never been enforced evenly. Drug use exists across racial and class lines, but arrests and imprisonment have fallen disproportionately on disadvantaged neighborhoods. Federal prosecution intensifies the consequences. Once a case is adopted federally, defendants face harsher sentencing ranges, mandatory minimums, broad conspiracy liability and prosecutors with extraordinary plea-bargaining leverage. A person at the edge of a drug operation may be punished for quantities handled by others. The result is not simply law enforcement; it is social policy by incarceration.

This is why the Justice Department’s recent promise of a less severe approach matters. It acknowledges that something has gone wrong. But guidance to prosecutors is not enough. As long as federal authorities can choose to transform a street-level case into a federal case, the risk of arbitrary and unequal punishment remains. Local drug possession and low-level sales should ordinarily stay out of federal court altogether.

The contrast with marijuana legalization makes the inequity more glaring. In Colorado and Washington, entrepreneurs are preparing to profit from a market that has sent many others to jail. In states without legalization, people will still be arrested for conduct that is becoming a regulated business elsewhere. That is not an argument for federal raids in Denver. It is an argument for federal reform: at minimum, marijuana should not remain in the same legal category as the most dangerous drugs, and prior low-level marijuana convictions deserve attention.

Still, “leave it to the states” is not by itself a racial justice program. Some states have been worse than the federal government. Some local police departments use drug enforcement as a pretext for stops and searches. The federal government has an important civil-rights role: investigating discriminatory policing, collecting data, funding public defenders, supporting treatment, and encouraging alternatives to incarceration. It should use federal money to reward health-based approaches like Vermont’s, not to subsidize arrest numbers.

The right answer is selective disengagement. Federal prosecutors should handle serious interstate trafficking, violence, weapons, corruption and large-scale money laundering. They should not be the hammer for addiction, poverty or neighborhood disorder. If Washington is going to lead, it should lead away from mass incarceration and toward repair. Local drug cases should not be federal cases unless there is a truly federal harm.
