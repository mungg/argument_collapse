Vanilla GPT

"A marriage should be ended when it ceases to be a partnership of mutual safety and dignity and instead becomes a source of destruction, contempt, or one-sided labor."

A marriage is not over because it is disappointing. Every marriage is, at times. The fantasy that the right partner will spare us loneliness, boredom, sexual frustration or the need to apologize has done real damage. But the opposite fantasy — that endurance is always noble — is just as dangerous.

A definitive breaking point is not ordinary unhappiness; it is the collapse of safety, good faith or mutual responsibility. Physical violence, credible threats, coercive control, sexual violation or intimidation should end the presumption that the marriage deserves preservation. In those cases, the urgent question is not whether the couple has “tried hard enough,” but how the endangered partner can leave safely.

There are other breaking points that may be less visible but still decisive: an untreated addiction that repeatedly endangers the family; chronic infidelity accompanied by deception rather than repair; financial recklessness hidden from the other spouse; or a persistent refusal to engage in counseling, accountability or change. A marriage can survive a betrayal. It cannot survive indefinitely when only one person is doing the work of keeping it alive.

The crucial distinction is between pain that is part of growth and pain that is evidence of destruction. Couples often pass through grim seasons — after childbirth, illness, unemployment, grief, or years of accumulated resentment. In such cases, patience, therapy and renewed habits of kindness may be warranted. But contempt, humiliation and fear are not rough patches. Research on marital stability has long suggested that contempt is especially corrosive; common sense says the same. A home organized around dread is not a sanctuary.

Children are sometimes invoked as a reason to stay no matter what. They are better served by witnessing respect, repair and emotional steadiness than by being raised inside a war zone.

The question is not whether a marriage is imperfect. All are. The question is whether both people remain willing and able to protect each other’s dignity. When that answer is no, and remains no, leaving is not failure. It is recognition.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Physical violence, coercive control, and threats of harm constitute definitive breaking points that should end the presumption of preserving a marriage.
  2. 02A marriage cannot survive indefinitely when one partner persistently refuses to engage in accountability, repair, or the work of maintaining the relationship.
  3. 03Chronic betrayals such as untreated addiction, hidden financial recklessness, or deceptive infidelity undermine the essential foundation of good faith.
  4. 04The presence of contempt, humiliation, and fear distinguishes a destructive environment from the ordinary, survivable unhappiness of an imperfect marriage.
  5. 05Children are better served by witnessing emotional steadiness and respect than by being raised in a high-conflict environment or a 'war zone.'
  6. 06The ultimate indicator that a marriage is over is when one or both partners are no longer willing or able to protect each other's dignity.

"While marriage requires navigating imperfection and disappointment, it should be ended when the relationship becomes unsafe, lacks mutual respect, or fails to improve despite good-faith efforts."

There is no universal breaking point, but there are some clarifying questions that can cut through the fog of a struggling marriage. The first is whether the relationship is unsafe. Physical violence, sustained emotional abuse, or coercive control are not problems to be worked through with better communication; they are reasons to leave, and the cultural reluctance to say so plainly has kept too many people in danger for too long.

Short of that, the harder cases involve what John Gottman has called the "Four Horsemen"—contempt, criticism, defensiveness, and stonewalling. Contempt in particular is the strongest predictor of divorce in his research, and a marriage in which one or both partners no longer respects the other is in graver condition than one marked merely by conflict. Frequent fighting is survivable; settled disdain usually isn't.

A useful test is whether both partners are still willing to do the work—therapy, honest conversation, behavioral change. If only one person is trying, the marriage is effectively already over; it just hasn't been named yet. Repeated betrayal without remorse, or addiction that the addicted partner refuses to treat, falls into the same category.

The op-ed that prompted this debate is right that we marry imperfect strangers and that romantic disappointment is not, by itself, grounds for divorce. But its argument can curdle into a counsel of endurance for its own sake. The point of staying is not to prove resilience; it is to build something good. When the relationship is actively diminishing both people, and when good-faith efforts to change that have failed, staying is not a virtue.

The op-ed’s insistence that we must abandon romantic perfectionism is a necessary corrective, but it risks obscuring a harder truth: some marriages are not simply imperfect; they are destructive. The line between a challenging union and one that should end is not found in fleeting unhappiness or routine disagreements. It is found in the entrenched, contemptuous patterns that corrode the very selfhood of the partners.

Decades of research by psychologist John Gottman offer a powerful framework. His work identifies four communication styles that signal a marriage’s collapse—criticism, defensiveness, stonewalling, and, most lethally, contempt. Contempt, expressed through mockery, sneering, or hostile name-calling, is not merely a sign of frustration. It is a statement that one partner regards the other as beneath respect. Once that sentiment becomes routine, the relationship ceases to be a source of support and mutates into an engine of emotional degradation. This is not a rough patch to be weathered with mature acceptance; it is a foundational rupture.

A definitive breaking point, then, is not a single dramatic betrayal. It emerges when attempts at repair—therapy, honest conversation, genuine effort—repeatedly fail to shift a dynamic of disrespect or abuse. Physical violence, of course, is an immediate and non-negotiable end. But sustained emotional cruelty and the withering of any shared vision for the future are equally final. When the union persistently diminishes your capacity for joy, erodes your self-worth, or harms your children by modeling toxic conflict, the moral obligation shifts from preserving the marriage to protecting the human beings within it. The wisdom of accepting a “good enough” partner must not be confused with the folly of accepting a soul-destroying one.

It can justify both heroic perseverance and quiet resignation, which is precisely why the recent debate has produced such heat and so little light. This is different from the temporary retreats that occur in every long marriage. The distinction lies in consistency and reciprocity: is this a season of difficulty that both partners are navigating, or has one partner become an observer in their own family?

Children should not be the deciding factor in staying or leaving, but they are often the deciding factor in how departure happens. The breaking point, then, is not the existence of conflict but the presence of contempt—Gottman's research on marriage predictors remains instructive here—because contempt signals that respect has been abandoned, and without respect, no amount of therapy or tolerance can reconstruct partnership.

Alain de Botton’s widely shared essay rightly diagnoses a modern cultural affliction: the Romantic illusion that a perfect soulmate exists to seamlessly fulfill all our needs. By dismantling this myth, he offers a pragmatic, even comforting, defense of enduring the inevitable friction of sharing a life with another deeply flawed human being. However, while embracing imperfection is an essential skill for marital endurance, it should never become a blank check for enduring abuse or profound misery.

The definitive breaking point of a marriage is not the inevitable discovery that you have married the "wrong" person, but rather the realization that the relationship has structurally eroded your sense of safety, agency, and self-worth. Relationship science provides clear markers for this boundary. Dr. John Gottman’s decades of observational research on marital stability famously identifies "contempt" as the single greatest predictor of divorce. When everyday frustration curdles into genuine disgust, and a partner’s fundamental character is routinely demeaned rather than constructively criticized, the marital bond is usually irreparably severed.

Beyond this emotional toxicity, absolute deal-breakers must remain clear: physical or psychological abuse, unrepentant infidelity, and severe, untreated addiction. In these scenarios, the institution of marriage becomes a trap rather than a sanctuary. Staying for the sake of vows—or, as is often argued, for the children—frequently perpetuates a cycle of trauma rather than demonstrating resilience.

Giving up on a marriage should rarely be an impulsive response to a dormant phase or a heated argument. Yet, we must carefully distinguish between the ordinary sorrow of unmet romantic ideals and the acute damage of a destructive dynamic. When a relationship persistently demands the sacrifice of your fundamental well-being, walking away is not a failure of commitment; it is an act of necessary self-preservation.

"A marriage is over when staying requires the permanent abandonment of one's self and the erasure of one's identity, conscience, or sanity."

A relationship is over when staying requires the permanent abandonment of the self. That may sound individualistic, but it is not the same as saying one should leave whenever one feels constrained. Marriage always constrains. It asks people to compromise, to be bored sometimes, to apologize when pride resists, to consider another person before acting. Those constraints can enlarge us. They teach loyalty and patience.

But there is another kind of constraint: the gradual erasure of one’s conscience, voice, friendships, body, ambitions or sanity. Some people wake up after years of marriage and realize they have become an edited version of themselves, acceptable only if they do not ask too much, shine too brightly, complain too loudly or need anything inconvenient. No vows require that.

This is especially important in relationships that are not visibly catastrophic. There may be no violence, no affair, no addiction. Friends may say, “But he’s nice,” or “She’s a good mother.” Yet the private reality is a life of emotional starvation. One spouse tries to talk and is stonewalled. One wants children and the other has been pretending to agree. One carries all domestic labor while the other calls it “helping” to do a chore. One partner’s career is always the one sacrificed. Slowly, the marriage becomes a machine for producing one person’s comfort at the other’s expense.

The first response should be clarity, not flight. Say what must change. Ask for counseling. Name the stakes. Give the other person a real chance to participate in a different marriage. But if the answer is indifference, ridicule or temporary improvement followed by the same old bargain, the moral question changes.

Leaving is not always a rejection of commitment. Sometimes it is a refusal to confuse commitment with disappearance. A good marriage asks people to give themselves to each other. A bad one asks one person to give himself or herself away.

"A marriage is over when contempt permanently replaces mutual respect, making it impossible to view the partner as a person worthy of care or repair."

The most reliable sign that a relationship is over is not anger. Anger can mean that the parties still believe something is at stake. The more ominous sign is contempt: the settled conviction that the other person is ridiculous, inferior or unworthy of care.

Researchers who study marriage, including John Gottman, have long emphasized the destructive power of contempt. That finding matches common sense. Couples can recover from conflict when they still grant each other dignity. They can argue about money, sex, in-laws or parenting and still say, “This is my person.” But when eye-rolling, mockery, disgust and character assassination become the normal language of the household, the marriage turns into a small theater of degradation.

The breaking point, then, is not a single fight or even a single betrayal. It is the moment when one or both partners no longer want repair, only vindication. Counseling depends on the willingness to be curious: What did I do? What did you hear? What are we protecting? Contempt has no curiosity. It already knows the answer: “You are the problem.” Once that view hardens, every apology looks tactical, every gesture looks fake and every grievance becomes evidence in a permanent prosecution.

Still, contempt should be treated as a warning before it is treated as a verdict. People become contemptuous when they feel abandoned, overburdened or unheard. A couple that catches it early may be able to replace scorn with grief, and grief with negotiation. But if months or years of effort reveal that respect cannot be restored, ending the marriage may be the least destructive option.

A decent marriage requires more than passion and less than constant happiness. It requires the basic moral discipline of seeing the other person as fully human. When that has disappeared and cannot be rebuilt, the relationship has lost the foundation on which all the other repairs depend.

"A marriage should end when its continuation requires sustained dishonesty and it no longer serves to enlarge or support the growth of the individuals within it."

The framing of this debate — "when is it time to give up?" — quietly assumes that giving up is the alternative to a stable continuation. But for many marriages, particularly those with children, the real alternatives are: a difficult, mutual transformation; a continued slow decay; or a divorce that is itself a transformation, not a termination. None of these is "giving up."

I think the more useful question is: what does this marriage owe to the people inside it, and what do they owe back? A marriage exists to enlarge the people in it — to make them more themselves, more capable of love, more able to face the world. When it stops doing that for both partners, and when sustained, honest effort fails to restart it, then continuing the marriage becomes a kind of fraud against everyone involved, including the children we often imagine we are protecting by staying.

Children, in fact, are usually worse off in a household defined by contempt or coldness than in two functional separate households. The folk wisdom that one should "stay for the kids" deserves more skepticism than it usually receives. Kids absorb the emotional weather of the home; they do not need their parents to be married, they need their parents to be reasonably whole.

So my answer to "what should be the definitive breaking point?" is that the breaking point arrives when continuing the marriage requires sustained dishonesty — when staying together means pretending, in front of each other and the children, that something is alive which is not.

That standard is harder than "abuse" or "infidelity" because it asks something of the people inside the marriage that those external markers do not: it asks them to be honest about what they actually feel and what they are actually doing. Most marriages that end probably should end. Many that continue probably shouldn't.

"A marriage should end when the relationship requires the unacceptable stifling of a partner's personal growth or core identity."

The traditional view of marriage demands that we stay together until "death do us part," treating almost any dissolution as a tragic moral failure. But a more modern, honest assessment recognizes that human beings are not static creatures. We evolve, and sometimes we evolve in fundamentally incompatible directions. The definitive breaking point in a marriage should be the realization that staying together requires the unacceptable stifling of one or both partners' personal growth.

People frequently marry in their twenties or thirties with a specific set of values, goals, and worldviews. By the time they reach their forties or fifties, they may be entirely different people. This isn't a failure of character; it is the natural, healthy trajectory of a life well-lived. If one partner's evolution leads them toward a life that makes the other miserable, or if the relationship becomes a cage that prevents authentic self-actualization, it is time to part ways amicably.

We do ourselves a massive disservice by insisting that enduring a suffocating arrangement is somehow noble. The viral op-ed on marrying the "wrong" person assumes that all alternatives are equally flawed, so we might as well stay put and suffer quietly. But there is a vast difference between compromising on your partner's annoying household habits and compromising on your core identity.

A marriage is over when the price of admission is who you are. When staying in the relationship requires you to shrink yourself, abandon your core principles, or permanently sideline your most deeply held ambitions, the relationship has outlived its purpose.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Human beings naturally evolve over time, and partners may develop fundamentally incompatible values or worldviews that were not present at the start of the marriage.
  2. 02Enduring a suffocating or restrictive arrangement is not a noble act and should not be treated as a moral necessity.
  3. 03There is a critical distinction between compromising on minor behavioral habits and compromising on one's authentic self-actualization.
  4. 04A relationship has outlived its purpose if staying requires an individual to permanently sideline their core principles or ambitions.
