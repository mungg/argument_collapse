Diversified DeepSeek

"The Democratic Party's failure is rooted in a lack of material economic delivery rather than just poor messaging, necessitating a bold redistributive agenda that prioritizes working-class interests over the donor class."

The Bonica and Grumbach essay is a timely and rigorous demolition of the most shopworn clichés in Democratic strategy. Yet for all its passion and data, it embodies a subtle form of the very elite myopia it criticizes: the assumption that the key to electoral success is the party’s message and positioning, rather than the raw material conditions of voters and the structural dynamics of capitalism. An anti-corruption frame may be popular, but it cannot substitute for a redistributive economic program that materially transforms people’s lives. The Democrats don’t have a message problem; they have a delivery problem, rooted in their unwillingness to confront the donor class that funds them.

The authors correctly note that the 2024 election saw a devastating turnout gap, and they argue that a mobilization strategy around anti-corruption could close it. But the main reason turnout collapsed among Democrats—especially young, Black, and working-class voters—was not that they were insufficiently energized by a corruption frame; it’s that after two years of Democratic control (2021-23) and a longer period of effective governance earlier, their lives hadn’t improved enough for them to feel that voting was worth the effort. Wages were still stagnant after inflation, rent was crushing, medical debt was omnipresent, and the much-touted Inflation Reduction Act had created some green energy jobs but hadn’t touched the daily pain of ordinary people. The student debt relief was modest and tied up in courts. The expanded child tax credit had expired because the party couldn’t hold its own moderates. In such a context, telling people “the system is rigged” and “we’ll fight corruption” rings hollow: they already know it’s rigged, and they don’t believe the Democratic Party will actually fight the powerful because they’ve watched it fail to do so every time.

Real anti-establishment credibility requires taking on power, not just denouncing it. The party’s economic policy must be anti-corporate and pro-worker in ways that are felt immediately. A federal $15 minimum wage? Still not done. Universal pre-K? Biden’s plan died in the Senate. Medicare for All, or at least a serious public option? Nowhere near, blocked by the party’s own rank and file. The authors mention “championing popular measures like banning congressional stock trading,” which is good but utterly trivial compared to the demand for economic justice. If the Democratic Party were serious about building anti-establishment credibility, it would make its central legislative promises things like a job guarantee, a massive investment in social housing, free public college, and a wealth tax to fund them—and it would force every member to go on record, over and over, until the public understood who was blocking it. Instead, the party often triangulates away from its most popular economic policies, not because they’re unpopular, but because they threaten the donor class that includes many of its own major funders. As long as the party is perceived—correctly—as being in the pocket of the very elites it now wants to attack, the anti-corruption message will be just so much noise.

The essay’s own evidence should push it further left. The authors find that progressive candidates slightly boost turnout among registered Democrats. That’s a tantalizing hint that an unapologetic left economic agenda could energize the base. But they then swerve into an anti-corruption frame that is largely procedural and cross-partisan, rather than a working-class agenda that is fundamentally adversarial toward capital. Why not just advocate for the economic platform that actually motivates the base? The answer, one suspects, is that the authors are themselves constrained by the discourse of elite political science, which treats anything too explicitly socialist as unrealistic. Yet the moment calls for a politics that names the class enemy: the billionaires who have rigged the economy, not just a few bad actors who broke rules. “Corruption” is too easy a target; it allows the focus to remain on illegal acts rather than on the legalized pillage that is the normal functioning of American capitalism.

Consider the authors’ own invocation of successful anti-authoritarian movements abroad. They mention Latin American and European cases, but they omit the most salient feature of those movements: they were rooted in class-based, anti-neoliberal struggles. Lula’s Brazil was successful because of Bolsa Família and real wage increases, not just anti-corruption rhetoric. The U.S. left has its own tradition of this: the Populists, the CIO, the civil rights movement that demanded economic justice alongside voting rights. The Democratic Party abandoned that tradition in the 1970s and 80s when it embraced neoliberalism, deregulation, and free trade. Recovering it requires more than an anti-corruption frame; it requires a full-scale repudiation of the economic policies that immiserated the working class and fueled the right’s rise.

Some will say this is exactly the kind of “extreme” platform that popularists warn against. But the polls tell a different story. Majorities of Americans support Medicare for All, a job guarantee, higher taxes on the rich, and massive infrastructure spending. These are not fringe positions; they are the popular consensus that billionaires and their political allies have successfully kept off the agenda. The problem isn’t that these positions are unpopular; it’s that the political system is undemocratic. The essay’s anti-corruption frame would be far more powerful if it were directly tied to this reality: the system is corrupt because the will of the people is thwarted by moneyed interests. The solution is not just cleaner elections but a transfer of power from capital to labor. That is a fight Democrats have been too timid to wage, and it’s the fight that could actually rebuild the multiracial working-class coalition that is the only durable foundation for democratic self-governance.

In short, the authors provide a valuable debunking but an inadequate alternative. The path forward is not to replace one message with another but to embrace a politics of material redistribution so bold that it puts the party on a collision course with its own donors. Only then will ordinary people believe that the Democratic Party is on their side. And that belief, not any cunning frame, is what turns out voters and defeats authoritarians.

Diversified MiniMax

"The Democratic Party must move beyond mere anti-corruption framing to embrace a genuinely radical populist economic program that fundamentally challenges the structural status quo."

The lead essay makes a compelling case against moderation, but it stops short of the more radical conclusion the evidence actually supports: that incrementalism itself is the problem, and that Democrats need a genuinely populist economic program that breaks from the bipartisan consensus of recent decades.

Bonica and Grumbach correctly identify that popularism—tailoring messages to poll-tested positions—fails because it confuses measurement with leadership. They are right that voters seek authenticity and direction, not focus-grouped triangulation. But they understate what follows from this insight.

The Democratic Party has spent decades triangulating toward a technocratic center that serves institutional interests more than popular ones. The result is that the party has lost the ability to articulate a genuinely different vision of how the economy should work. When Democrats talk about "kitchen table issues," they typically mean small-bore proposals that do not threaten the fundamental structure of an economy that increasingly serves capital over labor.

Consider what a genuinely populist economic agenda would look like: universal healthcare as a right, not a privilege; wages tied to productivity; antitrust enforcement that breaks up concentrated industries; housing policy that attacks the landlord class; industrial policy that rebuilds American manufacturing. These are not moderate positions—they are popular positions that the professional class has convinced itself voters will not accept.

The authors are correct that turnout matters more than persuasion. But turnout depends on offering something worth voting for. The anti-corruption frame is useful but insufficient. Voters who have given up on the system are not primarily motivated by corruption as an abstract concept; they are motivated by concrete economic anxiety and the sense that the system is rigged against them.

A party that wants to mobilize non-voters must offer policies that would actually change their material circumstances. The research on persuasion is correct that voters respond to valence more than position—but valence is shaped by perceived competence and direction, not just competence at managing the status quo.

Democrats should embrace economic populism, not because it is poll-tested but because it is right. The evidence from successful movements suggests that bold programs inspire participation in ways that careful positioning never can. The choice is not between moderation and populism; it is between a party that believes in something and one that merely believes in winning.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The party's current focus on 'kitchen table issues' consists of small-bore, technocratic proposals that fail to address the underlying power imbalance between capital and labor.
  2. 02Voters are motivated more by concrete material improvements to their economic circumstances than by abstract concepts like government corruption.
  3. 03Turnout among disengaged voters can only be secured by offering bold, non-moderate policies—such as universal healthcare and aggressive antitrust enforcement—that provide a vision worth voting for.
  4. 04Economic populism creates a sense of authenticity and direction that focus-grouped triangulation and incrementalism cannot achieve.
  5. 05The Democratic professional class has incorrectly convinced itself that popular economic policies are electorally unviable.
Diversified Gemini

"While the lead essay correctly identifies the failure of political moderation, its proposed 'anti-corruption' strategy is a hollow procedural fix that fails to address the material class conflicts necessary to defeat authoritarianism."

In "How Not to Defeat Authoritarianism," Adam Bonica and Jake Grumbach elegantly dismantle the myth of the median voter, exposing the bankruptcy of a Democratic strategy that relies on endless triangulation. Their critique of "popularism" is entirely correct: calibrating policy to a mythical center demobilizes the working-class base while failing to win over an increasingly radicalized right. However, their proposed solution—a vague, cross-partisan "anti-corruption" crusade—is a startlingly hollow substitute for what is actually required to rebuild a mass democratic movement.

The authors rightfully point out that voters are angry at a "rigged system." They cite the Clarence Thomas scandals and congressional stock trading as flashpoints of elite corruption. But anti-corruption is a procedural critique, not a substantive political vision. It is the language of good-government liberalism, not a mobilizing ideology capable of countering right-wing nationalism. Why? Because the modern Republican Party has already completely co-opted the language of anti-corruption. Donald Trump’s entire political brand is built on the assertion that the "Deep State" is corrupt. When Democrats attack corruption, Republicans simply point to the legal prosecutions of Trump as evidence of a corrupt, weaponized justice system. In a post-truth media ecosystem, "anti-corruption" is a dead end.

What is required is not a procedural battle over ethics, but a material battle over class. The authors touch briefly on "price-gouging families" and "billionaires," but they shy away from the structural reality: the Democratic Party has hemorrhage working-class voters—across racial lines—because it has refused to challenge the fundamental tenets of neoliberal capitalism.

The true vulnerability of authoritarianism is not its corruption, but its service to capital. Authoritarians use cultural grievance to mask their transfer of wealth to the oligarchy. To defeat them, Democrats must re-center class conflict. They need an aggressively populist economic agenda: universal healthcare, massive wealth taxation, robust labor rights, and a total repudiation of corporate trade monopolies. The voters who stayed home in 2024 did not do so merely because they were disgusted by stock-trading scandals; they stayed home because neither party offered a credible alternative to their material immiseration. You cannot "reframe the battlefield" without addressing the economic soil in which authoritarianism grows.

  2. 02Anti-corruption is a procedural critique of 'good-government liberalism' rather than a substantive, mobilizing political ideology.
  3. 03The Democratic Party's loss of working-class voters is rooted in its refusal to challenge neoliberal capitalism rather than a lack of focus on ethics scandals.
  4. 04To defeat authoritarianism, Democrats must replace vague anti-establishment rhetoric with an aggressive populist economic agenda that addresses the material immiseration of voters.
