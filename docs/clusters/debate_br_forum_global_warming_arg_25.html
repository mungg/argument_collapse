"Kerry Emanuel’s call for urgent climate action is based on an overreliance on flawed computer models and a failure to account for natural climate variability."

In "Can We Stop Global Warming?", Kerry Emanuel presents a compelling and articulate defense of the prevailing scientific consensus regarding anthropogenic climate change. As one of the preeminent scholars on hurricane dynamics, his voice carries significant weight. However, while Emanuel’s summary of atmospheric physics is fundamentally sound, his essay suffers from a pervasive overconfidence in our ability to model the climate's future and a troubling tendency to attribute recent, localized weather phenomena to global anthropogenic forcing. A rigorous examination of the scientific data, particularly concerning ocean cycles and model uncertainties, suggests a far more cautious approach than the global "insurance policy" Emanuel recommends.

At the heart of Emanuel’s argument—and indeed, at the heart of the forthcoming 2007 IPCC report he anticipates—is a profound reliance on General Circulation Models (GCMs). These computer models are the basis for the projection that the globe will warm another 3 to 7°F over the next century. But as any atmospheric scientist knows, these models are only as good as the assumptions programmed into them, and they struggle immensely with the most crucial components of the climate system: clouds and water vapor.

Emanuel himself touches upon this vulnerability when he discusses the exponential rise of water vapor with temperature. He notes that "condensing water vapor heats the atmosphere, and in the grand scheme of things, this must be compensated by radiative heat loss." He admits this creates a "conundrum" for the models. Yet, he breezes past this immense theoretical hurdle by stating that "models resolve this conundrum by making it rain harder in places that are already wet." This is not a resolution; it is a parameterization. It is a mathematical patch applied to a physical process we do not fully understand.

Water vapor is the most abundant and potent greenhouse gas in the atmosphere. The catastrophic warming scenarios projected by the IPCC do not come primarily from the direct radiative forcing of carbon dioxide; a doubling of CO2 by itself would only produce a modest warming of about 1°C. The alarming projections of 3 to 7°F (or higher) depend almost entirely on highly uncertain positive feedback loops, primarily the assumption that a slight warming from CO2 will lead to more water vapor, which will cause more warming, in a runaway effect. Yet, eminent atmospheric physicists, such as Richard Lindzen of MIT, have proposed compelling counter-theories. Lindzen's "iris hypothesis," for example, suggests that as the tropics warm, the area covered by high cirrus clouds decreases, allowing more infrared radiation to escape into space—a powerful negative feedback loop that would stabilize the climate and render the models overly alarmist. The fact that the climate has remained relatively stable for thousands of years strongly implies that negative feedbacks dominate the system, contrary to the runaway positive feedbacks programmed into the IPCC models.

This overconfidence in modeling extends to Emanuel’s most prominent area of expertise: hurricanes. He claims that "hurricanes are responding to warming sea surface temperatures faster than we originally expected," citing a 60 percent increase in the power output of North Atlantic tropical cyclones since the 1970s. He explicitly links this to the 2005 hurricane season and the devastating impact of Katrina.

This assertion is fiercely contested within the meteorological community. In fact, the attribution of recent hurricane activity to anthropogenic global warming was the precise issue that prompted Dr. Chris Landsea, a leading hurricane researcher, to resign from the IPCC in 2005. The primary driver of the recent uptick in Atlantic hurricane activity is not a linear response to CO2, but rather the Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation (AMO). The AMO is a naturally occurring oceanic cycle that shifts North Atlantic temperatures between warm and cool phases every 25 to 40 years.

The baseline Emanuel uses—the 1970s—coincides perfectly with the nadir of the AMO's cool phase, a period when Atlantic hurricane activity was anomalously low. Starting in exactly 1995, the AMO shifted into its warm phase, which naturally resulted in warmer sea surface temperatures and a sudden, dramatic increase in major hurricanes. To compare the highly active 2005 season to the quiet 1970s and attribute the difference entirely to global warming is a textbook example of conflating natural cyclic variability with a long-term anthropogenic trend. When we look further back into the historical record, to the mid-20th century when the AMO was last in a warm phase, we see hurricane seasons that rival the intensity of the past decade, long before greenhouse gases could have had a significant impact.

Furthermore, while Emanuel lists facts that are "not in dispute," such as the 1.2°F increase in global temperature over the past century, he glosses over the temporal disconnect between human emissions and this warming. Most of the early 20th-century warming occurred prior to 1940, before the post-WWII boom in fossil fuel use that drove the vast majority of anthropogenic CO2 emissions. Following 1940, despite accelerating emissions, global temperatures actually cooled for three decades—a phenomenon that models struggle to replicate without heavily adjusting the assumed cooling effects of sulfate aerosols.

None of this is to say that the greenhouse effect is not real, or that human activity is not having some impact on the climate. CO2 is a greenhouse gas, and its concentrations are increasing. However, the magnitude of the human influence, and the severity of the expected impacts, are subjects of legitimate, robust scientific debate. The IPCC process, which Emanuel defends, has become increasingly politicized, frequently prioritizing a unified, alarming message over the nuanced, heavily caveated reality of the primary literature.

Emanuel concludes that we should treat emissions reductions as an "insurance policy." But we must evaluate this policy rationally. If the climate sensitivity to CO2 is on the lower end of estimates—due to negative cloud feedbacks and natural oceanic buffers—then the "insurance premium" of completely restructuring the global energy economy will vastly exceed the actual damages of a slightly warmer world. Before we commit trillions of dollars and artificially constrain the global economy, we must demand a higher standard of empirical evidence. We must ensure our models accurately reflect the profound complexity of the natural climate system, rather than just the assumptions of their creators.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Climate models are fundamentally unreliable because they rely on mathematical patches for physical processes, such as cloud formation and water vapor, that scientists do not fully understand.
  2. 02The catastrophic warming scenarios projected by models depend on unproven positive feedback loops that ignore likely stabilizing negative feedback mechanisms.
  3. 03The recent increase in Atlantic hurricane activity is more accurately attributed to natural oceanic cycles like the Atlantic Multidecadal Oscillation than to anthropogenic global warming.
  4. 04Historical temperature trends, such as the warming prior to 1940 and the subsequent cooling period, do not align neatly with the timeline of human carbon emissions.
  5. 05The economic cost of restructuring the global energy economy may far exceed the actual risks of climate change if climate sensitivity to CO2 is lower than alarmist models suggest.
