"While the 'Economics for Inclusive Prosperity' manifesto correctly identifies the failures of neoliberalism, it remains fundamentally flawed because it fails to challenge the unsustainable pursuit of infinite economic growth on a finite planet."

The manifesto "Economics After Neoliberalism" represents a welcome, if belated, awakening within the mainstream economics profession. Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman concede that the market fundamentalism of the past four decades has been a disaster, generating staggering inequality and social fracture. Yet, in their quest to design an "Economics for Inclusive Prosperity," they remain stubbornly tethered to the most dangerous fundamentalism of all: the unquestioned pursuit of economic growth on a finite planet.

Writing in 2019, just months after the IPCC’s terrifying report on the consequences of 1.5 degrees of warming, the authors’ treatment of the ecological crisis is dangerously inadequate. They mention climate change twice in passing—once as a source of "disruption" and once to suggest that economists support "putting a steep price on carbon." This framing reveals that, despite their empirical pivots, these economists still view ecological breakdown as a mere "market failure" (an unpriced externality) to be corrected, rather than a hard biophysical limit to the entire macroeconomic project.

The authors boast that their framework stands as an alternative to neoliberalism, but they share neoliberalism’s core objective: "prosperity," which in the lexicon of mainstream economics is inextricably linked to expanding GDP. They believe we can have "prosperity-enhancing" government expansion and that we are operating "well inside the justice-efficiency frontier."

This is an illusion. The thermodynamic realities of the Earth system do not care about the justice-efficiency frontier. Ecological economists have demonstrated for decades that absolute decoupling of economic growth from material throughput and carbon emissions is a myth. You cannot have infinite growth on a finite planet, regardless of whether that growth is distributed via Milton Friedman’s free markets or Arindrajit Dube’s wage boards.

Let us look at Anton Korinek’s proposal to steer technological innovation. He wants the government to promote AI that complements labor so workers can retain the surplus. This is a purely distributive concern. What about the massive energy consumption of server farms required to train these AI models? What about the rare earth minerals extracted under horrific conditions to build the physical infrastructure of this "cognitive" augmentation? The EfIP economists view technology purely through the lens of capital vs. labor, completely ignoring the metabolic rift it creates between human society and the Earth.

Similarly, Gabriel Zucman’s proposal to tax multinationals based on sales is presented as a way to make globalization "sustainable." But making the current volume of global trade politically sustainable via taxation does nothing to make it ecologically sustainable. The fossil fuels burned to ship commodities across the globe to satisfy consumer demand in the Global North cannot be taxed back into the ground.

The authors claim that contemporary economics is "rife with new ideas." But where are the ideas for managing a post-growth transition? Where is the engagement with degrowth, steady-state economics, or the circular economy? The authors proudly state that the members of their network are "tenured academic economists, working in broadly mainstream subfields." Perhaps that is precisely the problem. By defining the boundaries of legitimate economic thought to exclude those who challenge the growth imperative, they limit their "creative ferment" to rearranging the deck chairs on the Titanic.

If we are to survive the 21st century, we need an economics that does not just seek to distribute the spoils of planetary extraction more fairly among Americans. We need an economics that recognizes that the economy is a wholly owned subsidiary of the biosphere. A truly inclusive prosperity would prioritize the well-being of future generations and non-human life, meaning we must proactively scale down resource-intensive sectors of the economy. A carbon tax is a start, but it is not a vision. Until mainstream economists abandon the religion of GDP, their "inclusive prosperity" will be just another path to ecological ruin.

  3. 03Proposed solutions for technological innovation and global taxation focus exclusively on distributive justice while ignoring the massive energy and resource consumption inherent in those systems.
  4. 04By restricting their network to mainstream academic economists, the authors exclude vital post-growth and degrowth perspectives necessary for a post-crisis transition.
  5. 05A truly inclusive economics must prioritize the well-being of the biosphere and future generations over the continued extraction required for national economic growth.
