"Effective altruism is a vital ethical framework because it provides a rational, psychologically sustainable, and highly impactful method for fulfilling our basic moral obligation to help others."

Peter Singer's essay articulates something I have believed for years but struggled to express clearly: that the standard excuses for not giving more are philosophically weak, and that a commitment to doing the most good we can with our resources is not heroic virtue but basic ethical decency.

Let me defend the core of effective altruism against its critics, and in doing so, clarify why this movement matters.

The most common objection is that effective altruism asks too much—that it is unreasonable to expect people to donate significant portions of their income to strangers. Singer addresses this by noting that effective altruists are not saints; they are ordinary people trying to do somewhat better than they otherwise would. This is important. Effective altruism is not a call to martyrdom but a framework for thinking about how to use our resources wisely. If everyone with spare income donated even an additional 5 percent to highly effective causes, the aggregate impact would be enormous.

The critics who say that effective altruism is psychologically unrealistic misunderstand the nature of moral motivation. Singer notes that effective altruists often find their giving fulfilling—that in doing good, they feel good. This is not a corruption of altruistic motive but evidence that human beings are capable of finding meaning in helping others. The psychological literature on volunteering and giving supports this: people who give strategically to causes they care about report higher levels of life satisfaction than those who don't give at all.

Singer's critique of "warm glow" giving is uncomfortable but necessary. When we donate $50 to a charity that sends us a photo of a hungry child, we feel good about ourselves regardless of whether our $50 actually saves anyone. The transaction is largely about our feelings, not the recipient's wellbeing. This is not necessarily bad—we are social creatures whose moral emotions evolved for good reasons. But we should be honest about what we are doing. If we want our giving to actually help people, we need to care about effectiveness, not just intention.

The comparison between Batkid and malaria nets might seem to trivialize the former, but Singer's point is more subtle. He does not say that Make-A-Wish should be abolished or that we should never do things that bring joy to specific individuals. He is simply asking us to be honest about tradeoffs. If we choose to give to Make-A-Wish rather than malaria prevention, we should do so knowing that we are sacrificing lives for the warm feelings that come from helping an identifiable child.

Some critics argue that effective altruism ignores structural causes of poverty and suffering. This is a fair point but not a fatal objection. Singer explicitly says that effective altruists can choose different causes, including those that address systemic problems. The framework is compatible with political engagement, advocacy for policy change, and support for organizations working on structural transformation. The question is not whether to address structural causes but how to do so effectively.

Another concern is that effective altruism might justify problematic means. Singer addresses this directly by noting that even thoroughgoing utilitarians recognize the dangers of violating basic rights for speculative future benefits. The movement's emphasis on evidence and reason provides some protection against ideological excess.

Effective altruism is not a perfect ethical theory, but it is a significant improvement over unreflective giving based on emotional response and proximity bias. The movement has already directed tens of millions of dollars to effective interventions, and it has prompted important conversations about how to think about our moral obligations to strangers. That is nothing to dismiss.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Effective altruism is not an unreasonable demand for martyrdom but a practical framework that encourages ordinary people to use their spare resources more wisely.
  2. 02The movement aligns with human psychology by demonstrating that strategic giving provides donors with a sense of fulfillment and life satisfaction rather than just self-sacrifice.
  3. 03Prioritizing effectiveness over 'warm glow' giving is an ethical necessity because it ensures that resources actually save lives rather than just making the donor feel good.
  4. 04The framework does not ignore systemic issues, as it is fully compatible with addressing the structural causes of poverty through evidence-based advocacy and political engagement.
  5. 05The movement’s reliance on evidence and reason serves as a safeguard against violating human rights or pursuing ideological excesses in the name of the greater good.
