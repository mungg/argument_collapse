Diversified Gemini

"While offshore balancing is a sound strategy for conventional geopolitics, Stephen Walt’s proposed 'grand bargain' for nuclear non-proliferation is a dangerous and naive approach that misdiagnoses the motives of rogue states and undermines global stability."

Stephen M. Walt’s essay offers a refreshing dose of realism in a foreign policy climate dominated by ideological fervor. His critique of preventive war is devastatingly accurate, and his call for offshore balancing is a prudent alternative to the overextension we currently face in Iraq. However, when addressing the paramount threat of our time—the proliferation of nuclear weapons and the specter of nuclear terrorism—Walt’s realism fails him. He abandons hard-nosed power politics for a utopian "grand bargain" that misdiagnoses the motives of proliferators and overestimates the diplomatic value of American self-disarmament.

Walt argues that the U.S. cannot persuade states like Iran and North Korea to abandon their nuclear programs while simultaneously maintaining its own massive nuclear arsenal. He proposes that the U.S. halt the development of new nuclear weapons and significantly reduce its stockpile in exchange for rogue states verifiable abandoning their nuclear ambitions. This argument rests on the flawed premise that American nuclear posture is the primary driver of global proliferation. It is an argument born of academic symmetry rather than geopolitical reality.

First, let us examine the motives of the states currently pursuing nuclear weapons. North Korea is not starving its population to build a nuclear device because the United States has 5,000 warheads instead of 500. Kim Jong-il is pursuing the bomb for regime survival. He looks at history and sees that the U.S. invades non-nuclear states (Iraq) but negotiates with nuclear ones (China, Russia). A nuclear weapon is the ultimate insurance policy against regime change. Similarly, Iran’s nuclear ambitions are driven by a desire for regional hegemony in the Persian Gulf, a deep-seated nationalism, and the memory of the devastating Iran-Iraq war, where it faced chemical weapons without a credible deterrent.

To suggest that Pyongyang or Tehran will halt their multi-billion-dollar, decades-long strategic programs because Washington decides to dismantle a few thousand surplus Minuteman missiles is incredibly naive. The strategic calculus of rogue regimes is based on their own immediate security environments, not on the overall size of the U.S. arsenal.

Second, Walt’s "grand bargain" fundamentally misunderstands the role of the American nuclear umbrella. The primary reason we have not seen a cascade of nuclear proliferation among advanced industrial states—why Japan, South Korea, Germany, and Taiwan do not have nuclear weapons despite having the technology to build them in months—is because they rely on the extended deterrence of the U.S. nuclear arsenal.

If the United States radically reduces its arsenal and explicitly de-emphasizes nuclear weapons in its security strategy, as Walt advocates, it will severely undermine the credibility of this nuclear umbrella. If Tokyo or Seoul begins to doubt America's willingness or capacity to deter a nuclear-armed North Korea or a rising China, they will inevitably seek their own independent nuclear deterrents. By trying to appease rogue regimes through self-disarmament, Walt’s strategy risks triggering the very proliferation cascade it seeks to prevent, leading to a highly unstable, multi-nuclear Asia and Middle East.

Furthermore, Walt’s call to abandon the development of new nuclear weapons ignores the changing nature of the threat. The U.S. arsenal is primarily a legacy of the Cold War, designed to obliterate Soviet cities and missile silos. Today, the threats we face include deeply buried, hardened bunkers holding WMDs in rogue states. Developing lower-yield, earth-penetrating nuclear weapons (so-called "bunker busters") is not about starting a new arms race; it is about creating credible deterrents against specific 21st-century threats. If a rogue state knows the U.S. cannot destroy its WMD stockpiles without causing massive civilian casualties via strategic weapons, deterrence is actually weakened.

Walt correctly identifies nuclear terrorism as the most horrifying threat we face. He is also correct that securing loose nuclear materials, particularly in the former Soviet Union (the Nunn-Lugar program), should be a top priority. But preventing rogue states from acquiring the bomb requires coercion, not just carrots. It requires a credible threat of military force—yes, even preventive strikes if intelligence is absolute and all other options are exhausted. It requires robust economic strangulation and covert action.

The Libyan example, which Walt cites as a success of diplomatic carrots and sticks, actually proves the opposite of his point regarding U.S. disarmament. Muammar Gaddafi did not give up his WMD program because the U.S. showed nuclear restraint. He gave it up in late 2003 because he saw the U.S. military swiftly dismantle the Baathist regime in Iraq, and he feared he was next. It was the projection of overwhelming, uncompromising American power—not a "grand bargain" of self-restraint—that altered his calculus.

Walt’s offshore balancing is a sound strategy for managing conventional geopolitical rivalries. But the proliferation of WMDs is a unique, asymmetric threat that does not conform to traditional balance-of-power rules. We cannot balance against a nuclear-armed terror group, nor can we assume rogue states will play by established deterrent rules. Addressing this threat requires unapologetic American primacy, the maintenance of a flexible and overwhelming nuclear deterrent, and the willingness to use hard power to deny these weapons to our worst enemies. Walt’s grand bargain is a dangerous illusion that we cannot afford to entertain.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Rogue states like North Korea and Iran pursue nuclear weapons for regime survival and regional hegemony rather than as a reaction to the specific size or posture of the U.S. nuclear arsenal.
  2. 02Radically reducing the U.S. nuclear stockpile would weaken the 'nuclear umbrella,' prompting allies like Japan and South Korea to develop their own nuclear weapons for security.
  3. 03Maintaining and modernizing the U.S. nuclear arsenal, including developing specialized weapons like 'bunker busters,' is essential for creating credible deterrence against 21st-century threats.
  4. 04The successful disarmament of Libya demonstrates that rogue regimes respond to the projection of overwhelming American power and the fear of regime change rather than diplomatic self-restraint.
  5. 05Preventing the spread of weapons of mass destruction requires the credible threat of military force and coercive measures rather than a reliance on academic symmetry and diplomatic concessions.
