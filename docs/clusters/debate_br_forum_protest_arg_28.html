"While the lead essay offers a compelling moral defense of the right to protest, it fails to distinguish between aspirational principles and the actual legal landscape, which grants private universities broad authority to restrict speech and enforce trespass rules."

Alex Gourevitch’s essay is a passionate and timely defense of the right to protest, but it makes a legal argument that requires more careful parsing. As a matter of First Amendment law, his position has substantial support—but that support applies only to public institutions, and even there, important distinctions must be drawn. For private universities, the legal landscape is quite different, and many of the practices Gourevitch condemns may be perfectly lawful even if they are, in his view, morally indefensible. To assess the situation properly, we must disentangle the existing legal doctrines from the aspirational principles Gourevitch urges universities to adopt. When we do, we find that the current crackdowns are largely a creature of private governance decisions and political pressure, not a violation of any established legal right.

Start with the public forum doctrine. The First Amendment restricts the ability of government actors to regulate speech in public forums—parks, streets, and similar spaces traditionally open for expression. Public universities are government actors, and their quads, lawns, and open areas are generally treated as designated public forums, where reasonable time, place, and manner restrictions are permissible but content-based discrimination is not. Gourevitch is correct that this framework would likely prohibit a public university from shutting down an encampment simply because it is disruptive or noisy, unless the disruption is truly severe and the restrictions are carefully tailored. The fact that many public universities called in police to clear encampments without offering alternative protest areas raises serious First Amendment concerns. Some of those cases may result in successful lawsuits.

Private universities, however, are not bound by the First Amendment. They can restrict speech in ways that would be unconstitutional for a state school. The only legal check on their actions comes from contract law (student handbooks and policies may create contractual rights), state constitutional provisions, or specific statutes like Title VI. Gourevitch acknowledges this but argues that private universities should voluntarily adopt public forum principles. Many have done so by promising free speech protections in their mission statements or conduct codes. When a private university breaks those promises, students may have a valid breach-of-contract claim, but that is a far cry from a constitutional right. Moreover, private universities can—and often do—reserve the right to restrict speech on their property even in designated common areas. The “commons is not ungoverned, but it is less governed,” Gourevitch writes, but the legal reality is that it is governed entirely by the institution’s will, subject to whatever constraints it has voluntarily assumed.

The trespass rationale is therefore legally sound on private property. When protesters refuse to leave after being asked, they are trespassing. The fact that universities have historically tolerated encampments does not create a legal entitlement to continue them; tolerance is a revocable privilege, not a right. Gourevitch’s appeal to history is a moral argument about the kind of community universities ought to be. It has no legal force.

The Title VI hostile environment issue is more complex. Under federal regulations and agency guidance, a hostile environment exists when discriminatory conduct is so severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive that it effectively denies a person equal access to education. The standard is not based on mere subjective offense; it requires an objective component. The Supreme Court has also made clear, in cases like Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education, that student-on-student harassment triggers liability only when the school acts with deliberate indifference to known harassment. Gourevitch is right that the statements of subjective fear quoted by groups like the Harvard Jewish Alumni Alliance would not, on their own, meet the legal standard. However, that does not mean that none of the protest activities could ever constitute a hostile environment. If a Jewish student can show that they were repeatedly targeted with epithets, physically intimidated, or prevented from accessing parts of campus because of their identity, a Title VI claim might be viable. The question is an evidentiary one, and it requires a case-by-case analysis. The problem Gourevitch identifies is that universities, fearing lawsuits and bad publicity, have treated all protest activity as presumptively hostile without making individualized findings. That is a failure of process, not a flaw in the legal standard itself.

The Trump administration’s use of the hostile environment concept is a different matter. Its threats to pull federal funding from Columbia unless it adopted specific policy changes amount to a viewpoint-based condition that likely violates the First Amendment rights of the university community. Even if Columbia is private, faculty and students have First Amendment interests in being free from government-compelled censorship, and the government cannot use its spending power to impose unconstitutional conditions. Lawsuits challenging those threats are already proceeding, and some may succeed on that ground. But that does not directly affect a university’s internal decisions to discipline its own students for violating its rules.

In the end, the legal battle over campus protest will turn on whether private universities, under donor and political pressure, choose to uphold the aspirational speech norms Gourevitch champions. The law will offer them a wide berth to be repressive if they wish. The true defense of protest therefore lies not in litigation but in building political pressure from faculty, students, and the public to hold institutions to their own professed values. That is a hard road, but it is the one we are on. Gourevitch’s essay is an important contribution to that fight, even if its conflation of legal doctrine with personal conviction sometimes obscures the path forward.
