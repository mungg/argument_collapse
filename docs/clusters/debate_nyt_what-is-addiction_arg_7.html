"The current addiction epidemic is a structural and socio-economic crisis manufactured for profit rather than a result of individual human frailty or brain chemistry."

The sudden surge of heroin overdoses in New England and the broader United States cannot be explained merely by individual human frailty or sudden changes in brain chemistry. To ask "what causes addiction" in 2014 without looking at the pharmaceutical industry and our healthcare system is to miss the forest for the trees. The current addiction epidemic is fundamentally a structural and socio-economic crisis, manufactured for profit.

For the last fifteen years, pharmaceutical companies aggressively marketed powerful prescription opioids like OxyContin, misleading both doctors and patients about their addictive potential. Physicians, pressured to treat pain as a "fifth vital sign," wrote millions of prescriptions. We essentially flooded our communities with synthetic heroin.

When patients inevitably became physically dependent on these medications, the medical system offered little support. As regulations finally tightened and pills became too expensive or difficult to procure, cartels recognized a massive, ready-made market. They moved cheap, potent heroin into rural and suburban areas that had never seen the drug before. The progression from a legally prescribed painkiller for a work injury to a heroin overdose in a gas station bathroom is a well-documented pipeline.

Therefore, addiction today is not just a psychological or medical phenomenon; it is a predictable consumer response to an engineered market. It flourishes because we have allowed corporate profit motives to dictate medical care, particularly in working-class communities where physical labor results in chronic pain, and economic stagnation leaves people vulnerable to despair.

Treating the individual addict is necessary, but it is akin to pulling drowning people out of a river without seeing who is throwing them in upstream. We must hold the architects of the opioid boom accountable, reform how we manage physical and economic pain in this country, and recognize that this plague was not born of individual weakness, but of systemic greed.

  2. 02The medical system's failure to support patients who became dependent on legal prescriptions created a ready-made market for illegal heroin cartels.
