"The Democratic Party’s recent leftward shift on economics was primarily driven by a revitalized and insurgent American labor movement rather than internal elite strategy or demographic management."

The Hacker and Pierson essay is a masterful, if ultimately a touch claustrophobic, dissection of the political logic operating inside the Democratic coalition. They meticulously trace the party’s institutional incentives, the pressures of the primary electorate, and the strategic choices of elites to manage the cross-pressures of their new U-shaped base. The result is a narrative that is almost entirely driven by internal dynamics and the formal pathways of electoral politics and policymaking. But parties are not sealed laboratories. The most forceful repudiator of the Brahmin left thesis, and the most powerful driver of the Democrats’ new economic nationalism, is not to be found in the Congressional Progressive Caucus or the Roosevelt Institute. It is roaring down the assembly lines of Michigan and Georgia. The Democratic Party’s leftward turn was overwhelmingly propelled by an external, unruly force that the authors barely mention: a revitalized and insurgent American labor movement.

To read this otherwise comprehensive essay and find organized labor treated as just another item on a list of “policy demanders”—a group that, along with environmentalists, found “common ground” in the Green New Deal—is to seriously misread the balance of power and the origins of the current agenda. The authors note that unions “weakened dramatically,” which is true, but the story does not end there. The 2018 wave of teachers’ strikes from West Virginia to Los Angeles, the 2019 UAW strike against General Motors, the Starbucks and Amazon unionization drives, and the dramatic surge in strike activity and public approval of unions in the early 2020s constitute the most significant labor resurgence in half a century. This resurgence, and the explicitly class-based, confrontational politics it embodies, did not just support the Democratic agenda. It created the political atmosphere and the popular permission structure for it, forcing the party to speak in the language of work, power, and dignity rather than the bloodless language of technocratic redistribution.

The central policy frame of the Biden era—the idea of creating “good jobs,” of predistribution through labor standards, of industrial policy tied to union wages and domestic manufacturing—is not a clever rhetorical pivot dreamed up by D.C. message gurus to bridge a demographic divide. It is the direct political translation of labor’s demands. The American Rescue Plan, the CHIPS Act, and the Inflation Reduction Act are all structured around a core premise that market outcomes must be politically shaped to reward work, and that the primary vehicle for that is union organization and collective bargaining. This is a worldview that the professional-class left, with its often subliminal preference for education-as-solution and credential-based sorting, has had to be dragged into. The authors’ own framing, which lingers on the “college-educated” nature of the new suburban base, betrays this tension. The labor movement’s project is to de-bias a party that has become pathologically attached to the college degree as the sole marker of human worth and economic desert.

This labor-driven realignment fundamentally changes the nature of the coalition. The Brahmin left narrative, which the authors are arguing against, is essentially a story of a party of professionals. But a party that is re-anchoring itself in the labor movement, even a movement still small in absolute numbers, is embarking on a very different project. It is a project of organizing and giving voice to the working class, multiracial and metropolitan, in a direct, institutional way that goes far beyond casting a ballot every four years. The importance of the PRO Act, the Protecting the Right to Organize Act, as a central priority of the party’s economic agenda is the clearest example of this. It is legislation whose entire purpose is to shift the balance of power away from capital and toward labor, making future economic gains durable and self-reinforcing. This is the absolute antithesis of the neoliberal, market-smoothing policies of the Clinton and Obama eras. It is a politics of power, not just policy.

Hacker and Pierson are concerned with how the party manages a multi-class coalition. The labor movement, uniquely in American society, is a multi-class and multiracial institution that bridges the very divides they describe. A union local in a hospital brings together the college-educated nurse and the home health aide; a building trades local brings together the skilled tradesperson and the new apprentice. The union hiring hall and the collective bargaining agreement are, in practice, the most effective instruments for closing economic gaps across racial and gender lines, more powerful than any means-tested government program. The failure of the Democratic elite’s legislative agenda to fully pass was, in part, a failure of this labor-based infrastructure to be strong enough to counteract the power of capital and the structural biases of the Senate. But the direction of the party is unmistakable.

The irony of the Hacker and Pierson essay is that in centering themselves on the “density divide,” they underplay the one force that can actually overcome the atomization and polarization of American geography. A union is a dense social network in a dispersed world. It creates solidarity and shared identity in a way that a targeted tax credit from a distant federal government, no matter how generous, simply cannot. The new Blue Divide is being bridged not just by checks and subsidies, but by picket lines and organizing drives. The future of the Democratic Party’s economic ambition will not be determined by the next round of elite strategic planning. It will rise or fall on whether the surge of worker militancy can be translated into a durable, permanent expansion of labor power, transforming the party from a coalition of disparate interest groups into a genuine political arm of a mobilized, combative, multiracial working class.
