Diversified Gemini

"The Democratic Party's shift toward macroeconomic maximalism and massive spending has been a strategic failure that prioritized progressive wish-lists over economic stability, ultimately harming the working-class coalition through high inflation."

In "The New Blue Divide," Jacob S. Hacker and Paul Pierson deliver a sweeping defense of the Democratic Party's recent economic pivot, arguing that the bold, redistributive agenda of the Biden administration represents a masterful navigation of a complex electoral coalition. Their narrative portrays the near-passage of the multi-trillion-dollar Build Back Better (BBB) framework as a triumph of progressive statecraft, thwarted only by the archaic rules of the Senate and a couple of recalcitrant moderates. However, this rosy retrospective ignores a crucial economic reality that has defined the post-pandemic era: the return of inflation and the limits of macroeconomic maximalism.

Hacker and Pierson treat the Democratic Party's departure from the "fiscal restraint" of the Clinton and Obama years as an unalloyed good, a necessary shedding of neoliberal dogmas. But they fail to grapple with the consequences of that departure. The authors praise the sheer size of the Biden administration's proposals—noting they were "more than twice as large as Clinton’s in 1993 and around 80 percent larger than Obama’s in 2009" as a share of GDP. They correctly identify the March 2021 American Rescue Plan (ARP) as the opening salvo of this era. What they omit is that the $1.9 trillion ARP, injected into an economy that was already recovering and supply-constrained, helped ignite the worst inflationary spiral in forty years.

The authors seem to view economic policy primarily through the lens of coalition management and distributive justice, treating "spending" as inherently virtuous so long as it targets the right constituencies. But macroeconomic constraints are not merely political fictions invented by the "Brahmin Left" or wealthy suburbanites to protect their wallets. When the Biden administration, emboldened by the progressive wing and eager to prove it would not repeat the perceived timidity of the Obama years, pushed through the ARP without bipartisan support, it fundamentally misread the economic moment. Leading Democratic economists—most notably Larry Summers, whom Hacker and Pierson dismissively invoke as a symbol of past mistakes—warned loudly that such massive stimulus risked overheating the economy.

Summers was right. And the political fallout from that inflation has been devastating for the very coalition Hacker and Pierson hope to sustain. Inflation is a profoundly regressive tax. It batters the multi-racial working class—the very voters at the bottom of the authors' "U-shaped coalition"—far more than it hurts the affluent suburbanites who can absorb higher grocery and energy prices. By failing to acknowledge the inflationary context of 2021 and 2022, the authors miss the central tragedy of the Biden economic agenda: the aggressive pursuit of a progressive wish-list contributed to a cost-of-living crisis that alienated the working-class voters the policies were intended to woo.

Furthermore, Hacker and Pierson's treatment of Joe Manchin is emblematic of a broader progressive blind spot. They cast Manchin as a spoiler who "ironically" jettisoned elements that would have benefited his poor home state. But Manchin's resistance to the original BBB was explicitly rooted in fears of inflation—fears that proved entirely justified. Had the full $3.5 trillion or even the $2 trillion version of BBB passed in late 2021, on top of the ARP, the inflationary pressure would have been even more severe, potentially necessitating an even harsher response from the Federal Reserve and triggering a deep recession. The scaled-down Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) was not a tragic compromise; it was a macroeconomic necessity that salvaged the party's credibility on deficit reduction while securing historic climate investments.

The essay also glosses over the political danger of the Democratic Party's regulatory leftward shift. The authors praise the appointment of Lina Khan at the FTC and the rewriting of cost-benefit rules, framing this as a popular defense of the little guy against corporate concentration. Yet, an administration that declares war on American business while simultaneously relying on the private sector to execute its massive industrial policy (like the CHIPS Act and IRA clean energy provisions) operates at cross-purposes. An overly aggressive, ideologically driven regulatory apparatus creates uncertainty and slows down the very infrastructure and green energy deployment the administration champions.

Hacker and Pierson suggest that the Democrats have successfully utilized "pocketbook politics" to paper over cultural and racial divides. But the inflation crisis turned "pocketbook politics" into a liability. When working-class voters look at their diminished real wages, the promise of abstract future benefits from the "care economy" or green infrastructure feels hollow. The administration's failure to build lasting political power through policy, which the authors concede with some bafflement in their conclusion, is not a mystery. It is the direct result of voters judging the party on the immediate material reality of inflation, rather than the grandiose blueprints of legislative proposals.

Moving forward, the Democratic Party cannot afford the delusion that fiscal constraints are obsolete. The affluent suburbanites in the U-shaped coalition may tolerate higher corporate taxes, but they will not tolerate prolonged economic instability, high interest rates, and diminished purchasing power. To build a durable majority, the party must integrate the ambitions of the progressive wing with a rigorous commitment to macroeconomic stability. This means prioritizing investments that genuinely expand the economy's productive capacity—like infrastructure and basic research—while exercising discipline over broad-based demand stimulus.

The Clinton and Obama administrations, for all the critiques leveled by today's progressives, oversaw periods of sustained economic growth, job creation, and eventually, rising real wages. Dismissing their approach as mere capitulation to the wealthy ignores the fundamental requirement of governing: delivering a stable, prosperous economy for the median voter. Hacker and Pierson are right that the Democratic Party has changed. But if it wants to keep winning, it needs to learn that good intentions and massive spending bills do not automatically equal good economic stewardship.
