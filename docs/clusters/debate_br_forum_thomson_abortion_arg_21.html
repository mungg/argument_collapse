"The state is justified in restricting abortion based on the moral claim that a fetus has a right to life, even if that claim is reasonably disputed by some citizens."

Judith Jarvis Thomson’s essay is powerful, lucid, and, in one respect, admirable: she refuses to trivialize the pro-life claim. She does not pretend, as Ronald Dworkin sometimes seems to, that opponents of abortion cannot really mean what they say. Many of us do mean it. We believe that abortion intentionally destroys an innocent human life. We also condemn, without qualification, the shootings and bombings committed in the name of that belief. Those crimes are not the logical flower of respect for life but its grotesque betrayal.

Yet Thomson’s central argument fails. She says that because reasonable people may reject the claim that the fetus has a right to life from conception, the state may not impose severe restrictions on abortion on that ground. This is a demanding standard—too demanding, and not one our law or politics could possibly meet.

Many laws rest on moral judgments that some citizens reasonably reject. Pacifists reasonably reject the justice of war; yet they are taxed to support armies. Some citizens reasonably reject capital punishment; yet states continue to impose it. Many religious believers regard pornography as a grave social harm; civil libertarians reasonably deny that judgment. Economic regulation, environmental law, civil rights law, and criminal punishment all rest on contested moral and empirical premises. If every serious burden on liberty required a justification that the burdened could not reasonably reject, democratic government would be paralyzed.

Thomson might answer that pregnancy is different because the burden is bodily, intimate, and uniquely imposed on women. She is right that pregnancy is different. But the pro-life argument is also different, because what is at stake, if our claim is correct, is the life of a human being who cannot speak. The law regularly imposes burdens to protect the vulnerable. Parents are not free to abandon infants because care is exhausting. A father may be compelled to support a child he did not plan. Citizens may be required to testify, to serve on juries, to submit to quarantine, or to refrain from acts they regard as central to their freedom. Bodily liberty is profoundly important, but it is not absolute when another human life is at stake.

The dispute, then, returns to the status of the unborn. Thomson argues that fertilized eggs lack wants, hopes, fears, and interests, and that this gives us reason to deny that they have rights. But this account of rights is too narrow. Sleeping persons have no present wants. Temporarily comatose patients may have no present experiences. Newborn infants have only rudimentary mental lives. The severely disabled may lack many capacities that philosophers often use to define “interests.” Yet we rightly refuse to make their rights depend on currently exercisable psychological functions.

The better account is that rights attach to beings of a kind: living human organisms whose natural development, if not interrupted by death or disease, is toward the mature capacities of persons. A human embryo is not a skin cell. A skin cell is part of a larger organism; an embryo is an organism at an early stage. It is not “potential life” in the sense that sperm and ovum separately are potential life. It is already a living member of the human species, developing from within according to its own principle of growth. That fact may not compel every mind, but it is not sectarian dogma. It is a moral interpretation of biological reality.

Thomson objects that the encyclical says the truth is knowable by reason, whereas many intelligent people deny it. But a truth can be knowable by reason even if denied by reasonable people. Human equality itself was long denied by philosophers, slaveholders, and statesmen. The fact of disagreement does not prove that coercive law must abstain. If it did, the abolitionist could never have demanded law against slavery in a society where many “reasonably” defended property in human beings.

Of course, the analogy to slavery is inflammatory, and it should be used carefully. Women are not slaveholders, and pregnancy is not ownership. But the analogy shows the weakness in Thomson’s public-reason test. When a class of beings is excluded from legal protection, the central question is not whether those doing the excluding can offer an internally coherent account. The question is whether the excluded beings are in fact members of the community of rights.

That said, pro-life politics must face several hard truths. First, a society serious about protecting unborn life must also support pregnant women and children after birth. It is morally scandalous to demand childbirth while cutting nutrition programs, health care, adoption services, and assistance to poor mothers. Second, rape, grave fetal abnormality, and danger to the mother’s life are not marginal cases for the women who endure them. Even those of us who oppose abortion in nearly all cases should speak of these tragedies with humility, not slogans. Third, persuasion matters. A constitutional amendment or sweeping criminal prohibition unsupported by public conviction may deepen division and drive abortion underground.

But none of this vindicates Roe or Casey. Those decisions did not merely protect women from theological imposition. They removed from democratic deliberation a question on which the Constitution is, at best, silent. They also entrenched a regime in which the unborn child’s claim can rarely be given full legal weight. Thomson says courts exist to vindicate fundamental liberties. True. But courts also err when they invent liberties by disregarding other human beings who may be owed protection.

The violence at clinics has tempted some defenders of abortion to treat all pro-life language as dangerous. That is unjust. If abortion is the taking of innocent life, we must be allowed to say so. Moral clarity is not incitement. The remedy for murder committed by extremists is prosecution, not the silencing of conscience.

Thomson has challenged pro-lifers to give reasons, not merely declarations. That challenge should be accepted. But she should accept a challenge in return: do not make constitutional liberty depend on excluding the smallest human beings from the circle of protection simply because their claims are disputed. Every great question of justice is disputed. The task is not to escape moral controversy but to decide it rightly.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The standard that laws must be based on reasons no one can reasonably reject is unworkable, as most democratic legislation rests on contested moral and empirical premises.
  2. 02Rights should be understood as attaching to living human organisms based on their kind and natural development rather than their currently exercisable psychological functions.
  3. 03A human embryo is a distinct, living member of the human species rather than a mere potential life or a part of another organism.
  4. 04The existence of reasonable disagreement over a moral truth does not preclude the state from enacting that truth into law, as evidenced by historical movements like abolitionism.
  5. 05While bodily liberty is a profound value, it is not absolute and can be legally constrained to protect the lives of vulnerable human beings.
  6. 06The Supreme Court erred in Roe and Casey by removing the question of abortion from democratic deliberation and inventing liberties that disregard the claims of the unborn.
