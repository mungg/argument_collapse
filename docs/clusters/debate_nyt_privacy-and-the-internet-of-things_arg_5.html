"Instead of accepting the Internet of Things as an inevitable benefit, we must critically evaluate whether the convenience of each networked device outweighs its significant surveillance and privacy costs."

**Slow Down**

Before we get carried away imagining the wonders of a refrigerator that orders milk, let's ask a simpler question: do we actually want this? The Internet of Things is being sold to us as inevitable, but inevitability is a marketing strategy, not a fact.

Consider what we are being asked to accept. Every object in your home becomes a data-emitting node. Your toothbrush reports your brushing habits to a server in a country you can't name, run by a company you can't sue, protected by a privacy policy you didn't read and couldn't enforce if you had. The dishwasher knows when you eat. The thermostat knows when you sleep. The car knows where you went. Aggregated, this is a portrait of your life more intimate than anything the NSA programs revealed this summer have produced—and it will be held by private actors with even fewer constraints.

The standard response is that consumers can choose. But choice presumes meaningful alternatives, and the trajectory of consumer electronics suggests that within a decade, buying a "dumb" television or a "dumb" car may not be possible. We will be opted in by default.

The standard response is also that regulation can fix the harms. Perhaps. But our regulatory apparatus has not even managed to pass baseline data-protection legislation for the web we already have. The idea that Congress will competently regulate networked toasters strains credulity.

I am not arguing we should ban the Internet of Things. I am arguing we should stop treating each new product launch as an unalloyed good and start asking, product by product, whether the convenience genuinely outweighs the surveillance cost. For a pacemaker that detects arrhythmia and calls 911, yes. For a fork that tweets your dinner, probably not. Discrimination, not enthusiasm, is the virtue we need.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Internet of Things creates an intimate portrait of a person's life that is held by private actors with very few constraints.
  2. 02The promise of consumer choice is undermined by a market trajectory that makes 'dumb' or non-networked alternatives increasingly unavailable.
  3. 03Current regulatory bodies have proven incapable of passing basic data-protection legislation, making them unlikely to effectively manage the risks of networked objects.
  4. 04We should adopt a stance of discrimination rather than blind enthusiasm, distinguishing between life-saving technologies and unnecessary gadgets that compromise privacy.
