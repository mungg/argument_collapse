Diversified DeepSeek

"True ecological reason requires a total rejection of large-scale computation in favor of 'convivial tools' and human-to-human practices that respect planetary and social limits."

Evgeny Morozov’s call for ecological reason is bracing, but it doesn’t go far enough. The problem with AI is not simply that it has been shaped by the Efficiency Lobby; it is that the entire enterprise of large-scale computation—its material demands, its surveillance architectures, its epistemological reductionism—is incompatible with a just and sustainable world. Morozov’s own example, the language app cobbled together from three AI services, is a case in point: even in his hands, it is an extravagance of personalized consumption, a private delight whose carbon footprint and data extortion he acknowledges but cannot escape. Ecological reason, as he envisions it, remains a high-tech affair, reliant on the very infrastructures it purports to subvert. A true alternative would look not to public AI but to the convivial tools of a post-digital age.

Morozov draws on Ivan Illich’s intellectual milieu—he mentions David Hawkins, a friend of Illich’s—but he stops short of Illich’s radical conclusion. For Illich, the passage beyond industrial society required the deliberate choice of tools that foster autonomy and interdependence while respecting natural limits: the bicycle over the car, the library over the textbook monopoly. By analogy, the convivial alternative to AI is not a public large language model but a retreat from the model altogether. It is the cultivation of human-to-human practices of learning, creating, and deciding that do not depend on data centers and rare-earth mining.

This is not pastoral nostalgia. It is a hard-nosed assessment of what the climate emergency demands. Each new generation of AI models consumes more electricity, more water, more chips. The industry’s plans for growth would require a build-out of energy infrastructure that locks in fossil fuel dependence for decades, even as the world supposedly races toward net zero. No amount of democratic governance or public ownership can square that circle. The only responsible stance is to say: enough. We do not need generative AI to learn German; we need time, community, and perhaps a good old-fashioned book. The app Morozov built is a marvel, but it is a marvel of the same industrial civilization that is cooking the planet.

Even setting aside environmental costs, the epistemological costs of large language models are severe. They are trained on vast corpora scraped from the internet, corpora that embed the biases, hatreds, and commercial imperatives of the online world. Fine-tuning and prompt engineering can nudge them toward more benign outputs, but the underlying logic remains statistical and amnesiac. The model has no understanding, no experience, no stake in the world. To treat its outputs as partners in ecological reason is to anthropomorphize a spreadsheet. The flâneur does not converse with the stonefield; he picks up a stone and redirects it. Morozov’s workshop metaphor is truer than he admits: the chatbot is not an eolith but a factory that endlessly stamps out pseudo-eoliths, their apparent variety masking a deep uniformity.

A politics worthy of the name would begin by challenging the very premise that we need these systems at all. It would resist the techno-fix narrative that frames every social problem as amenable to computational solution. Instead of a Humanity Lobby demanding better AI, we need a movement for technological sufficiency—one that asks, in each case, whether a given tool enriches our capacity for convivial life or diminishes it. That movement would ally with the Degrowth movement, with movements for food sovereignty and agroecology, with care collectives, and with all who are building post-capitalist social relations at the grassroots.

Morozov is right to look to history for alternatives, but the most instructive examples are not the abortive cybernetic experiments of 1970s Chile. They are the many communities around the world that have maintained rich intellectual cultures without large-scale computation: the Zapatistas with their schools of autonomous learning, the peasant networks of La Via Campesina, the oral traditions that sustain Indigenous law and science. These are not anti-intellectual; they are, in the deepest sense, ecological, rooted in particular places and histories, attuned to the limits of the land. They do not need AI, and they would be foolish to embrace it.

The essay’s eulogy for Vergesslichkeit is telling. Morozov bids farewell to forgetting because his app will force-feed him tricky words. But forgetting is not only a weakness; it is a feature of a mind that prioritizes, that lets go, that makes room for the new. The efficiency of the app is the efficiency of the hamster wheel. To truly honor ecological reason is to accept that some things are worth forgetting, that not every gap must be filled, that there is wisdom in letting the mind wander without an algorithmic prompt.

The AI we deserve, on this view, is the one we manage to refuse. The real utopian horizon is not a public ChatGPT but a society in which people have the time, resources, and social bonds to learn languages together, to write stories for one another, to deliberate about their futures without a statistical oracle whispering in their ear. That horizon is distant, but it is far more appealing than the one Morozov sketches, in which we all become state-subsidized flâneurs tinkering with our personalized digital stones. Let’s not stop at ecological reason; let’s demand an ecological life.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The material and environmental costs of AI, including energy consumption and resource extraction, are fundamentally incompatible with a sustainable world regardless of who owns the technology.
  2. 02Large language models are epistemologically hollow and reductionist, producing a uniformity that stifles genuine human imagination and understanding.
  3. 03The pursuit of AI-driven efficiency, such as eliminating forgetfulness through apps, devalues essential human cognitive processes and creates a 'hamster wheel' of consumption.
  4. 04A just society should prioritize technological sufficiency and degrowth, looking to grassroots movements and indigenous traditions rather than cybernetic experiments for models of intellectual culture.
  5. 05The goal of a democratic society should be to provide the time and social bonds necessary for people to learn and deliberate together without the mediation of statistical oracles.
