"A progressive annual wealth tax is a dangerous and impractical policy that would undermine property rights, create administrative chaos, and damage the economy without effectively addressing the root causes of political inequality."

Saez and Zucman write with admirable clarity, and their diagnosis of a tax system that has become regressive at the very top of the income scale deserves serious engagement. But their remedy—a progressive annual wealth tax, possibly reaching double-digit rates—is a dangerous cure, one that misunderstands the nature of wealth, violates basic principles of justice, and would damage the economic engine that has lifted billions out of poverty worldwide. A wealth tax would not tame the power of the rich; it would centralize power in the state, erode property rights, and ultimately make everyone poorer.

The first difficulty is conceptual. The authors define wealth as market value minus debts, a definition that works well enough for listed stocks or bank accounts but founders on the vast majority of assets that are not continuously priced. They acknowledge this and propose a creative fix: let the government accept shares in kind and sell them to create a market. But this solution is not nearly as frictionless as they suggest. For a private company like Cargill, the forced sale of even a small fraction of shares every year would amount to a slow-motion expropriation of family control. Many of these enterprises are held together by intricate shareholder agreements that restrict transfers to outsiders; the wealth tax would override those contracts, turning a private firm into a quasi-public one by government mandate. This is not the creation of a market; it is the conscription of private property into a government auction, with the owners bearing all the risk of depressed fire-sale prices in an economic downturn.

Then there is the matter of liquidity. The authors dismiss liquidity concerns as “bad faith,” but that is too glib. Consider a founder who owns a seventy percent stake in a fast-growing but profitless medical-device startup valued by venture capitalists at five hundred million dollars. On paper, her net worth is three hundred fifty million. Under the Warren proposal she would owe roughly seven million dollars a year. The company pays no dividends, and her shares are subject to strict transfer restrictions. Selling a stake to pay the tax might require a lengthy negotiation with other investors, and if she is forced to sell in a hurry—perhaps because the IRS demands cash rather than shares—she could lose control of her own company. The authors’ in-kind remedy partially addresses this, but it assumes the government is willing to hold and gradually liquidate a portfolio of illiquid, idiosyncratic assets. Is the IRS ready to become the largest minority shareholder in thousands of private firms? The administrative complexity and potential for political meddling are staggering.

Constitutional doubts are more serious than the authors allow. The Constitution requires that direct taxes be apportioned among the states according to population. The income tax is permitted only because of the Sixteenth Amendment. A wealth tax is emphatically a direct tax on property, like the head taxes and land taxes the Framers had in mind. Scholars who argue for its constitutionality typically rely on a narrow reading of nineteenth-century precedents that the Supreme Court has not revisited in generations. There is a very real chance the current Court would strike down a federal wealth tax, leaving the country with years of legal chaos and the political fallout of having promised a fiscal revolution that could not be delivered.

Beyond legality, there is the question of incentive effects. The authors point to the high growth of the 1950s and 1960s, when top income tax rates were confiscatory. But that growth occurred in a world without global capital mobility, before the rise of international financial markets and tax havens. Today a billionaire can renounce her citizenship, move to a low-tax jurisdiction, and take much of her capital with her. The authors’ wealth tax would apply to American citizens wherever they reside—an admirable design that prevents straightforward emigration—but it would also incentivize wealthy entrepreneurs to never become U.S. citizens or residents in the first place. The United States has long benefited from being the destination of choice for the world’s most ambitious and talented people. Impose a ten percent annual tax on billionaire wealth and many will simply build their next Google or Tesla elsewhere, from the start.

Moreover, the claim that extreme wealth imposes a negative externality comparable to carbon emissions is an analogy that does not hold. Carbon emissions physically damage the atmosphere for everyone. A large fortune, by contrast, does not in itself harm third parties; it is the misuse of that fortune—corrupt political spending, say—that causes harm. The proper response to political corruption is not to tax wealth out of existence but to regulate the channels of political influence directly: strengthen disclosure laws, reverse Citizens United, cap campaign donations, or move to public financing of elections. To tax wealth because some of it might be spent on politics is to punish the innocent—the philanthropist, the quiet investor, the entrepreneur who reinvests profits in research—for the sins of a few.

The European experience is instructive. A dozen OECD countries adopted wealth taxes in the late twentieth century. Today only three—Norway, Spain, and Switzerland—retain them, and their rates are low, often below one percent, with high exemptions. France repealed its wealth tax in 2018 after years of evidence that it was driving capital and high-skilled professionals out of the country. Even Sweden, often held up as a social-democratic paradise, abandoned its wealth tax in 2007 because it was too easy for the wealthy to avoid and too costly to administer. Saez and Zucman correctly note that these European taxes were poorly designed, with low thresholds that hit the merely affluent. Their proposal avoids that error by targeting only the top 0.1 percent. But the lesson of Europe is not merely about thresholds; it is that in an open economy, wealth is mobile, and the credible threat of exit forces governments to moderate their ambitions. The United States is not immune from this dynamic.

Finally, there is an ethical dimension that progressives too often neglect. A wealth tax is a tax on the accumulation of past savings, savings that have already been taxed once as income, or will be taxed again as capital gains when realized. To tax the same dollar of value year after year simply because it exists in a large pool violates an intuitive sense of fair dealing. It tells the entrepreneur who built a company over thirty years that the fruit of her labor is never truly her own; the state will claim a share annually, without regard to whether she has consumed anything or realized any gain. This is not the silent operation of laws that the Founders envisioned. It is a relentless and visible pressure that would breed resentment and, ultimately, resistance.

A better path exists. We can restore progressivity to the tax code by taxing capital gains at death, eliminating stepped-up basis, and treating carried interest as ordinary income. We can strengthen the estate tax so that enormous inheritances are taxed before they pass to heirs. We can close the international loopholes that allow multinational corporations to shift profits to tax havens. All of these reforms could be achieved without the administrative nightmare, constitutional risk, and economic disruption of a wealth tax. Saez and Zucman are right that our current system fails to tax the superrich adequately, but that failure is not an argument for an entirely new tax base. It is an argument for enforcing and improving the ones we already have.
