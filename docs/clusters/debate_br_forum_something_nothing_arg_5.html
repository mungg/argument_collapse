"The United States should immediately withdraw all combat forces from Afghanistan because the counterinsurgency strategy is a violent, arrogant farce that fuels resistance rather than providing security."

The most powerful thing about Nir Rosen’s dispatch from Highway 601 is how it makes the grotesque machinery of counterinsurgency feel almost ordinary: a young policeman torn to pieces, a bewildered student arrested for his ringtone, an American captain playing water-diplomat one minute and ordering reconnaissance by fire the next. What the essay shows, against its own official skepticism, is that the very idea of “protecting the population” has become a violent farce. The Westby-Rosen world is one in which the United States is not protecting anyone; it is producing victims on all sides while insisting that more of the same will finally turn things around. The moral and strategic case for immediate withdrawal has never been stronger than it is in these pages, and we should have the courage to say so.

The Obama administration’s December 2009 decision to surge 30,000 additional troops into Afghanistan was presented as a middle way between full-scale counterinsurgency and complete withdrawal. But as Rosen’s reporting makes painfully clear, there is no middle way on the ground. The instructions to drive more slowly, to avoid civilian casualties at all costs, and to hold endless shuras with elders do not transform the fundamental fact of the occupation: foreign soldiers with guns are in Afghan villages, and their presence generates resistance. The new rules of engagement, far from calming the conflict, leave soldiers like Sergeant Dyer feeling handcuffed, while the Taliban learn to exploit every scruple. The dream of a population-centric war assumes a population that sees the foreign army as a potential friend. But Rosen’s shepherds and farmers speak only of being trapped between two violent forces—the Taliban who shoot and the police who steal sheep. After eight years, the locals are not neutral; they are exhausted and frightened, and they will tell whichever armed man in front of them what he wants to hear. The whole COIN concept rests on a fantasy of winning hearts and minds that has no correspondence to the experience Rosen documents.

It is worth dwelling on the moment when the two old farmers come to complain about the blocked water supply. Captain Westby plays his role: he squats with them, promises to help, and tells them “we all have to work together to stop the Taliban.” He is sincere, within the limits of his impossible job. But the scene is tragic not because the water is blocked—that might be fixed with a pipe—but because the very system of military governance makes such a petty problem into a matter of life and death and then expects gratitude. The water was blocked by the British when they filled craters in the road, itself a response to IEDs planted because the road is a supply route for the occupation. The chain of cause and effect leads inexorably back to the presence of the foreign armies. To imagine that one can break that chain by being nice, or by giving out aid money, is to ignore the logic of insurgency.

Rosen also exposes the deep class and cultural contempt that runs through the American effort. The soldiers joke about the Afghans’ fighting ability, dismiss them as “children,” and speak of “Hajis” even as they are supposed to be winning trust. When Zeibullah, the religious student, is arrested for the Taliban ringtone on his phone, the Americans know the charge is absurd, but they send him off to prison anyway, where he will likely rot or be ransomed. This is not a series of unfortunate misunderstandings; it is the routine work of an occupation that has no real standards of justice. It breeds a steady supply of fresh insurgents. Rosen’s Afghan patrolman who has lost nearly a hundred comrades, and the graveyard for police, speak to the human cost of a project that promises security but delivers only death.

The author is right to question the strategic rationale behind the whole endeavor. The claim that a Taliban government in Kabul would bring back al Qaeda is a speculative nightmare that ignores how much the world has changed since 2001. Al Qaeda has reconstituted itself in the tribal areas of Pakistan, a nuclear-armed state with a far more complex internal dynamic than pre-invasion Afghanistan. If the Taliban take power in parts of Afghanistan—as they already effectively have—they would be foolish to invite American drone strikes by hosting al Qaeda camps openly. The threat can be managed, as other threats around the world are managed, through intelligence, limited military action, and diplomacy, not through a permanent occupation of Central Asia.

There is a deeper moral argument that Rosen touches on but does not fully voice: the sheer arrogance of the project. The idea that the United States can create a functioning centralized state in a place that has never had one, in the face of local opposition, over a timeline of a few years, is an imperial fantasy. The president says we are there to disrupt, dismantle, and defeat al Qaeda, but the general he appointed has recommended remaking Afghan society from top to bottom. The gap between ends and means is not a policy flaw; it is a structural feature of the American way of war, which always inflates its objectives to justify the violence it cannot control.

The alternative is simple and painful: announce that the United States will complete the withdrawal of all combat forces within twelve to eighteen months, shifting to a genuine counterterrorism posture that does not require holding ground, and couple that withdrawal with a diplomatic push involving Afghanistan’s neighbors, including Iran, to broker a political settlement. This would not bring peace—Afghanistan will likely see intensified civil conflict—but it would end America’s direct role in fueling that conflict and allow the Afghan people to begin the long process of sorting out their own future without the boot of a foreign army on their necks. Rosen’s essay is a cry from the ground: listen to the people who know the war up close. The answer they give is that it is time to go.

Diversified DeepSeek

"The United States should begin an orderly and accelerated withdrawal from Afghanistan because the war has become an unwinnable counterinsurgency quagmire that mirrors the structural failures of the Vietnam War."

Reading Nir Rosen’s relentless, granular narrative of the war on Highway 601 is like reading a dispatch from a place the United States has already lost. The names and the landscape are Afghan, but the deeper story is eerily familiar: a foreign power trying to stand up a client government, chasing insurgents who melt into the population, and slowly realizing that the very presence of foreign troops creates the resistance they are fighting. This is not exactly Vietnam—no historical analogy ever is—but the structural parallels are striking enough that we should call this war what it is: a counterinsurgency quagmire that cannot be won at an acceptable cost, and from which we should begin an orderly withdrawal now, not after another two or three years of bloody stalemate.

The Vietnam analogy is often resisted because people focus on superficial differences: jungle versus mountains, communism versus radical Islam. But the dynamics that made Vietnam unwinnable for the United States are present in Afghanistan in a form that Rosen’s essay brings to life with painful clarity. First, the insurgents enjoy a permanent sanctuary. In Vietnam, the North Vietnamese Army and Viet Cong could retreat north of the Demilitarized Zone or into Cambodia and Laos; in Afghanistan, the Taliban retreat into Pakistan’s tribal areas, where they rest, rearm, and train free from American ground pursuit. General McChrystal’s strategy does not, and realistically cannot, address this sanctuary. Without American forces crossing the Durand Line—which would risk a catastrophic breach with nuclear-armed Pakistan—the Taliban insurgency can never be defeated, only managed. The best the U.S. can hope for is to kill Taliban fighters faster than they can be replaced, and after eight years the movement has only grown.

Second, the local government we are propping up lacks legitimacy and is incapable of reform. Rosen’s account of the 2009 elections is devastating but accurate: the election was a fraud that exposed the Karzai regime as a collection of warlords and criminals. The American response—publicly criticizing the fraud while continuing to back the same government—merely deepens the cynicism of ordinary Afghans. In Vietnam, the United States backed a succession of corrupt and authoritarian South Vietnamese governments, and despite massive nation-building programs, never succeeded in creating a state that could stand on its own. The same pattern is repeating: the more we try to build the Afghan state, the more we tie ourselves to a government the people despise, making the insurgency more attractive.

Third, the very tactics of counterinsurgency—searching compounds, detaining civilians, and causing collateral damage—generate more insurgents than they kill. Rosen’s scene of the arrested religious student, Zeibullah, who was sent to prison on the flimsiest of pretenses, is a small, eminently avoidable tragedy that is repeated thousands of times across the country. Every such incident creates a family with a grievance, and in a society organized around kinship and honor, grievance is social dynamite. Even when the Americans try to be nice, as Westby does with the cattle-herders, the effort is undercut by the structural reality that the Americans are an occupying force whose very presence is an affront. The British learned this in their own Afghan wars; the Soviets learned it; and we are learning it again at great cost.

Rosen is also correct to question the threat inflation that drives the policy. The official rationale for staying is that a Taliban takeover would allow al Qaeda to regain safe haven and threaten the U.S. homeland. But as he notes, al Qaeda is already in Pakistan, and the Taliban have shown little interest in global jihad. The claim that a few thousand foreign troops in Afghanistan are the thin line protecting America from another 9/11 is a rhetorical club used to silence dissent, not a serious strategic assessment. The United States retained a powerful capacity to strike terrorist targets from offshore bases long before 2001, and that capacity has only grown. A terrorist camp in a remote Afghan valley would be a bomb magnet, not a secure base. The obsession with physically controlling terrain is a holdover from Cold War thinking, not a rational response to the diffuse, networked threat of modern terrorism.

The real reason we are still in Afghanistan is less noble: we are trapped by the sunk costs of blood and treasure, by the institutional prestige of the military, and by the fear of looking weak. The proper response is a political and strategic honesty that says: we have tried, we have failed, and it is time to bring the troops home. The president’s July 2011 date for beginning a transition is widely seen by Afghans as a withdrawal timeline, and that recognition will soon render the Kabul government a dead man walking. Rather than drag out the inevitable, we should accelerate the withdrawal and combine it with a major diplomatic push to negotiate a power-sharing arrangement among the Afghan factions, including elements of the Taliban. The goal would not be a Western-style democracy, but a more decentralized Afghan state that denies territory to transnational terrorists. Even that modest outcome is far from certain, but it is more honest than pretending that a few more brigades and some “civil affairs” projects can create a stable, pro-Western Afghanistan out of the wreckage of thirty years of war.

Nir Rosen’s essay is a document of despair, but it is a truthful despair, earned through close observation. The United States should listen to what the ground is saying. The war is not about winning or losing anymore; it is about finding the least bad way out. That way out is to leave, and to leave soon.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Taliban insurgency cannot be defeated as long as they maintain a permanent sanctuary in Pakistan that American forces cannot realistically eliminate.
  2. 02The Karzai government is a corrupt, illegitimate client state that is incapable of reform and alienates the very population the U.S. is trying to win over.
  3. 03The inherent tactics of counterinsurgency, such as compound searches and civilian detentions, inevitably generate more resistance and grievances than they suppress.
  4. 04The strategic rationale for the war is based on inflated threats, as al Qaeda already possesses sanctuaries elsewhere and the Taliban do not share their global jihadist ambitions.
  5. 05The U.S. should replace its failed military strategy with a diplomatic push for a decentralized power-sharing agreement among Afghan factions, including the Taliban.
