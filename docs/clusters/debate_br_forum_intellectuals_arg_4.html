Vanilla GPT

"In the face of modern fascism and genocide, the intellectual's responsibility must evolve from solitary truth-telling into disciplined, organized solidarity that seeks to interrupt institutional complicity rather than merely disclose facts."

Robin D. G. Kelley is right to insist that “speaking truth to power” is no longer an adequate description of intellectual responsibility. It was never enough by itself, and in the present crisis its insufficiency is glaring. The facts are not hidden in the way they once were. We have watched Gaza destroyed in real time. We have heard Israeli officials use eliminationist language, seen starvation used as a weapon, seen universities, hospitals, archives, libraries, and schools reduced to rubble. International courts have found the charge of genocide plausible and have ordered provisional measures; human rights groups, UN officials, doctors, journalists, and Palestinian witnesses have provided overwhelming evidence of mass killing, forced displacement, and deliberate deprivation. The problem is not that nobody knows. The problem is that knowledge has not compelled power to stop.

That fact should shake any residual faith in the liberal model of the intellectual as a solitary truth-teller whose task is to correct error in the public sphere. The fantasy was always too flattering. It imagined that lies rule because truth has not yet been spoken clearly enough, that reasonable people will change course once the evidence is assembled, that institutions fail because they misunderstand their own principles. But many institutions understand perfectly well what they are doing. Governments know when they are arming atrocities. University presidents know when they are sacrificing students and faculty to donors, trustees, congressional committees, and federal threats. Editors know when “balance” becomes euphemism. Experts know when their expertise is being used to launder cruelty. In these cases the intellectual’s task is not merely disclosure. It is interruption.

Still, I would put the matter slightly differently than Kelley does. Truth-telling has not become obsolete; it has become inseparable from organization. Fascism does not fear facts in the abstract. It fears facts attached to institutions, movements, solidarities, unions, legal strategies, strikes, boycotts, classrooms, archives, and disciplined political power. A report that documents state violence can be ignored. The same report, taken up by tenants’ unions, student workers, faith communities, nurses, dockworkers, civil liberties lawyers, and elected insurgents, can become a weapon. Truth is not weak because it is truth. It is weak when it is socially disarmed.

That distinction matters because the call to “choose a side,” while necessary, can be dangerous if severed from obligations to accuracy, complexity, and self-criticism. Fascists also choose sides. Empires choose sides. Universities choose sides while pretending to be neutral. The question is not whether intellectuals have commitments; everyone does. The question is what disciplines those commitments. An emancipatory intellectual politics cannot be satisfied with loyalty, even loyalty to the oppressed, if loyalty means saying only what one’s camp wants to hear. The responsibility of intellectuals is not to become propagandists for the good side. It is to help build movements capable of acting truthfully.

That means refusing two temptations at once. The first is liberal innocence: the claim that universities, courts, newspapers, and professional associations are neutral homes of reason temporarily corrupted by outside pressure. Kelley rightly demolishes this myth. The modern university has always been entangled with empire, capital, policing, and racial hierarchy, even as it has also housed dissent. The second temptation, however, is anti-institutional romance: the idea that because institutions are compromised, the only honest work happens outside them. That, too, is false. Institutions are not pure, but they are terrain. A library budget, a union contract, a faculty senate rule, a graduate worker strike, a tenure case, a legal clinic, an admissions policy, a divestment campaign—these are not distractions from struggle. They are forms of struggle.

The campus repression of Palestine solidarity since October 2023 has made this plain. Administrators did not simply “fail” to defend academic freedom. Many actively redefined it as a privilege contingent on political obedience. They treated encampments against mass death as threats to order while treating donor intimidation, doxxing campaigns, police raids, and congressional show trials as normal governance. They invoked student safety while inviting armed police onto campuses. They denounced antisemitism, which is real and must be fought, while often ignoring anti-Palestinian racism, anti-Arab racism, Islamophobia, and the physical danger faced by students marked as supporters of Palestine. In doing so, they taught a brutal lesson: the university’s speech principles are often strongest when least needed and weakest when most tested.

But the answer cannot be to abandon the language of academic freedom, civil liberties, or antisemitism to bad-faith actors. If the right weaponizes antisemitism to criminalize criticism of Israel, then intellectuals must fight that weaponization while also fighting antisemitism itself. If university leaders invoke “safety” to suppress dissent, then dissenters must offer a better account of safety: one that includes Jewish students threatened by antisemitic harassment, Palestinian students grieving murdered relatives, Muslim and Arab students surveilled and smeared, Black and immigrant students familiar with police occupation, and all students whose future is imperiled by militarism and climate collapse. A politics that cannot hold these together will be too brittle for the moment.

Kelley’s recovery of Black antifascist thought is therefore indispensable. Black radicals understood fascism not as an inexplicable European aberration but as a political form nourished by colonialism, racial capitalism, and counterinsurgency. They saw that lynching, segregation, colonial massacre, police repression, and imperial war belonged to a common repertoire. That analysis remains clarifying. Today’s authoritarianism is not only a matter of vulgar rhetoric or constitutional crisis; it is also a project of social abandonment and racial ordering. Mass deportation, attacks on birthright citizenship, trans exclusion, book bans, voter suppression, prison expansion, anti-DEI purges, fossil fuel impunity, and the criminalization of protest are not separate culture-war episodes. They are components of a governing vision.

Yet Black antifascist history also warns against despair. Those earlier intellectuals worked under conditions far harsher than those faced by many of us in universities today. They lost passports, jobs, platforms, and freedom. They were surveilled, deported, imprisoned, and slandered. They often spoke to publics that did not want to hear them. Their example does not tell us that courage guarantees victory. It tells us that political clarity is possible without guarantees. It also tells us that intellectual labor is not confined to publication. Petitioning the United Nations, organizing workers, defending political prisoners, teaching in movement spaces, writing for Black newspapers, building internationalist networks—these were intellectual acts because they changed what people could understand and do together.

What, then, is required now? First, intellectuals must name crimes plainly. In Gaza, that means refusing the fog of passive voice: people are not merely “dying”; they are being killed, starved, displaced, and deprived of the conditions of life. It means rejecting the idea that Israeli security can be purchased through Palestinian destruction. It means insisting that Jewish safety and Palestinian freedom are not opposites. It means recognizing that U.S. responsibility is not rhetorical but material: weapons, diplomatic cover, intelligence cooperation, money, and ideological legitimation.

Second, intellectuals must practice organized refusal. Refusal can take many forms: declining grants tied to military or surveillance projects; resisting institutional partnerships that normalize occupation and apartheid; supporting divestment; defending students facing discipline; refusing compelled denunciations; protecting vulnerable colleagues; challenging visa revocations and deportations; exposing donor interference; and making professional associations more than ceremonial bodies. Refusal is strongest when collective. A lone untenured scholar can be destroyed. A department, union, learned society, or cross-campus coalition can impose costs.

Third, intellectuals must build counter-institutions. The right has understood this better than liberals. It has built legal networks, media ecosystems, policy shops, donor pipelines, school board strategies, leadership programs, and ideological training grounds. Its intellectuals do not simply publish; they recruit, litigate, legislate, and govern. Those committed to democracy, equality, and liberation need comparable seriousness without mimicking authoritarian methods. We need institutes that serve movements rather than donors, journals that are not afraid of moral judgment, legal defense funds, public scholarship platforms, sanctuary networks, labor education programs, and curricula that connect Palestine, policing, climate, disability justice, migration, and racial capitalism without collapsing their differences.

Fourth, intellectuals must recover the classroom as a democratic site. This does not mean turning every class into a rally. It means teaching students how to evaluate evidence, recognize euphemism, follow money, read law politically without dismissing law entirely, distinguish criticism of states from hatred of peoples, and understand how archives are made and destroyed. In an age of propaganda, the classroom’s radical potential lies not in telling students what to think but in refusing the conditions that make thought obedient. That is why authoritarian movements target education. They do not fear indoctrination; they desire a monopoly on it.

Fifth, intellectuals must accept risk while being honest about its unequal distribution. Calls for courage can become cruel when addressed identically to tenured professors, adjuncts, international students, undocumented workers, junior staff, and Palestinian scholars under bombardment. Responsibility increases with protection. Those with tenure, citizenship, savings, institutional standing, or public platforms have obligations that cannot be outsourced to the precarious. If a university is going to punish someone, let it first confront those who are hardest to fire. If police are called, let faculty bodies place themselves between armed force and students. If congressional committees demand names, let senior scholars make themselves visible. Courage should move down the hierarchy as protection, not up the hierarchy as expectation.

Finally, intellectuals must help movements think strategically about power. Moral witness is necessary, but it is not strategy. Encampments, teach-ins, statements, and marches matter, but their power depends on escalation, demands, alliances, and durability. What are the pressure points? Who can be moved, divided, exposed, or defeated? Which demands are symbolic, which are winnable, and which build capacity for larger fights? How do we keep coalitions together when repression intensifies? How do we prevent grief from becoming sectarianism? These are intellectual questions as much as tactical ones.

Kelley ends with Tiffany Willoughby-Herard’s question: “What job do I have if the students don’t have a future?” It is devastating because it pierces professional self-deception. A professorship is not an island of meaning in a burning world. A university that prepares students for careers while helping foreclose their future has betrayed education. But the question also points beyond sacrifice. If students are to have a future, intellectuals must do more than risk their jobs in moments of crisis. They must remake the conditions under which those jobs exist: the budgets, boards, contracts, investments, policing arrangements, and political dependencies that decide what universities are for.

The responsibility of intellectuals in the age of fascism and genocide is therefore neither detached truth-telling nor pure militancy. It is disciplined solidarity: telling the truth, joining with others to make truth consequential, defending the vulnerable, refusing complicity, and building institutions capable of sustaining freedom. It requires moral clarity without moral vanity, courage without recklessness toward others, partisanship without contempt for evidence, and hope without guarantees.

Fascism thrives by isolating people—by making each worker, student, migrant, dissident, and scholar feel exposed and replaceable. Genocide thrives by narrowing the circle of grievable life. Intellectual responsibility begins by breaking both spells. No one’s safety can be purchased through another people’s disposability. No institution’s reputation is worth more than the lives it helps endanger. And no truth worthy of the name can remain content with being spoken. It must be organized into the world.

Vanilla MiniMax

"To effectively resist fascism and genocide, intellectuals must move beyond individual truth-telling and moral appeals to embed their work within robust, collective organizational infrastructures that can sustain long-term political struggle."

Kelley's essay performs a crucial act of historical recovery while issuing a sharp challenge to contemporary intellectuals: stop treating truth-telling as a substitute for solidarity, stop pretending that reason alone can counter fascism, and recognize that your institutional position is always already embedded in social relations of power. I want to push his argument further by examining what it means to take seriously the Gramscian framework he invokes, especially given the current conjuncture where the university itself has become a site of active capitulation to authoritarian pressure.

Kelley is right that Chomsky's essay has been misread as a call for detached truth-telling rather than as part of a broader political project. But the problem runs deeper than misreading. The very framing of "responsibility of intellectuals" assumes intellectuals as a distinct social category with special obligations—a framing Gramsci explicitly critiqued. What Gramsci offered was not an upgraded theory of intellectual duty but a fundamental reconceptualization: intellectuals are not those who possess special knowledge but those who perform intellectual functions within specific social relations. This means the question is not "what should intellectuals do?" but rather "how do intellectual functions get distributed, and whose interests do they serve?"

This reconceptualization matters because it explains why the university has become such a contested site. University administrators are not simply corrupt or cowardly; they are organic intellectuals of capital and the state, performing the intellectual function of maintaining hegemony over knowledge production in service of donor interests and political pressure. When Kelley describes how administrators capitulated to donor demands and Trump administration threats, he is describing the正常工作 of a particular kind of organic intellectual. The question then becomes: can we build alternative intellectual functions within and against the university, or must we abandon the institutional anchor entirely?

The historical examples Kelley recovers are instructive here. The Black antifascist intellectuals he discusses—Du Bois, James, Jones, Patterson, Drake—did not primarily work through the university. They worked through movements, parties, unions, and publications. Their intellectual production was embedded in organizational relationships that gave their work political weight and protection. Du Bois could be attacked and marginalized, but he could not be easily silenced because he was connected to the NAACP, to the Left, to international networks of anti-colonial radicals. This suggests that the protection against repression is not individual courage but collective organization.

This point bears emphasis because current discourse around academic freedom tends to individualize the problem. We are told that professors need to be braver, that tenure should be strengthened, that free speech needs defending. These are not wrong, but they miss the structural question: academic freedom was always a concession won through struggle, and it can be rolled back through institutional mechanisms designed to make examples of individuals. The scholars Kelley names—Finkelstein, Abdou, Benjamin, Willoughby-Herard—were targeted not because they lacked courage but because they lacked institutional protection and collective defense.

The response cannot simply be "build more solidarity" as an abstract moral appeal. We need to examine what solidarity actually requires organizationally. The scholars Kelley mentions who have carved out spaces for insurgent intellectual work—UCLA's Institute on Inequality and Democracy, Scholars for Social Justice—demonstrate that institutionalembedding can enable as well as constrain. But these spaces exist in tension with the administrative apparatus, and their sustainability depends on constantly negotiating with a hostile environment.

Here is where I think Kelley's essay could go further. He correctly identifies that defeating fascism requires solidarity, but solidarity has organizational preconditions. The Black antifascist intellectuals he discusses were embedded in left political organizations that provided infrastructure for their work, political cover when targeted, and material support when they faced repression. Contemporary academics are often isolated from such organizations, and the left itself has been weakened by decades of anti-organizational ideology premised on the idea that movements are spontaneous rather than built.

The implication is uncomfortable: building the intellectual functions we need to resist fascism requires rebuilding the organizational infrastructure of the left, including political parties, unions, and movement organizations. This is slow, difficult work that cannot be accomplished by intellectuals alone and cannot be reduced to producing correct analysis. Kelley is right that "it is not enough to have just done the reading or even tell compelling stories." But neither is it enough to "put it into practice in and with our communities" without attending to the question of what kind of organizations can sustain long-term struggle against well-funded, well-organized fascism.

I want to conclude by considering what this means for the university itself. Kelley suggests the university remains "contested terrain," which is true but may understatement the degree to which the terrain has shifted. The current moment features not just repression of dissent but active reconstruction of the university's function. Project 2025 and similar initiatives do not simply aim to remove critical scholars; they aim to remake the university as an instrument of nationalist ideology and labor discipline. This is a counter-hegemonic project requiring counter-hegemonic response.

The organic intellectuals of the right understand this. The Heritage Foundation, the Manhattan Institute, the Claremont Institute—these are not just think tanks producing policy papers. They are organizational nodes connecting intellectuals to movements to political power. The left lacks equivalent infrastructure, and building it requires intellectuals to see their work not as supplementing movement organizing but as one function within a broader organizational ecology.

Kelley ends with Willoughby-Herard's statement that she has no job if students have no future. This is the right instinct, but we must ask what it would take to make such solidarity effective rather than merely sacrificial. The answer lies not in individual moral awakening but in building the collective capacity to make such sacrifices unnecessary—by creating institutions, organizations, and movements capable of protecting those who resist and sustaining the long struggle against fascism.
