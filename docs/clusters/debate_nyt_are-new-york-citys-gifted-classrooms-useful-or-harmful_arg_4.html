"New York City should replace its current rigid gifted and talented system with a hybrid model that combines more equitable, multi-measure screening for advanced learners with schoolwide enrichment strategies for all students."

The debate over New York City’s gifted classrooms is too often reduced to a false choice: preserve an inequitable status quo or tear down a source of academic rigor that many families value. Carmen Fariña’s impulse—to infuse neighborhood schools with the strategies that make gifted education effective—points toward a middle path that deserves careful exploration. The city should neither freeze its current segregated tracks in amber nor casually eliminate them without building a sturdy alternative that serves advanced learners and expands access simultaneously.

First, acknowledge what the programs do well. In a vast system where the median classroom spans a wide range of readiness, a self-contained gifted class can provide the pacing, peer discourse, and abstract reasoning that keep high-ability students from tuning out. Many working- and middle-class families, including black and Latino families whose children are underrepresented in the program, pursue these seats precisely because their zoned schools lack the resources to challenge students who need more. Canceling the programs without a credible plan would punish those families and deepen the perception that public schools cannot accommodate high achievement.

The problem is not grouping itself but the blunt instrument of early, single-score testing. The city should immediately move away from the one-test gate at age four and adopt a multiple-measures approach that includes teacher observations, portfolio reviews, and universal screening with automatic consideration of top-performing students at every school. Several districts across the country have shown that such reforms dramatically increase racial and economic diversity in advanced programs. At the same time, the city should expand its commitment to schoolwide enrichment models that embed gifted education strategies—like compacted curriculum, tiered assignments, and independent investigations—into general classrooms. Full-time separate tracks for kindergartners should give way to flexible, subject-specific acceleration in later grades, when developmental differences have stabilized and students can demonstrate readiness in multiple domains.

This hybrid model is not a retreat from excellence. It recognizes that some students genuinely need content beyond their chronological grade level while insisting that every child deserves access to rich, open-ended inquiry. Fariña’s challenge is to help the system mature beyond its obsession with labeling. The goal should be to create a public education landscape where a parent no longer feels that the only way to secure a rigorous education for a bright child is to win a lottery-style admission test at age four. That requires rebuilding trust and redesigning programs, not merely issuing a directive against them. If the chancellor couples her skepticism with concrete investment in classroom differentiation, teacher training, and equitable gateways, she can move the city toward a system that elevates talent wherever it lives.
