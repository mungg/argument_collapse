"The Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) project fails to address the structural power dynamics and inherent disciplinary biases that allowed neoliberalism to dominate, making it an insufficient vehicle for genuine social transformation."

The lead essay for this forum is, above all, an act of professional self-definition. By distinguishing “sound economics” from “market fundamentalism,” Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman perform a boundary-maintenance operation that is central to any discipline’s identity. They tell a story in which economics went astray in its public face but remained a wellspring of useful, empirically grounded ideas that only now are being fully harnessed for inclusive ends. As a sociologist of knowledge, I find this narrative fascinating, but I also find it incomplete in ways that have real consequences for how we think about policy change.

The question the essay does not ask is: why did the “primitive, simplistic perversion” of economics become so dominant in the first place? The authors answer implicitly: conservative foundations and think tanks, bad habits of economists, the seduction of mathematical elegance. But this is a supply-side story. It ignores the demand side—the powerful interests that needed a legitimating ideology for deregulation, tax cuts, and financialization. Economics did not fall from grace by accident; it was actively recruited to serve a project of class power consolidation. The intellectual history of the Mont Pelerin Society and the rise of the Chicago School is inseparable from the material history of capital seeking to reverse the post-war gains of labor. The authors’ polite, almost collegial, critique of their profession’s “bad habits” seems blissfully unconcerned with this political-economic backdrop.

Without this analysis, the EfIP project risks being a policy laundry list without a theory of change. The authors seem to believe that if they simply present better evidence and smarter proposals, the political winds will shift. But the same corporate and financial interests that underwrote the neoliberal era are still in place, still funding think tanks, still lobbying Congress, still commanding the editorial pages. They will not simply roll over because a few academic papers show that wage boards work in Australia or that taxing multinationals is possible. The battle is not over the best economics; it is over power. And on that terrain, the EfIP network, composed of well-meaning but structurally timid academics, is woefully outgunned.

The essay’s most telling moment comes when the authors recount their experience at the “new thinking beyond neoliberalism” workshop. They “found ourselves on the defensive,” they write, because other scholars identified economics with neoliberalism. Their response is to defend the discipline, to reclaim its true essence. But what if those other scholars were right? What if the very structure of economic knowledge—its methodological individualism, its insistence on efficiency as the master value, its treatment of preferences as exogenous and distribution as secondary—makes it inherently congenial to neoliberal governance, regardless of the intentions of individual practitioners? The authors want to save economics from itself, but perhaps what is needed is not a better economics but less economics—a humbler discipline that sees itself as one voice among many in a democratic conversation about our collective future.

To be clear, I do not dismiss the policy proposals. Many of them align with those advanced by non-economist progressive thinkers for decades. But the packaging matters. When these ideas are presented as the unique product of economic science, the discipline’s authority is subtly reinforced. The implicit message is: trust us, the experts, to fix the problems that our predecessors, also experts, helped create. A truly inclusive prosperity would democratize not just the distribution of income but the production of knowledge itself. Until economists are willing to share the podium, their inclusive agenda will remain an internal renovation of the guild, not the beginning of genuine social transformation.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The authors provide a supply-side explanation for the rise of market fundamentalism while ignoring the powerful corporate and financial interests that actively recruited economic theory to consolidate class power.
  2. 02Without a theory of political change to counter entrenched interests, the project's reliance on better evidence and smarter proposals is unlikely to shift the political landscape.
  3. 03The fundamental structure of economic knowledge—including methodological individualism and the prioritization of efficiency—may be inherently congenial to neoliberalism regardless of the practitioners' intentions.
  4. 04By framing progressive policies as the unique product of economic science, the authors reinforce the discipline's technocratic authority rather than democratizing the production of knowledge.
  5. 05The essay functions more as a defensive act of professional boundary-maintenance for the economics guild than as a humble contribution to a broader democratic conversation.
