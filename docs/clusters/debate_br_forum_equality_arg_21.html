"While the lead essay's philosophical critique of 'opportunity talk' is intellectually vigorous, egalitarians should not abandon the language of opportunity because it is a politically indispensable tool for building the democratic majorities necessary to achieve substantive reform."

Christine Sypnowich’s lead essay is a bracing philosophical call to arms: equal opportunity is not enough; we must demand equal flourishing. Her arguments are learned and heartfelt, and they tap into a deep frustration with a political discourse that seems to accept vast disparities as long as the starting line is allegedly fair. Yet for all its intellectual vigor, her essay speaks from a realm of ideal theory that is ruinously disconnected from the practical realities of building democratic majorities for egalitarian reform. In the world of actual politics, the language of opportunity is not a trap or a dodge—it is an indispensable tool for coalition-building, and the insistence on outcomes risks scuttling the very projects that could make people’s lives better. We should not abandon opportunity talk; we should instead invest it with the full meaning of a substantive, life-spanning project of social investment.

Sypnowich begins by quoting Jared Bernstein, a key economic advisor to Joe Biden, who said that Americans are not upset by inequality of outcomes, but by inequality of opportunity. She takes this as emblematic of a shallow consensus. But Bernstein was describing a political reality, not a philosophical dictum. Public opinion research consistently shows that Americans and Britons are far more comfortable with policies framed as “creating opportunity” than with direct redistribution or guaranteed outcomes. This is not because they are dupes of neoliberal propaganda; it is because opportunity resonates with widely held values of personal effort, hope, and fairness. To spurn that vocabulary is to cede the emotional high ground to opponents of the welfare state. The Biden administration’s agenda—the American Rescue Plan, the infrastructure bill, proposed child tax credits—has been couched largely in terms of giving families a fair shot, building ladders to the middle class. That framing is what enabled a fragile coalition to pass transformative legislation. Imagine if Biden had instead said, “We aim to equalize flourishing outcomes through a socialist program.” The political blowback would have been immediate and overwhelming, likely rendering even modest advances impossible.

Sypnowich’s alternative—explicitly embracing an outcome-focused socialist vision—is a non-starter in nearly every advanced capitalist democracy. The electoral fate of Jeremy Corbyn’s Labour Party, which openly championed a return to a more socialist economic platform (albeit not precisely “equal flourishing”), offers a cautionary tale. Voters, including many working-class voters, rejected the perceived radicalism and the accompanying narrative that Labour sought not just opportunity but state-imposed equality. Keir Starmer’s subsequent pivot back to opportunity language is not evidence of moral cowardice; it is a recognition that winning power is necessary to achieve any lasting change. Sypnowich might retort that moral argument should not be held hostage to electoral tactics. But in a democracy, moral argument that cannot persuade a majority is politically stillborn.

Moreover, the history of successful egalitarian reform in the United States is a history of expanding opportunity, not imposing outcomes. The New Deal’s champion, Franklin Roosevelt, framed Social Security and the Works Progress Administration as measures to secure “freedom from want” and to give every American a chance to live in dignity. The GI Bill was the single greatest engine of upward mobility, enabling millions to attend college and buy homes—it was explicitly an opportunity-expanding program. The Civil Rights Act of 1964, which Sypnowich rightly praises, was sold as removing unfair barriers so that all could compete on an equal footing. Even Medicare and Medicaid were justified as providing security so that illness would not destroy a family’s prospects. This is not to say that these programs did not profoundly reduce inequality of outcomes; they did. But their political defense relied on opportunity rhetoric. That rhetoric did not prevent the building of a welfare state; it helped build it.

Sypnowich might reply that we can now move beyond that rhetoric because the public is ready for a bolder vision. But the evidence suggests otherwise. Polls consistently show that while majorities support higher taxes on the rich and expanded social programs, support drops when the conversation turns to “equality of outcomes” or socialism. The anti-“socialism” sentiment remains potent enough to be weaponized effectively, as the 2022 midterm elections in the U.S. demonstrated in some districts. The task of committed egalitarians is not to insist on philosophical purity but to make common cause around concrete policies that will materially improve the lives of the least well-off, while framing those policies in the most compelling way possible.

And those concrete policies, ironically, can go a very long way toward securing the flourishing outcomes Sypnowich desires. Universal childcare, public education from pre-K through college, a child allowance, robust unemployment insurance, a public option for healthcare, and a federal job guarantee would dramatically reduce the vastly unequal outcomes that emerge from the current system. They would ensure that every child, regardless of family background, has not just a formal chance but a real capability to develop their talents and live a decent life. These policies can be described as “guaranteeing a real opportunity for every American” while in fact delivering much of what Sypnowich wants. The difference is that the opportunity framing avoids the trap of the state having to define what flourishing means for each person. It leaves it to individuals to use the resources and supports provided to pursue their own vision of the good.

Sypnowich’s own examples—music education, community connection—fit neatly into this schema. A society can fund free arts programs, recreational spaces, and mental health services as part of the infrastructure of opportunity, without making them compulsory or grading citizens on whether they achieve a certain level of flourishing. The state’s role is to ensure that the background conditions are such that everyone has a fair chance to live a meaningful life, not to decide what that meaning should be.

There is a danger that “opportunity talk” becomes a hollow centrism if it stops at mere deregulation or education reform that ignores structural poverty. But that is a failure of political will, not of the concept. The proper response is to insist on a maximalist version of equal opportunity, one that recognizes, as LBJ did, that you need “the ability to walk through those gates.” This requires not just antidiscrimination laws but massive public investment, progressive taxation, and a relentless focus on dismantling inherited privilege. It will involve, in effect, a radical transformation of the political economy. But we can get there by mobilizing the deep-rooted language of opportunity, not by assaulting it.

Sypnowich’s closing call for “socialism” is likely to divide rather than unite. In a diverse and fractious democracy, egalitarians must be adept rhetoricians as well as principled philosophers. By all means, let us criticize the hollowed-out versions of equal opportunity that conservative and neoliberal politicians peddle. But let us not discard a powerful vernacular that can carry the public toward a fairer society. The path to a more equal world runs through the language of opportunity, not around it.
