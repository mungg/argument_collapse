Diversified Gemini

"While Sypnowich correctly critiques the failures of 'equality of opportunity,' her proposed alternative of 'equal flourishing' is dangerous because it allows the state to impose normative, ableist definitions of a 'good life' on disabled and neurodivergent individuals."

As a scholar and advocate operating within the framework of disability justice, I read Christine Sypnowich’s critique of "equality of opportunity" with profound ambivalence. Sypnowich powerfully dismantles the liberal myth of the "level playing field," accurately noting that capitalist societies define merit and value in ways that inherently disadvantage anyone who falls outside the normative standard of a productive, autonomous citizen. However, her proposed alternative—a socialist commitment to "equal flourishing"—relies on normative assumptions about what a "good life" looks like that have historically been weaponized against disabled and neurodivergent people.

Sypnowich argues that we must reject liberal neutrality because it "washes its hands" of how people actually fare. Instead, she claims the state should "equip people to take opportunities" and "introduce young people to pursuits that they are not inclined to take up." The goal is a substantive equality of outcomes, measured by "flourishing."

But who defines "flourishing"? In the context of disability, the concept of flourishing is heavily freighted with medicalized and ableist assumptions. For centuries, the state and medical establishments have defined "flourishing" as neurotypicality, physical normativity, and productive independence. When the state takes an active role in "correcting cultures of fatalism" or pushing individuals toward what it deems "valuable pursuits," disabled people are almost always subjected to coercive therapies, institutionalization, or forced normalization. The autistic child forced into Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) therapy is being pushed by an outcome-oriented system toward a state-defined version of "flourishing"—often at the cost of profound trauma and the erasure of their authentic neurodivergent self.

Liberal egalitarianism, for all its flaws, offers the protection of neutrality. As Sypnowich correctly notes, liberal theorists like Ronald Dworkin and John Rawls argue the state should distribute resources without dictating the "good life." For disabled people, this neutrality is not an abstract philosophical luxury; it is a vital shield. It allows the disabled individual to say, "Give me the resources, the accessible housing, and the personal care attendants, and I will define what my flourishing looks like on my own terms."

Sypnowich is right to attack "luck egalitarianism" and its cruel distinction between "choice" and "circumstance." Luck egalitarianism often categorizes disability as "bad brute luck," framing disabled lives as tragic deficits requiring compensation to bring them up to a "normal" baseline. This is highly offensive to the disability community. But Sypnowich’s alternative isn't much better. By arguing that we must ensure people achieve a specific *outcome* of flourishing, she risks replacing the tragedy model with a paternalistic rehabilitation model.

She writes, "We cannot fall prey to the syndrome of leveling down... some of us cannot help but take a glum view on life." This betrays a very narrow view of human variance. What Sypnowich might categorize as a "glum view" or a failure to flourish might simply be a crip way of being in the world. Living with chronic pain, limited energy (the "spoon theory"), or profound mental health struggles does not mean one's life is less valuable or requires state intervention to "fix" their outcomes. Sometimes, the goal is not to "flourish" in the vibrant, socially active, contributing way Sypnowich envisions, but simply to exist in comfort and dignity.

Furthermore, her emphasis on community contribution as a prerequisite for well-being ("one’s life goes better if one is contributing to the community") can be deeply alienating for those whose disabilities prevent them from participating in traditional forms of contribution. While she says this should be understood "as broadly as possible," tying the value of a life to active contribution fundamentally undermines the radical disability assertion that existence itself is sufficient for worth.

Where Sypnowich gets it exactly right is in her assertion that "shortfalls in respect or recognition are often, perhaps inevitably, deeply intertwined with material inequalities." The disability rights movement has long argued that lack of access is not an unfortunate accident but a material deprivation enforced by society. We need the universal material support Sypnowich advocates for. But we do not need the state to dictate the aesthetic and social parameters of what we do with those resources.

A truly radical egalitarianism must embrace the sheer unpredictability and variance of human bodyminds. It must provide robust, unconditional material support—universal healthcare, guaranteed income, housing, and accessible infrastructure—without attaching outcome-based strings. We must abandon the illusion of equal opportunity, yes, but we must be equally wary of "equal flourishing." The goal should be maximum agency and material security for all, allowing the disabled subject the dignity to define their own outcomes.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The concept of 'flourishing' has historically been weaponized by medical and state institutions to justify coercive therapies and the forced normalization of disabled people.
  2. 02Liberal neutrality, despite its flaws, serves as a vital shield for disabled individuals by providing resources without dictating how they must live or function.
  3. 03Sypnowich’s outcome-oriented approach risks replacing the 'tragedy model' of disability with a paternalistic 'rehabilitation model' that views non-normative ways of being as failures to be fixed.
  4. 04Tying well-being to community contribution, even when defined broadly, alienates those whose disabilities prevent traditional participation and undermines the principle that existence alone confers worth.
  5. 05A truly radical egalitarianism should provide unconditional material support and security while granting individuals the maximum agency to define their own outcomes.
