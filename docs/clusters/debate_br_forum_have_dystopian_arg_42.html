Diversified DeepSeek

"Daron Acemoglu’s technocratic proposal to redirect AI through policy and regulation fails to address the deeper problem of modern hubris and the need for a cultural philosophy of limits that restricts the domain of algorithmic influence."

The careful, policy-minded architecture of Daron Acemoglu’s argument aims to convince us that the future of AI is a matter of deliberate choice among institutional levers: fund differently, regulate more wisely, shift norms, and strengthen democracy. But what if the deeper problem is not the choice of direction, but the drive to choose itself? Acemoglu’s essay, for all its erudition, remains trapped within a technocratic frame that assumes humans can and should steer technology like a vehicle. A genuinely different response to the AI dilemma would question whether the aspiration to redirect an epochal force like machine intelligence is itself part of the modern hubris that created today’s discontents.

Acemoglu’s historical narrative of the postwar settlement already hints at a more organic, less steering-oriented origin of the good life. He writes that “we don’t know for sure” why technology generated new tasks and fueled wage growth; it was a confluence of institutional competition, government support, and social movements, none of which proceeded from a master plan. Yet when he turns to the present, the prose becomes one of will and decision: we must alter the trajectory, develop a measurement framework, remove distortionary incentives, and embed oversight. This shift in tone reveals a deep-seated Enlightenment presumption: that with enough knowledge and collective rationality, society can subject its tools to its values. But AI, unlike past technologies, is a tool that increasingly learns and acts on its own within the very domains — communication, judgment, social coordination — that constitute society’s capacity to form collective purpose. The subject that is supposed to steer is being reshaped in the act of steering.

This is not mysticism. It is a sociological observation. Consider Acemoglu’s own illustration: AI-powered social media polarizes, weakens democratic discourse, and spreads disinformation. Yet he then proposes that we use AI — via privacy-preserving technologies, adversarial cryptography, etc. — to remedy those effects. This assumes a vantage point from which we can deploy one set of algorithms to neutralize the harms of another, while ourselves remaining unaffected. But the very experience of trying to govern algorithmic systems with more algorithmic tools deepens our dependence on automated intermediaries and trains us to think in the logic of optimization and control. The “we” that is supposed to do the redirecting becomes less like a democratic citizenry and more like a set of system managers.

A more radical response, therefore, would be to slow down and deliberately limit the domain of AI, not by picking which applications are “socially beneficial,” but by drawing boundaries around human life that we refuse to let algorithmic systems cross. The language Acemoglu uses to describe potential human-complementing AI — adapt teaching materials in real time, provide on-demand contextual information, enable precision collaboration with robots — is still a language that inserts AI into the intimate texture of human activity. Do we want teachers to be continuously informed by AI about students’ cognitive states? Do we want workers to be augmented in real time by virtual overlays? These are not just efficiency questions; they are questions about what it means to be attentive, to be present, to struggle and to learn together without a machine whispering in our ear. The postwar good life Acemoglu admires was built in a world where technology was powerful but mostly external; it was a machine you used, not an environment you inhabited. Our problem now is that AI is becoming the environment, and attempts to redirect it only deepen environmental saturation.

Acemoglu is right that norms matter, but the norm most in need of renovation is not the social responsibility of AI researchers. It is the cultural expectation that every domain of life should be technologically optimized. As long as we hold that expectation, the pressure to develop and deploy AI in ever more intimate spaces will overwhelm any regulatory framework. The true alternative to a dystopian AI future is a collective decision to say: enough. We will accept certain inefficiencies, certain failures of prediction, certain limits on convenience, for the sake of keeping large swaths of existence — education, care, politics, friendship — technologically simple. This is not Luddism; it is a deliberate moral and spiritual stance, one that many premodern traditions understood and that contemporary degrowth and tech-reform movements are beginning to reclaim.

None of this absolves us from the political work Acemoglu describes. Strengthening labor, democratizing institutions, and curbing corporate power are essential. But without a deeper shift in collective desire, those reforms will amount to rearranging deck chairs on a ship whose destination is already set. The most important redirection is not of AI research but of our own longing for control, mastery, and frictionless ease. That work is cultural, not technocratic, and it cannot be done by governments or funding agencies. It must be done in communities, in families, and in the slow, unglamorous practice of living well with less algorithmic mediation. Acemoglu has given us an inspiring policy agenda; what we also need is a philosophy of limits.

  2. 02Using AI-based technical solutions to fix the social harms caused by AI only deepens human dependency on automated intermediaries and the logic of optimization.
  3. 03The goal should not be to find 'socially beneficial' AI applications but to draw firm boundaries around human activities like education and care to keep them free from algorithmic mediation.
  4. 04True resistance to a dystopian future requires a moral shift away from the expectation of total technological optimization in favor of accepting inefficiency and simplicity.
  5. 05While institutional reforms are necessary, they will be ineffective without a fundamental redirection of the human desire for mastery and frictionless ease.
