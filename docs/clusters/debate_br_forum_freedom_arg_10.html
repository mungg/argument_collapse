"While Aziz Rana correctly identifies the imperial and exclusionary roots of American freedom, his focus on collective self-rule is too narrow and risks abandoning essential liberal protections for individual rights and pluralism."

Aziz Rana offers a sweeping and often illuminating account of why “freedom” in the United States so frequently carries reactionary meanings. His central claim—that empire, first continental and then global, helped form an exclusionary and individualistic account of liberty—is powerful. Yet the very sweep of the argument creates a problem. By making settler conquest and imperial primacy the master key to American freedom talk, Rana risks flattening a more plural and conflicted tradition. The result is a story that explains a great deal, but perhaps too much.

There is no serious denying that American liberty has been entangled with domination. The republic was founded amid Indigenous dispossession and slavery. White settlers often understood freedom as independence secured by land taken from others. Later, Cold War anticommunism fused “freedom” with markets, military power, and suspicion of public provision. Rana is right that the state has never been simply absent from American individualism. It has been present as land office, army, slave patrol, police force, deportation apparatus, strikebreaker, mortgage guarantor, and global enforcer. The mythology of self-reliance has always concealed public power.

But freedom’s American career cannot be reduced to that mythology. The same founding period that produced settler republicanism also produced arguments about constitutional limits, religious liberty, freedom of speech, jury trial, and representative government that were not merely masks for racial domination. They were incomplete, hypocritical, and often viciously bounded. But they also became tools for those excluded. Frederick Douglass did not abandon the language of constitutional liberty. Abolitionists, suffragists, labor radicals, civil libertarians, Black civil rights lawyers, antiwar activists, gay rights advocates, and disability rights organizers have all drawn on liberal as well as republican ideas of freedom. Some of those ideas emphasized collective self-rule; others emphasized protection from coercive majorities and intrusive officials. Both strands matter.

Rana’s essay sometimes treats individual freedom as almost inherently suspect unless embedded in solidarity. But there are excellent reasons for the left to preserve a robust account of individual liberty. A woman seeking an abortion needs social support, health care access, and freedom from patriarchal domination. She also needs a zone of decision that neither church, state, employer, nor family can invade. A gay teenager in a hostile town needs community and democratic recognition. He also needs privacy, speech rights, and protection against local majorities. A Muslim citizen after 9/11 needed solidarity against surveillance and bigotry. She also needed constitutional limits on state power. Negative liberty is not enough, but it is not nothing.

Indeed, some of the most dangerous forms of domination in American history have been carried out in the name of community self-rule. Jim Crow was defended not only as white individual freedom but also as local democracy. Moral censorship, anti-gay laws, school prayer mandates, and restrictions on reproductive autonomy have often been enacted by majorities claiming to preserve the community’s way of life. If freedom is identified too closely with collective decision-making, dissenters become vulnerable. The left should therefore argue for democratic institutions while remembering that democracy itself needs liberal restraints.

Rana is persuasive when he says that the left cannot solve its freedom problem with messaging alone. But his preferred solution—institution building—requires a clearer account of institutional pluralism. Unions can be schools of democracy. They can also become bureaucratic, exclusionary, or inattentive to minority workers. Public schools can cultivate equal citizenship. They can also impose conformity or become battlegrounds for majoritarian control. Local associations can nurture solidarity. They can also enforce parochialism. The point is not to reject these institutions but to recognize that freedom requires multiple, overlapping protections: democratic voice, individual rights, material security, and exit options.

A liberal-left freedom politics should therefore be less monistic than Rana’s. It should say that people are free when they have enough resources not to be dominated by private necessity; enough democratic power to influence common institutions; enough civil liberty to resist both government and majority coercion; and enough social equality to stand before others without humiliation. This is not as rhetorically elegant as “solidaristic self-rule,” but it better captures the range of unfreedoms people face.

Consider work. Rana rightly emphasizes that most Americans spend much of their lives in workplaces where they have little say. A democratic society should make unionization easier, protect strikes, experiment with works councils, and give workers more power over scheduling, safety, and technological change. But workplace freedom also includes the ability to leave a bad job. That requires universal health care decoupled from employment, portable benefits, affordable housing, child care, and a tight labor market. Voice and exit are complements, not enemies. If workers can neither organize nor leave, they are dominated. If they can leave but never govern, they remain vulnerable to market coercion. If they can govern but are trapped in a closed occupational structure, collective institutions may become oppressive. A mature freedom politics needs all these dimensions.

The same is true in foreign policy. Rana’s anti-imperial critique is a needed corrective to the self-congratulation of American exceptionalism. The Iraq War, in particular, revealed how easily “freedom” could be militarized into catastrophe. Yet a blanket suspicion of American power can also obscure hard cases. What should the United States do when smaller democracies face coercion by larger authoritarian neighbors? How should it respond to genocide, ethnic cleansing, or mass atrocities? How should it balance demilitarization with obligations to allies who see U.S. withdrawal not as liberation but abandonment? Anti-imperialism is indispensable, but it cannot be the whole of foreign policy. The left needs an internationalism that rejects domination without lapsing into indifference.

Rana’s discussion of Gaza, written amid immense suffering after the October 7 Hamas attacks and Israel’s devastating bombardment, underscores the need for such care. U.S. policy has too often given Israel military and diplomatic backing while minimizing Palestinian freedom. But a freedom-centered approach must hold several truths at once: Israeli civilians have a right not to be massacred; Palestinians have a right not to live under occupation, blockade, displacement, and bombardment; Jewish safety cannot be built on Palestinian subordination; Palestinian liberation cannot be built on attacks against civilians. Freedom is not served by selective empathy.

Where Rana is most convincing is in his rejection of fatalism. It would be disastrous for progressives to cede freedom to the right. The language remains too deeply woven into American political imagination and too valuable to oppressed people. But reclaiming it requires more than recovering the anticolonial or labor traditions. It also requires defending the liberal freedoms that make dissent, pluralism, and personal autonomy possible.

The American right’s freedom is often narrow, property-bound, and punitive. But the answer is not to replace it with a freedom so thoroughly social that the individual disappears. The better answer is a layered freedom: freedom from hunger and domination, freedom to participate, freedom to dissent, freedom of conscience, freedom from racial and gender subordination, freedom to form solidarities and to refuse imposed identities. The left can speak this language with confidence. It need not choose between liberal rights and democratic power. At its best, it has always fought for both.
