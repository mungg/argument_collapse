Diversified Gemini

"Aziz Rana’s vision for reclaiming freedom is fundamentally flawed because it relies on state-driven and economic institutions while ignoring the vital intermediary institutions of civil society—family, religion, and local community—which are the true foundations of human solidarity."

In his essay "A Different Freedom," Aziz Rana rightly identifies a profound crisis in how Americans understand and experience liberty. He correctly diagnoses that the hyper-individualistic, "zero-sum" conception of freedom dominant on the political right has left millions feeling alienated, precarious, and subjected to the whims of remote, hierarchical institutions. However, while Rana’s critique of market fundamentalism and his desire to replace it with a more "solidaristic" vision are compelling, his prescribed solutions reveal a fatal blind spot endemic to the modern Left. By locating the experience of "collective self-rule" almost entirely within the realms of the state and the unionized workplace, Rana ignores the vital, intermediary institutions—family, religion, and local community—that are the actual bedrock of human solidarity and true freedom.

Rana’s historical narrative frames the American tradition of individual liberty as merely a byproduct of settler colonialism and racial domination. To cure this, he argues we must rebuild institutions that foster "inclusive solidarity." What are these institutions? He points specifically to "the political system" (expanding voting rights and reforming government) and "the American workplace" (expanding unions and striking).

This is a remarkably impoverished view of human life and social organization. It assumes that human beings are primarily political and economic actors—voters and workers—and that liberation comes through mass democratic participation and collective bargaining. But most people do not derive their deepest sense of meaning, belonging, or mutual obligation from the state or their employer. They find it in their families, their neighborhoods, their churches, synagogues, and mosques, and their local civic associations.

These intermediary institutions—what Edmund Burke called the "little platoons" of society—are the very places where individuals actually experience the organic solidarity Rana claims to desire. It is in the family that we first learn that we are not autonomous, unencumbered individuals, but beings bound by deep, unchosen obligations to others. It is in the local congregation or community group that we practice mutual aid, caring for the sick, the elderly, and the vulnerable without waiting for a government bureaucracy to intervene.

Yet, Rana’s essay is entirely silent on these spheres of life. This omission is not accidental; it reflects a long-standing tension between the project of left-wing emancipation and the traditional structures of civil society. The kind of state-driven, universalist "collective agency" Rana envisions often views these local, particularistic attachments with suspicion. To the radical left, the traditional family is often seen as a site of patriarchal oppression; organized religion as a bastion of reaction; and local community control as a cover for exclusion (e.g., NIMBYism or local school boards).

Consequently, the Left’s project of "institution building" usually involves transferring power away from these local, organic communities and centralizing it in the hands of the state or large, national labor organizations. But when you hollow out civil society in the name of equality and state-managed solidarity, you do not create a society of free, empowered individuals. You create a society of atomized individuals standing naked before a massive, impersonal state apparatus. This is not freedom; it is a different form of dependency.

Rana rightly criticizes the right-wing version of freedom for leaving individuals "subject to the whims of massive and hierarchical institutions" in the market. But substituting the massive hierarchy of the multinational corporation with the massive hierarchy of the federal government or the mega-union does not solve the problem of alienation. A worker in a sprawling, bureaucratized public sector union or a citizen interacting with a faceless federal agency rarely feels a sense of "collective self-rule"; they feel like a cog in a machine.

True self-rule must be scaled to the human level. The principle of subsidiarity—the idea that social and political issues should be dealt with at the most immediate, local level consistent with their resolution—must be central to any meaningful recovery of freedom. If we want Americans to feel they have control over their lives, we should be empowering parents to have a say in their children's education, rather than dismissing their concerns as right-wing agitation. We should be fostering local economic development and small business ownership, rather than just demanding that massive corporations be managed by national unions. We should be supporting policies that make it easier to form and sustain families, recognizing that a stable family is the greatest bulwark against both state overreach and market precarity.

Furthermore, Rana’s demand that domestic freedom be tied to a radical overhaul of foreign policy and open borders ("presumptive admission for all") actively undermines the social cohesion necessary for solidarity. Solidarity is not an abstract, global concept; it requires a bounded community, a shared sense of history, and reciprocal trust. Rapid, unmanaged demographic change and the erasure of national borders dissolve the social trust that makes robust welfare systems and mutual sacrifice possible. You cannot ask citizens to deeply invest in the collective well-being of a nation while simultaneously insisting that the nation itself is an illegitimate, imperial construct with no right to define its own membership.

Aziz Rana is correct that the purely individualistic, consumerist version of freedom has failed us. We do need a recovery of solidarity, mutual respect, and common concern. But we will not find it by becoming foot soldiers in an anti-imperialist crusade or by turning every workplace into a site of endless political struggle. We will find it by tending to our own gardens—by rebuilding the families, faiths, and local communities that actually bind us together. True freedom is not just the absence of restraint, nor is it merely political participation; it is the freedom to live a good, embedded life, rooted in the places and people we love.
