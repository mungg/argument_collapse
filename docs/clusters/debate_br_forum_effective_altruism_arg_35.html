"While effective altruism’s focus on evidence is valuable, its narrow reliance on measurable metrics and short-term data risks ignoring systemic complexities and the genuine needs of the communities it aims to serve."

Peter Singer’s effective altruism has energized a new generation of donors and challenged the complacency of traditional philanthropy. Its insistence on evidence and measurable outcomes is a welcome antidote to the sentimentality that often guides charitable giving. But as someone who has spent years working in international development, I worry that the movement’s approach, as articulated by Singer, embodies a dangerous hubris about our ability to measure what matters and may inadvertently promote interventions that are less effective in the long run than they appear.

Singer’s poster child is the Against Malaria Foundation, which distributes insecticide-treated bed nets. GiveWell, the influential charity evaluator, has long ranked AMF among its top recommendations based on rigorous analysis of cost per life saved. There is little doubt that bed nets are a highly effective tool for reducing child mortality. Yet the effective altruist narrative often stops there, as if the problem of malaria could be solved through the mass distribution of nets without attending to the complex social, political, and ecological systems in which malaria thrives. In many regions, heavy reliance on externally provided nets has undermined local health infrastructure, created dependence on foreign supply chains, and contributed to insecticide resistance. A narrow focus on the immediately measurable metric—nets distributed, lives saved—can obscure these longer-term consequences.

The problem is not unique to bed nets. Effective altruism tends to favor interventions with clear, randomized controlled trial (RCT) evidence, such as deworming programs or direct cash transfers. But the very features that make these interventions easy to study also render them potentially fragile when scaled up or sustained over time. An RCT can tell you that deworming pills improved school attendance in rural Kenya in 2004. It cannot tell you whether that effect will persist when the program is run by a less motivated government, or whether the pills will remain effective as resistance develops. The movement’s veneration of evidence-based charity can thus lead to a winner-take-all dynamic in which a handful of highly studied interventions suck up all the oxygen while other, potentially more systemic approaches are starved of resources because their impact is harder to measure.

Consider, too, the voices of the people effective altruism aims to help. Singer briefly nods to values like justice and freedom but treats them primarily as instrumental to well-being rather than as goods that people might pursue for their own sake. Yet in my experience, poor communities often articulate their needs in terms of dignity, autonomy, and voice—not merely the avoidance of death or suffering. A village that receives bed nets may still lack clean water, secure land rights, or a functioning school. When donors focus single-mindedly on the metric of lives saved, they risk ignoring the broader capabilities that people need to flourish. Amartya Sen and Martha Nussbaum have long argued that development should be about expanding freedoms, not just filling utility gaps. Effective altruism, as Singer presents it, has not yet adequately engaged with this richer understanding of human well-being.

None of this means that we should abandon the project of improving aid effectiveness. GiveWell and similar organizations have done invaluable work in exposing charities that fail to deliver on their promises. The impulse to take evidence seriously is profoundly ethical. But effective altruists must be more humble about the limits of their tools. A truly “effective” altruism would invest in multiple forms of knowledge: in long-term ethnographic research, in partnerships with local organizations, and in the difficult, often messy work of supporting political movements for health system strengthening and accountable governance. It would recognize that the best way to save lives may not always be the most direct one, and that sometimes the smartest bet is on building the capacity of communities to demand and manage services themselves.

Singer’s closing reflection on the power of reason to expand our moral horizons is inspiring. But reason also requires us to acknowledge its own boundedness. The world of poverty and disease is not a neat optimization problem; it is a landscape of irreducible complexity, power, and culture. Effective altruism will fulfill its promise only when it learns to pair its quantitative rigor with genuine epistemic humility, listening as well as calculating, and trusting that the people it seeks to help may know something about their own good that a spreadsheets cannot capture.
