"Nevada should replace Iowa and New Hampshire as the first state in the presidential primary process because it better reflects the demographic and economic diversity of modern America."

There is no perfect opening state, because America is too varied to be previewed by one electorate. But if we must choose a better first state than Iowa or New Hampshire, Nevada has the strongest claim.

Nevada is small enough for retail politics but diverse enough to test modern presidential skills. It has a significant Latino population, a large service-sector workforce, strong unions in parts of the state, libertarian currents, rural counties, fast-growing suburbs and an economy shaped by tourism, housing booms and busts, immigration and water scarcity. A candidate who campaigns seriously in Nevada must talk not only about abstract middle-class anxiety but about wages, health care, immigration, public lands and the vulnerabilities of a postindustrial service economy.

New Hampshire’s defenders say their state makes candidates answer unscripted questions. Good. Nevada can do that too. Iowa’s defenders say organization matters. Nevada can test organization as well. What Nevada adds is a closer connection to the demographic future of the country. It is not perfectly representative — no state is — but it is less frozen in the politics of an older, whiter America.

The main objection is that Nevada’s caucuses have sometimes been messy and lower-profile than Iowa’s and New Hampshire’s. That is a reason to invest in the process, not to dismiss the state. The parties could require Nevada to hold a primary, improve voter education and schedule it first for several cycles so campaigns, journalists and voters build the habits that Iowa and New Hampshire acquired over decades. Traditions do not descend from heaven; they are created by repetition.

South Carolina should follow Nevada, because African-American voters and Southern conservatives are also essential to understanding national politics. Iowa and New Hampshire can remain early, but they should no longer be the first filters through which all candidates must pass.

Putting Nevada first would not solve every problem in the nomination system. Money would still matter; media narratives would still be too breathless; turnout would still vary. But it would correct the most glaring imbalance. The first contest should force candidates to face the America that is emerging, not only the America that has long held the microphone.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Nevada’s diverse population and economy force candidates to address a broader range of relevant national issues like immigration, labor unions, and service-sector vulnerabilities.
  2. 02Nevada maintains the benefits of small-state retail politics and organizational testing while being more representative of the country's demographic future than Iowa or New Hampshire.
  3. 03The historical logistical issues with Nevada's voting process are a reason to invest in and reform its primary system rather than a reason to disqualify it from being first.
  4. 04Moving Nevada to the front of the calendar would correct the current system's racial and geographic imbalance by forcing candidates to engage with an emerging, multi-ethnic electorate.
  5. 05Following Nevada with South Carolina would ensure that African-American and Southern conservative perspectives are prioritized early in the selection process.
