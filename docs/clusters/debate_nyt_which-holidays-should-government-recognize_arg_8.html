Add Election Day, and Stop There

Every few years, someone proposes a new federal holiday: Lincoln's birthday, Susan B. Anthony Day, Cesar Chavez Day, Election Day, Juneteenth, Eid, the lunar new year. Most of these proposals have real merit. Almost none of them should be enacted. There is exactly one new holiday the federal government should add to its calendar, and it has nothing to do with religion or ethnicity. It's Election Day.

The case is simple and embarrassingly overdue. The United States has among the lowest voter turnout rates of any wealthy democracy — barely 58 percent in the 2012 presidential election, and far worse in midterms. A persistent reason cited by non-voters in census surveys is that they couldn't get to the polls because of work or family obligations. We hold elections on a Tuesday, the day chosen in 1845 for the convenience of 19th-century farmers, and then we act puzzled that hourly workers, single parents, and people juggling two jobs find it hard to participate. Making Election Day a federal holiday — and pressuring states and private employers to follow suit — would be the single cheapest, most democratic reform available to us. It would also send an unmistakable civic signal: voting is not a hobby to squeeze between commutes; it is the foundational act of self-government.

What about the others? The temptation to keep adding holidays should be resisted, for two reasons.

First, the math gets ugly fast. The federal government already recognizes 10 holidays. If we added every religious observance important to a major American community — Mawlid, Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, Rosh Hashanah, Yom Kippur, Passover, Diwali, Vesak, Orthodox Christmas, the lunar new year — we would push the total past 20 before considering ethnic and historical commemorations. That's not a serious calendar; it's a shutdown schedule.

Second, federal recognition is the wrong tool for cultural acknowledgment. Communities don't need the IRS to close in order to celebrate Mawlid; they need their employers to grant flexible leave, their schools to excuse absences, and their neighbors to know what the holiday is. Those goals are better served by anti-discrimination law, floating-holiday policies, and cultural literacy than by federal proclamations. The lunar new year is celebrated more vibrantly in San Francisco and New York than it would be if Washington simply added another red square to the calendar.

The existing federal holidays are a defensible, if imperfect, mix: national founding moments (July 4), civic sacrifice (Memorial Day, Veterans Day), figures who define the American story (Washington, King), and a few culturally entrenched observances (Thanksgiving, Christmas, New Year's). Adding Election Day completes the civic core. Everything else belongs to communities, families, and employers — which is, frankly, where it thrives best.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Designating Election Day as a holiday would remove work-related barriers to voting and significantly increase voter turnout among hourly workers and parents.
  2. 02Making Election Day a federal holiday would signal that voting is the foundational act of self-government rather than a secondary activity.
  3. 03Adding numerous religious and ethnic holidays would create an unsustainable schedule that would effectively shut down government operations too frequently.
  4. 04Federal recognition is an ineffective tool for cultural acknowledgment, which is better achieved through flexible leave policies and anti-discrimination laws.
  5. 05Cultural and religious celebrations thrive more authentically within communities and families than through federal mandates.
  6. 06The current federal calendar already covers the essential civic and historical milestones, and adding Election Day would complete this core national identity.
