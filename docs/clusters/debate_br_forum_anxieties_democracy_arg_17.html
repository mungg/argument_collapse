**Two Cheers Is Plenty: A Skeptical Reply**

Reading Katznelson's lament for representative democracy, I am struck by a peculiar nostalgia at its core. He invokes Forster's "two cheers for democracy" approvingly, but his own essay reads as a plea for the third cheer—a defense of legislative representation as the irreplaceable heart of a decent politics, against the various populist, deliberative, and plebiscitary impostors. I want to suggest that Forster's restraint was wisdom, and that we should be wary of theorists who, in defending democracy against its critics, end up sounding like apologists for the institutional arrangements that have produced our current discontents.

Consider the implicit move Katznelson makes throughout. He acknowledges that legislatures are bedeviled by money, polarization, and procedural dysfunction. He concedes that ordinary citizens have good reasons for dissatisfaction. He grants that participatory budgeting and similar innovations can be valuable. But every time the argument reaches a fork, he turns toward defense of the central institutions: don't abandon parliaments, don't bypass representation, beware the slide to Peronism. The cumulative effect is to make any serious challenge to existing legislative practice look like a step toward Orbán.

This is a familiar rhetorical move, and it has costs. It treats the present configuration of representative democracy as more or less normative—the thing to be preserved against deviation—when in fact the present configuration is a contingent, recent, and badly compromised arrangement that has lost legitimacy in the eyes of many of those it claims to represent. The interesting question is not whether to defend "representation" in the abstract. It is whether the particular institutions we have are adequate to the representational task, and if not, what configurations might do better.

Take Katznelson's framing of deliberative democracy. He summarizes it (via Urbinati) as the idea that "difficult problems would be solved and correct outcomes consensually discerned if only citizens were offered chances to reason together on the basis of an assumption of shared interests and information." Then he dismisses it as making "opinion transcend will." But this caricatures the substantial empirical literature on deliberative practices. James Fishkin's deliberative polling work, the experiments in citizens' assemblies in British Columbia and Ireland, the participatory budgeting in Porto Alegre that Katznelson himself mentions favorably—these are not utopian replacements for legislatures. They are concrete, tested ways of feeding more informed and considered public opinion into representative processes. The choice is not deliberation *versus* representation; it is representation *with* or *without* serious mechanisms of public reasoning.

Similarly with plebiscitary instruments. Katznelson cites California as a cautionary tale, and there are real problems with that state's initiative process—particularly the way Proposition 13 has constrained fiscal policy for decades. But citizen initiatives in Switzerland, used regularly and routinely, have not produced authoritarian backsliding; they have produced a remarkably stable and well-governed federation. The lesson is not that direct democratic instruments are intrinsically dangerous, but that they need to be embedded in institutional contexts that channel them constructively.

There is something class-inflected about the way Katznelson sets up his oppositions. The defense of "representation" against "populism" is, in practice, often a defense of professional politicians, lawyers, and credentialed elites against the unruly preferences of ordinary citizens. I do not mean that Katznelson intends this; his own work, particularly on the New Deal, shows deep sympathy for the working-class politics that drove that era's reforms. But the framework he deploys here, with its repeated warnings against bypassing legislatures, lands in a place that is functionally conservative—a defense of the institutional status quo dressed up as democratic theory.

Forster gave democracy two cheers because he knew better than to give it three. Democracy admits variety and permits criticism; that is enough. It is not the highest political value, nor an end in itself, nor the embodiment of any particular wisdom. It is a workable arrangement for managing disagreement among rough equals. When particular democratic institutions cease to perform that function—as American legislative institutions have, in important ways, ceased to perform it—the appropriate response is not to defend them more passionately but to ask what reforms or supplements might restore the function.

The third cheer is precisely the one we should withhold. Reserved enthusiasm for democratic institutions is what allows us to fix them when they break. Excessive piety is what prevents repair.
