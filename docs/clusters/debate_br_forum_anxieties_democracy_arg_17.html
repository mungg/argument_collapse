"The expansion of the national security state through secrecy and executive overreach represents a quiet surrender of representative democracy that must be corrected through legislative reclamation of authority."

Ira Katznelson lists the balance between liberty and national security among the great unresolved challenges of liberal democracy. That issue deserves more prominence, because the national security state may be the arena in which representative democracy has most quietly surrendered. Since September 11, the United States has built systems of surveillance, secrecy, targeted killing, detention, and military action that operate with limited public knowledge and often limited legislative control. This is not simply a policy dispute. It is a democratic problem.

Representative government requires that citizens know enough to judge what is done in their name. National security necessarily involves some secrecy, but secrecy has expanded far beyond tactical necessity. Classified legal interpretations, secret courts, secret watchlists, secret drone criteria, secret surveillance programs, and secret negotiations between agencies and private companies create a domain of governance where ordinary democratic accountability is attenuated. Citizens are asked to trust institutions they cannot see.

The Snowden disclosures in 2013 revealed the scale of the problem. Whatever one thinks of Snowden’s actions, the public learned that the National Security Agency had been collecting bulk telephone metadata and operating broad surveillance programs under legal interpretations unknown to most citizens and many legislators. Officials had offered reassurances that were, at best, incomplete. The subsequent debate and the USA Freedom Act showed that democratic correction is possible. But the correction occurred only after unauthorized disclosure. That should trouble anyone committed to constitutional government.

Congress has not been absent from the national security state; it has been complicit and often deferential. Intelligence committees receive classified briefings, but secrecy limits their ability to mobilize public debate. Members outside those committees often lack information. Votes on broad authorizations substitute for sustained judgment. The 2001 Authorization for Use of Military Force has been stretched across countries, groups, and years far beyond the immediate perpetrators of 9/11. The Obama administration’s campaign against ISIS rests on legal theories that many members of Congress question but few have forced to a decisive vote.

This pattern undermines Katznelson’s ideal of legislation as the place where opinion and will combine. In national security, will often precedes opinion. Action is taken by the executive; legal rationale follows; Congress complains, funds, and avoids responsibility. Courts defer or dismiss on standing and state secrets grounds. The public receives fragments. Elections rarely clarify the mandate because both parties fear appearing weak on security.

The danger is not dictatorship in the classic sense. It is normalization of exception. Emergency powers become permanent infrastructures. Technologies developed for foreign intelligence migrate toward domestic policing. Watchlists and no-fly lists impose burdens without ordinary due process. Muslim communities experience surveillance as a condition of citizenship. Border zones become constitutional gray areas. Once built, security systems acquire bureaucratic constituencies and political immunity.

Katznelson’s warning about illiberal democracy is relevant here. A frightened public may accept restrictions on liberty if leaders promise safety. But the more distinctive American danger is bipartisan security liberalism: leaders who affirm rights in principle while maintaining secret exceptions in practice. The rhetoric remains constitutional; the machinery becomes increasingly insulated.

The solution is not naïve transparency. States face real threats. ISIS’s brutality, Al Qaeda’s persistence, cyberattacks, and nuclear proliferation cannot be wished away. Intelligence work is necessary; military force may sometimes be justified. Liberal democracy must defend itself. But defense must be governed, and governed in a way that preserves the distinction between a republic and a security apparatus with elections attached.

Several reforms follow. Congress should enact a new, specific authorization for the use of force against ISIS, with geographic limits, reporting requirements, and sunset provisions. The 2001 AUMF should be narrowed or repealed. Major surveillance authorities should include adversarial representation before the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court, stronger disclosure of significant legal interpretations, and regular public reporting in aggregate form. Whistleblower protections for intelligence personnel should be strengthened so that lawful channels exist for exposing abuse. Classification should be reduced and subject to more independent review.

Targeted killing requires clearer legal standards and after-the-fact accountability. If the executive claims authority to kill even citizens abroad who are deemed enemy combatants, the evidentiary and legal basis cannot remain wholly internal. Drone warfare lowers the political cost of force; democratic institutions must raise the deliberative threshold.

The presidency is the central actor, but citizens and legislators must change their expectations. We cannot demand perfect safety and then be shocked when executives accumulate power to promise it. A mature democracy accepts risk as the price of liberty. It also recognizes that abuses committed in the name of security can make the country less safe by breeding distrust at home and resentment abroad.

The scaffolding around the Capitol is visible. The scaffolding of the security state is not. That invisibility is precisely the problem. Representative democracy cannot govern what it is forbidden to discuss. If legislatures are to recover dignity, they must reclaim authority over war, surveillance, and secrecy. Otherwise the most consequential decisions of the republic will continue to be made in rooms where the people’s representatives arrive late, speak softly, and leave unable to tell us what happened.
