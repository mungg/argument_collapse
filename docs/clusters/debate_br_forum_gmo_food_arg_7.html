"While genetic engineering can be a useful tool, the current commercial GMO system is problematic because it prioritizes industrial monocultures, corporate power, and chemical dependency over genuine agricultural sustainability."

Pamela Ronald asks us to stop judging seeds by the method used to create them and start judging them by their social, environmental, and economic effects. That is a good standard. Applied seriously, however, it leads to a more skeptical conclusion than Ronald’s essay permits. The problem with genetically engineered agriculture is not primarily that recombinant DNA is spooky. The problem is that the dominant actually existing GMO system has been built around monoculture, proprietary seed, chemical simplification, and the political power of a few firms.

Ronald’s best examples are disease-resistant papaya, future wilt-resistant bananas, virus-resistant cassava, and Golden Rice. These are the examples biotechnology advocates almost always choose because they frame genetic engineering as a humanitarian instrument: public science rescuing vulnerable people from hunger or micronutrient deficiency. Some such projects may indeed be worth supporting. But they are not the main story of commercial genetic engineering in 2013. The main story is herbicide-tolerant soy, corn, cotton, and canola, and Bt crops planted over vast acreage in industrial systems.

Those systems have generated benefits, but also predictable ecological problems. Ronald acknowledges glyphosate-resistant weeds, but understates their significance. Resistance is not incidental; it is the biological consequence of simplifying weed control around a single chemical. Farmers who adopted Roundup Ready crops often did so because glyphosate was comparatively less toxic and because the system was convenient. But convenience became dependence. As resistant weeds spread, farmers face pressure to use additional herbicides, tank mixes, and crops engineered to tolerate older chemicals. That is not Rachel Carson’s dream of biology replacing chemistry. It is a treadmill.

The same danger applies to Bt. Bt crops can reduce insecticide sprays, and that is a genuine benefit. But planting pest-resistant crops over large monocultures creates strong selection for resistance. Refuges can help, but they depend on compliance, monitoring, and institutional capacity. In places where small farmers lack extension support or where seed companies oversell the technology, resistance management can fail. Ronald mentions pink bollworm resistance in India as a failure of refuges, but that failure is not a footnote; it reveals that social systems determine whether biological tools remain durable.

More broadly, genetic engineering tends to encourage a seed-centered imagination. The field is treated as a delivery platform for traits. Yet many agricultural crises are not simply trait deficits. They arise from soil depletion, insecure land tenure, lack of credit, poor roads, weak local markets, gender inequality, conflict, and dependence on a narrow genetic base. Banana wilt in Uganda is devastating partly because farmers grow bananas as a staple and often lack resilient livelihood options. A resistant banana may help, but so might diversified farming, sanitation practices, farmer-to-farmer extension, and investment in rural infrastructure. A seed can be a tool; it cannot substitute for agrarian development.

Ronald is right that conventional breeding is not automatically benign. Mutation breeding is imprecise. “Natural” is not a serious regulatory category. But agroecological critics need not rely on naturalness. We can ask instead whether a technology increases farmer autonomy, biodiversity, soil health, and resilience. Many current GMO systems fail that test. Patented seeds and licensing agreements can limit saving and sharing. Consolidation in the seed industry narrows the range of available varieties. Public breeders increasingly operate in a world shaped by intellectual property restrictions and private partnerships. Even when farmers voluntarily buy seed, their choices occur within markets structured by corporate power.

Ronald says farmer adoption proves usefulness. It proves something, but not everything. Farmers adopt technologies under economic pressure. If neighbors plant herbicide-tolerant crops, if grain elevators and input dealers are organized around them, if labor is scarce, if credit packages favor them, adoption may be rational even when the system as a whole is ecologically brittle. Individual choice can aggregate into collective vulnerability.

Nor is opposition to GMOs equivalent to anti-vaccine activism. Vaccines have a public health logic: one person’s refusal can expose others to disease. GMO agriculture is a production system embedded in landscapes and markets. Its critics include plant scientists, ecologists, development scholars, farmers, and consumers concerned about governance. Some activists exaggerate health risks and circulate bad studies; they should be criticized. But Ronald’s analogy risks delegitimizing all dissent by associating it with fraud and ignorance.

What would a better position look like? First, regulate crops by trait and ecological context, not by simplistic pro- or anti-GMO categories. Second, fund public breeding that is not dependent on proprietary platforms. Third, require serious resistance-management plans and post-commercial monitoring. Fourth, protect farmers’ rights to save seed where appropriate and prevent seed-market concentration. Fifth, compare engineered crops not only with high-chemical conventional systems but with diversified agroecological alternatives.

The real question is not whether genetic engineering can ever be useful. It can. The question is whether it will deepen the industrial model that has already eroded biodiversity, polluted waterways, and pushed farmers into dependency, or whether it will be subordinated to a genuinely sustainable agriculture. Ronald invites us to look at consequences. On that ground, enthusiasm should be selective, conditional, and politically alert.

A wilt-resistant banana developed for smallholders through transparent public institutions may deserve support. A new generation of herbicide-tolerant crops designed to preserve chemical sales deserves resistance. The distinction matters. But making it requires seeing biotechnology not as “the truth about GMOs,” but as one contested instrument inside a much larger struggle over the future of food.

  7. 07A truly sustainable approach requires evaluating crops based on their specific ecological context and subordinating biotechnology to agroecological goals rather than corporate interests.
