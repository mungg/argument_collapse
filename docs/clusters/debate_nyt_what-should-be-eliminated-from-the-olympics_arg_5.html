"The modern pentathlon should be the first sport eliminated from the Olympics because it is an incoherent, outdated relic that lacks broad global relevance."

If the Olympics must shed a sport, it should begin with modern pentathlon. Not because its athletes lack courage or skill; quite the opposite. Fencing, swimming, riding, running and shooting are each demanding. The problem is that their combination is not a sport so much as a historical joke that stopped being funny.

Modern pentathlon was designed to approximate the skills of a 19th-century cavalry officer: ride an unfamiliar horse, fight with a sword, shoot, swim and run. That origin gives it charm, but charm is not enough to justify Olympic permanence. The Games should not be a museum of aristocratic military fantasies. They should showcase sports with broad participation, intelligible stakes and a living connection to how people actually compete around the world.

Pentathlon also suffers from incoherence. In triathlon, the disciplines are at least part of a continuous endurance challenge. In decathlon, the events are variations on track-and-field athleticism. Modern pentathlon is a grab bag. Success depends not only on training but on drawing a horse one has never ridden, an element that can make the event feel less like a test of excellence than a lottery with stirrups.

Defenders will say that the Olympics should preserve oddities. I agree, to a point. Oddity is part of the pleasure of the Games. But there are many lesser-known sports — weightlifting, wrestling, badminton, table tennis, canoeing — that have deep competitive cultures and passionate global followings. Pentathlon’s constituency is tiny, its symbolism dated and its television appeal limited.

The Olympics cannot be everything. If the movement wants to control costs and complexity without attacking the heart of the Games, it should retire the sport whose best argument is that Baron de Coubertin liked it. Modern pentathlon had a long run. That is not the same as a permanent claim.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The sport is based on an obsolete 19th-century military fantasy rather than a living competitive culture.
  2. 02The combination of disciplines is a disjointed grab bag that lacks the athletic coherence found in other multi-sport events like the triathlon or decathlon.
  3. 03The inclusion of unfamiliar horses introduces an element of chance that makes the competition feel more like a lottery than a true test of athletic excellence.
  4. 04The sport has a very small global constituency and limited television appeal compared to other niche Olympic sports.
  5. 05Historical sentimentality and the preference of the Games' founder are insufficient justifications for maintaining a sport's permanent Olympic status.

"Modern pentathlon should be eliminated from the Olympic Games because it is an outdated historical reenactment that lacks the relevance and accessibility required for a modern sporting event."

Modern pentathlon is the Olympic event most perfectly designed for its own obituary. It was created to simulate the skills of a 19th-century cavalry officer: ride an unfamiliar horse, fence, shoot, swim and run. That origin story is charming, but charm is not enough reason to preserve a sport in a crowded 21st-century Games.

The event’s defenders say it honors versatility. Fair enough. A good modern pentathlete is a remarkable all-around athlete. But the Olympics already has better tests of all-around excellence. The decathlon is brutally transparent: run, jump, throw, repeat, and let the points tell the story. Triathlon combines three widely practiced endurance disciplines in a form that ordinary people actually recognize. Modern pentathlon, by contrast, feels less like a universal test than a historical reenactment with medals.

It is also difficult to square with the Olympic movement’s professed interest in global accessibility. Swimming and running are widespread; fencing and show jumping are not. The riding portion, with horses assigned by draw, introduces an element that can look arbitrary to outsiders. A competitor’s medal hopes can depend not only on horsemanship but on the temperament and suitability of an animal he or she has just met. That may be part of the event’s lore, but it is a strange basis for Olympic justice.

Every sport has a constituency, and modern pentathlon’s is passionate. Yet if a new sport were proposed today that combined fencing, swimming, riding, running and shooting because of an old tale about a soldier behind enemy lines, would the IOC accept it? Almost certainly not. With golf and rugby sevens already scheduled to join the program in Rio, the Games need room. Retiring modern pentathlon would be less an act of vandalism than an admission that some traditions have reached a dignified end.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The sport's origin as a simulation of 19th-century cavalry skills is no longer a sufficient justification for its inclusion in the 21st-century Games.
  2. 02Other Olympic events, such as the decathlon and triathlon, provide superior and more transparent tests of all-around athletic excellence.
  3. 03The high costs and specialized equipment associated with fencing and show jumping make the sport inaccessible on a global scale.
  4. 04The practice of assigning unfamiliar horses by draw introduces an arbitrary element of chance that undermines the fairness of the competition.
  5. 05The sport would never be accepted under modern IOC criteria if it were proposed as a new event today.
