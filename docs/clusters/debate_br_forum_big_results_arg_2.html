"While behavioral economics and randomized evaluations provide productive insights into individual decision-making, they risk becoming a political distraction from the structural reforms necessary to address the root causes of poverty."

The behavioral economics approach to development that Glennerster and Kremer describe is intellectually elegant and empirically productive, but it carries a troubling implication that the authors do not fully explore: by focusing on how to change individual behavior, this framework may serve as a politically convenient distraction from the structural changes needed to address poverty.

Consider the range of policy responses the authors endorse: conditional cash transfers, school uniform subsidies, chlorine dispensers, small incentives for immunization. These are all interventions that work through the decisions of individuals, nudging them to make choices that mainstream economics and public health agree are beneficial. But none of these address the underlying conditions that produce poverty in the first place: unequal land distribution, extractive economic policies, discrimination against women and minorities, the concentration of political power in the hands of elites who benefit from the status quo.

A development strategy built around behavioral nudges can succeed in improving some outcomes—school enrollment, immunization rates, water treatment—without changing the structures that keep people poor. A young girl may complete primary school because her family received a conditional cash transfer, but she may still face a labor market that offers her only dead-end jobs at subsistence wages. She may have lower rates of teen pregnancy because her school fees were subsidized, but she may still live under a legal system that denies her the property rights that would allow her to accumulate assets.

The RCT methodology itself reinforces this focus on measurable individual outcomes rather than structural change. Randomized evaluations can measure whether a scholarship program increases girls' school attendance; they cannot easily measure whether the program changes gender norms, reduces political barriers to women's economic participation, or shifts the balance of power in communities. The measurable becomes the target, even when the measurable is not the most important.

The authors' reference to "big-think" fads is revealing. They contrast their experimental approach favorably with structural adjustment, community-driven development, and other paradigms that attempted broader transformations. But structural adjustment failed for reasons that were partly political—the reforms were imposed without democratic legitimacy and without regard for their distributional consequences—not because the underlying theory was entirely wrong about the importance of macroeconomic stability and market liberalization. By positioning behavioral economics as a corrective to "big-think" failures, the authors risk throwing out the structural baby with the ideological bathwater.

None of this means that the evidence the authors present is wrong or that the interventions they recommend are harmful. Conditional cash transfers have demonstrably improved school attendance in many countries. Chlorine dispensers have saved lives. The world is better off with these programs than without them. But a development strategy that relies entirely on behavioral nudges while neglecting structural transformation may make the poor more efficient participants in an unjust system rather than participants in a system that is becoming more just. The authors should grapple more seriously with this political dimension of their work.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The interventions endorsed by behavioral economics focus on nudging individual choices rather than addressing systemic issues like unequal land distribution, extractive policies, and political elite capture.
  2. 02Improving individual outcomes like school enrollment or immunization rates does not guarantee long-term economic mobility if the underlying labor markets and legal systems remain discriminatory or exploitative.
  3. 03The randomized controlled trial (RCT) methodology inherently prioritizes measurable individual behaviors over complex, hard-to-quantify shifts in social norms and power dynamics.
  4. 04By framing their approach as a superior alternative to 'big-think' structural paradigms, the authors may be neglecting the essential role of macroeconomic and institutional transformation in development.
  5. 05A development strategy limited to behavioral nudges may simply make the poor more efficient participants in an unjust system rather than creating a more equitable one.

"While behavioral economics and randomized evaluations provide valuable insights into what programs work, their successful application at scale requires addressing deep-seated government implementation failures and institutional capacity constraints that the authors underemphasize."

As someone who has spent years working on implementation of development programs in challenging environments, I read the Glennerster and Kremer essay with both appreciation for its insights and frustration at how far the evidence it describes still is from ordinary government practice in most developing countries.

The gap between what randomized evaluations show works and what actually happens on the ground is enormous. The authors are right that NGOs have been more flexible than governments in adopting evidence-based approaches. But the development challenges that matter most—universal primary education, basic health coverage, clean water access—cannot be solved primarily through NGO programs. They require government systems that reach hundreds of millions of people consistently and sustainably.

The authors note that "many countries have abolished fees on paper only to see informal fees creep back." This is the central implementation challenge, and it is far more common than the essay suggests. Free primary education policies have been announced in dozens of African countries; in most of them, informal fees—collected by teachers and school administrators who receive little or no government salary—continue to extract money from poor families. The behavioral solution to this problem might be to design better monitoring and accountability mechanisms, but the authors do not address this possibility.

The supply-side challenges are equally daunting. The chlorine dispenser program achieved remarkable adoption rates, but scaling this up requires government capacity to maintain and refill dispensers across thousands of villages, often in regions with poor transport infrastructure and weak logistics systems. The Kenyan government's ability to sustain the program depends on institutional capacities that take years to build and that cannot be replicated quickly by simply copying the program design.

There is also a human resource problem that the essay understates. Many developing-country health systems suffer from severe shortages of trained workers, and those workers who exist often have incentives to maximize their private income rather than to provide high-quality public services. The authors mention "motivating workers" as a challenge, but this is not a technical problem with a simple solution. In many countries, paying health workers better is politically difficult because it requires either raising taxes or reallocating budgets from other uses; it may also face resistance from civil service bureaucracies that resist performance-based components.

The implication is not that behavioral economics insights are wrong but that their translation into policy requires sustained institutional development that the authors' framework underemphasizes. We need to understand not only what programs work but how to implement them through government systems that are often dysfunctional, corrupted, and unresponsive to citizen needs.

A research agenda focused purely on what works at small scale is necessary but not sufficient. What is also needed is research on how to build implementation capacity and how to sustain programs over time. The authors should acknowledge that moving from evidence to scaled impact requires a different kind of knowledge and different institutional investments than the RCT approach alone can provide.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Large-scale development goals like universal education and health cannot be solved by flexible NGOs alone and must be integrated into often-dysfunctional government systems.
  2. 02The persistence of informal fees in countries that have officially abolished them demonstrates that behavioral solutions must account for the lack of monitoring and accountability within bureaucracies.
  3. 03Scaling successful pilot programs, such as chlorine dispensers, depends on long-term institutional logistics and infrastructure that cannot be bypassed by simple program design.
  4. 04Human resource challenges, including worker shortages and political resistance to performance-based pay, present complex systemic hurdles that technical behavioral fixes cannot easily resolve.
  5. 05Research must expand beyond small-scale efficacy to focus on how to build the implementation capacity and political will necessary to sustain programs over time.

"While behavioral nudges and demand-side incentives are valuable, they must not distract from the fundamental necessity of addressing supply-side constraints and institutional capacity in development policy."

While Glennerster and Kremer provide valuable evidence about consumer behavior and demand-side interventions, their essay understates the importance of supply-side constraints in limiting the effectiveness of health and education programs. A focus on behavioral nudges and small incentives can distract attention from the fundamental need to build institutional capacity and ensure that services are actually available to the people who need them.

Consider the immunization example that the authors cite with enthusiasm. Raising immunization rates from 5 percent to 38 percent by providing lentils and plates to Rajasthani mothers is impressive, but it presupposes that the vaccines themselves are available and that the cold chain for storing them is functioning. In many of the world's poorest regions, the primary constraint on immunization coverage is not parental procrastination but broken supply chains, missing health workers, and clinics without basic equipment. If we redirect development resources toward incentive schemes for demand while neglecting the infrastructure needed to deliver services, we may end up with more people seeking care that the system cannot provide.

The authors acknowledge that "we don't know the impact of fees on the supply side" and that small payments can "motivate workers—who often pocket them—to show up." This is an understating of the problem. In many developing countries, health and education workers are poorly paid, inadequately supervised, and often absent from their posts. The authors note that "a number of studies are now focused on this problem"—but this research focus comes late, after years of designing demand-side interventions based on behavioral economics insights.

The chlorine dispenser example itself illustrates the supply-side challenge. The authors describe how dispensers reduce packaging costs and make chlorine available at communal water sources. But someone has to refill those dispensers, maintain them, and ensure a consistent supply of chlorine. The remarkable 93 percent adoption rate in communities with dispensers is a tribute to the program design, but it also depends on a reliable supply chain that the authors do not fully describe. Scaling this approach to thousands of villages would require building logistics systems that are far more complex than the behavioral interventions themselves.

A related supply-side issue is quality. Even when people access health or education services, the quality may be so low that the interventions have minimal impact. Millions of children in developing countries attend school but learn little; millions receive health care but are misdiagnosed or receive ineffective treatments. Addressing these quality failures requires investments in teacher training, curriculum reform, clinical supervision, and facility maintenance—interventions that are institutionally intensive and resist the kind of small-scale testing that randomized evaluations enable.

The behavioral economics perspective, by focusing on individual decision-making, may inadvertently blame the victim. When poor families fail to adopt beneficial health practices, we attribute this to procrastination or cognitive limitations. But sometimes the rational response to a low-quality, unreliable, and unresponsive health system is to invest one's limited resources elsewhere. Improving demand-side uptake without addressing supply-side quality is like putting a beautiful facade on a building with a crumbling foundation.

Development policy needs both: better understanding of how people make decisions, and sustained investment in the institutions that deliver services. The authors' work on the first is valuable; what is needed now is a parallel effort to understand how to build supply-side capacity in ways that work with the grain of human behavior rather than against it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Demand-side incentives like lentils for vaccines are ineffective if the underlying infrastructure, such as cold chains and supply lines, is broken.
  2. 02The focus on consumer behavior overlooks the critical problem of service providers who are often poorly paid, inadequately supervised, and frequently absent from their posts.
  3. 03Scaling behavioral interventions, such as chlorine dispensers, requires complex logistics and maintenance systems that are more difficult to build than the interventions themselves.
  4. 04Increasing the quantity of service adoption is of little use if the quality of the health care or education provided remains unacceptably low.
  5. 05What behavioral economists view as irrational procrastination may actually be a rational choice by the poor to avoid investing in unreliable or unresponsive systems.
  6. 06Development success requires a parallel effort to build supply-side capacity that works in tandem with our understanding of human decision-making.

"While behavioral economics offers a powerful, evidence-based method for improving development outcomes, it must be integrated with other economic frameworks to address the structural and institutional causes of poverty that individual-focused interventions overlook."

The Glennerster and Kremer essay describes a powerful approach to development that deserves serious consideration alongside other frameworks for understanding poverty and designing interventions to address it. Comparing the behavioral economics paradigm to its main alternatives reveals both its distinctive strengths and its characteristic blind spots.

The main alternative frameworks in development include: neoclassical economics (which emphasizes market liberalization and getting prices right), institutional economics (which focuses on property rights, rule of law, and governance quality), human capital theory (which emphasizes education and health investments), and more structuralist approaches (which focus on political economy, class relations, and the historical roots of underdevelopment). Each of these has contributed insights; each has also failed in characteristic ways.

The behavioral economics approach has a distinctive advantage over pure neoclassical economics: it recognizes that markets do not always produce optimal outcomes even when prices are correct, because individuals themselves may not behave as the models assume. This is a genuine advance that explains why many market-oriented reforms have underperformed expectations.

The approach also has advantages over purely structuralist frameworks: by focusing on measurable interventions and rigorous evaluation, it avoids the vagueness and ideological contestation that often characterize political economy analysis. When the authors show that small incentives increase immunization rates, that evidence is hard to dispute and relatively easy to act on.

But the behavioral approach has characteristic limitations. By focusing on individual decision-making, it tends to treat poverty as a condition of individuals rather than as a product of social structures. The authors note that "the poor weigh economic factors and make decisions about whether and how to invest in education and health" and that "their decisions are not always optimal for them or for society." But this framing implicitly blames the poor for making suboptimal choices rather than asking why the choices available to them are so constrained.

The structuralist alternative would ask: what political and economic forces have produced a situation in which a school uniform represents a significant barrier to education? Why does a family lack the cash to pay $6 for a uniform that would increase their child's lifetime earnings substantially? Why is clean water not available through public systems that would eliminate the need for individual treatment? These questions point toward different interventions—land reform, progressive taxation, public investment in infrastructure—than the behavioral framework generates.

A mature development strategy would draw on multiple frameworks rather than privileging any single one. Behavioral economics can tell us how to improve the uptake of existing services; institutional economics can tell us how to build the governance capacity that makes services available; structural analysis can tell us why the underlying distribution of assets and power produces poverty in the first place.

The authors make a contribution by demonstrating what behavioral economics can offer. They would make a greater contribution by acknowledging what it cannot provide and by building bridges to other approaches that address the dimensions of poverty that their framework cannot reach.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The behavioral approach improves upon neoclassical economics by accounting for non-rational human behavior and explaining why market-oriented reforms often fail.
  2. 02The use of rigorous, randomized evaluations provides clear, actionable evidence that avoids the ideological vagueness of structuralist political economy analysis.
  3. 03The behavioral framework tends to treat poverty as a result of suboptimal individual decision-making rather than as a product of constrained choices and social structures.
  4. 04By focusing on small-scale incentives, the approach may ignore broader solutions like public infrastructure investment, land reform, and progressive taxation.
  5. 05A comprehensive development strategy requires a multi-disciplinary approach where behavioral insights improve service uptake while institutional and structural analyses address governance and power imbalances.
