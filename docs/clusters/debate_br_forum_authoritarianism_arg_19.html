"While moderation is not a sufficient strategy on its own, it remains an essential tool for democratic communication and brand management that Democrats cannot afford to abandon."

Bonica and Grumbach make a useful correction to lazy punditry, but they overcorrect. “Moderation” is often invoked as a magic word, and it is true that some consultants speak as though elections can be won by sanding down every controversial edge. But the fact that moderation is not sufficient does not mean it is electorally irrelevant. Nor does the failure of Kamala Harris in 2024 prove that popularism was tried and found wanting. It may prove something more prosaic: the Democratic brand was already badly damaged, the incumbent party was punished for inflation and disorder, and voters did not believe a late effort at repositioning.

The lead essay attacks a caricature of popularism. At its best, popularism does not say that voters are consumers with fixed preferences and candidates should abandon all leadership. It says that political parties should be disciplined about emphasizing popular commitments and avoiding needless association with unpopular ones. That is not pandering; it is democratic communication. If voters hate a slogan, stop using the slogan. If voters like a policy when described plainly but dislike activist jargon surrounding it, describe it plainly. If a party’s professional class has drifted into norms and vocabulary far removed from most citizens, the party should notice.

There is nothing anti-democratic about taking public opinion seriously. Leadership is not the opposite of listening. A leader who refuses to learn what citizens fear, resent, value, or misunderstand is not brave; he is self-indulgent. Franklin Roosevelt was a transformative leader, but he was also a brilliant reader of public mood. The civil rights movement changed public opinion, but its leaders were careful, strategic, and attentive to persuasion. Successful politics requires both conviction and translation. Popularism, properly understood, is about translation.

The authors are correct that the median voter theorem in its simplest form cannot explain modern politics. Voters are not tidy ideological calculators. Many hold cross-pressured views: economically left, culturally conservative; libertarian on drugs, punitive on crime; pro-choice but anxious about gender identity; anti-corporate but anti-tax. This complexity, however, strengthens rather than weakens the case for message discipline. If voters do not think in coherent left-right terms, then parties must be especially careful not to let opponents define them by their most alienating associations.

The essay also treats “moderation” as if it mostly means moving right. But moderation can mean many things. It can mean ideological centrism, rhetorical restraint, cultural familiarity, pragmatic sequencing, or simply refusing to take unpopular bait. A candidate who supports universal health care but speaks in concrete terms about premiums and hospitals may be “moderate” in style while progressive in policy. A candidate who supports immigration reform but also acknowledges disorder at the border may be moderate in affect, not reactionary in substance. A candidate who defends trans people’s dignity while recognizing parental anxieties and biological realities in sports may be practicing democratic persuasion, not betrayal.

This distinction matters because many voters do not experience politics through policy white papers. They experience it through symbols. When Democrats appear unwilling to criticize activists, bureaucratic excess, campus illiberalism, or disorder in blue cities, many voters infer that the party is captured by forces hostile to ordinary common sense. Republicans exploit this relentlessly, often dishonestly. But dishonest attacks work best when they attach to something recognizable. Popularism asks Democrats to remove the handles.

Bonica and Grumbach point to research showing that candidate ideology has little consistent effect on vote share in the Trump era. That may be true, but the conclusion is narrower than the essay implies. Congressional candidate ideology is not the same as national party image. A moderate Democrat in a swing district may not outrun a toxic national brand; that does not mean the national brand’s perceived extremism is irrelevant. Indeed, the nationalization of politics makes brand management more important, not less. If every Democrat is tied to “the Democrats,” then what voters think Democrats stand for becomes central.

The essay’s use of 2024 is also too quick. Harris did distance herself from some progressive positions and courted moderate Republicans. But voters do not instantly update their views because a candidate changes emphasis for several months. Harris had a long public record, including positions from the 2019 primary that Republicans replayed constantly. The Democratic Party had years of accumulated cultural baggage from debates over policing, immigration, schooling, COVID restrictions, crime, language norms, and gender. A short campaign saying “kitchen table issues” could not erase that. If anything, 2024 supports the popularist warning: do not let your party become identified with unpopular ideas in the first place.

The authors are strongest when arguing for anti-corruption politics. Democrats should absolutely attack insider trading, judicial corruption, corporate rent-seeking, monopoly power, billionaire influence, and the revolving door. These issues can unite voters across ideological lines and give democracy a material meaning. But an anti-corruption message will not exempt Democrats from hard questions on immigration, crime, energy, and cultural conflict. Voters can believe Congress is corrupt and still think Democrats are too permissive on the border. They can hate corporate greed and still resent progressive language codes. They can want billionaires taxed and still worry that Democrats look down on religious or rural life. Anti-corruption is a frame, not a force field.

Nor is “anti-establishment credibility” automatically a left-wing asset. Donald Trump’s entire appeal rests on anti-establishment performance, even as he governs for cronies and loyalists. Robert F. Kennedy Jr.’s rise showed that distrust of institutions can take strange, conspiratorial, and dangerous forms. If Democrats decide that the answer to low trust is to sound more anti-system, they may feed the very cynicism that authoritarians exploit. The challenge is to criticize corruption without delegitimizing the possibility of competent government. That requires precision, not just anger.

Turnout, too, is not a substitute for persuasion. The essay notes that nonvoters in 2024 may have leaned slightly toward Trump. That admission is important. The old progressive assumption that higher turnout automatically benefits Democrats has become less reliable as the parties realign by education, class, and culture. Many low-propensity voters are not left-wing citizens waiting to be inspired. Some are alienated, culturally conservative, politically inattentive, or drawn to strongman politics. Mobilizing them may require moderation, at least on some issues and in some tones.

A better conclusion would be pluralism. Democrats should stop worshiping moderation, but they should also stop treating popularism as cowardice. They need a party capable of moral conflict and cultural reassurance, economic populism and institutional competence, base mobilization and swing-voter persuasion. In some districts, a fiery anti-corporate progressive may be the strongest nominee. In others, a veteran or mayor with moderate instincts may be the only plausible Democrat. At the presidential level, the party needs to reduce the number of fronts on which it is fighting the electorate.

The authors warn against “careful positioning.” But careful positioning is not the enemy of democracy. In a closely divided country with counter-majoritarian institutions, it is often the price of power. The enemy is soulless positioning: poll-chasing without conviction, message discipline without a theory of change, triangulation that insults voters’ intelligence. Democrats should be bolder against corruption, more serious about democratic reform, and less deferential to donors. They should also be more willing to say no to unpopular activist demands and to speak in a language normal people recognize.

Authoritarianism will not be defeated by moderation alone. On that Bonica and Grumbach are right. But neither will it be defeated by dismissing moderation as a relic. The task is not to choose between courage and popularity. The task is to make democratic politics courageous enough to matter and popular enough to win.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Popularism is not about pandering or abandoning leadership, but about the disciplined translation of convictions into language and symbols that resonate with the public.
  2. 02The failure of the 2024 campaign reflects a long-term damage to the Democratic brand rather than a failure of moderate strategy itself.
  3. 03In a nationalized political environment, moderation is necessary to prevent the national party brand from being defined by its most unpopular or alienating associations.
  4. 04Voters often hold complex, cross-pressured views that require message discipline and cultural reassurance rather than a simple move to the ideological left.

  7. 07The Democratic Party must embrace a pluralistic approach that combines base mobilization with the strategic avoidance of unnecessary conflict with the electorate.
