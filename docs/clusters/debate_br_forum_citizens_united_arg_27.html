The False Promise of “Dependency Corruption”

Lawrence Lessig’s essay is a learned and passionate brief against the current campaign finance system, and it deserves careful engagement. But its central innovation — the concept of “dependency corruption” — is a dangerous misstep that would empower government to suppress political speech on the basis of vague perceptions of undue influence. Lessig’s argument, if accepted, would erode the very First Amendment protections that have safeguarded robust democratic debate.

First, Lessig’s definition is impossibly broad. He says institutional corruption exists when an “influence, financial or otherwise, within an economy of influence … weakens the effectiveness of an institution, especially by weakening public trust.” By this standard, nearly any advocacy group that persuades lawmakers to adopt its view could be accused of corrupting the institution, because the public might perceive that influence as improper. The NRA’s lobbying on gun rights, the Sierra Club’s pressure on environmental regulations, or unions’ push for labor protections all could be swept into the “dependency corruption” net if a legislature decided that these influences distract representatives from their proper dependence on the people. But in a pluralist democracy, representatives are supposed to be responsive to a wide range of constituents, including organized groups. The fact that some citizens pool their resources to make their voices heard is not a defect; it is a hallmark of free association.

Lessig might reply that his concern is only with the dependence created by campaign contributions and independent expenditures, not with ordinary lobbying. But the line is blurry. If a corporation spends money on ads praising a congressman’s vote, is that a corrupting dependency or legitimate political speech? Lessig’s test — whether the system “weakens public trust” — is entirely subjective. It would hand to legislators the power to define which speech is too influential, and thus regulable, based on nothing more than opinion polls. The Court in Citizens United was not “Lochnerizing” when it insisted on a narrower, more objective standard for corruption; it was doing precisely what a court must do: ensuring that the government’s asserted interest is sufficiently weighty and defined to justify abridging fundamental rights.

Second, Lessig greatly overstates the gap between the interests of voters and contributors. It is true that a majority of campaign funds often come from outside a candidate’s district or state. But that does not mean the money is opposed to voters’ interests. Out-of-state donors to John Kerry, for example, were largely individuals who shared his voters’ policy preferences. In a nationalized media environment, voters in Massachusetts care about national issues that are also important to donors across the country. There is no inherent conflict, and Lessig provides no systematic evidence that legislative votes are systematically skewed by out-of-district contributions once constituent preferences are controlled for. The political science literature on this question is mixed, but the notion that Congress is simply a marionette of its funders is an article of faith, not a proven fact.

Moreover, the “iceberg theory” of campaign influence, while clever, proves too much. If the mere threat of spending can influence a legislator, then almost any public statement of political preference becomes a potential source of “dependency corruption.” Should we then regulate newspapers that publish critical editorials, or celebrities who threaten to fund opponents? The First Amendment does not allow us to silence some speakers because others might feel intimidated. The proper response to a credible threat of spending is counter-speech — more speech, not less.

Lessig’s proposed remedies, such as democracy vouchers, are at least voluntary. But the logic of dependency corruption inevitably points toward mandatory limits on both contributions and expenditures. That path leads back to the very regime that Citizens United struck down, and that the Court had been dismantling since Buckley: a system in which incumbent politicians get to decide how much speech about them is too much. That is a cure far worse than the disease. The Framers gave us the First Amendment precisely to prevent government from insulating itself from the winds of public opinion, even when those winds are carried by money. A republic that cannot tolerate the clamor of independent political spending is a republic that has lost faith in its citizens.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The definition of dependency corruption is impossibly vague and could be used to criminalize legitimate advocacy by any organized group that successfully persuades lawmakers.
  2. 02The standard of 'weakening public trust' is entirely subjective and would allow legislators to regulate speech based on fluctuating opinion polls rather than objective legal principles.
