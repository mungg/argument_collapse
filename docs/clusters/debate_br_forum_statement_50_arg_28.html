"While Tom Hayden celebrates participatory democracy as a timeless ideal, contemporary protest movements demonstrate that direct, non-hierarchical participation is politically ineffective unless it is channeled into disciplined, institutional organization and representative power."

Tom Hayden’s anniversary essay is a valiant attempt to trace the genealogy of today’s protest movements to a founding text of the New Left. In doing so, he performs a remarkable act of rhetorical alchemy, transforming the scattered, often ephemeral actions of the past few years into a coherent tradition. But somewhere in the midst of his hopeful comparisons, a crucial distinction gets buried: the difference between participation and efficacy. The Occupy encampments, the mass immigrant marches, the Wisconsin rallies, and the pepper-sprayed tuition protesters are all evidence that large numbers of Americans are angry and want to be heard. What they are not, by and large, is evidence that participatory democracy works. In fact, the experience of these movements suggests that the embrace of direct, non-hierarchical participation has repeatedly come at the expense of tangible political victories, and that the Left’s allergic reaction to formal organization is a recipe for perpetual marginality.

Let me be blunt. Representative democracy, for all its manifest and dispiriting flaws, remains the most durable mechanism ever devised for translating the diffuse preferences of a large population into binding decisions. It does so through institutions—parties, legislatures, courts—that are capable of aggregating interests, mediating conflict, and imposing compromises. These institutions are frequently captured by the wealthy and powerful, but they are also the only arenas where the poor and the marginalized can hope to win enforceable rights. The civil rights movement, which Hayden invokes as a model, understood this perfectly. The sit-ins, freedom rides, and marches were not ends in themselves; they were designed to create a moral crisis that would force the federal government to enact legislation. The participatory ethos was in service of a representative outcome—the Civil Rights Act, the Voting Rights Act. The movement was brilliantly strategic because it combined disruptive direct action with a clear-eyed focus on the levers of actual state power.

Contrast this with the Occupy movement, which Hayden presents as the latest embodiment of Port Huron’s spirit. Occupy’s defining gesture was its refusal to issue demands. The General Assemblies, for all their internal fascination, were constitutively incapable of brokering the kind of bargains that produce legislative change. The movement’s impact on public discourse—the “99 percent” frame—was real, but in strictly political terms, it produced nothing. The banks were not broken up. The foreclosure crisis was not halted. The influence of money in politics was not diminished. Instead, the energy of millions was channeled into an open-ended process that, within a few months, had largely dissipated. This is not an anomaly; it is the predictable result of a political philosophy that prizes expression over instrumentality.

The same pattern recurs across the contemporary movements Hayden celebrates. The 2006 immigrant marches did not produce comprehensive immigration reform. The Wisconsin uprising, for all its fervor, failed to recall Governor Walker or reverse his dismantling of collective bargaining rights. The student tuition protests have not halted the upward spiral of college costs. In each case, a surge of participation was unable to translate itself into the currency of institutional power. Why? Because participation without organization is steam without an engine. It makes a lot of noise and then it vanishes.

Hayden’s approving reference to the “underground press and independent media” is particularly telling. The new media environment he celebrates is not a replacement for the party structures and unions that have atrophied over the past four decades. It is, rather, a compensation for their absence—a way of feeling connected and informed without actually subscribing to a disciplined organization that can deliver votes, mobilize sustained pressure, and hold elected officials accountable between election cycles. The Internet may be the greatest expansion of participatory *expression* in history, but it is not an expansion of participatory *power* unless it feeds into concentrated, strategic action. The Right, incidentally, understands this. While leftists tweet and blog, the Koch brothers and their network have spent decades funding a vast infrastructure of think tanks, policy shops, and state-level legislative campaigns. They are not practicing participatory democracy in Hayden’s sense; they are practicing representative democracy with ruthless effectiveness.

The inevitable reply is that representative democracy is broken, that the system is rigged, and that the parties are bought. All true. But the solution is not to abandon the field of institutional politics for the consolations of direct action. The solution is to build counter-institutions that can compete on that field. That means creating durable organizations with the capacity to recruit, train, and deploy candidates at every level of government. It means rebuilding a labor movement that can actually deliver economic leverage. It means fielding primary challenges against complacent incumbents and showing that a mobilized base can match corporate money with boots on the ground. None of this is as romantic as a General Assembly, and it requires the kind of hierarchical, bureaucratic structures that the Port Huron tradition has always distrusted. But it is the only way to turn the moral energy of the masses into actual, enduring change.

The Port Huron Statement’s call for participatory democracy was a cry against alienation and a plea for human scale. I am not unsympathetic. But what we need now is not more participation for its own sake but more power—power to check the banks, to decarbonize the economy, to provide healthcare and education as public goods. That power cannot be conjured through consensus alone. It must be organized, disciplined, and, yes, represented. Until the children of the ’60s can make their peace with that uncomfortable truth, the spirits that keep appearing before Tom Hayden will remain just that: spirits, haunting the margins of a world they cannot change.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The focus on participatory expression and consensus-based decision-making often comes at the expense of achieving tangible legislative victories and political outcomes.
  2. 02Successful historical movements, such as the civil rights movement, used direct action strategically to force changes within representative institutions rather than as an end in itself.
  3. 03Modern movements like Occupy Wall Street and the Wisconsin rallies failed to produce lasting change because they lacked the formal structures necessary to aggregate interests and broker political bargains.
  4. 04The expansion of independent media and digital participation provides a sense of connection but fails to replace the atrophied party and union infrastructures required to hold elected officials accountable.
  5. 05To achieve real power and counter corporate influence, the Left must move beyond romanticized direct action and build durable, hierarchical organizations capable of competing within the arena of representative democracy.
