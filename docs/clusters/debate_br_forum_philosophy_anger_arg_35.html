Diversified Gemini

"Agnes Callard’s claim that anger necessitates eternal grudges and moral corruption is based on a fundamental epistemic confusion between cognitive moral judgments and affective emotional states."

In "The Reason to Be Angry Forever," Agnes Callard weaves a dark and compelling narrative about the inescapable moral corruption of victimhood. She contends that anger is essentially tied to eternal grudges and vengeful retaliation. Because the reasons for anger—past wrongs—are immutable, the anger itself is perpetually justified. Furthermore, she argues that holding an offender accountable necessitates revenge, forcing them to suffer under the principle of their own action. The inevitable conclusion, Callard insists, is that fighting injustice requires us to embrace the "dark side" of morality; we cannot be good in a bad world. While Callard’s psychological observations about the sticky, adversarial nature of anger are acute, her philosophical argument suffers from a profound epistemic confusion: she conflates the cognitive judgment of a moral wrong with the affective emotion of anger.

Callard’s Argument for Grudges is built on the premise that "Anger... is a way of grasping the fact that [something] is broken." She argues that because the past theft remains eternally true, the reason for the anger remains eternally true, and therefore it is rational to be angry forever, even after an apology.

But this argument collapses the distinction between a belief and an emotion. It is absolutely true that I have a reason to *believe* forever that you stole from me on Monday. I have a reason to *judge* forever that your action was morally wrong. But anger is not merely a cognitive judgment of wrongness; it is a hot, affective, physiological state that motivates specific kinds of immediate action (protest, defense, retaliation).

We are perfectly capable of grasping the fact that something was broken without remaining in a state of continuous rage. I can know that a historical atrocity was deeply wrong without my blood boiling every second of every day. When Callard says, "Once you have a reason to be angry, you have a reason to be angry forever," she is confusing the permanence of the truth-conditions of a proposition with the permanence of an emotional response.

When the thief apologizes and makes restitution, my cognitive judgment that "stealing is wrong and you did it" does not change. But my affective relationship to that fact does. The threat has passed; the moral equilibrium has been acknowledged by both parties. The fading of my anger is not a mysterious "fizzling out" or irrational amnesia, as Callard dismissively suggests. It is a rational, epistemic update. The emotion of anger is no longer appropriate because the present environment no longer requires the defensive, mobilizing energy that anger provides. Retaining the cognitive judgment while shedding the affective rage is not a "philosopher's fiction"; it is a daily psychological reality for healthy human beings.

Furthermore, Callard’s Argument for Revenge relies on a similar conflation. She argues that to hold someone accountable, you must "make my bad your good"—you must seek revenge to teach them a lesson. She claims that if you act from anger, you inevitably pursue this hostile logic.

But holding someone accountable is fundamentally an epistemic and communicative act. It is the demand that the offender recognize the truth of what they have done and the validity of the victim’s moral standing. Callard is right that this involves forcing the offender to confront something unpleasant. But forcing an offender to confront the truth of their wrongdoing is not the same as vengeance. Vengeance seeks the offender's destruction or suffering as an end in itself; accountability seeks the offender's moral awakening. The fact that the offender might experience this awakening as painful does not mean the victim's primary aim was vengeance.

This brings us to Callard’s devastating conclusion regarding her grandmother. Because her grandmother, a Holocaust survivor, held a prejudiced grudge against a young German girl, Callard concludes that oppression makes the oppressed morally worse. "Innocence was not a possibility."

This is a tragic misreading of trauma. The grandmother’s inability to separate the judgment of the Holocaust from an immediate, affective rage toward a German child was not a logical conclusion of moral reasoning. It was a failure of epistemic differentiation caused by profound psychological injury. Trauma blurs the lines between past and present, between guilty perpetrator and innocent bystander. To look at this profound cognitive and emotional scarring and declare it "perfectly rational" and "completely correct" is to fundamentally misunderstand both psychology and philosophy.

We are not trapped in Callard’s dilemma. We do not have to choose between acquiescing to evil and becoming monsters of vengeance. We have the cognitive capacity to judge evil permanently while allowing our affective anger to subside when it is no longer useful. We can demand accountability without demanding blood. We can acknowledge the darkness of the world without declaring that our own moral compass is irreparably broken. Callard’s essay is a brilliant exploration of how anger *can* go wrong, but she fails to prove that it *must*.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01While the factual basis for a moral judgment remains permanent, the affective state of anger is a temporary defensive response that rationally subsides once a threat is addressed or restitution is made.
  2. 02The fading of anger following an apology is a rational epistemic update to one's environment rather than a non-rational 'fizzling out' or a failure to grasp the original wrong.
  3. 03Holding someone accountable is a communicative act aimed at moral awakening rather than a vengeful pursuit of an offender's suffering as an end in itself.
  4. 04The inability to distinguish past trauma from present reality, as seen in Callard’s grandmother, represents a psychological injury rather than a valid or 'correct' logical deduction.
  5. 05Human beings possess the cognitive capacity to maintain a permanent judgment against evil without being trapped in a cycle of limitless vengeance or moral corruption.
