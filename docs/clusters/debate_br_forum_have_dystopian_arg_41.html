"The lead essay underestimates the immediate urgency of AI-driven surveillance, which requires a distinct regulatory framework and more aggressive legal restrictions than those proposed for long-term technological redirection."

Acemoglu's essay addresses AI's effects on jobs and democracy but gives short shrift to surveillance—the dimension where AI's harms are most immediate and least speculative. Surveillance deserves prominence as a distinct concern requiring separate analysis and response.

Facial recognition is deployed today, not in some speculative future. Law enforcement agencies use it for suspect identification. Employers use it for workplace monitoring. The Chinese government uses it for Uyghur surveillance. The harms are documented: wrongful arrests based on flawed matches, political repression enabled by real-time identification, chilling effects on political speech.

These harms don't require waiting for AI to develop along its current trajectory—they're happening now. Acemoglu's essay mentions surveillance but treats it as one of several concerns alongside job automation. This framing understates the urgency. Job displacement occurs over years; surveillance operates in real-time. Democratic norms about privacy and anonymous speech are being eroded today, in ways that make the democratic reform Acemoglu calls for harder to achieve.

The essay's proposed solutions—government direction, norm changes, democratic oversight—are appropriate for long-term AI redirection but inadequate for immediate surveillance harms. We don't need to redirect AI research to stop facial recognition by law enforcement agencies; we need to ban or restrict its use through existing legal mechanisms.

Several approaches warrant consideration. First, facial recognition should be banned for law enforcement use absent judicial warrant and individualized suspicion—the same protections that apply to physical searches. Second, commercial deployment should require affirmative consent from subjects, with meaningful penalties for non-compliance. Third, public agencies should be prohibited from purchasing surveillance technologies from firms that sell to authoritarian governments.

The essay's treatment of the Pegasus and NSO Group scandal illustrates the problem. Acemoglu presents this as evidence that "public pressure" can redirect AI norms. But NSO Group continues operating; the hacks continue; the Israeli government continues supporting the firm. Public pressure produced one academic's resignation, not the structural change that would actually limit surveillance harms.

Surveillance concerns deserve their own framework, distinct from job-focused analysis. The values at stake—privacy, anonymous speech, freedom from tracking—are different from those at stake in job automation—economic security, meaningful work, shared prosperity. Treating surveillance as merely another AI concern risks subordinating these values to employment goals. Privacy deserves protection regardless of AI's effects on jobs.

  4. 04Relying on public pressure to change corporate behavior is insufficient, as evidenced by the continued operation of surveillance firms despite public scandals.
  5. 05The values of privacy and freedom from tracking are distinct from economic security and should not be subordinated to labor market concerns in AI policy debates.
