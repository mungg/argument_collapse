"While experimentalist governance provides a sound institutional framework for climate policy, it incorrectly identifies regulatory committees as the primary drivers of change when the true engines of transformation are social movements and disruptive entrepreneurs."

The forum essay by David Victor and Charles Sabel is the most important contribution to the intellectual architecture of climate policy in a decade. Its rejection of the false choice between top-down Kyoto and bottom-up Paris is overdue. Its recovery of the Montreal Protocol as an exemplar not of binding global targets but of an iterative, sectoral machinery of discovery is a genuine breakthrough. The concepts of “penalty defaults” and “thin consensus” will be standard fare in policy schools for years to come. Having acknowledged the grandeur of the intellectual achievement, I must now take up the burden of the respondent: to show that the achievement, while real, is systematically misapplied. The authors’ analytical lens, trained on the techno-regulatory puzzle of managing uncertainty, has generated a vivid image of the solution, but it has done so at the cost of erasing the principal agents of change. Experimentalist governance is a framework that can design the playing field, but it cannot kick the ball. In the real world, the critical driver of transformative innovation has not been the regulatory committee but the activist, the social movement, and the disruptive entrepreneur who forces the committee to exist in the first place. The authors have described the hardware of a potential transition; they have forgotten the software—the political consciousness that brings it to life.

Victor and Sabel’s reading of the Montreal Protocol is telling. In their account, the committees of experts, industry, and government are the locus of agency. DuPont breaks ranks, the tech panels assess alternatives, and the regime ratchets forward. But this narrative inverts the historical sequence. DuPont did not wake up one morning and demand a phaseout of CFCs as a strategic business move in a regulatory vacuum. It did so after a decade of intense public pressure, driven by a scientific and environmental movement that had popularized the image of the ozone hole, made aerosol cans a symbol of ecological menace, and generated sufficient consumer alarm that the firm’s brand was visibly suffering. The “penalty default” was not an ingeniously designed institutional trigger. It was the unruly, unpredictable surge of democratic outrage that made the existing status quo politically toxic. The committees were not the architects of transformation; they were the floodgates opened by a river of public pressure that had already broken the dam.

If we look at the cases that Victor and Sabel cite from the climate sphere, the same pattern holds. The EU’s Water Framework Directive, with its river-basin experimentation, did not emerge from a vacuum of benevolent regulators. It was the product of decades of Green Party organizing across Europe, of local campaigns to clean up poisoned rivers, of a broad cultural shift toward ecological consciousness that made the regulation of agricultural runoff politically inevitable. The California Air Resources Board, likewise, was not created because regulators decided to experiment with automotive innovation. It was forced into existence by the choking smog of Los Angeles and the civic mobilization of residents who refused to accept poisoned air as the price of the automobile age. The key actors in these stories are the lung associations, the community organizers, the journalists, and the early green entrepreneurs who lobbied, protested, and invented their way through a political establishment that was, at every step, comfortably aligned with the industries they targeted. Victor and Sabel’s framework treats this seething political background as a kind of ambient condition, when it is in fact the prime mover.

This matters profoundly when we turn to the question of deep decarbonization. The authors are quite correct that global, legally binding consensus is a mirage. They are correct that the real work will be done in sectors, through iterative learning and peer review. But they are silent on the question of what will force the oil and gas sector, the aviation sector, the agriculture sector to enter this experimentalist cycle in the first place. The fossil fuel industry has no DuPont. It has no financial incentive to invent its own disruption, because its assets are not brands vulnerable to consumer boycott but reserves of commodities whose value depends on their being burned. A sectoral committee on oil and gas decarbonization, convened under current political conditions, would be a tomb for the energy transition, not its engine. It would produce elegant reports on carbon capture and storage, on blue hydrogen, on the potential of offsets, all designed to extend the combustion of fossil fuels into the indefinite future. The power to force a real transition in this sector will come not from within the committee but from without—from a climate movement that makes the cost of continued extraction politically prohibitive.

The last several years have offered a preview of what this looks like. The campaign to block the Keystone XL Pipeline, the divestment movement that has moved over $14 trillion of institutional funds out of fossil fuel assets, the youth-led strikes that brought millions into the streets worldwide in 2019, and the legal campaigns to hold fossil fuel firms accountable for climate damages are not merely a source of “popular protest” that, as the authors briefly note, has to date reinforced gridlock. This is the emerging infrastructure of the penalty default. It is the divestment pledge that makes a bank hesitate before financing a new tar sands project. It is the threat of a court ruling that creates a material risk of stranded assets, driving up the cost of capital for the most carbon-intensive firms. These are not alternatives to experimentalist governance; they are the engine that makes the experimentalist cycle turn. Without the movement, the innovators have no incentive to join, the laggards feel no threat of exclusion, and the “thin consensus” remains too thin to hold any weight.

The entrepreneurial dimension is the other half of this software. Victor and Sabel note that CARB worked with car companies and that Maersk coordinated technology demonstrations in the IMO. But the reason those efforts were possible was the presence of a competitive fringe of innovators—Tesla in the car market, wind and solar manufacturers from China and Europe in the power sector—who were already demonstrating that the clean alternative could be superior on its own terms, not just cleaner. Elon Musk, for all his flaws, has done more to decarbonize the global automobile fleet than all the sectoral committees of the UNFCCC combined. He did it not by joining a regulatory process but by bankrupting himself to build an electric car that people desired, forcing every major automaker on earth to follow suit. The role of experimentalist governance in this story is to take the innovation created by such entrepreneurs and generalize it, to build the charging infrastructure, to set the phase-out dates for the internal combustion engine, to penalize the laggards who would prefer to keep selling the old technology. But the sequence matters. The innovation comes first, born of vision and risk; the governance follows, to lock in the gains. Victor and Sabel’s argument reads as though the governance can be the primary driver, when it is more often a secondary, though essential, consolidator.

Ultimately, the Victor and Sabel proposal is a magnificent chassis in search of an engine. The design principles they articulate—disaggregation, iterative review, penalty defaults, the interplay of local knowledge and central coordination—are sound. They will be essential once the political victory is won. But to mistake the chassis for the whole machine is to risk building a vehicle that will never move. What the climate crisis demands now is not a retreat from politics into the committee room but an intensification of politics in the streets, the courts, and the corporate boardrooms. We need to make the fossil fuel industry as politically toxic as the tobacco industry, and as financially vulnerable as the coal industry. When we have done that, the experimentalists can take the wheel and show us how to drive.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The historical success of the Montreal Protocol was driven by public outrage and environmental activism that made the status quo politically toxic, rather than by the internal initiative of regulatory committees.
  2. 02Experimentalist governance fails to explain what would compel powerful, entrenched industries like fossil fuels to participate in decarbonization without external political and legal pressure.
  3. 03The 'penalty defaults' necessary to destabilize the status quo are currently being built by the climate movement through divestment campaigns, litigation, and protests rather than by institutional design.
  4. 04Technological innovation is typically born from the vision and risk of independent entrepreneurs and only later consolidated and generalized by governance structures.
  5. 05Victor and Sabel’s framework mistakes the secondary role of governance for the primary driver of change, risking a retreat into technocracy when the crisis demands an intensification of political struggle.
