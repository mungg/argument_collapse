Diversified Gemini

"While Emre correctly identifies that all reproduction is assisted, true reproductive solidarity requires a radical overhaul of family law to dismantle the biological supremacy that the state uses to delegitimize and police queer kinship."

Merve Emre’s "All Reproduction Is Assisted" does the vital work of shifting the feminist conversation away from the exhausted, bio-essentialist debates over "natural" childbirth toward a nuanced understanding of how marginalized people navigate assisted reproductive technology (ART). Emre’s essay shines in its empathetic portrayal of queer and trans individuals—such as N & K, Joanne Spataro, and micha cárdenas—who are forced to hack a deeply heteronormative medical system to create their families. However, while Emre successfully highlights the *desire* for biological kinship among queer and trans people, her essay stops short of examining how the medical-legal establishment actively enforces heteronormative family structures even within the realm of ART. If we are to realize Emre's vision of reproductive solidarity, we must confront the ways the law weaponizes biology to police what a family can be.

Emre recounts the absurd indignities suffered by N and K at their fertility clinic: a resident repeatedly assumes N is K’s friend rather than her partner, ignoring N completely during the insemination process. Emre frames this as a product of internalized and externalized homophobia. But this medical erasure is only the tip of the iceberg; it is the precursor to a much more dangerous legal erasure.

When queer couples use ART, their relationship to their child is immediately subjected to state scrutiny in ways straight couples never experience. N and K explicitly choose to use a sperm bank rather than a known donor because the "legal complications of a known donor were terrifying: paternity claims, visitation rights, a child dragged in and out of family court." This fear is entirely justified. In 2018, family law in the United States remains overwhelmingly tethered to the biological paradigm. In many states, the non-gestational mother in a lesbian couple (like N would be) must undergo a costly and invasive "second-parent adoption" to secure legal rights to her own child, even if she is listed on the birth certificate, and even if she and her partner are legally married. The state requires queer families to legally adopt the children they intentionally created through ART to protect themselves against a system that privileges genetic connection above all else.

This legal vulnerability exposes the hollow core of the fertility industry's promise of control. As Emre notes, S calls her frozen eggs an "insurance policy." But for queer families, biology is often a liability. When micha cárdenas points out that "The legal rights you have to your baby, are more tenuous if you don’t have a biological input," she is identifying the crux of the issue: the state uses biological connection as the ultimate arbiter of legitimacy.

Emre writes movingly about the desire of trans women like Spataro to have a "biological child with the woman I love." She criticizes the online commenters who demand that queer and trans people "model reproduction’s most inconvenient configurations and its most subversive politics." Emre is right that it is unfair to demand marginalized people perform radical politics with their personal lives. However, the tragedy is not just that Spataro wants a biological child; the tragedy is that our legal and social systems make a biological child the only unassailable form of kinship.

We must ask why the law struggles so mightily to accommodate multi-parent families, known sperm donors, or platonic co-parents. If, as Emre argues, "all reproduction is assisted," why does the law only recognize the assistants who wear white coats and operate within the capitalist framework of the clinic, while actively penalizing community-based, non-medical assistance (like a friend donating sperm)? The answer is that the legal framework of ART was designed by and for heterosexual couples dealing with infertility. The system is built to mimic the nuclear family, obscuring the "assistance" (the donor, the surrogate) to uphold the illusion of traditional, two-parent, biological reproduction.

To move forward, we must push beyond the narrative of individual access to technology. Narrative empathy, while crucial, is insufficient to protect families from the state. We need a radical overhaul of family law that decouples parental rights from genetic material. We need legal frameworks that recognize intentionality and caregiving as the primary basis for kinship, allowing for three or four legal parents, protecting known donors who do not wish to be parents, and securing the rights of non-gestational, non-genetic parents without the humiliation of adoption.

Emre concludes that acknowledging the positive right to reproduce could "allow the political to catch up to the technological." But technology is already miles ahead of the law. The political project must not just be about accessing the technology to create biological families; it must be about dismantling the biological supremacy that makes ART the only safe harbor for queer kinship. Until the law protects all families, regardless of how they are made, the emancipatory promise of reproductive technology will remain an illusion.
