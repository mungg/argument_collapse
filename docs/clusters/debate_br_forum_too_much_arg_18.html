"Nancy Hirschmann’s critique of 'care feminism' suffers from a class blindness that centers the dilemmas of elite, affluent households while ignoring the low-wage, often minority women whose labor sustains those very households."

**The Class Blindness of Both Sides**

Nancy Hirschmann is worried about her students. They are women at an elite university, mostly white, mostly affluent, who tell her they intend to marry well, stay home, and raise one or two children whom they will polish for the next round of elite reproduction. She finds that their conception of motherhood is morally smug, indifferent to other women, and that they invoke care feminism—her own tradition—to justify it. She is right to be worried. But her essay's response to this problem inherits the very class blindness that produces the problem in the first place.

I am the daughter of a woman who cleaned houses for a living. My mother did not "stay home"; she went to other people's homes to clean up after the children of women who stayed home. When she came back at night, she cooked, did laundry, helped me with my homework, and slept. By Hirschmann's framework, she was a working mother and so on the right side of the feminist ledger. By any honest accounting, she was also doing two unpaid shifts and one paid shift of care work, much of which subsidized the comfortable child-rearing of women like Hirschmann's students.

The reason Hirschmann's essay frustrates me is that it stages the debate inside a household of two adults—one a male earner, the other a wife choosing whether to "opt out"—and then asks how to redistribute labor within that household. But this household is not the typical American household. It is the elite, two-parent, single-earner-with-supporting-spouse household that Edward McCaffery's tax analysis foregrounds. The vast machinery of actual care work in America runs through nannies, housecleaners, home health aides, daycare workers, and the disproportionately immigrant and minority women who do this labor for low wages and few benefits. None of these women appear in Hirschmann's essay. They are not her students; they are not her students' mothers; they are not the women whose alleged "choice" to stay home is reinforcing patriarchy. They are the ones whose underpaid labor allows her students to imagine they can have both elite careers and intensive mothering—just by hiring it out, as Joan Williams, Bridget Anderson, and Pierrette Hondagneu-Sotelo have documented.

This omission is not incidental. Hirschmann's diagnosis—women care too much, and they must care less so men will care more—presupposes that the alternative to caring is paid professional labor. For a woman with options, this framing makes sense. For a woman without them, "care less" is not advice; it is unintelligible. Care less by whose lights, and toward whom? My mother could not care less about me; she could only care while exhausted.

When Hirschmann does discuss working-class women, it is mostly to note her students' contemptuous treatment of welfare mothers. She is right that this contempt is appalling. But the analysis then moves on without integrating welfare mothers into the substantive theory. The argument concludes with a Hirshmanite prescription about elite women refusing to leave the workforce. This prescription has nothing to say to a single mother whose paid work is grueling, low-wage, and offers nothing remotely like the leverage Hirschmann imagines paid work confers. It has nothing to say to a married couple in which both adults already work full-time at low wages and still cannot afford childcare. It has nothing to say about the structural fact that for many American families the "second shift" is performed not by a stay-at-home spouse but by grandparents, neighbors, older siblings, or simply nobody, with children left alone.

I want to push further. The "opt-out revolution" that Belkin diagnosed and that Hirschmann here critiques is, even by the most generous read of the data, a phenomenon affecting an extremely small slice of American women. Most women who leave the labor force do not do so to manage a Park Slope brownstone; they do so because they have been laid off, because childcare exceeds their wage, because a family member became ill, because the local labor market collapsed, or because their employer refused to accommodate a pregnancy. To treat the elite "opting out" narrative as the defining problem of women's labor in 2010 is to mistake an op-ed phenomenon for a labor-market reality.

What would a class-aware response look like? It would foreground the conditions of paid care workers, who are themselves mothers, and whose labor is the load-bearing wall under American gender arrangements. It would demand a universal, well-funded childcare system on the European model, rather than fixating on whether elite women should stay home. It would treat health care, sick leave, and a living wage as the actual preconditions of any meaningful "choice" about family form. It would ask whether the supposed superiority of paid work in conferring "leverage" survives contact with a Walmart shift. It would not ask, plaintively, whether feminism can convince Anthony's mother to care about civic virtue; it would ask whether the social wage Anthony's mother and millions of others rely on—Social Security, Medicare, public schools, public universities—can be defended against a generation of policy attacks.

I do not think Hirschmann would object to any of this in principle. But the essay's frame keeps drifting back to the elite household and its dilemmas, and then proposing prescriptions for women in general. This is the recurring pattern of a certain strand of academic feminism: a diagnosis derived from an unrepresentative sample, generalized into a universal counsel.

The most honest sentence in Hirschmann's essay is the admission that her students, encountering descriptions of welfare mothers, "draw on the idea of care to shore up class divisions and selfish interest." Yes. But care theory's complicity in this is less the issue than the broader fact that academic feminism has spent decades worrying about elite women's career trajectories while the women who clean up after them have remained largely invisible in the theory. Until that changes, "women must care less" will keep meaning, in practice, that elite women must hire other women to care more.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The essay focuses on a redistribution of labor between two parents in a single-earner household, which is not representative of the typical American family experience.
  2. 02The prescription for women to 'care less' and prioritize paid work is only viable for those with elite career options and is unintelligible to working-class women whose labor is grueling and low-leverage.
  3. 03The 'opt-out' phenomenon is a narrow trend among the wealthy, whereas most women leave the workforce due to economic necessity, lack of childcare, or employer inflexibility.
  4. 04By focusing on the career trajectories of elite women, the theory renders invisible the nannies, housecleaners, and care workers who perform the actual labor that allows elite women to 'opt in' to professional life.
  5. 05A meaningful feminist response must prioritize structural solutions like universal childcare, living wages, and a robust social wage rather than individualistic negotiations within elite marriages.
