Diversified Gemini

"Aziz Rana’s proposal to redefine freedom as a collectivist, state-driven project is a dangerous misreading of the American tradition that would ultimately extinguish the individual liberties essential for human progress."

Aziz Rana’s "A Different Freedom" presents a sweeping and provocative indictment of the American tradition of liberty, seeking to redefine it for the modern left. According to Rana, the American conception of freedom—characterized by individualism, self-reliance, and skepticism of state power—is not the product of philosophical enlightenment or a genuine desire for self-determination. Rather, it is the dark offspring of settler colonialism, indigenous expropriation, and imperial expansion. In this telling, American freedom has always been a zero-sum game, a weapon wielded by "insiders" (coded as white) against "outsiders" (everyone else). To reclaim freedom, Rana argues, the left must abandon this individualistic legacy and embrace a solidaristic, collective version of liberty, tearing down U.S. global hegemony and fundamentally restructuring domestic institutions.

While Rana’s historical narrative highlights genuine injustices in the American past, his central thesis rests on a fundamental misreading of the American founding and the enduring appeal of individual liberty. By reducing the American concept of freedom to a mere byproduct of racial domination and spatial expansion, Rana strips it of its philosophical core and moral power. In doing so, he proposes a cure that is arguably worse than the disease: a highly collectivist, state-driven model of society that would extinguish the very individual liberties that have driven human progress and emancipation for over two centuries.

The fatal flaw in Rana’s argument is his conflation of the *application* of freedom with the *concept* of freedom itself. It is an undeniable historical tragedy that the universal principles articulated in the Declaration of Independence were not immediately applied to all people. Slavery, the displacement of Native Americans, and the systemic discrimination against minorities are profound moral failings of the American project. However, the fact that the founders and subsequent generations of Americans hypocritically failed to live up to their own ideals does not render the ideals themselves corrupt.

When Thomas Jefferson wrote that all men are endowed with unalienable rights to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness, he was articulating a revolutionary concept of negative liberty—the idea that individuals possess inherent rights that the state cannot violate. This was a radical break from the hierarchical, aristocratic societies of Europe. Rana dismisses this negative liberty as merely the freedom "not having your immediate desires thwarted by others," contrasting it unfavorably with his preferred vision of "collective self-rule." But negative liberty is the bedrock of all other freedoms. Without the protection of the individual against the arbitrary power of the collective or the state, "democratic participation" quickly devolves into the tyranny of the majority.

Rana argues that the American conception of freedom was essentially parasitic, relying on the availability of expropriated land and the exploitation of marginalized people to provide "insiders" with a false sense of self-mastery. But this zero-sum economic logic ignores the immense wealth creation driven by free markets, innovation, and voluntary exchange. The prosperity of the United States, particularly in the 19th and 20th centuries, was not solely or even primarily the result of imperial extraction; it was the result of a system that rewarded entrepreneurship, protected property rights, and allowed individuals to keep the fruits of their labor. The "small-scale brand of white capitalism" Rana mentions was not uniquely white in its potential; it was a system of economic liberty that, once barriers to entry were gradually removed, allowed waves of immigrants and eventually marginalized domestic groups to achieve unprecedented social mobility.

Furthermore, Rana’s dismissal of the American skepticism of state power as a mere mechanism for maintaining racial hierarchies is deeply cynical. He claims that settlers viewed the state as a "sword" to be used against outsiders but never against themselves, and that any internal regulation was viewed as an attempt to treat white Americans as colonial subjects. While there are certainly instances where state power was weaponized against minorities, the enduring American suspicion of centralized authority is rooted in a much deeper philosophical tradition. It is born of the understanding that concentrated power is inherently dangerous, regardless of who wields it. The checks and balances of the Constitution were designed precisely to prevent the kind of overarching, transformative state power that Rana’s "solidaristic" project would require.

Rana’s alternative vision for freedom—a collective, interdependent model rooted in democratized workplaces, open borders, and a dismantled military—sounds appealing in the abstract. But in practice, redefining freedom as collective control over economic and social life inevitably requires a massive expansion of coercive state power. If freedom means that the workplace must be collectively managed, then the state must mandate the expropriation of private enterprise. If freedom means equal outcomes and guaranteed material security, the state must aggressively redistribute wealth. In every historical instance where society has prioritized collective "positive liberty" over individual "negative liberty," the result has not been an inclusive utopia, but economic stagnation and the suppression of dissent.

Moreover, Rana’s argument that U.S. foreign policy and military primacy are fundamentally incompatible with domestic freedom ignores the realities of the global geopolitical order. He laments the U.S. role in the Cold War and criticizes the contemporary defense budget, suggesting that American power is merely an engine of global exploitation. But the American-led liberal international order, for all its flaws and missteps, has presided over the greatest expansion of global prosperity and democratic governance in human history. To dismantle this architecture in the name of a vague "anti-imperial ethic" would not usher in an era of global solidarity; it would create a vacuum quickly filled by authoritarian powers like China and Russia, whose conceptions of freedom are vastly more restrictive and oppressive than anything in the American tradition.

Rana is correct that freedom is a contested term, and the left has every right to articulate its own vision of what liberty entails. But by tethering his vision to a complete repudiation of the American tradition of individual rights, private property, and skepticism of state power, he is asking Americans to abandon the very principles that have historically protected them from tyranny. The civil rights movement, which Rana rightfully praises, did not succeed by rejecting the American creed; it succeeded by forcing the nation to finally live up to it. Martin Luther King Jr. famously described the Constitution and the Declaration of Independence as a "promissory note to which every American was to fall heir." He did not demand a new definition of freedom; he demanded that the existing definition be universally applied.

In his zeal to deconstruct the American past, Rana offers a future in which the individual is entirely subsumed by the collective, where economic independence is replaced by reliance on the state, and where the necessary protections against domestic and foreign threats are discarded in the name of global utopianism. This is indeed a "different freedom"—one that looks suspiciously like a soft form of tyranny. The right’s current rhetorical excesses do not justify the left’s abandonment of classical liberal principles. True freedom remains what it has always been: the right of the individual to live their life according to their own dictates, secure in their person and property, and free from the arbitrary dictates of both the mob and the state.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Rana incorrectly conflates the historical failure to apply universal rights to all people with a fundamental corruption of the concept of negative liberty itself.
  2. 02The American tradition of skepticism toward state power is rooted in a sound philosophical understanding of the dangers of concentrated authority rather than a mere desire to maintain racial hierarchies.
  3. 03Economic prosperity in the United States was driven by free markets and individual entrepreneurship rather than being a zero-sum byproduct of imperial extraction and expropriation.
  4. 04Redefining freedom as collective control over social and economic life necessitates a massive expansion of coercive state power that historically leads to stagnation and the suppression of dissent.
  5. 05Dismantling the American-led global order in the name of anti-imperialism would create a power vacuum for truly oppressive authoritarian regimes rather than fostering global solidarity.
  6. 06Successful emancipatory movements, such as the civil rights movement, achieved progress by demanding the universal application of existing American principles rather than by repudiating them.
Diversified Gemini

"Aziz Rana’s attempt to redefine freedom as a collectivist, solidaristic project is a dangerous rebranding of state-centric socialism that threatens to destroy the individual liberties essential to preventing tyranny."

In "A Different Freedom," Aziz Rana weaves a complex narrative asserting that the American conception of freedom—centered on individualism, property, and limited government—is intrinsically linked to settler colonialism, racial domination, and imperial expansion. By framing negative liberty as a tool of exclusion and exploitation, Rana attempts to pave the way for a redefined, "solidaristic" freedom based on collective self-rule and massive institutional transformation. While Rana’s critique captures the hypocrisies of American history, his outright dismissal of individualistic freedom is profoundly dangerous. By equating classical liberal principles with racism and empire, Rana discards the most potent defense against state tyranny and offers in its place a collectivist vision that historically leads to coercion, not liberation.

Rana’s central thesis relies on a sleight of hand: he takes the historical reality that early American prosperity was fueled in part by westward expansion and the tragic displacement of Indigenous populations, and uses this to invalidate the *philosophical concept* of individual liberty itself. He argues that the desire for economic independence and "self-mastery" was inherently a "zero-sum" game, dependent on expropriating others. But this fundamentally misunderstands the nature of free markets and individual rights.

The core of classical liberal freedom is the idea that individuals own themselves and the fruits of their labor. This is not a zero-sum concept; wealth is not a fixed pie to be conquered, but something created through human ingenuity, labor, and voluntary exchange. While the specific historical context of 19th-century America involved land conquest, the underlying principle of economic liberty is what allowed the United States to transition from an agrarian frontier to a staggering engine of industrial and technological innovation. The prosperity of the modern era is not derived from colonial extraction—if it were, empires of the past would be the wealthiest nations today, rather than those that embraced market economies and the rule of law.

Rana dismisses the American suspicion of state power as merely a desire among white settlers to maintain their dominance unhindered. He writes that "the prevailing approach saw the apparatus of the state as a sword: one wielded by empowered Americans toward outsiders." This is a reductive reading of American political thought. The framing of the U.S. Constitution, with its strict limitations on federal power and its Bill of Rights, was explicitly designed to prevent the state from becoming a sword against *anyone*. The founders were steeped in the history of European tyranny; their fear of centralized power was not a cover for racial animus, but a rational response to the historical reality that unchecked states oppress their citizens.

By portraying negative liberty—the freedom *from* interference—as an exclusionary relic, Rana sets the stage for his preferred alternative: a positive liberty defined as "inclusive solidarity" and "collective self-rule." But this vision is fraught with peril. When freedom is redefined from "the right to be left alone" to "the requirement to participate in collective decision-making," the individual is inevitably subordinated to the group.

Consider Rana’s focus on the workplace. He argues that freedom requires "shared control over the decisions that shape the workplace." In theory, voluntary cooperatives are perfectly compatible with individual liberty. But Rana’s vision implies state-mandated restructuring of the economy (e.g., the PRO Act and "more far-reaching endeavors"). If the state dictates how businesses must be run and overrides the property rights of business owners in the name of "collective self-rule," it is utilizing the very coercive state power that classical liberals fear. Freedom is not achieved by replacing the authority of an employer—whom one can voluntarily leave—with the authority of a collective or a state committee from which there is no escape.

Furthermore, Rana’s vision of a new freedom requires an expansive, interventionist government. He laments that the American social safety net was left to market forces rather than "public goods entrenched through collective action and government provision." But expanding government provision necessitates expanding government extraction via taxation and regulation. A state powerful enough to provide total economic security and mandate collective workplace governance is a state powerful enough to dictate every aspect of an individual's life. The historical record of regimes that prioritized "collective solidarity" over individual rights—from the Soviet Union to contemporary socialist experiments—is a grim testament to the fact that when the individual is erased in favor of the collective, true freedom perishes.

Rana’s critique also extends to U.S. foreign and immigration policy. He argues that domestic freedom requires ending military spending and embracing "presumptive admission for all" immigrants. From a libertarian perspective, open borders and a restrained foreign policy are indeed desirable goals, aligning with the free movement of labor and a rejection of imperial overreach. However, Rana pairs these libertarian internationalist goals with a massive expansion of the domestic welfare state and economic regulation. This is an impossible contradiction. As Milton Friedman famously observed, you cannot have free immigration and a welfare state simultaneously; the burden on public resources would quickly collapse the system. Rana’s "solidaristic" freedom would require either stringent border controls to protect the collective wealth, or it would rapidly bankrupt the institutions he wishes to build.

Ultimately, Rana’s essay is an attempt to hijack the emotional resonance of the word "freedom" to sell a decidedly un-free, state-centric socialist program. He is right that the American project has often failed to live up to its ideals, and that minorities have frequently been denied the protections of classical liberalism. But the solution to this hypocrisy is not to abandon the ideals; it is to universalize them.

The Civil Rights movement, which Rana attempts to draft into his collectivist project, was fundamentally a demand for inclusion in the classical liberal framework—the demand for equal protection under the law, the right to vote, and the right to participate freely in the market without discrimination. It was a triumph of individual rights over state-sponsored collective oppression.

True freedom does not require us to view ourselves merely as cogs in a democratic machine, constantly engaged in "collective agency." It is the freedom to opt out, to chart one's own course, to keep what one earns, and to live according to one's own values without asking permission from the collective. Aziz Rana’s "different freedom" is merely a rebranding of the oldest form of tyranny: the subjugation of the individual to the demands of the crowd. We must reject it and unapologetically defend the classical liberal tradition that remains the true engine of human emancipation.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Individual economic liberty and property rights are not zero-sum tools of colonial extraction but are the non-coercive foundations of human ingenuity and modern prosperity.
  2. 02The American tradition of limiting state power is a rational defense against universal tyranny rather than a mere historical cover for racial exclusion.
  3. 03Redefining freedom as a requirement for collective self-rule inevitably subordinates the individual to the group and replaces voluntary associations with state coercion.
  4. 04A state powerful enough to mandate Rana's vision of economic security and collective workplace governance would necessarily possess the power to dictate every aspect of an individual's life.
  5. 05Rana’s proposal for open borders is economically incompatible with his desire for a massive expansion of the domestic welfare state.
  6. 06The solution to historical exclusions in the American project is to universalize classical liberal protections for all individuals rather than abandoning them for a collectivist framework.
