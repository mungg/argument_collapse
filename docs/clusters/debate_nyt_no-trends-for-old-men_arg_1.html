"Men over 40 should not avoid trendy looks, but should instead integrate them with judgment and personal identity rather than treating age as a strict costume violation."

Men over 40 should not avoid trendy looks; they should avoid being bullied by them. That is a different matter. The problem with the Details premise is not that clothes have no age, but that it treats age as a costume violation rather than one factor among many: body, profession, personality, setting, taste and confidence.

Fashion has always depended on borrowing across generations. The modern suit was once a youth rebellion against aristocratic dress. Jeans, sneakers, leather jackets and military coats have all migrated from subculture or utility into adult wardrobes. If every man put down a garment the moment it became associated with younger people, the adult closet would be a museum of navy blazers and apology.

Still, “wear whatever you want” is too easy. Clothes communicate. A 48-year-old banker in a neon tank top and drop-crotch sweatpants at a client meeting is not being brave; he is ignoring context. But the same man in well-cut trousers, serious shoes and a sharply chosen printed shirt may look more alive and more appropriate than someone hiding in anonymous khakis. Trendiness fails when it looks like panic — when a man appears to be auditioning for an age he no longer is, rather than dressing as the person he has become.

That is why Simon Doonan is a poor target for fashion scolding. His style is not a desperate reach for youth; it is consistent, witty and legible. He has earned the right to eccentricity because it appears integrated into his identity. The same outfit on someone else might read as costume. That distinction matters more than the birthday.

The better rule for men over 40 is not “avoid trends,” but “translate them.” A trend can be adopted in proportion: slimmer tailoring without sausage-casing oneself; color without clownishness; patterned socks, interesting frames, a modern jacket, better denim. Hair, grooming and fit matter more with age, not because older men must be invisible, but because sloppiness is less easily mistaken for cool.

The fear beneath articles like this is that older men will look foolish. But the opposite danger is just as real: that they will look resigned. Fashion should not be a youth preserve. Men over 40 should dress with judgment, not obedience — and judgment sometimes includes a trend.

"Men over forty should not be restricted to conservative uniforms but should instead continue to engage with fashion and trends as a form of confident self-expression."

The premise of the Details article—that men past forty should retreat into a tasteful uniform of dark suits, cashmere, and sensible loafers—mistakes conformity for elegance. It assumes that style is a youth phenomenon, something one ages out of like skateboarding or all-night drinking. But fashion, properly understood, is not a privilege of the young; it's a form of self-presentation that gets more interesting, not less, as a person accumulates a sense of who he actually is.

The men who look best in their fifties and sixties are almost never the ones playing it safe. Think of Paul Smith, Nick Wooster, Glenn O'Brien, or Doonan himself—men whose pleasure in clothes is visible and undimmed by the calendar. What they share isn't trend-chasing so much as a confident relationship to their own taste. They wear bright socks, slim trousers, unusual eyewear, and the occasional questionable hat because they enjoy it, and that enjoyment reads on them. By contrast, the forty-five-year-old who has been frightened into navy blazers and pleated khakis because a magazine told him neon sneakers are undignified looks exactly like what he is: someone taking orders.

The real rule isn't about age but about fit—both literal and figurative. A sixty-year-old in a sprayed-on Hedi Slimane suit looks uncomfortable; so does a twenty-five-year-old, frankly. Trends are best treated as a vocabulary to borrow from, not a costume to wear whole. An older man can absolutely pick up this season's wider trouser, textured knit, or boot silhouette; he just shouldn't dress head-to-toe like a Topman mannequin. That advice, incidentally, applies to the twenty-two-year-old too.

What the Details piece really betrays is the menswear industry's anxiety about its own customer. After the explosion of #menswear blogs, Pitti Uomo peacocking, and the rise of brands like Engineered Garments and Band of Outsiders, older men are spending and experimenting more than they have in decades. Telling them to sit down and behave isn't sound advice. It's a failure of nerve.

The notion that men over 40 should abandon trends altogether misunderstands both the nature of personal style and the shifting landscape of men's fashion. Age is a poor proxy for taste, and the question itself reveals a snobbery that has long plagued fashion discourse.

Simon Doonan, for all his provocation, has consistently championed individuality over arbitrary rules. A man who dresses with intention and self-knowledge at 50 or 60 can be far more stylish than a 25-year-old in head-to-toe logo-wear. The real offense against style is not following trends but doing so without understanding what works for one's body, lifestyle, and personality.

In recent years, men's fashion has matured considerably. The rigid generational divides that once separated "classic" from "youth" fashion have blurred. Designers from Thom Browne to Paul Smith have created menswear that appeals across age brackets while maintaining contemporary sensibilities.

That said, there's a difference between "trendy" and "relevant." A 45-year-old in neon varsity jackets looks as absurd as a 20-year-old in a too-big leisure suit. The solution isn't age-based avoidance but rather discernment—which has no expiration date.

The Details piece, however well-intentioned, codifies an outdated premise: that aging automatically means fashion retreat. What men over 40 actually deserve is not permission to dress down, but encouragement to dress according to their own evolving aesthetic judgment.

The Details article and the resulting hand-wringing miss what's actually changed in menswear over the past decade. We are living through the most genuinely democratic moment in men's fashion in a century. The old hierarchies—business attire by day, age-appropriate leisurewear by evening, sneakers only for the gym—have collapsed. Tech billionaires wear hoodies to board meetings. Sixty-year-old architects wear Common Projects. The CEO of a Fortune 500 might show up in a Thom Browne suit with cropped trousers and exposed ankle.

In this environment, the question "is he too old for that?" is a category error. The relevant question is whether the clothes fit, whether they suit the occasion, and whether the wearer looks comfortable in them. Age is one variable among many, and not the dominant one.

What Details is really registering, I suspect, is a generational discomfort within the fashion press itself. For decades, men's magazines existed to teach younger men how to dress like older, more established men. The arrow pointed up the age ladder. Now it points down: dad rock, dad jeans, and dad style are all pejoratives, while youth aesthetics dominate. Editors trained in the old system don't know what to do with a sixty-year-old man in slim jeans and a bomber, because their entire framework assumed he'd be in a sack suit by now.

Doonan represents the new normal, not the exception. He's a man who has dressed exuberantly his whole adult life and sees no reason to stop. The fashion world should be celebrating that continuity, not pathologizing it.

If there's a real critique to make, it's of looks that announce themselves as costumes—the midlife crisis in leather pants, the suburban dad in head-to-toe streetwear with the tags practically still on. But that's a critique of self-awareness, not of age. The man who knows what he's doing can wear anything.

"Men over 40 should not avoid trends entirely but should instead adopt a disciplined approach that prioritizes moderation, quality, and personal discernment."

Men over 40 do not need a ban on trends. They need a filter.

A useful rule is this: never wear more than one conspicuously trendy thing at a time. If the trousers are very slim, keep the jacket calm. If the shoes are loud, let the rest of the outfit be quiet. If the glasses are bold, skip the novelty hat. This is not cowardice; it is composition. The older face can handle strong choices, but it does not benefit from visual shouting.

Another rule: buy the version of the trend that could survive the season. A bomber jacket in good fabric may be worth owning; a shiny, overdesigned one with zippers everywhere will soon look like evidence from a crime scene. Colored chinos may work; fluorescent drop-crotch pants probably will not. A great pair of boots can modernize a wardrobe; sneakers designed to resemble spacecraft may require more commitment than most adults should give.

Fit is the nonnegotiable point. Many men over 40 grew up with clothes that were too large, and modern tailoring can be a revelation. But slim is not the same as tight. A jacket should not pull like sausage casing, and jeans should not require a strategy for breathing. The most common mistake is not dressing too young; it is mistaking discomfort for sophistication.

Context also matters. There are clothes for dinner downtown, clothes for a child’s school event, clothes for work, clothes for vacation and clothes for staying home. A man with style understands these distinctions. A man chasing trends behaves as if every sidewalk is a runway.

So should men over 40 avoid trendy looks? No, but they should avoid trend dependence. Trends are tools, not identities. Use them to update the wardrobe, sharpen the silhouette, add surprise or prevent stagnation. Do not use them to announce that time has stopped. It has not, and that is fine. One of the pleasures of getting older is that you can stop asking, “Am I allowed to wear this?” and start asking, “Is this actually any good?”

There is a quiet cruelty in the Details premise that deserves to be named. To tell a man he is "too old for his outfit" is, in the end, to tell him he is too old—period. Clothing is just the medium through which the message gets delivered.

We don't say this to women anymore, at least not in polite company. The fashion press spent the 2000s slowly retiring the "what not to wear after fifty" genre because readers and advertisers alike recognized it for what it was: a way of escorting older women off the cultural stage. The lesson apparently hasn't reached the men's magazines.

I think about my father, who is in his late sixties and dresses with more verve than he did at forty: brightly patterned socks, interesting eyewear, the occasional pink shirt. He looks alive. He looks like someone still curious about the world. Would Details prefer he default to navy polos and pleated khakis, the uniform of resignation?

The argument that older men in trendy clothes look "ridiculous" or like they're "trying too hard" almost always reduces, on inspection, to the observer's own discomfort with visible aging. We want older men to dress in ways that minimize their presence, that don't make us confront the fact of their continued existence as desiring, expressive beings. A sixty-year-old in a leather jacket disrupts that wish. So we call him a fashion don't.

Doonan's response to the article is gentle, but the underlying point is sharp: he's not going to disappear to spare anyone's sensibilities. Good. The cultural project of the next decade should be expanding, not contracting, the range of ways older people are allowed to show up in public. A printed shirt on a fifty-five-year-old is a small thing, but it's on the right side of that project.
