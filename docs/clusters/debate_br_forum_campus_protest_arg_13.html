"A radical critique of the university and the Black Freedom movement must expand beyond domestic concerns to adopt an internationalist perspective that connects campus struggles to global systems of imperialism, racial capitalism, and militarism."

Kelley's essay is a powerful intervention, but I want to offer a response that centers the international dimensions of the struggle he describes. The campus protests of 2015 occurred in a global context shaped by austerity, militarism, and ecological crisis—forces that operate across borders and require transnational analysis and organizing.

Kelley mentions Walter Rodney and Amílcar Cabral, two theorists who understood that struggles against racism in the United States were connected to anti-colonial movements worldwide. Rodney's "How Europe Underdeveloped Africa" situated black American struggles within a global analysis of imperialism and capital accumulation. Cabral's theory of national liberation understood that colonialism operated through racial domination and that liberation required not just political independence but economic transformation. These insights remain relevant today.

The campus protests were not isolated events but part of a global moment of resistance. In 2015, students in South Africa demanded the decolonization of their universities, connecting the legacy of apartheid to ongoing white economic dominance. In Chile, students had been mobilizing for years against the privatization of education. In the UK, movements against tuition fees and austerity had already forced debates about who controls the university. These movements learned from each other, adapted each other's tactics, and shared analysis across languages and borders.

Kelley writes that "the university is dedicated to professionalization, order, scientific efficiency, counterinsurgency, and war." This is true, but it is also incomplete. The university is a global institution, and its role in producing knowledge that serves imperial power is also global. Research conducted in American universities develops weapons used against people in Palestine, Yemen, and Syria. The CIA has long maintained relationships with American academic institutions. The Pentagon funds research in robotics, surveillance, and human performance that shapes warfare worldwide.

A radical analysis of the university must therefore be internationalist. The demands Kelley celebrates at UNC—divestment from prisons, investment in communities—make sense only if we understand that American prisons are connected to the global carceral state, that mass incarceration in the US is modeled on colonial detention practices, and that the prison-industrial complex is part of a larger apparatus of control that includes ICE detention centers, military prisons, and police forces trained by American consultants worldwide.

Political education that takes these connections seriously would build solidarity with movements across borders, would understand American racism as part of a global system of racial capitalism, and would develop strategies for transnational organizing. This is not easy, but it is necessary. The forces we confront are global; our resistance must be as well.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 2015 campus protests were part of a global wave of student resistance, from South Africa to Chile, that shared tactics and analysis across borders.
  2. 02The university’s role in serving state power is a global phenomenon, as academic research directly facilitates imperial warfare and surveillance technologies used worldwide.
  3. 03Domestic demands like prison divestment must be understood within the context of a global carceral state that links American mass incarceration to colonial detention practices.
  4. 04Effective political education must move beyond national boundaries to build solidarity with transnational movements and address racism as a global system of capital accumulation.
  5. 05Historical figures like Walter Rodney and Amílcar Cabral provide essential frameworks for linking local anti-racist struggles in the United States to international anti-colonial transformations.
