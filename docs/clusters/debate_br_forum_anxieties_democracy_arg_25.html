"The primary cause of democratic decline and legislative dysfunction is the 'permanent campaign,' which forces representatives to prioritize fundraising over the actual craft of lawmaking."

Ira Katznelson’s essay is a fine-grained map of democratic anxiety, but it curiously sidesteps the most distorting force in American political life: the permanent campaign. The representative institutions he wants to fortify are hollowed out not primarily by procedural arcana or outsider populism but by the relentless, career-defining imperative of fundraising. Until we name and break the link between money and the daily experience of legislating, all talk of restoring “the dignity of legislation” will remain wishful. The anxiety is warranted because the legislative body has become a fundraising body, and that is a cancer on self-government.

By August 2015, the evidence is overwhelming. Members of Congress, particularly in the House, are expected by party leadership to spend as much as four hours a day in call centers across the street from the Capitol, dialing for dollars. Freshman orientation for new members famously includes tutorials on “call time.” The result is a legislature whose members are physically present but mentally captive to the next cycle’s donors. Committee hearings are scheduled around fundraising windows. Policy expertise erodes because there is no time to cultivate it. Junior members are too busy chasing checks to master complex legislation, so they defer to leadership and lobbyists who write the bills. The “poor legislative craftsmanship” Katznelson laments is a direct output of a system in which the craft itself has been monetized.

Campaign finance is usually debated as a problem of corruption or unequal influence, and those are grave concerns. But the deeper corruption is temporal: the permanent campaign hijacks the attention of our representatives, turning them from lawmakers into permanent candidates. This is why the post–Citizens United flow of money, especially from independent expenditure groups, is so damaging. Super PACs and dark-money nonprofits don’t just tilt policy outcomes in favor of the wealthy; they turn every member into a desperate supplicant who must continually prospect for donors to fund outside groups that can either protect or destroy them. The result is a pervasive insecurity that rewards extreme positions, punishes compromise, and makes long-term thinking impossible.

Katznelson contrasts the present with the interwar years, but there is a more recent instructive contrast: the Congress of the 1960s and 1970s, which, for all its flaws, managed to pass Medicare, Medicaid, the Civil Rights Act, and a cascade of environmental laws. That was a Congress whose members largely stayed in Washington, socialized across party lines, and spent their time legislating rather than fundraising. The shift began not with the arrival of ideological polarization but with the rise of television advertising, the decline of party organizations, and the professionalization of the permanent campaign. Today, a typical House member in a competitive district must raise over a million dollars per cycle, and that number climbs each year. Is it any wonder that the public senses those members are working for someone else?

There is no single silver bullet, but the path forward must start with a frontal attack on the time-money nexus. One bold idea is to move to a system of full public financing with free media time, freeing candidates from the money chase entirely. Even short of that, reforms like the Government by the People Act, which would create a $25 refundable tax credit for small donations and a matching system, would alter incentives by making small donors the primary constituency. Another approach is to change the Congressional calendar, mandating extended periods in Washington with strict limits on fundraising, so that members actually deliberate. These are not radical ideas; they are efforts to give legislative service back its integrity. Katznelson is right that democracies need legislatures that can make law. But first they need legislators who have the time and freedom to be legislators. Until then, the anxiety will only deepen.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The constant demand for fundraising hijacks the time and attention of legislators, leading to an erosion of policy expertise and a reliance on lobbyists to write bills.
  2. 02The financial insecurity created by the post-Citizens United environment incentivizes extreme political positions and punishes the compromise necessary for a healthy representative democracy.
  3. 03The historical contrast with the mid-20th century suggests that legislative success was possible when members of Congress spent more time socializing and legislating rather than chasing campaign donations.
  4. 04Restoring the integrity of self-government requires structural reforms, such as public financing or small-donor matching systems, that break the time-money nexus and allow representatives to focus on deliberation.
