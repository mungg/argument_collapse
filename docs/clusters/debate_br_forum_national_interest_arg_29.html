"The United States should reject a strategy of diplomatic self-restraint and instead adopt an aggressive counterproliferation policy that includes the credible threat of preventive military action to stop hostile regimes from acquiring nuclear weapons."

The most urgent passage in Stephen Walt’s lengthy essay is his discussion of nuclear terrorism. He is absolutely right that a single nuclear detonation in an American city would be a catastrophe that dwarfs all other security concerns. He is also right that the United States must redouble efforts to secure loose nuclear materials and strengthen the non-proliferation regime. But his proposed “grand bargain”—in which the United States slashes its own nuclear arsenal, abandons new weapons programs, and signs nonaggression pacts with Iran and North Korea in exchange for their nuclear rollback—is a dangerous fantasy. It misdiagnoses the drivers of proliferation and places a naive faith in the power of American self-restraint to sway regimes that are motivated by their own strategic ambitions and internal logic. A genuinely prudent grand strategy would place counterproliferation, including the credible threat of preventive military action, at the center of national security policy, not consign it to the dustbin in a misguided bid for global approval.

Walt’s central claim is that American nuclear policies make the rest of the world less willing to cooperate on non-proliferation and that de-emphasizing nuclear weapons would strengthen anti-nuclear advocates abroad. This echoes the familiar complaint that the United States cannot ask others not to smoke while lighting up itself. But this analogy misrepresents the strategic function of nuclear weapons. States pursue nuclear arms not because they are morally convinced by America’s example but because nuclear weapons are, in the Hobbesian world of international politics, the ultimate guarantor of regime survival. The Iranian mullahs and North Korea’s Kim Jong Il did not begin their quests for the bomb after the U.S. Senate rejected the Comprehensive Test Ban Treaty or because the Bush administration sought to modernize the stockpile. They began those programs because they face enemies—the United States, Israel, South Korea—with overwhelming conventional superiority and because they see nuclear weapons as the only way to deter American-sponsored regime change. Walt himself acknowledges this when he notes that placing both countries in the “axis of evil” gave them an incentive to seek a deterrent. The logical implication is not that the United States should offer a nonaggression pact and cut its arsenal; it is that the United States must convince these regimes that the cost of pursuing nuclear weapons will be higher than the benefit, and that the goal of regime change will remain on the table unless and until they verifiably dismantle their programs. American nuclear restraint might soften the diplomatic resistance of Canada or Sweden; it will not move Pyongyang or Tehran an inch.

Walt’s “grand bargain” also underestimates the verification problem. The history of the Agreed Framework with North Korea and the EU-3 negotiations with Iran demonstrates that these regimes are adept at using diplomacy as a cover to buy time for weapons development. North Korea violated the 1994 agreement with a secret uranium-enrichment program. Iran similarly deceived the IAEA for nearly two decades. A grand bargain that requires verifiable abandonment would demand an inspection regime so intrusive that no sovereign state, let alone a paranoid dictatorship, would consent to it without the credible threat of overwhelming force making the alternative unpalatable. Walt wants the United States to take the threat of regime change off the table as a gesture of good faith, but it is precisely that threat that has driven Libya’s WMD surrender and has forced Iran to keep its program at least partially in the open. Withdrawing the threat in the hope of winning a bargain is like a negotiator throwing away his strongest card before sitting down at the table.

Equally troubling is Walt’s contention that the danger of a regime handing nuclear material to a terrorist group is “extremely remote” because no leader would risk the retaliation that would follow. This is a gamble that the nation cannot afford. Terrorist groups do not require an entire nuclear weapon; a softball-sized sphere of highly enriched uranium, slipped into a shipping container, could enable a crude device that would kill hundreds of thousands. A state sponsor could deliver such material under careful deniability, perhaps through a cutout that leaves no clear fingerprint, and bet that the United States, even if suspicious, would not risk war with a nuclear-armed adversary over ambiguous evidence. The North Korean regime has already demonstrated a willingness to sell ballistic-missile technology and even drugs for hard currency; it is not a massive leap to imagine it trading fissile material if the price were right. Walt’s deterrence logic fails precisely in the case of opaque, non-attributable threats, which is the most likely avenue for nuclear terrorism.

What then is the proper grand strategy for addressing this paramount danger? It must begin with an aggressive counterproliferation policy that makes clear that the United States will use all instruments of national power—including precision military strikes if necessary—to prevent the emergence of new nuclear-armed states, especially those with ties to international terrorism. This does not require an invasion of Iran on the scale of Iraq; it means a sustained campaign of sabotage, interdiction, economic pressure, and the demonstrated will to destroy nuclear facilities from the air if peaceful means fail. The Proliferation Security Initiative, which Walt supports, is a good start but insufficient without a credible military option. The United States should also accelerate research into reliable earth-penetrating warheads—the very new weapons that Walt wants scrapped—not for first-use against great powers, but for the specific challenge of deeply buried, hardened targets that proliferators use to shield their programs. To foreswear such capabilities in an era when North Korea can tunnel under a mountain is to announce that the United States is not serious about denial.

Walt’s instinct to reduce the nuclear danger is honorable, but his means would increase it. A grand bargain that treats Iranian and North Korean proliferation as a diplomatic problem solvable by American example ignores the brutal strategic calculus of these regimes. The question is not whether the United States should make non-proliferation a core goal; it is whether it will do what is necessary to achieve it. Preventive action against nuclear programs, undertaken judiciously but with unmistakable resolve, is not the path to global resistance that Walt fears. It is the path to a world in which no American city ever vanishes in a mushroom cloud.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02Offering nonaggression pacts and reducing the U.S. nuclear stockpile would remove the essential leverage needed to force paranoid dictatorships into accepting intrusive inspections.
  3. 03The risk of a state sponsor clandestinely transferring nuclear material to terrorists is a plausible threat that traditional deterrence cannot reliably prevent.
  4. 04Maintaining and developing specialized military capabilities, such as earth-penetrating warheads, is necessary to credibly threaten the hardened, deeply buried facilities used by proliferators.
  5. 05A strategy based on American self-restraint and moral example is a dangerous fantasy that ignores the Hobbesian reality of international politics and the internal logic of rogue states.
