"The Internet of Things poses a systemic threat to domestic privacy by enabling constant surveillance, necessitating a shift toward non-networked appliances and stronger legal protections for personal data."

In the wake of this summer's revelations by Edward Snowden regarding the breathtaking scope of the NSA’s surveillance apparatus, the push for an "Internet of Things" feels less like a technological leap forward and more like the voluntary installation of a panopticon in our own homes.

The primary danger of the IoT is not merely the rogue hacker terrorizing a sleeping infant, though that is horrifying enough. The much larger, systemic threat is the invisible, ubiquitous harvesting of our most intimate behavioral data by corporations and governments. A networked home is a home that constantly reports on its inhabitants. A smart meter tells an observer exactly when you wake up, when you leave the house, and when you go to sleep. A networked television knows what you watch and when. Networked running shoes track your physical location and health metrics.

When every object we own is transmitting data to the cloud, the very concept of domestic sanctuary evaporates. We are creating a world where data brokers can build an infinitely detailed profile of our private lives, ready to be monetized by advertisers or subpoenaed by authorities. The privacy policies accompanying these devices are routinely unreadable, serving as legal shields for corporate data mining rather than protections for the consumer.

Minimizing these risks requires a fundamental shift in how we view digital privacy. We cannot rely on after-the-fact regulation or flimsy corporate promises. We must demand "dumb" alternatives—appliances that wash our dishes and heat our homes without requiring a Wi-Fi connection and an end-user license agreement. Furthermore, we must establish a legal doctrine that extends Fourth Amendment protections to the data generated by our connected devices, ensuring that the digital exhaust of our daily lives cannot be swept up without a specific, targeted warrant. Until such protections are in place, the smartest thing you can do with a "smart" device is leave it on the store shelf.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Networked devices allow corporations and governments to harvest intimate behavioral data by tracking every aspect of a person's daily routine.

  4. 04Consumers should opt for 'dumb' non-connected appliances to avoid the inherent surveillance risks of smart technology.
  5. 05Legal doctrines must be updated to extend Fourth Amendment protections to the data generated by connected devices, requiring specific warrants for access.
