Vanilla GPT

"Poetry remains a vital and indispensable art form because it provides a unique, compact language for human experience that persists even while it lacks mass-market dominance."

Poetry matters, but not in the way anxious defenders often want it to matter. It is unlikely to regain the kind of central cultural authority that novels, films, television and popular music now possess, and it is a mistake to measure it by those standards. A form can be marginal in the marketplace and still be central to how people think, mourn, remember and speak when ordinary language fails.

David Orr is right that contemporary poetry often looks like the “weak sister” of the arts. Few poets sell many books. Most Americans cannot name a living poet beyond a small handful of public figures. Surveys of arts participation have shown a long decline in the percentage of adults who say they read poetry. In newspapers and magazines, poetry has largely retreated from general cultural conversation into specialized journals, university programs and small presses. The result is a strange situation: there are probably more people writing poems seriously than ever before, through M.F.A. programs, workshops, slams and online communities, while fewer people outside those circles feel that poetry is meant for them.

But the conclusion should not be that poetry is dead or irrelevant. It is that poetry has become episodic rather than habitual in American public life. People turn to it at moments of pressure: funerals, weddings, memorial services, inaugurations, protests, disasters. After September 11, poems circulated not because critics told people to admire them, but because compact, memorable language was needed. When Seamus Heaney died last year, lines from his poems moved quickly through newspapers and social media. Maya Angelou’s death this spring produced a similar response, reminding us that a poet can be lodged deeply in public memory even among people who do not regularly buy poetry books.

Nor is poetry absent from popular culture; it is often present under other names. Rap, spoken word, performance poetry and even the compressed wit of social media all testify to an appetite for rhythm, metaphor, verbal surprise and memorable phrasing. These are not substitutes for the whole art, but they disprove the idea that Americans are immune to poetic language. The problem is less that people hate poetry than that they have often been taught to approach it as a puzzle with a hidden answer, rather than as speech charged with music and thought.

Celebrity endorsements may help a little. So might better prizes, better criticism, more public readings and more poems in newspapers. But poetry’s future depends less on marketing than on access. Schools should give students poems they can hear before they are asked to decode them. Editors should write about poems without treating difficulty as either a badge of honor or a crime. Poets, meanwhile, need not become entertainers, but they should remember that obscurity is not the same as depth.

Poetry will not “regain” relevance by becoming a mass entertainment. It will remain a minority art. Yet minority arts can be indispensable. Poetry preserves possibilities of language that public life constantly degrades: precision against cliché, ambiguity against propaganda, inwardness against noise. That is not weakness. It is a quieter kind of strength.

Vanilla DeepSeek

"Poetry remains profoundly relevant because it serves as an essential, intimate tool for human expression and meaning-making that does not require mass-market success to be powerful."

To ask whether poetry matters is, in a way, to ask whether breathing matters. The question itself, posed as part of a perennial cultural anxiety, assumes that relevance must be measured by mass-market share or visibility in the same arenas that celebrate blockbuster novels and viral videos. David Orr’s lament that poetry is “the weak sister of its sibling arts” mistakes a different kind of strength for frailty. Poetry does not need to regain its relevancy, because it has never lost the kind of relevancy that actually counts: its intimate, enduring presence in the lives of individuals and communities.

Consider where poetry truly lives. It is recited at weddings and funerals, scrawled on protest signs, whispered in therapy sessions, and tattooed on skin. It flourishes not on bestseller lists but in high school poetry slams, open mic nights in dimly lit cafes, and the sprawling ecosystem of online journals and YouTube channels like Button Poetry, which by 2014 had been sharing spoken word performances with millions for years. These are not the arenas of celebrity-driven commerce, but they are spaces of deep, visceral connection. When a teenager memorizes a line by Mary Oliver or an Iraq War veteran shares a workshop poem about trauma, poetry is doing the essential work of forging meaning from chaos—work that no blockbuster film or prestige novel can replicate in quite the same compressed, unforgettable way.

The argument that poetry must mimic the promotional machinery of other arts to become relevant again misunderstands both history and human nature. Poetry was never a mass art in the modern sense, even when Byron was a celebrity or Frost drew crowds. It has always belonged more to the solitary reader, the small circle, the ritual moment. What has changed is the proliferation of media that make those small circles visible to one another, and what they reveal is a vibrant, pluralistic poetry culture that is anything but weak. From the viral spread of Nayyirah Waheed’s brief, piercing lines on Instagram to the massive audiences for live poetry slam finals, the art form is proving remarkably adaptable to the attention economy without selling its soul.

Moreover, poetry’s supposed weakness is, in fact, its core strength: it does not require a massive apparatus of production or consumption. It can be made of nothing but language and breath, shared instantly, remembered for a lifetime. In an age of information overload, the poem offers an antidote—slowness, ambiguity, the space to think and feel deeply. That is not irrelevancy; it is cultural necessity. The tools Orr suggests, including celebrity endorsements, would merely drape poetry in the trappings of a marketplace that has little to do with its actual power.

So let us stop asking whether poetry can be “persuaded” into relevance, as if it were a wilting product in need of a better ad campaign. Poetry matters because humans will always need a language for what cannot be said plainly, for grief and joy and wonder that resist the straight line of prose. It matters because a single couplet can rewire a mind for a lifetime. It matters in the same way that music matters—not because everyone is a musician, but because everyone has a song that saved them. Poetry’s relevancy is not in crisis; it is simply, stubbornly, alive where it has always been: in the hearts of those who turn to it for solace, clarity, and the shock of recognition. And that is more than enough.

Vanilla MiniMax

"Poetry remains inherently relevant because it provides a unique depth of human experience and precision of language that cannot be found in other art forms."

The question of poetry's relevance is as old as the form itself. Every generation seems to declare poetry dead or dying, yet the form persists. The real question is not whether poetry matters—it always has and always will—but rather to whom and in what ways. David Orr's diagnosis of poetry as the "weak sister" captures a real anxiety, but the prescription requires more nuance than simply finding better marketing strategies.

Poetry has never been a mass art form in the way that the novel or popular music has been. Even during the supposed golden ages of poetry, it reached relatively small audiences. What poetry offers is not breadth but depth—language that distills experience into concentrated form, allowing readers to encounter something true about human life in compressed space. This function remains valuable precisely because we live in an age of information overflow. When everything is available all the time, the discipline of poetry's precision becomes more, not less, relevant.

The challenge is not poetry's existence but its accessibility. Too often, contemporary poetry has become a conversation among poets about poetry, written in specialized vocabularies that exclude rather than invite. This is not an inherent quality of the form—it is a consequence of how poetry is taught, published, and reviewed. The academy and the major poetry prizes have rewarded complexity and difficulty, sometimes at the expense of emotional resonance or verbal pleasure.

Yet signs of renewal are visible. The poetry slam movement, despite its tensions with the "page poetry" establishment, demonstrated that audiences will come out for live poetry when given the opportunity. Spoken word artists have built substantial followings by returning poetry to its oral roots. Digital platforms are creating new audiences for poetry, particularly among young people who encounter it through social media and online journals. These developments suggest not that poetry needs to abandon its complexity but that it needs multiple entry points.

Can poetry regain relevancy? The phrasing assumes it once possessed a cultural position it has lost. In truth, poetry's cultural role has always been particular rather than dominant. What poetry can do—and what it should be helped to do—is serve those readers who seek the kind of meaning that only poetic language can provide. The question is whether institutions that support poetry, from university creative writing programs to small presses to literary magazines, will continue to serve this function or will calcify into self-perpetuating bureaucracies. Poetry will matter as long as thoughtful people find in it something they cannot find elsewhere. The work of those who care about the form is to ensure that connection remains possible.

Vanilla Gemini

"Poetry remains profoundly relevant and vital, not as a commercial product, but as a resilient medium for processing the human experience and a democratized form of digital expression."

David Orr’s assertion that poetry is the "weak sister" of the arts rests on a fundamentally flawed premise: that artistic relevancy should be measured by mass-market commercial success. If we judge poetry by the metrics of summer blockbuster films or bestselling dystopian young adult novels, then yes, it appears to be struggling. But to ask whether poetry matters based on its market share or celebrity presence is to misunderstand the very nature of the art form. Poetry has never been meant to compete in the economy of mass distraction; its purpose is altogether different.

Far from fading into irrelevancy, poetry is experiencing a quiet, decentralized renaissance. While major publishing houses may not see staggering sales for print chapbooks, the internet has democratized the form. On platforms like Tumblr and Twitter, a new generation of readers and writers is engaging with verse daily. Young people are sharing lines, debating imagery, and composing their own works at an unprecedented scale. The traditional barrier to entry has vanished. Poetry is no longer confined to the elite halls of academia; it is living and breathing in the digital ether.

Moreover, poetry proves its enduring necessity during the moments when everyday, utilitarian language fails us. When we face profound grief, collective trauma, or transcendent joy, we do not turn to op-eds or corporate press releases; we turn to verse. In the aftermath of sudden tragedies or national crises, poems circulate organically because they offer a container for otherwise incomprehensible sorrow. At weddings, funerals, and graduations, we instinctively reach for the words of Mary Oliver, Walt Whitman, or Maya Angelou. Poetry matters because it distills the overwhelming chaos of human experience into precise, musical language that bypasses our cynical defenses and speaks directly to our emotional core.

The push to manufacture relevancy through celebrity endorsements or flashy marketing campaigns, as Orr suggests might be considered, risks cheapening the medium. Poetry does not need a superficial PR makeover to survive, nor does it require the validation of Hollywood actors to prove its worth. It is the language of the inner life, requiring silence, prolonged attention, and a willingness to sit with ambiguity. These are qualities that are inherently countercultural in our hyper-connected, fast-paced 2014 society, which makes the art form more vital now than ever.

Poetry will likely never dominate the cultural conversation the way a prestige television drama does, but that is not a sign of its weakness. It is a testament to its specialized power. Poetry matters deeply, not as a fleeting commercial enterprise, but as the quiet, resilient bedrock of our shared humanity.

I distrust the word “relevance.” It usually means obedience to the present moment. A relevant poem, in this sense, would advertise its usefulness: it would address the news, confirm our politics, flatter our identities or fit neatly into a classroom discussion about social issues. Some poems do that well. But poetry’s deepest value often lies in its refusal to be immediately useful.

A poem matters because it preserves kinds of attention that public life erodes. It slows language down. It makes us notice the difference between one word and another. It complicates feeling. It keeps alive the possibility that experience is not exhausted by opinion. In a political culture of slogans and a media culture of speed, these are not decorative virtues. They are civic virtues, though they do not look like policy.

The demand that poetry “regain” relevancy assumes that poetry once enjoyed a healthy public centrality and then fell from it. That history is too simple. Poetry has always had different audiences: courtly, religious, popular, academic, avant-garde. Some poems circulate widely; many do not. Shakespeare’s sonnets and nursery rhymes are both poetry, but they matter differently. Emily Dickinson was not a public celebrity in her lifetime. Her lack of relevance to her contemporaries tells us very little about her importance.

So I would not ask poets to become more relevant. I would ask readers, editors and teachers to become more patient. Too much poetry is introduced as a puzzle with a hidden answer, which trains people to feel either stupid or resentful. Poems should be read aloud, memorized, lived with, returned to. The pleasure of poetry often arrives after the first encounter, not before.

Nor should poets be required to simplify themselves for the market. Some contemporary poetry is hermetic, yes, and some of it is merely fashionable. But difficulty is not the enemy. The enemy is bad difficulty: opacity without reward, fragmentation without necessity, cleverness without music. Great difficult poems matter because they change the reader’s capacities.

Poetry may never command the audience of television, and it should not measure itself by that standard. Its relevance is not numerical. It matters wherever language matters: in love, mourning, prayer, memory, revolt and solitude. If that is weakness, it is a weakness civilization cannot do without.

"Poetry remains deeply relevant in contemporary life, but its presence has migrated from traditional literary institutions to popular culture and digital spaces."

Poetry doesn't need to "regain" relevancy because it never lost it — it only lost market share. These are different things, and conflating them is the source of the perennial hand-wringing exemplified by David Orr's lament.

Consider what poetry actually does in American life today. It is read at every presidential inauguration. It is recited at weddings and funerals — the two events where people most need language adequate to the moment. Hip-hop, arguably the dominant popular art form of the past three decades, is poetry by any reasonable definition: metrically organized speech using rhyme, assonance, metaphor, and persona. Spoken word events fill rooms in every major city. Instagram and Tumblr are awash in shared lines from Mary Oliver, Rumi, and Rilke. Tracy K. Smith won a Pulitzer in 2012 for a book of poems about her dead father and outer space, and people actually bought it.

What has declined is a very specific thing: the cultural prestige of the mid-century literary poet publishing in quarterlies and competing for Guggenheims. That world — the world of Lowell and Bishop on the cover of Time — was itself a historical anomaly, propped up by midcentury middlebrow institutions (the big magazines, the Book-of-the-Month Club ethos, English departments newly flush with GI Bill students) that have eroded across the board. The novel suffers from the same erosion; we just notice less because novels still sell enough copies to look healthy.

Orr's proposed remedies — celebrity writers, marketing pushes — misdiagnose the problem. You cannot manufacture relevance for an art form through promotional campaigns; you can only notice where the form is already alive and stop demanding it appear in the venues where it used to live. Poetry migrated. It went into song lyrics, into spoken word, into the strange little corners of the internet where a single Frank O'Hara poem gets reblogged ten thousand times. The university MFA system absorbed the high-literary version and made it institutional, which is a different problem.

So: yes, poetry matters. It just doesn't matter in the way critics who came of age reading Helen Vendler want it to matter. That's a loss worth mourning, but it's not the death of an art.

"Poetry can regain its cultural relevance by abandoning its current posture of solemn reverence and reclaiming its historical role as a vibrant, public, and provocative art form."

The notion that poetry needs celebrities is almost exactly backward. Poetry suffers not from too little glamour but from too much reverence. People are forever being told that poetry is profound, enriching, necessary, morally serious. The result is that many readers approach it as they approach a church service in a language they do not speak: respectfully, briefly and with relief when it ends.

What poetry needs is less incense and more mischief. It should be funny, rude, argumentative, erotic, satirical, local, occasional and unafraid of embarrassment. Historically, poetry has done all of these things. It has mocked politicians, praised bodies, commemorated battles, insulted rivals and made jokes. The idea that a poem must be a hushed lyric about consciousness is a relatively narrow modern prejudice.

If contemporary poetry seems marginal, one reason is that it has ceded too many lively public functions. Satire went to television. Story went to fiction and film. Public anger went to opinion journalism and protest music. Verbal bravado went to rap. Poetry kept the quiet epiphany and then wondered why the room emptied.

This is not an argument against subtle lyric poetry. Some of the greatest poems are quiet. But a healthy poetic culture needs many temperatures. It needs poems one can declaim in a bar, poems that can be memorized after two hearings, poems that bite. The broadside, the ballad, the comic couplet, the political chant, the children’s rhyme—these are not inferior forms of the art. They are part of its public muscle.

The Internet offers a chance here. Poems can circulate instantly; recordings can restore voice; small communities can form around shared taste. But the poem that travels online usually has energy on the surface. It gives readers a reason to pass it along. Much literary poetry seems designed not to be shared but to be approved.

So yes, poetry matters, but it will matter more when it stops acting so fragile. Forget the rescue mission. Write poems that seduce, provoke, console, amuse and offend. Write poems that people want to steal lines from. If poetry is the weak sister, perhaps she has spent too long indoors being praised for her sensitivity. Let her go outside and start a fight.
