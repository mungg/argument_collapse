"While the biological evidence for early childhood development is sound, James Heckman’s proposed policy framework risks an intrusive expansion of state authority into the private lives and cultural values of disadvantaged families."

The Quiet Statism of "Predistribution"

I find myself in an uncomfortable position with regard to James Heckman's essay. Much of what he says about early childhood development is empirically well-grounded, and I have no quarrel with the basic biological story: severe neglect in the first years of life leaves lasting marks, and supportive early environments matter. What troubles me is the policy edifice he builds on these facts, and especially the quiet expansion of state authority into family life that his program implies.

Heckman speaks of "respecting the sanctity of early family life" and "cultural diversity," and I take him at his word that he wants to. But the architecture of the programs he champions — nurse home visiting, intensive center-based preschool with home components, identification of "risky family environments," targeting based on "the quality of parenting" rather than income — is one in which professionals trained by the state assess the adequacy of parenting and intervene to correct it. We should think carefully about what we are signing up for.

Consider the targeting question Heckman raises in his "practical issues" section. He says income and parental education are crude proxies, and that "the quality of parenting is the important scarce resource." Therefore, "we need better measures of risky family environments in order to achieve more accurate targeting." Stop and think about what this means operationally. It means a regime in which the state, through some combination of pediatricians, social workers, and assessment instruments, evaluates which families have "risky" environments and directs interventions accordingly. Heckman is too thoughtful not to see the civil liberties implications, but he does not engage them. The history of state assessment of poor families' parenting in this country — from the moral evaluations of nineteenth-century charity workers, to the home visits of the original AFDC era, to the family regulation system today — is not encouraging.

There is a class dynamic here that should make liberals nervous, not just conservatives. Affluent families will not have their parenting "assessed" by government professionals. They will simply hire nannies and enroll their kids in Montessori. The interventions Heckman describes will fall, by design, on poor and working-class families — disproportionately Black and Hispanic ones, given the demographics of disadvantage. We are proposing a two-tier system in which one set of families is left to its own devices and another is enrolled in state-supervised developmental programs. Even if every program works exactly as advertised, the political relationship this creates between government and the citizenry it is meant to serve is worth pausing over.

Then there is the compliance problem Heckman himself flags. "Many successful programs change the values and motivations of the child. Some of these changes may run counter to the values of certain parents." This is a remarkable sentence, glided past in a paragraph. What he is saying is that some of these programs work in part by overwriting the cultural transmission from parent to child. That is a serious thing to do, and a serious thing for the state to fund. It is precisely the kind of thing that erodes pluralism and homogenizes the messy diversity of American family life into a single, professionally approved developmental script. Heckman trusts that "cultural sensitivity" will solve this. I do not.

I would also press on the assumption that the alternative to state-run early intervention is laissez-faire neglect. It is not. The institutions that have historically supported families — extended kin, churches, neighborhood associations, ethnic mutual-aid societies, civic groups — have eroded badly, partly because of the long expansion of public programs that crowded out their functions. Robert Putnam's recent work documents the resulting "bowling alone" landscape. A serious mobility agenda might focus on rebuilding these mediating institutions rather than substituting for them with more professionalized public programs. The Nurse-Family Partnership is a good idea; a million paid nurse home-visitors funded out of Washington is a different proposition with different political and cultural consequences.

To his credit, Heckman opens the door to private and philanthropic involvement: "Engaging the private sector, including privately constituted social groups and philanthropists, augments public resources, creates community support." He should walk further through that door. Vouchered support for diverse, parent-chosen, locally rooted programs is a defensible model. Universal state-administered programs are not the same thing, even with a sliding fee scale.

There is also something to be said about parental agency. Heckman's framing tends to treat parents as inputs to a developmental production function — sources of stimulation, dispensers of vocabulary, regulators of behavior. The dignity of the parental role as a relationship of love, formation, and moral authority over one's own children drops out. Even disadvantaged parents are, in the first instance, the people responsible for and entitled to raise their own children. Policy should support them in that role, not subtly relocate it.

The accident of birth is indeed unequal. But the cure should not be a state more deeply entwined with the inner life of poor families. We can do better — and the better path runs through cash support to parents themselves, vouchered access to a plural ecosystem of programs, and a rebuilding of the civic institutions that once helped families raise their own kids.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Targeting families based on 'parenting quality' rather than income necessitates a problematic regime of state surveillance and assessment of poor and minority households.
  2. 02The proposed interventions create a two-tier citizenship model where affluent families maintain autonomy while poor families are subjected to state-supervised developmental programs.
  3. 03State-funded programs that aim to change a child's values may undermine parental agency and erode cultural pluralism by overwriting the transmission of values from parent to child.
  4. 04The focus on professionalized public programs ignores the potential for rebuilding the mediating civic institutions, such as churches and neighborhood associations, that historically supported families.
  5. 05Social policy should prioritize supporting parental dignity and agency through cash transfers and vouchers for a pluralistic ecosystem of programs rather than through direct state administration of family life.
