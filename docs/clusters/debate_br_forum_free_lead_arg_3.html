Diversified Gemini

"Governor Roemer’s proposal to replace special-interest funding with a small-donor, internet-driven model would likely worsen congressional polarization and gridlock by incentivizing demagoguery over compromise."

Governor Roemer’s passionate appeal for campaign finance reform relies on a familiar and deeply appealing narrative: once upon a time, Congress was a place where independent-minded statesmen debated the issues and governed for the common good, but today, it has been corrupted by the endless pursuit of campaign cash. While Roemer’s dismay at the current state of Washington is understandable, his historical nostalgia and his proposed reliance on a small-donor, internet-driven funding model overlook the structural realities of modern political behavior. In his attempt to cure the dysfunction of Congress, Roemer champions a system that would likely exacerbate its worst polarized tendencies.

Roemer’s core argument rests on the assumption that money from special interests and PACs prevents members of Congress from compromising and governing effectively. By eliminating PACs and capping donations at $100, he believes politicians will be freed to act rationally and deliberately. However, this assumes that small donors represent a moderating, pragmatic force in American politics. Decades of political science research and the recent history of internet fundraising suggest the exact opposite is true.

The reality is that the vast majority of Americans do not participate in political giving. The small fraction of the public that does donate—especially those who give $50 or $100 online—tends to be highly ideological, intensely partisan, and driven by outrage. Large corporate donors and PACs, for all their faults, are primarily transactional; they want stability, predictable regulations, and access. They generally prefer incumbents and pragmatists who can quietly deliver results. In contrast, the small-donor base is activated by emotional appeals, ideological purity tests, and inflammatory rhetoric.

If we were to adopt Roemer’s ideal system, where every politician is entirely dependent on thousands of $100 internet contributions, we would not produce a Congress of sober, independent thinkers like Jim Leach. We would produce a Congress of demagogues. Politicians would be forced to constantly generate manufactured outrage to keep their small-donor base engaged and the $100 checks flowing. Compromise would become politically fatal, as any attempt to reach across the aisle would be instantly condemned by the activist base, causing the campaign’s revenue stream to dry up overnight. The very polarization and gridlock Roemer decries would be amplified by the funding mechanism he proposes.

Furthermore, Roemer dismisses the role of political parties, treating all institutional structures as inherently corrupt. Yet, historically, strong political parties acted as a buffer between individual politicians and extreme factions. When parties controlled campaign funds, they could allocate resources to protect vulnerable moderates and discipline members who obstructed the governing process. The post-Watergate campaign finance reforms, combined with the rise of the internet, have severely weakened party leadership, transforming politicians into free-agent entrepreneurs who must build their own personal brands and donor networks. Roemer’s proposal would accelerate this atomization of the legislature.

Roemer points to his days as a "boll weevil" conservative Democrat in the 1980s as evidence of a better era. But the ability to cross party lines back then was less a product of a cleaner campaign finance system—there was plenty of special interest money flowing in 1981—and more a result of a less ideologically sorted electorate. The conservative Democrats of the South and the liberal Republicans of the Northeast provided natural bridges for compromise. Those bridges have burned down due to long-term demographic and partisan realignments, not simply because campaign finance laws changed.

We undoubtedly need reform. The capture of our financial regulatory system by Wall Street is a profound failure. But we must be clear-eyed about the incentives we are creating. A system funded entirely by atomized, highly polarized small donors responding to internet appeals is a recipe for perpetual grievance politics, not the return of the statesman. If we want a Congress capable of governing, we need institutions that reward compromise, rather than funding models that monetize outrage.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Small donors tend to be more ideologically extreme and partisan than large transactional donors, who often prefer stability and pragmatism.
  2. 02A funding system dependent on small online contributions forces politicians to generate manufactured outrage and ideological purity to maintain their revenue streams.
  3. 03Weakening institutional structures like political parties in favor of individual donor networks transforms legislators into free-agent entrepreneurs and removes the buffers that once protected moderates.
  4. 04The decline of cross-party cooperation is primarily the result of long-term demographic and partisan realignment rather than changes in campaign finance laws.
  5. 05Effective reform requires institutions that reward governing and compromise rather than funding models that monetize perpetual grievance politics.
