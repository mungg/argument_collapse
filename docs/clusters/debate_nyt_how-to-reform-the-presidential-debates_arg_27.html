"To foster substantive policy discussion, presidential debates should move away from a theatrical, sound-bite-driven format toward a structured, policy-focused environment with fewer participants and more flexible time limits."

The most important reform is not a more clever question. It is a less frantic structure. When ten or eleven candidates stand on a stage, and each is given 60 seconds to define a health care plan, explain Iran, or describe tax reform, the format guarantees slogans. The candidates are not failing an exam; they are responding rationally to a bad test.

Primary debates should be divided by subject and by smaller, randomly assigned groups. If a party has 15 plausible candidates, hold two debates on consecutive nights, with the groups chosen by lottery rather than by a crude “top tier” and “kids’ table” poll ranking. Early national polls mostly measure name recognition and media exposure, not democratic seriousness. Random assignment would also prevent networks from building the whole evening around one celebrity candidate.

Each debate should have a announced policy focus: one night on the economy and taxes, another on foreign policy, another on health care and entitlements, another on constitutional powers and appointments. The questions need not be released in advance, but the subject should be. Voters learn more when candidates must go beyond “I’ll cut waste” or “I’ll be tough.”

The time rules should change as well. Give each candidate a bank of time, perhaps eight or ten minutes total, to use across the debate. A moderator can press for specifics, but the candidate decides when an issue deserves more than a sound bite. If a candidate wants to spend three minutes explaining how to replace the Affordable Care Act, let him or her do it. If another candidate wants to use all the time on attacks, voters can judge that too.

Finally, the studio audience should be silent. Applause turns debates into rallies and rewards the best line rather than the best answer. A debate is not a convention speech. It should be a job interview for the most powerful office in the country.

These changes would not magically make campaigns noble. Candidates will still evade, exaggerate and posture. But the current format practically begs them to do so. A more substantive debate requires fewer people onstage at once, more time per answer, topic discipline and less theatrical production.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Candidates should be divided into smaller, randomly assigned groups to ensure equal opportunity and prevent the debates from revolving around a single celebrity candidate.
  2. 02Each debate should have a pre-announced policy focus to force candidates to move beyond slogans and provide detailed explanations of their positions.
  3. 03The rigid 60-second response rule should be replaced with a bank of time that candidates can allocate as they see fit to explain complex issues.
  4. 04Studio audiences should be required to remain silent to prevent the event from turning into a rally that rewards applause lines over substantive answers.
