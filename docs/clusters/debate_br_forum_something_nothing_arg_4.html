Diversified DeepSeek

"The United States should commit to General McChrystal’s counterinsurgency strategy in Afghanistan because it is a realistic, necessary path to stabilizing the country and protecting American security interests."

Nir Rosen’s essay on the war in Afghanistan is a brilliant piece of war reporting, but it is a lousy guide to strategy. As a soldier who served two tours in Iraq and has spent much of the past three years studying counterinsurgency, I recognize the scenes he paints: the exasperated American NCO, the unreliable local police, the absurd meeting with village elders, the kid throwing a rock, the frustrating rules of engagement. Any honest veteran will tell you that the war is often exactly that ugly. But to conclude from these anecdotes that the whole enterprise is futile is to confuse the texture of war with its purpose and possibility. The United States has made enormous mistakes in Afghanistan, but the McChrystal strategy represents a belated recognition of those mistakes, not a repetition of them. We should give it a chance to work—and it can work, if we sustain the commitment long enough to see it through.

The central mistake in Rosen’s analysis is his insistence that Iraq and Afghanistan are incomparable, and that the Iraq Surge succeeded only because of sectarian exhaustion he says is absent in Afghanistan. This is a half-truth. Sectarian civil war had indeed reshaped Baghdad by 2007, but the Surge did not merely ride that wave; it actively changed the tactical and political dynamics. U.S. forces moved off large bases into small outposts among the population, partnered with Sunni tribes who had been on the fence, and applied consistent pressure on both Sunni insurgents and Shia militias. The reduction in violence was not inevitable; it was the result of a deliberate strategy that required American troops to take greater risks. In Afghanistan today, there is no Baghdad, but the principle of living among the people and providing persistent security is transferable. The Helmand operation Rosen describes, for all its frustrations, was a first attempt to apply that model in the south. The Marines and their Afghan partners did not defeat the Taliban, but they did push them out of key areas and demonstrated that the government could project force. The challenge now is to hold those areas and extend governance, and that requires exactly the kind of patient mentoring and joint patrolling that Rosen belittles.

Rosen’s contempt for the Afghan forces is particularly shortsighted. He quotes soldiers who say the police “sucked,” and he details the tragic death of Lieutenant Farid. But look at what else his own reporting reveals: Farid kept fighting without body armor, his men took risks every day, and when the chips were down, many of them stayed in the fight despite the casualties. The Afghan National Police are a deeply flawed institution, but they are not hopeless. In Iraq, the police were also a disaster in the early years, riddled with militia infiltration and corruption. Over time, with American mentorship and a shift in local political conditions, many units became effective. The same can happen in Afghanistan if we commit to a long-term partnership instead of mocking their failures. The solution is not to abandon them but to professionalize them, link them to local communities, and pay them enough that bribery ceases to be economic necessity. This is the “build” part of clear-hold-build that takes time—years, not months—and it is precisely the work that the McChrystal plan envisions.

The essay’s most dangerous argument is the soft-pedaling of the al Qaeda threat. Rosen writes that al Qaeda is “a few hundred angry, unsophisticated Muslim extremists” who hardly threaten a vigilant superpower. Tell that to the families of the nearly three thousand people murdered on September 11. The lesson of that day was not that al Qaeda is an existential threat to American existence, but that even a small, determined group can inflict catastrophic damage when it enjoys a geographic safe haven and a state sponsor. The Taliban are not al Qaeda, but they have repeatedly shown themselves unwilling to break with their guests. A Taliban-dominated Afghanistan would not merely be an ugly regime; it would be a magnet for every jihadist group looking for a base from which to train, plan, and inspire attacks far beyond the region. The drone war in Pakistan has degraded al Qaeda’s leadership but not eliminated it, and the Pakistani state’s will to fight the group is at best ambivalent. A stable Afghan state that denies space to terrorists is the only sustainable way to protect the American homeland from the next mass-casualty attack.

Finally, it is important to address the tone of moral weariness that pervades Rosen’s piece and much of the left-of-center commentary on the war. I understand it. This war has gone on too long, cost too many lives, and asked too much of too small a portion of the American population. But disgust with war does not make the enemies disappear. The president has made a hard choice: not to escalate endlessly, but to commit enough resources to reverse the momentum of the insurgency and then, beginning in 2011, to begin transitioning responsibility to Afghan forces. This is a responsible path, not a blank check. Rosen’s answer—effectively, to leave and hope for the best—would abandon the Afghans who have cast their lot with us, signal to every enemy of the United States that we can be waited out, and create a humanitarian and security disaster. The soldiers of Team Prowler know the war is ugly. They also know they are doing work that matters. The argument for staying the course is not romantic; it is brutally realistic. We are in Afghanistan because our vital interests are at stake, and leaving before the job is done would be the real fantasy.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The core counterinsurgency principle of living among the population to provide security is a transferable and effective tactic that can succeed in Afghanistan just as it did during the Iraq Surge.
  2. 02While currently flawed, the Afghan National Police can become an effective force through long-term professionalization and consistent American mentorship.
  3. 03Preventing the Taliban from regaining control is essential to denying al Qaeda the geographic safe haven it requires to plan future catastrophic attacks against the United States.
  4. 04A premature withdrawal would betray Afghan allies, embolden enemies of the United States, and result in a humanitarian and security disaster.
  5. 05The current strategy is a responsible, time-bound commitment aimed at reversing insurgent momentum rather than an open-ended escalation.
Diversified DeepSeek

"The United States must commit to the counterinsurgency strategy in Afghanistan because it represents the best chance to prevent a return to the conditions that produced the September 11 attacks."

Nir Rosen has written a vivid and deeply troubling account of the American war in Afghanistan, and his conclusion—that the counterinsurgency strategy is doomed and the war itself a folly—is becoming conventional wisdom among a certain set of commentators. But his essay, for all its rich detail, is at bottom a counsel of despair that misreads the nature of the threat and the possibilities of the moment. The United States can and must succeed in Afghanistan, not because nation-building is a pleasant hobby, but because the alternative is a return to the conditions that produced September 11. The McChrystal plan, endorsed in large part by President Obama, represents the best chance we have to prevent that outcome, and Rosen’s objections fall short of making a case for abandoning the effort.

Rosen’s central claim is that a population-centric counterinsurgency campaign cannot work in Afghanistan because the country is too rural, the central government too corrupt, and the Taliban too resilient. He contrasts Iraq, where sectarian exhaustion and urban geography allegedly made the Surge possible, with Afghanistan, where no comparable exhaustion exists. This argument is half true and half a distortion. It is true that Afghanistan is not Iraq; no serious analyst thinks it is. But it is wrong to suggest that the Iraq Surge merely capitalized on preexisting exhaustion. The Surge succeeded because it combined a new strategy—securing the population in the cities and making political deals with former insurgents—with the necessary troop levels. If exhaustion were sufficient, Iraq would have calmed on its own. It did not. The commitment of forces and the change in approach were decisive. In Afghanistan, there is no city as large as Baghdad, but the principle of securing the population against insurgent intimidation is the same. The key terrain is not the empty desert but the densely populated river valleys like the Helmand River valley, where the majority of the population lives and where the insurgency thrives. The strategy of clearing, holding, and building in those areas, while difficult, is not inherently impossible.

Rosen also places too much weight on the incompetence and corruption of the Afghan police and army. Here his reporting is valuable, and no one can read his account of Lieutenant Farid’s death without sympathy. But the very existence of men like Farid, who stayed in the fight despite the odds, points to what is possible if we invest seriously in mentoring and equipping partner forces. The Afghan forces are not some immutable disaster; they are a work in progress. The U.S. military has learned hard lessons in Iraq about training indigenous forces, and those lessons are being applied. The aim of expanding the Afghan forces to 400,000 is ambitious, but it is not a fantasy; it is a recognition that without a capable local partner, no counterinsurgency can be sustained. The alternative Rosen seems to prefer—a light-footprint counterterrorism approach relying on drones and special forces—has been tried in Pakistan and Yemen, and the results are not encouraging: it kills terrorist leaders but creates new grievances and leaves ungoverned spaces for the next generation.

The most troubling aspect of Rosen’s argument is his dismissal of the threat from al Qaeda. He writes that al Qaeda is “already ensconced in Pakistan” and suggests that a Taliban takeover of Afghanistan would not meaningfully increase the danger. This is dangerously complacent. Before 9/11, al Qaeda enjoyed a safe haven in Afghanistan under the Taliban. While the Taliban may not share al Qaeda’s global jihadist ideology, they have repeatedly demonstrated a willingness to host the group in exchange for tactical support, and the longer the war drags on, the more that alliance of convenience hardens into ideological fusion. A collapsing Afghan state would not simply hand the country back to the Taliban; it would create a chaotic vacuum in which multiple extremist groups could operate, with consequences for Pakistan’s stability and, by extension, for the security of the entire region, including nuclear-armed Pakistan. President Obama was right to call Afghanistan and Pakistan “the epicenter of violent extremism.” We cannot afford to pretend otherwise just because the task is hard.

Finally, Rosen’s portrait of the war focuses relentlessly on the tactical frustrations and on the ugly, everyday moments of a messy conflict. That is understandable—war is ugly—but it misses the forest for the trees. The anecdote about the police commander who arrested a man for his cell-phone ringtone illustrates the challenge, not the impossibility, of building a rule-of-law culture. The Afghan government is deeply flawed, but the electoral crisis of 2009 has led to a renewed focus on governance and anti-corruption, however difficult. The path forward is not to walk away in disillusionment, but to use our leverage to insist on better performance while providing the security umbrella that makes political progress possible. A premature withdrawal, whether immediate or phased, would not buy peace; it would buy defeat, with consequences that would haunt the United States and the region for a generation. Rosen’s essay is a powerful cry of frustration, but frustration is not a policy. The United States owes it to its own security and to the Afghan people who have risked their lives beside us to see this effort through, not to abandon it to the Taliban night.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The success of the Iraq Surge demonstrates that a combination of population-centric strategy and sufficient troop levels can overcome insurgent intimidation, regardless of geographic differences.
  2. 02The incompetence of Afghan security forces is a reason for increased investment in mentoring and training rather than a justification for abandoning the mission.
  3. 03A light-footprint counterterrorism approach is insufficient because it fails to address the ungoverned spaces that allow extremist groups to thrive.
  4. 04Allowing Afghanistan to collapse would create a dangerous vacuum that strengthens the alliance between the Taliban and al-Qaeda and threatens the stability of nuclear-armed Pakistan.
  5. 05The United States must use its security presence as leverage to demand better governance and anti-corruption measures from the Afghan government rather than withdrawing in frustration.
Diversified DeepSeek

"While Nir Rosen accurately depicts the current tactical failures in Afghanistan, his conclusion that the war is unwinnable mistakes the predictable results of past under-resourcing for an inherent impossibility of the counterinsurgency strategy."

Nir Rosen’s “Something from Nothing” is a masterfully reported portrait of the day-to-day futility of America’s war in Afghanistan. At the ground level, the essay is almost unanswerable: the combination of Afghan police incompetence, civilian misery, and American frustration is as real as the dust on Highway 601. But as a strategic argument, the essay suffers from a critical flaw: it mistakes the tactical messiness of a complex war for proof that the war itself is unwinnable. In fact, what Rosen documents is not the impossibility of counterinsurgency but the predictable consequences of waging it with too few resources, too little coordination, and a chronically short time horizon. The Obama administration’s new strategy is an attempt to fix those problems—not a delusional exercise in nation-building, but a serious effort to put the war on a sounder footing. Whether it will work depends on what we do next, not on accepting Rosen’s implicit forecast of failure.

The heart of Rosen’s critique is that the Afghan security forces are a mess and the central government is a criminal enterprise. On this, he is largely right. But the question is whether these conditions are immutable. Here Rosen, like many critics, falls into a kind of functional determinism: because the Afghan state has never been strong, it can never become strong; because the police are corrupt today, they will be corrupt forever. History suggests otherwise. Every successful counterinsurgency has involved exactly the kind of slow, painful, two-steps-forward-one-step-back process of building local capacity that Rosen chronicles. The British in Malaya, the French in Algeria (though strategically lost), the Americans in the Philippines—all faced indigenous forces that were initially incompetent, venal, or unreliable. The difference in those cases was that the external power committed sufficient resources over a long enough period to allow a generation of officers and institutions to mature. The U.S. has never done that in Afghanistan. From 2002 to 2008, the American effort was an afterthought, starved of troops and money while the focus remained on Iraq. Only now, in the last year of the decade, are we beginning to apply the kind of serious mentorship and resources that a real counterinsurgency demands.

Rosen’s anecdotes about the Afghan police illustrate the problem but not the solution’s impossibility. When Captain Westby complains that the police have no radios, no body armor, and show up late, he is describing a force that has never been properly resourced, not one that is inherently hopeless. The same is true of the police commander who arrests a man for a Taliban ringtone. That is an ignorant and abusive act, but it is also the kind of behavior that can be corrected through better training, supervision, and the slow inculcation of professional norms—if American mentors are there to do it. The alternative is to abandon those police to their worst instincts, which is precisely what the Taliban want.

The argument about the central government is more difficult. The 2009 presidential election was an electoral catastrophe, and Hamid Karzai’s legitimacy is at an all-time low. But Rosen’s conclusion that “there was no hope of improvement or change” is more polemic than analysis. The U.S. has significant leverage over Karzai, and the new policy explicitly links aid and troop commitments to measurable improvements in governance and anti-corruption. This linkage may fail—it often has in the past—but it is not inherently absurd. In the Balkans, sustained international pressure and the promise of European integration eventually reshaped political behavior in ways that seemed inconceivable in the 1990s. Afghanistan is not the Balkans, but the principle that outside leverage can change local incentives is sound, if we are serious about applying it.

The deepest confusion in Rosen’s piece lies in his treatment of the al Qaeda threat. He writes that “Al Qaeda is already ensconced in Pakistan,” so a Taliban return in Afghanistan would not significantly increase the danger. This misses the point entirely. Al Qaeda is in Pakistan precisely because the U.S. toppled the Taliban regime that gave it sanctuary. It operates there now under constant pressure from drone strikes and Pakistani military operations, pressure that is possible because the U.S. has bases and allies in the region. A Taliban takeover of southern and eastern Afghanistan would not only provide a new safe haven; it would destabilize Pakistan further by emboldening its own Islamist insurgency and possibly giving extremists access to Pakistani nuclear secrets. The threat is not that al Qaeda will suddenly launch a second 9/11 from Kandahar; it is that a failing Afghan state would become a permanent engine of regional chaos that would keep American national security under siege for decades. That is why President Obama committed to a long-term partnership with the Afghan government, not merely a quick exit.

Rosen is a superb writer, and his essay deserves to be read by every policymaker and voter. But the proper response to his dark story is not to throw up our hands and go home. It is to insist that the new strategy be given the time and resources it requires, that governance benchmarks be enforced, and that the Afghan people see that the United States is serious about helping them build something—not from nothing, but from the shattered pieces of a country that has known little but war for thirty years. That is a worthy goal, and it is not yet beyond our reach.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The incompetence and corruption of Afghan security forces are not immutable traits but the direct consequence of a chronic lack of serious mentorship and resources since 2002.
  2. 02Historical precedents show that successful counterinsurgencies require a long-term commitment to allow local institutions and professional norms to mature through a slow, difficult process.
  3. 03The U.S. can use its significant leverage to reform the Afghan central government by explicitly linking aid and troop commitments to measurable governance benchmarks.
  4. 04Preventing a Taliban resurgence is strategically vital because a failing Afghan state would provide a safe haven for al Qaeda and destabilize nuclear-armed Pakistan.
  5. 05The Obama administration’s new strategy is a necessary and serious effort to put the war on a sounder footing by finally providing the coordination and time horizon required for success.
