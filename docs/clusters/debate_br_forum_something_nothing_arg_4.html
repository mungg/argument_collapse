Diversified Claude

"Nir Rosen’s dismissal of the counterinsurgency (COIN) strategy in Afghanistan is a premature conclusion based on anecdotal reporting rather than a sound strategic analysis of the mission's long-term potential."

Nir Rosen has written a vivid and dispiriting piece of reporting, and anyone who reads it will come away with a sharper sense of what the soldiers and police on Highway 601 are actually doing each day. But vivid reporting is not the same thing as strategic analysis, and Rosen's essay quietly conflates the two. From the fact that Team Prowler's experience is grim, frustrating, and often absurd, he concludes that the entire counterinsurgency enterprise in Afghanistan is doomed. That is a leap that the evidence on the page cannot bear.

Consider what Rosen actually documents. He spends time with a single mentor team in one of the worst districts of one of the worst provinces in the country, during the first months of a strategy whose declared horizon is years, not weeks. He shows us police who cannot read maps, who fail drug tests, who mutiny in the face of orders. He shows us American sergeants whose patience is exhausted. From this he infers that COIN cannot work. But the entire premise of population-centric counterinsurgency is that the situation looks like this at the beginning. If the Afghan National Police were already competent, sober, and motivated, and if the population in Helmand were already disinclined to harbor Taliban, we would not need a new strategy. We would simply need to leave. Rosen is, in effect, judging a marathon at the two-mile mark.

He is on stronger ground when he points to circumstantial differences between Iraq in 2007 and Afghanistan in 2009. The rural character of the Afghan insurgency, the absence of a civil-war exhaustion that pushed Iraqi factions toward the Americans, the dispersion of authority outside Kabul — all of this is true and important. The Field Manual's authors would not deny it. What follows from this, however, is not that COIN is impossible in Afghanistan, but that the timelines, troop ratios, and political-engagement requirements are different. Rosen never seriously engages this distinction. He treats "COIN is harder here than in Iraq" as equivalent to "COIN is impossible here."

The essay also slides too easily between two very different criticisms. One is that the American military is institutionally incapable of doing COIN — that the bureaucracy, the one-year rotations, the casual contempt of soldiers who call Afghans "Hajis," and the deep risk-aversion of the force make population protection unworkable in practice. The other is that even a competent counterinsurgency campaign would fail in Afghanistan because the political conditions are wrong. These are quite different claims with quite different implications. If the first is true, the answer is institutional reform, longer deployments, language training, civilian surge, and the empowerment of the comparatively small cadre of officers who have learned to do this work. If the second is true, no amount of institutional reform will help and we should leave. Rosen oscillates between these, drawing rhetorical energy from each without committing to either.

His treatment of al Qaeda is the weakest part of the essay. He is right that al Qaeda is currently more entrenched in Pakistan than in Afghanistan, and right that the Taliban are not a global jihadi movement in the bin Laden mold. But the conclusion he draws — that the threat of al Qaeda re-establishing sanctuary in Afghanistan is essentially a pretext — does not follow. The Taliban–al Qaeda relationship in the late 1990s was also officially one of "convenience," and yet Mullah Omar refused to hand bin Laden over even at the cost of his regime. Whatever one thinks of the wisdom of nation-building, the proposition that Taliban-controlled territory in eastern and southern Afghanistan would be neutral ground for transnational jihadi groups is not seriously defensible. Rosen's suggestion that "any new al Qaeda bases in Afghanistan could be bombed by the Americans" is exactly the over-the-horizon counterterrorism logic that produced the pre-2001 status quo. We tried that. It produced September 11.

Rosen's final move — that al Qaeda itself is not a serious enough threat to a "vigilant superpower" to justify any of this — deserves a direct response. It is true that the 9/11 attacks killed fewer Americans than die in car crashes every few months. It is also true that the response to those attacks did far more damage to American interests, prestige, and treasury than the attacks themselves. But the lesson of the last decade is not that catastrophic terrorism is a manageable nuisance; it is that small numbers of determined extremists, working from ungoverned spaces, can inflict damage out of all proportion to their numbers when they catch a target unawares. The vigilance Rosen takes for granted is itself a product of the policies he wants to abandon. Letting that vigilance lapse on the theory that the threat was always overblown is precisely the bargain that pre-9/11 policymakers thought they were making.

None of this is to defend McChrystal's assessment in detail, or Obama's compromise in detail. Rosen lands real blows. The composition of McChrystal's review team was indeed an exercise in Beltway coalition-building rather than country expertise. The plan to double the size of the ANP without addressing why the existing ANP is corrupt is, on its face, magical thinking. The contradiction between announcing an exit timeline and demanding that Afghans build durable institutions is real and damaging. Anyone honest about the strategy has to concede that it is being asked to do something very hard with too few resources and too short a clock.

But the alternative Rosen gestures toward — withdrawing because the project is hopeless, and accepting that al Qaeda is not really a strategic problem — is not actually an alternative. It is an abdication. The harder, less satisfying argument is that we are stuck with a difficult counterinsurgency campaign whose minimum requirement is the construction of Afghan security forces capable of preventing the country from again becoming a jihadi sanctuary, and that we should ruthlessly strip away the more ambitious goals (modern democratic governance, women's rights as we understand them, a Weberian central state) that we have neither the capacity nor the standing to deliver. That is not a counsel of despair; it is a counsel of focus. Rosen's reporting actually supports this kind of stripped-down realism more than it supports the wholesale rejection he ends on. He has earned the right to be skeptical of the people running this war. He has not earned the right to dismiss the war itself.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Judging the failure of COIN based on the initial incompetence of Afghan forces ignores the fact that the strategy is designed to address those very deficiencies over a period of years.
  2. 02The fact that Afghanistan presents greater geographic and political challenges than Iraq does not mean COIN is impossible, only that it requires different timelines and troop requirements.
  3. 03Rosen fails to distinguish between the military's institutional shortcomings, which can be reformed, and the inherent viability of the political mission in Afghanistan.
  4. 04The claim that al Qaeda does not pose a significant threat to a 'vigilant superpower' ignores the historical lesson that ungoverned spaces allow small groups to inflict disproportionate global damage.
  5. 05The presence of al Qaeda in Pakistan does not negate the strategic necessity of preventing the Taliban from providing the group with a renewed sanctuary in Afghanistan.
  6. 06The correct response to the war's difficulties is a 'stripped-down realism' that focuses on security and preventing jihadi sanctuaries rather than a total abdication of the mission.
Diversified Claude

"While Nir Rosen correctly identifies the operational failures of the war in Afghanistan, his conclusion that the mission is impossible is premature because a serious counterinsurgency strategy has only just begun to be implemented."

I have spent a fair amount of time in the same kind of company Nir Rosen describes — embedded mentor teams, district police chiefs, the unglamorous bottom of the war — and I want to register both an agreement and a serious disagreement with his essay.

The agreement is about the operational picture. Rosen has the texture right. The Afghan National Police really are, in many places, what he says they are: untrained, often illiterate, frequently corrupt, sometimes drug-addled, and used by local power brokers as muscle. The mentor teams really are stretched too thin, rotated too quickly, and operating on improvised institutional knowledge. The contradictions between McChrystal's tactical directives and the on-the-ground reality really are vicious; the kid in the squad who has just lost a buddy is not in a state of mind to weigh ISAF guidance about civilian casualties against the impulse to suppress the building from which the round came. The "year and out, learn nothing, hand off to a successor who relearns everything" pathology of American military rotation is real, and it is corrosive. Anyone who claims otherwise has not been there.

The disagreement is about what follows from this. Rosen treats these operational failures as proof that the broader strategic enterprise is impossible. I think they are proof that the broader strategic enterprise has, until very recently, not actually been attempted. We have been in Afghanistan eight years, but we have been doing serious counterinsurgency in Afghanistan for less than one. From 2002 through about 2008, the resource picture was a joke: a few tens of thousands of troops, almost all of the diplomatic and developmental bandwidth absorbed by Iraq, an Afghan army funded at a fraction of what it needed, almost no senior American attention. To say that this period was a failed COIN campaign is to misuse the term. There was no campaign. There was a holding action while the real war was fought in Mesopotamia.

McChrystal's plan is the first serious attempt to do in Afghanistan what Petraeus did in Iraq, and it is being judged after six months in a campaign whose own commander says will take three years to "have a decisive impact." Rosen's piece is essentially a verdict rendered at the moment the prosecution rests, before the defense has called a single witness.

That is not a defense of the McChrystal plan in its specifics. Rosen lands clean hits on several of them. The Afghan National Police really are unlikely to improve by simple addition; doubling the size of a broken institution mostly just doubles the brokenness. The exit timeline really is in tension with the population's calculation of who will be standing in five years. The McChrystal team's composition really was weighted toward DC celebrity rather than Afghan expertise, and that is a problem.

But here is what Rosen leaves out, or undersells. The Iraq Surge worked, in significant part, because the population calculated that the Americans were not leaving — that the cost of betting on the insurgency exceeded the cost of betting on the government. Population-centric COIN is not a magic technology. It is, fundamentally, an exercise in altering the local calculation of who will win. Once a meaningful number of villagers and tribal leaders bet on the government, intelligence flows, recruits dry up for the insurgency, and the security forces start receiving cooperation that allows them to do their job. The Anbar Awakening was not the Americans bribing tribes; it was tribes deciding that AQI had become more of a threat than the occupation, and that the Americans were going to be around long enough to make collaboration safe.

What Rosen documents in Helmand is what it looks like before this calculation has shifted. The old men in the field saying they will send "a small child" with information rather than be seen talking to the police are not in a population that has decided. They are in a population that is hedging, sensibly, against both sides. The whole point of the McChrystal approach is to alter that hedge — to put enough troops in enough places long enough that the calculation tilts. We do not yet know whether this can work in Helmand. We do know it had not been tried.

Rosen's other major argument — that the Iraq Surge worked because civil war was exhausting itself anyway, and that Afghanistan lacks any comparable exhaustion — has more force, but I think it overstates the case. The Surge accelerated and consolidated trends that had begun. It did not create them ex nihilo, and no serious COIN advocate claims it did. But Afghanistan does have its own form of exhaustion: a population battered by thirty years of war, increasingly tired of the Taliban's brutality where the Taliban actually govern, and not yet committed to any side because no side has demonstrated staying power. The strategic question is whether American commitment over the next two to three years can be the thing that breaks the indecision.

I do not know the answer to that question. Neither does Rosen, despite the certainty of his conclusion. What I do know is that the alternative he gestures at — a counterterrorism-only posture, drone strikes from over the horizon, walking away from the state-building enterprise — has been tried, more or less, by the Bush administration from 2002 to 2008, and produced the deteriorating situation we have now. Withdrawal would not return us to a stable status quo ante. It would produce a Taliban return to Kabul, a refugee crisis, an enormous propaganda victory for jihadi movements globally, and a Pakistani security establishment vindicated in its hedge.

The choice we actually face is not between McChrystal's plan and Rosen's prudent skepticism. It is between McChrystal's plan, executed seriously over enough years to know whether it can work, and a withdrawal whose consequences Rosen barely engages with. His essay is a powerful indictment of how we got here and a fair warning about the gap between the plan and the on-the-ground capacity. It is not, despite its rhetorical force, an argument for any particular alternative course. Soldiers do not have the luxury of writing essays about how everything is impossible. They have to choose among bad options. The McChrystal plan is a bad option. It may also, given the alternatives, be the best one we have.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The operational dysfunction Rosen describes is the result of a multi-year 'holding action' rather than a failed counterinsurgency campaign, which was never properly resourced until recently.
  2. 02The primary goal of the McChrystal plan is to shift the local population's calculation of who will win, a psychological shift that requires more time and troop presence than Rosen’s snapshot allows.
  3. 03The success of the Iraq Surge demonstrates that population-centric counterinsurgency works by making collaboration with the government safer than supporting the insurgency.
  4. 04Afghanistan possesses its own form of 'exhaustion' from decades of war that a committed, long-term American presence could potentially leverage to break the current indecision.
  5. 05The alternative to the current plan—a counterterrorism-only approach or withdrawal—has already failed in the past and would likely lead to a Taliban victory and global instability.
  6. 06The McChrystal plan, despite its flaws, represents the best available option among a set of difficult choices.
