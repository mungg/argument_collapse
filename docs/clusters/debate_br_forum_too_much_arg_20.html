"Nancy Hirschmann’s analysis of the gendered division of labor fails to account for the lived experiences, structural obstacles, and evolving identities of men who are actively attempting to share caregiving responsibilities."

**A Father Reads This Essay**

I am a man, a father of two young children, and I have read Nancy Hirschmann's essay with some discomfort. My wife and I both work full-time. We share childcare roughly evenly, though if I am honest, "roughly evenly" probably hides a real imbalance my wife would describe more accurately than I would. We are precisely the demographic Hirschmann hopes the next generation will resemble: dual earners trying to do this thing differently. And yet much of her essay rings hollow to me, because it treats men either as the absent beneficiaries of women's labor or as obstacles to be coerced through wifely refusal. There is, in this essay's long account of how the sexual division of labor might be changed, almost no portrait of men actually engaged in caregiving, no analysis of the obstacles men face in trying to do so, and no acknowledgment that the change Hirschmann calls for will require a transformation of male identity as profound as the one feminism asked of women a generation ago.

Let me start with the obstacles, which Hirschmann mentions only glancingly. Arlie Hochschild's work, which she cites, established that men using family-friendly workplace policies are penalized in ways comparable to mothers, and often more severely. The man who takes paternity leave is regarded as not serious about his career. The man who leaves a meeting because his child is sick is "not committed." The man who works part-time to care for his family is, in the eyes of many employers and colleagues, simply not a man. These are real penalties, and they keep many fathers who would like to do more from doing more. Hirschmann notes the phenomenon and moves on. But the implications matter: if men face professional ruin for caregiving, then telling women to "refuse" and force men into more caregiving is not a coherent plan. It is a plan to immiserate two adults simultaneously, on the theory that this immiseration will somehow shame the labor market into changing. I see no evidence the labor market is reachable by this route.

The cultural side is harder. The script for fatherhood in the United States has loosened a great deal in my lifetime—my own father did almost no caregiving and felt no shame about that, whereas I cannot imagine modeling the same for my children. But the script is still narrow. Male caregivers report being treated with mild suspicion in playgrounds and pediatricians' offices. The "Mr. Mom" tradition of comic incompetence persists in advertising. Many of the men I know who are heavily involved fathers describe a curious dual judgment: women admire them; men, especially older men, regard them with quiet condescension. None of this is a tragedy. It is, however, the texture of cultural change, and the texture is rougher than Hirschmann's account suggests.

What I want to say to feminist readers, including Hirschmann, is that the men your project depends on are mostly invisible in your prose. The men in Hirschmann's essay are the husbands of Susan and Anthony (absent from the anecdotes entirely), the unspecified husbands of the elite stay-at-home mothers, the abstract figures whose share of housework is 7-10 hours per week, the colleagues banking early tenure. There is no portrait of a man trying. There is no recognition that, alongside the gendered penalties women face, men face a different but real penalty for departing from the breadwinner script.

This omission has political consequences. The strategy Hirschmann ultimately recommends—women becoming more individualist, gaining economic clout, leveraging that clout to force men to do more care work—treats men as obstacles to be coerced by economic pressure. It does not treat men as potential partners with their own interest in this change. But many men, in my generation and the one behind me, do have such an interest. We want to know our children. We want lives in which work is not the entirety of what we are. We want marriages of equals because the alternative is suffocating. The feminist project's success depends, in part, on enlisting us actively. And we are more likely to enlist when feminism speaks to us as people with stakes in the outcome, not only as recalcitrants who must be moved by wifely leverage.

The other reason I want a richer account of fatherhood here is that Hirschmann's main empirical claim—that the gender of the primary caretaker has "profound implications" for children's understanding of gender—cuts both ways. Yes, if children only ever see mothers caring and fathers earning, they will internalize that pattern. But the corollary is that when children see fathers caring, fathers cooking, fathers staying up with sick toddlers, this changes their internal map. My own children's expectations are different from mine. They know fathers do this work because they have watched their father do it. This is the slow generational mechanism by which the change actually takes hold. It is happening, unevenly, in households all over the country. Hirschmann's essay barely acknowledges it.

I want to register one further disagreement. Hirschmann is dismissive of family-friendly workplace policies on the grounds that they have not, on their own, equalized the division of labor. This is fair as a critique of overreliance on such policies. But I would not abandon them. In my own life, my employer's policies allowing flexible scheduling and parental leave have made my participation in caregiving possible. Without them, I would be a more absent father. Multiply that across millions of households and the policies matter. They are necessary but not sufficient, like most policies. The fact that they have not solved the problem alone is not an argument against them; it is an argument for combining them with the cultural changes Hirschmann calls for.

I close with a small request. The next generation of feminist writing about care and gender ought to take fatherhood seriously as a site of theory and practice—not just as the absent term in the equation. Men are not natural antagonists of feminism. Many of us are trying. The trying is harder than the public discourse acknowledges. And it is more important than ever, because Hirschmann is right about one thing above all: nothing will change until men do.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The essay overlooks the severe professional and social penalties men face when they deviate from the traditional breadwinner role to engage in caregiving.
  2. 02Treating men solely as obstacles to be coerced through economic leverage ignores their potential as partners who have a personal stake in achieving gender equality.
  3. 03The author's dismissal of family-friendly workplace policies ignores how these tools are essential, if insufficient, for making male participation in caregiving practically possible.
  4. 04A more effective feminist theory must recognize that active fatherhood is already beginning to reshape children's internal maps of gender roles through daily practice.
  5. 05The transformation of the sexual division of labor requires a profound shift in male identity that cannot be achieved through wifely refusal alone.
