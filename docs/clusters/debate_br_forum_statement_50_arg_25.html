"Tom Hayden’s celebration of participatory democracy is a naive retreat into nostalgia that fails to address the strategic necessity of building institutional power to defeat a well-organized conservative counterrevolution."

Tom Hayden’s anniversary essay is a triumph of lyrical reminiscence, but it is also a study in evasion. Nowhere in his generous catalog of movements, martyrs, and metaphors does he grapple with the single most important political fact of the last fifty years: the sustained, deliberate assault on American democracy by a well-funded, highly organized, and ideologically ruthless conservative counterrevolution. Hayden writes of participatory democracy’s “fits and starts,” of the establishment conceding on issues, and of an “inevitable countermovement” that tries to contain achievements. But this language is far too polite. What we have witnessed is not a natural cycle of action and reaction but a deliberate counter-mobilization designed to dismantle the institutional supports for any kind of democracy, participatory or representative. To celebrate the Port Huron Statement without fully naming the enemy is to write an obituary for hope while pretending it is a birth announcement.

Consider the historical sequence. The Port Huron Statement was born in the brief window between the New Deal order and the Reagan revolution, a moment of expanding social citizenship when the federal government could plausibly be seen as a potential ally. That world is dead. Beginning in the 1970s, a coalition of corporate interests, right-wing foundations, and electoral strategists set out to roll back the gains of the ’60s with a ferocity that has no parallel in American history. Labor law was rewritten to favor capital over workers. The tax code was restructured to concentrate wealth at the top. The welfare state was rhetorically tarred and systematically starved. The courts were packed with judges hostile to civil rights, environmental regulation, and campaign finance limits. The Voting Rights Act itself is now under a sustained judicial assault that would have been unthinkable to the authors of Port Huron.

This counterrevolution did not merely happen; it was built, painstakingly, by people who understood that formal democracy is an obstacle to the concentration of wealth. They created an entire parallel infrastructure—think tanks, media outlets, legal networks, grassroots training centers—designed to impose a radical anti-democratic agenda on a reluctant public. They exploited the very tools of participation that Hayden celebrates: ballot initiatives were weaponized to pass tax limitations and anti-affirmative-action measures; town halls were flooded with organized operatives; recall elections were bankrolled by billionaire donors. In this environment, the progressive enthusiasm for “direct and transparent participatory democracy” begins to look tragically naive. Participatory democracy, in the hands of the powerful, is not a revival of the ’60s; it is a sophisticated form of political combat that has consistently disadvantaged those with the fewest resources.

Hayden’s response to this reality seems to be a retreat into sentiment. He sees the Dreamers and the Occupy activists and pronounces them the spirits of the ’60s reborn. But courage is not a strategy. What the Dreamers need is not spiritual affirmation from an aging radical; they need a concrete legislative victory, and that requires an organized political force willing to play hardball—filibusters, primary challenges, mass voter registration drives, and, yes, the kind of backroom maneuvering that the participatory-democratic ethos often disdains. The same goes for the climate movement, the labor movement, and every other progressive cause. The inside-outside strategy Hayden sketches is not wrong, but it is woefully under specified. It treats “inside” politicians as noble door-openers and ignores the hard, unglamorous work of building party discipline, developing policy expertise, and winning elections at the state and local level where the counterrevolution has been most devastating.

The near-deification of participatory democracy has, I fear, become a self-soothing mechanism for a left that has lost its nerve. Rather than confront the messy, compromised realities of institutional power, we celebrate horizontalism and consensus as moral absolutes. We mistake a process for a politics. The Port Huron Statement at least had the intellectual honesty to engage with the Cold War architecture of power; its authors understood that their moral appeal was situated within a concrete field of forces. Hayden’s essay, by contrast, floats free of any serious strategic assessment. It reads like a sermon delivered to the already converted, a warm bath of nostalgia that asks nothing of its audience except a knowing nod.

So let me be blunt. The core message of the Port Huron Statement is not timeless; it is a product of a specific moment that is gone. What remains of value is not the doctrine of participatory democracy but the imperative to organize, to build power, and to wield that power relentlessly on behalf of the marginalized. That means recognizing that democracy is not a gentle conversation among friends; it is a battlefield, and the other side is armed with superior weaponry and a clear plan. If we are to honor the generation that marched, sat in, and refused to be silent, we must match their courage with a strategic clarity that they often lacked. We must build the political machinery—the legal fund, the labor-community alliances, the disciplined electoral operations—that can defeat the counterrevolution at its own game. And we must be honest with ourselves that this will require leaving behind some of the comforting pieties of 1962.

Tom Hayden is right about one thing: the spirit of the ’60s keeps appearing. But a spirit is not enough. What we need are soldiers, organizers, tacticians—people willing to engage in the grinding, often dispiriting work of democratic construction. Until we can produce that, the Port Huron Statement will remain what it has been for fifty years: a beautiful dream, floating just out of reach above a nightmare from which we cannot awaken.
