"New York City should replace its current model of exclusive, test-based gifted classrooms with a system that provides flexible, accelerated enrichment to all students who need it within their neighborhood schools."

Public schools should offer gifted education, but New York City’s current model of scarce, test-in, separate classrooms is a poor way to do it. The real question is not whether some children need more advanced work; they plainly do. The question is whether the city should identify them at age four, largely through a high-stakes exam, and then concentrate opportunity in a few classrooms and schools.

That system is both educationally and civically weak. Admissions to gifted programs in New York have been strongly skewed toward children from more affluent, white and Asian families, while black, Latino, low-income and English-learning children are underrepresented. It strains belief to conclude that talent is distributed that unevenly. What is unevenly distributed is information, test preparation, stable preschool experience and parental ability to navigate the bureaucracy. A public program that is supposed to cultivate ability ends up rewarding early advantage.

Separate gifted classrooms can also drain neighborhood schools of involved families and reinforce the idea that “real” rigor is reserved for a small group. Children who are not selected get a message too, often before kindergarten has even begun. And because gifted seats are limited, the debate becomes a zero-sum contest among parents rather than a serious conversation about improving instruction.

Still, abolishing gifted education altogether would be a mistake. Advanced learners, including those in poor neighborhoods, can become bored and disengaged when schools offer only grade-level work. Research on ability grouping suggests that high-achieving students benefit most when grouping is tied to genuinely accelerated curriculum, not merely a prestigious label. The answer is to provide challenge more broadly and identify need more intelligently.

Carmen Fariña’s phrase, “gifted practices for all students,” points in the right direction if it means richer curriculum, project-based work, faster pacing when appropriate, and teachers trained to differentiate. But it cannot mean pretending every child needs the same lesson. The city should use universal screening, multiple measures, teacher observations, portfolios and flexible grouping that changes over time. It should expand enrichment in every neighborhood school, while preserving access to acceleration for students who need it.

Gifted education is useful when it is a service. It is harmful when it becomes a sorting system.

"New York City should replace its segregated gifted and talented programs with a model that integrates advanced instructional practices into all neighborhood classrooms to ensure educational equity."

The debate over New York City’s gifted and talented programs is too often framed as a choice between nurturing exceptional potential and abandoning bright children to boredom. But the real question is whether the current model actually serves that purpose equitably, or whether it instead deepens the city’s profound educational segregation under a veneer of meritocracy. When Chancellor Carmen Fariña suggests that neighborhood schools should “provide gifted practices to all students,” she is pointing toward a more honest and effective approach—one that recognizes that our selective classrooms have become engines of stratification.

The entrance process alone is damning. As of 2014, admission for most programs relies on a standardized test given to four- and five-year-olds, an age when a child’s cognitive development is wildly uneven and heavily shaped by environmental privilege. It is an open secret in many middle-class and affluent neighborhoods that an entire cottage industry of tutors and test-prep books has sprung up to coach toddlers for the Otis-Lennon School Ability Test and the Bracken assessment. The result is glaring: in a school system that is over 70 percent black and Hispanic, those groups together make up less than 30 percent of the students in gifted kindergarten seats. The racial and economic sorting that begins at age four solidifies into a tracking system that often defines a child’s entire educational trajectory.

Beyond the admissions game, the separate classrooms themselves become a mechanism for hoarding resources. Schools facing budget pressures concentrate their most experienced teachers and most academically engaged families in isolated gifted tracks, while leaving general education classrooms with larger class sizes and fewer enrichment opportunities. Research on tracking has long shown that such segregation often depresses achievement for the children who are excluded, without yielding dramatic cognitive benefits for the top students that could not be gained in a well-mixed classroom with differentiated instruction.

Proponents argue that without dedicated programs, advanced learners will wither. But the far more scalable and just alternative is to embed gifted practices—inquiry-based projects, flexible grouping, deep dives into complex topics—into every classroom. This would require investing in teacher training and smaller class sizes across the board, a political challenge far greater than simply sorting children on a bell curve. Yet if the city truly values the potential of all its students, it must stop rationing engaging instruction as if it were a luxury reserved for the few who test well at an age when they can barely tie their shoes.

"New York City’s current gifted and talented programs should be dismantled in favor of providing enrichment practices to all students because the current system functions as an engine of socioeconomic and racial segregation."

Chancellor Carmen Fariña’s skepticism regarding New York City’s isolated “gifted and talented” classrooms is not only justified, it is long overdue. While the impulse to challenge advanced learners is pedagogically sound, the city's current G&T system operates less as an educational necessity and more as an engine of socioeconomic segregation.

The foundational flaw of the city’s G&T programs lies in how students are selected: through standardized tests administered to four-year-olds. Developmentally, assessing the innate cognitive abilities of a preschooler is a notoriously unreliable science. In practice, these exams rarely measure intrinsic giftedness; instead, they measure a child’s early exposure to vocabulary and logic puzzles, which heavily correlates with family income. Affluent parents routinely spend thousands of dollars on test-prep tutors for toddlers to secure a coveted G&T seat. Consequently, the system effectively screens for wealth and parental resources rather than raw academic potential.

The fallout from this flawed selection process is extreme demographic sorting. A recent report from the Civil Rights Project at U.C.L.A. highlighted that New York state has the most segregated public schools in the nation, with New York City at the epicenter. Gifted classrooms frequently exacerbate this reality, functioning as segregated enclaves within diverse neighborhood schools. Tracking young students into separate classrooms based on dubious early-childhood exams creates a two-tiered system that deprives general education students of high-achieving peer models and concentrates educational resources among those who already possess systemic advantages.

Fariña’s vision to bring “gifted practices to all students” is the superior approach. Differentiated instruction within mixed-ability classrooms allows teachers to provide enrichment and accelerated projects for students who need a challenge, without lowering the floor for others. By dismantling rigid tracking mechanisms and spreading enrichment models across all classrooms, the city can fulfill its mandate to educate every child equitably, rather than reinforcing an artificial hierarchy established before kindergarten.

New York City should stop treating gifted education as a scarce prize to be won at age 4. The current system is less a serious educational strategy than an early sorting mechanism that rewards family advantage, test preparation and parental savvy.

The basic problem is not that some children are ready for harder work. Of course they are. Any good school must recognize that a child who is reading chapter books in kindergarten, or doing multiplication in first grade, needs more than the standard worksheet. The problem is that New York has built a separate and unequal structure around that fact. Admission to many gifted programs depends heavily on a one-shot test taken by very young children. Families with money can buy test preparation, learn the rules, visit schools, rank programs strategically and appeal decisions. Families without those resources often cannot.

The result is predictable: gifted classrooms in New York are disproportionately white and Asian, and disproportionately middle class, in a school system where most students are black, Latino or poor. That should trouble anyone who believes public education is supposed to expand opportunity rather than certify advantage before children have even lost their baby teeth.

Defenders of gifted programs often say that advanced children will be bored without them. But this argument assumes that ordinary classrooms must remain ordinary. Chancellor Fariña’s phrase, “gifted practices for all students,” points in a better direction. The best features of gifted education — project-based learning, rich discussion, open-ended problems, independent research, exposure to the arts and sciences — are not magical techniques that only benefit children with high test scores. They are good teaching.

The city should invest in making neighborhood schools more challenging, not in removing a small group of children from them. That means smaller classes where possible, better professional development in differentiation, more schoolwide enrichment, flexible grouping within subjects, and access to accelerated work when students demonstrate readiness. A child who is advanced in math should be able to move ahead in math without being labeled globally “gifted” at age 5. A child who is not an early reader should not be consigned to a less stimulating classroom.

Separate gifted programs also create political costs. They give affluent parents a semi-private track within the public system, reducing pressure to improve schools for everyone. In some neighborhoods, gifted programs are effectively used as retention tools for middle-class families. That may be understandable, but it is not a just organizing principle.

Public schools should cultivate talent wherever it appears. New York’s gifted system too often identifies privilege and calls it merit. The city should phase out separate gifted classrooms and replace them with rigorous, enriched neighborhood schools for all children.

"New York City should replace its rigid, test-based gifted programs with flexible, performance-based instructional models that integrate advanced learning into general education settings."

The debate over New York's gifted classrooms tends to get framed as a binary — keep them or scrap them — when the more useful conversation is about what we actually mean by "gifted" and what problem the programs are supposed to solve. Once you press on those questions, both Chancellor Fariña's critics and her defenders start to look like they are arguing past each other.

Consider the word itself. In NYC, "gifted" operationally means "scored at the 90th or 97th percentile on the OLSAT and NNAT at age four." That is a measurement, not a description of a child. We know from decades of psychometric work that test scores at that age are noisy, heavily influenced by environment, and only modestly predictive of later academic performance. Calling a four-year-old "gifted" based on a ninety-minute sitting with a test administrator gives the label a fixedness it does not deserve. Children grow into and out of academic readiness; a system that sorts them once and for all at kindergarten entry is making a category error.

That said, the kids who score well are, on average, kids who arrive at school more prepared, and pretending otherwise helps no one. Putting a child who is reading fluently into a classroom where the focus is on letter recognition wastes that child's year. So the policy question is really: what is the least disruptive, most flexible way to match instruction to readiness?

Several models deserve more attention than they are getting. Schoolwide enrichment, pioneered by Joseph Renzulli, brings advanced work to all students and identifies talent through performance rather than testing. Subject-specific acceleration — letting a third grader join fifth-grade math — preserves the social benefits of mixed classrooms while addressing genuine asynchrony. Cluster grouping within heterogeneous classes can give teachers a manageable range without creating segregated tracks. None of these are utopian; districts around the country use them.

What is hard to defend is the status quo: a parallel system, often within the same building, that mirrors the city's racial and class hierarchies with uncomfortable precision, justified by a test most education researchers would not stake their reputations on. Fariña is not wrong to want to fold "gifted practices" into general instruction. She is wrong if she thinks slogans alone will do it. The real work — training teachers, redesigning curriculum, redoing identification — is unglamorous, expensive, and exactly what the city should commit to.

The debate over gifted classrooms often gets trapped between two unsatisfying slogans: “save gifted education” and “gifted for all.” New York City needs a more practical answer. Some separation by readiness can be useful, but the current model of full-time gifted classrooms beginning in kindergarten is too blunt, too early and too socially divisive.

The first question should be: What educational problem are we trying to solve? If the problem is that many classrooms are not challenging enough, then gifted admissions will not solve it. It will simply create islands of challenge for the children who get in. If the problem is that some students are dramatically ahead in particular subjects, then a full-time gifted label may also be the wrong solution. A child may be advanced in math but average in writing, or verbally precocious but not unusually strong in spatial reasoning. Children develop unevenly, especially in the early grades.

That is why New York should move toward a flexible enrichment and acceleration model. Schools can use cluster grouping, subject-based acceleration, pull-out seminars, after-school and Saturday enrichment, and partnerships with museums, libraries and universities. A second grader ready for fourth-grade math should get fourth-grade math. A group of strong readers should be able to tackle harder books together. A child fascinated by science should have opportunities for independent investigation. But none of this requires declaring one classroom “gifted” and another “not gifted” for the entire school day.

Full-time gifted programs may still be appropriate for a small number of students whose academic needs are extreme. Some children really are several years ahead across domains, and a regular classroom cannot easily meet them. The city should not pretend those children do not exist. But those placements should be later, carefully evaluated and periodically reviewed, not treated as a lifetime passport earned through a preschool test.

Equity has to be central. New York’s existing gifted system relies too much on parent initiative and early testing. That naturally advantages families with time, money and information. Universal screening, local norms, multiple entry points and outreach in every district would make identification more credible. At the same time, every neighborhood school should be expected to offer serious enrichment, not just test preparation and basic skills.

Chancellor Fariña is right to question the worship of gifted programs, especially when they become symbols of status. But “gifted practices for all” will be empty unless schools receive training, curriculum and staffing to make it real. The goal should not be to flatten differences among children.   4. 04Differentiated instruction within mixed-ability classrooms is a viable pedagogical alternative that challenges advanced learners without creating rigid academic hierarchies.
  5. 05Eliminating separate gifted tracks fosters school community cohesion and helps prevent the social stratification that drives advantaged families away from integrated public education.
