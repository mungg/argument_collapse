"New York City should replace its current model of exclusive, test-based gifted classrooms with a system that provides flexible, accelerated enrichment to all students who need it within their neighborhood schools."

Public schools should offer gifted education, but New York City’s current model of scarce, test-in, separate classrooms is a poor way to do it. The real question is not whether some children need more advanced work; they plainly do. The question is whether the city should identify them at age four, largely through a high-stakes exam, and then concentrate opportunity in a few classrooms and schools.

That system is both educationally and civically weak. Admissions to gifted programs in New York have been strongly skewed toward children from more affluent, white and Asian families, while black, Latino, low-income and English-learning children are underrepresented. It strains belief to conclude that talent is distributed that unevenly. What is unevenly distributed is information, test preparation, stable preschool experience and parental ability to navigate the bureaucracy. A public program that is supposed to cultivate ability ends up rewarding early advantage.

Separate gifted classrooms can also drain neighborhood schools of involved families and reinforce the idea that “real” rigor is reserved for a small group. Children who are not selected get a message too, often before kindergarten has even begun. And because gifted seats are limited, the debate becomes a zero-sum contest among parents rather than a serious conversation about improving instruction.

Still, abolishing gifted education altogether would be a mistake. Advanced learners, including those in poor neighborhoods, can become bored and disengaged when schools offer only grade-level work. Research on ability grouping suggests that high-achieving students benefit most when grouping is tied to genuinely accelerated curriculum, not merely a prestigious label. The answer is to provide challenge more broadly and identify need more intelligently.

Carmen Fariña’s phrase, “gifted practices for all students,” points in the right direction if it means richer curriculum, project-based work, faster pacing when appropriate, and teachers trained to differentiate. But it cannot mean pretending every child needs the same lesson. The city should use universal screening, multiple measures, teacher observations, portfolios and flexible grouping that changes over time. It should expand enrichment in every neighborhood school, while preserving access to acceleration for students who need it.

Gifted education is useful when it is a service. It is harmful when it becomes a sorting system.

Vanilla Claude

"New York City should retain its gifted and talented programs because they provide essential academic benefits, but the system must be reformed to ensure equitable access and diverse representation."

New York City's gifted and talented programs are useful, but they are also doing real harm in their current form — and Chancellor Fariña is right to be uneasy about them, even if abolishing them is the wrong response.

The case for the programs is straightforward. Children who learn faster than their peers genuinely benefit from instruction pitched at their level; without it, many disengage, coast, or are quietly held back by a curriculum aimed at the middle. Decades of research on ability grouping, including James Kulik's meta-analyses and more recent work by Jonathan Plucker and others, suggest that high-ability students gain meaningfully from accelerated tracks, while losses to other students are small or nil. For working- and middle-class New York families, citywide gifted programs at schools like Anderson or NEST+m are also one of the few reasons not to flee to the suburbs or to private schools. Eliminate them and you accelerate the very segregation reformers say they oppose.

The problem is who gets in. Admitting four-year-olds on the basis of a single high-stakes test — the OLSAT and NNAT — almost guarantees that children whose parents can afford test prep, or who simply know the system exists, will dominate the seats. The result is the demographic picture the UCLA Civil Rights Project and the city's own data keep showing: gifted classrooms that are overwhelmingly white and Asian inside a school system that is overwhelmingly black and Hispanic. That is not a meritocracy identifying talent; it is a parallel school system for the already-advantaged, sitting inside buildings where other children watch them walk down a different hallway.

The fix is not Fariña's vague aspiration to spread "gifted practices" to everyone — that usually means nothing changes except that the label disappears. The fix is to identify giftedness later than kindergarten, use multiple measures including teacher nominations and local norms, screen every child universally rather than only those whose parents apply, and seed real gifted seats in every district. Keep the programs; fix the gate.

"New York City should replace its segregated gifted and talented programs with a model that integrates advanced instructional practices into all neighborhood classrooms to ensure educational equity."

The debate over New York City’s gifted and talented programs is too often framed as a choice between nurturing exceptional potential and abandoning bright children to boredom. But the real question is whether the current model actually serves that purpose equitably, or whether it instead deepens the city’s profound educational segregation under a veneer of meritocracy. When Chancellor Carmen Fariña suggests that neighborhood schools should “provide gifted practices to all students,” she is pointing toward a more honest and effective approach—one that recognizes that our selective classrooms have become engines of stratification.

The entrance process alone is damning. As of 2014, admission for most programs relies on a standardized test given to four- and five-year-olds, an age when a child’s cognitive development is wildly uneven and heavily shaped by environmental privilege. It is an open secret in many middle-class and affluent neighborhoods that an entire cottage industry of tutors and test-prep books has sprung up to coach toddlers for the Otis-Lennon School Ability Test and the Bracken assessment. The result is glaring: in a school system that is over 70 percent black and Hispanic, those groups together make up less than 30 percent of the students in gifted kindergarten seats. The racial and economic sorting that begins at age four solidifies into a tracking system that often defines a child’s entire educational trajectory.

Beyond the admissions game, the separate classrooms themselves become a mechanism for hoarding resources. Schools facing budget pressures concentrate their most experienced teachers and most academically engaged families in isolated gifted tracks, while leaving general education classrooms with larger class sizes and fewer enrichment opportunities. Research on tracking has long shown that such segregation often depresses achievement for the children who are excluded, without yielding dramatic cognitive benefits for the top students that could not be gained in a well-mixed classroom with differentiated instruction.

Proponents argue that without dedicated programs, advanced learners will wither. But the far more scalable and just alternative is to embed gifted practices—inquiry-based projects, flexible grouping, deep dives into complex topics—into every classroom. This would require investing in teacher training and smaller class sizes across the board, a political challenge far greater than simply sorting children on a bell curve. Yet if the city truly values the potential of all its students, it must stop rationing engaging instruction as if it were a luxury reserved for the few who test well at an age when they can barely tie their shoes.

The debate over New York City's gifted classrooms reflects a deeper tension in American education between the ideals of heterogeneous classrooms and the practical reality that children learn at different paces and in different ways. Chancellor Carmen Fariña's position—that neighborhood schools should "provide gifted practices to all students"—is philosophically appealing but practically vague. What exactly does "gifted practices" mean when applied uniformly? Acceleration, depth of content, and independent research projects are interventions that genuinely serve high-ability learners. Simply labeling these practices as appropriate for everyone may satisfy political sensibilities while delivering neither rigor nor enrichment to any student.

The legitimate concern driving skepticism about gifted programs is not that advanced instruction is harmful but that New York's specific program has been a mechanism of segregation. The underrepresentation of Black and Latino students in gifted classrooms reflects systemic barriers—test preparation access, information asymmetries, and bureaucratic complexity—that disadvantage the families least equipped to navigate them. Any defense of specialized programming must grapple with this inequity directly.

However, the answer to segregation is not to eliminate programming but to reform access. Research consistently shows that without appropriate challenges, high-ability students disengage from school, develop underachievement patterns, and fail to reach their potential. The "gifted and talented" label, whatever its limitations, at least creates a structure within which accelerated learning can occur.

The more promising path is neither uncritical acceptance of current gifted programs nor their wholesale elimination, but rather a dual strategy: first, dramatically improve outreach and access to gifted programming for historically underserved populations, and second, invest in building capacity within regular classrooms so that enrichment reaches more students. Fariña's rhetoric points toward the right direction, but execution matters more than slogans. The question is not whether to serve advanced learners but how to do so equitably and effectively.

"New York City should replace its rigid, test-based gifted programs with flexible, performance-based instructional models that integrate advanced learning into general education settings."

The debate over New York's gifted classrooms tends to get framed as a binary — keep them or scrap them — when the more useful conversation is about what we actually mean by "gifted" and what problem the programs are supposed to solve. Once you press on those questions, both Chancellor Fariña's critics and her defenders start to look like they are arguing past each other.

Consider the word itself. In NYC, "gifted" operationally means "scored at the 90th or 97th percentile on the OLSAT and NNAT at age four." That is a measurement, not a description of a child. We know from decades of psychometric work that test scores at that age are noisy, heavily influenced by environment, and only modestly predictive of later academic performance. Calling a four-year-old "gifted" based on a ninety-minute sitting with a test administrator gives the label a fixedness it does not deserve. Children grow into and out of academic readiness; a system that sorts them once and for all at kindergarten entry is making a category error.

That said, the kids who score well are, on average, kids who arrive at school more prepared, and pretending otherwise helps no one. Putting a child who is reading fluently into a classroom where the focus is on letter recognition wastes that child's year. So the policy question is really: what is the least disruptive, most flexible way to match instruction to readiness?

Several models deserve more attention than they are getting. Schoolwide enrichment, pioneered by Joseph Renzulli, brings advanced work to all students and identifies talent through performance rather than testing. Subject-specific acceleration — letting a third grader join fifth-grade math — preserves the social benefits of mixed classrooms while addressing genuine asynchrony. Cluster grouping within heterogeneous classes can give teachers a manageable range without creating segregated tracks. None of these are utopian; districts around the country use them.

What is hard to defend is the status quo: a parallel system, often within the same building, that mirrors the city's racial and class hierarchies with uncomfortable precision, justified by a test most education researchers would not stake their reputations on. Fariña is not wrong to want to fold "gifted practices" into general instruction. She is wrong if she thinks slogans alone will do it. The real work — training teachers, redesigning curriculum, redoing identification — is unglamorous, expensive, and exactly what the city should commit to.

Public schools should offer gifted programs, including separate classrooms in some cases, because children at the far end of the learning spectrum have real educational needs. The mistake is to pretend that equality requires every child to receive the same lesson at the same pace.

Anyone who has spent time in an elementary classroom knows the range can be enormous. In one third-grade room, some students may still be mastering basic computation while others are ready for fractions, negative numbers or algebraic reasoning. In reading, one child may be decoding simple texts while another is ready for Dickens, science writing or serious historical nonfiction. A teacher can and should differentiate, but there are limits to what one person can do for 25 or 30 children simultaneously.

Gifted programs exist because some students need acceleration, depth and peers who can work at a similar level. That is not elitism; it is responsiveness. We would not tell a child with a speech delay to wait patiently because individualized services are unfair to everyone else. We should not tell a highly advanced child to spend years reviewing material already mastered. Boredom can become disengagement, and disengagement can become underachievement.

The strongest argument against New York City’s gifted programs is not that gifted education is harmful in itself. It is that the city’s method of access is flawed. Testing 4-year-olds is a shaky way to identify long-term academic need. Scores at that age can reflect preschool exposure, vocabulary, test familiarity and parental coaching. The racial and economic disparities in enrollment are impossible to ignore. If a public program mostly serves families who already know how to navigate the system, it is not fulfilling its public mission.

But the answer is reform, not abolition. New York should use multiple measures: teacher observations, classroom performance, portfolios, parent input, nonverbal assessments and repeated opportunities for entry in later grades. The city should actively screen all children rather than rely on parents to request testing. It should provide strong gifted options in more districts, especially in underserved neighborhoods, so families are not competing for a handful of prestigious seats. It should also expand enrichment in general education classrooms.

There is no contradiction between gifted classrooms and gifted practices for all students. We need both. Many children benefit from richer assignments and inquiry-based learning. Some children, however, require a pace and level of abstraction that is hard to sustain in a fully mixed classroom all day.

A fair school system does not deny differences among children. It responds to them wisely. New York’s gifted programs should be made broader, more equitable and more flexible — not dismantled in the name of equality.

"New York City should reform and maintain its gifted and talented programs rather than eliminating them, as they are essential for retaining middle-class families and providing high-achieving students with an appropriate education."

Eliminating gifted and talented programs would be a serious mistake, and Chancellor Fariña's musings about "gifted practices for all" amount to wishful thinking dressed up as equity policy. The honest question is not whether NYC's G&T system is perfect — it isn't — but whether the city's public schools can hold onto academically ambitious families and serve high-achieving children of all backgrounds without it. The evidence suggests they cannot.

Start with the practical reality. New York is one of the few large American cities where middle-class and professional families still send their children to public elementary school in significant numbers. Gifted programs are a major reason. Take them away, and many of those families will decamp to private schools, to the suburbs, or to charters that offer rigorous tracking by another name. Local norms could help identify children with unusual promise in every district, not only those already surrounded by enrichment.

At the same time, most district gifted programs should be converted into schoolwide enrichment models. That does not mean every child gets identical work. It means schools use flexible grouping, subject acceleration, project-based learning, arts and science enrichment, and small seminars that children enter and leave as their strengths develop. A student might need advanced math but ordinary writing instruction; another might show unusual verbal ability but not test as globally “gifted.” The current all-or-nothing classroom model is too blunt for real children.

Fariña is right to challenge the mystique of gifted programs, but the city should not respond by pretending advanced learners do not exist. The goal should be fewer permanent labels and more responsive instruction. New York can offer challenging education without building a caste system in kindergarten. It should preserve genuine acceleration while making enrichment the property of the whole school, not the prize for winning an early admissions contest.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Public schools have a responsibility to provide academic acceleration as a necessary accommodation for children whose development is significantly ahead of their age peers.
  2. 02Current gifted programs often function as selective environments that provide basic educational quality—such as safety and ambitious instruction—that should be available to all students regardless of labels.
  3. 03The admissions process for gifted programs should be delayed beyond kindergarten and utilize universal screening and local norms to ensure students are selected based on promise rather than socioeconomic advantage.
  4. 04Replacing rigid gifted classrooms with schoolwide enrichment models allows for more flexible, subject-specific instruction that better addresses the diverse strengths of individual children.
  5. 05The city should move away from permanent labels and early-childhood sorting in favor of a system that balances genuine academic acceleration with broad access to enriched curriculum.
