The hijab is worth fighting over only if the fight is really about women’s freedom, not about turning women’s bodies into billboards for someone else’s politics.

Femen is right that coercion exists. In Iran, Saudi Arabia and in many families and communities elsewhere, girls and women face law, violence or ostracism if they do not cover. That is a serious human-rights issue, and it deserves more than polite relativism. A woman like the Tunisian activist Amina Tyler should not be threatened for refusing modesty codes.

But Femen’s topless protests also repeat the mistake they denounce: they presume that liberation has one visible uniform. Many Muslim women wear hijab for reasons that are religious, cultural, intellectual or simply personal. To treat them as dupes is not feminism; it is a different form of paternalism. France’s bans on conspicuous religious symbols and face veils show how “saving” women can become another way of policing them.

“Muslimah Pride Day” is strongest when it insists that covered women speak for themselves. It is weakest if it denies the pressures placed on dissenters within Muslim communities.

So yes, the issue matters. But the defensible position is neither compulsory veiling nor compulsory unveiling. The fight should be for the woman’s right to decide—and to change her mind without punishment.
