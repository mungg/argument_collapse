"Mother Teresa should be canonized because her life provided a credible witness to the Gospel through her radical commitment to the dignity of the unwanted, though this recognition must not be used to shield her institutions from legitimate medical criticism."

Mother Teresa should be canonized if the claim is the Catholic Church’s own: that her life displayed heroic charity and holiness. She is not being proposed as a model hospital administrator, development economist or pain-management specialist. She founded an order that chose to live among people whom others often would not touch: the dying, abandoned, leprous, orphaned and destitute. In a city where misery could become invisible, she insisted that each person had a face and a soul.

But canonization should not become a whitewash. Serious criticisms deserve more than dismissal as anti-Catholic sniping. Doctors and visitors reported inadequate diagnosis, poor record-keeping, scarce analgesics and a troubling spiritualization of suffering in some homes. Given the donations and global admiration the Missionaries of Charity received, it is fair to ask why standards of medical care were not higher. A saint’s halo should not be used to shield institutions from accountability.

The answer, then, is yes — but with honesty. Catholic sainthood has never meant that a person’s every judgment was sound or every institution she built was beyond reform. It means the church sees in that life a credible witness to the Gospel. Mother Teresa’s witness was the radical claim that the unwanted are not disposable.

If Rome canonizes her, it should also invite her order to meet the highest standards of care for the poor. Reverence for Mother Teresa should deepen, not replace, scrutiny. The best tribute to her would be both love for the abandoned and better medicine for them.

"Mother Teresa's canonization is justified because she meets the Catholic Church's specific criteria for personal sanctity and heroic virtue, regardless of secular critiques of her medical administration or ideology."

Canonization is not a humanitarian award; it is the Catholic Church's declaration that a particular soul is in heaven and worthy of veneration. Once that is understood, much of the secular argument against Mother Teresa's sainthood loses its force. Christopher Hitchens's critique — that her hospices offered poor medical care, that she accepted donations from unsavory figures like Charles Keating, that she preached against contraception and abortion in places ravaged by poverty — is a critique of her ideology and her institutional choices, not of her personal sanctity. The Church has never claimed saints are competent hospital administrators or progressive social reformers.

What canonization requires is heroic virtue and, in most cases, verified miracles. By the ordinary standards the Church uses, Mother Teresa qualifies. She left a comfortable teaching post to live among Calcutta's destitute, built an order that operates in over a hundred countries, and, as her posthumously published letters revealed, persevered through decades of agonizing spiritual darkness without abandoning her vocation. That last detail, far from undermining her case, strengthens it: she continued to serve when she felt nothing in return.

One can reasonably wish she had pushed harder for better medicine or spoken less rigidly about reproductive ethics. But those are arguments with Catholicism itself. Within the Church's own framework — which is the only framework canonization answers to — Francis's decision is defensible and overdue.

On that measure, her legacy—of galvanizing thousands of women to serve the most abandoned—warrants the honor.

Her institutions frequently lacked the medical sophistication available elsewhere, and her public statements on issues like contraception and abortion often aligned with positions many found regressive. These are fair questions about the actual impact of her work.

She gave visibility to forgotten people — the dying on Calcutta's streets, the abandoned children. Whatever limitations existed in her approach, they emerged partly from operating at a scale that demanded compromise.

Sainthood, of course, operates within a theological framework the Vatican considers paramount: intercession and divine favor. From a purely humanist perspective, perhaps the more honest question is whether we should reserve the term "saint" for the uncomplicated, or whether sainthood might more honestly acknowledge the contradictions inherent in any massive human undertaking. Teresa was neither the flawless humanitarian of hagiography nor the cynical operator critics describe. She was something more complicated — and perhaps more worthy of honest engagement than either extreme allows.

She offered dignity, physical touch, and compassion to those who had been entirely abandoned by the state and the modern medical establishment. To judge her by the metrics of a contemporary secular non-profit is to completely miss the point of her religious calling. She provided a place for the destitute to die with a sense of worth, surrounded by people who cared for them as if they were Christ himself.

Furthermore, sainthood in the Catholic Church is not a declaration of flawless administrative practice, but a recognition of heroic virtue, holiness, and devotion to God. Perhaps the most compelling reason for her canonization is the revelation of her deep, agonizing spiritual struggles. The publication of her private letters revealed that she spent decades enduring a "dark night of the soul," feeling entirely abandoned by God and devoid of spiritual consolation. Yet, despite this crippling internal emptiness, she got up and continued her grueling work in the slums every single day.

This profound crisis of faith makes her not a plastic, one-dimensional icon of piety, but a deeply relatable, thoroughly modern saint. Pope Francis's decision to canonize her during the Jubilee Year of Mercy is perfectly fitting. She remains the modern era's most recognizable symbol of selfless love, and her canonization rightly honors a woman who gave everything to those who had nothing.
