"While the Port Huron Statement provides a meaningful historical lineage, its idealistic vision of participatory democracy must be critically updated to address the logistical failures, internal hierarchies, and increased state hostility facing modern movements like Occupy Wall Street."

I read Tom Hayden’s anniversary meditation as a young man who has spent the past eight months in the thick of the Occupy movement, first in Zuccotti Park and now in a dispersed network of working groups that struggle to keep the flame alive. I recognize the lineage he draws, and I feel its weight. But I also feel a growing distance between the way Hayden narrates our lives and the reality we experience on the ground. His essay is a history painted in broad, reverent strokes; what we need now is an honest, unsparing account of what happens when the poetry of participatory democracy meets the prose of logistical exhaustion, internal conflict, and the fierce resistance of a system that is far more resilient than we imagined.

Hayden writes that participatory democracy is embodied in “tactics of direct action and of decision-making aimed at achieving consensus.” I have sat through hundreds of hours of General Assembly meetings, and I can testify that achieving consensus is a process that is at once the movement’s greatest moral achievement and its most paralyzing flaw. The hand signals—twinkling up for agreement, down for disagreement, rolling for a block—are elegant tools for ensuring that no voice is steamrolled. Yet they also empower a single obstinate individual to derail hours of painstaking negotiation. The demand for consensus is rooted in a noble impulse to prefigure a world without coercion, but in practice it often becomes a tyranny of the most privileged, those with the time and the rhetorical training to dominate a process that has no formal mechanisms for closure. The movement is riven by the tension between prefiguration and effectiveness, and every assembly is a microcosm of that dilemma.

Hayden sees the Occupy movement as the direct heir of the Port Huron Statement’s vision. I am less certain. The Statement was the product of a small, tight-knit group of intellectuals who spent months drafting and redrafting a coherent document. They had a program, albeit a broad one. Occupy’s founding document, the “September 17 manifesto” as Hayden calls it, was a list of grievances and aspirations that deliberately avoided making concrete policy demands. That avoidance was strategic; we did not want to be pigeonholed or co-opted. But it also left us adrift. Is participatory democracy a means or an end? If it is a means, we need to be clearer about what we are using it to achieve—a debt jubilee, a transaction tax, a living wage, an end to the foreclosure crisis. If it is an end in itself, then we have set ourselves an impossible task: building a never-ending meeting that must somehow satisfy every participant’s deepest longing for recognition.

Inside the movement, we are having painful conversations that do not fit Hayden’s triumphalist frame. People of color have walked out of General Assemblies to form their own caucuses, arguing that the supposedly “horizontal” space of consensus actually reproduces the white, middle-class cultural norms of the activists who dominate the conversation. The homeless who sought food and shelter in the encampments were often viewed as a “security problem” rather than as co-strugglers, a stark reminder that poverty does not automatically generate solidarity. These fissures are not signs of the movement’s failure; they are the birth pangs of something deeper, and they demand a reckoning with hierarchies of race, class, and gender that the ’60s rhetoric of unity often evades.

Moreover, I worry that the narrative of continuity that Hayden offers is a form of comfort that absolves us of harder thinking. The Port Huron Statement was written at a moment when the American state was genuinely vulnerable to moral suasion in a way it is not today. The Cold War consensus was cracking, and the civil rights movement had forced the federal government into a position of potential alliance. Today, the state is not a reluctant partner; it is a fully weaponized apparatus of surveillance, extraction, and incarceration. The police who cleared Zuccotti Park in November acted with a coordinated precision that drew on post-9/11 counterterrorism tactics. The Department of Homeland Security has issued reports treating our movement as a potential domestic threat. This is not the same terrain as the 1960s. The participatory democracy we practice is not a benign expansion of civic life; it is, from the state’s perspective, a threat to be neutralized.

All of this might sound like despair, but it is not. It is a call for a different kind of humility. The Port Huron Statement matters to me not because it provides a blueprint but because it is a testament to the human capacity to imagine a radically different world in the midst of a dispiriting one. But we must read it critically, not as scripture. We must learn from its silences—its lack of attention to gender, its unexamined assumptions about vanguard leadership, its failure to anticipate the ferocity of the backlash. And we must allow ourselves to outgrow it.

The spirit I see in my fellow Occupiers is not the spirit of 1962 reborn; it is something new, forged in the crucible of a collapsed economy, a degraded environment, and a deep cynicism about electoral politics that the ’60s generation could hardly fathom. We do not have the luxury of Hayden’s optimism about the Internet, because we have seen how Facebook and Twitter atomize us even as they connect us. We do not share his faith that the establishment will eventually concede, because we have seen it stonewall and fracture us time and again. Our participatory democracy is not a timeless universal; it is a desperate, flawed, beautiful experiment in surviving a world that seems determined to grind us down.

So I thank Tom Hayden for his words, but I ask that he and his generation listen to us now, not just to find their own echoes but to hear the dissonance. We are not simply continuing the Port Huron Statement. We are, in our messy assemblies and painful arguments and half-broken tents, trying to write the next one.
