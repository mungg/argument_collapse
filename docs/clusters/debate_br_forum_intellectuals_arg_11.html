"The responsibility of intellectuals must extend beyond the internal politics of the North American university to embrace a global, material solidarity with the colonized and dispossessed in the Global South."

Robin D. G. Kelley’s essay performs an essential retrieval of the Black antifascist tradition, yet its horizon remains circumscribed by the North American university and its internal dramas. This parochialism is not a minor flaw; it fundamentally distorts the question of what intellectuals owe a world engulfed in fascism and genocide. By focusing so intently on the repression of Palestine solidarity on U.S. campuses and the building of counter-institutions within academia, Kelley sidesteps the most urgent dimension of intellectual responsibility: the material, cross-border solidarity required to confront a planetary crisis in which entire knowledge systems are being physically annihilated, not merely defunded or disciplined. The answer to the question “What is the responsibility of intellectuals?” must begin with the recognition that the primary site of fascism and genocide today is not the American university but the Global South, and that the intellectual’s first duty is to join the colonized and the dispossessed where the slaughter is most acute—even if that means abandoning the career protections and discursive comforts of the tenure track entirely.

The destruction of higher education in Gaza—what Oxford’s Karma Nabulsi has aptly termed “scholasticide”—makes the point brutally clear. As of mid-2025, every university in the Gaza Strip has been reduced to rubble. Professors in engineering, medicine, literature, and Islamic jurisprudence have been killed alongside their students. Libraries and archives, the accumulated memory of a people, have been incinerated. This is not an assault on academic freedom; it is cultural genocide, intended to erase the Palestinian capacity to create and transmit knowledge. Against such a reality, what does it mean to speak of the “responsibility of intellectuals” in terms of unionizing graduate workers at UCLA or protecting the Institute on Inequality and Democracy? Those are not irrelevant struggles, but they are, at best, secondary consequences of an imperialist system that the Western left remains structurally incapable of confronting because it remains embedded in the institutions and funding streams of the empire.

Kelley’s framework inadvertently reproduces this myopia. He invokes Aimé Césaire’s *Discourse on Colonialism* to note the continuity between Nazi genocide and colonial violence, but he does not carry the analysis to its conclusion. Césaire’s argument was not that fascism simply returned home to Europe from the colonies; it was that the colonial relation *was always already fascist* for the colonized. The distinction Kelley preserves between “fascist times” in the United States and the longer history of racial terror glosses over the fact that for Black and Indigenous peoples within the U.S. itself, and for the billions living under neocolonial regimes abroad, fascism is not a recent turn but a permanent condition. From this vantage, the current panic over the MAGA administration’s attacks on academia, while real, reflects a privileged shock at the arrival of a brutality that much of the world has never been allowed to forget. For the Congolese intellectual whose university was ransacked by militia backed by multinational mining corporations, for the Rohingya scholar in a refugee camp without electricity or internet, for the Palestinian surgeon trying to teach medical students in a tent hospital under drone surveillance, Chomsky’s “privilege” of access to information and platforms is a distant, almost obscene luxury.

The Black antifascist tradition Kelley celebrates was, in its most radical moments, deeply internationalist. W. E. B. Du Bois did not limit his activism to the NAACP’s legal battles; he spent his final years in Ghana, a Pan-Africanist committed to building a postcolonial intellectual infrastructure on the continent. Claudia Jones, deported from the United States, went on to organize the Caribbean community in Britain and founded the *West Indian Gazette*, linking anti-racist struggles to anticolonial movements across the empire. The Black Panther Party forged alliances with the Vietnamese National Liberation Front, the Palestine Liberation Organization, and the anti-apartheid movement, understanding that their own fascist state was a node in a global system of oppression. For these organic intellectuals, the responsibility was never merely to reform or even to radicalize the American university; it was to help dismantle the entire imperialist architecture, of which the university was merely a subsidiary.

Today’s organic intellectuals must recover that global horizon. The Boycott, Divestment, Sanctions movement that Kelley mentions should be more than a campus petition drive; it should be the basis for a comprehensive severance of institutional ties between Western academia and the Israeli state, including the refusal of all collaborative research, the cancellation of all study-abroad programs at Israeli universities directly implicated in occupation and war, and the public shaming of scholars who continue to lend their legitimacy to genocidal institutions. This would demand more than the mild resolutions academic senates are willing to pass; it would require faculty and graduate students to risk their own careers, to refuse grant money from foundations that invest in the occupation, to walk away from endowed chairs named after Zionist philanthropists. Kelley’s own examples, however, suggest a reluctance to follow through on such ruptures. The campaign to fire or suspend professors who spoke for Palestine is rightly condemned, but the opposite action—the voluntary withdrawal of intellectual labor from institutions complicit in war crimes—is never explicitly advocated.

Such a stance would align the Western intellectual with the true organic intellectuals of the Global South, those who have not been afforded the luxury of theorizing about “contested terrain” within elite universities. Consider the late Refaat Alareer, a professor of English literature at the Islamic University of Gaza, who was killed by an Israeli airstrike in December 2023. Alareer could have sought safety abroad; he had connections and opportunities. He chose to stay, to document the genocide, to teach his students via social media as the bombs fell, and to continue writing poetry that insisted on the humanity of his people. His was not a tactical choice to “cast his lot” with the insurgent class from the safety of a tenure line. It was the existential decision of someone for whom the responsibility of the intellectual could not be separated from the shared fate of his community. What would it mean for tenured radicals in the United States and Europe to emulate even a fraction of that commitment? It would mean, at minimum, joining the physical defense of Palestinian life—not just showing up at encampments but traveling to border crossings to act as human shields, organizing material aid convoys, and participating in the direct-action campaigns that disrupt the arms shipments and financial networks making the genocide possible.

This is not an abstract moral exhortation. The anti-apartheid struggle provides a more proximate model. In the 1980s, thousands of international scholars and students heeded the call to isolate South Africa, ending institutional collaborations, pressuring universities to divest, and in some cases relocating to frontline states to assist the liberation movements directly. That solidarity work was not without risk; many lost jobs, faced state surveillance, and were branded as supporters of terrorism. But it contributed to the diplomatic and material pressure that eventually brought down the apartheid regime. Today’s genocide demands a similar, if not more urgent, response—and yet the Western intellectual left by and large remains consumed with its own minoritization within the academy.

Kelley ends with Tiffany Willoughby-Herard, arrested for standing with her students, asking the essential question: “What job do I have if the students don’t have a future?” The question is correct, but the frame is still too narrow. The future of students at UC Irvine is tied, materially and morally, to the future of students at Al-Azhar University in Gaza, at the University of Khartoum in Sudan, at the University of Cabo Delgado in Mozambique. If those students have no future because their schools have been bombed, their professors assassinated, and their land rendered uninhabitable by extractive capitalism and imperial war, then the job of a UC Irvine professor is largely irrelevant. The real responsibility is to ensure that those students *do* have a future—which means, in the short term, providing emergency funds for displaced scholars, campaigning for humanitarian visas, and documenting war crimes for international tribunals; and in the long term, working with movements in the Global South to dismantle the political and economic structures that produce scholasticide in the first place.

The university, in this view, is not merely contested terrain; it is, at its core, an instrument of the empire that must be broken apart and reassembled on entirely different principles. The “new university” Kelley imagines cannot be built from the inside out; it requires a rupture as profound as the one that destroyed the medieval monastery-university and gave birth to the modern research institution. That rupture will not come from faculty unions or radical institutes, valuable as they are. It will come only when a critical mass of intellectuals decides that their loyalty is not to the institution that pays them but to the global movement against fascism and genocide—a movement whose center of gravity lies in Gaza, the Sahel, Amazonia, and the other frontlines of imperialist predation.

Ultimately, the Black antifascist tradition Kelley so ably reconstructs points beyond itself. It reminds us that the struggle has never been confined to one nation’s borders, and that the most powerful intellectual interventions have been those that broke the chains of parochialism and imagined an international community of resistance. Du Bois’s *Black Reconstruction* was addressed to the world; C. L. R. James’s *The Black Jacobins* made the Haitian Revolution a universal touchstone; Aimé Césaire’s *Discourse on Colonialism* spoke to all colonized peoples. In our age, the responsibility of the intellectual is to write, speak, and act for a global public, to treat the destruction of a library in Gaza as a personal attack on one’s own ability to think, and to recognize that the only job worth having is the one that contributes, concretely and at whatever cost, to the survival and liberation of those whose humanity is being systematically erased. That is the answer Willoughby-Herard’s question demands, and it is the one Kelley’s essay, for all its eloquence, ultimately evades.
