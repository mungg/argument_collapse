"While James Heckman correctly identifies the importance of early childhood, his policy framework relies on a moralistic and deficit-based view of poor families that risks becoming an intrusive form of social regulation rather than a system of genuine support."

James Heckman’s essay makes a vital contribution by shifting the policy debate from narrow school reforms to the critical early years. But his framing of the problem—and particularly his views on family structure and parenting—is laced with a moralism that threatens to undermine the inclusive, supportive policies we actually need. By elevating “non-cognitive skills” and targeting “parenting quality,” Heckman’s agenda risks becoming a new language for old-fashioned moral regulation of poor and nonwhite families, especially mothers. A truly progressive approach would focus on providing universal, high-quality childcare and income support as rights, not as instruments for producing better-behaved workers.

Heckman’s narrative of diverging destinies is built around the decline of marriage and the rise of single motherhood, especially among the less educated. He presents figures on never-married mothers and contrasts their child-rearing practices unfavorably with those of married, college-educated women. The implication is clear: two-parent, married families are the gold standard, and the growth of alternative family forms is a key driver of disadvantage. This is not just descriptive; it is a moral judgment. What Heckman does not say is that the rise in single parenthood is inextricably linked to the very economic forces he deplores—the collapse of decent jobs for non-college men, mass incarceration of black men that makes them unmarriageable, and a threadbare safety net that forces women to shoulder caregiving alone. Blaming family structure for inequality without addressing these causes is a form of victim-blaming.

The emphasis on “parenting quality” as the scarce resource is especially problematic. Heckman cites the Hart and Risley word-gap study, which has been thoroughly critiqued for its small, unrepresentative sample and for promoting a deficit model of low-income and minority parenting. Research in linguistic anthropology shows that working-class and poor parents communicate with their children in rich, complex ways that are simply different from the professional-class model, not inferior. By framing the issue as a language deficit, Heckman provides a scientific veneer for intervention programs that can easily become intrusive and condescending. Home visitors who aim to teach poor mothers how to parent properly may inadvertently undermine maternal confidence and ignore the adaptive strategies those mothers have developed under conditions of extreme stress.

Moreover, the entire concept of “non-cognitive skills” deserves scrutiny. Qualities like perseverance, attentiveness, and self-regulation are presented as neutral, universally desirable traits. But as sociologists have argued, these are often proxies for compliance with middle-class work norms and a docile acceptance of authority. A teenager’s “motivation” may be high if she sees a reasonable chance of success, but telling an adolescent in a crime-ridden neighborhood to persist and self-regulate without changing the structural opportunities is little more than a demand for conformity. Heckman’s frame can easily slide into blaming the poor for their supposed characterological flaws, rather than recognizing that the same person might display stellar non-cognitive skills—creativity, persistence, social intelligence—in informal contexts that are not captured by standard measures.

What should we do instead? The most effective way to improve early childhood environments is to give all families, regardless of marital status or employment, the resources and time to care for their children. That means universal, publicly funded childcare from infancy onward, staffed by well-paid professional workers, not just targeted home-visiting programs aimed at the poor. It means a generous child allowance—a universal basic income for children—that reduces material deprivation and gives parents, particularly single mothers, more choice and less stress. The evidence on the Earned Income Tax Credit shows that such income transfers improve child health and school performance. Heckman dismisses redistribution as ineffective, but his own casino example suggests that when parents have more money, they often become better parents.

Heckman says he wants to avoid the stigmatization of targeted programs, so he proposes sliding fees for universal services. That is a step in the right direction, but it does not go far enough. The point is not to test the deservingness of parents or to shape their values through culturally sensitive programs; the point is to create a society where all parents have the tools to thrive. That means decent pay, reasonable work hours, paid family leave, and a robust public infrastructure of care. Early childhood intervention, when framed as a collective responsibility rather than a rescue mission for the culturally deficient, can unite diverse constituencies.

Ultimately, Heckman’s vision, for all its insights, remains trapped in a technocratic logic that sees social problems as individual deficits to be corrected. A truly humane policy would honor the ways that poor and working-class families already nurture their children, while ensuring they have the economic security and social supports that wealthy families take for granted. Social mobility is not about teaching children to persist; it is about tearing down the barriers that make persistence so often futile.
