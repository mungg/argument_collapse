Diversified Claude

"The discontinuation of the print edition of the Encyclopaedia Britannica represents a loss of the 'uncurated adjacency' that teaches intellectual humility by confronting readers with knowledge they did not know they lacked."

I want to defend a small, specific thing: the physical book as a teacher of humility.

Open a volume of the Britannica at random. You will find yourself confronted with subjects you did not know existed: the Hanseatic League, hagfish, Hilbert spaces, the Hittites. You did not search for them. You did not click them. They are simply there, on the page facing the one you wanted, indifferent to your interests. The encyclopedia, by its very form, told you that the world is larger than your curiosity.

The internet, for all its glories, does not do this well. It is built to give you what you ask for. Search engines reward specificity; recommendation algorithms learn your habits and reinforce them; even Wikipedia's "random article" function, which I love, is something you have to actively choose. The default experience online is one in which your existing interests are mirrored back to you with increasing precision. This is useful, but it is not humbling. It does not enlarge the sense of how much you do not know.

This is the loss I would name most precisely. Not the smell of paper, not the heft of the binding, not the aesthetics of a matched set — though those are real — but the encounter with uncurated adjacency. The Britannica was a stranger in your house, with its own opinions about what mattered. The internet, increasingly, is a mirror.

Can this be reproduced online? Partly. A well-designed digital encyclopedia could resist personalization, surface unexpected entries, and preserve the experience of being confronted with the unfamiliar. The new Britannica online could be precisely that, and one hopes it will be. But the economic logic of the web pulls hard in the opposite direction: engagement, clicks, returning users. Serendipity does not optimize for any of these.

I am not arguing that Britannica should have kept printing. The economics are impossible, and the trees are better used elsewhere. I am arguing that, as we celebrate the convenience of having all knowledge at our fingertips, we should be aware of what kind of knowledge that turns out to be. It is the knowledge we already wanted. The knowledge we did not know to want — the entry on the Hittites we never would have searched for, but read because it happened to be on the next page — is harder to come by now. That is worth more than 129 pounds of paper. Whether we figure out how to preserve it online is a question the next generation will have to answer.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The physical format of an encyclopedia forces readers to encounter unfamiliar subjects that exist independently of their specific interests.
  2. 02Digital platforms and search engines are designed to mirror and reinforce a user's existing interests rather than expanding their horizons through serendipity.
  3. 03The economic incentives of the internet, such as engagement and clicks, actively discourage the kind of unoptimized discovery that print volumes provided.
  4. 04While digital encyclopedias could theoretically preserve the experience of the unfamiliar, the current logic of the web makes such a result unlikely.
