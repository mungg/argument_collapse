Diversified Claude

"Public pension crises should be resolved by honoring legal and moral obligations to retirees through revenue increases and political accountability rather than cutting the benefits of workers who fulfilled their side of the employment contract."

Let's be clear about who is being asked to sacrifice in Detroit and Illinois: it is the retired bus driver, the widowed spouse of a sanitation worker, the 72-year-old former clerk living on $19,000 a year. These people performed their side of the bargain. They showed up to work for decades, accepted lower wages than the private sector in exchange for the promise of a secure retirement, and contributed their own money to the pension fund out of every paycheck. Now they are being told the deal is off.

The framing of this crisis as a math problem—"unfunded liabilities," "discount rates," "actuarial assumptions"—is itself a political choice. It treats pensions as a discretionary line item rather than a contractual and, in many states, constitutional obligation. Michigan's constitution explicitly states that public pensions "shall not be diminished or impaired." Illinois' language is even stronger. The fact that a federal bankruptcy judge can override a state constitution should alarm anyone who cares about the rule of law, regardless of how they feel about public unions.

Were bad investments part of the problem? Of course. But the larger story is that state and city governments simply did not make their required contributions. New Jersey under Christie Whitman, Christie himself, Illinois under governors of both parties, California municipalities during the dot-com boom—the pattern is the same. Politicians took pension holidays to fund tax cuts or to avoid difficult budget choices. When the bill came due, they pointed at the workers.

And yes, Wall Street is a real villain here, even if not the only one. The interest rate swaps Detroit entered into with UBS and Bank of America were exactly the kind of toxic products sold to public entities across the country. When rates collapsed after the Fed's emergency intervention—an intervention designed to save the banks—those same banks collected enormous termination payments from cities that were already on their knees. The asymmetry is grotesque: banks got bailed out, cities got bankrupted, and now pensioners are asked to absorb the loss.

The solution must start from a different premise. Raise revenue. Tax financial transactions. Renegotiate the swap terminations the way GM's bondholders were renegotiated. Require full actuarial funding by law, with penalties for politicians who skip payments. And stop pretending that a 75-year-old retiree has the same capacity to absorb a benefit cut as a 35-year-old banker has to absorb a smaller bonus. This is a question of justice, not arithmetic.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Pensions represent a contractual and constitutional obligation to workers who accepted lower wages in exchange for retirement security.
  2. 02The primary cause of pension shortfalls is not worker benefits, but the failure of politicians to make required contributions in order to fund tax cuts or other budget priorities.
  3. 03Financial institutions bear significant responsibility for the crisis due to the sale of toxic interest rate products that drained municipal resources while the banks themselves received federal bailouts.
  4. 04The legal precedent of federal bankruptcy judges overriding state constitutional protections for pensions undermines the rule of law.
  5. 05Solutions to pension funding should prioritize taxing financial transactions and enforcing mandatory funding laws rather than placing the burden of loss on vulnerable retirees.
Diversified Claude

"The public pension crisis is primarily a result of political choices and revenue failures rather than economic necessity, and it should be solved through honest funding and taxation rather than cutting retiree benefits."

There is something deeply wrong with a society in which a federal judge can announce, almost casually, that a 68-year-old retiree's pension is "just another unsecured claim" while the same legal system treated AIG's counterparties as untouchable in 2008. The Detroit ruling is not just a legal event; it is a statement about whose promises matter and whose don't.

I want to push back on the increasingly fashionable view that public pensions are simply unaffordable. The aggregate unfunded liability of U.S. state and local pensions is large—somewhere between $1 trillion and $4 trillion depending on whose discount rate you use—but the United States is a $16 trillion economy. We are not Greece. We are a country that chooses, repeatedly, to make these obligations seem unmanageable by refusing to raise the revenue to meet them.

Consider Illinois. The state has among the lowest flat income tax rates in the industrialized world and exempts retirement income entirely. It refuses to tax services. Its corporate tax structure is riddled with loopholes. The "pension crisis" in Illinois is, to a substantial degree, a revenue crisis dressed up in actuarial clothing. The new law trims benefits—including the cherished 3% compounded COLA that retirees were specifically promised—rather than asking the state's wealthy to pay more. That is a political choice, not an economic necessity.

The "Wall Street looting" frame is partly correct and partly misleading. Pension funds did pay enormous fees to private equity, hedge funds, and consultants who steered them into illiquid, opaque investments that often underperformed. Pay-to-play scandals from New York to New Mexico to California revealed a culture of corruption around public pension investment. And the financial crisis itself—a crisis made on Wall Street—knocked roughly a third off many funds' assets just as boomer retirements accelerated.

But the deeper looting was political. Every governor who skipped a contribution effectively borrowed from retirees at zero interest. Every legislator who voted for benefit sweeteners without funding them did the same.

The solution requires honesty in three directions. Honest accounting, with discount rates that reflect the riskless nature of the obligation. Honest funding, with required contributions that cannot be politically deferred. And honest taxation, with revenue structures that can actually pay for what governments have promised. Bankruptcy and benefit cuts are not solutions. They are confessions of failure—and the cost of those failures should not fall first on the people least able to bear them.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The legal system unfairly prioritizes financial institutions over the contractual promises made to public retirees.
  2. 02The perceived unmanageability of pension liabilities is a result of a deliberate refusal to raise sufficient tax revenue rather than an actual lack of national wealth.
  3. 03State pension crises, such as the one in Illinois, are often revenue crises caused by low tax rates and corporate loopholes rather than unaffordable benefits.
  4. 04Wall Street contributed to pension instability through high fees, underperforming opaque investments, and the 2008 financial crisis.
  5. 05Politicians effectively looted pension funds by skipping required contributions and approving unfunded benefit increases for political gain.
  6. 06A sustainable solution requires mandatory contributions, realistic accounting practices, and tax structures capable of meeting long-term obligations.
Diversified DeepSeek

"The current public pension crisis is primarily the result of systematic underfunding by politicians rather than investment losses, and the solution lies in enforcing existing funding obligations rather than cutting retiree benefits."

If there is looting going on, it’s politicians doing it through systematic underfunding, not Wall Street. The Detroit and Illinois stories have prompted a convenient amnesia about two decades of states and cities skipping their required pension contributions to balance budgets, fund tax cuts, or launch pet projects. Investment losses certainly hurt, but they are a fraction of the total shortfall compared to the cumulative effect of employer contributions that were never made. Even after the market recovery of 2013, the biggest liability on pension ledgers is the money governments owed but didn’t pay. Forcing retirees to accept cuts now is a breach of a basic compact: these workers traded lower salaries for deferred compensation, often forgoing Social Security. The solution is not to rewrite contracts retroactively but to enforce existing funding obligations through stronger legal mandates, binding arbitration, and, if necessary, federal oversight that bars states from reneging on promises they made when the political winds were favorable.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01State and local governments have spent decades skipping required pension contributions to fund other budgetary priorities like tax cuts and pet projects.
  2. 02The cumulative effect of unpaid employer contributions represents a significantly larger portion of the pension shortfall than losses from bad investments.
  3. 03Cutting retiree benefits constitutes a breach of contract because public workers accepted lower salaries in exchange for the promise of deferred compensation.
  4. 04Stronger legal mandates and federal oversight are necessary to prevent governments from retroactively rewriting contracts and reneging on their financial promises.
