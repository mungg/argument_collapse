"While Joseph Carens provides a strong secular case for amnesty based on the passage of time, a Christian theological framework offers a more profound justification for amnesty rooted in the inherent dignity of the person and the divine mandate of hospitality."

As a pastor in a border-state community, I read Joseph Carens’s essay with deep gratitude and some frustration. Gratitude, because he has put into secular philosophical language what many of us in the churches have long believed on the basis of biblical teaching: that the stranger among us, who works and prays and raises children alongside us, becomes part of our community, and that to tear him away is an offense against God’s justice. But frustration, too, because Carens’s argument, for all its moral clarity, remains trapped in a legalistic framework that cannot capture the full spiritual reality of membership. The Christian tradition offers a richer foundation for amnesty—one rooted in the concept of the sojourner and the call to hospitality—and it presses us toward obligations that go beyond what any statute of limitations can express.

Scripture is unmistakably clear about the treatment of the stranger. The Hebrew Bible commands, again and again, that the sojourner dwelling among the people is not to be oppressed, “for you were strangers in the land of Egypt” (Exodus 22:21). The status of the ger—the resident alien—carried with it specific protections and a share in the community’s worship and social life. In the New Testament, Jesus identifies with the stranger, saying that whatever we do for “the least of these,” we do for him (Matthew 25:40). The parable of the Good Samaritan explodes ethnic boundaries. The early church was a community of Jews and Gentiles, citizens and non-citizens, who were all “no longer strangers and aliens, but citizens with the saints and also members of the household of God” (Ephesians 2:19). From a Christian standpoint, there is no such thing as an “illegal” person made in God’s image. The legal category is a human invention; the divine category is beloved child.

Carens’s emphasis on time as the creator of social membership echoes a deep biblical truth. The land itself belonged to God, and the people were tenants. Over generations, the sojourner who settled and worked the land became woven into the fabric of the covenant community. The book of Ruth is particularly instructive: Ruth, a Moabite, an undocumented migrant from a despised nation, clings to her mother-in-law Naomi and finds work gleaning in the fields. She marries Boaz, a citizen, and becomes the great-grandmother of King David. Her membership is not granted by a legal procedure; it is forged through loyalty, labor, and love. The law eventually recognizes what has already happened—Boaz’s marriage to Ruth is celebrated, and the child is blessed. No one deports Ruth back to Moab after her husband dies. The community enfolds her.

This biblical vision challenges Carens’s concession that the state has a right to deport irregular migrants in the early years. From a faith perspective, the state’s prerogatives are never absolute; they are always subordinate to God’s commandment to love the neighbor. When we enforce immigration laws that tear families apart, that consign children to foster care, that send workers back to poverty and danger, we are not exercising legitimate authority; we are violating the most basic command to protect the vulnerable. The Catholic bishops in the United States have long called for immigration reform that prioritizes family unity and human dignity, and they have supported legalization for the undocumented. Pope John Paul II and Pope Benedict XVI have repeatedly emphasized that the right to migrate is a fundamental human right, though it must be balanced with the common good. This is not a position of naive open borders, but a recognition that the person comes before the state, and that the state exists to serve the person, not the other way around.

I also believe Carens’s secular framing underplays the power of conversion—of metanoia—that can happen within communities. When a congregation includes undocumented families, we see them not as lawbreakers but as brothers and sisters. We baptize their children, we share meals, we visit them in detention. In that shared life, the legal category dissolves. The state may call Miguel Sanchez “illegal,” but we call him “neighbor.” And when we are formed by that encounter, we are changed: our own sense of who belongs expands. An amnesty policy that institutionalizes a waiting period of five to seven years imposes a kind of probation on relationships that are already sacred. It says to the family that has worshipped with us for three years: “Not yet. You are still not fully part of us.” That is a false and harmful message.

I do not wish to dismiss the need for orderly processes or the legitimate role of government. But as Christians, we must insist that the law serve mercy, not the other way around. Carens gives us good secular reasons to grant amnesty to long-settled irregular migrants. I want to add the theological reasons, which are even more urgent: every person, regardless of legal status, bears the image of God and is entitled to a place in the human community. Let us, then, push beyond Carens’s qualified amnesty to a more radical welcome. Let us tear down the walls of separation and build a society where no one is a stranger.
