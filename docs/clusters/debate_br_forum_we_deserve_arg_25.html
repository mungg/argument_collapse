"The vision of repurposing generative AI for 'ecological reason' is fundamentally flawed because it ignores the technology's catastrophic material and environmental costs."

Evgeny Morozov’s call for ecological reason is tantalizing, but the essay reads differently when one does the carbon math. His language app, built in a single afternoon, relies on three AI-powered services that consume staggering amounts of energy. Even as he tweaks vocabulary for “Vergesslichkeit,” the servers churning through his prompts are siphoning electricity, much of it from fossil fuels, and generating emissions that linger in the atmosphere for centuries. By his own admission, one of those services charges $99 per month, a price that does not remotely cover the true environmental costs. The stonefield where he finds his eolith, in other words, is on fire, and the fire is stoked by the very technology he hopes to redeem.

Morozov is not blind to the climate crisis. He notes the “nontrivial amount of carbon emissions” produced even by trivial queries, and he fleetingly suggests that we might “reduce the energy cost of re-running these services for each user.” Yet these acknowledgments sit awkwardly alongside the essay’s utopian optimism. The central message is that generative AI, despite its origins in the Efficiency Lobby, can be repurposed for open-ended, playful, ecological reason—if only we can wrest it from corporate control and embed it in public, solidarity-based infrastructures. But this vision ignores a more fundamental question: whether the material substrate of AI is compatible with any genuinely ecological future, no matter who owns the servers.

The numbers are sobering. Training a single large language model can emit as much carbon as five cars over their entire lifetimes. Each inference—each query, each short story generated—requires significant computation. By 2024, data centers were already consuming around one percent of global electricity, a share projected to rise sharply as AI adoption accelerates. Even if all those data centers ran on renewable energy—and most do not—the sheer scale of the infrastructure demands would still entail massive extraction of rare minerals, water consumption for cooling, and land use conflicts. The embodied energy and ecological footprint of the hardware itself are often ignored, yet they are enormous.

Morozov’s ecological reason is so far largely metaphorical: it describes a mode of thought attuned to context, serendipity, and the open-ended play of means and ends. But there is a literal ecology that deserves equal billing. The eolithic flâneur wanders a stonefield unspoiled by mining; the junkman repurposes discarded objects from a consumer society. These figures are pre-industrial or post-industrial in their material relations. The AI systems that Morozov celebrates, by contrast, are among the most resource-intensive artifacts ever built. To propose that they be scaled up into public infrastructure is to accept an industrial logic that the planet cannot sustain. It is to imagine that we can have our stonefields and burn them too.

This is not a Luddite rejection of all technology. But it is a call for a more rigorous materialist analysis. The Latin American experiments Morozov admires—Cybersyn, the Bariloche Foundation’s modeling—were run on the mainframes and minicomputers of the 1970s, which had a fraction of the computing power of a modern smartphone. Their energy demands were modest by today’s standards, and they were embedded in political projects that explicitly questioned the North’s model of resource-intensive development. To invoke those examples while championing large language models is to miss the crucial difference in scale and material impact.

A truly ecological reason would begin by asking what kinds of problems we need computation to solve, and at what cost. The answer might lead us away from generative AI and toward simpler, more convivial tools—the “low-tech” movement inspired by Ivan Illich and others. It might prioritize information systems that run on edge devices, that minimize data transfer, and that can be powered by local renewable microgrids. It would certainly require us to abandon the fantasy that every language learner needs sixty personalized stories generated on demand, when a well-stocked public library and a community language exchange might serve just as well, with far less damage.

Morozov’s closing invocation of John Dewey and the pragmatists is apt, but it demands a more consistent application. Dewey argued that intelligence is embodied in the ongoing transaction between organism and environment. Today, that environment is in crisis, and the transaction is lethal. If ecological reason is to mean anything, it must align intelligence with the imperative of planetary survival. That means subjecting all technologies, including AI, to a stringent test: do they, on balance, help us reduce our collective footprint and live within the Earth’s limits? By that measure, most generative AI fails catastrophically.

The reorientation Morozov seeks cannot be achieved by changing the governance of AI alone. It requires a degrowth agenda that shrinks the material throughput of the digital economy. Such a project is politically daunting, but it is the inescapable condition for any future we would want to inhabit. The AI we deserve, in the end, may be the one we dare to use less of.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The staggering energy consumption, carbon emissions, and resource extraction required by AI infrastructure are incompatible with a genuinely ecological future.
  2. 02Morozov’s use of 'ecological reason' is merely metaphorical and fails to account for the literal, physical ecology being destroyed by data centers.
  3. 03Invoking 1970s Latin American computing experiments is misleading because those systems operated at a fraction of the scale and material impact of modern large language models.
  4. 04A truly ecological approach to technology would prioritize 'low-tech,' convivial tools and community-based solutions over resource-intensive generative AI.
  5. 05Democratic control or public ownership of AI cannot solve the underlying problem that the technology's industrial logic is unsustainable for the planet.
  6. 06True intelligence in the age of climate crisis requires a degrowth agenda that subjects AI to the imperative of planetary survival, which likely means using the technology less.
