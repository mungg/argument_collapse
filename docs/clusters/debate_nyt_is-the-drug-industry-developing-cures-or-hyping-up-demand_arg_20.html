"Reducing the drug industry's influence requires restructuring the health care system's economic incentives so that profit is tied to genuine clinical value and health outcomes rather than the expansion of chronic drug markets."

The phrase “developing cures or hyping demand” sounds like a moral question, but it is also an economic one. Drug companies are not charities. They are firms built to find patentable products, win regulatory approval and sell as much as the law allows. Sometimes that produces genuine medical progress. Sometimes it produces a search for new customers.

A.D.H.D. shows how the incentives work. The basic stimulant medications are old. What the modern market has added are extended-release formulations, patches, prodrugs and nonstimulants, many of which may offer real conveniences: fewer doses at school, smoother coverage through the day, less stigma for a child visiting the nurse. But these are treatments, not cures, and often they are refinements rather than breakthroughs. The financial prize is not a one-time cure. It is a large population taking a daily medicine for years.

That does not make the medicines bad. It does mean we should expect companies to emphasize the broadest plausible definition of the disorder, the longest plausible duration of therapy and the newest branded product over an older generic. They would be acting irrationally, from a shareholder’s standpoint, if they did otherwise.

Reducing industry influence therefore requires changing the rules under which influence pays. Insurers and public programs should require evidence of meaningful advantage before paying premium prices for new branded A.D.H.D. drugs when older generics work. Comparative-effectiveness trials should be funded independently, not left to companies that have little reason to ask whether their product is only marginally better than a cheap alternative. Formularies should be transparent and based on clinical value, not rebates hidden from patients.

We also need to separate innovation rewards from marketing volume. For areas where society truly wants cures—antibiotics, vaccines, disease-modifying treatments, or interventions that reduce long-term disability—public prizes, targeted tax credits, or longer exclusivity tied to major clinical advances may make more sense than rewarding whichever company can most successfully expand a chronic market. Patent law rewards novelty; health policy should reward health.

On the prescribing side, payment should favor careful diagnosis. A pediatrician who spends an hour gathering school reports, screening for sleep and learning problems, and counseling parents should not be paid less than the system spends on a month of branded pills. If we underpay judgment and overpay products, we should not be surprised when products dominate.

Industry will always have a role in drug development. No public system is ready to replace all private research, manufacturing and distribution. But society does not have to let companies dominate the definition of illness, the education of doctors and the expectations of patients. The way to reduce their influence is not to scold them for pursuing profit. It is to stop designing a health system in which the most profitable path is to make ordinary distress look like a lifelong drug indication.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current pharmaceutical business model prioritizes long-term daily medication use and product refinements over one-time cures or medical breakthroughs.
  2. 02Insurers and public programs should demand evidence of meaningful clinical advantage before paying premium prices for new branded drugs over cheaper generics.
  3. 03Comparative-effectiveness trials and clinical formularies should be managed independently and transparently to prevent industry-driven bias.
  4. 04Innovation rewards, such as patents and tax credits, should be decoupled from marketing volume and instead tied to major clinical advances and public health needs.
  5. 05Medical payment structures should prioritize and fairly compensate careful diagnostic judgment over the volume of products prescribed.
  6. 06The health system must be redesigned to prevent companies from dominating the definition of illness and the education of medical professionals.
