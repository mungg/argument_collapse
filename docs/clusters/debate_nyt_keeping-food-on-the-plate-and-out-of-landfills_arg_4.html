Diversified Claude

"The United States should prioritize the federal standardization and clarification of food date labels to significantly reduce household food waste."

Fix the Date Labels First

Before we build elaborate composting infrastructure or pass sweeping new regulations, the United States should do something embarrassingly simple: standardize and clarify food date labels. The Natural Resources Defense Council and Harvard Law School's Food Law and Policy Clinic have estimated that confusion over "sell by," "use by," "best by," and "expires on" labels causes American households to throw away enormous quantities of perfectly edible food. A 2016 survey suggested more than 80 percent of consumers discard food at least occasionally based on a date label they don't actually understand.

The labels themselves are essentially unregulated at the federal level. With the exception of infant formula, manufacturers set their own dates using their own methodologies, and the dates generally refer to peak quality, not safety. Yogurt does not become poisonous on its "sell by" date. Eggs are usually fine for weeks afterward. Yet a parent looking at a carton in the fridge has no easy way to know that.

The fix is straightforward. Congress should pass legislation — Representative Chellie Pingree and Senator Richard Blumenthal have already introduced versions — that requires two clear phrases nationwide: a quality indicator like "best if used by" for most products, and a true safety date like "use by" only for the small category of foods where spoilage actually poses a risk. Pair that with a public education campaign and a uniform format, and we could likely reduce household waste by double-digit percentages without spending much money at all.

None of this solves the harder problems. Restaurants will still over-portion. Supermarkets will still cull misshapen produce. Farms will still leave crops unharvested when prices don't justify labor. Those problems deserve their own interventions — expanded tax deductions for donations under the Bill Emerson Good Samaritan Act, investment in regional gleaning networks, and contracts that don't penalize "ugly" carrots. But labels are the lowest-hanging fruit in a landscape where most reforms involve coordinating across millions of actors. They are within direct federal control, they cost almost nothing, and they could be implemented within a single legislative session.

If 40 percent of our food is being thrown away, we should start by making sure consumers aren't tossing edible food simply because they misread a number printed by a marketing department. The smallest reforms sometimes deliver the biggest returns.

Diversified Gemini

"To significantly reduce food waste in the United States, the federal government must implement a standardized, uniform system for food date labeling."

To make a meaningful dent in the staggering amount of food wasted in the United States, we must tackle the primary driver of household food waste: widespread confusion over expiration dates. The fact that up to 40 percent of our food supply ends up in landfills is not merely a moral failure; it is a regulatory one.

Currently, the date labels on our groceries—“sell by,” “use by,” “best by,” and “enjoy by”—are essentially unregulated at the federal level, with the sole exception of infant formula. These dates are generally determined by the manufacturers themselves to indicate peak freshness, not food safety. Yet, a vast majority of American consumers misinterpret these dates as strict safety deadlines. A groundbreaking 2013 study by the Harvard Food Law and Policy Clinic and the Natural Resources Defense Council revealed that nine out of ten Americans needlessly throw away perfectly safe food simply because the date on the package has passed.

This arbitrary, patchwork system of state laws and manufacturer preferences terrifies consumers into wasting billions of dollars of edible food every year. The most immediate and effective solution is federal legislation to standardize date labeling.

Congress and the Food and Drug Administration should mandate a clear, uniform, two-tier labeling system. One phrase, such as "Best if Used By," should be used exclusively to indicate quality and freshness. A distinctly different phrase, such as "Expires On," should be strictly reserved for the very few highly perishable foods where a post-date consumption actually poses a health risk.

By eliminating the chaotic guesswork from our refrigerators, we can instantly empower consumers to make rational decisions rather than fear-based ones. Retailers, too, would benefit, as clear guidelines would allow them to safely donate near-date items to food banks and shelters without fear of violating opaque local ordinances. Solving the food waste crisis requires systemic changes across the entire supply chain, but the lowest-hanging fruit is simply speaking clearly to the consumer. Standardized labeling is a simple, low-cost legislative fix that could save millions of tons of food from the landfill.

  3. 03Standardized labeling would enable retailers to safely donate near-date food to charities by removing the legal ambiguity caused by inconsistent local ordinances.
  4. 04Federal legislation on date labeling represents a simple, low-cost solution that addresses the primary driver of household food waste.
