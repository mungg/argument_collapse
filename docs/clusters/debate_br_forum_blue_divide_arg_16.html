"The Democratic Party’s economic agenda is morally and practically incomplete because it avoids confronting the "opportunity hoarding" and exclusionary local policies practiced by its affluent suburban base."

The most important passage in Hacker and Pierson’s essay is not their discussion of the child tax credit, the Inflation Reduction Act, or the density tax. It is their acknowledgment that affluent Democratic voters are often fiercely protective of “opportunity hoarding” in metropolitan America. That is where the new blue divide becomes more than a puzzle of party strategy. It becomes a moral contradiction.

The Democratic Party now depends heavily on the prosperous regions that have won the knowledge economy: coastal metros, university towns, tech corridors, medical hubs, finance centers, and high-income suburbs. These places are engines of innovation and growth. They are also engines of exclusion. Their housing markets ration opportunity through price. Their school districts ration opportunity through residence. Their zoning codes ration opportunity through scarcity. Their local governments ration opportunity through process. If Democrats do not confront that structure, their economic agenda will remain incomplete no matter how much money they spend at the federal level.

Hacker and Pierson are right that national Democrats have found ways to pursue redistribution without directly threatening affluent suburbanites. That is exactly the problem. The party has learned how to tax billionaires rhetorically, subsidize clean energy, expand health credits, and propose family benefits while leaving the local property regime of blue America mostly intact. This makes coalition management easier. It also leaves untouched one of the central causes of inequality in the places Democrats govern.

Housing is the clearest case. In many high-opportunity metro areas, restrictive zoning limits apartments, duplexes, accessory dwelling units, and smaller homes. Environmental review and neighborhood process are used to delay or block construction. Minimum lot sizes, parking requirements, height limits, historic preservation claims, and discretionary approvals preserve low-density affluence. The result is not merely high rent. It is exclusion from jobs, schools, transit, parks, safety, and social networks. A family shut out of a wealthy suburb is not just denied a house; it is denied the public goods attached to that house.

This exclusion falls hardest on lower-income households and people of color, but it also harms young adults, renters, service workers, teachers, nurses, home health aides, firefighters, and even many middle-class families. It forces long commutes, increases emissions, intensifies segregation, and transfers wealth to incumbent homeowners through scarcity. It turns metro prosperity into a gated good.

The Democratic Party cannot credibly claim to be a party of opportunity while tolerating this system in its own strongholds. It is not enough to fund affordable housing if local governments can prevent housing from being built. It is not enough to denounce inequality nationally while local Democratic officials defer to homeowners who treat every apartment building as an invasion. It is not enough to celebrate diversity while maintaining school attendance zones and municipal boundaries that keep poor children out.

The authors suggest that the local character of these policies allows national Democrats to keep them off the core agenda. That is true descriptively. But it should be treated as a failure, not merely a strategic fact. Localism has long been one of the great alibis of American inequality. “Local control” sounds democratic, but in fragmented metro areas it often means control by those who already possess property, time, lawyers, and political access. It allows affluent communities to make exclusion look like procedure.

There is a danger here for Democrats that goes beyond policy hypocrisy. Younger voters increasingly experience blue metros not as lands of opportunity but as machines for extracting rent. They may share the social liberalism of affluent Democrats, but they do not share their home equity. They see climate banners next to bans on dense housing. They see equity statements next to school district exclusion. They see transit promises undermined by homeowners who oppose apartments near stations. For them, the Brahmin left is not an abstract Piketty category; it is the landlord, zoning board, or neighborhood association that prices them out while voting blue.

This tension also weakens Democrats’ claim to care about working-class people. A care economy agenda, for example, depends on workers who can live near the families they serve. But in many Democratic metros, child care workers, nursing aides, janitors, restaurant workers, delivery drivers, and home health aides cannot afford to live near their jobs. Public subsidies for care will help, but if housing scarcity absorbs wage gains, inequality persists. Similarly, infrastructure and climate investments will be less effective if workers cannot live where projects are built or if clean-energy deployment is slowed by permitting vetoes.

The opportunity-hoarding problem also complicates the authors’ optimism about shared metropolitan interests. Yes, affluent and lower-income metro residents are economically interconnected. But interconnection does not automatically produce solidarity. It can produce dependency without equality. Wealthy households need service workers, teachers, nurses, transit operators, and delivery drivers, but they may prefer those workers to commute from somewhere else. They value diversity in the abstract but resist the buildings and school integration that diversity would require. They support climate action but oppose the density that would reduce car dependence. This is not false consciousness. It is material self-interest operating through property.

What would it mean for Democrats to take this seriously?

First, housing abundance must become a core economic policy, not a niche urbanist cause. Federal money should be tied more aggressively to housing production and fair housing compliance. Infrastructure funds, transportation grants, and climate investments should reward jurisdictions that allow dense, mixed-income, transit-accessible housing. The federal government cannot rewrite every zoning code, but it can stop subsidizing exclusion without conditions.

Second, Democrats need to challenge local veto points. A politics that treats every neighborhood objection as democratic participation will reproduce inequality. Renters, future residents, young families, and excluded workers are usually absent from zoning hearings. The people in the room are not “the community”; they are the housed incumbent fraction of the community. State governments have a crucial role here, and some blue states have begun to move, but the effort remains too timid relative to the scale of the crisis.

Third, school opportunity has to be part of the discussion. Housing segregation and school segregation are inseparable. Affluent suburbs often defend school boundaries with a ferocity that reveals the real stakes of zoning. If Democrats want racial and class equality, they must be willing to discuss regional school access, funding equity, magnet programs, transportation, and the hoarding of educational advantage. This is politically difficult, but so is every serious attack on inherited privilege.

Fourth, tenant power deserves as much attention as homeowner comfort. Building more housing is necessary, but not sufficient. Renters need protections against arbitrary eviction, habitability failures, discrimination, and extreme rent shocks. Community land trusts, social housing, nonprofit development, and public acquisition can all help create permanently affordable options outside speculative markets. A purely market-rate abundance agenda will not reach the poorest households quickly enough.

Finally, Democrats need a different moral language about homeownership. Owning a home can provide stability, but in much of blue America it has become a system of asset inflation protected by exclusion. When every homeowner is encouraged to see rising property values as a personal entitlement, housing policy becomes a conflict between private wealth and social need. A progressive party should not be neutral in that conflict.

Hacker and Pierson describe a Democratic coalition that can support broad economic redistribution as long as its most divisive internal conflicts remain submerged. But some conflicts should not remain submerged. The struggle over metropolitan land is one of them. The party’s affluent voters cannot be allowed to define progressivism as generous national spending plus local exclusion.

The Biden agenda showed that Democrats can think bigger about the federal government. The next step is to think more honestly about the places Democrats govern. A child tax credit helps a family pay rent; zoning reform helps determine whether the rent is extortionate in the first place. Infrastructure spending can connect regions; housing reform determines who gets to live near opportunity. Climate investment can decarbonize energy; land-use reform can decarbonize daily life.

The new blue divide will not be resolved in Washington alone. It runs through planning boards, school districts, transit agencies, neighborhood meetings, and the quiet rituals by which affluent liberals defend advantage. If Democrats want to be the party of multiracial democracy, they must become the party that opens the gates of blue America.
