"The Democratic Party’s shift toward a bold economic agenda does not negate the reality of its cultural insularity, which remains a primary obstacle to winning over working-class voters."

The Cultural Insularity Problem Does Not Go Away by Being Ignored

Hacker and Pierson's argument has a clever architecture. The standard critique — that Democrats have become a party of college-educated cultural progressives detached from the economic concerns of working-class voters — is dismissed as a mischaracterization. The party's real agenda, they insist, has been overwhelmingly economic. Look at the legislation. Look at the tweets. Look at Build Back Better. The cultural insularity story is a creation of right-wing media noise and confused journalism, not of the party itself.

I find this unconvincing for several reasons, and I want to take it seriously because Hacker and Pierson are serious analysts and the question is genuinely important. Where Have All the Democrats Gone?, the Judis and Teixeira book they criticize in passing, is not a reactionary lament. It is an account of how a party can lose voters by being seen to hold them in contempt, and how the things a party fails to say can matter as much as the things it says.

Begin with the methodology. Hacker and Pierson and their coauthors looked at the Twitter feeds of congressional Democrats between 2015 and 2023 and found that economic content dominated. Fine. But to whom does congressional Twitter speak? Mostly to journalists, activists, and other politically engaged elites. It is not a useful proxy for the messages that reach the median white voter without a college degree in Erie County or Maricopa County or Macomb County. Those messages come through cable news, local broadcast, Facebook clips, TikTok algorithms, and the slow drip of viral moments. In those channels, the dominant images of the Democratic Party are not the Child Tax Credit. They are college presidents stumbling over questions about antisemitism, school boards arguing over pronouns, district attorneys declining to prosecute, professors saying intemperate things about Israel, corporate diversity statements, and the brief but vivid "defund the police" moment.

It does not matter that Joe Biden never said "defund the police." It does not matter that Kamala Harris does not actually want to abolish ICE. It does not matter that Chuck Schumer's tweets are about infrastructure. The party is perceived through its loudest and most visible adjacent figures, and the loudest and most visible adjacent figures are the ones who get clicks, which means the ones who say things on cultural questions that are alienating to voters who do not share a college-educated metropolitan worldview. Hacker and Pierson half-acknowledge this when they write that "GOP leaders and right-wing commentators relentlessly pillory the Democrats as obsessed with identity and culture," but they treat this as an external imposition rather than something the party has any agency over.

It is something the party has agency over. When a Democratic president gives a speech about manufacturing, his cabinet secretaries can choose to repeat that message or they can choose to spend their available political capital on other priorities. When activist groups affiliated with the Democratic coalition speak in language that is highly legible to graduate-educated metropolitans and highly illegible to everyone else, leadership can push back on that language or it can defer to it. The choices the party makes about what to celebrate, what to ignore, and what to gently push aside reveal something important about its center of gravity. The Hacker-Pierson account treats this dimension as essentially solved — they note that immigration and criminal justice reform were "put on the legislative backburner" as if this were a strategic masterstroke rather than a tell. A party confident in its connection to working-class voters does not need to hide its position on immigration. It defends a position those voters can accept.

The polling data through 2023 should give Hacker and Pierson pause. Biden is underwater on the economy despite an objectively decent macroeconomic performance. His approval among Hispanic voters has slipped substantially since 2020, and among Black voters under thirty-five it has slipped dramatically. Working-class voters of color — the supposed beneficiaries of the Democrats' redistributive turn — are not flocking to a party that has just delivered them historic levels of spending. Why? The Hacker-Pierson account would predict that they should be, or at least that they should be moving in that direction. They are not. Something is interfering with the transmission of the economic message, and "the media is loud" is not a sufficient explanation, because the media has always been loud.

The interference, I would argue, is cultural distance. The Democratic Party has become, in the perception of many of its erstwhile supporters, a party whose central concerns are those of college-educated professionals: climate, racial equity formalisms, gender identity, foreign policy posturing on Israel and Ukraine, abortion as a values statement rather than a concrete policy. Many of these are good and important commitments. But they are not what most working-class voters, white or non-white, lie awake at night thinking about. And when a party's visible affect signals that those are the things it really cares about, no amount of CTC delivery will close the gap.

Consider an experiment that the Hacker-Pierson framework cannot easily run. Suppose Biden had spent 2021 and 2022 publicly fighting with progressive cultural activists. Suppose he had cracked down hard on the border, given a major speech distancing himself from "defund," signed a federal parental rights bill, and used the bully pulpit to push back on the more excessive forms of campus and corporate progressivism. He would, by Hacker and Pierson's measure, look more "moderate" — and yet his ability to deliver the economic agenda might have been substantially enhanced, because the cultural distance that prevents the economic message from landing would have been narrowed. The Clinton template, hated by today's progressives, understood this. Sister Souljah was not an accident.

I do not think Hacker and Pierson are wrong that the party has tacked left economically. They are right about that, and the description is valuable. But they conclude from this that the cultural critique is misplaced, and that does not follow. A party can tack left economically and still be culturally insular, and the cultural insularity can prevent the economic tack from being electorally rewarded. The relatively poor political return on the Biden agenda is consistent with exactly this diagnosis.

What follows? Not the abandonment of the economic agenda — that should continue. But a willingness, currently absent, to discipline the cultural impulses of the party's most online and least representative wing. Until the Democratic Party can credibly signal to working-class voters that it shares their basic moral universe — that it does not regard their views on crime, immigration, schools, and patriotism as embarrassments to be managed — it will continue to underperform its policy offering. Hacker and Pierson have written a fine description of a party that has its economic policy partly right and its political posture significantly wrong. The latter does not fix itself by being denied.
