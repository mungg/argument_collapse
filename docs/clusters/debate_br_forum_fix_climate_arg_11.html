Diversified Claude

"While experimentalist governance correctly identifies how sectoral problem-solving occurs, it requires the 'climate club' model to provide the necessary political muscle and predictable penalty defaults to scale those solutions globally."

Sabel and Victor's essay does the climate policy field a real service by shifting attention from the global treaty drama to the sectoral and sub-sectoral machinery where decarbonization actually has to happen. They are right that Paris is not the engine, that NDCs aggregate information along the wrong axis, that Montreal worked through sectoral peer-reviewed learning rather than comprehensive design, and that "penalty defaults" are what destabilize incumbent positions. I want to take their framework seriously and push it in a direction they gesture toward but do not fully embrace: the climate club, as developed in different forms by William Nordhaus, Scott Barrett, and most recently in proposals for trade-linked carbon border adjustments. If experimentalist governance is the right description of how problem-solving proceeds within sectors, the climate club is the right description of how the political muscle gets supplied across sectors.

Begin with what Sabel and Victor identify as the indispensable function of "penalty defaults." In their Montreal account, these took several forms: domestic electoral pressure on Western governments, brand-and-license pressure on consumer-facing firms like DuPont, and trade sanctions threatened by powerful Northern states against laggard Southern ones. These pressures worked because they were credible, because the targets had something to lose, and because the costs of compliance were modest enough that the threatened parties could be brought around without political revolution.

The trouble with applying this to climate is that the analogous pressures are diffuse, uncoordinated, and often unenforceable. Domestic electoral pressure exists in some countries (Germany, the UK, Denmark) but not in others (Russia, Australia, Saudi Arabia, and large portions of the United States). Brand pressure works on B2C firms but not on commodity producers selling into anonymous markets. Trade sanctions are theoretically available but politically toxic and legally fraught under the WTO regime that the authors themselves note is paralyzed.

What a climate club does is consolidate these scattered pressures into a single, predictable, rule-based instrument: differential market access. Members of the club adopt a common carbon price (or its regulatory equivalent), apply a border adjustment to imports from non-members, and condition further trade integration on emissions performance. Non-members face a clear choice — adopt comparable measures and gain access to the club's markets, or pay the border adjustment as the price of standing apart. The penalty default is no longer an ad hoc threat issued by individual states; it is the standing structure of the trade regime that members have built among themselves.

This is, I would argue, the most plausible path to making Sabel and Victor's sectoral experimentalism work at the speed and scale we need. Consider how it interacts with each layer of their framework. At the level of frontier industrial sectors — steel, cement, ammonia, aluminum — the club's border adjustment raises the cost of dirty imports to club markets, which makes investments in clean production (like the HYBRIT steel project in Sweden or the various low-carbon cement initiatives in the EU) commercially viable. Sabel and Victor's sectoral committees can do their patient work of testing alternatives and codifying standards, but the commercial logic that makes the work matter comes from the price differential the club creates. Without the differential, the patient work produces interesting demonstration projects that do not propagate; with it, the work produces templates that competitors race to adopt.

At the level of place-based sectors — buildings, grids, distribution — the club's effect is more indirect but still consequential. Club members are forced by their own internal carbon prices to find ways to decarbonize their domestic economies, which drives demand for the kinds of place-based experiments and local re-elaborations Sabel and Victor describe. The club does not directly govern these efforts, but it creates the macro-conditions under which they are funded and pursued.

At the level of hybrid sectors — forestry products, palm oil, agricultural commodities — the club's border adjustment can be extended to embedded emissions and to certified sustainability schemes. The EU's deforestation regulation, then being prepared, points in this direction. The club becomes the mechanism by which the patient, place-based work of changing land-use practices in producer countries gets connected to the market access decisions of consumer countries.

In each of these cases, the club provides what Sabel and Victor's framework requires but cannot itself produce: a credible, durable, predictable penalty default that does not depend on the ad hoc willingness of individual governments to threaten sanctions. It is the institutional consolidation of the threat that makes the sectoral experimentation pay off.

There is a further advantage that Sabel and Victor's framework actively benefits from. A climate club does not require global membership at the outset; it can start with a coalition of the willing — plausibly the EU, the UK, perhaps the incoming Biden administration if domestic politics permit, Canada, possibly Japan and South Korea — and grow as the costs of standing outside the club rise. This is precisely the "thin initial consensus among an open group of founding participants motivated to act" that Sabel and Victor identify as the right starting point. The membership "expands as new actors outside the circle of founders are invited in." The mechanism by which expansion occurs is the same in both frameworks: it becomes too costly to remain outside. The climate club just makes the cost explicit and rule-based rather than diffuse.

Sabel and Victor's hesitation about the club approach, to the extent it can be read in the essay, seems to be that it is too "top-down" — too reliant on a single instrument applied across sectors, insufficiently attentive to the variation in conditions across industries and places. I think this is a misreading of what the club proposal actually involves. A climate club is not a substitute for sectoral experimentation; it is the macro-political envelope within which sectoral experimentation becomes commercially and politically rational. The club says nothing about which technology should replace blast-furnace steelmaking, or how to integrate renewables on the Indian grid, or what kind of cookstove program will work in rural Tanzania. These are exactly the questions that sectoral committees and place-based institutions must answer through the iterative learning Sabel and Victor describe. The club just ensures that the answers, once found, propagate through the market system rather than dying as boutique pilots.

There is one further point worth making. Sabel and Victor are correct that one of Paris's residual functions is to provide the legitimacy under which firms and governments can be pressured by activists and trading partners. "Without Paris it would be much harder for protesters to rattle companies." Agreed. But a climate club adds an additional layer of legitimacy: it provides the legal authorization under which border adjustments and conditional market access can be defended in trade disputes. The current debate about whether the proposed EU CBAM is WTO-compatible turns precisely on this question. A climate club agreement among major economies — even an informal one — that establishes common principles for border adjustment would substantially reduce the legal risk and political controversy of using these instruments. Paris provides the normative cover; a climate club provides the operational framework.

In the end, I read Sabel and Victor's essay as making a case for experimentalism in the conduct of sectoral and sub-sectoral work, and I agree with that case. But experimentalism within sectors needs to be matched by something more structured across sectors and across borders. The climate club is the most promising candidate for that something. The incoming Biden administration's most consequential international climate decision will not be rejoining Paris — that is, as the authors note, easy. It will be whether to align U.S. policy with the European border adjustment that is coming, and whether to use the prospect of trade-linked carbon pricing as leverage to bring China and India along over the coming decade. That is the place where the politics will be made or unmade. Sabel and Victor have given us the right framework for what happens inside sectors once the politics is made. We need the climate club to make the politics.
