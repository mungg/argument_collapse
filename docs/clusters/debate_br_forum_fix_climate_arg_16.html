Diversified Gemini

"While Victor and Sabel correctly identify experimentalist governance as the solution to climate change, they overlook the fact that global city networks are the most effective and advanced practitioners of this model."

David G. Victor and Charles Sabel present a brilliant framework for climate action in "How to Fix the Climate." By dismantling the false dichotomy between top-down diplomatic mandates and aimless bottom-up voluntarism, they articulate a path forward—experimentalist governance—that perfectly captures how complex, uncertain problems are actually solved. However, while their theory is sound, their application is overly constrained. In focusing primarily on industrial sectors like maritime shipping, steel, and chemical propellants, they overlook the most dynamic, effective, and inherently experimentalist entities on the planet: cities.

If experimentalist governance is defined by frontline actors innovating under conditions of uncertainty, with their successes monitored, codified, and scaled by comprehensive bodies, then the global network of megacities is already operating decades ahead of national governments and international diplomats. Urban areas account for over 70 percent of global carbon emissions. They are where energy is consumed, where transportation networks operate, and where buildings are constructed. To talk about "ground-level problem solving" without centering municipal governance is to miss the largest laboratory we have.

Consider the C40 Cities Climate Leadership Group, a network of the world’s largest cities committed to addressing climate change. C40 is a textbook example of experimentalist governance in action. It operates exactly according to the design principles Victor and Sabel outline. It starts with a "thin consensus"—mayors agreeing that climate change is an urgent threat requiring deep emissions cuts, even if national leaders (like the Trump administration in the U.S.) are paralyzed or hostile.

Problem-solving is entirely devolved to the local level. A city like Oslo experiments with electrifying its municipal ferries and construction equipment. Paris experiments with radical urban redesign through the "15-minute city" concept, drastically reducing the need for automobile travel. New York City passes Local Law 97, setting strict, legally binding emissions caps on its largest buildings, effectively creating a massive market signal for retrofitting and energy efficiency technologies.

These local experiments are closely monitored and evaluated by the C40 network. When a pilot project succeeds—such as Shenzhen’s complete electrification of its 16,000-vehicle bus fleet—the technical data, the financing models, and the policy frameworks are shared across the network. Peer review is continuous; mayors and municipal transit authorities exchange notes, adapting the successes of one city to the unique geographic and political context of another. Just as the Montreal Protocol’s committees reviewed whether a new refrigerant could work reliably, city networks review whether a congestion pricing scheme or a revised building code is robust enough for general use.

Furthermore, cities possess the unique ability to apply the "penalty defaults" that Victor and Sabel rightly identify as essential for destabilizing the status quo. While a national government might balk at banning internal combustion engines due to the lobbying power of the auto industry, a city can simply restrict access. When London expands its Ultra Low Emission Zone (ULEZ), or when dozens of global cities pledge to ban diesel vehicles from their centers by 2030, they are creating massive penalty defaults for automakers. They are telling the industry: innovate, electrify, or lose access to the most lucrative urban markets in the world. This is not waiting for international consensus; it is using local regulatory power to force global industrial shifts.

The authors distinguish between "globalized and highly concentrated industries" (like shipping) and sectors geared to "local markets" (like residential construction). But cities bridge this gap. By altering local building codes or municipal procurement rules, cities aggregate local demand into global market forces. If Los Angeles, Tokyo, and London all demand zero-carbon cement for public infrastructure projects, they single-handedly create the market for the very "sectoral experimentation" Victor and Sabel wish to see in the heavy industries.

As the authors note, the U.S. political environment is deeply polarized, and aggressive administrative action in Washington will face intense legal and political opposition. In this context, the shift to "place-based solutions" is not merely a fallback option; it is the primary strategy. The Biden administration, even if constrained by a divided Senate, can turbocharge this urban experimentalism by directing federal funding, grants, and transit dollars directly to municipalities willing to serve as laboratories for decarbonization.

Victor and Sabel have successfully diagnosed the illness of the international climate regime and prescribed the right medicine. But they are delivering it to the wrong patient. We do not need to wait for bureaucratic sectoral committees to find their footing. The framework of experimentalist governance is already thriving in city halls around the world. It is time we recognize mayors, city councils, and urban planners as the true architects of our post-carbon future.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Urban areas are the primary source of global emissions and serve as the most significant 'ground-level' laboratories for testing climate solutions in energy, transport, and construction.
  2. 02City networks like C40 embody experimentalist principles by facilitating a thin consensus among mayors, devolving problem-solving to local contexts, and scaling successful pilots through peer review.
  3. 03Municipalities effectively utilize 'penalty defaults' by using local regulatory power, such as low-emission zones, to force global industries to innovate or lose market access.
  4. 04Cities bridge the gap between local and global sectors by aggregating municipal demand to create viable markets for green industrial products like zero-carbon cement.
  5. 05In a polarized national political environment, empowering municipal laboratories through direct federal funding is the most viable primary strategy for decarbonization.
