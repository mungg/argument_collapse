Diversified Claude

"While experimentalist governance offers a pragmatic alternative to failed global climate treaties, it must be restructured to ensure that the Global South is a co-architect of standards and technology rather than a passive recipient of Northern-designed rules and penalties."

Reading Sabel and Victor from a Global South perspective produces a particular kind of unease. The essay is intelligent, learned, and humane, and its critique of Paris is one many of us in developing-country policy circles share. The diplomatic theater that has consumed three decades of climate negotiation has yielded little for the countries that will bear the heaviest physical costs of warming. The authors are right that comprehensive global treaties cannot do the work, that sectoral experimentation is where decarbonization actually proceeds, and that the politics of climate action have to be built through demonstrated successes rather than declarations. But the essay also reproduces, in subtler form, an assumption that has dogged climate governance from the beginning: that the rules, the standards, the "sectoral committees," and the "penalty defaults" will be designed in the North and that the South's role is to be brought along through capacity-building funds and graduated compliance schedules. Montreal worked that way; the authors say so explicitly. The question is whether climate governance can or should work that way too.

Consider the Montreal account on which the entire essay is built. The committees that defined what counted as a viable substitute for CFCs were dominated by Northern industry and Northern regulators. The Multilateral Fund was administered with the participation of recipients, but the standards being met were set elsewhere. Developing countries were given longer compliance schedules and financial support, and these were genuine accommodations. But the substantive content of the regime — what technologies, what timelines, what counts as compliance — was determined in fora where Southern voices were marginal. This was tolerable, perhaps, on ozone because the stakes were narrow, the substitutes were known, and the costs were modest. It is not tolerable on climate, where the stakes are existential, the technological choices reshape entire development trajectories, and the costs implicate the basic terms on which countries can industrialize.

The authors gesture at this when they mention that "essentially all costs of compliance for developing countries were paid by a 'Multilateral Fund' financed by the rich countries." Paris's $100 billion annual climate finance commitment, made in Copenhagen in 2009 and reaffirmed at every subsequent COP, has been chronically unmet, and what has been delivered is overwhelmingly in the form of loans rather than grants and is often double-counted against existing development aid. If Montreal-style sectoral experimentation is to be transposed to climate, the financing question cannot be waved away. It is not a side issue; it is the central question of whether the architecture is legitimate to those it would govern.

There is also a deeper question about what the "penalty defaults" the authors celebrate look like from the receiving end. When DuPont moved on CFCs and the United States threatened Russia and India with trade sanctions for CFC violations, this looked from one angle like principled environmental enforcement. From another angle, it looked like a familiar pattern: Northern industries having developed substitutes (often patented and licensed by them), Northern governments used trade access to compel Southern adoption of those substitutes. The result was real environmental gain, but also continued Northern technological dominance in the affected industries. The replacement refrigerants and propellants were sold by the same Northern firms that had sold the originals.

Translate this to climate, and the implications are stark. A border adjustment mechanism along the lines the European Union is preparing will, on current designs, fall hardest on exports from countries like India, Vietnam, and Bangladesh whose industries are emissions-intensive precisely because they are still building up. The Northern claim is that these countries should decarbonize their industries; the Southern reply is that they would gladly do so if the financing and technology transfers promised since Rio in 1992 had materialized. The "penalty default" of border adjustment, in this context, looks less like a virtuous destabilization of incumbent fossil interests and more like the export of Northern decarbonization costs onto Southern producers. There may be no entirely satisfactory alternative, but the framing matters. Sabel and Victor describe penalty defaults as if they are politically neutral pressure points for accelerating problem-solving. They are not. They are exercises of power, and the question of who wields them and against whom shapes the legitimacy of the entire regime.

This brings me to the essay's invocation of John Dewey and the "broad participation of stakeholders" in Montreal's sectoral committees. Dewey's distinction between the shoemaker and the wearer of shoes is apt. The question is who, in the climate case, counts as the wearer of shoes. The essay implicitly identifies stakeholders as industry participants and ground-level regulators within the sectors being transformed. But the people most affected by climate change are not stakeholders in industrial decarbonization decisions in any meaningful sense. They are smallholder farmers in the Sahel, fisherfolk in the Bay of Bengal, urban dwellers in the heat-stressed megacities of South Asia. Their stake in the outcome of decarbonization decisions is total — and their representation in any sectoral committee envisioned by Sabel and Victor is approximately zero.

I do not say this to dismiss the framework. The work of finding viable low-carbon substitutes for steel, cement, shipping, and aviation does have to be done somewhere, and committees of technical experts representing the producers and regulators of those industries are the appropriate forum for much of that work. But we should be clear that this is not the whole of climate governance, that it cannot bear the legitimacy weight the essay places on it, and that the broader question of whose voices count in setting the direction of decarbonization remains unanswered.

There is a more positive reading available, however, of what experimentalist governance could look like if it took seriously the questions raised here. The essay's emphasis on "place-based solutions" and "local contextualization" is the right starting point. The implementation of any global decarbonization framework has to be re-elaborated locally, and local re-elaboration is exactly where Southern actors have the most knowledge and authority. The challenge is to ensure that the iterative loop between local experimentation and global standard-setting runs in both directions — that Southern experience reshapes Northern standards as well as the reverse. The Indian grid integration of renewables is solving problems that European grid operators have not faced; the African mobile-money revolution has produced financial inclusion models the North is still learning from. A climate governance regime worth its name would treat these as primary sites of learning, not secondary applications of Northern standards.

What this would require, concretely, is a serious commitment of Northern resources — not loans, not double-counted aid, but actual transfers — into Southern-led experimentation, with the products of that experimentation feeding back into the global sectoral standards Sabel and Victor describe. It would require that the boards and technical committees governing those standards include real Southern representation with real authority, not just consultative status. It would require that border adjustment mechanisms be paired with technology transfer mechanisms of comparable ambition. None of this is impossible, but none of it is implied by the essay as written.

Sabel and Victor have given us a useful framework. The work that remains is to embed it in a politics of climate justice that the framework alone does not supply.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Montreal Protocol model is an insufficient template for climate change because the existential stakes and economic costs of decarbonization are far higher than those of ozone depletion, requiring more inclusive decision-making.
  2. 02The 'penalty defaults' celebrated by the lead essay, such as trade sanctions or border adjustments, risk becoming tools of Northern power that export decarbonization costs to developing nations and reinforce technological dominance.
  3. 03Climate finance is not a secondary administrative issue but a central requirement for the legitimacy of any experimentalist regime, necessitating genuine resource transfers rather than loans or double-counted aid.
  4. 04True experimentalist governance must ensure that the iterative learning process runs in both directions, where innovations from the Global South actively reshape global sectoral standards.
  5. 05The 'stakeholders' in sectoral committees must be expanded beyond industrial insiders to include those most vulnerable to climate impacts, ensuring the framework serves climate justice rather than just technical efficiency.
