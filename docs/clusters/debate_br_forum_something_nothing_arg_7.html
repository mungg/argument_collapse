Diversified Gemini

"While Nir Rosen correctly identifies tactical and military failures in Afghanistan, his critique is a cynical abdication of responsibility that ignores the moral imperative to protect Afghan civil society and the tangible progress made since 2001."

Nir Rosen’s dispatch from Helmand province offers a grim, street-level view of the tactical frustrations facing the NATO coalition. His portrayal of the exhausted, under-equipped, and poorly led Afghan National Police (ANP) rings true to anyone who has spent time in the country’s southern and eastern provinces. Yet, while his critique of the military’s application of counterinsurgency (COIN) is trenchant, his essay fundamentally fails the people of Afghanistan. In his relentless focus on American military folly, Rosen erases Afghan civil society and implicitly advocates abandoning millions of people to a medieval theocracy.

To read Rosen, one would assume that Afghanistan is populated entirely by two groups: corrupt government officials and legitimate, aggrieved Taliban insurgents. He writes that "the Taliban only attack Afghan civilians who collaborate with the Americans and their puppet government or who are suspected of violating the extremely harsh interpretation of Islamic law that many Afghans accept." This sentence alone reveals a shocking moral blindness.

First, who are these "collaborators" the Taliban are killing? They are schoolteachers, polio vaccine workers, civil servants trying to repair irrigation canals, and female politicians brave enough to stand for provincial councils. To dismiss their murders as the natural consequence of collaboration is to validate the Taliban's campaign of terror.

Second, the assertion that "many Afghans accept" the Taliban’s harsh interpretation of Islamic law is an insult to the Afghan people. Did the women who were beaten in the streets of Kabul in the 1990s for showing their ankles "accept" this law? Do the millions of girls currently attending school—who were legally barred from education a decade ago—"accept" the Taliban's vision? Rosen conflates rural conservatism with a desire to live under a totalitarian shadow government. Poll after poll demonstrates that while Afghans are deeply frustrated with the Karzai government’s corruption, they do not want the Taliban back in power.

Rosen’s essay reflects a growing, cynical consensus among Western commentators: because state-building is hard, messy, and plagued by corruption, it is an imperialist vanity project that should be abandoned. This "realist" fatigue ignores the very real, albeit fragile, gains made since 2001.

Consider the context in which we operate today, in early 2010. Despite the fraud of the recent election—which Rosen rightly condemns—there is a burgeoning independent media in Afghanistan. There are millions of children in school, a significant percentage of them girls. Basic health care access, while still woefully inadequate, has vastly improved, leading to a demonstrable drop in infant and maternal mortality rates compared to the dark days of the 1990s. There is a generation of young Afghans in Kabul, Herat, and Mazar-i-Sharif who are connected to the outside world and desperately want to build a modern, moderate state.

Rosen is entirely correct that you cannot build a state solely through military force. The fundamental flaw in our strategy is not the desire to protect the population, but the militarization of that effort. We have asked 19-year-old infantrymen to act as mayors, agronomists, and judicial reformers. We have poured billions of dollars through Pentagon contractors while starving the civilian agencies—USAID and the State Department—that actually have expertise in institutional development.

The solution to the incompetence of the ANP, as witnessed by Team Prowler, is not to shrug our shoulders and leave. It is to fundamentally reform how we recruit, train, and pay them. We have treated the police as paramilitaries to fight the Taliban, rather than as community servants to enforce the rule of law. A true population-centric strategy requires a massive "civilian surge"—deploying diplomats, rule-of-law experts, and development specialists to work alongside the military, ensuring that when the military clears an area, legitimate governance follows.

Furthermore, Rosen’s comparison of Afghanistan to Iraq ignores the international community's responsibility. The U.S. intervention in Iraq was a war of choice that destroyed a functioning state. The intervention in Afghanistan was a response to an attack on the U.S., which overthrew a regime that was harboring international terrorists. The international community, under a UN mandate, pledged to help Afghans rebuild. To walk away now, because the task is harder than anticipated, would be a catastrophic betrayal of those Afghans who risked their lives to work with us.

I share Rosen’s disgust with the corruption in Kabul. Hamid Karzai’s reliance on warlords and his tolerance of narco-trafficking are destroying the legitimacy of the state. The Obama administration must use its leverage to force genuine anti-corruption measures, bypassing the central government to fund legitimate local leaders and provincial governors where necessary.

But we cannot throw the baby out with the bathwater. The alternative to a flawed, struggling Afghan state is a return to civil war and Taliban dominion. The Taliban do not have a governance platform; they have a system of control based on fear. If the U.S. withdraws, as Rosen implies we should, the burden will not fall on the Western pundits who championed the exit. It will fall on the Afghan women who will be forced back under the burqa, the teachers who will be assassinated, and the Hazara minorities who will face renewed persecution.

State-building is a generational endeavor. We have wasted much of the last eight years through distraction and under-resourcing. The current surge is a belated attempt to correct those mistakes. It will be agonizingly difficult, and there will undoubtedly be more tragedies along Highway 601. But the moral imperative remains: we broke it, we owe it to the Afghan people to help them fix it. Cynicism masquerading as realism is not a strategy; it is an abdication of responsibility.

Diversified Gemini

"While Nir Rosen’s critique of U.S. military strategy is valid, his essay relies on a cynical, one-sided portrayal of Afghanistan that erases the agency of its people and whitewashes the brutality of the Taliban."

Nir Rosen’s long, detailed account of his time embedded with American and Afghan forces in Helmand paints a picture of utter despair. To read "Something from Nothing" is to consume a narrative where Afghanistan is nothing more than a wasteland of corrupt cops, illiterate farmers, and relentless Taliban fighters, all being disastrously managed by clueless American soldiers. As an Afghan-American watching my country of birth debated in Western policy circles in 2010, I find Rosen’s critique of the U.S. military compelling, but his portrayal of Afghans deeply problematic and insulting. Once again, Western journalism reduces the Afghan people to caricatures: either hapless victims, corrupt thieves, or religious fanatics.

Rosen’s essay suffers from a severe case of tunnel vision. By embedding almost exclusively with a combat unit in Helmand—the most violent, drug-ridden, and insurgent-heavy province in the country—he extrapolates the worst aspects of the war to represent the entirety of the Afghan experience. He states, "For the average Afghan, life remains a miserable struggle for subsistence. More troops cannot fix this."

While life is undeniably hard, who is the "average Afghan" Rosen refers to? Is it the millions of young people living in Kabul, Herat, and Mazar-i-Sharif who are attending universities? Is it the Afghan journalists running vibrant, independent television networks like TOLO TV, who risk their lives daily to hold the Karzai government accountable? Is it the female entrepreneurs starting businesses, or the millions of citizens who defied Taliban death threats to cast ballots in the 2004 and 2009 elections?

Rosen casually dismisses the 2009 elections as a "failed" exercise that only "empowered warlords and preserved a corrupt order." Yes, the fraud orchestrated by Karzai's camp was a disgrace, and the international community’s tepid response was shameful. But to say the elections were merely a message of "no hope" ignores the incredible bravery of the Afghan voters. People had their fingers cut off by the Taliban for voting, yet millions still went to the polls because they desperately want a civilian, democratic alternative to both warlordism and Taliban theocracy. Rosen’s cynicism erases their agency and their sacrifice.

Furthermore, Rosen’s treatment of the Afghan security forces is painfully one-sided. The Afghan National Police (ANP) he describes—high on drugs, cowardly, and corrupt—certainly exist. The ANP is a deeply flawed institution. But he glosses over the fact that these men are dying at five times the rate of NATO soldiers. They are sent to isolated checkpoints in the most dangerous parts of the country in unarmored Ford Rangers, facing a heavily armed insurgency, often without backup or proper pay. It is easy for an embedded Western journalist to mock them as "squirrelly" or cowardly. It is much harder to recognize that despite the massive systemic failures, tens of thousands of Afghans are putting on uniforms every day to fight an enemy that wants to plunge their country back into the dark ages.

Perhaps the most troubling aspect of Rosen’s argument is his assessment of the Taliban. He writes that the Taliban "only attack Afghan civilians who collaborate with the Americans and their puppet government." This is a staggering whitewash of Taliban atrocities. The Taliban wage a deliberate war of terror against the Afghan civilian population. They throw acid in the faces of schoolgirls. They hang tribal elders who refuse to pay extortion money. They detonate suicide bombs in crowded civilian markets. To frame their violence as a legitimate resistance to an occupying force and its "collaborators" is to adopt the insurgency's own propaganda.

The vast majority of Afghans despise the Taliban. The memories of the 1990s—the public executions in soccer stadiums, the banning of music, the erasure of women from public life—are still fresh. Rosen claims that "the Taliban have once again become attractive to many Afghans." This is false. The Taliban are not attractive; they are terrifying. In areas where the government provides no security, people submit to the Taliban out of self-preservation, not ideological alignment.

Rosen is entirely correct that the U.S. military strategy is floundering. General McChrystal’s COIN doctrine, designed in the think tanks of Washington, often fails upon contact with the complex, localized realities of Afghan tribal dynamics. The U.S. reliance on brutal regional strongmen has fundamentally undermined the legitimacy of the state-building project. The civilian casualties caused by American airstrikes are unforgivable and serve as the greatest recruitment tool for the insurgency.

But the conclusion Rosen draws—that because the American intervention is flawed, the entire project is a lost cause—is a luxury only a Westerner can afford. He suggests that addressing root causes like "U.S. support for the Israeli occupation of Palestine" will do more for Afghans than military presence. This is an absurd, orientalist conflation of global grievances. A farmer in Kandahar does not care about Palestine; he cares about security, a fair price for his crops, and a local judge who won't demand a bribe.

The United States cannot abandon Afghanistan. Not because of a nebulous threat of al-Qaeda, but because a precipitous withdrawal would collapse the fragile state, spark a massive civil war along ethnic lines, and hand the country back to a regime of unspeakable cruelty. We do not need a magical COIN formula. We need long-term, patient partnership. We need the U.S. to stop funneling money through corrupt strongmen and start investing in Afghan civil society, education, and civilian institutions. We need a strategy that views Afghans not merely as "Hajis" to be pacified or corrupt cops to be mocked, but as human beings fighting desperately for the survival of their country.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Rosen suffers from tunnel vision by extrapolating the extreme conditions of Helmand province to represent the entire Afghan experience, ignoring the progress of urban civil society and independent media.
  2. 02Dismissing the 2009 elections as a total failure insults the bravery of millions of Afghans who risked their lives to vote for a democratic alternative to theocracy.
  3. 03The critique of the Afghan National Police is unfairly derogatory, failing to acknowledge that these men face extreme casualty rates and systemic neglect while fighting a terrifying enemy.
  4. 04Rosen’s claim that the Taliban only target 'collaborators' is a dangerous whitewash of a movement that systematically uses terror and violence against the general civilian population.
  5. 05The assertion that the Taliban have become 'attractive' to Afghans is false; rather, people submit to them out of fear and a lack of government-provided security.
  6. 06Suggesting that global political grievances like the Israeli-Palestinian conflict are the root of Afghan instability is an 'orientalist' conflation that ignores the local needs of Afghan citizens.
  7. 07A total U.S. withdrawal would be catastrophic, necessitating a shift from military-centric strategies to long-term investment in Afghan civil society and civilian institutions.
