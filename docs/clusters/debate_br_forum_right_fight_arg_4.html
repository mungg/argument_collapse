"The proposed integration of local police into a national domestic-intelligence network is fundamentally incompatible with a democratic society because it replaces traditional criminal law enforcement with a permanent system of domestic espionage."

In his thoughtful and nuanced essay, Daniel Richman attempts something that might seem impossible after the last three years: to imagine a domestic intelligence network that is both effective and rights-respecting, and to find the key to that balance in the very structure of American federalism. He argues that state and local governments, if brought in as genuine partners, can act as a mediating layer between the federal government’s national security imperatives and the civil-liberties concerns of the communities they serve. The argument is seductive because it avoids the usual zero-sum rhetoric. But it remains dangerously wrong, not because local police are unreliable (though they often are), but because the entire project of a nationwide, proactive intelligence-gathering apparatus is incompatible with the kind of open, democratic society to which we ostensibly aspire. Richman’s error is not in his institutional analysis, but in his willingness to accept the fundamental premise of the “War on Terror” at home.

The heart of the problem is the blurring of the line between policing and spying. Traditional criminal law enforcement is reactive: it investigates crimes that have occurred or are imminent, based on individualized suspicion, and operates within a framework of probable cause, warrants, and the adversarial trial process. Intelligence-gathering, by contrast, is proactive and speculative. It aims to collect vast amounts of information about people, associations, and ideas in the hope of “connecting dots” that will prevent future attacks. This model has always been the norm for foreign intelligence—we expect the CIA to spy on other countries—but when turned inward, it represents a fundamental shift in the relationship between the citizen and the state. Richman’s proposal to involve local police simply extends this model to every precinct house in the country, making the beat cop an intelligence node. The friendly, community-oriented officer who once helped old ladies across the street now also files reports on their acquaintances, their reading habits, and their political discussions.

This transformation has already begun. Section 215 of the USA PATRIOT Act allows the FBI to obtain “any tangible thing” (including library and bookstore records) upon a minimal certification to the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Court, with no showing of individualized suspicion. Local police, acting as agents of the Joint Terrorism Task Forces, routinely share with federal counterparts the information they gather during routine encounters—traffic stops, domestic calls, building inspections. The “felony arrest as intelligence opportunity” that Richman celebrates is precisely what should alarm us. It means that every arrest, for any offense, becomes a chance to question the suspect about his community, his travels, his opinions, and to enter that information into a permanent, unaccountable database. This is not community policing; it is the domestic equivalent of the informant networks that sustained the East German Stasi.

Richman acknowledges the COINTELPRO analogy and attempts to distinguish our era from the late 1960s by pointing to the changes in police culture and the broader consensus about the threat. But the similarities are more striking than the differences. Then, as now, the federal government argued that an existential threat—communist subversion, terrorism—required broad surveillance of political and religious groups. Then, as now, local police were recruited to do much of the dirty work, bypassing the restrictions that applied more directly to federal agencies. The Church Committee found that local intelligence units “provided a convenient manner for the FBI to acquire information it wanted while avoiding criticism for using covert techniques.” Substitute the names of a few agencies, and the statement could apply today. The Portland example Richman cites—the city attorney’s refusal to allow local officers to ask certain questions—is the exception that proves the rule: most departments complied, and the ones that resisted did so only because they had preexisting state laws barring such inquiries, laws that are now under attack in Congress.

Moreover, Richman’s reliance on “accountability” misunderstands the nature of accountability in intelligence work. Accountability presupposes transparency: we can hold officials responsible only if we know what they are doing. But domestic intelligence operations are inherently secret. Richman notes that even if the most criticized provisions of the Patriot Act were repealed, the FBI could still obtain the same materials through grand-jury subpoenas issued in secret. That is precisely the point: the problem is not this or that statute, but the underlying architecture of surveillance and the lack of public access to decision-making. The negotiations Richman envisions between local police chiefs and federal agents would take place behind closed doors, excluded from the scrutiny of even the most dedicated city council. What leverage would a mayor have over a counterterrorism operation she cannot see? The very concept of “local voice” is reduced to a private plea.

I do not propose that we simply do nothing to prevent terrorism. But the alternative to Richman’s model is not passivity; it is a return to the principles of criminal investigation. Terrorism is a crime, and suspects can be investigated, prosecuted, and punished using the ordinary tools of law enforcement—search warrants, wiretaps with probable cause, grand juries—with all the protections that the Bill of Rights provides. To the extent that current law falls short, we can strengthen it without abandoning the adversary system. The argument that such tools are insufficient is the argument for police powers, and it is an argument that has been made many times in our history, always to be regretted later.

The “right fight” is not to give local governments a say in how their residents are spied upon; it is to insist that, in a free society, the state has no business building a permanent system of domestic espionage, whether run from Washington or from city hall. If Richman’s federalist intelligence network comes to pass, we will discover, too late, that the velvet glove of community policing does not alter the iron fist beneath.

Diversified DeepSeek

"The proposal to integrate local police into a national domestic intelligence network is a dangerous expansion of state power that threatens civil liberties, regardless of whether it is framed as a decentralized or accountable federalist model."

There is a kind of sophistication that is, in the end, more dangerous than crude authoritarianism. It clothes the expansion of state power in the language of nuance, balance, and pragmatic accommodation. Daniel Richman has written an essay that is, in many ways, a model of this genre. He wants a domestic intelligence network that “courts the assistance of state and local governments,” that respects their “distinct interests,” that is both effective and accountable. Who could object? The answer is anyone who remembers that the most insidious systems of surveillance are not those imposed by alien conquerors, but those that recruit the natives into their own subjugation.

Richman’s central trope is that state and local police, if properly engaged, will be a “bulwark” against federal abuses. The claim has a certain historical irony. It was not so long ago that progressives, and Richman himself partly acknowledges, looked to the federal government as the only force capable of checking local police violence. The Justice Department’s Civil Rights Division, the FBI investigations of Klan terrorism, the federal criminal prosecutions of Rodney King’s abusers—these were the bulwarks. Now, in a complete inversion, Richman asks us to believe that the same local police forces, notorious for racial profiling, brutality, and a deep-seated hostility to civilian oversight, will become the guardians of our liberties against a too-aggressive Washington. This is not federalism; it is an intellectual shell game.

The reason Richman needs this inversion is that he has accepted, largely uncritically, the central myth of the post-9/11 era: that we are engaged in a “war” against a globally networked, invisibly embedded enemy, and that this war requires a qualitatively different kind of state capacity—a capacity for pervasive anticipation. In this mythology, the old methods of criminal law enforcement are too slow, too reactive, too respectful of individual rights. We must “connect the dots” before the bombs go off, and that means collecting millions of dots, constantly, everywhere. It means, as Richman approvingly says, that “every felony arrest is an intelligence opportunity.” It means that the police officer who stops you for a broken taillight, or who responds when you report a stolen bicycle, is also an agent of a national intelligence apparatus, filing reports on what he sees and hears, what he infers about your associations and your opinions. This is the architecture of a police state, no matter how many “local voices” are piped in.

Richman asks us to believe that the community policing revolution of the 1990s has changed the fundamental nature of American policing, such that we can now trust local departments to resist the federal security state. But the revolution, such as it was, has been largely a rhetorical and public-relations phenomenon. The same departments that adopted the slogan “community policing” also embraced broken-windows policing, which translated, in minority neighborhoods, into aggressive stop-and-frisk campaigns, crackdowns on petty disorder, and a pervasive sense of occupation. To imagine that these same institutions, now supplemented with federal counterterrorism grants and surveillance technology, will suddenly become defenders of civil liberties is to ignore everything we know about institutional inertia and the corrupting effects of power.

Moreover, Richman’s reliance on “electoral accountability” ignores the deep democratic deficit that pervades local government in the United States. The residents of the very urban neighborhoods where domestic intelligence will focus—comunities of poor people, immigrants, and racial minorities—are, to a staggering degree, disenfranchised. They don’t vote at the same rates as affluent suburbanites; they are often not citizens; they are overpoliced and underrepresented. To argue that these communities will exercise a democratic check on police intelligence-gathering through their mayors and city councils is to display a naivete about the actual distribution of political power that would be comical if the stakes were not so high.

The only historically reliable check on domestic surveillance has been mass political resistance, combined with aggressive legislative and judicial intervention. The Church Committee, the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act, the Privacy Act, the defeat of the TIPS program—these were not the products of enlightened federal-local negotiation. They were the result of public scandal, organized protest, and a willingness to say, “This far, no further.” Richman’s essay, for all its detail and apparent even-handedness, functions as an attempt to foreclose that kind of resistance. It tells us that the surveillance net is inevitable, that the only question is how to make it a little more polite, a little more decentralized. The proper response is to refuse that framing.

We do not need a “domestic intelligence network”; we need law enforcement that investigates crimes based on specific, articulable facts. The threat of terrorism, terrible as it is, does not justify converting every local police department into a station for the national security state. If we do so, we will not have prevented the next attack; we will have laid the institutional groundwork for a permanent infrastructure of suspicion that, like COINTELPRO and CHAOS before it, will inevitably be used against dissenters, journalists, and the politically unpopular. The right fight is not to manage the contradictions of a surveillance state but to dismantle it, to demand the repeal of the USA PATRIOT Act, to defund the Department of Homeland Security’s domestic spying programs, and to recommit ourselves to a society in which the police are servants of their communities, not informants for an intelligence bureaucracy.

Richman’s lead essay is, in this sense, a symptom of our democratic crisis, not a cure. It speaks the language of balance while shifting the fulcrum decisively in favor of the state. I urge its readers to resist its sophistical charm.
