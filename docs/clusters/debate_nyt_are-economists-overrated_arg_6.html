It Depends Which Economists, and For What

The question "are economists overrated?" is too blunt to answer well. The profession is not monolithic. A development economist running a randomized trial in Kenya, a macroeconomist building a DSGE model at a central bank, a financial economist calibrating risk at a hedge fund, and a think-tank pundit writing op-eds about the deficit are all called "economists." Lumping them together produces a debate that goes nowhere.

Where economists deserve their influence: well-identified microeconomic empirical work. The credibility revolution of the past two decades — natural experiments, regression discontinuities, randomized trials — has produced reliable knowledge about questions that matter: what schools work, how taxes affect labor supply, whether health insurance improves health, how cash transfers affect poverty. This is real science, slow and cumulative, and policy is better when it draws on it.

Where economists deserve much less deference: macroeconomic forecasting and grand structural pronouncements. The dismal record of predicting recessions, the public quarrels over multipliers and austerity, the Reinhart-Rogoff fiasco, and the inability of standard models even to incorporate financial intermediation before 2008 all point to a field whose confidence has consistently outrun its evidence. When a senator cites a macroeconomist's forecast that a tax cut will pay for itself, we are not in the realm of science.

Where economists deserve outright skepticism: when they leave their lane. Economic imperialism — applying price-theoretic reasoning to marriage, crime, religion, addiction — has produced genuine insights but also a lot of overreach. The world has problems that aren't optimization problems, and the assumption that everything reduces to incentives misses much of what sociologists, historians, and psychologists understand about institutions, culture, and power.

The Times statistic the prompt mentions — one in 100 articles citing an economist — actually reflects this mixed picture. Some of those citations are exactly right: an economist explaining tax incidence or auction design. Some are misuse: a macro forecaster treated as an oracle. Some are crowd-out: an economist asked about questions that historians or political scientists could answer better.

So: overrated in some venues, properly rated in others, underrated in a few. The reform that actually matters isn't taking economists down a peg in general. It's developing better public literacy — among journalists especially — about which economic claims are well-grounded and which are educated guesses dressed up in math.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Microeconomists utilizing the 'credibility revolution' of empirical trials and natural experiments provide reliable, scientific knowledge that deserves significant policy influence.
  2. 02Macroeconomists have a poor track record of forecasting and structural modeling, suggesting their confidence often exceeds their actual evidence.
  3. 03Economists often overreach when applying price-theoretic reasoning to social and cultural issues that are better understood by other academic disciplines.
  4. 04The public and media need to develop better literacy to distinguish between well-grounded economic science and speculative guesses disguised as technical expertise.
