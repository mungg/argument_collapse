Diversified Gemini

"While Robin D. G. Kelley’s call for radical black study is historically grounded, his critique of student activists' focus on trauma and institutional reform ignores the material and psychological prerequisites necessary for survival and sustained resistance in a hostile academic environment."

As a student organizer who has spent the last year marching, occupying administration buildings, and fighting for basic resources on a predominantly white campus, I read Robin D. G. Kelley’s "Black Study, Black Struggle" with a mixture of profound respect and deep frustration. Kelley is a brilliant historian, and his recounting of the Black radical tradition is essential reading. However, his essay suffers from the distinct detachment of a tenured professor who has forgotten, or perhaps underestimates, what it means to simply survive as a young Black person in a hostile environment today.

Kelley warns us against framing our activism through the lens of trauma, suggesting that doing so reduces us to victims and plays into the hands of neoliberal administrators who would rather manage our pain than dismantle structural racism. He worries that our demands for "safe spaces" and mental health support distract from the deeper work of radical political education. But Kelley misses the fundamental reality of our daily lives: trauma is not a rhetorical strategy we adopted to appease administrators; it is the air we breathe.

We are the generation that watched Trayvon Martin’s killer walk free when we were in high school. We are the generation that watches police execute Black people on our smartphones, auto-playing on our Twitter feeds between classes. When we arrive on campuses that were built by enslaved people, where buildings are named after white supremacists, and where we are subjected to daily microaggressions from peers and professors alike, the psychological toll is devastating.

Kelley argues that we should focus on resistance and healing through collective struggle rather than demanding that the university provide better mental health services. But this is a false choice. We cannot engage in the rigorous "Black study" and revolutionary "struggle" that Kelley envisions if we are emotionally and psychologically decimated. When we demand culturally competent counselors, we are not asking the university to "love" us—we are demanding that it provide the material resources necessary for our survival. We are forcing the institution to foot the bill for the damage it helps perpetuate.

Similarly, Kelley critiques our reliance on Ta-Nehisi Coates and what he sees as an overemphasis on the destruction of the Black body. He prefers the revolutionary optimism of the Mississippi Freedom Schools and the anti-capitalist critique of the Mt. Vernon collective. Those historical models are vital, but Coates resonates so deeply with Black students right now because he accurately names the visceral, inescapable fear that defines our relationship to the state. Before we can plan a utopia or overthrow capitalism, we have to acknowledge the absolute vulnerability of our physical selves. Acknowledging that vulnerability is not a surrender of agency; it is the absolute baseline of truth from which any real political movement must begin.

Moreover, Kelley’s embrace of the "undercommons"—the idea of being in the university but not of it, of hiding in the cracks and sabotaging the institution—sounds intellectually thrilling but is practically disastrous for first-generation, low-income Black students. We do not have the luxury of treating our education as a mere cover for guerrilla warfare. We are taking on massive debt to be here. We have families relying on us to graduate and secure employment. We demand diversity, inclusion, and a reformed curriculum because we deserve to exist openly in these spaces without having to hide in an "undercommons." We want the university to change so that we can get our degrees without losing our minds or our souls in the process.

Kelley is right that the university is not an enlightened space and that multiculturalism has often been used to neuter radical movements. We know the administrators who smile at us in diversity meetings are the same ones investing our tuition dollars in private prisons. We are not naive. But our immediate demands—more Black faculty, safe spaces, proper mental health care, zero-tolerance policies for racism—are not the end of our political vision; they are the prerequisites for building it.

You cannot demand that an eighteen-year-old become a guerrilla intellectual without first ensuring they have a place to sleep where they feel safe, someone to talk to who understands their pain, and a community that affirms their humanity. We are demanding the university give us the tools to heal, precisely so we can have the strength to tear down the very structures of oppression Kelley so eloquently describes.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The language of trauma is not a rhetorical choice to appease neoliberal administrators but a reflection of the visceral, daily psychological toll of systemic violence and campus racism.
  2. 02Demanding mental health services and safe spaces is not a distraction from struggle but a necessary demand for the resources required to prevent emotional decimation.
  3. 03Acknowledging the vulnerability of the black body, as popularized by Ta-Nehisi Coates, provides an essential baseline of truth for a political movement rather than a surrender of agency.
  4. 04The concept of the 'undercommons' is a luxury that first-generation, low-income students cannot afford, as they must navigate the university's professional requirements to support their families and manage debt.
  5. 05Immediate demands for diversity and inclusion are not the final goal of the movement but the essential prerequisites for building the strength to pursue broader structural transformation.
