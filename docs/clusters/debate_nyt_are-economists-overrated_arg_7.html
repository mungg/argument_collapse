Diversified Gemini

"Economists have been granted excessive authority in public policy, leading to a governance model that prioritizes abstract mathematical efficiency over the actual well-being of the working and middle classes."

For decades, the American political establishment has outsourced its moral imagination to the economics profession, with devastating results for the working and middle classes. We have absolutely given economists too much authority, not merely because their forecasting track record is poor, but because their dominant ideological framework has systematically prioritized aggregate efficiency over human equity.

The ubiquitous presence of economists in our public discourse—as highlighted by their outsized representation in the media—has normalized a deeply skewed set of values. In the economic paradigm that has governed Washington since the late 1970s, the ultimate metric of national success is Gross Domestic Product. If GDP is growing, the economy is "healthy," regardless of who captures that growth. This relentless focus on aggregate growth provided the intellectual scaffolding for the deregulation of Wall Street, the crushing of organized labor, and the hyper-globalization of trade. Economists promised that these policies would maximize efficiency, and that the rising tide would lift all boats.

The tide did rise, but it only lifted the yachts. As Thomas Piketty’s recent groundbreaking work, *Capital in the Twenty-First Century*, has painstakingly demonstrated, the natural tendency of these economic systems is to concentrate wealth at the very top. Yet, for years, mainstream economists dismissed concerns about inequality as the whining of those who didn't understand the necessary trade-offs of a dynamic market. They told communities hollowed out by offshoring that they should simply retrain for the knowledge economy, ignoring the social and psychological devastation of localized job collapse.

The "struggles of many Americans" mentioned in the prompt are not a mysterious phenomenon that economists are failing to solve; they are, in many ways, the direct result of the policies that economists championed. By treating economics as an objective science, politicians have been able to present ideological choices as mathematical inevitabilities. "We have to cut social spending," or "We cannot regulate derivatives," were presented not as political preferences, but as the sober laws of economics.

We do not need better economic models; we need to reclaim our political process from the technocrats. We must remember that an economy is supposed to serve society, not the other way around. It is time to reduce the volume of the economists and amplify the voices of the workers, families, and communities who have been forced to live inside their failed equations.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The economic profession's focus on Gross Domestic Product as the primary metric of success ignores the inequitable distribution of wealth and the social costs of growth.
  2. 02Mainstream economic theories provided the intellectual justification for policies like deregulation and hyper-globalization that concentrated wealth at the top while devastating local communities.
  3. 03By framing economics as an objective science rather than an ideological framework, economists have allowed politicians to present subjective political choices as unavoidable mathematical necessities.
  4. 04The technocratic dominance of economists has stifled moral and political imagination, necessitating a shift toward prioritizing the voices of workers and families over failed economic equations.
