"While the neurodiversity movement correctly critiques the limitations of liberal accommodation, its shift toward radical anticapitalist revolution risks erasing and abandoning the most severely disabled individuals who require medical intervention and direct support rather than just political liberation."

Robert Chapman’s essay is a work of fierce intelligence and moral clarity, and I say this as someone who deeply disagrees with its conclusions. I am the mother of a twelve-year-old boy, Leo, who has profound, non-speaking autism. Leo wears a diaper, cannot tell me when he is in pain, and requires constant supervision because he has no awareness of danger. He is also the sweetest human I have ever known, with a laugh that can fill a room. I have been part of local neurodiversity groups, and I have read the foundational texts. And I have watched, with growing dismay, as the movement that promised to center the most marginalized has instead built a politics that, in its current trajectory, threatens to erase my son.

Chapman’s critique of Liberal Neurodiversity is, in many ways, a critique of a movement that never fully included families like mine. The “strengths-based” turn, the celebration of “superpowers,” the public faces of neurodiversity who are articulate, employed, and camera-ready—these have always made me uneasy. My son has no superpower. He is not secretly a savant. He will not be hired by a tech company that values his attention to detail. The narrative of neurodivergent advantage, however well-intentioned, has created a hierarchy within the neurodivergent community, with verbal, high-masking autistics at the top and kids like Leo at the bottom, their needs rendered invisible because they are too “tragic” to fit the pride narrative. I was therefore prepared to cheer Chapman’s call to leave behind liberal accommodationism and fight for “all neurodivergent people, especially those of us who sit at multiple axes of oppression.” Finally, I thought, someone gets it.

And then I read what Chapman actually proposes. Let us set aside the jarring comparison to the Black Panthers—an analogy that, as a white woman, I will let others unpack. The core of the vision is an anticapitalist revolution, a mass movement that demands “the freeing of those of us forcibly incarcerated in prisons and psychiatric hospitals; the end of homelessness through free housing for all; and the end of imperialist wars.” I am a socialist, broadly speaking, and I support all these goals. But I struggle to see how they address the daily, grinding reality of life with a severely disabled child.

If capitalism were abolished tomorrow, would Leo be able to speak? Would he be continent? Would the self-injurious behaviors that leave him with bruises stop? Chapman might respond that these are the wrong questions—that we should not seek to “cure” or “normalize” Leo, but to build a world that fully accommodates him. But what does accommodation mean for someone like him? It means not just quiet hours at the supermarket, but a lifetime of skilled, one-on-one support, speech and occupational therapy, and, yes, medical interventions to manage his co-occurring conditions—epilepsy, severe gastrointestinal distress, and anxiety that, left untreated, makes him miserable. Much of the neurodiversity movement, including Chapman’s essay, is notably silent about treatment. The antipsychiatry distrust of medical models still pulses beneath the surface, manifesting as a subtle but pervasive message that seeking therapies or, God forbid, a cure, is a betrayal of neurodivergent solidarity.

I need to say this plainly: I want treatments for my son. I want research into the biological underpinnings of profound autism, so that we can develop medications or interventions that might ease his suffering. This does not mean I want to “erase” his autistic identity, whatever that means for a boy who has no concept of identity, who cannot tell me if he feels proud or ashamed of being autistic. I want to ease his suffering, and I refuse to let a political movement make me feel guilty for that. When the Spectrum 10k boycott crowd succeeded in pausing a study that might have helped us understand my son’s condition, they celebrated a victory. I wept. It felt like the neurodiversity movement had declared war on families like mine.

Chapman’s vision of Neurodivergent Power, built around collective resistance and cultural institutions, seems designed for neurodivergent people who can organize, who can attend protests, who can write essays. Leo will never do any of these things. He will never participate in consciousness-raising. He will not join a revolutionary party. If his liberation depends on the overthrow of global capitalism, then he will die waiting. What he needs right now—today—are more direct support professionals who are paid a living wage, better medical care that doesn’t dismiss his pain as “just autism,” and a school system that doesn’t try to expel him because his meltdowns disrupt the neurotypical classroom. These are not revolutionary demands, but they are material ones, and they are achievable through the kind of unglamorous, piecemeal advocacy that Chapman dismisses as Liberal Neurodiversity.

Worse, Chapman’s framework risks reinforcing a dangerous double standard. When a verbal autistic adult says “autism is not a disease,” we are expected to listen and affirm. When a parent of a severely disabled child says “my child’s autism is profoundly disabling and I wish it could be cured,” we are called ableist or accused of not accepting our children. The movement must find a way to hold both truths: for some, autism is a way of being that has brought community and a rich inner life; for others, it is a severe neurodevelopmental disorder that brings immense suffering and requires a medical response. The future of neurodiversity depends on its ability to embrace this complexity without policing grief or silencing the call for treatments. Until then, I will fight for Leo in the messy, imperfect world of IEP meetings, Medicaid waivers, and yes, medical research. That is my neurodivergent power.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The movement’s focus on 'superpowers' and 'pride' creates a hierarchy that centers high-functioning individuals while rendering those with profound, non-speaking autism invisible.
  2. 02Radical anticapitalism fails to address the immediate, material needs of the severely disabled, such as one-on-one care, specialized therapy, and medical treatment for co-occurring conditions.
  3. 03The movement’s ideological opposition to medical models and research, such as the boycott of Spectrum 10k, actively harms families seeking to understand and ease their children's suffering.
  4. 04The concept of 'Neurodivergent Power' is exclusionary because it is designed for those capable of political organizing and consciousness-raising, leaving behind those who cannot participate in such activities.
  5. 05A truly inclusive neurodiversity movement must acknowledge that autism can be a profound disability requiring a medical response without labeling parents or caregivers as ableist for seeking a cure or treatment.
