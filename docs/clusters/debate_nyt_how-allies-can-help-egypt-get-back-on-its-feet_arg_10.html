"Egypt's allies should facilitate a credible IMF program backed by bilateral assistance and trade concessions to stabilize the economy, while conditioning this support on transparent democratic and fiscal benchmarks."

Egypt needs cash, and it needs it now. The country's foreign reserves have fallen from roughly $36 billion before the revolution to under $18 billion, the Egyptian pound is under serious pressure, and the budget deficit is projected to exceed 10 percent of GDP. The window for orderly action is closing. The single most useful thing Egypt's allies can do is help conclude a credible IMF program — the roughly $3.2 billion package that has been negotiated and renegotiated for nearly a year — and back it with complementary bilateral assistance from the United States, the European Union, and the Gulf.

An IMF agreement matters less for the headline amount than for the signal it sends. Private investors, ratings agencies, and tourists are all watching for evidence that Cairo has a coherent macroeconomic plan. Without that anchor, every additional billion in Saudi or Qatari deposits simply postpones the day of reckoning. With it, Egypt can credibly restructure energy subsidies that consume roughly a quarter of the budget, broaden a narrow tax base, and gradually move toward a more flexible exchange rate without triggering a run.

Egypt's allies should also do two things they have been reluctant to do. First, the United States should follow through on the modest debt swap President Obama floated last spring — converting up to $1 billion in Egyptian debt into investment in job-creating projects — and Europe should match it. Second, the EU should open serious negotiations on a "deep and comprehensive" trade agreement that goes beyond the existing Association Agreement to liberalize agriculture and services, where Egypt has comparative advantages.

None of this should be unconditional. The current standoff over American NGO workers facing prosecution in Cairo is a reminder that the SCAF and the emerging parliament will test how serious Western donors are about democratic conditionality. Aid should be tied not to specific electoral outcomes — that way lies the cynicism of the Mubarak years — but to transparent benchmarks: protection of civil society, an independent central bank, published budgets, and progress on the subsidy reforms the IMF program will require.

The alternative is drift. Egypt cannot run reserves into the ground indefinitely, and a disorderly devaluation in the middle of a political transition would be catastrophic for both the economy and the democratic experiment. Allies have leverage right now precisely because Cairo needs them. They should use it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01An IMF agreement is essential to signal macroeconomic stability to private investors, ratings agencies, and the tourism sector.
  2. 02Securing an IMF anchor allows Egypt to safely implement necessary structural reforms, such as restructuring energy subsidies and broadening the tax base.
  3. 03The United States and Europe should implement debt-for-investment swaps to stimulate job creation within Egypt.
  4. 04The European Union should negotiate a comprehensive trade agreement that liberalizes the agricultural and service sectors where Egypt holds a comparative advantage.
  5. 05Financial and diplomatic support must be tied to specific benchmarks regarding the protection of civil society and transparent governance to ensure democratic progress.
  6. 06Failure to provide coordinated international assistance will lead to a catastrophic currency devaluation that could derail Egypt's democratic transition.
