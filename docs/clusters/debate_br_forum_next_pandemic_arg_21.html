"The true lesson of the COVID-19 pandemic is that the state must recognize the limits of its moral authority and competence by abandoning coercive technocratic management in favor of individual liberty and decentralized decision-making."

Sheila Jasanoff’s “Public Policy after Pandemic” delivers a devastating and necessary critique of the technocratic hubris that drove the global public health response to COVID-19. She correctly identifies that the architects of pandemic preparedness viewed society not as a complex web of free individuals, but as a machine to be driven, managed, and ordered by experts. However, while Jasanoff brilliantly diagnoses the arrogance of the public health establishment, her proposed cure—“technologies of humility”—fails to escape the very paradigm she critiques. Ultimately, Jasanoff accepts the foundational premise of the biomedical security state: that government possesses the moral authority to micromanage citizens’ lives in the name of collective safety, so long as it does so “humbly.”

The true lesson of the COVID-19 pandemic is not that state experts lacked sufficient civic participation or historical memory when engineering our lives. The lesson is that the centralized state cannot attempt to eliminate biological risk without destroying the social, economic, and moral fabric of a free society.

Jasanoff notes, almost in passing, that securing public health requires “human beings to give up aspects of their liberty in the interests of a common good.” This chillingly casual acceptance of coercion is the very rot at the core of the pandemic response. For nearly two years, under the guise of emergency preparedness, the state has wielded unprecedented, draconian power over the daily lives of citizens. Governors arbitrarily decided which businesses were “essential” and which were to be bankrupted. Public health bureaucracies decreed how many people could gather in private homes. And, most unforgivably, the state locked children out of their schools for over a year, causing catastrophic, generational learning loss and an explosion of mental health crises.

Jasanoff advocates for taking the perspectives of the marginalized into account. But who suffered most from the coercive non-pharmaceutical interventions (NPIs) imposed by the laptop class? It was not the public health modelers who could seamlessly transition to Zoom. It was the working-class families who lost their livelihoods to lockdowns. It was the low-income minority children in urban school districts who were denied an education while private schools quietly remained open. The brutal collateral damage of the pandemic response was not an accident; it was the inevitable result of empowering a centralized technocracy to view society through the single, myopic lens of viral mitigation.

To replace the aggressive “can-do” preparedness with Jasanoff’s vision of the Precautionary Principle is merely to trade an arrogant tyrant for a paralyzed one. The Precautionary Principle, which demands that we act to prevent harm even in the absence of scientific certainty, is a recipe for an endless, rolling state of emergency. If the burden of proof is always on proving that an activity is 100 percent safe, then freedom is no longer the default state of human existence; it is a temporary privilege granted by health bureaucrats. We are seeing this play out right now in the winter of 2021. With the emergence of the Omicron variant, the immediate reflex of many public health officials and politicians has been to slam borders shut and threaten the return of mandates, despite enormous uncertainty about the variant’s actual severity. Precaution easily devolves into a paralyzing fear that justifies permanent restrictions on human liberty.

Jasanoff’s “technologies of humility” propose to fix this by incorporating greater citizen participation and querying whose perceptions were systematically ignored. But in practice, “civic participation” mechanisms orchestrated by the state are almost always captured by activist NGOs and professional managerial elites. They do not represent the silent majority of ordinary citizens who simply want to be left alone to assess their own risks, run their businesses, and raise their families. The public is not a monolith to be “mobilized” by Harvey Fineberg’s wartime command, nor is it a patient to be consulted in Jasanoff’s deliberative forums. It is a vast, decentralized network of individuals capable of making rational trade-offs.

When we look at the few genuine successes of the pandemic, they arose not from state mandates or precautionary ethical committees, but from spontaneous order and voluntary action. The rapid adaptation of businesses to outdoor dining, the massive private-sector mobilization to produce masks and hand sanitizer once price controls and regulations were bypassed, and the astonishingly rapid development of the mRNA vaccines—achieved when the government largely got out of the way of private pharmaceutical companies while providing advance purchase agreements—these were triumphs of decentralized innovation. Conversely, state mandates, such as the fiercely contested vaccine and mask mandates tearing through courts and school boards today, have generated massive political resistance, destroyed institutional trust, and fractured communities.

Humility is indeed what is required, but a radically different kind of humility than what Jasanoff envisions. True humility requires the state to recognize the limits of its own competence and moral authority. It means acknowledging that government cannot guarantee zero risk in a world filled with pathogens. It means accepting that public health officials do not know how to run the economy, do not know how to educate children, and therefore should not have the unilateral power to shut them down.

The proper role of public health in a free society is not to dictate behavior, whether arrogantly or humbly. It is to inform the public, to provide accurate data, to rapidly develop and distribute therapeutics and vaccines, and then to step back and trust free citizens to manage their own lives. We must firmly reject the notion that every new hazard justifies a suspension of our liberties. As the pandemic drags on into another year, it is time to dismantle the biomedical security state and return to the fundamental American principle that our rights are not contingent on the absence of disease.
