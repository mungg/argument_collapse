"The presidential primary calendar should be reformed to lead with Nevada and South Carolina because the current focus on Iowa and New Hampshire excludes diverse electorates and distorts national political priorities."

The argument against Iowa and New Hampshire is usually framed as a matter of race, and with good reason. The United States is not 90 percent white, and neither party can honestly say its presidential nomination process should begin almost exclusively in white electorates. But the deeper problem is not merely that Iowa and New Hampshire are unrepresentative. It is that they are unrepresentative in ways that distort what campaigns talk about.

Iowa elevates ethanol, farm subsidies and rural organizing. New Hampshire elevates anti-tax politics, libertarian-leaning independents and a style of town-hall combat that is admirable but particular. These are legitimate concerns. They are not the country’s only concerns. In the first week of voting, the parties should also hear from black churchgoers in the South, Latino workers in the Southwest, Asian-American suburbanites, union households, immigrants’ children, and urban voters who rarely see themselves reflected in the first contests.

Nevada and South Carolina were added to the early calendar partly to address this imbalance, and they should not be treated as afterthoughts. If anything, they make a better case for going first than Iowa and New Hampshire do. South Carolina tests candidates with black voters, evangelical conservatives, military families and Southern politics. Nevada tests them with Latinos, service workers, unions, libertarians and the politics of growth and immigration. Together, they present a broader picture of modern America than the two traditional opening states.

A reasonable reform would make Nevada and South Carolina the first two contests, alternating which party and which state goes first each cycle, followed by Iowa and New Hampshire. This would not abolish retail politics, because both Nevada and South Carolina are manageable compared with mega-states. It would, however, send an important message: candidates should not be allowed to build “momentum” before facing voters of color.

The early calendar is not just a scheduling detail. It shapes who survives, which issues become urgent and which voters are courted with respect. If America’s parties want nominees who can speak to the whole country, they should stop beginning the process in places that resemble only a slice of it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Iowa and New Hampshire are demographically unrepresentative of the United States, particularly regarding the country's racial diversity.
  2. 02The current early states force candidates to focus on niche regional issues, such as ethanol and anti-tax politics, at the expense of broader national concerns.
  3. 03Nevada and South Carolina offer a more comprehensive test for candidates by including significant populations of Black, Latino, union, and urban voters.
  4. 04Moving diverse states to the front of the calendar ensures that candidates cannot build momentum without first addressing the concerns of voters of color.
  5. 05Nevada and South Carolina are small enough to preserve the benefits of retail politics while being more representative of the modern American electorate.

"The presidential primary calendar should be restructured to start with Nevada and South Carolina to ensure the nominating process reflects the nation's actual social, economic, and racial diversity."

If the question is which states deserve outsize power, the answer cannot be Iowa and New Hampshire. Their voters are engaged and their traditions are charming, but charm is not a democratic principle. A nominating system that begins almost entirely with white electorates tells candidates, donors and journalists which voters matter first. That ordering shapes everything that follows: the issues emphasized, the coalitions courted, and the candidates declared “viable.”

Nevada and South Carolina should move to the front of the line. Nevada, especially, is a better first test for a modern party system. It has Las Vegas and Reno, but also vast rural counties. It has a large Latino population, significant Asian-American communities, black voters, unionized hotel and casino workers, immigrants, retirees, libertarian-leaning western conservatives and voters hit hard by the housing collapse. A candidate who can talk only to corn growers, college towns and New England independents has not yet been tested by America’s actual social and economic complexity.

South Carolina belongs early for a different reason. For Democrats, black voters are not a constituency to be visited after white liberals have narrowed the field; they are central to the party’s national coalition. For Republicans, South Carolina tests evangelical strength, military issues, Southern conservatism and the ability to campaign in a state where ideology, race and class are all visible. It is not perfectly representative — no state is — but it reveals strengths and weaknesses Iowa and New Hampshire often miss.

Putting Nevada first and South Carolina second would not silence Iowa or New Hampshire. They could vote shortly afterward. Nor is this about punishing rural white voters. Rural white voters already have enormous influence in the Senate, the Electoral College and the current primary calendar. The question is whether they should continue to define the opening frame of presidential politics.

A country as diverse as the United States should not begin every nomination season in two of its least diverse states. Start in Nevada. Then go to South Carolina. Let candidates prove they can build a coalition before the pundits start handing out tickets to the next round.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Starting with Iowa and New Hampshire unfairly prioritizes white electorates and shapes the entire campaign's issues and candidate viability around a non-representative demographic.
  2. 02Nevada serves as a superior first test because its diverse population of Latino, Asian-American, and Black voters, along with its mix of urban and rural interests, mirrors modern American complexity.
  3. 03South Carolina should vote early because it forces candidates to engage with Black voters and Southern conservatives who are central to their respective national party coalitions.
  4. 04Moving more diverse states to the front of the line does not silence rural white voters, who already possess disproportionate influence through the Senate and the Electoral College.
  5. 05A diverse primary start requires candidates to prove they can build broad coalitions before the field is narrowed by less representative states.
