Diversified DeepSeek

"Drug enforcement should not merely be devolved to the states, but rather the federal government should lead a unified national policy of decriminalization to dismantle the systemic injustices of the drug war."

Leaving drug enforcement to the states is an improvement over the federal drug war only if the goal is simply to shift the location of coercion. But if the goal is justice, neither the states nor the federal government should be in the business of punishing drug use. We should be talking about decriminalization and the wholesale dismantling of the war on drugs—at home and abroad. Merely devolving enforcement to the states risks localizing the same systemic injustices we have fought for decades at the federal level.

The case for federal retreat is grounded largely in the disastrous consequences of the drug war: mass incarceration, the gutting of the Fourth Amendment, the militarization of local police, and a racial disparity so stark that the system appears designed to target Black and Latino communities. Those who argue that states will handle things better should look at the history of state-level prohibition before federal involvement. It was often just as punitive, and in some cases more so, driven by local moral panics. Sending the drug war back to the states could lead to a Balkanized system where a person faces a mandatory life sentence for a crack pipe in one state but can buy marijuana legally in another. That is not freedom; it is a more chaotic form of tyranny.

Instead, we need a unified national policy of decriminalization—where drug use and possession are treated as health matters, not crimes—combined with strict regulation of sale and distribution to undermine the violent black market. The federal government, with its treaty obligations and interstate commerce power, is actually best positioned to lead this transition. It can deschedule marijuana and other substances from the Controlled Substances Act, cut off funding for militarized drug task forces, and redirect money to evidence-based treatment. The states can then serve as laboratories for regulation, like Colorado, but with a federal floor that guarantees no one is locked in a cage for a substance use disorder.

The Vermont model of emphasizing treatment is a step in the right direction, but it stops short of true decriminalization. If we simply leave enforcement to the states, we are merely relocating the problem. The collective demand should be for an end to drug war enforcement, everywhere.
