"To effectively address Western water scarcity, the agricultural sector must significantly reduce its water consumption through modernization and crop transitions while receiving economic support for these changes."

Agriculture must bear its share of the burden in any honest accounting of how we address Western water scarcity. Farming in arid regions consumes roughly 80 percent of developed water supplies, and many crops grown in California and Arizona are extraordinarily water-intensive. Alfalfa, for instance, requires more water per acre than almost any other crop and often serves export markets rather than domestic food security. We cannot expect urban dwellers and environmental ecosystems to absorb all the pain of scarcity while agribusiness continues largely unchanged.

This doesn't mean hollowing out the agricultural sector. Farms can and should adapt through drip irrigation, drought-resistant seed varieties, and shifts toward less water-intensive crops. The Colorado River Basin, already over-appropriated beyond its actual capacity, cannot sustain current extraction patterns. Farmers who transition away from water-guzzling commodities deserve support and credit for contributing to regional sustainability.

Some will object that farming communities, particularly small family operations, cannot absorb these changes without devastating economic consequences. That concern is legitimate and must inform how we structure transitions. But it cannot become an excuse for inaction. Every year that passes with water allocated as it has been for decades compounds the eventual reckoning. We should be investing now in agricultural transition assistance, buying out the most senior water rights where feasible, and creating clear signals that permanent water waste is not a viable business model.

Environmental restoration also belongs on the agenda. Snowpack is declining, river ecosystems are degraded, and species like the delta smelt are heading toward extinction. Sustainable water policy cannot treat ecological needs as an afterthought to be addressed only after human demands are fully satisfied. The West must find an equilibrium that acknowledges the region's limits rather than perpetually demanding more than the land can provide.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Agriculture consumes a disproportionate 80 percent of developed water supplies, making it impossible for urban and environmental sectors to solve the crisis alone.
  2. 02Farming practices must evolve by adopting drip irrigation, drought-resistant seeds, and shifting away from water-intensive crops like alfalfa.
  3. 03The government should provide financial assistance and buy out senior water rights to help farming communities transition without facing economic devastation.
  4. 04Sustainable water policy must prioritize environmental restoration and ecological health rather than treating them as secondary to human demands.
  5. 05Current water extraction patterns in the Colorado River Basin are unsustainable and require a shift toward acknowledging the region's natural limits.
