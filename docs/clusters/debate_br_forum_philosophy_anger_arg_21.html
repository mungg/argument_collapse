"Agnes Callard’s analysis of anger is limited because it relies on a dyadic, interpersonal framework that fails to account for the unique moral and functional complexities of collective anger directed at structural and historical injustice."

There is a political philosophy dimension to Callard's essay that I find underdeveloped, and I want to press on it.

Callard frames anger primarily as an interpersonal phenomenon — you wrong me, I am angry with you, we negotiate the terms of accountability. This is a plausible frame for dyadic interaction, and her thought experiments (the theft, the apology, the restitution) are well-constructed. But much of the anger that matters most in human life is not dyadic. It is collective, structural, and historical. And the question of whether the arguments Callard makes about interpersonal anger extend to collective anger is not one her essay adequately addresses.

Consider structural injustice. When a system — a legal system, an economic system, an educational system — consistently produces harm for a specific group of people, members of that group are often angry. Not at a specific individual who has wronged them, but at the system itself, and at the individuals who benefit from it. This anger is not well captured by the dyadic framework. There is no single theft, no single apology, no single moment of restitution that would address the accumulated wrongs of centuries of racial oppression, for instance. And there is no specific individual whose accountability relationship with the victim can be established and navigated.

Callard acknowledges this in her discussion of the Holocaust and intergenerational trauma. She describes her grandmother's anger as historically embedded — rooted in an evil so vast that no individual act of amends could touch it. And she draws the conclusion that the victims are morally compromised by the very anger that responds to such evil. This is logically consistent. But it raises a question her essay does not answer: What is the alternative? If the victims of structural and historical injustice cannot permit themselves anger without moral compromise, and they cannot acquiesce without moral compromise, then what form of moral response remains available to them?

The sentimentalist tradition Callard draws on offers a partial answer through the concept of moral indignation on behalf of others — what she calls "indignation." But this merely shifts the problem: the person who is indignant about injustice they did not directly suffer is still angry, still exposed to the logic of permanent grudges and vengeance, just at one remove. And if the injustice is structural and historical, the remove does not help, because the structural harm is ongoing.

I want to suggest that collective anger at structural injustice operates by different rules than interpersonal anger, and that Callard's arguments, while compelling in the dyadic case, do not straightforwardly transfer. When a community is angry about systemic oppression, the anger functions differently — it is partly a expression of political identity, partly a mechanism of mobilization, partly a form of testimony. It is not trying to restore a balance between two individuals; it is trying to change a system. And the relationship between this form of anger and the grudge-vengeance logic Callard describes is not straightforward.

None of this saves the Stoic project. But it suggests that the moral phenomenology of anger is more varied than Callard's essay admits, and that the crisis she identifies — the impossibility of responding rightly to being treated wrongly — may apply differently to different kinds of anger in different contexts. The person who is angry about a specific betrayal by a specific person is in a different moral situation than the person who is angry about centuries of racialized violence. Treating these cases as equivalent obscures more than it reveals.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Structural injustice lacks the specific individuals and discrete acts of restitution that define the interpersonal accountability model Callard uses.
  2. 02Callard identifies a moral crisis for victims of historical evil but fails to provide a viable alternative form of moral response for those who can neither acceptably acquiesce nor acceptably remain angry.
  3. 03Collective anger functions as a tool for political identity and systemic mobilization rather than a mechanism for restoring a personal balance between two parties.
  4. 04The logic of grudges and vengeance applies differently to ongoing systemic oppression than it does to one-off personal betrayals.
  5. 05Treating interpersonal and structural anger as morally equivalent obscures the distinct phenomenological realities of different types of injustice.
