The Grace That Breaks the Cycle

Agnes Callard paints a world where morality is a closed circuit of wrongdoing and vengeful response, offering no exit except a muddled corruption. Her argument is rigorous and, within the secular moral framework she adopts, almost airtight. But the Christian tradition has long recognized precisely this dark logic and insisted that it can only be broken from outside the human moral agent: by the grace of forgiveness. To the Christian, Callard’s crisis is real but not final, because the story of salvation is one in which God absorbs the infinite debt of sin and offers a way for humans to forgive without being corrupted. Her argument, therefore, is a powerful negative apologetic: it shows why a world without divine grace is indeed trapped in the economy of eternal grudge.

Callard’s Argument for Grudges depends on the permanence of the moral gap created by sin. “What I did will always diverge from what I ought to have done,” she writes, and so the reason to be angry persists forever. Christian theology agrees that no finite restitution can undo the ontological reality of a wrong. The Apostle Paul writes, “the wages of sin is death”—a debt that can never be repaid by merely human effort. This is why the Christian account of atonement centers on the crucifixion: Christ, the innocent one, takes upon himself the punishment that would otherwise be visited upon the guilty. On the cross, the moral gap is not erased but transferred and exhausted. In theological terms, God’s justice is satisfied so that mercy may flow. For the human victim who participates in this new life, the anger that was once anchored in an eternal reason finds a new anchor: the belief that the ultimate reckoning has been placed in God’s hands. “Vengeance is mine; I will repay, says the Lord” (Romans 12:19). This is not a suppression of anger but a redirection of it toward the one who can justly bear it.

Callard considers only two options: clinging to anger or acquiescing in evil. But forgiveness, in the Christian sense, is a third and distinct act. It does not deny that the wrong was wrong; it does not paper over the injustice. Instead, it makes a promise to the offender: I will not hold this against you, I will not seek revenge, and I will work toward reconciliation insofar as it depends on me. This promise is made possible precisely because the forgiver trusts that God will deal with the wrong perfectly, either through the repentance of the offender or through final judgment. The victim does not become complicit in evil but rather participates in a divine economy that does not require her to be the agent of retribution. This is not a philosopher’s fiction; it is a way of life attested by countless Christians who have forgiven enemies—from Corrie ten Boom forgiving the Nazi guard who tormented her to the families of the Emanuel Nine forgiving the white supremacist who murdered their loved ones. Such acts often strike secular observers as incomprehensible, even morally suspect. But if Callard is right that the alternative is permanent moral corruption, then the capacity to forgive is anything but suspect—it is liberation.

The Argument for Revenge as the mechanism of moral accountability is also transformed by the Christian lens. Callard says that revenge “educates” by turning the wrongdoer’s principle upon themselves. But this education is coercive and, as she admits, imposes a heavy psychological cost on the avenger. The Christian proposal is that the ultimate education happens through the disclosure of sin in the light of God’s love. The cross itself is the paradigm: it reveals human sin in its horror—the execution of the innocent—and simultaneously offers forgiveness. The goal is to bring the wrongdoer to genuine repentance, not simply to impose suffering. And repentance is a real change: the person who repents is no longer the same moral agent who committed the crime. In the Christian understanding, through repentance and faith, the sinner is “a new creation; the old has passed away.” This is not a mere verbal apology; it is a metaphysical transformation that Callard’s static model of moral identity cannot accommodate.

Callard’s story of her grandmother and the German girl is heartbreaking, but it illustrates the limits of a purely secular morality. Her grandmother, shaped by the Holocaust, could not see the German child as separate from the enemy. Her anger was rational on Callard’s terms, yet it perpetuated a barrier that kept even the innocent locked out. The Christian response would not be to dismiss the grandmother’s anger but to point to a God who can bear that anger and who commands love of enemies—not a sentimental love that forgets the evil, but a costly love that refuses to be defined by it. The grandmother might still have struggled, and fail, to forgive. But the grace of God would mean that her struggle was not without hope, and that her moral standing did not depend on perfectly balancing anger and acceptance.

Callard’s essay, in the end, is a profound testimony to the inescapable tragedy of moral life under the conditions of sin. It forces the reader to see that our own righteousness, left to itself, becomes twisted into the very violence it condemns. What she fails to see is that this is precisely the condition that the Gospel addresses. If there is no God, then Callard is correct: being wronged always corrupts us. But if there is a God who forgives, then the cycle can be broken not by our own efforts, but by the infusion of a grace that makes possible a genuine, non-corrupting moral response. That does not make anger disappear, but it places it within a larger story that ends not in eternal grudge but in restoration.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'Argument for Grudges' is resolved by the Christian doctrine of atonement, where Christ exhausts the moral debt of sin, allowing victims to release anger to a higher divine authority.
  2. 02Forgiveness is a distinct third option beyond anger or acquiescence that allows a victim to acknowledge wrongdoing without becoming morally corrupted by the pursuit of retribution.
  3. 03The Christian view of repentance allows for a metaphysical transformation of the wrongdoer, challenging Callard’s assumption that the moral identity of the offender remains static and forever tied to the past act.
  4. 04The capacity to forgive enemies, as seen in Christian practice, serves as a liberation from the 'economy of eternal grudge' that secular morality cannot escape.
  5. 05Callard’s tragic view of moral life serves as a powerful negative apologetic that demonstrates the human necessity for a divine source of grace to break the cycle of violence and resentment.
