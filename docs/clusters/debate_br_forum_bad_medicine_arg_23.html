"While Marcia Angell correctly identifies the corruption of academic medicine by the pharmaceutical industry, her proposed reforms are insufficient because they fail to address the root cause: a patent-based legal and economic framework that prioritizes monopoly profits over public health."

Marcia Angell’s searing indictment of the pharmaceutical industry and its academic handmaidens is a welcome corrective to years of complacency, but it does not go nearly far enough. The problem is not primarily that individual researchers take consulting fees or that medical schools cut self-serving deals. Those are symptoms. The disease is a legal and economic framework that transforms medicines into monopoly-protected commodities and then wonders why the system is pervasively corrupted. As long as patents grant companies decades-long control over the fruits of publicly funded research, and as long as drug prices are determined by what the market will bear rather than by therapeutic value, no amount of arm’s-length grant-making will rescue medical science.

Angell herself gestures toward this deeper problem when she critiques the Bayh-Dole Act and the proliferation of patents on early-stage discoveries. She correctly observes that the drug industry has largely outsourced innovative research to universities and start-ups, concentrating instead on “me-too” drugs and late-stage development. But her proposed reforms—non-exclusive licensing and the elimination of personal payments to researchers—still operate within the same patent-centric paradigm. Even if every clinical trial were conducted with monastic purity, the results would still be funneled into a system where the primary goal is not to advance public health but to secure blockbuster exclusivity. That is why we get a cascade of statins, antidepressants, and proton-pump inhibitors that are marginally different but enormously profitable, while genuinely needed antibiotics for resistant infections languish because they offer poor returns on investment.

The root of this distortion is the patent itself. A patent does not merely reward invention; it creates a temporary monopoly that allows the holder to charge prices far above the cost of production. In medicine, that means that decisions about what to research, how to test, and what to reveal are all shaped by the imperative to maximize the value of that monopoly. It should surprise no one that negative clinical trial results are suppressed, that studies are designed to make the new product look good, and that marketing budgets dwarf research budgets. The entire enterprise is oriented, rationally, toward protecting and extending a patent’s revenue stream. Angell’s call for arm’s-length research support, while honorable, would leave this engine of perverse incentives untouched.

What we need instead is a fundamental delinkage of the financing of research and development from the price of drugs. One promising model, advocated by economists and activists like James Love, is a system of publicly funded “prizes” for important therapeutic advances. In such a system, government or international bodies would offer large cash rewards for the development of drugs that meet specific health needs—effective new antibiotics, for example, or treatments for neglected diseases. The reward would be conditioned on the drug being made available at generic prices immediately. Clinical trials could be publicly financed and conducted by independent networks, removing the commercial bias that Angell so meticulously documents. The prize system would harness the ingenuity of the private sector while severing its connection to monopoly pricing and marketing-driven research agendas.

Angell’s essay is a powerful call to cleanse academic medicine. But unless we simultaneously dismantle the patent monopolies that make the corruption so lucrative, we will find ourselves in a perpetual cycle of scandal and reform. The recent passage of the Affordable Care Act, which includes funding for comparative effectiveness research, hints that policymakers are beginning to see the need for independent evidence. But that is only a small first step. The real challenge is to reclaim medicines as public goods. Angell reminds us that Jonas Salk refused to patent the polio vaccine, asking, “Could you patent the sun?” Today the sun is fenced off, and patients are burning in the glare. It is time to tear down the fences, not just polish the gates.
