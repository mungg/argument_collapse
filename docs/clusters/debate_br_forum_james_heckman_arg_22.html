"While early childhood intervention is valuable, it is an insufficient solution to inequality because it focuses solely on individual skill development while ignoring the structural labor market issues and power imbalances that drive economic disparity."

James Heckman presents a compelling moral case for giving every child a fair start, but his policy program rests on a highly individualistic diagnosis of inequality. In his account, the growing divide between skilled and unskilled is a story of diverging family environments and inadequate child-rearing. The solution is to fix the supply side: equip disadvantaged children with the cognitive and non-cognitive tools to compete. What is entirely missing from his analysis is the demand side of the labor market. No amount of preschool enrichment will create good jobs for all those newly skilled workers. Without tackling the structure of employment, wages, and power, early childhood programs risk becoming a social Band-Aid on a hemorrhaging economy.

Heckman’s own data points toward this gap, but he does not follow the thread. He notes that the real wages of high school graduates have risen relative to dropouts, and that the economic return to skill has increased. Yet the median wage for a male high school graduate in 2011 was still lower in real terms than in 1973. The bottom has fallen out not because workers lacked non-cognitive traits like perseverance, but because decades of deindustrialization, union busting, and trade policy destroyed the stable, well-paying manufacturing jobs that once anchored working-class communities. A man with no more than a high school diploma could once support a family at a steel mill or auto plant. Today, even college graduates face a labor market saturated with precarious service-sector work. The skills gap is real, but it is dwarfed by a power gap.

Heckman’s glowing review of early intervention experiments also ignores the macroeconomic context in which those programs operated. The Perry Preschool children entered the labor force in the 1970s and early 1980s, an era when strong union density and a still-robust industrial base meant that even those with modest skills could find family-wage employment. The treatment group showed lower crime and higher employment, but part of that success reflected the availability of jobs for which they were now better prepared. In today’s polarized labor market, a young man who has learned to persist and cooperate may still find only a minimum-wage job with no benefits and an erratic schedule. The personal traits Heckman extols cannot overcome the fact that the U.S. economy has systematically shifted risk onto workers and concentrated rewards at the very top.

If we are serious about social mobility, we need to complement early childhood initiatives with policies that restructure the labor market. That means a higher minimum wage indexed to inflation, strengthened collective bargaining rights, and industrial policy aimed at reviving domestic manufacturing and green energy jobs. It means public investment in infrastructure that puts money directly into communities where disadvantaged families live, raising both incomes and hope. The casino windfall study Heckman cites, where increased family income improved parenting, actually undermines his insistence that giving families more money “is not the same as enhancing the quality of the environments.” That study found that the income boost led to better parenting and reduced disruptive behavior. Money, when it relieves toxic stress and material deprivation, does indeed improve the family environment.

Heckman frames his argument as predistribution versus redistribution, but this is a false dichotomy. Redistribution of income and wealth—through progressive taxation, a robust social safety net, and universal benefits—can itself be predistributive by ensuring that all families have the minimum resources to provide a stable, nurturing home. His preferred metric of “parenting quality” is deeply correlated with income. Chronic financial insecurity breeds depression, conflict, and harsh, inconsistent discipline. Simply giving poor families more money, as numerous cash-transfer studies from the Earned Income Tax Credit to the recent experiments with guaranteed income suggest, improves child outcomes. That is not to say that targeted enrichment programs are useless; they have a role. But to cast redistribution as ineffective while celebrating the casino example is a contradiction.

Ultimately, Heckman’s vision is too narrow. It concentrates our attention on the inner lives of poor children and the parenting practices of their mothers, while leaving untouched the economic arrangements that produce poverty and inequality in the first place. Social mobility is not just about climbing a ladder of skill; it is about ensuring that the ladder is not missing rungs and that the destination provides a decent life. Early childhood programs are one worthwhile piece of a just social order, but they are no substitute for a broad movement to rebalance economic power.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The decline in working-class prosperity is primarily caused by deindustrialization and the erosion of labor unions rather than a lack of cognitive or non-cognitive skills among workers.
  2. 02The success of historical early intervention programs like Perry Preschool was dependent on a more robust macroeconomic environment that provided stable, well-paying jobs for graduates.
  3. 03Improving social mobility requires demand-side labor market reforms, such as raising the minimum wage and strengthening collective bargaining, to ensure that increased skills actually lead to decent employment.
  4. 04Direct income redistribution is a form of predistribution because financial security reduces toxic stress and enables the stable, nurturing family environments that Heckman advocates for.
  5. 05Focusing exclusively on the parenting practices of the poor distracts from the systemic economic arrangements that produce poverty and inequality in the first place.
