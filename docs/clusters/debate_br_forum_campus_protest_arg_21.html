Diversified Gemini

"To effectively challenge the university's neoliberal and racial architecture, student activists must move beyond demands for representation and cultural competency to form structural alliances with campus labor movements."

Robin D. G. Kelley’s "Black Study, Black Struggle" correctly identifies the modern university as a corporate entity masquerading as a post-racial haven. He is right to urge student activists to look past the admissions brochures and see the university as a "cog in the neoliberal order." However, while Kelley touches on the "PhD precariat" and the exploitative nature of university endowments, his analysis largely leaves out the most critical lever of power within the institution: labor. If students genuinely want to challenge the racial and economic architecture of the university, they must move beyond demands for representation and cultural competency, and forge deep, structural alliances with campus labor movements.

When campus protests erupted in the fall of 2015, the most prominent demands across the country centered on making the environment more hospitable for students of color. As Kelley notes, this meant demanding more diverse faculty and staff, better mental health resources, and mandatory cultural-competency training. But we must examine how the corporate university actually responds to these demands.

When students demand more faculty of color, how does a neoliberal administration meet that quota? They rarely create new, well-compensated, tenure-track lines. Instead, they hire Black and brown scholars as adjuncts—the very "PhD precariat" Kelley mentions—paying them poverty wages with no job security, no benefits, and no institutional power. The university gets to boast about its newly improved diversity metrics on its website, while functionally expanding its underclass of exploited academic labor.

Similarly, the demand for "intense 'inclusion and belonging' training" has spawned a lucrative cottage industry of diversity consultants. Universities are perfectly willing to pay thousands of dollars to outside firms to conduct seminars on unconscious bias because such measures protect them from liability and generate good PR. But while the administration funds these seminars, it simultaneously fights tooth and nail to bust the unions of its dining hall workers, groundskeepers, and janitorial staff—workers who are overwhelmingly Black, brown, and immigrant.

Kelley warns that the programmatic adoption of diversity and multiculturalism "vampirized the energy of a radical movement." He is absolutely correct, but the antidote is not simply retreating to an "undercommons" of radical graduate students reading Fanon in an outdoor seminar. The antidote is recognizing that the university is a workplace. The struggle against campus racism cannot be divorced from the struggle against capitalist exploitation, because they are fundamentally the same mechanism.

If the Black Liberation Collective and other student groups want to unveil the university’s deeply embedded structures of inequality, their demands must center the material conditions of the workers who keep the university running. A demand for a safe space is easily accommodated by carving out a room in the student union; a demand for a living wage for all campus employees, comprehensive healthcare, and the abolition of anti-union practices directly threatens the university’s bottom line.

Some student groups have begun to make this connection. As Kelley points out, the protesters at UNC Chapel Hill demanded a minimum wage of $25 per hour for workers and an end to ties with sweated labor. This is the blueprint. But too often, the national conversation remains fixated on symbolic victories—renaming buildings, removing statues, and policing the syllabi. These symbolic fights are important, but when they become the horizon of the movement, they allow the university to make cosmetic changes while maintaining its exploitative labor practices.

Furthermore, aligning with labor forces students to confront their own class position. Even as marginalized individuals within the institution, students at elite universities are being trained to enter the professional-managerial class. The danger of the "cultural competency" framework is that it trains students how to be benevolent managers, teaching them how to politely interact with diverse populations without ever questioning why they get to be the managers while the local residents get to clean their dorms.

Kelley advocates for "guerrilla intellectuals" who use university resources to instruct themselves and self-radicalize. But true radicalization happens in solidarity with those who are actively fighting for their livelihoods. If campus activists want to build a movement that is truly transformative, they must walk out of the diversity seminar and onto the picket line. They must understand that a university will never be a "home" for marginalized students as long as it operates as a sweatshop for marginalized workers.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The corporate university co-opts diversity demands by hiring faculty of color as exploited, low-wage adjuncts rather than providing secure tenure-track positions.
  2. 02Administrative investments in diversity consultants and inclusion training serve as a PR shield that obscures the university's active efforts to suppress the unions of its predominantly Black and brown service workers.
  3. 03Focusing on symbolic victories like renaming buildings allows the university to maintain its exploitative economic bottom line while appearing socially progressive.
  4. 04The cultural competency framework risks training students to become benevolent members of the professional-managerial class rather than challengers of the class hierarchy.
  5. 05True radicalization and institutional transformation occur when students recognize the university as a workplace and align their struggle with the material demands of campus laborers.
