"To successfully reclaim the language of freedom, the American left must integrate an anti-imperialist framework that recognizes domestic liberty and foreign policy as structurally inseparable."

Solidarity Begins at the Border

The most arresting move in Aziz Rana's essay is his insistence that domestic freedom and foreign policy are not separate questions. He is right, and he is right at exactly the moment when this connection is most painfully visible. As I write, the United States is providing billions of dollars in military aid and diplomatic cover for an Israeli bombing campaign that has killed something approaching twenty thousand Palestinians in Gaza, the majority of them women and children, in the two months since October 7. The American government calls this a defense of freedom. People across the Global South — and increasingly, young Americans — see it as the continuation of a long imperial pattern in which the United States projects violence abroad while invoking liberty. Rana's framework helps us understand why this dissonance is not accidental but structural.

Let me extend his argument by pressing on three points where I think the implications are even sharper than he allows.

First, the moral economy of American empire. Rana describes how the rents extracted from American global primacy after 1945 fed a domestic culture in which corporate well-being could be experienced as public well-being, and in which individual prosperity could feel like a personal achievement rather than a structurally subsidized outcome. He notes that these rents have been declining for ordinary Americans even as the imperial project continues. What he does not quite say, but should, is that this creates a profound legitimacy crisis for the existing arrangement. American empire is being maintained at enormous cost — fiscal, moral, geopolitical — at precisely the moment when the material payoff to ordinary Americans has shrunk to near nothing. The trillion-dollar defense budget is no longer buying domestic peace, and increasingly it is purchasing only resentment and disillusionment.

This is a political opening, and the anti-war movement that has emerged around Gaza in recent weeks suggests how broad that opening might be. Polling indicates that majorities of Democratic voters and pluralities of Americans overall now favor a ceasefire and disapprove of unconditional support for Israel's military campaign. The generational divide is even more striking: voters under thirty oppose current US policy by overwhelming margins. This is not because young Americans have suddenly become more knowledgeable about international relations. It is because the legitimating story — that American power abroad serves freedom at home — has lost its grip on people who have come of age watching that power produce Iraq, Afghanistan, Libya, and now Gaza, while at home they cannot afford rent or health insurance.

Second, the connection between imperial violence and domestic policing. Rana mentions this in passing but does not develop it. The militarization of American police forces over the past two decades has been driven in large part by surplus military equipment, training, and personnel returning from foreign wars. The 1033 program transferring military equipment to local police, the proliferation of SWAT teams and armored vehicles, the use of counter-insurgency doctrine in urban policing — all of these reflect the seepage of imperial methods back into domestic governance. The Black Lives Matter movement has named this connection repeatedly; activists in Ferguson in 2014 documented military equipment used against protesters and connected it to the war in Iraq. The line from Fallujah to Ferguson is not metaphorical. It is institutional.

This means that the project of demilitarizing American foreign policy and the project of demilitarizing American policing are not separate. They are the same project at different scales. A freedom politics that names this connection — that says ending the global war on terror and ending the militarized occupation of Black communities are part of the same struggle — gains both moral coherence and political power. It also avoids the false choice that establishment Democrats often present, in which one can oppose Republican excess at home while accepting the bipartisan imperial consensus abroad.

Third, the global solidarities that an anti-imperial freedom politics can build. Rana mentions the New International Economic Order, Manley, Nyerere, and the broader Third Worldist project that flourished briefly in the 1960s and 1970s before being crushed by the debt crisis, structural adjustment, and the consolidation of neoliberal globalization. This history matters not just as a historical reference point but as a living tradition. The contemporary Global South is again articulating demands that echo the NIEO — for climate reparations, for debt cancellation, for sovereign control over natural resources, for an international financial system that does not systematically transfer wealth from poor countries to rich ones. South African President Cyril Ramaphosa's recent invocation of the genocide convention against Israel, Brazilian President Lula da Silva's call for a multipolar global order, the BRICS expansion this year, the African Union's accession to the G20 — these are not random developments. They are the emergence of a new round of Global South political assertion, and they create opportunities for American left movements to build solidarities that domestic politics alone cannot generate.

The connection to domestic freedom is direct. If American voters can come to understand that the same Pentagon budget that bankrolls forever wars also crowds out their child care, their health care, their housing — and if they can understand that the same imperial logic that detains migrants at the border also disenfranchises Puerto Ricans and props up dictators in countries those migrants are fleeing — then a coherent left freedom politics becomes available. Without that international dimension, domestic reformism keeps running into the wall of the security state, which absorbs resources and political authority that could otherwise go elsewhere.

Where I would push Rana further is on the question of Israel-Palestine specifically. He mentions it briefly and rightly notes the dissonance between American support for the bombing campaign and the freedom-language Washington deploys. But the Palestinian question is, I would argue, the central test of any contemporary anti-imperial freedom politics. Palestine has functioned for decades as the limit-case of liberal universalism: the place where the language of human rights, self-determination, and freedom is most loudly affirmed in principle and most systematically denied in practice. American politicians who happily invoke Ukraine's right to self-determination cannot bring themselves to affirm Palestinian self-determination on remotely comparable terms. The double standard is not a hypocrisy to be corrected through better messaging; it reflects structural commitments of American foreign policy that no amount of rhetorical adjustment will dissolve.

A left freedom politics worthy of the name has to be willing to break the Palestinian taboo — to insist that Palestinian self-determination, Palestinian sovereignty, and Palestinian freedom from occupation and siege are non-negotiable. This is happening, in the streets, on campuses, in unions that have begun passing ceasefire resolutions, in writers and intellectuals refusing to participate in cultural institutions that whitewash the bombardment. It is the most vital anti-imperial mobilization of this generation, and any project to reclaim freedom-language for the left should begin from it.

Rana's essay points in this direction. The next step is to follow the pointing.
