"Aziz Rana’s proposal for reclaiming freedom is insufficient because it identifies the history of empire as the primary cause of American individualism rather than the structural logic of capitalism itself."

Aziz Rana’s “A Different Freedom” is a serious and learned attempt to understand the peculiar career of “freedom” in American life. Its centering of settler colonialism and empire is a valuable corrective to narratives that treat the U.S. simply as a defective liberal democracy. Yet for all its critical ambition, the essay remains trapped within a liberal reformist horizon that ultimately blunts its emancipatory edge. By locating the pathology of American freedom in the history of empire rather than in the structural logic of capitalism itself, Rana offers a political project that is at once too sweeping in its cultural aspirations and too modest in its material ones. A more incisive analysis would recognize that the zero-sum, individualistic freedom he critiques is not an accident of American geography or even of imperial power; it is the ideological expression of a system of generalized commodity production, and any effort to build a solidaristic freedom must aim at the abolition of that system, not its kinder, more democratic management.

Rana’s narrative traces the roots of a corrosive freedom-talk to the settler project: the availability of land—conquered and expropriated—enabled a vision of independence for white men that did not require interdependence with others. The frontier, he argues, acted as a safety valve, smoothing class contradictions and making government regulation feel like an imposition rather than a necessity. Empire in the twentieth century performed a similar function, with U.S. global primacy subsidizing a domestic middle class and entrenching the idea that economic security was a matter of private bargaining rather than public provision. This is an important story, and it rightly points to the material underpinnings of ideology. But in treating empire as the independent variable, Rana risks naturalizing capitalism as a neutral background condition that was only corrupted by its association with racialized expansion.

Capitalism, however, is not a neutral engine of prosperity that was hijacked by settlers; it is, from its inception, a system built on the separation of direct producers from the means of subsistence and their reinsertion into relations of competitive market dependence. The very experience of freedom Rana idealizes as a potential alternative—collective self-rule through democratic control of the workplace and society—is systematically foreclosed by the daily operation of the market. Under capitalism, firms that do not prioritize profitability and the extraction of surplus value are disciplined by capital flight, credit ratings, and hostile takeovers. Even the most well-intentioned co-ops and union shops must compete in a global market that compels cost-cutting and labor discipline. The “experiments in linking freedom and democracy” that Rana celebrates will remain fragile islands so long as they are subject to the tides of capital accumulation.

Rana’s discussion of the New Deal era and its collapse illustrates the problem. He notes that the solidaristic moment emerged when settler expectations broke down and white workers began to see common cause with Black and other workers. Yet the New Deal order he implicitly admires was also a project of capitalist stabilization, brokered by corporate-friendly liberals who prioritized macroeconomic growth and limited the scope of workplace democracy to collective bargaining over wages and conditions, not control over investment or production. It was, in crucial respects, a class compromise that left capitalist property rights essentially intact. When the post-war boom waned and corporate America went on the offensive, that compromise proved reversible. The lesson is not that we need a more robust social democracy buttressed by anti-imperialism; it is that any freedom regime that leaves the investment decisions and profit motive of capital in place is contingent and precarious.

The contemporary moment bears this out starkly. Rana observes that Americans are disenchanted with corporate power and that the right’s Trumpist variant of freedom is fragile because it cannot deliver on its promises. But here he stops short of the necessary conclusion: the problem is not just that the mid-twentieth-century bargain with business is dead, but that any bargain with business is a contradiction in terms when it comes to genuine self-rule. The real content of freedom under capitalism has always been the freedom of capital to move, to invest or disinvest, to hire and fire—a freedom that enforces a despotism over the vast majority. Democrats and social democrats who promise to tame capitalism through regulation and redistribution are not so different from the right-wing libertarian in the grand scheme; both accept the fundamental premise that the major allocative decisions of society should remain in private hands, driven by the imperative of profit. The difference is one of degree, not kind.

An authentic left freedom project, therefore, must be anti-capitalist at its core. That means more than supporting the PRO Act and improved voting rights, however worthy those fights are. It means building political capacity to challenge the very institution of private ownership of the means of production. It means developing the organizational forms and the insurgent power to resist capital’s structural veto, whether through general strikes, occupations, or the creation of dual power institutions that prefigure a society beyond the market. Rana’s call for institution-building is right, but the institutions must be revolutionary, not reformist. They should aim at creating a new economy from the ground up—worker-controlled enterprises that federate into a democratic plan, community land trusts that decommodify housing, platforms for participatory budgeting that challenge the state’s allocation of resources.

Excising the anti-capitalist core from the left’s freedom vision also has political consequences that Rana’s own essay implies but does not fully own. If freedom is framed as a matter of democratic participation within a capitalist framework, the inevitable failures of reform will be blamed on the “lack of political will” or the cunning of the right, rather than on the systemic limits of capitalism itself. This sets the stage for periodic bouts of progressive enthusiasm followed by demoralization. Only by naming the adversary—capitalism, not merely imperial or settler culture—can the left equip people to understand the setbacks not as personal failings or electoral flukes, but as the predictable resistance of an owning class to any serious challenge.

Rana’s essay is a valuable contribution to the debate over freedom, and his insistence on the material basis of ideology is a welcome antidote to the idealist messaging consultants. But it does not go far enough. It provides a radical genealogy only to retreat into a reformist politics that treats the symptoms—racialized individualism, exclusionary bellicosity—rather than the disease. The disease is the rule of capital itself, and until the left makes its abolition the centerpiece of its freedom talk, it will remain trapped in the long shadow of the very empire Rana so incisively describes.
