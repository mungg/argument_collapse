"Creationism should only be considered controversial when it is presented as a scientific alternative to empirical evidence rather than as a source of religious or moral meaning."

Biblical creation should be controversial only when it is presented as an account of nature on the same footing as science. As a matter of worship, identity or poetry, Genesis is not a scandal. Millions of intelligent people read it devotionally, allegorically or liturgically. The controversy begins when a text written in an ancient religious tradition is asked to settle empirical questions about the age of the earth, common descent, fossils, genetics and cosmology.

On those questions, the evidence is not evenly balanced. Radiometric dating, the geological column, observed speciation, comparative anatomy, the distribution of species, and especially modern genetics all point to an old earth and to evolution by common descent. The scientific story is incomplete in many details, as all live science is, but its central claims are supported by converging lines of evidence. Creationism, in its young-earth or anti-evolution forms, does not generate comparable predictions or survive the same tests. That is why courts have repeatedly treated attempts to teach “creation science” or intelligent design in public-school science classes as religious advocacy rather than science.

Still, it is not hard to see why Genesis remains powerful. It offers a moral universe: human beings are made with purpose, the world is not an accident, and suffering and disorder have an intelligible place in a larger drama. Scientific accounts often feel emotionally thinner. “The universe is 13.7 billion years old” and “humans share ancestry with other primates” may be true, but they do not by themselves tell people how to live, whom to love, or what to do with death.

The mistake is to force one kind of explanation to do the work of the other. Science is not a complete philosophy of life, but it is the best method we have for answering questions about physical origins. Scripture may shape meaning for believers, but it should not be granted a veto over evidence. Creationism is therefore not controversial because it is religious. It is controversial because, when literalized into biology or geology, it asks faith to overrule inquiry.

"While finding moral and spiritual meaning in creation narratives is not controversial, it is and should be controversial to treat those narratives as valid aesthetic substitutes for corroborated scientific evidence."

Belief in biblical creation is, in one sense, utterly unremarkable. Gallup has polled Americans on origins for three decades, and the share who say God created humans in their present form within the last 10,000 years has held stubbornly around 45 percent. A view held by nearly half the country cannot be called fringe. In that demographic sense, Virginia Heffernan's confession is no more surprising than a neighbor saying grace before dinner.

What made her essay controversial was not its content but its framing. Heffernan didn't argue that Genesis is literally true; she said she preferred its "narrative" to what she portrayed as the dreary, unreliable storytelling of science. That move — choosing an origin story on aesthetic grounds, and dismissing evolutionary biology as just another yarn — is what drew the outcry, and rightly so. There is a real difference between a person of faith who accepts evolutionary biology and reads Genesis as theology (the position of most mainline Christians, the Catholic Church since Pius XII, and plenty of working scientists), and someone who treats a 150-year-old, massively corroborated body of evidence as a competing fairy tale. The first is not controversial. The second is, because it confuses categories Pinker was trying to keep straight.

Why are people drawn to Genesis? Because origin narratives do work that data cannot. They tell you who your people are, why suffering exists, what you owe your parents and children, where the moral weight of the cosmos lies. Common descent from a population of African hominins, however true, does not console anyone at a funeral. Scripture's pull is not ignorance; it is the human need for stories that locate us morally. Conversely, people are drawn to the scientific account because it is shockingly powerful: it predicts antibiotic resistance, dates fossils to within a percent, and gets the genetic distances among species to line up with the morphological tree drawn before DNA was discovered. That is not "a story"; it is a converging body of independent measurements.

So the answer is yes and no. It should not be controversial to find meaning in Genesis, any more than to find it in the Bhagavad Gita or in Lucretius. It should be controversial — and remain so — to claim that preferring such a narrative excuses you from taking the evidence about the natural world seriously. Heffernan got grief because she blurred that line, and the line is worth defending.

"Creationism is and should remain controversial because it attempts to present a non-falsifiable theological narrative as a legitimate scientific alternative in the public and educational spheres."

To call creationism “controversial” is to acknowledge a fracture in our public conversation, not merely a disagreement among private citizens. A personal belief in a literal six-day creation, a young earth, or the special creation of kinds is, in a pluralistic society, unremarkable. What elevates creationism from private conviction to public controversy is its insistence on being treated as a competing scientific explanation, one that should be taught alongside or in place of evolutionary biology in public-school classrooms. That move transforms a theological claim into an empirical one, and it is there that the controversy ignites.

The reason is straightforward. Science is not a catalog of facts but a method—a self-correcting process of testing hypotheses against observable evidence, peer review, and provisional consensus. By that standard, modern evolutionary theory is one of the most robustly supported frameworks in human knowledge, drawing on genetics, paleontology, developmental biology, and geology in a coherent explanatory web. Creationism, by contrast, begins from a revealed text and interprets data to fit that text. Its central claims—a global flood, a 6,000-year-old earth, the absence of common descent—are contradicted by mountains of evidence from multiple independent disciplines. When a Virginia Heffernan writes of being drawn to the poetic power of Genesis, she is making an aesthetic and spiritual confession; she is not offering a testable model of biogeography. The outrage that followed her essay stemmed not from her private faith but from a sense that a prominent writer was blurring that crucial boundary at a moment when science literacy already feels fragile.

Yet the controversy also persists because the scientific story, for all its explanatory power, does not satisfy every human longing. Origin narratives like Genesis give us intentionality, moral order, and a cosmos shaped by a purposeful mind. They speak to the “why” of existence in a register that a description of mutation and natural selection cannot. For many, that existential resonance is more immediate than the patient, impersonal mechanisms of science. The draw is not ignorance; it is the hunger for meaning.

The real question, then, is not whether creationism should be controversial in a person’s inner life—we all navigate the world with a mix of reason, intuition, and inherited story. It is whether it should be controversial as a matter of public knowledge and educational policy. As long as its proponents insist on inserting a non-falsifiable, scripture-grounded narrative into the science curriculum, the answer will remain yes. That tension is not an insult to faith; it is the natural friction between two different ways of knowing, each making claims on the same public square.

It does not tell us we are the pinnacle of creation; it tells us we are primates who survived through adaptation.

The scientific method provides a reliable, testable framework for understanding the physical universe. Evolutionary theory does not command the consensus of the global scientific community because it tells a soothing story, but because it is corroborated by an overwhelming convergence of fossil, genetic, and geological evidence. We can fully appreciate the literary and cultural power of ancient origin myths without surrendering the hard-won intellectual triumphs of the Enlightenment.
