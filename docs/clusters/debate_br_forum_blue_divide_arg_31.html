"The Democratic Party's shift toward a bold economic agenda is an elite-driven strategy that fails to build a durable coalition because modern political behavior is driven by negative partisanship and cultural identity rather than material self-interest."

In their meticulously argued essay, Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson offer an account of the Democratic Party’s transformation that is, above all, a story of elite agency. Facing the structural pressures of a “density tax” and a sprawling U-shaped coalition, Democratic elites, they argue, deliberately and skillfully pivoted toward a robust economic agenda. They curated a set of policies—broad-based spending, hidden taxation, muted racial appeals—designed to bind their coalition together while avoiding the dangerous shoals of cultural division. This is a powerful narrative of political strategy, but it is one built on a foundation of rationalism that is everywhere contradicted by the actual, chaotic behavior of the Democratic Party’s voters. The great unexamined variable in this story is the decoupling of political behavior from material self-interest by the churn of negative partisanship and the modern media environment. The Democrats may have executed a clever elite strategy, but they are preaching economic gospel to a congregation that is no longer in the pews for the sermon. They are there because they are terrified of the other church.

Hacker and Pierson themselves note that “so far there is little evidence that the Democrats’ efforts to expand their coalition through ambitious policy have yielded significant political results.” They observe that the expanded child tax credit had little apparent impact on voting behavior. They acknowledge that the media landscape makes negative perceptions of Democrats “so hard to shake.” These are not minor caveats to be acknowledged and then set aside. They are fundamental evidence that challenges the entire premise of a policy-driven coalition. If the boldest anti-poverty program in a generation can expire without any electoral consequence for the party that let it happen, then the material feedback loop that the authors’ theory relies upon is broken. The machine is running, but the belt isn’t turning any gears.

The reason is that political identity is now primarily a social and affective attachment, not a transactional one. The affluent suburban voters who have swung toward the Democrats did not do so after a careful cost-benefit analysis of the American Families Plan versus the Trump tax cuts. They did so because the Republican Party has become, in its style and its substance, repulsive to them. The college-educated professionals at the core of this shift are repelled by right-wing populism, by the disinformation, by Christian nationalism, by the cruelty of family separation policies, and above all, by the omnipresent, vulgar, and norm-shattering spectacle of Donald Trump and his imitators. They are voting on democratic norms, on basic decency, and on a cultural identity that sees the contemporary GOP as an existential threat to a cosmopolitan, educated way of life. In this affective calculus, the specific contours of Biden’s industrial policy are a rounding error.

Conversely, the less-affluent voters of color who form the other pole of the coalition are not sticking with the Democrats primarily because of the care-economy provisions tucked into Build Back Better. They are voting for survival against a party whose dominant faction openly traffics in replacement theory and whose policies on policing, voting rights, and immigration are perceived as direct material and physical threats to their communities. Negative polarization is the gravitational center of the Democratic coalition, not shared policy aspirations. The fear of what the other party is doing and saying is so overwhelming that it simply drowns out the policy signal, no matter how loud Democrats try to make it.

If this is the case, then Hacker and Pierson’s entire strategic calculus needs to be inverted. The danger is not that a too-bold economic agenda will activate a latent tax revolt among suburbanites and shatter the coalition. The danger is that the coalition is held together entirely by anti-MAGA glue, and once the proximate threat of authoritarianism recedes or simply becomes normalized, the coalition will dissolve because there is nothing genuinely constructive binding it. Elites thinking they are building a durable New Deal for a multiracial metro era are actually just managing a temporary, defensive huddle. The strategy of organizing cultural issues “out” of politics betrays a misunderstanding of what actually drives voter loyalty. In a low-trust, high-polarization environment, the cultural conflict *is* the politics. The economic agenda is just a thin layer of retail policy on top of a raw, identity-based, anti-Republican mobilization.

This is not a counsel of despair for progressive economics. It is a call for a different kind of politics. If Democrats want to build a truly durable coalition, they must stop thinking of material policy as a quiet substitute for cultural conflict and instead understand that it must be embedded within a larger, publicly articulated story about who is fighting for you and against whom. The child tax credit couldn’t just be a quietly generous check; it needed to be a weapon wielded against the predators of late-stage capitalism, named and shamed in a public drama. A policy so removed from a broader narrative of power and conflict is too easily erased in the noise. The Democrats’ best-laid economic plans are currently being drawn in sand, because the tide of identity-based negative polarization is what brought their voters to the beach in the first place, and it is the only tide that matters.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The lack of electoral impact from major initiatives like the expanded child tax credit proves that the material feedback loop between policy and voter loyalty is broken.
  2. 02Affluent suburban voters are joining the Democratic coalition as a visceral, affective rejection of Republican extremism and vulgarity rather than out of support for specific redistributive policies.
  3. 03Voters of color remain in the coalition primarily as a defensive measure against the perceived existential threats posed by the GOP's racial and social rhetoric.
  4. 04Negative polarization acts as the primary 'glue' for the Democratic base, meaning the coalition is a temporary defensive huddle rather than a constructive, policy-aligned movement.
  5. 05By attempting to organize cultural issues 'out' of politics, Democratic elites ignore the reality that identity-based conflict is the primary driver of voter engagement in the current media environment.
  6. 06For economic policy to be politically effective, it must be framed as a weapon in a larger narrative of power and conflict rather than as a technocratic or quiet material benefit.
