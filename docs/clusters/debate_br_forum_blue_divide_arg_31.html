"The Biden administration's economic agenda is not a genuine shift toward working-class redistribution, but rather a cynical expansion of the progressive administrative state and a patronage network for the affluent professional-managerial class."

Jacob S. Hacker and Paul Pierson’s "The New Blue Divide" reads less like an objective analysis of the Democratic Party and more like a press release from the Biden administration's domestic policy council. The authors marvel at how a party reliant on affluent suburbanites could embrace a "broad and costly" redistributive agenda, framing the near-passage of the Build Back Better (BBB) mammoth as a noble crusade for the working class. What they fail to realize, or deliberately obscure, is that the Biden economic agenda is not a repudiation of the "Brahmin Left" but its ultimate, cynically packaged triumph. The new Democratic agenda is not working-class redistribution; it is an unprecedented transfer of wealth and power to the progressive administrative state, green-energy cronies, and the professional-managerial class, disguised in the rhetoric of equity.

Hacker and Pierson continually assert that economic issues, not cultural ones, are the core of the party's agenda. They attempt to artificially separate the two. But in the modern Democratic Party, the economic is cultural, and the cultural is economic. You cannot separate the massive spending bills from the progressive ideology that dictates how those funds are distributed.

Take the much-lauded investments in green energy and infrastructure. The authors claim these are designed to generate high-quality jobs for those without college degrees. In reality, the regulatory frameworks surrounding these bills are laced with ideological mandates. The Biden administration has weaponized the federal bureaucracy to ensure that grants and subsidies flow through a filter of Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI) requirements, environmental justice metrics, and stringent union mandates that freeze out non-union, small-business contractors. This isn't "predistribution" to the working class; it is the creation of a vast patronage network that enriches left-leaning NGOs, progressive consultants, and politically connected green-energy firms—the very denizens of the affluent metro areas the authors describe.

The authors seem baffled as to why the white working class—and increasingly, the Hispanic working class—continues to drift toward the GOP, despite the Democrats supposedly throwing trillions of dollars at them. They assume working-class voters simply haven't noticed the Democrats' largesse, perhaps due to a "noisy" right-wing media landscape. This condescending view misses the forest for the trees. Working-class voters see clearly that the inflationary impact of the American Rescue Plan destroyed their purchasing power. They see an administration determined to forcibly transition the economy away from reliable fossil fuels, jeopardizing the heavy industries and transportation networks that blue-collar workers rely on, all to satisfy the climate anxieties of affluent coastal elites.

When the Biden administration attempts to ban gas stoves, mandates electric vehicle production, or halts liquid natural gas exports, it is using the power of the state to wage economic warfare on the cultural preferences and livelihoods of Middle America. Hacker and Pierson call this an "embrace of active regulation"; voters call it federal overreach by an arrogant elite.

Furthermore, the authors' treatment of the tax code is highly revealing. They note that the Democrats protected their affluent suburban base by adhering to Biden’s pledge not to raise taxes on anyone making under $400,000. So who pays for this massive expansion of the state? The authors vaguely point to "corporations and the very rich," but they ignore the reality that corporate taxes are ultimately borne by workers in the form of lower wages and by consumers in the form of higher prices.

Even more glaring is the Democrats' obsession with reinstating the State and Local Tax (SALT) deduction during the BBB negotiations—a priority Hacker and Pierson conveniently omit. The push to repeal the SALT cap was a naked demand for a massive tax cut for wealthy homeowners in high-tax blue states like New York and California. The fact that this was a red-line issue for many metro Democrats shatters the illusion that the party is deeply committed to progressive redistribution. They are perfectly willing to redistribute wealth, as long as it isn't coming out of the pockets of their own donor class.

Hacker and Pierson’s depiction of the "density divide" is accurate in its geography, but flawed in its moral framing. They view the concentration of wealth and GDP in deep blue metro areas as proof of Democratic superiority, while dismissing the vast, rural, and exurban heartland as "left behind." But this divide is largely a product of a bifurcated economy that the Democratic Party actively cultivates: a hyper-financialized, tech-driven coastal economy that produces massive inequality, alongside a deliberate strangulation of the resource-extraction, agricultural, and traditional manufacturing sectors that sustain the rest of the country.

The "New Blue Divide" is not a story of a party struggling nobly to uplift the working class despite its wealthy base. It is the story of a party that has figured out how to use the coercive power of the federal government to subsidize the cultural and economic preferences of its elite, metropolitan constituents, while trying to buy off the working class with temporary tax credits. The working class isn't buying it, and Hacker and Pierson’s academic apologetics cannot hide the reality of this massive bait-and-switch.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Democratic Party's economic policies are inseparable from its cultural ideology, using regulatory frameworks and DEI mandates to funnel resources to progressive consultants and NGOs.
  2. 02Working-class voters are rejecting the Democratic agenda because its inflationary spending and green-energy mandates actively jeopardize their purchasing power and traditional livelihoods.
  3. 03The party's commitment to redistribution is hypocritical, as evidenced by its protection of affluent suburbanites from tax hikes and its pursuit of the SALT deduction cap repeal for wealthy blue-state donors.
  4. 04The 'density divide' is not a natural phenomenon but a result of Democratic policies that favor a hyper-financialized coastal economy while strangling the resource and manufacturing sectors of the heartland.
  5. 05The use of temporary tax credits is a 'bait-and-switch' tactic designed to buy off the working class while the federal government subsidizes the preferences of the metropolitan elite.
