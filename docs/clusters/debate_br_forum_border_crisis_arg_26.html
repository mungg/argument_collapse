Diversified DeepSeek

"While Lea Ypi correctly identifies the injustices of class-based migration policies, her argument fails by reducing migrants to passive victims of structural forces rather than recognizing them as individual agents with a fundamental right to freedom of movement."

Lea Ypi’s “The Real Border Crisis” is a powerful call to refocus the migration debate on class and power, and it lands many justified blows on the hypocrisy of a world that sells citizenship to the wealthy while condemning the poor to drown in the Mediterranean. Yet for an essay that proudly invokes the Enlightenment, it is curiously hostile to the individual. Ypi subsumes the migrant into a grand narrative of class conflict, treating people as vectors of structural forces rather than as agents with their own diverse hopes, projects, and rights. In doing so, she risks building a new orthodoxy that, while opposing the chauvinism of the right, reproduces its own form of collectivist erasure. A truly liberal response to the border crisis begins not with class but with the freedom of the individual, and it demands that we take seriously the moral claim of every person to be treated as an end, not as a symbol of global injustice.

Ypi’s foundational move is to equate migration with involuntary displacement. “People move not because they want to leave,” she writes, “but because conditions in their homes have been made unlivable.” There is truth in this: war, climate collapse, and economic devastation force many to flee. But to treat all migration as a symptom of a broken order is to deny the legitimate aspiration of countless individuals who choose to move in search of a better life, even when their basic survival is not at stake. A young software developer from Lagos who wants to work in Berlin, a Mexican nurse who sees better pay and professional respect in Canada, a Filipino student who dreams of studying in Tokyo—these are not mere pawns of neoliberal policy. They are people exercising a fundamental liberty that has been a driver of human progress for centuries. By collapsing all movement into a story of victimhood, Ypi inadvertently echoes the right’s frame that only the most desperate “deserve” to migrate. She does not intend this, but her rhetoric of forced movement leaves the door open to a paternalistic distinction between good, helpless migrants and bad, ambitious ones.

This is where the Enlightenment legacy, properly understood, cuts in a different direction. Nathan the Wise is indeed a cosmopolitan, but he is also an individual who thinks for himself, outside his assigned identity. Ypi’s essay, for all its talk of Enlightenment courage, remains trapped in the logic of group membership. She wants to expand citizenship and political representation, to make the state more inclusive of migrants. But the liberal tradition from Kant to Rawls has a more radical idea: that the primary moral unit is the individual, and that borders are a prima facie violation of individual liberty unless they can be justified to each person affected. The burden of proof lies on those who would restrict movement, not on those who would move. The state does not have a natural right to exclude; it exercises a power that must be justified by showing that the exclusion serves some weighty purpose that could be accepted by the excluded themselves.

Ypi is right that the current justification—a cultural defence of “our way of life”—is thin and exclusionary. But she does not take the next step of arguing that any system of border control is morally problematic. Instead, she says the problem is not “the simple existence of borders, or the presence of borders that are more open or more closed,” but the class character of the state. This is a dodge. If the state is inherently captured by capital, and if borders are an instrument of class discipline, then the only consistent position is to oppose the state’s right to control movement altogether, rather than to reform it. A liberal, by contrast, can argue that a well-ordered state, genuinely constrained by a commitment to individual rights, might have some authority to regulate entry under conditions of global justice—provided it respects the basic claims of non-members. But such a view must start from the premise that the default is freedom, not restriction.

Ypi’s class lens also misses the distinctive nature of the persecution and discrimination that many migrants face. A Rohingya woman fleeing genocide is not just a “poor person” caught in a class conflict; she is a member of a persecuted ethnic group, and her need for asylum is a matter of basic civil and political rights, not just economic redistribution. The international refugee regime, flawed as it is, exists precisely because the liberal order recognised that some harms require individual determination of status, not just collective struggle. When Ypi dismisses the distinction between economic migrants and asylum seekers as missing the point, she threatens to undermine the legal protections that the most vulnerable have fought hard to secure. A class-first narrative risks flattening these crucial differences into a generalised demand for open borders, which, while perhaps morally coherent, is politically infeasible and institutionally reckless.

A truly liberal alternative would insist on the right to freedom of movement as a core human right, but it would also recognise that its instantiation requires careful institutional design. It is not enough to decry the commodification of citizenship; we must dismantle the legal barriers that make citizenship a scarce good to begin with. That means moving towards a world where basic rights—the right to work, to health care, to education—are not tied to a single national passport. It means expanding the reach of international human rights law so that states have positive obligations to non-citizens on their territory. It means experimenting with transnational citizenship models, such as regional freedom of movement agreements that guarantee portable rights. The European Union’s free movement regime, for all its faults, is a real-world example of how borders can be thinned without being abolished, and it has not destroyed the welfare state in the process.

Above all, a liberal response must respect the individual migrant as a person with a story, not just a statistic in a global class war. Ypi’s Ben the Lame and her own childhood memories of Albanian migrants are poignant, but they are used in the service of a structural argument that leaves little room for the messy, contradictory choices that real people make. Some migrants are victims; some are adventurers; many are both. Some want to assimilate and join their new society as full members; others maintain transnational lives and do not seek citizenship. A just policy must be responsive to this diversity. It cannot be built on the fantasy that all workers of the world will unite under a single political banner. It must start with the hard work of respecting the individual conscience and building institutions that give each person a fair chance to flourish, whether they stay or move.

The Enlightenment that Ypi invokes was, at its best, a rebellion against all forms of assigned identity—class, nation, religion. To be faithful to that heritage, we must not replace one group vocabulary with another. We must see the migrant not as a representative of a class but as a human being who, like Nathan, simply wants to live a life of dignity and meaning. The real border crisis is that we have not yet found the moral imagination to welcome him as such.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Equating all migration with involuntary displacement ignores the legitimate aspirations and personal agency of individuals who choose to move for reasons beyond mere survival.
  2. 02The essay prioritizes collective class identity over the Enlightenment principle that the individual is the primary moral unit whose liberty should not be restricted by borders without rigorous justification.
  3. 03Focusing exclusively on class conflict flattens the distinct moral and legal claims of refugees fleeing specific ethnic or political persecution, potentially undermining essential protections for asylum seekers.
  4. 04A truly liberal response should focus on dismantling legal barriers to make citizenship less of a scarce commodity and decoupling basic human rights from national passports.
  5. 05Effective migration policy must respect the diverse motivations and messy, contradictory choices of real people rather than subsuming them into a singular narrative of global class war.
