"Compensation systems for victims of mass violence must be designed to prioritize victim autonomy and self-determination rather than bureaucratic convenience."

Any compensation system must respect victim autonomy and self-determination. Too often, we design policies that feel good to lawmakers and advocates while ignoring what survivors themselves say they need. Before establishing government programs or distributing donations, we should ask the victims—and then actually listen to the answers.

Research and testimony from 9/11, Virginia Tech, Fort Hood, and other mass violence events consistently reveals common themes. Victims want recognition that their suffering matters, not bureaucratic check-cutting. They want input into decisions affecting their lives, not formula-driven allocations made by distant administrators. They want speedy resolution, not multi-year battles with uncertain outcomes. And they want long-term support, not one-time payments that evaporate as soon as media attention fades.

The One Fund Boston's allocation controversies—where administrators made unilateral decisions about "worth" and "need"—violated these principles. A compensation system designed with victim input would establish transparent criteria, include survivor representatives in governance, and build in appeals processes for disputed decisions.

Perhaps most importantly, compensation systems should treat victims as partners in recovery rather than passive beneficiaries. Allowing some choice in how funds are used—somerecipients might prioritize medical care while others need educational support for children—respects the diversity of circumstances that standardized formulas cannot capture.

Government has a role, but that role should be enabling rather than controlling. Taxpayer dollars can fund compensation while community oversight ensures that those dollars serve human needs, not bureaucratic convenience.

  3. 03A fair system must include transparent criteria, survivor representation in governance, and formal appeals processes for disputed decisions.
  4. 04Treating victims as partners by allowing them choice in how funds are used respects the diverse individual needs that standardized formulas fail to capture.
  5. 05The government's role should be to provide funding while allowing for community oversight to ensure the system serves human needs.
