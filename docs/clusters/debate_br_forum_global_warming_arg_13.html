## Questioning the Frame: Can We, or Should We?

The title of Kerry Emanuel's essay poses a question: "Can We Stop Global Warming?" But before we can answer whether we can stop it, we must ask whether the question itself is coherent. What exactly would "stopping" global warming mean? And is this the right goal to be pursuing?

One interpretation of "stopping" would be returning the climate to some pre-industrial baseline—bringing CO2 concentrations back down to 280 parts per million, restoring ice sheets, halting sea level rise. This is clearly impossible in any practical sense. The carbon we have already emitted will remain in the atmosphere for centuries; the warming already locked in by past emissions will play out over decades. We cannot un-burn the fossil fuels that powered the industrial revolution.

A more modest interpretation would be stabilizing climate at some level warmer than today but below the worst-case projections. Even this is enormously ambitious. Global carbon emissions have been growing at roughly 3 percent annually over the past decade. A stabilization scenario would require not merely reducing emissions but changing the trajectory entirely—turning a rising curve into a declining one. To what level? By when? These questions have no easy answers.

Perhaps the more useful question is whether we should stop global warming at all. Emanuel's essay presents the case for concern, but it also notes that there will be winners as well as losers—that some regions will benefit from warmer temperatures and that some adaptation is possible. If warming is modest—say, two or three degrees—might the net effects actually be positive for many people, at least in the near term? This is not a comfortable question to ask, but it is one that policy makers must grapple with.

Consider the geography of climate impacts. High northern latitudes—Siberia, Canada, Scandinavia, Alaska—will warm most dramatically and may see significant agricultural benefits. These are also regions with small populations and abundant resources. The tropical and subtropical regions that will warm least in absolute terms but most relative to their current climate are among the most densely populated on Earth. The asymmetry of impacts is not random; it reflects the fact that wealthy countries are generally located at higher latitudes and have more resources to adapt, while poor countries are generally located at lower latitudes and have fewer resources. This has implications for how we think about both mitigation and adaptation.

I do not raise these points to argue that climate change is nothing to worry about. The risks are real, and the ice cores, hurricane records, and sea level measurements Emanuel cites are genuinely alarming. But we do ourselves a disservice by framing the debate in terms that obscure rather than illuminate the choices before us. "Stopping" climate change is not a policy goal; it is a fantasy. Preventing dangerous climate change is a policy goal, and it requires us to think carefully about what "dangerous" means and for whom.

One thing that strikes me about the climate debate is how confident everyone seems to be about very uncertain things. The IPCC provides probability ranges for temperature rise, but these ranges reflect model uncertainties, not true probabilistic knowledge. We do not know how the climate will respond to doubled or tripled CO2; we have theories and models that give us estimates, but these have been revised repeatedly and will be revised again. Emanuel himself acknowledges that "our understanding of the physics of ice under pressure is poor." If we cannot model ice sheets accurately, how can we project sea level rise a century hence?

This uncertainty should temper our confidence in both predictions and prescriptions. A warming of four degrees might be manageable; it might trigger feedbacks that make it eight degrees. A carbon tax at $50 per ton might be enough to stabilize emissions; it might be far too small. We need policy that is robust to uncertainty—policy that performs reasonably well across a range of possible futures rather than assuming we know exactly which future will occur.

Risk management is the appropriate framework here. We should invest in reducing emissions because the downside risk of unmitigated climate change is severe, and we should invest in adaptation because some warming is now inevitable. We should also invest in research to reduce the fundamental uncertainties about how the climate system works. And we should be humble about our ability to predict the future and honest about what we do not know.

Emanuel concludes that we might be inclined to make sacrifices "as an insurance policy against potentially harmful surprises." I agree with this framing, but I would add a caveat: insurance is only valuable if the premium is proportionate to the risk. We should be wary of proposals that impose enormous costs today on the basis of speculative scenarios about the future. The appropriate level of investment in mitigation is a function of both the expected damages and the probability that they will occur. High-end scenarios should receive attention, but they should not crowd out consideration of more moderate outcomes.

The climate debate will not be resolved by better science alone. We need to think more clearly about what we are trying to achieve and why. Stopping global warming is not a realistic goal; preventing dangerous climate change is. But "dangerous" is a normative concept, and its definition involves choices about values, risk tolerance, and distributional justice that science alone cannot adjudicate. These are the real questions before us, and they deserve more serious treatment than the binary framing of "can we stop it?" suggests.
