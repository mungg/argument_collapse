"Society should prioritize economic growth and adaptation over aggressive greenhouse gas mitigation because the costs of stopping global warming are staggeringly high relative to the benefits, especially given the scientific and economic uncertainties involved."

Kerry Emanuel is a scientist of considerable repute, and his essay makes an honest effort to present the state of climate knowledge without the histrionics that so often plague this debate. He even acknowledges, with admirable frankness, that a warming world would produce not only losers but winners: lower heating bills, longer growing seasons in high latitudes, and the fertilising effect of carbon dioxide on crops. Yet having opened the door to a measured discussion of the balance of costs and benefits, he quickly closes it again, retreating to the conventional litany of projected harms and the ritual invocation of uncertainty as a reason for inaction—though he inverts the standard logic by suggesting that uncertainty should spur us to act as a kind of insurance.

It is worth pausing over that insurance analogy, because it reveals the intellectual sleight of hand at the heart of much climate advocacy. When an individual buys fire insurance, she makes a conscious choice based on a known risk profile, a clearly defined premium, and a contractual guarantee of a payout under specified conditions. None of these conditions holds for climate policy. The risk profile is subject to enormous and irreducible uncertainty; the premium is not a few hundred dollars a year but a potential restructuring of the global economy; and the payout is neither guaranteed nor even clearly defined—it consists of an avoided future state in which some bad things might have happened but others might not. A more apt analogy would be a homeowner being asked to take out a second mortgage to retrofit her house against an asteroid strike that might occur sometime in the next century, on the advice of a group of experts who have a strong professional interest in the existence of the threat.

The sceptical case does not require one to believe that greenhouse gases have no effect on climate, nor that the planet is somehow immune to human intervention. The case is subtler and more robust: it holds that even if one accepts the full panoply of IPCC projections at face value, the costs of attempting to “stop” global warming—which in practice means dramatically slowing the rate of increase of atmospheric carbon dioxide—are so staggeringly high relative to the benefits that a rational society would choose a different path, one centred on adaptation and continued economic growth. And this conclusion holds even before one examines the considerable reasons to doubt that the IPCC’s projections are as settled or as alarming as the public has been led to believe.

Start with the science. Emanuel lists the things that are known and the things that are disputed. Yet the list of knowns is less impressive than it seems. That greenhouse gases are rising and that the earth has warmed over the past century is not in dispute. That sea level is rising and Arctic ice is retreating is also well documented. But these observations tell us nothing on their own about the sensitivity of the climate to a doubling of CO2—the central parameter on which all catastrophic projections depend. The IPCC’s range for that sensitivity, 2.5 to 9°F (roughly 1.5 to 4.5°C), has remained essentially unchanged for over two decades, despite enormous advances in computing power and modelling. That this wide range persists is not a sign of a mature science converging on certainty; it is a sign that the key feedback processes—clouds, water vapour, ocean circulation—are still poorly understood and that models are still being tuned to reproduce the past rather than being subjected to the kind of out-of-sample testing that would allow genuine prediction.

The observational record, meanwhile, has not been kind to the high end of the sensitivity range. The earth warmed by about 0.6°C over the twentieth century, a value that falls well within natural variability on many timescales and that is consistent with a relatively low climate sensitivity. The satellite record of tropospheric temperatures, the most comprehensive global dataset we have, shows a warming trend that is at the very bottom of the climate-model envelope, or even below it. The Antarctic ice sheet, far from collapsing, appears to be thickening in its interior, as Emanuel notes. The widely publicised “hockey stick” temperature reconstruction of the last millennium has been subjected to devastating methodological critiques, and the evidence that the current warmth exceeds anything in the past thousand years is far more fragile than the IPCC’s summary would suggest. This is not to say the planet is not warming or that man has no role; it is merely to say that the plausible range of future warming is significantly lower than the media narrative routinely suggests, and that the probability of the catastrophic tail that motivates much of the insurance chatter is correspondingly smaller.

Now consider the economics. The numbers most widely invoked to justify aggressive action come from the Stern Review, published at the end of 2006. Stern concluded that the costs of inaction could equal 5 to 20 percent of global GDP, while the costs of stabilising greenhouse-gas concentrations at a safe level would be only about 1 percent of GDP. This convenient asymmetry has been celebrated by activists and politicians. Yet Stern’s analysis has been roundly rejected by most serious climate economists. Its extreme results derive almost entirely from an extraordinary choice of discount rate: by valuing the welfare of future generations at nearly the same rate as our own, Stern effectively assumes that a dollar of consumption a century from now is almost as valuable as a dollar today. Under such a low discount rate, any long-term problem, no matter how small, can be made to appear catastrophic. Under the more conventional discount rates used in public policy analysis—rates that reflect the observed return on capital and the real behaviour of savers and investors—the case for drastic near-term mitigation collapses. The optimal policy, according to most integrated assessment models, is a very gradual reduction in emissions, one that does not begin to bite for several decades and that never imposes a net cost approaching that of the proposed Kyoto-style caps.

Even if one sets aside the discount rate controversy, there is a deeper problem. The resources that would be consumed by a massive decarbonisation effort are resources that cannot be used for other pressing human needs. The billions of people in the developing world who still lack clean water, adequate nutrition, and access to electricity are not concerned about a hypothetical rise in sea level a century hence. They need cheap, reliable energy now—and for the foreseeable future, that means fossil fuels. To demand that China and India stunt their growth in the name of climate stabilisation is to ask the world’s poor to pay the heaviest price for a problem caused overwhelmingly by the historical emissions of the rich. Nor is this a problem that can be solved by subsidies for solar panels; even the most optimistic projections for renewable energy see it playing only a supplemental role for many decades to come. The idea that we can “stop” global warming without condemning billions to continued poverty is a fantasy.

The more realistic path is adaptation. Human beings have always adapted to climate change. We adapted to the end of the last ice age, to the Medieval Warm Period, to the Little Ice Age. Today, wealthy societies adapt to heat waves with air conditioning, to rising seas with coastal defences, to crop stress with new varieties and irrigation. The lesson of history is that economic growth confers adaptive capacity. A richer world will be far better able to cope with a gradually changing climate than a poorer one, even if that wealth was created, in part, by burning the very fuels that cause the problem. The appropriate policy response, therefore, is not to crush economic growth under the weight of carbon reduction targets, but to accelerate development, invest in basic research that might make future energy technologies genuinely competitive, and address the most glaring vulnerabilities—such as the cyclone-prone coasts Emanuel knows well—through better forecasting, warning systems, and infrastructure. This is not a counsel of despair. It is a recognition that the question, “Can we stop global warming?” is a red herring. The real question is: how can we build a world resilient enough to prosper whatever the climate does? That is a project within our intellectual and material means, and it does not require us to sacrifice the aspirations of billions on the altar of uncertain model projections.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The insurance analogy used to justify climate action is flawed because climate risks lack the known profiles, defined premiums, and guaranteed payouts of actual insurance.
  2. 02Persistent wide ranges in climate sensitivity projections suggest that key feedback processes are poorly understood and that models are not yet reliable for genuine prediction.
  3. 03Observational data, such as satellite temperature records and Antarctic ice thickening, suggest that future warming may be at the lower end of projections rather than the catastrophic high end.
  4. 04Economic justifications for aggressive action, like the Stern Review, rely on unnaturally low discount rates that do not reflect real-world capital returns or investor behavior.
  5. 05Mandating rapid decarbonization unfairly burdens the developing world by depriving them of the cheap fossil fuel energy necessary to escape poverty.
  6. 06Economic growth is the most effective way to increase adaptive capacity, making a wealthier world better equipped to handle climate changes than a poorer one constrained by emissions targets.
