"The Democratic Party’s shift toward progressive economic policy will remain fragile and politically ineffective unless it is accompanied by a serious revival of working-class organizations, particularly labor unions, that can translate material benefits into durable political loyalty."

The phrase “build power through policy” appears near the end of Hacker and Pierson’s essay, and it is the hinge on which the whole argument turns. Democrats tried to enact policies that would materially improve life for working- and middle-class people. Yet, as the authors note, there is little evidence so far that these efforts have significantly expanded the party’s coalition. The expanded child tax credit reduced poverty but did not create a visible political constituency strong enough to save it. Infrastructure and climate investments are substantial but have not obviously transformed the party’s image. The question is why.

The answer is that policy alone rarely builds power. Organizations build power. Policy can strengthen organizations, reward constituencies, and create new expectations, but only if there are institutions capable of translating material benefits into durable political loyalty. The Democratic Party’s economic turn will remain fragile unless it is joined to a serious revival of working-class organization, especially labor unions.

Hacker and Pierson give progressives, think tanks, social movements, and policy experts proper credit for shifting Democratic economic thinking after the Great Recession. But the social base of that shift is still too thin. The New Deal and Great Society were not simply collections of federal programs. They were linked, however imperfectly, to unions, civil rights organizations, urban machines, churches, farmers’ groups, and local party structures. These institutions mobilized voters, developed leaders, disciplined politicians, and gave people a way to understand government action as the product of collective struggle.

Today, many of those institutions are weakened or gone. Union density in the private sector is extremely low. Local parties often function as campaign shells. Working-class civic life has been battered by deindustrialization, suburbanization, religious sorting, media fragmentation, and economic precarity. The result is a politics in which Democrats can pass or propose helpful policies without creating a durable relationship with the people helped.

This is particularly damaging because the modern welfare state is often hard to see. A public job program is visible. A union contract is visible. A Social Security check is visible. But a tax credit, an ACA subsidy, a procurement rule, a clean-energy incentive, or an antitrust enforcement philosophy may be invisible or confusing. Voters may experience benefits as coming from an employer, a tax preparer, a state agency, a utility, or a private insurer. Without organizations explaining who fought for what and why it matters, policy disappears into the background.

The child tax credit illustrates both the promise and the failure. Monthly payments gave families breathing room. They helped pay rent, buy food, cover school supplies, and reduce stress. But the program was temporary, and its expiration was treated in Washington as one more bargaining outcome rather than as a moral emergency. Why was there no massive national mobilization of parents demanding renewal? Partly because the program was new. But also because low-income parents, especially those juggling unstable work and caregiving, are not automatically organized by receiving money. They need institutions—unions, parent associations, community groups, churches, tenant organizations—that can turn shared benefit into shared demand.

The labor question is therefore central. Democrats cannot become a durable working-class party merely by adopting better redistributive policies. They need more workers to have collective power in their workplaces. Unions do several things that progressive policy shops cannot. They raise wages. They reduce arbitrary managerial authority. They create cross-racial solidarities, though never automatically. They train members in politics. They provide trusted messengers. They connect national policy to shop-floor experience. They make class conflict legible.

The Biden administration has been more pro-union than any recent Democratic administration in rhetoric and appointments. The National Labor Relations Board has taken a more worker-friendly direction. Biden walked a picket line with striking autoworkers, a symbol that would have been hard to imagine from Clinton or Obama. The administration’s industrial policy includes labor standards and domestic production incentives. These are meaningful changes.

But they do not yet amount to a labor revival. The PRO Act stalled. Sectoral bargaining remains off the national agenda. Misclassification, subcontracting, franchise structures, captive-audience meetings, and employer retaliation still make organizing brutally difficult. Many new clean-energy and semiconductor jobs may or may not become union jobs depending on implementation and local power. A tax credit with prevailing wage incentives is not the same as a labor movement capable of organizing entire sectors.

If Democrats are serious about using policy to build power, they should judge industrial policy partly by whether it creates organized workers. A battery plant that receives public subsidy while fighting unionization is not a full progressive victory. A child care program that expands slots but leaves workers underpaid and unorganized is not a care revolution. A home care investment that relies on poverty wages is not justice. Public money should come with public obligations: neutrality in union drives, card-check recognition where legally possible, labor peace agreements, wage floors, training pipelines, and worker voice.

The care economy is especially important. Hacker and Pierson rightly emphasize that care work sits at the intersection of class, race, and gender. It is also one of the largest potential sites for new labor organization. Home care workers, child care workers, nursing home workers, and early educators perform socially necessary work that markets undervalue. Public funding could raise standards and create pathways to unionization. But if care policy is designed primarily as consumer subsidy—helping families purchase care in a fragmented low-wage market—it will reproduce many of the same inequalities. The care agenda must be a worker power agenda.

The same applies to climate policy. The green transition could become a source of broadly shared prosperity, or it could become another round of publicly subsidized private accumulation. The difference will depend on ownership, labor standards, community benefits, and planning. Workers in fossil fuel industries are right to distrust promises of “just transition” if they have seen previous trade and technology shocks devastate communities with little follow-through. A climate politics capable of winning durable support must offer not only cleaner energy but concrete power and security for workers.

This is where Hacker and Pierson’s U-shaped coalition faces its hardest test. Affluent Democratic professionals may support unions in principle, especially for service workers they do not manage. But many are also managers, nonprofit executives, university administrators, lawyers, tech supervisors, and public-sector reformers who become less enthusiastic when worker power challenges their own authority. The party’s upper tier often likes redistribution better than workplace democracy. It is easier to support taxing billionaires than to accept a union in one’s own institution.

That tension cannot be avoided forever. A party that wants working-class loyalty must sometimes side with workers against the preferences of its professional-class supporters. That means supporting graduate student unions against universities, teacher aides against school districts, nurses against hospital systems, warehouse workers against logistics giants, journalists against media owners, and domestic workers against households and agencies. Class politics becomes real when it creates discomfort inside the coalition.

There is also a racial dimension. The Democratic Party often talks about racial equity through representation, diversity, and anti-discrimination frameworks. Those matter, but labor organization can attack racial inequality at the level of wages, scheduling, benefits, safety, and voice. Some of the most exploited workers in the economy are women of color and immigrants in care, service, logistics, agriculture, and cleaning. A multiracial working-class politics cannot be built solely through symbolic inclusion. It must change the bargaining power of these workers.

The authors are correct that the right has worked hard to brand Democrats as culturally radical and economically irrelevant. But the best answer is not just better messaging about policy. It is creating institutions that make the Democratic economic agenda impossible to miss. A union steward is a better messenger than a television ad. A worker center can reach people a campaign never will. A tenant union can turn housing policy from abstraction into collective action. A parent organization can defend a child allowance more effectively than a white paper.

The Democratic Party’s leftward economic turn is real, but it remains elite-mediated. It has been shaped by senators, staffers, think tanks, advocates, and presidential task forces. That is not illegitimate; policy expertise matters. But without mass organization, the agenda will remain vulnerable to congressional veto points, donor pressure, hostile courts, and media distortion. It will also fail to inspire the very voters it is supposed to help.

The lesson of 2021 is not simply that Democrats need a few more Senate seats. They do. But they also need organized constituencies strong enough to make betrayal costly and success durable. The expanded child tax credit should have had an army. Paid leave should have had an army. Care workers should have had an army. Climate jobs should have had an army. Without those armies, even good policies enter Washington as proposals and leave as compromises.

The new blue divide can be bridged only through power, not sentiment. Affluent liberals and working-class voters may share an interest in functional government, but solidarity becomes durable when people act together in institutions that change their lives. Democrats have begun to remember what economic policy is for. Now they must remember who makes economic politics possible.
