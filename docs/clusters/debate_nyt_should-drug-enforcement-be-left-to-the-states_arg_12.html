"Drug enforcement should not be left to the states because federal law must be consistently applied and formally amended by Congress rather than bypassed through executive nonenforcement or state-level defiance."

No. However misguided parts of federal drug policy may be, drug enforcement should not be left to the states by executive indulgence or state-by-state defiance. A nation governed by law cannot treat federal statutes as optional because a few states have decided to go another way.

The Controlled Substances Act is federal law. Marijuana remains prohibited under it. The Supreme Court has already recognized broad federal authority in this area, including over locally grown marijuana, because drug markets are not easily confined within state borders. Colorado and Washington voters may have chosen legalization, but state votes do not repeal acts of Congress. If Congress wants to reschedule marijuana, reduce penalties or let states opt out, it has the power to do so. Until then, the executive branch should not create a de facto repeal through nonenforcement.

This matters even for those who favor reform. The Justice Department’s hands-off approach leaves citizens and businesses in a legal fog. A marijuana seller may be licensed by the state and still violate federal law. Banks hesitate to serve the industry. Future administrations may reverse policy. That is not federalism; it is uncertainty masquerading as tolerance.

Nor are drugs a purely local issue. Traffickers do not respect state lines. One state’s lax regime can supply another state’s black market. Opiate addiction in places like Vermont is linked to broader networks of prescription drug diversion and heroin distribution. Local prosecutors may lack the resources to pursue such networks, especially when violence, money laundering or interstate transport is involved.

To say this is not to defend every feature of the war on drugs. Mandatory minimum sentences have often been too harsh. Low-level addicts should not be treated like kingpins. Treatment should play a far larger role. The Justice Department is right to reconsider severe charging practices in appropriate cases.

But reform should be enacted, not improvised. Congress should debate marijuana classification, sentencing laws and the proper scope of federal prosecution. States should be free to innovate where federal law permits. What they should not be free to do is nullify national law while Washington looks away for political convenience.

The federal government should use discretion wisely, but it should not abandon drug enforcement. A system in which federal law means one thing in Colorado, another in Kansas and something else under the next attorney general is not a principled solution. It is an invitation to confusion and disrespect for law itself.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Controlled Substances Act remains the law of the land, and state-level legalization cannot legally repeal or nullify acts of Congress.
  2. 02The executive branch's hands-off approach creates legal uncertainty and instability for citizens and businesses operating in a conflict between state and federal law.
  3. 03Drug trafficking is an interstate issue that requires federal resources and authority to address networks that local prosecutors cannot effectively manage.
  4. 04Policy reforms regarding drug classification and sentencing should be achieved through transparent legislative debate in Congress rather than through improvised nonenforcement.
  5. 05Allowing federal law to vary by state undermines the rule of law and creates a fragmented system that invites confusion.
