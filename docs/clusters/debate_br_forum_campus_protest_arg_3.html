"While universities are flawed institutions that often prioritize diversity over justice, they remain essential sites for democratic reform and should be contested rather than abandoned as engines of social transformation."

Robin D. G. Kelley offers a moving account of black radical study, but his skepticism toward the university goes too far. It is true that universities are not innocent spaces. They reproduce class hierarchy, rely on exploited labor, chase donors, police students, and market diversity while often failing the students they recruit. But to say that universities “will never be engines of social transformation” is to concede too much. Universities have never transformed society alone. No institution has. Yet they have been, and can still be, crucial sites of democratic reform.

The recent protests at Missouri, Yale, Princeton, Claremont McKenna, and elsewhere should not be reduced to a naïve desire for institutional love. Many students were asking for ordinary civic fairness: protection from harassment, a curriculum that does not treat nonwhite peoples as marginal, faculty who reflect the country, and administrators who respond seriously when racism appears. These demands do not require us to believe in a postracial haven. They require us to believe that institutions can be made less unjust.

Kelley contrasts “supportive” environments with “liberated” ones, as if the former necessarily blocks the latter. But support matters. A university that recruits black students and then leaves them isolated is failing in its educational mission. A student who spends four years being treated as a representative of a race rather than a person is not receiving the same education as her peers. A student who must repeatedly explain why racist parties, slurs, Confederate iconography, or dismissive faculty behavior matter is doing unpaid pedagogical labor. To insist that universities address such conditions is not liberal sentimentalism. It is a demand for equal access to education.

The comparison between adding black faculty and adding black police officers is rhetorically powerful but misleading. Police exist to enforce state power, including through violence. Faculty exist, at least in principle, to teach, research, mentor, and expand knowledge. Representation cannot solve every problem, but it is not trivial. A black student at a predominantly white institution may go years without being taught by a black professor. That absence shapes intellectual possibility. It tells students who belongs in the realm of knowledge. Hiring more faculty of color is not merely cosmetic; it can alter research agendas, mentoring networks, departmental cultures, and the range of questions considered legitimate.

Similarly, curricular reform should not be dismissed as granting the university “authority over our reading choices.” Students are not wrong to expect formal education to include the histories and thinkers that universities have long excluded. Of course students should read outside the syllabus. Of course study groups, freedom schools, and community-based education are indispensable. But it matters whether the official curriculum treats Africa, Asia, Latin America, indigenous peoples, and diasporic black thought as central rather than supplemental. Canon formation is a political act. Contesting it is also political.

Kelley is at his best in warning that diversity can become a substitute for justice. Universities love the photograph of multiracial students on the brochure more than they love redistributing power. They can celebrate first-generation students while raising tuition. They can fund an inclusion office while opposing unionization. They can create bias response teams while investing in companies that profit from prisons. Student activists should therefore be wary of symbolic victories that leave the institution’s structure intact.

But wariness is not the same as refusal. The language of institutional reform has produced real gains: Title IX enforcement against sexual harassment and assault, disability accommodations, need-based financial aid, ethnic studies programs, LGBTQ resource centers, and mechanisms for reporting discrimination. These are imperfect and uneven, but they matter in the lives of students. The fact that liberal reform is incomplete does not mean it is worthless. A more humane campus can be a condition for more radical politics, not an obstacle to it.

I am also uneasy with Kelley’s critique of trauma discourse. He is right that trauma can individualize political injury and invite managerial responses. But we should be careful not to make students choose between being agents and being wounded. People can be both. The civil rights movement was full of people who endured terror and still organized. Feminist movements taught us that personal violation can reveal structural domination. The problem is not that students name pain; the problem is when institutions answer pain with therapy alone. Mental health services are necessary, especially for students who face racism, poverty, sexual violence, family pressure, and debt. They become politically insufficient only when detached from institutional accountability.

What would a better approach look like? Universities should take the reform demands seriously while also opening themselves to more fundamental critique. They should hire and retain faculty of color, not as public relations assets but as intellectual leaders. They should fund black studies and ethnic studies. They should protect speech while making clear that harassment is incompatible with education. They should reduce financial barriers, support workers, disclose investments, and create genuine channels of student participation in governance. They should also welcome independent study groups, community partnerships, and forms of learning not controlled by departments.

The university is not a family, and students should not have to seek parental love from deans. But neither is it merely an enemy camp from which one steals resources. It is a public trust, even when privately governed; a workplace; a knowledge institution; and a contested democratic arena. The task is not to believe in its purity. The task is to fight over what it does.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02Increasing faculty representation is a substantive intellectual necessity that reshapes research and mentorship, rather than a merely cosmetic change comparable to diversifying a police force.
  3. 03Contesting the official curriculum is a vital political act because canon formation determines whose histories and ideas are treated as central to knowledge.
  4. 04Institutional reforms and 'supportive' environments provide material gains that can serve as necessary conditions for, rather than obstacles to, radical politics.
  5. 05Naming personal trauma does not preclude political agency; rather, it can reveal structural domination and should be met with both mental health support and institutional accountability.
  6. 06The university should be viewed as a contested public trust and democratic arena where students and faculty fight for accountability and the redistribution of power.
