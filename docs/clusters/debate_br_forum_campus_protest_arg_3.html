Diversified Gemini

"While Robin D. G. Kelley correctly identifies the failures of liberal multiculturalism, he fails to recognize that current campus activism is a bourgeois project whose race-first framework serves as a roadblock to genuine working-class liberation."

Robin D. G. Kelley is entirely correct when he points out that the modern university cannot be radically transformed simply by adding "darker faces, safer spaces, [and] better training." In "Black Study, Black Struggle," he elegantly exposes the limits of liberal multiculturalism and the danger of framing political grievances purely through the lens of psychological trauma. Yet, while Kelley gestures toward a radical, anti-capitalist vision of struggle, he fails to follow his own critique to its logical conclusion: the current wave of campus activism, in its dominant form, is fundamentally a bourgeois project, and its obsession with race-first representation serves as a roadblock to genuine working-class liberation.

Kelley notes that the student movement's core demands—diversity, inclusion, and cultural-competency—converge with a neoliberal logic that individualizes systemic issues. But we must be clearer about what these demands actually achieve. When students at elite institutions like Yale, Brown, or Mizzou demand that the administration hire more faculty of color or mandate anti-racist training, they are essentially asking for a more diverse managerial class. They are fighting for equitable representation within the ruling elite. A university with a Black chancellor and proportional representation of Black students is still an institution that exploits adjunct labor, hoards multibillion-dollar endowments, and gentrifies surrounding working-class neighborhoods.

The political education Kelley rightfully champions—the rigorous study of Marx, Fanon, Rodney, and Davis—teaches us that capitalism requires a permanent underclass to function. The triumph of multiculturalism, as Kelley observes, brought us Black CEOs and politicians who oversaw mass incarceration and the destruction of the welfare state. Why should we expect the diversification of the ivy-covered halls of the academy to yield a different result?

The tragedy of the current campus movement is not just that it relies on a language of trauma, but that its identitarian framework actively alienates the broader working-class coalitions necessary to challenge capital. By prioritizing "safe spaces" and focusing almost exclusively on microaggressions and interpersonal prejudice, the movement isolates itself from the material struggles of the very communities it claims to represent. As Naomi Wallace and George Lipsitz, whom Kelley quotes, make clear, elevating interiority and personal pain removes the focus from economic justice and places it squarely in the realm of psychology.

We see this explicitly in the demands of groups like the Black Liberation Collective. While their call for universities to divest from prisons and invest in communities is a positive step toward material critique, their demand that "tuition be free for black and indigenous students" reveals a fatal ideological flaw. Why only Black and Indigenous students? What of the white, Latino, and Asian working-class students who are equally crushed by the burden of student debt? By fracturing demands along racial lines, these activists unwittingly do the work of the ruling class, dividing the proletariat and preventing the formation of a mass, multiracial movement capable of genuinely threatening the neoliberal university structure.

Kelley finds hope in Harney and Moten’s "undercommons" and the graduate students at UCLA who are attempting to model radical assembly. But even here, the focus remains heavily skewed toward abolitionist racial politics rather than a unifying class struggle. The undercommons, as described, remains a subculture of the "PhD precariat" and campus activists. It is a radicalism of the margins that often speaks in an academic jargon entirely disconnected from the shop floor, the service industry, or the logistics hubs where the actual leverage of the working class lies.

If we are to take Kelley’s invocation of Marx seriously—to engage in the "ruthless criticism of everything existing"—we must critique the class character of the student movement itself. We must recognize that feelings of alienation at an elite university, while real, are not the equivalent of material deprivation. The answer to the neoliberal university is not to demand that it become a more comfortable home for marginalized students to ascend into the professional class. The answer is to recognize the university as a site of labor and capital accumulation, and to organize accordingly.

This means campus activists must shift their focus away from syllabi and building names, and toward unionizing campus workers—the janitors, the dining hall staff, the graduate students, and the adjuncts. It means demanding free tuition for *all* students, funded by the expropriation of massive university endowments. It means abandoning the dead-end politics of trauma and representation, and embracing a universalist, class-first politics that recognizes our shared exploitation under capitalism as the only viable basis for true solidarity and liberation.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Demands for diversity and inclusion at elite universities merely seek equitable representation within the ruling managerial class rather than challenging the university's role in capital accumulation and labor exploitation.
  2. 02The movement's focus on psychological trauma and interpersonal prejudice individualizes systemic issues and alienates the broader multiracial working-class coalitions necessary for economic justice.
  3. 03Demands for race-specific benefits, such as free tuition only for Black and Indigenous students, fracture the proletariat and prevent the formation of a unified movement against neoliberalism.
  4. 04Radical spaces like the 'undercommons' remain isolated subcultures of the academic precariat that lack connection to the material leverage of the broader working class.
  5. 05True liberation requires shifting focus from identitarian representation toward universalist, class-first politics, such as unionizing all campus workers and demanding free tuition for all students.
Diversified Gemini

"While Robin D. G. Kelley correctly identifies that universities are not engines of social transformation, his focus on internal academic subversion fails to recognize that the campus movement's preoccupation with university reform is a distraction from the urgent, material struggles of the Black working class."

In "Black Study, Black Struggle," Robin D. G. Kelley offers a necessary reality check to the student activists who brought their righteous anger to college campuses in the fall of 2015. His central assertion—that the university "will never be engines of social transformation" and that such work ultimately belongs outside the academy—is the most urgent takeaway of his essay. Yet, despite making this point, Kelley spends the bulk of his piece debating the ideological fissures *within* the campus movement, validating the "undercommons" as a site of radical potential. As a community organizer working in the neighborhoods surrounding these towering academic institutions, I argue that we must take Kelley’s premise further: the intense focus on reforming the university is a massive distraction from the life-and-death struggles happening in the streets.

The campus uprisings were, as Kelley notes, ignited by the fires of Ferguson and the relentless police murders of Black Americans. But as the movement migrated from the streets of our cities to the quads of Yale, Mizzou, and Claremont McKenna, the demands fundamentally shifted. What began as a desperate outcry for Black survival—a demand to stop the state-sanctioned execution of Black people—was translated into the language of the academy. Suddenly, the national discourse was consumed by debates over Halloween costumes, the names of residential colleges, trigger warnings, and the racial composition of reading lists.

Kelley eloquently critiques the neoliberal co-optation of these demands, pointing out how cultural-competency training and diversity initiatives personalize and depoliticize systemic racism. But the deeper problem is not just *how* the students are fighting the university; it is *that* they are fighting the university while the world burns outside its gates.

For the Black working-class residents living in the shadows of these multi-billion-dollar institutions, the campus debates over "safe spaces" feel painfully insular. A revised syllabus does not stop a police bullet. A newly appointed dean of diversity does not stop gentrification, much of which is driven by the very universities these students attend. While activists exhaust themselves occupying administration buildings to secure a new cultural center, community members just blocks away are fighting evictions, mass incarceration, and the gutting of public schools.

Kelley holds up the UCLA graduate students’ "undercommons" as a radical model, praising their outdoor meetings and integration of activists from Black Lives Matter and Critical Resistance. It is certainly a step up from demanding better PR from the administration. But we must be honest: an outdoor seminar at UCLA is still UCLA. It is still a highly exclusive space, gated by academic privilege. To tell young, brilliant Black minds that their highest political calling is to become "subversives in the academy"—sneaking around to steal university resources—is to trap them in a cloistered game of academic hide-and-seek.

The historical models Kelley invokes, like the Mississippi Freedom Schools and the Mt. Vernon collective, were powerful precisely because they were deeply embedded in the community, not the university. They were addressing the immediate, material needs of sharecroppers, domestic workers, and welfare mothers. The education was a tool for the people, not a way to endure life in the ivory tower.

If students want to honor the legacy of the Black radical tradition, they need to recognize the immense privilege of their position and direct their energy outward. We do not need students to spend four years trying to make the university "love" them. We need them to leverage the immense resources of the university—its money, its legal clinics, its research capabilities, its prestige—and funnel them directly into local, grassroots organizing.

If a student coalition can force a university president to resign over campus climate, imagine if they used that same leverage to force the university to make payments in lieu of taxes to underfunded city schools. Imagine if they demanded that the university use its land trust to build affordable housing for the displaced, or barred campus police from coordinating with local law enforcement.

Kelley is right that trauma is real, and the alienation Black students feel on campus is painful. But healing will not come from forcing a neoliberal institution to accommodate that pain. Healing comes through solidarity with the broader struggle. The true undercommons is not hiding in the margins of the university; it is out here on the blocks, in the neighborhoods, and in the streets. We are waiting for the students to join us.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The transition of activism from the streets to the campus shifted the focus from Black survival and state violence to insular academic concerns like syllabi and building names.
  2. 02Campus-centric demands for safe spaces and diversity initiatives do nothing to address the immediate crises of gentrification, evictions, and mass incarceration facing neighboring communities.
  3. 03The 'undercommons' model remains an exclusive, privileged space that traps radical intellectual energy within the academy rather than directing it toward grassroots organizing.
  4. 04Student activists should stop seeking institutional belonging and instead leverage university resources—such as funding and legal clinics—to support local community struggles.
  5. 05True healing and political efficacy for Black students come from solidarity with the broader struggle outside the university gates rather than trying to make neoliberal institutions accommodate their trauma.
