"Early childhood predistribution programs should be viewed as a complement to, rather than a substitute for, income redistribution, as financial stability is often a prerequisite for effective parenting and skill development."

Predistribution and Redistribution Are Not Substitutes

James Heckman has done as much as any economist alive to put early childhood on the policy map, and his case for intensive, well-designed interventions in the first years of life is largely persuasive. The longitudinal evidence from Perry and Abecedarian, the developmental neuroscience, the data on diverging family environments — all of it points the same direction. Where his essay goes wrong is in the rhetorical move at the end: dressing up predistribution as a substitute for redistribution, and presenting the 1996 welfare reform as vindication of the view that "giving money to poor families does not, by itself, promote social mobility."

That framing is too clean, and the evidence is more ambivalent than Heckman lets on.

Start with the Cherokee casino study he himself cites approvingly. When the Eastern Band of Cherokees began distributing per-capita casino payments, the children in newly non-poor households showed marked reductions in behavioral problems and improvements in school performance. Heckman reads this as a story about parenting: income changed, parenting improved, child outcomes followed. Fair enough. But notice what the study does not show — it does not show that you could have produced the same parenting improvements without the cash. The income was the intervention. To then conclude that "giving families more money is not the same as enhancing their children's environments" reverses the actual finding: in that experiment, giving families money was precisely what enhanced their children's environments.

The same logic applies to a growing body of work on the Earned Income Tax Credit, Section 8 housing vouchers, SNAP, and the Canadian and British child benefits introduced over the past fifteen years. Where careful identification has been possible, additional income to poor families has been linked with better birth weights, higher test scores, and reductions in maternal stress. These effects are not as large as the Perry results per dollar, but they are real, and they accrue across the entire low-income population — not the few thousand children a boutique program can serve.

Heckman's own framework helps explain why. If "the scarce resource is love and parenting — not money," we should ask what depletes love and parenting in poor households. The answer, much of the developmental literature suggests, is chronic stress: eviction, food insecurity, unstable work schedules, untreated maternal depression, the cognitive load of constant financial triage. Sendhil Mullainathan and Eldar Shafir's recent work on scarcity is directly relevant here. Income transfers do not buy nurturing directly, but they relax precisely the conditions that make nurturing hardest. To pit predistribution against redistribution is to ignore how the latter underwrites the former.

There is also a political-economy point Heckman glides past. The Perry Preschool intervention cost roughly $17,000 per child in 1960s dollars and required highly trained staff making weekly home visits. Scaling that nationally would be enormously expensive — easily comparable to a meaningful expansion of the EITC or a child allowance. If we are going to spend the money either way, the choice is not between costly redistribution and cheap predistribution, but between two equally serious commitments. The honest case for early childhood programs is that they complement income support, not that they let us avoid it.

This matters because Heckman's framing has a political afterlife he may not intend. His essay can be — and has been — quoted by people who want to defund welfare and call the savings "predistribution." That misreads his evidence. Nothing in the Perry or Abecedarian results tells us that food stamps are useless or that child poverty per se is harmless. They tell us that intensive cognitive and socio-emotional enrichment, on top of whatever else children have, pays off handsomely. The "on top of" is doing a lot of work.

I would also push back on the implicit causal story about welfare reform. Yes, employment among single mothers rose after 1996. But deep poverty also rose, particularly after the safety net was tested by the 2008 recession. Kathryn Edin's recent ethnographic work documents families living essentially on no cash income at all. If the goal is healthy early environments, exposing the most vulnerable children to that kind of destitution is not a triumph of social policy.

Heckman is right that you cannot solve the skills gap by writing checks. He is right that high-quality, early, sustained interventions yield extraordinary returns, especially when they engage parents directly. He is right that adolescent remediation is a poor substitute for early formation. But the right conclusion is not "predistribution, not redistribution." It is that a serious mobility agenda needs both: a generous floor of income and material security for families with young children, and a serious public investment in the developmental scaffolding — nurse home visiting, high-quality center-based care, paid parental leave, parenting support — that converts that security into nurturing capacity.

To borrow Heckman's own language: skill begets skill, but stability begets the conditions under which skill can be built. We should stop pretending we have to choose.
