"The U.S. should establish a federal framework for victim compensation that is administered locally and prioritizes long-term, comprehensive support over one-time payments."

The debate over compensation often becomes a debate over checks: who writes them, who receives them and how large they should be. But survivors of mass violence need more than a one-time payment. They need a system that stays after the cameras leave.

The best model is a federal framework administered locally. When a mass bombing, shooting or similar attack occurs, a victims’ trust should be created with federal support, local participation and independent fiduciary oversight. Survivors and families should have seats at the table. Rules should be public. Payments should be prompt. But the trust should reserve significant resources for long-term needs: surgeries, prosthetics, trauma counseling, school support, transportation, job retraining, home modifications and help for children who lost parents.

This structure recognizes that tragedies differ. Boston involved many catastrophic physical injuries, including amputations. Newtown involved murdered children, traumatized classmates and a shattered town. Sept. 11 produced immediate bereavement and, later, serious health problems among responders and workers. A single rigid formula from Washington will miss important realities. Yet leaving everything to scattered charities invites confusion and mistrust.

The federal government should guarantee core services and provide matching funds, technical assistance and oversight. Private donations should be welcomed into the same trust when possible, so families are not forced to navigate multiple funds with conflicting rules. Donors who want to support a specific purpose — scholarships, mental health, a memorial — can do so, but the central fund should coordinate the response.

This approach should not depend too heavily on whether an act is labeled “terrorism.” For victims, the practical needs after a bombing and a shooting spree can be very similar. Eligibility should turn on harm, proximity and need, not on ideology.

Nor should compensation replace accountability. If lawsuits are appropriate, they should proceed. If criminal restitution or seized assets are available, they should be used. But no family should have to wait for litigation before receiving counseling or paying for a funeral.

Money cannot make victims whole. It can, however, prevent abandonment. A humane compensation system should promise not only that help will arrive quickly, but that it will endure.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A centralized trust with federal oversight and local participation ensures that compensation rules are transparent and that payments are distributed promptly.
  2. 02A flexible, locally-informed model is necessary because different types of mass violence create distinct physical, psychological, and community needs that a rigid national formula would overlook.
  3. 03Integrating private donations into a single coordinated fund prevents the confusion and mistrust caused by navigating multiple charities with conflicting rules.
  4. 04Compensation eligibility should be based on the actual harm and needs of the victims rather than the specific ideological label, such as 'terrorism,' assigned to the act of violence.
  5. 05The compensation system should provide immediate relief for essential services without requiring victims to wait for the outcome of legal accountability measures like lawsuits or criminal restitution.
