"The United States should adopt a realist strategy in Afghanistan that narrows the mission to targeted counterterrorism against al Qaeda while abandoning the impossible and unnecessary goal of state-building through counterinsurgency."

Nir Rosen’s central insight is that the United States has confused two missions in Afghanistan. One is a limited security mission: prevent al Qaeda from using Afghan territory to mount attacks against the United States. The other is an unlimited political mission: build an Afghan state capable of defeating the Taliban, policing its territory, delivering services, and earning legitimacy among a rural population with little reason to trust Kabul. The first mission is necessary. The second is not only unnecessary but likely impossible at acceptable cost.

The Obama administration says its goal is to “disrupt, dismantle, and defeat al Qaeda.” That is a defensible objective. The problem is that the means chosen—tens of thousands more troops for counterinsurgency against the Taliban—do not follow from it. Rosen is right to ask why a campaign against a largely Afghan and Pakistani Pashtun insurgency has become the chosen instrument for defeating a transnational terrorist organization whose most important leaders are believed to be across the border in Pakistan.

A realist strategy would begin by ranking threats. The Taliban are ugly, repressive, and hostile. They are not, however, equivalent to al Qaeda. Their ambitions are local or regional. They seek power in Afghanistan and influence among Pashtuns; they do not appear to have the capability or primary intention to attack the American homeland. Al Qaeda does. That distinction should guide policy. The United States need not like the Taliban, and it need not be indifferent to their victims, to recognize that treating every Taliban advance as a direct threat to Chicago or Los Angeles stretches the concept of national security beyond usefulness.

The standard rebuttal is sanctuary. If the Taliban return to power, they may again host al Qaeda. That risk is real. But it does not automatically justify a vast counterinsurgency campaign. Sanctuary is not binary. Terrorist networks can plan in Pakistani cities, tribal areas, Yemen, Somalia, Europe, or cyberspace. They do not require a recognized state. Since 2001, American intelligence and military capabilities have also changed. Training camps are easier to detect than conspiratorial cells; large facilities in Afghanistan could be struck. A Taliban government contemplating renewed sanctuary would know that it had previously lost power as a result. It might still make reckless choices, but policy should weigh probabilities and costs, not worst-case scenarios alone.

The costs of the current approach are enormous. It commits American forces to protect populations in places with little connection to al Qaeda. It makes U.S. success dependent on Afghan police, courts, ministries, and elections that Washington cannot control. It requires cooperation from Pakistan while simultaneously intensifying Pakistan’s fear of Indian influence in Kabul and pushing militants across a border Pakistan only partly governs. It creates more Afghan grievances through raids, detentions, and civilian casualties. And it consumes attention and money that could be used for more targeted counterterrorism, homeland security, intelligence cooperation, and diplomacy.

Rosen’s reporting from Helmand illustrates the mismatch. Highway 601 matters to NATO logistics and local control. It does not obviously matter to al Qaeda’s capacity to attack the United States. Yet American soldiers risk their lives mentoring police who may steal from villagers, flee firefights, or arrest innocents. Every local dispute becomes an American problem. A blocked irrigation channel becomes a test of legitimacy. A corrupt checkpoint becomes a strategic liability. This is what happens when a great power accepts responsibility for political order in a country it does not understand.

Realism is often caricatured as indifference to morality. But a narrower strategy may be more humane than an expansive one. Counterinsurgency promises protection but often delivers escalation. It tells villagers that if they side with the government, NATO will protect them; then NATO rotates out, or lacks enough troops, or cannot patrol at night, and the Taliban return. It tells soldiers to be social workers with rifles, then places them amid IEDs and ambushes until fear overwhelms restraint. It tells Afghan officials that foreign money will keep flowing because Washington cannot afford failure, thereby reducing incentives for reform. A limited strategy would make fewer promises and break fewer of them.

What would such a strategy look like? First, the United States should reduce its objective to preventing transnational terrorist operations from Afghan soil. That requires intelligence networks, surveillance, special operations forces, and the ability to strike camps or high-value targets. These tools are imperfect and can be abused, but they are better matched to the threat than an army of occupation.

Second, Washington should pursue political accommodation inside Afghanistan. The Taliban cannot all be reconciled, and some factions are deeply tied to al Qaeda or Pakistani intelligence. But many fighters are motivated by local grievances, money, coercion, or opposition to foreign troops. The United States should support negotiations among the Afghan government, local power holders, and insurgent elements willing to accept limits: no sanctuary for al Qaeda, no attacks on international targets, and some protection for basic humanitarian access. These terms are not ideal, but neither is endless war.

Third, the United States should shift resources from building large Afghan security forces to sustaining smaller, more reliable units and local arrangements. Rosen is right that a 400,000-member army and police force would be financially unsustainable. A state that cannot pay its armed men without foreign subsidy is not sovereign; it is a payroll waiting to become a militia problem. Better to support forces that Afghanistan can plausibly maintain, supplemented by community defense structures only where they do not empower predatory commanders.

Fourth, Pakistan must become central. This does not mean invading Pakistan or pretending Washington can reorder its politics. It means recognizing that al Qaeda’s sanctuary is there, that Afghan Taliban leadership has enjoyed refuge there, and that Pakistan’s military views Afghanistan through the prism of rivalry with India. American aid should be tied to genuine cooperation against transnational militants, while diplomacy should address Pakistan’s security fears without giving it veto power over Afghan sovereignty. This is frustrating work, but it is closer to the real problem than clearing villages in Helmand.

Fifth, the United States should maintain humanitarian commitments that do not depend on military victory. Refugee aid, demining, public health, and education support should continue where partners can operate safely. Afghans should not be punished for the failure of American strategy. But aid should not be sold as the decisive weapon in a counterinsurgency campaign. Development is not magic dust sprinkled after a firefight.

Rosen sometimes overstates his case. Al Qaeda may be small, but small groups can cause catastrophic harm under the right conditions. The United States cannot simply address “root causes” and assume the threat dissolves. Intelligence work, arrests, and sometimes force will remain necessary. Nor should we romanticize Taliban pragmatism. Movements can be both local and reckless; they can make alliances that exceed their original aims. But acknowledging these risks does not justify confusing Afghanistan’s internal war with the entire struggle against terrorism.

The United States needs strategic discipline. Not every bad outcome is a vital threat. Not every enemy of our client is our enemy. Not every ungoverned space can be governed by us or for us. Rosen’s essay is a warning against the intoxication of doctrine. Counterinsurgency gives policymakers a way to avoid choosing: fight and build, surge and exit, centralize and localize, protect and kill. Real strategy requires choosing. In Afghanistan, that means narrowing the mission to al Qaeda, reducing the military footprint, negotiating where possible, striking where necessary, and abandoning the fantasy that American security depends on manufacturing an Afghan state from the barrel of a gun.
