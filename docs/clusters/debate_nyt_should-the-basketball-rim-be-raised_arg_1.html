"The basketball rim should remain at ten feet because raising it would disrupt the game's balance and fail to achieve its intended goals."

Raising the rim is one of those reforms that sounds elegant on a chalkboard and falls apart on the floor. The proposal usually goes to 11 or 12 feet, on the theory that 7-footers have outgrown Naismith's 10-foot peach basket. But height is only part of why dunking dominates: players are also stronger, quicker, and better coached on vertical leap than they were in 1950. Raise the rim a foot and Blake Griffin and LeBron James still throw it down; the marginal dunkers — the guards and wings whose finishes make the highlight reel — simply stop attacking the basket, and offenses retreat to the perimeter.

That is the deeper problem. We already have a game tilting toward the three-point line; Daryl Morey's Rockets have made an arithmetic argument that mid-range jumpers are nearly irrational. A higher rim would punish interior play even more, because every floater, layup, and putback gets harder while the corner three stays exactly where it is. Shooting percentages would crater, rebounds would carom farther, and the post would empty out further.

If the goal is to reward finesse over power, the cleaner fixes already exist: enforce the defensive three-second rule, widen the lane, or shrink the restricted area. Leave the rim where Naismith hung it.
