"While amnesty is a compassionate response to long-term irregular migrants, it should be granted as a one-time legislative act paired with strict enforcement rather than an automatic legal right based on the passage of time."

Joseph Carens offers an elegant moral argument for amnesty rooted in the idea of social membership over time. His appeal is powerful, and I share his compassion for those who have built lives in the shadows. But moral clarity does not always translate into sound policy, and Carens’s proposal contains a fatal flaw: it would almost certainly encourage more unauthorized immigration in the future, thereby undermining the very rule of law he seeks to temper. Amnesty must be paired with robust enforcement and a credible commitment to prevent future illegal entry; otherwise, we simply set the stage for a recurring cycle of violation and mass legalization.

Carens dismisses the concern about incentivizing future irregular migration far too quickly. He suggests that migrants are motivated mainly by immediate economic opportunities and the chance to evade authorities in the short term, and that the distant prospect of amnesty years down the road would have “little impact.” This is a heroic assumption unsupported by evidence. In fact, the experience of the 1986 Immigration Reform and Control Act (IRCA), the last large-scale amnesty in the United States, suggests otherwise. IRCA legalized nearly three million people, but it failed to implement the promised enforcement measures, and the result was a dramatic increase in the unauthorized population over the following two decades. It is true that many factors contributed to that increase, but the signal sent by amnesty—that the United States will eventually relent and legalize those who manage to stay long enough—was undeniably part of the calculus. Prospective migrants and the smugglers who profit from them understand that laws change, and they factor that into their decisions. Human beings are not machines acting only on immediate incentives; they also respond to long-term rational expectations. If we create a reliable pathway to legal status after five or seven years, we effectively create a permanent magnet.

Carens’s analogy with statutes of limitations is illuminating but ultimately misleading. Statutes of limitations serve to protect individuals from the indefinite threat of prosecution for past offenses; they do not generally apply to continuing violations. Someone who repeatedly speeds every day cannot claim immunity because the first offense occurred years ago. Illegal presence is, in an important sense, a continuing condition, not a single past act. Every day an irregular migrant remains in the country without authorization, the original violation is renewed. The state’s interest in enforcing its immigration laws therefore does not neatly expire with the passage of time.

This is not to say that the state should never grant amnesty. I believe we should do so, but as a one-time act of national grace, not as an automatic right that becomes entrenched in law. An amnesty now would allow us to clear the decks, bring millions out of the shadows, and begin afresh with a reformed system. But such an amnesty must be paired with truly effective border security, mandatory E-Verify for employers, a reliable system for tracking visa overstays, and a temporary worker program that responds to genuine labor market needs. Only when these elements are in place can we credibly promise that the amnesty is a unique event, not the first of many. Carens’s proposal, by contrast, would embed the expectation of future amnesties into the very structure of immigration law. Every successful illegal entrant would become a future legal resident after a fixed waiting period. The consequences for migration patterns would be predictable: smuggling networks would market the five-year path to residency, and the flow would increase.

Carens argues that the state’s right to control immigration is not absolute and that the harms of deportation grow over time. I accept that. But we must weigh not only the harms of deportation but also the harms of incentivizing future irregular migration. When we create a rule that effectively guarantees legalization after a set number of years, we invite the very situations we later find heartbreaking—families divided, communities torn apart—because those situations will multiply. A policy that is individually merciful may be collectively harmful if it leads to larger unauthorized populations, greater exploitation, and more social conflict.

There is also a deeper question about democratic legitimacy. In a democracy, the people through their representatives enact immigration laws. Those laws may be unwise or even unjust, but a blanket rule that automatically overrides them after a few years of illegal presence is a form of judicial usurpation—or, in this case, a legislative shortcut that bypasses the public’s rightful role in setting the terms of membership. Carens is sensitive to this concern, but his proposed institutionalization of a right to stay would permanently strip the political branches of the capacity to enforce their own laws over time. It would, in effect, declare that de facto settlement trumps democratic will, no matter how firm that will might be. That is a dangerous principle in a self-governing society.

I do not dismiss the moral weight of the stories Carens tells. Miguel Sanchez, Hiu Lui Ng, and countless others deserve our empathy, and in many cases they deserve legal status. But the way to provide that status is through a carefully crafted, one-time legislative package that balances compassion with the need to restore credibility to the immigration system. Let us grant amnesty now, but let us also build a system that makes future amnesties unnecessary. Carens’s proposed automatic path to residency, for all its moral appeal, would make future amnesties inevitable—and with them, a permanent state of legalized lawlessness.
