"While Edward Miguel highlights significant progress in Africa, his analysis underestimates the structural obstacles to genuine development, particularly the continent's continued dependency on commodity exports and the complicated, often exploitative nature of Chinese engagement."

Reading Edward Miguel's essay from May 2008, I am struck by the paradox at its heart: the continent's most prominent development economist writes eloquently about African renewal while documenting in granular detail the forces that could derail it. The essay is simultaneously a celebration of progress and a catalog of fragilities. Perhaps this is the appropriate stance for development scholarship at a moment when the evidence is genuinely mixed and the stakes could not be higher. But I want to push back on one tendency in Miguel's analysis: the tendency to treat China's rise as primarily beneficial for Africa and to frame Western hypocrisy on agricultural trade as the main external obstacle to African prosperity.

Miguel is correct that rich-country farm subsidies hurt African cotton and grain producers. The $3 billion annually doled out to 25,000 American cotton farmers while millions of West African smallholders see prices driven down is an incontrovertible injustice. The expired Multi-Fiber Agreement that exposed African textile producers to Chinese competition is similarly indefensible. But these are symptoms of a deeper problem that Miguel does not fully confront: the international economic order remains structurally arranged in ways that perpetuate African dependency on primary commodity exports while rewarding industrial competitors in Asia and the West.

The agricultural subsidy issue is real but limited. Cotton represents a significant share of exports for a handful of Sahelian countries—Benin, Burkina Faso, Mali—but it is not the engine of African economic transformation. The bigger story is what Miguel acknowledges almost in passing: "industrial transformation is not driving Africa's growth." This is the crux. African countries that have grown fastest over the past seven years have done so by extracting and exporting oil, copper, coffee, and flowers—the raw materials their colonial predecessors exported. The factories, processors, and sophisticated service sectors that would add value locally and create large numbers of skilled jobs remain underdeveloped. This is not because African entrepreneurs lack talent or effort; it is because the international trading system, dominated by powerful industrial nations protective of their markets, has never offered Africa a genuine opportunity to industrialize.

Here China's rise becomes more complicated than Miguel's largely positive framing suggests. Yes, Chinese demand has driven up commodity prices and created market opportunities. But Chinese investment is not building African industries in the way Japanese and Korean development assistance helped build infrastructure in Taiwan, South Korea, and Southeast Asia during their formative decades. Chinese firms are extracting African resources and, in some documented cases, treating African workers with contempt. The Chambishi mine story is not an isolated scandal; it is a warning about the nature of Chinese engagement when accountability mechanisms are weak.

The Western critique of Chinese investment practices in Sudan deserves equal attention. Miguel is right to note that Western sanctions have boosted Chinese bargaining power, but he understates the moral weight of this observation. If China is effectively enabling atrocities in Darfur through its diplomatic support and oil revenue flows to Khartoum, then the calculus of Chinese engagement must include this dimension. Africa's potential development miracle cannot be built on the ashes of Sudanese villages.

Miguel's treatment of democratic gains is appropriately cautious. Kenya's post-election violence is correctly identified as evidence of profound fragility. But I want to highlight what this violence reveals about the limits of electoral democracy without substantive economic development. Kenya in 2007 was more democratic than it had been in decades, with flourishing independent media, an active opposition, and civil society organizations challenging corruption. Yet when the election results were disputed, ethnic violence erupted with stunning rapidity. This suggests that democracy without economic security, without widely shared prosperity, and without deep institutionalization of conflict resolution mechanisms can be combustible rather than stabilizing.

The Botswana drought insurance program Miguel describes is genuinely promising, but its applicability is limited. Botswana is a small, relatively wealthy, homogeneous country with a competent state apparatus and diamond revenues to fund social programs. Replicating this model in the Democratic Republic of Congo, Somalia, or Chad would require state capacities those countries do not currently possess. The lesson is not that drought insurance can prevent conflict, but that state competence and economic resilience are preconditions for any insurance mechanism to work.

Looking forward from May 2008, I share Miguel's hope that Africa may finally be turning a corner, but I urge greater attention to the structural obstacles that remain. The commodity boom is not development. Chinese engagement is not philanthropy. Electoral democracy without economic inclusion is fragile. Climate change is not a distant threat. Africa needs genuine partnership with industrial nations willing to open their markets, transfer technology, and respect African agency—not just commodity buyers and investors seeking quick profits.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The international economic order is structurally rigged to keep Africa dependent on raw material exports rather than allowing for the industrial transformation necessary for long-term growth.
  2. 02China’s involvement in Africa is more problematic than Miguel suggests, as it often prioritizes resource extraction over local industrial building and can enable human rights abuses.
  3. 03Electoral democracy in Africa remains dangerously combustible and fragile as long as it lacks the foundation of substantive economic security and shared prosperity.
  4. 04The success of social safety nets like Botswana’s drought insurance depends on state capacities that many of Africa's most vulnerable nations currently lack.
  5. 05True African development requires a shift from mere commodity trading to genuine partnerships that include technology transfer and the opening of industrial markets in wealthy nations.
