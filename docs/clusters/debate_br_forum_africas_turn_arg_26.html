Diversified Gemini

"The path to African prosperity lies in dismantling Western trade barriers and empowering local entrepreneurs rather than implementing new top-down foreign aid schemes like Rapid Conflict Prevention Support (RCPS)."

Edward Miguel is halfway to the truth in his essay "Is It Africa's Turn?". His analysis of the failure of traditional foreign aid is spot on. He rightly skewers the Jeffrey Sachs narrative that just one more massive infusion of Western cash will break the "poverty trap," correctly noting the lack of statistical correspondence between massive aid flows and African economic growth. But after so effectively dismantling the top-down planning mindset of the aid industry, Miguel inexplicably proposes a new top-down planning scheme: Rapid Conflict Prevention Support (RCPS).

Miguel suggests that international donors track rainfall and commodity prices and automatically disburse foreign aid to vulnerable governments to prevent conflict. This is classic development planner hubris. It assumes that the primary cause of civil conflict is a sudden lack of income, and that pouring foreign cash into a government’s coffers during a crisis will trickle down to pacify desperate citizens.

This completely ignores the political realities of fragile states. If a drought hits Chad or the DRC, and a Western RCPS fund wires a hundred million dollars to the regime in power, that money will not become a broad-based rural employment scheme. It will become a slush fund for the ruling elite to fortify their military position, buy off political rivals, and suppress dissent. Miguel uses Botswana as his model for success, noting its effective Drought Relief Program. But he confuses the *mechanism* with the *institution*. Botswana’s program works because Botswana is a functional, accountable democracy with a capable civil service. The RCPS model tries to export a mechanism without the underlying institutions that make it work.

If we want to support Africa’s economic turnaround, we need to stop thinking about what the West can *give* and start thinking about what the West can stop *doing*.

Miguel touches on the most crucial point almost as an afterthought: trade barriers. The true path to African prosperity lies in full integration into global markets. The African Growth and Opportunity Act (AGOA) did more to spur actual industrial employment in Kenya and Lesotho than a decade of USAID grants. We should radically expand AGOA, eliminate all tariffs and quotas on African manufactured goods and agricultural products, and completely dismantle the U.S. and E.U. farm subsidies that crush African agricultural competitiveness.

The economic dynamism Miguel observed in Busia—the supermarkets, the cell phones, the cross-border trade—was not created by an international aid committee. It was created by local entrepreneurs responding to market incentives. We must trust these "Searchers," as economist William Easterly calls them, to find their own paths to prosperity.

Africa is indeed turning a corner, but it is doing so precisely where the state and the international aid apparatus are getting out of the way. We should not jeopardize this progress by inventing new, complex foreign aid schemes. We should open our markets, buy African goods, and let African entrepreneurs build their own future.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Miguel’s proposed RCPS ignores the political reality that in fragile states, emergency cash infusions often become slush funds for ruling elites rather than broad-based relief for citizens.
  2. 02The success of Botswana’s drought relief is due to its robust democratic institutions, which cannot be replicated simply by exporting the program's mechanism to less stable nations.
  3. 03True economic development is driven by local 'searchers' and entrepreneurs responding to market incentives rather than by international aid committees.
  4. 04The West should focus on radically expanding trade agreements like the African Growth and Opportunity Act (AGOA) and eliminating agricultural subsidies that undermine African competitiveness.
  5. 05Africa’s progress is most evident in areas where the state and international aid apparatus have stepped aside to allow for global market integration.
