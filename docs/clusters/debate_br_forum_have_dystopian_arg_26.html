"While the risks of AI to labor and democracy are significant, any strategy to redirect the technology must account for the reality of global strategic competition with authoritarian powers."

Daron Acemoglu ends with the international problem, but it deserves to be moved closer to the beginning. AI is developing in a world of strategic rivalry, authoritarian resurgence, cyber conflict, and military competition. Any proposal to redirect AI must grapple with the fact that democratic societies are not choosing in isolation. If liberal democracies restrain themselves while authoritarian powers aggressively develop surveillance, cyber, and military AI, the result may not be a more humane technological future. It may be a world in which the least restrained actors set the terms.

This does not invalidate Acemoglu’s warning. On the contrary, authoritarian uses of AI are among the clearest evidence for his concern. China’s deployment of surveillance technology in Xinjiang, its censorship systems, and its broader ambitions for social control show how AI can strengthen repression. Other governments have purchased spyware, facial recognition, and monitoring tools to track dissidents and journalists. The danger is real. But it means democracies need both restraint and capability. They must regulate AI at home without falling behind in the technologies needed to defend open societies.

The national security stakes are broad. AI can improve cyber defense and cyber offense, intelligence analysis, logistics, drone systems, satellite imagery, electronic warfare, disinformation detection, and command decision support. It can help identify biological threats, secure infrastructure, and monitor arms control. It can also enable autonomous weapons, automated propaganda, deepfakes, and more effective repression. The same dual-use character that complicates domestic regulation complicates international strategy.

A purely moral appeal to researchers will not be enough. Many scientists may refuse to work on weapons or surveillance, but states will continue to pursue them. If democratic researchers withdraw entirely from security applications, the field may be left to less accountable actors. The better position is not “AI should not be used for national security,” but “AI used for national security must be governed by democratic law, human rights, and strategic prudence.”

This requires drawing distinctions. There is a difference between AI that helps defend networks from cyberattacks and AI that hacks journalists’ phones. There is a difference between using computer vision to analyze satellite images for arms control and using facial recognition to identify protesters. There is a difference between defensive military systems under human command and autonomous weapons that select and engage targets without meaningful human control. Democracies should not collapse these categories. They should ban or restrict the most dangerous uses while investing in legitimate defense.

Acemoglu’s concern about government demand for surveillance technology is well placed. Security agencies often overreach. They tend to collect first and ask legal questions later. The Snowden revelations showed that even democracies can build vast surveillance systems in secret. AI increases the temptation because it promises to make sense of enormous data streams. The lesson is not that democracies can do without intelligence capabilities; they cannot. The lesson is that secret power must be bounded by law: warrants, minimization, legislative oversight, inspectors general, adversarial review where possible, and public reporting.

The United States also needs credibility. It cannot condemn Chinese surveillance while allowing domestic law enforcement to deploy facial recognition without serious limits, or while permitting private data brokers to sell location data with minimal oversight. A democratic AI strategy must begin at home. Strict privacy laws, procurement rules, and civil-liberties protections are not luxuries; they are strategic necessities. They allow democracies to say that their technological model is different.

At the same time, democracies should avoid unilateral disarmament in AI research. Public funding should support privacy-preserving AI, secure hardware, robust machine learning, verification, cyber defense, and systems that keep humans meaningfully in control. The Department of Defense has historically funded technologies that later produced civilian benefits, including the Internet. The problem is not public security funding per se; it is secrecy without accountability and missions that conflict with democratic values. Civilian agencies such as NSF, NIH, and NIST should play larger roles, but defense research will remain part of the ecosystem.

International coordination is essential but difficult. Arms control in AI will not look like nuclear arms control. AI systems are often software-based, widely diffused, dual-use, and hard to verify. A treaty banning “AI weapons” in general would be vague and unenforceable. More promising are narrower agreements: prohibitions on autonomous nuclear launch decisions; requirements for meaningful human control over lethal force; norms against targeting civilian infrastructure with AI-enabled cyberattacks; export controls on surveillance tools used for repression; shared standards for military testing and safety; incident communication channels to prevent escalation.

Export control deserves particular attention. Democratic countries should not allow companies to sell facial recognition, spyware, and population-monitoring systems to abusive regimes. The Pegasus and Hacking Team controversies show that private vendors can become enablers of repression. Licensing regimes, sanctions, and human-rights due diligence should apply to surveillance technology. Universities and investors should also scrutinize partnerships with entities tied to military repression or mass surveillance.

But export controls must be designed carefully. If they are too broad, they may block beneficial technology transfer and push countries toward authoritarian suppliers. The goal should be to restrict tools clearly linked to repression while supporting open scientific collaboration in health, climate, agriculture, and education. Democratic alliances should offer alternatives: secure digital infrastructure, privacy-respecting identity systems, public health tools, and technical assistance for data protection. Otherwise, governments in the global South may accept Chinese or other authoritarian technologies because they are affordable and available.

Acemoglu’s labor-market concerns also intersect with geopolitics. If democratic societies fail to provide good jobs and shared prosperity, they become vulnerable to populism, polarization, and foreign influence. Economic insecurity is a national security issue. Communities devastated by deindustrialization are more likely to distrust institutions and embrace anti-democratic politics. A successful democratic AI strategy must therefore include domestic renewal: education, infrastructure, worker bargaining power, regional investment, and a safety net. The competition with authoritarian capitalism will not be won by surveillance and weapons alone. It will be won, if at all, by showing that open societies can deliver dignity.

However, we should be cautious about making “more jobs” the overriding standard for national AI policy. Strategic competition may require automation in some areas: semiconductor manufacturing, defense logistics, cyber operations, pandemic response, and resilient supply chains. If AI can reduce dependence on fragile or coercive supply networks, that may be valuable even if some tasks are automated. The goal should be to pair such investments with worker transition and domestic capability-building, not to reject automation categorically.

Semiconductors are a reminder that AI is not only algorithms. It depends on chips, rare earths, energy, cloud infrastructure, manufacturing equipment, talent, and supply chains. As of 2021, the global chip shortage has revealed how vulnerable modern economies are. A democratic AI agenda should include industrial policy: support for semiconductor production, secure supply chains, open standards, and public research. Without material capacity, ethical aspirations may depend on infrastructure controlled elsewhere.

The ideological contest also matters. Authoritarian governments often argue that liberal democracy is chaotic, inefficient, and unable to govern technology. If democracies respond to AI with paralysis—endless hearings, weak laws, corporate self-regulation, and widening inequality—that argument gains force. But if they build rules that protect privacy, encourage innovation, share gains, and preserve open debate, they can offer a compelling alternative. The aim should be democratic technological competence.

Acemoglu is right that AI’s future is in our hands, but “our” hands include adversaries’ hands. This makes the task harder. Democracies must walk a narrow path: strong enough to defend themselves, restrained enough not to become what they oppose, innovative enough to lead, and democratic enough to deserve leadership.

That balance will not be achieved by markets alone, nor by ethics statements alone. It requires public investment, alliances, export controls, civil-liberties law, labor renewal, and strategic clarity. The dystopian future is not only mass unemployment or domestic surveillance. It is also a world in which authoritarian systems use AI to entrench power at home and project influence abroad while democracies argue themselves into weakness. Avoiding that future means building democratic AI capacity—not abandoning the field, but governing it with the values we claim to defend.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02The dual-use nature of AI requires drawing clear distinctions between legitimate national security applications and those that violate human rights, rather than a total withdrawal from security research.
  3. 03To maintain international credibility and a distinct technological model, democracies must implement strict domestic regulations on surveillance and private data usage.
  4. 04International coordination should focus on narrow, enforceable agreements and export controls to prevent the sale of repressive technologies to abusive regimes.
  5. 05Economic renewal and shared prosperity are national security imperatives, as failing to provide good jobs makes democratic societies vulnerable to polarization and foreign influence.
  6. 06A successful democratic AI strategy requires proactive industrial policy and public investment in infrastructure, such as semiconductors, to ensure material independence from authoritarian supply chains.
