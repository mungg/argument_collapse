"Federal tax policy can effectively distinguish the rich from the middle class by utilizing precise, income-based phaseouts of deductions and credits rather than blunt, sweeping eliminations of tax benefits."

The swift collapse of the 529 proposal reveals a dangerous political reflex, but it does not mean tax policy is incapable of drawing a clear line between the rich and the middle class. The failure was one of design and framing, not principle. The administration applied a blunt instrument—eliminating a deduction entirely—rather than a targeted phaseout. A far more durable approach is to define the distinction directly through income thresholds built into the tax code itself. The current marginal rate structure already begins this work, but it can be sharpened by phasing out deductions, credits, and exemptions as adjusted gross income rises. For instance, the mortgage interest deduction could be capped at a loan value well below the current $1 million limit, and the deduction for state and local taxes could be reduced for couples earning more than $250,000. These measures affect only a small sliver of taxpayers, roughly the top 3 percent, and they capture the genuinely affluent without ensnaring the two-earner professional household that the 529 debate clumsily weaponized. The key is precision: a family earning $200,000 in a high-cost city is not poor, but their financial life is radically different from someone pulling in $500,000 in capital income. By using multiple income thresholds tied to different types of income and deductions, Congress can protect the upper-middle while raising revenue from the truly wealthy. That differentiation is bureaucratically simple and politically possible if legislators focus on the data showing that the top 1 percent have captured nearly all income gains since the recovery began. The 529 debacle should not discourage such efforts; it should teach us that distinguishing the rich from the middle class requires scalpel-like phaseouts, not sweeping eliminations.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Implementing multiple income thresholds allows the tax code to differentiate between high-earning professional households and the truly affluent who rely on capital income.
  2. 02Capping or reducing specific deductions, such as those for mortgage interest and state taxes, targets the top 3 percent of earners without burdening the broader middle class.
  3. 03Gradual phaseouts are more politically viable and durable than the total removal of popular tax breaks like 529 plans.
  4. 04Focusing on data regarding the concentration of wealth in the top 1 percent provides a clear justification for sharpening the marginal rate structure.
