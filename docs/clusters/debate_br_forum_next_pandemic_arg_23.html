"While Sheila Jasanoff’s call for 'technologies of humility' is morally compelling, it lacks the practical utility and specific protocols necessary to function as an effective alternative to traditional public policy preparedness frameworks."

Sheila Jasanoff has written an elegant essay that will resonate with anyone who watched pandemic preparedness frameworks collapse under the weight of reality. Her critique of overconfident prediction, her invocation of precaution, and her plea for humility are intellectually seductive. But elegance is not the same as utility, and I worry that her prescription, however morally appealing, may be even less actionable than the frameworks she critiques.

Consider the core of her argument: we need "technologies of humility"—institutional mechanisms for incorporating memory, experience, and concerns for justice into governance. This sounds reasonable until we ask what it means in practice. Who designs these technologies? Who decides which memories count, whose experience matters, and what counts as justice? Jasanoff gestures toward "greater citizen participation," but the history of participatory governance is not encouraging. Citizens' assemblies, town halls, and deliberative polls have consistently been captured by organized interests with the time and resources to participate. The unvaccinated, the hourly worker unable to take time off for a public meeting, the parent without childcare—these are not the people who show up at forums designed by foundations seeking meaningful engagement.

The Obama playbook, which Jasanoff critiques, at least had the virtue of specificity. It said: create a dashboard, define alert levels, establish coordinating structures. You can disagree with its premises, but you can see what it was trying to do. By contrast, "cultivate humility" is a disposition, not a protocol. It cannot be implemented by executive order or measured by an indicator. The essay's own examples illustrate this problem. The Japanese government distributing masks to every household was not practicing humility—it was exercising the kind of unified command that Jasanoff associates with the heroic overreach of preparedness. What made it work was not humility but capacity: a functioning state apparatus, social trust, and the willingness of citizens to follow guidance. Countries that lacked these conditions could not have implemented the same approach by adopting a humbler mindset.

Jasanoff is right that preparedness frameworks ignored politics. The GHSI treated political will as an input, not a variable to be explained. The playbook assumed a unified national response that never materialized. But the solution to political failures is political analysis, not epistemological humility. The United States' pandemic failures had concrete causes: a president who saw mask mandates as a personal defeat rather than a public health tool, acdc that prioritized speed over clarity, state governments that competed for resources rather than coordinating responses, media ecosystems that weaponized uncertainty. These were not failures of humility. They were failures of governance, accountability, and political will—precisely the factors that Jasanoff acknowledges but does not explain.

The essay also conflates different kinds of uncertainty in ways that obscure rather than clarify. Not all unknowns are the same. The fact that the virus mutated was a biological uncertainty that better surveillance might have anticipated. The fact that a significant portion of the population would refuse vaccination reflected political and cultural forces that had nothing to do with scientific prediction. The fact that supply chains failed was a logistical problem that basic risk management could have addressed. lumping these together under the heading of "unknown unknowns" suggests that humility is the answer to problems that required specific expertise. The essay comes close to suggesting that if we just admitted we didn't know everything, things would turn out better. But the countries that handled the pandemic best—Taiwan, South Korea, Vietnam—did not do so by being humble. They did so by acting decisively on the information they had, building on experiences with SARS, and implementing complex but specific interventions.

Jasanoff's invocation of Lear is moving, but Lear's insight came after catastrophe, not before. We cannot manufacture the perspective of the marginalized through institutional design. We can, however, build systems that are robust to failure, that assume imperfection, and that can adapt when things go wrong. This is not humility in her sense, but it is a kind of epistemic modesty: not claiming to know how things will turn out, building in feedback mechanisms, and being willing to change course. That is a more modest ambition than technologies of humility, but it is also more achievable—and perhaps that is what we should expect from public policy.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The proposal for greater citizen participation is idealistic and fails to account for the historical reality that deliberative forums are often captured by organized interests rather than the marginalized groups Jasanoff seeks to include.
  2. 02Jasanoff’s examples of successful pandemic responses, such as Japan’s mask distribution, actually demonstrate the effectiveness of state capacity and unified command rather than the practice of humility.
  3. 03The failures of the COVID-19 response were primarily rooted in concrete political and governance breakdowns—such as lack of accountability and state-level competition—rather than a lack of epistemological humility.
  4. 04The essay conflates distinct types of biological, logistical, and political uncertainties, suggesting a singular mindset of humility as a solution for problems that actually require specific expertise and decisive action.
  5. 05Effective public policy should focus on building robust, adaptable systems with feedback mechanisms rather than attempting to institutionalize a vague moral disposition like humility.
