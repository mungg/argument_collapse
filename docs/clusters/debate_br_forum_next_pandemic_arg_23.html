The Humility of Leaving People Alone

Sheila Jasanoff’s meditation on pandemic policy is an honest effort to grapple with the spectacular failure of expert prediction. Her diagnosis—that the preparedness paradigm was fatally afflicted by hubris—deserves to be taken seriously. But her remedy, a suite of “technologies of humility” that would bring citizen participation, historical memory, and moral deliberation more deeply into the machinery of the state, misunderstands the very nature of the humility that a free society should cultivate. True political humility does not consist of more sophisticated mechanisms for collective decision-making. It consists of recognizing that there are hard limits on what government can know and should do, and that the most precautionary act of all is often to refrain from imposing one-size-fits-all edicts on millions of individuals who face locally distinct risks and hold widely varying values.

The pandemic did not expose a deficit of state humility; it exposed the enormous dangers of state overreach dressed in the language of science. In the United States, the most consequential early failures were not the absence of citizen advisory panels. They were the catastrophic decisions by federal and state officials to shut down economic and social life, often without clear exit criteria, and to issue continually shifting guidance about masks, surfaces, and outdoor transmission that eroded public trust. These measures were taken in the name of preparedness, but they were also the product of a particular kind of arrogance: the assumption that epidemiologists and public health mandarins could weigh the totality of costs and benefits for an entire population, substituting their own judgment for the diverse, dispersed knowledge held by individuals and communities.

Jasanoff’s call for “technologies of humility” seeks to correct this arrogance by incorporating more perspectives into the expert apparatus. But this merely broadens the circle of those who presume to plan for others. A citizen consultation process can lend a veneer of legitimacy to decisions that ought to be left to individuals. When a school board, after holding a series of listening sessions, decides to mandate masks for all children regardless of the local case rate or the particular vulnerabilities of families, it has not acted humbly; it has simply added a procedural step before imposing a uniform rule. True humility would mean allowing parents, teachers, and local communities to make their own risk assessments, with the best available information provided transparently, and without the threat of state sanction if they decline the official consensus.

This is not a libertarian caricature. It flows directly from the precautionary principle that Jasanoff herself invokes. The principle counsels against actions that may produce serious or irreversible harm, even when full scientific certainty is lacking. If we take that seriously, we must apply it to pandemic restrictions themselves. Lockdowns, school closures, and mask mandates generated enormous collateral damage: learning loss among disadvantaged children, mental health crises among adolescents, delayed cancer screenings, and the decimation of small businesses. Those harms were not distributed randomly; they fell hardest on those with the fewest resources to cushion the blow. A genuinely precautionary approach would have demanded far more restraint, far more attention to the possible irreversible harms of the interventions themselves, and far more willingness to accept that no central authority can possibly know the right balance for every household.

Jasanoff wants us to ask “who might get hurt” by our decisions, but she seems to imagine that only the virus hurts, and that only state inaction causes suffering. The evidence of the past two years strongly suggests otherwise. A humbler government would have stuck to its essential functions: ensuring that hospitals had surge capacity, investing in vaccines and therapeutics at speed, and providing clear, honest information about evolving scientific understanding. It would have resisted the temptation to micromanage how citizens conducted their daily lives, recognizing that the knowledge required to navigate risk is inevitably local, personal, and plural. Such a posture does not descend into the nihilistic passivity that critics of precaution fear. It is an active, disciplined commitment to distinguishing between the limited domains where collective action is indispensable and the vast terrain where freedom and individual judgment should hold sway.

By seeking to embed humility in yet another set of institutional processes—commissions, participatory mapping exercises, vulnerability assessments—Jasanoff inadvertently extends the domain of the managerial state. She would replace the heroic general with a humble social worker, but both still hold the reins. The deeper lesson of COVID-19 is that the reins must be loosened dramatically, not merely handed to a more empathetic driver.
