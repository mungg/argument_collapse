"The current crisis of democracy is primarily a moral and cultural collapse of civic virtue rather than a failure of institutional or legislative mechanics."

Ira Katznelson’s rich essay orbits a truth it never quite lands on: the primary locus of democratic anxiety is not institutional decay but moral and cultural collapse. He glances at the “disappearance of civic virtue” and the longing for “background agreement,” but he treats these as conditions that enable good legislative process rather than as the substance of democracy itself. In fact, liberal representative government was always a fragile cultural achievement, one that depends on a citizenry habituated to self-restraint, respect for opponents, and a willingness to accept losing. The crisis we face in 2015 is that these habits are dissolving under a cultural onslaught of untethered individualism, partisan tribalism, and a media environment that rewards outrage. Repairing the dome will mean nothing if we do not repair the soul.

Consider the way political disagreement now functions. For much of American history, even amid sharp conflicts, there existed a civic vocabulary that acknowledged the legitimacy of the other side. Lincoln’s first inaugural pleaded with Southerners not as enemies but as friends. The mid-twentieth century saw bitter clashes over civil rights and Vietnam, yet Congress still managed to pass the Great Society legislation, the Clean Air Act, and a host of bipartisan measures. What has changed is not simply that we disagree, but that we increasingly view opponents not as fellow citizens with differing visions of the common good but as corrupt, malignant, or un-American. The rise of cable news, talk radio, and social media as primary information sources has fed a polarization that is less about policy differences than about identity and contempt.

Katznelson’s ideal of “moral ambiguity” and acceptance of provisional outcomes is precisely what this cultural climate destroys. When your side is fighting pure evil, compromise is treason. The 2013 government shutdown, the scuttling of immigration reform, the transformation of Supreme Court nominations into blood sport—these are not failures of legislative craftsmanship; they are symptoms of a citizenry that has forgotten how to be a people. The background consensus Katznelson calls for is not a policy agreement but a shared moral framework that transcends politics: a belief that the democratic game is worth playing, that the adversary has a right to argue, that the common life of the nation matters more than any single issue. That framework is fraying.

Restoring it will take more than procedural tweaks or campaign-finance reforms. It requires a cultural countermovement of the sort that has sustained democracies in times of stress before. The settlement-house movement, the civil-rights movement’s commitment to nonviolence, the civic-education zeal of the early republic—these were efforts to form citizens, not just voters. Today, we need a renewed investment in civic education that teaches not just the mechanics of government but the virtues of deliberation, tolerance, and public-spiritedness. We need civic organizations that bring people of different backgrounds together in face-to-face projects, breaking the filter bubbles. We need elites—political, media, business—to model a different tone, one that values truth and humility over ratings and clicks.

Critics will call this a nostalgic fantasy, and it is true that a homogeneous civic culture can be oppressive. But what Katznelson evokes in his closing—a democracy that “legitimizes decisions on the basis of formal, procedural correctness without distinction of content”—is only possible if citizens can tolerate such abstraction. That tolerance is a moral achievement, and it takes work. Without it, we will continue to see populist movements and plebiscitary shortcuts that promise to bypass the messiness of representation, not because the mechanics are broken but because the spirit has fled. The scaffolding on the Capitol is a metaphor for the outer repairs. The inner work—on our hearts, our communities, our common life—must begin at once.
