"The COVID-19 pandemic demonstrated that effective public policy requires 'technologies of humility' that prioritize social, moral, and political realities over purely technical or militaristic preparedness models."

Sheila Jasanoff is right to say that the pandemic exposed a failure deeper than a shortage of masks, tests, or hospital beds. The United States did not merely fail to execute a plan; it failed to imagine the society in which any plan would have to operate. That is the most important insight in her essay. A country can have dashboards, stockpiles, tabletop exercises, and elaborate indexes of health security, yet still be unprepared if it has not asked who will be believed, who will be protected, who will be sacrificed, and who will be blamed when uncertainty arrives.

The most striking fact about COVID-19 is not that experts failed to predict a pandemic. They did predict one, repeatedly. Nor is it that no one knew respiratory viruses could move rapidly through a connected world. That had been obvious at least since SARS, and arguably since 1918. The striking fact is that many formal preparedness efforts treated the political and moral conditions of public health as secondary variables rather than as the ground on which policy stands. Jasanoff’s “technologies of humility” are valuable because they point precisely to this neglected ground.

Consider the early American response. The country’s problems were often described as failures of coordination: the CDC’s faulty test, shortages of personal protective equipment, incoherent federal messaging, state-by-state competition for supplies. These were real failures. But beneath them lay social failures that were no less operational. Many workers could not isolate because they lacked paid leave. Many families could not treat school closure as an inconvenience because school was also child care, food security, disability support, and a portal to social services. Many older people died in long-term care facilities not simply because the virus entered those facilities, but because care work is undervalued, understaffed, and segmented from mainstream medicine. Black, Latino, and Indigenous communities suffered disproportionate deaths not because the virus had preferences, but because American society had already distributed exposure and vulnerability along lines of race, class, housing, occupation, incarceration, and chronic disease.

These were not “unknown unknowns.” They were known facts that did not count enough in the dominant architecture of preparedness. A pandemic plan that does not begin with inequality is not a plan for the United States. It is a plan for an imaginary country.

Jasanoff’s critique of the Global Health Security Index is especially telling. Before COVID-19, the United States and United Kingdom ranked at the top of that index. By late 2021 both had suffered terrible mortality. Meanwhile countries rated as less prepared sometimes did better, at least in the first phases. The mismatch should not be dismissed as a mere failure of measurement. It suggests that what many experts called “capacity” was too heavily identified with formal institutions, technical expertise, laboratories, surveillance systems, and written plans. These matter enormously. But a society’s capacity to respond also depends on trust, legitimacy, administrative competence, habits of solidarity, and the ability to protect people materially while asking them to change behavior.

Humility, in this sense, is not anti-scientific. It is an argument for better science advice because it asks science to locate itself inside society rather than above it. Public health is never just the delivery of correct information to passive recipients. It is a relationship. People interpret guidance through memories of past neglect, present hardship, political identity, religious obligation, workplace pressure, family duty, and media ecosystems. If public officials do not understand those interpretive worlds, they will be surprised again and again by “irrational” behavior that was quite predictable to anyone listening closely.

The debate over masks offers a compact example. Early in 2020 American authorities discouraged public masking, partly from concern about supply shortages for health workers and partly because evidence on community masking was still developing. Later they recommended masks. Eventually masks became a symbol in the culture war. A humility-based approach would not have magically solved this problem, but it would have forced officials to confront the moral and communicative costs of strategic simplification. If masks were discouraged to preserve supplies, the public deserved candor. If evidence was changing, officials needed to explain uncertainty without pretending that reversal meant incompetence. If some communities distrusted mandates, governments needed intermediaries who were already trusted, not simply more press conferences from national experts.

The same applies to vaccination. The mRNA vaccines are among the great scientific achievements of our time. Their development and authorization in less than a year prevented many deaths and showed the power of public investment, biomedical knowledge, and regulatory urgency. Yet the vaccine campaign also revealed the limits of technological salvation. A country cannot vaccinate its way out of distrust overnight. It cannot spend decades allowing medical deserts, predatory health systems, racialized mistreatment, and pharmaceutical profiteering, then expect universal confidence when a new product appears under emergency conditions.

This is where Jasanoff’s emphasis on memory is crucial. Vaccine hesitancy is not one thing. For some Americans it reflects conspiratorial thinking amplified by partisan media. For others it reflects religious or bodily concerns. For some Black Americans it is linked to histories of medical exploitation and ongoing disparities in treatment. For many working-class people, the issue is not abstract mistrust but practical insecurity: Will I lose wages if side effects keep me home? Can I get to a clinic? Will immigration enforcement be involved? Can I trust a health system that normally sends me bills I cannot pay? A preparedness model centered on doses delivered misses these questions until they become “barriers.” A humble model treats them as constitutive.

Still, humility must not become an all-purpose virtue word. It needs institutional form. Jasanoff suggests greater citizen participation, attention to justice, and incorporation of memory. I would add several practical requirements.

First, pandemic planning should include social vulnerability as a central metric, not an appendix. Preparedness exercises should ask: Which workers cannot comply with isolation guidance without income support? Which neighborhoods have crowded housing? Which languages must be used? Which communities lack primary care? Which local organizations can distribute supplies? Which groups have reasons to distrust authorities? The answers should trigger automatic policies: paid sick leave, eviction protection, food distribution, mobile clinics, workplace enforcement, and targeted ventilation support.

Second, public health institutions need standing relationships before crisis. Trust cannot be manufactured during emergency briefings. Departments should fund partnerships with churches, unions, schools, immigrant organizations, disability advocates, tribal governments, and mutual aid networks. These partners should not merely transmit official messages; they should shape them. Listening is slower than issuing orders, but orders unsupported by legitimacy are slower still when they fail.

Third, uncertainty should be communicated as a normal feature of knowledge, not as an embarrassment. One reason experts fear candor is that bad-faith actors exploit ambiguity. But overconfidence is also exploitable. When officials speak with more certainty than evidence warrants, inevitable revisions look like deceit. The public can handle “we do not yet know” if it is paired with “here is what we are doing while we learn, and here is how we will revise course.”

Fourth, preparedness must include democratic accountability for tradeoffs. School closures, business restrictions, hospital triage, vaccine prioritization, and mask mandates involve values as well as facts. Experts can estimate risks, but they cannot by themselves decide whose risks count most. Humility requires visible processes for weighing harms: viral spread, learning loss, domestic violence, mental health, livelihood, disability, racial inequality, and death. The alternative is not value-free science; it is hidden values disguised as necessity.

Finally, humility must be global. By December 2021 the world has witnessed not only national failures but moral failure in vaccine distribution. Wealthy countries secured large supplies while many poorer countries waited. This is not merely charity neglected; it is epidemiological folly. Variants emerge where transmission continues. A pandemic is a test of global interdependence, and the wealthiest states have often acted as if sovereignty could purchase insulation. Humility would recognize that no national plan is sufficient in a world of unequal exposure.

Jasanoff’s essay should not be read as an attack on preparedness. We still need stockpiles, surveillance, testing capacity, genomic sequencing, hospital readiness, and rapid vaccine platforms. The lesson is not that prediction is useless, but that prediction without humility is brittle. It sees the pathogen more clearly than the people among whom the pathogen moves.

The next pandemic plan should therefore begin with a sentence rarely found in emergency manuals: “We may be wrong, and our errors will not fall equally.” From that premise follows a different politics—less heroic, less technocratic, more democratic, and more just. It would not guarantee success. Humility promises no conquest. But it may help us avoid the repeated tragedy of being surprised by what our society has already told us.

"True humility in public policy requires moving beyond elite self-correction to a redistribution of power and epistemic authority that prioritizes the perspectives and needs of marginalized communities and nations."

The most important word in Sheila Jasanoff’s essay is not “prediction,” “precaution,” or even “humility.” It is “who.” Who is imagined by preparedness? Who is protected by it? Who is asked to sacrifice? Who is treated as a source of knowledge, and who appears only as a risk factor, a problem population, or a belated obstacle to compliance? Once these questions are placed at the center, the pandemic looks less like a universal shock to humanity than a revelation of an already unequal world.

COVID-19 has often been described as a great leveler because everyone is biologically susceptible. That description was always misleading. The virus may infect bodies, but it travels through social arrangements: borders, workplaces, households, prisons, care homes, hospitals, meatpacking plants, dormitories, shelters, buses, churches, and refugee camps. It follows the routes laid down by labor markets, empire, racial capitalism, and state neglect. The pandemic therefore did not simply test preparedness. It tested the moral geography of power.

Jasanoff rightly criticizes the Global Health Security Index for ranking the United States and United Kingdom at the top shortly before both suffered grave losses. But the index’s failure was not only technical. It reflected a familiar hierarchy of global knowledge. Wealthy Western states appeared as models of capacity; poorer states appeared as deficits awaiting improvement. The result was a map that confused resources with resilience and institutional sophistication with social protection. It missed the ways rich countries can be brittle: polarized, unequal, privatized, distrustful, and politically arrogant.

Several countries that had experienced SARS, MERS, Ebola, or other outbreaks treated COVID-19 with a seriousness that many Western governments lacked. Some had memory embedded in institutions and public behavior. Some had border screening, masking norms, community health networks, or rapid-response systems shaped by prior epidemics. In West Africa, the memory of Ebola influenced public health practice even where resources were limited. In East Asia, SARS left lessons about speed, masks, quarantine, and public communication. These examples should not be romanticized; every country faced tradeoffs and made mistakes. But they challenge the assumption that preparedness knowledge flows from the rich world outward.

The United States’ early failure was partly a failure to learn from others. Masking, for example, was treated at first by many American and European commentators as culturally foreign, unnecessary, or even suspect. Travel restrictions became a substitute for domestic readiness. Testing failures were excused as temporary technical glitches while other countries built more effective systems. The possibility that Americans might learn from South Korea, Vietnam, Senegal, Taiwan, or Rwanda rarely fit the national imagination. Western expertise was comfortable globalizing advice, less comfortable receiving it.

This is where humility must become more than an attitude of elite self-correction. It must become a redistribution of epistemic authority. Communities and countries commonly treated as backward, peripheral, or merely vulnerable often know things that powerful institutions do not. They know how disease control is experienced by those who cannot work from home. They know how rumor circulates when officials have lied before. They know why quarantine without food is coercion, why isolation without income is fantasy, why vaccination campaigns fail when tied to histories of experimentation or neglect. They know that “public health” can look like policing if delivered by a state that usually arrives armed.

In the United States, racial disparities in COVID-19 mortality were sometimes discussed as tragic surprises. They were not. Black, Latino, and Indigenous communities entered the pandemic with higher burdens of chronic illness, greater exposure in essential work, crowded housing, less access to care, and justified mistrust of medical institutions. The category “essential worker” became a moral euphemism. It praised people whose labor sustained society while often denying them adequate protection, wages, health insurance, child care, or the right to refuse dangerous conditions. A humble preparedness system would have started there: not with the average citizen, but with the grocery clerk, home health aide, farmworker, bus driver, nurse, incarcerated person, undocumented worker, and elder in a nursing home.

Globally, the equivalent failure is vaccine inequality. By December 2021 wealthy countries have administered boosters to many while large portions of the world remain without first doses. The rhetoric of global solidarity has not matched the reality of procurement, intellectual property disputes, manufacturing concentration, and national hoarding. COVAX was presented as a mechanism for fairness, but it has struggled against the purchasing power and political priorities of rich states. Meanwhile the emergence of Omicron in southern Africa has triggered travel bans that many African leaders and scientists regard as punitive, especially given South Africa’s transparency in identifying and reporting the variant. The message is bleak: share data quickly, and you may be economically punished; wait for vaccines, and you may be blamed for variants.

This is not humility. It is the old imperial reflex in epidemiological form. The danger is imagined as coming from elsewhere; protection is imagined as closing the gates; responsibility is imagined as domestic first and global later, if at all. But in a pandemic, “later” is a dangerous fiction. Variants emerge where transmission continues. Health systems collapse where vaccines, oxygen, therapeutics, and staff are scarce. The lives lost in poorer countries are not unfortunate externalities of national policy; they are part of the same event.

Jasanoff’s “technologies of humility” could help here if they are made genuinely global. They would ask not only how the United States can prepare better, but how global rules produce vulnerability. Who controls vaccine patents and manufacturing know-how? Who has the capacity to sequence variants? Who gets oxygen and personal protective equipment? Who bears debt burdens that limit health spending? Who can close borders without starving workers dependent on tourism or remittances? Who defines success: low mortality in wealthy countries, or reduced suffering everywhere?

The pandemic has also exposed the weakness of charity as a model for global health. Donations are welcome, but they preserve hierarchy. A country waiting for surplus doses from rich states is not an equal participant in pandemic governance. A more humble order would build distributed manufacturing capacity, strengthen regional public health institutions, support universal health systems, and treat health workers’ safety as a global priority. It would not assume that innovation happens in one place and need in another. Scientific capacity exists across the world; what is lacking is often financing, legal permission, supply chains, and political respect.

Within countries, humility likewise requires power-sharing. Citizen participation cannot mean inviting a few representatives to comment after decisions have been made. It means giving affected communities authority over priorities and implementation. For example, vaccine outreach in marginalized communities works best when local organizations are funded and trusted to design strategies. Workplace safety requires workers to have enforceable rights, not merely posters about handwashing. School reopening requires ventilation investments and consultation with teachers, parents, students, and disability advocates. Quarantine requires income, housing, food, and protection from immigration enforcement. These are not soft supplements to “real” public health. They are public health.

There is, however, a tension in Jasanoff’s essay that deserves attention. Humility can sound like a virtue demanded of experts and policymakers, but the structures producing pandemic vulnerability are not just cognitive errors. They are interests. Employers resisted workplace protections. Politicians exploited resentment. Pharmaceutical firms defended intellectual property. Wealthy countries bought more doses than they needed. Media outlets profited from outrage. Some harms persisted not because powerful actors failed to imagine them, but because preventing them would have required redistributing money, authority, and security.

Thus humility must be joined to justice. To ask “who might get hurt?” is only the first step. The next question is: who benefits from arrangements that make them hurt? Without that second question, humility risks becoming an ethical language for institutions that listen politely and continue as before.

The pandemic is not over. Omicron has reminded the world how quickly uncertainty returns. Yet enough is already clear. Preparedness cannot be measured only by laboratories, plans, and command structures. It must be measured by whether societies protect those made vulnerable by their own arrangements. It must be measured by whether nations learn from one another without arrogance. It must be measured by whether global health is organized around solidarity rather than hierarchy.

A humble pandemic policy would begin from the margins, not because suffering confers automatic wisdom, but because the margins reveal the whole system. The virus has shown us that no one is safe in a world built to abandon many. If public policy after pandemic does not start there, it will be prepared only for the next failure.
