The Purity Trap: Why Roemer’s Limits Won’t Fix Washington

Buddy Roemer is right about the disease: a Congress that “grovells” rather than governs, a political system auctioned to the highest bidder, and a public that has lost faith that its voice matters. His speech at Harvard eloquently captures the moral corrosion of a legislature where members spend the majority of their time dialing for dollars. When a Republican congressman like Jim Leach could think independently about derivatives because he refused PAC money, and when a Reagan Democrat like Roemer himself could cross the aisle because his hands were clean, we glimpse what a leader “free to lead” might look like. Yet despite the deep appeal of his diagnosis, Roemer’s proposed cure—a $100 individual contribution limit, no PACs, and a reliance on the Internet to marshal small donors—is dangerously naive. By mistaking purity for a solution, his approach would likely leave the system even more skewed toward the wealthy and the well-connected.

Consider what a $100 limit would actually mean in a post–Citizens United world. In the 2010 election cycle, independent expenditures by Super PACs and nonprofit groups, fueled by unlimited corporate and individual donations, surged more than 130 percent. A candidate who refuses all but tiny individual contributions—while laudable in spirit—cannot compete with outside groups that face no such restraint. In the presidential race Roemer now runs, he claims he could outraise McCain and Romney if one in one hundred Americans gave $100. That math may work in theory, but in practice the most reliable small donors are those activated by ideological firestorms, not by appeals to centrist independence. Moreover, his plan would effectively disarm campaigns while leaving outside spending untouched. The result would be a politics in which undisclosed, unaccountable outside groups dominate the conversation, and the candidates themselves become irrelevant moral spectators.

The prohibition on PACs raises similar concerns. Roemer calls PACs “money bundling and money laundering,” and there is truth to that for many corporate and trade association PACs. But PACs are also the vehicle through which labor unions and membership organizations like the Sierra Club collect modest contributions and amplify the voices of ordinary citizens. Ending all PACs would silence those collective efforts while doing nothing to stop billionaires from writing eight-figure checks directly to independent expenditure committees. In this asymmetry, the “99 percent” Roemer champions could end up even more marginalized.

The deeper flaw in Roemer’s approach is its assumption that the sheer number of small donors can overcome the influence of concentrated wealth. But money buys influence not simply through campaign contributions; it buys the permanent architecture of lobbying, the revolving door, and the massive analytical and legal apparatus that shapes legislation behind closed doors. Even if every member of Congress adopted Roemer’s pledge, lobbyists would still draft the bills, and the largest banks—now bigger than before the crisis—would still enjoy an implicit federal subsidy simply by being too big to fail. The Dodd-Frank debate he cites is a case in point: dozens of congressional aides who helped write that bill soon left to work for the very financial firms they were regulating. Campaign contributions fueled those outcomes, but the deeper corruption is the structural capture of the regulatory state.

Roemer is not wrong to dream of a Congress free to lead. But his $100 limit is a personal purity test, not a scalable reform. We need instead a system that gives every citizen the ability to participate meaningfully in funding elections, without vesting that power in the accidental generosity of the wealthy or the organizational muscle of existing interest groups. The democracy vouchers he mentions in passing—a small amount of public money that each taxpayer can allocate to candidates—could achieve his goal far more effectively than his voluntary limit. Combined with a constitutional amendment to overturn Citizens United, such a system could genuinely drown out big money with a flood of small, publicly funded contributions. That would require a movement, not a lone saint. Roemer’s campaign may inspire, but until reformers build political will for structural change, his promises will remain exactly what the money powers expect: a noble gesture that leaves their power undisturbed.
