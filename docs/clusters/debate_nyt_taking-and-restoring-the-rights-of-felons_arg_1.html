A felony conviction should not amount to civil death. The punishment authorized by law is the sentence: imprisonment, probation, restitution and supervision. Rights should be lost only when the loss is tied to that sentence or to a demonstrated public risk.

Other restrictions should follow the same principle. Jury service, public employment and occupational licenses should not be barred forever unless the crime is directly relevant. A society committed to punishment and redemption must distinguish between accountability and permanent exclusion.

The current pattern is hard to defend on principle. We have built a system in which a felon in some states can re-arm himself with a hunting rifle more easily than he can cast a ballot for sheriff. That ordering reflects political pressure, not any coherent theory of what conviction should cost a person.

A sensible framework begins with the purpose of each right. Voting is a baseline attribute of democratic citizenship; its temporary suspension during incarceration may be defensible as part of the punishment, but permanent or near-permanent disenfranchisement of people who have completed their sentences—as practiced in Florida, Kentucky, and Virginia—severs millions of citizens from the polity for no public-safety reason. Restoration should not be automatic or, as the Times reported, rubber-stamped by overworked judges with thin records. It should require a hearing, a waiting period, and a showing of rehabilitation.

In short: restore the ballot quickly and broadly; restore the gun slowly and carefully. We have largely inverted that.

A waiting period, a clean record for a substantial number of years, and a judicial finding of no threat should be prerequisites before a felon regains the right to own a weapon.

Justice requires proportionality. Restoration of the franchise honors the ideal that punishment has an end; cautious, evidence-based reinstatement of gun rights ensures that liberty does not come at the expense of lives. The current patchwork is a failure of both principle and policy.
