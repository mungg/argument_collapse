"While Lea Ypi’s critique of the global border regime is powerful, it is limited by a Eurocentric intellectual framework that ignores the colonial and racial foundations of the world-system."

Lea Ypi’s essay is a necessary and fierce indictment of the global order that produces mass displacement while criminalising those who move. But for all its critical power, it remains locked within a European intellectual frame that it fails to interrogate. Ypi’s heroes are Lessing and the Enlightenment; her analytical tools are drawn from dependency theory and a certain tradition of Western Marxism; her political horizon is a reformed state system and a revitalised labour internationalism. What is missing is the voice of the Global South—not as a case study of neoliberal victimhood, but as a source of alternative worldviews that challenge the very categories of the debate. From a decolonial perspective, the real border crisis is not a crisis of liberal democracy but a crisis of a world-system built on colonial dispossession, racial hierarchy, and the ongoing theft of land, labour, and life. Until we centre that history, any call for cosmopolitanism or class solidarity will reproduce the same patterns of erasure it claims to oppose.

Ypi begins with Nathan the Wise, a play set in twelfth-century Jerusalem under Muslim rule, written by a German Enlightenment thinker and staged for European audiences. The choice is revealing. The “cosmopolitan” stance of Nathan is one in which a European man ventriloquises a Jewish merchant to deliver a message of tolerance among the three monotheisms—all of which, not incidentally, originated in the Middle East but are treated as abstract civilisational contenders. The actual colonised subjects of the Crusades, the Eastern Christians, the local populations who lived under shifting empires, are absent. Ypi uses Nathan to advocate a forward-looking universalism, but universalism has a history: it was forged in the crucible of European imperialism, and it has routinely been deployed to justify interventions from the Balkans to Libya, as she herself notes. The problem is not just that the Enlightenment was betrayed by later capitalism; the problem is that the Enlightenment’s promise of freedom and equality was from the start predicated on the exclusion and subjugation of the non-European world. Kant may have dreamed of perpetual peace, but he also produced a racial hierarchy that classified Black and Indigenous peoples as incapable of full moral autonomy. To wave the banner of “sapere aude” without acknowledging this legacy is to invite the suspicion of those who have been on the receiving end of Europe’s civilising mission.

Ypi’s core argument—that migration is a class question, and that the left must reject culturalist explanations—is deeply Eurocentric in its own way. The distinction between class and culture is a product of a particular social-scientific tradition that does not map neatly onto the experiences of many societies where race, caste, indigeneity, and religion are not separable from economic exploitation. In Latin America, for example, the dispossession of Indigenous peoples from their land is simultaneously an economic, cultural, and racial process. The migrant caravans heading north from Central America are fleeing violence that is directly traceable to U.S. intervention and corporate land grabs, but they are also fleeing the destruction of communal ways of life that cannot be reduced to “class” in the European sense. When Ypi insists that political conflicts are “not between states and groups with different cultural profiles but between different social classes,” she inadvertently silences the specific claims of Indigenous nations, ethnic minorities, and caste-oppressed groups who seek not just redistribution but self-determination, cultural survival, and repair for historical wrongs.

The West’s border regime is not just a tool of capital; it is a racial regime. Australia’s offshore detention centres, the European Union’s funding of Libyan coast guards who enslave migrants, Israel’s deportation of African asylum seekers—these are not merely class phenomena. They are the continuation of a colonial logic that categorises certain bodies as inherently out-of-place and expendable. Ypi mentions the Trump administration’s chained deportation videos, but she does not connect them to the long history of chain gangs and racialised spectacle in the Americas. The selling of citizenship, too, is not just a class issue; it is a racialised one, because the wealthy who buy golden visas are almost always from other white-majority or elite non-white nations, while those kept out are disproportionately Black and brown. A decolonial analysis insists that we see these policies as part of a global colour line that structures mobility.

What would a response from the Global South look like? First, it would reject the framing of migration as a “crisis” for receiving states and instead name it as a crisis of the postcolonial state and the international order. The vast majority of refugees are hosted by countries in the Global South—Turkey, Pakistan, Uganda, Colombia—who receive a fraction of the resources spent on border enforcement in Europe and North America. The real scandal is not that some migrants arrive in the West but that the international system has normalised the warehousing of millions of displaced people in limbo, often for generations, while Western states treat their arrival as an emergency. A just response would demand not just expanded political representation for migrants within Western states, but a complete overhaul of the international refugee regime, including resettlement quotas that actually match the scale of displacement, reparations for the harms caused by colonial and neo-colonial wars, and the cancellation of debt that traps sending countries in austerity. Ypi’s call for class coordination is not wrong, but it must be complemented by a call for global reparative justice that acknowledges the stolen wealth that built the Western welfare states in the first place.

Second, a decolonised migration ethics would elevate the right to stay over the right to move. Ypi says the commitment to a world where nobody is forced to leave is the ultimate test, but then focuses most of her energy on the terms of entry into rich countries. A Global South-centred perspective would prioritise the fight against land grabs, climate extraction, and resource wars that drive people from their homes. It would demand that Western governments and corporations be held legally accountable for the displacement they cause—through arms sales to repressive regimes, through carbon emissions that sink island nations, through trade agreements that destroy local agriculture. This is not a defence of closed borders; it is an insistence that the primary obligation of the international community is to ensure that people can flourish where they are, in communities of their own making, rather than being treated as a reserve army of labour to be absorbed or expelled as needed.

Finally, such a perspective would be wary of any universalism that does not emerge from sustained, horizontal dialogue among equals. Ypi’s faith in the labour movement is touching, but the history of international unionism is littered with examples of white workers in the metropole accepting imperialism in exchange for a share of the spoils. The “Make Amazon Pay” campaign is a rare bright spot, but it is fragile. Building genuine solidarity across borders requires more than joint strikes; it requires dismantling the epistemic privilege of the Western left and its categories. It means listening to movements like the Zapatistas in Mexico, who refuse both state power and the language of classical Marxism, or to the Idle No More movement of Indigenous peoples in Canada, who ground their resistance in specific relations to land that transnational class politics cannot capture. The Enlightenment, for all its virtues, is not the only source of universal values; care, reciprocity, and reverence for the earth are found in traditions that colonialism tried to destroy. A truly cosmopolitan future would be one in which these diverse inheritances can speak, not one in which a European canon sets the terms of the conversation.

Ypi’s essay ends with a plea for a world “in which nobody is forced to leave their home.” That is a beautiful vision. But to honour it, we must first recognise that the very homes of many people—in Palestine, in the Sahel, in the Pacific islands—are being destroyed by a global order that is not just capitalist but colonial. The real border crisis is not that the West cannot manage its gates; it is that the gates exist only for the West, while the rest of the world is left to absorb the violence. Decolonising the migration debate means bringing the margins to the centre, and that begins with surrendering the comforting narrative that the Enlightenment is the only compass we have.
