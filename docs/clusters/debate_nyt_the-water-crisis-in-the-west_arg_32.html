"To ensure a sustainable and just future for the American West, water management must shift from a system of liquidation for powerful interests to a shared trust that prioritizes basic human needs, tribal rights, and ecological health through enforceable watershed-level budgets."

The question “How should we share the water?” is often asked as if the only claimants are cities and farms. That framing is too narrow. Western water is also owed to tribes, fishing communities, wetlands, endangered species, river deltas and future generations. A drought policy that divides every remaining drop among today’s most powerful users is not sharing. It is liquidation.

The West’s water law was built around diversion. “First in time, first in right” made sense to miners and irrigators trying to establish claims in the nineteenth century, but it has often ignored the people who were here first and the rivers themselves. Many tribal water rights are senior rights, yet they remain unquantified, underfunded or politically resisted. If we are going to enforce old legal priorities, we must enforce them for tribes as well as irrigation districts.

Environmental water is not a luxury to be considered after economic uses are satisfied. Rivers that are drained to a trickle stop being rivers. Salmon runs collapse. Wetlands disappear. The Sacramento-San Joaquin Delta, the Klamath Basin and the Colorado River Delta all show what happens when legal promises exceed ecological limits. The modest pulse flow sent into the Colorado River Delta this spring under a U.S.-Mexico agreement was a reminder that restoration is possible, but also that it requires water to be reserved, not merely hoped for.

Justice also requires attention to poor communities. In drought, wealthy neighborhoods can drill deeper wells, pay higher rates or buy bottled water. Many rural communities cannot. Some Central Valley towns already struggle with unsafe drinking water even in normal years. Farmworkers may lose jobs when fields are fallowed but rarely share in the compensation paid to landowners. A fair water policy must guarantee affordable drinking water and include economic help for those asked to bear the burden of conservation.

The practical answer is to create enforceable water budgets at the watershed level. Before water is allocated to discretionary uses, each basin should set aside: first, basic human needs; second, legally recognized tribal rights; third, minimum ecological flows needed to keep rivers and fisheries alive. Only then should remaining water be divided among farms, cities and industry. This will be controversial because it converts vague environmental aspirations into firm limits. But vague aspirations have not saved the rivers.

We also need institutions that match watersheds rather than political boundaries. The Colorado River is governed by compacts, court decrees, treaties and contracts layered on assumptions from unusually wet years. Climate change makes those assumptions even less reliable. Basin councils that include tribes, states, irrigation districts, cities, environmental scientists and Mexico would not eliminate conflict, but they would make tradeoffs explicit.

The West has always treated water as a means of conquest and growth. It now has to treat water as a shared trust. That means some fields will not be planted, some lawns will not survive, and some development will not be approved. But it also means rivers may continue to flow, tribes may finally receive what they are owed, and communities may endure drought without sacrificing the powerless first.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Current water laws must be reformed to honor senior tribal water rights that have historically been unquantified, underfunded, or politically resisted.
  2. 02Environmental water requirements should be treated as a non-negotiable necessity rather than a luxury to prevent the collapse of rivers, fisheries, and wetlands.
  3. 03Water policy must guarantee affordable drinking water and economic support for poor rural communities and farmworkers who disproportionately bear the burden of drought.
  4. 04Enforceable water budgets should be established at the watershed level to prioritize essential needs before allocating water to discretionary agricultural and industrial uses.
  5. 05Governance institutions should be restructured to align with natural watershed boundaries and include all stakeholders to better manage the realities of climate change.
  6. 06The West must accept limits on growth and development, such as fallowing fields and restricting lawns, to preserve the long-term viability of the region's water resources.
