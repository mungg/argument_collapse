"While the state remains a necessary site of struggle, a truly effective climate strategy must transcend state-centric analysis to embrace a multi-scalar approach and a political imagination that operates beyond national borders."

Táíwò's argument rests on a crucial insight that deserves fuller development: the planetary scope of the climate crisis demands strategies of corresponding scale. The "two-step" framework emerges from this recognition, arguing that the specific character of our historical moment—a moment defined by the existential threat of climate change—requires a specific strategic response. I want to push this insight further, arguing that the planetary dimension of climate change demands not just a revised left strategy but a fundamental reconceptualization of political possibility itself.

The nation-state system emerged from specific historical conditions: the need to organize production and exchange within territories defined by pre-capitalist boundaries, the requirements of capital accumulation as it sought markets and resources, the coordination problems inherent in maintaining order across diverse populations. These functions remain relevant, but the climate crisis exposes their limits with new clarity. Carbon molecules respect no borders. The emissions from a Chinese factory or an American power plant warm the atmosphere that all humans share. Climate change is the first truly global commons problem: it can only be solved through coordinated action at the planetary scale, yet the institutions capable of such coordination do not exist and face structural barriers to their creation.

Táíwò is right that states remain the primary actors in international politics and that any viable climate strategy must work through and against state power. But working through the state system means accepting its limitations. The Paris Agreement, the most ambitious multilateral climate framework yet constructed, relies entirely on voluntary national commitments backed by no enforcement mechanism. Its signatories include governments whose continued political survival depends on fossil fuel revenues, whose populations lack the economic alternatives that would make rapid decarbonization politically viable, and whose emissions are rising rather than falling. The state system, by its nature, privileges national interest over global goods.

This suggests that effective climate politics must operate simultaneously at multiple scales: the local, where communities can build resilience and experiment with alternatives; the national, where regulatory power and public investment can reshape energy systems; the regional, where blocs like the European Union can coordinate policy and leverage market power; and the international, where frameworks like the Fossil Fuel Non-Proliferation Treaty can establish norms and create pressure. No single scale can suffice.

The concept of "energy democracy" that Táíwò celebrates represents one expression of this multi-scalar approach. When New York passes the Build Public Renewables Act, it operates at the state scale. When Los Angeles sets renewable targets for its municipal utility, it operates at the local scale. When international organizations endorse the Fossil Fuel Non-Proliferation Treaty, they operate at the global scale. Each addresses different aspects of the problem and creates different possibilities for further action.

But Táíwò's argument risks creating a misleading hierarchy among these scales. By centering the state and state politics as the primary terrain of struggle, he may inadvertently strengthen the very structures that impede climate action. The state system was not designed to address problems of planetary scope; it allocates authority to territorially bounded units whose interests often conflict with global welfare. Working within the state system means accepting these constraints.

An alternative approach would begin not with the state but with the networks of solidarity that cross and transcend state boundaries. Climate movements have always understood this intuitively: the connections between affected communities, the solidarity between Global South and Global North activists, the shared knowledge and strategy that flow through international networks. These connections represent a form of political organization that state-centric analysis systematically underweights.

The climate crisis demands that we imagine political possibility beyond the state system while acknowledging that we cannot escape it. This is not a comfortable position, but it is an honest one. The planetary scale of the crisis requires not just new strategies but new forms of political imagination—forms adequate to the unprecedented nature of the threat we face.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The planetary nature of the climate crisis exposes the inherent limitations of the nation-state system, which is structurally designed to privilege national interests over global commons problems.
  2. 02Effective climate politics must operate simultaneously across local, national, regional, and international scales rather than prioritizing the state as the primary terrain of struggle.
  3. 03Centering the state risks reinforcing the very territorial structures that impede the coordinated global action necessary to address carbon emissions.
  4. 04Political strategy should be rooted in transnational networks of solidarity and grassroots connections that exist independently of state boundaries.
  5. 05The unprecedented scale of the climate threat requires a fundamental reconceptualization of political possibility that moves beyond the historical constraints of the state system.
