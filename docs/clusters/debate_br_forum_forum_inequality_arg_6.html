"While David Grusky correctly identifies that market inequality is driven by government-protected monopolies and rent-seeking, his solution should be a radical dismantling of state-granted privileges and regulations rather than new government-managed equalization programs."

David Grusky’s essay is a valuable reminder that much of what we call “market outcomes” are actually engineered by cozy arrangements between concentrated interests and the state. His call to attack inequality by restoring genuine competition, rather than simply taxing the resulting incomes and redistributing them, is a sharp break from the conventional progressive playbook. But having invited us to take the logic of competition seriously, Grusky then consistently stops short of allowing it to guide his own policy prescriptions. The bottlenecks and rigged rules he identifies in education and CEO pay are real enough, but their origins lie overwhelmingly in government-created distortions and privileges. The proper cure is not a different kind of central planning aimed at “equal opportunity,” but a systematic disassembly of the illegitimate monopolies and cartels that generate rent in the first place. A true institutional critique should lead us to radical deregulation and decentralization, not to new, supposedly competition-enhancing mandates.

Grusky’s analysis of educational bottlenecks is the clearest example of this halfway thinking. He correctly observes that the supply of elite college slots is artificially restricted, inflating the earnings premium for the credentialed few. But why is supply so restricted? The answer is not mysterious: most prestigious universities, public and private, operate as credential-granting cartels whose value depends on scarcity. And they are able to do so largely because government policy props them up. Federal student aid is doled out without regard to whether the institution is actually expanding capacity or lowering costs—it actually fuels price inflation, which deepens the bottleneck. State governments grant charters and accreditation monopolies that prevent innovative, low-cost competitors from entering the higher education market. Primary and secondary schooling is governed by a monopoly district system that denies poor families the opportunity to escape failing schools, while the colleges of education and union contracts Grusky doesn’t mention create an artificial barrier to entry for talented teachers without traditional credentials. If he truly believes that competition would produce “child’s play” reforms, then the remedy should be straightforward: break the accreditation monopoly, fund students directly with portable vouchers, allow for-profit and non-profit providers to compete on equal footing, and let the market drive down the price of a quality degree. That would expand the class of college-educated workers much faster than any government-led “overhaul” that, in practice, would be captured by the very universities and interest groups that benefit from the status quo. The history of public education reform is a graveyard of ambitious equalization plans that ended up channeling more money into the same failing systems. Competition, not centralized equalization, is the mechanism that makes the car industry responsive and hybrid cars affordable—yet the analogy Grusky draws somehow doesn’t extend to his policy conclusion.

The same pattern emerges on executive compensation. Grusky paints a picture of board cronyism and argues that the CEO pay-setting process is a “perversion of capitalism.” Many shareholders would agree, and they are right. But the root cause of unaccountable boards is not insufficient regulation; it is the regulatory insulation that shields management from true owner control. Thanks to Securities and Exchange Commission rules, the proxy voting system is stacked against shareholder resolutions, and large institutional investors are often deterred from coordinating. Moreover, the limited liability structure and corporate personhood doctrine that Grusky takes for granted are themselves grants of privilege from the state—they socialize risk and diffuse responsibility. If we want to end the rent-seeking Grusky denounces, we should make shareholders fully liable, or at least dismantle the legal barriers that prevent them from easily ousting underperforming or overpaid executives. A genuinely competitive market for corporate control would discipline CEO pay far more effectively than any government-designed compensation committee reform. And it’s notable that, in some sectors where competition is fiercest and regulatory barriers lowest, we see less of the gross pay excesses Grusky points to—compare high-tech startups to the heavily regulated financial industry, where implicit government guarantees systematically reward risk-taking and inflated compensation. The very visible hand Grusky laments is, more often than not, the hand of the state picking winners and punishing competition.

The deeper problem with Grusky’s reform agenda is its unexamined conception of “fair competition.” He wants a market in which everyone has an equal starting point, so that any resulting inequality is the legitimate reflection of effort and talent. But that goal is not only unattainable in any non-totalitarian society; it is actually destructive of the dynamic competition that raises living standards for everyone. The pursuit of entrepreneurial profit and high returns to exceptional skill—what Grusky might call “licit” income—will always produce large inequalities, and those inequalities are the signal that draws resources to their most valued uses. Bottlenecks in elite education are partly a problem, but they also reflect the genuine scarcity of rare cognitive talent that cannot be expanded simply by offering more college seats. If we equalize opportunity so completely that we erode the premium for high-ability problem-solvers, we risk reducing the incentive to acquire the very skills the knowledge economy demands. Grusky scolds economists for assuming that existing compensation equals contribution; but then he assumes that we can identify a “competitive” compensation level that would be dramatically lower. On what basis? The real test of whether a market is competitive is openness to entry, not equal outcomes. If we remove the government-created licenses, subsidies, and entry restrictions that protect incumbents, the resulting inequality will be whatever emerges from voluntary exchange—and we have no independent yardstick for calling it excessive, unless we simply dislike the result.

Grusky is right that Americans accept inequality they believe to be generated fairly. But “fairness” to most people does not mean equal starting points in a radically redistributed way; it means no special favors, no insider deals, no government-granted privileges. That is the spirit of the Tea Party no less than Occupy Wall Street, and it points toward a platform that both camps could, surprisingly, converge upon: break up the big banks, end corporate welfare, reject bailouts, let failing institutions fail, and remove the regulatory thicket that allows the well-connected to profit at everyone else’s expense. Grusky’s impulse to restore competition is the right one. He just needs to follow it where it actually leads: not to a government-managed re-engineering of the entire educational and corporate ecosystem, but to a great dismantling of the state-corporate nexus that generates the worst inequality. After that dismantling, should substantial inequality remain, it would be the kind we can believe in—and it would also be accompanied by an economy far more dynamic and prosperous than the one we have today.
