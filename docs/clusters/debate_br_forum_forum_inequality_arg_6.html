# Don't Abandon the Tax Agenda Just Yet

David Grusky's essay performs a valuable service by widening the lens on inequality beyond marginal tax rates. He is right that pre-tax inequality has exploded since the late 1970s, right that much of that explosion reflects institutional choices rather than the workings of any pristine market, and right that "rent" is a useful word for what is happening at the top. But somewhere between diagnosis and prescription, his argument takes a wrong turn. The choice he poses—tax-based redistribution versus institutional repair—is a false one, and the rhetorical demotion of taxation as a "band-aid" is both politically and economically misguided.

Start with the politics. Grusky claims that institutional reform is more saleable than taxation because Americans love markets and competition. Maybe so in the abstract. But the specific institutional reforms he gestures toward—dismantling university admissions rationing, overhauling K-12 financing, reconstructing corporate governance so boards are no longer captured—are each more politically explosive than raising the top marginal rate from 35 to 39.6 percent. Tax rates can be changed by a reconciliation bill on a party-line vote. Changing how Harvard admits students, how Delaware corporate law structures board elections, how local property taxes fund schools—these involve dozens of veto players, entrenched constituencies, and constitutional questions. The Clinton-era tax hike of 1993 passed. Reforming corporate governance has been attempted continuously since the 1930s with modest results. Grusky has it exactly backwards: taxation is the politically tractable lever; institutional repair is the long, hard slog.

Now the economics. Grusky's most striking analytic move is to assert that the takeoff in pre-tax inequality "cannot be explained by tax policy alone." True—but this elides the substantial scholarship suggesting that tax policy shapes pre-tax compensation by altering the incentive to bargain hard for high pay. Saez himself has made this argument, and Grusky concedes it in passing before dismissing it as a "blunt instrument." The dismissal is too quick. If high marginal rates reduce the payoff to rent-seeking, they attack rent at its source even while leaving the corrupted institutions intact. That is a feature, not a bug, given how slow institutional reform tends to be. A 70 percent top marginal rate of the kind that prevailed before 1981 did not just redistribute income after the fact; it changed the entire calculus of CEO pay negotiations, board capture, and executive bonus design. The decline of that rate is not incidental to the rise of executive rent; it is plausibly one of its causes.

Grusky's education example is similarly oversold as a case against the tax agenda. Yes, the college wage premium is partly a bottleneck story. But the empirical literature—Goldin and Katz most prominently—suggests that the more important driver is skill-biased technical change running ahead of educational attainment. The United States, until the 1970s, had been steadily raising educational attainment faster than other nations; that growth has slowed. Expanding college access is desirable on every ground, but treating it as a substitute for redistribution requires believing that marginal expansions of higher-education slots can substantially compress a wage distribution shaped by global trade, technological change, and the collapse of union density. That is a heroic claim. Meanwhile, the EITC, the ACA's premium subsidies, and refundable tax credits already lift millions out of poverty—not through institutional repair but through exactly the "rough-and-ready redistribution" Grusky disparages.

Third, the framing of taxation as ideologically vulnerable while institutional reform is somehow inviolable gets the political economy backwards. Once an institution is reformed, powerful actors immediately set to work re-rigging it. Sarbanes-Oxley, the post-Enron corporate governance reform, has been quietly hollowed out for a decade. The Dodd-Frank rulemaking process has been a lobbyist's playground. The notion that institutional reform produces durable, lobbying-resistant outcomes while tax rates are uniquely reversible has the empirics inverted. Tax rates are simple, transparent, and easy to monitor. Institutional rules are complex, opaque, and ripe for capture.

None of this is to deny Grusky's core insight that markets are riddled with rent. They are. We should expand college access, reform board governance, strengthen antitrust, and rebuild labor's bargaining power. But these reforms complement rather than replace redistributive taxation. The honest progressive position is "and," not "or." A 50 percent top marginal rate, a financial transactions tax, a more progressive estate tax, and the closing of the carried-interest loophole would do more in the next five years to compress inequality than any plausible package of institutional reforms would do in twenty.

Grusky worries that focusing on taxation will leave the deeper corruption untouched. The bigger risk is that demoting taxation in favor of slow-burn institutional repair will leave us with neither. OWS may indeed be about more than tax rates. But we should not let the perfect become the enemy of the politically achievable.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Taxation is more politically tractable than institutional reforms, which face entrenched constituencies and complex legal hurdles that make them much harder to implement.
  2. 02High marginal tax rates act as a preventative measure against pre-tax inequality by reducing the incentives for executives to engage in rent-seeking and aggressive pay negotiations.
  3. 03The claim that educational expansion can replace redistribution is unrealistic given that wage distributions are heavily influenced by global trade and technological changes beyond the reach of school reform.
  4. 04Institutional reforms are more susceptible to being re-rigged by lobbyists and powerful actors than simple, transparent tax rates.
  5. 05Redistributive programs like tax credits provide immediate and proven relief to millions, whereas institutional repair is a slow-burn process that may never achieve the same scale of impact.
