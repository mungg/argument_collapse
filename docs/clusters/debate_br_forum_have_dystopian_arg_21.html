Acemoglu's Diagnosis Is Right; His Remedy Is Too Modest

Daron Acemoglu has written a clarifying essay, and its central insight deserves to be repeated until it lodges in policy debates: the direction of technological change is a choice, not a fate. For decades, economists treated technology as a kind of weather system, an exogenous force whose distributive consequences we could only try to soften through redistribution after the fact. Acemoglu's career-long argument that "technology is endogenous"—shaped by tax incentives, research subsidies, market structure, and prevailing norms—is one of the most important corrections to that complacency. His diagnosis of where AI is currently headed is also largely correct: too much algorithmic automation of routine tasks, too little investment in technologies that genuinely augment human capabilities, and a frightening expansion of surveillance infrastructure.

Where I part company with him is on the remedy. His three-pronged program—government policy, shifting norms, and democratic oversight—is correct as far as it goes, but it treats the problem as primarily one of technological direction, when the underlying problem is the distribution of power. You cannot redirect AI without first redirecting who has standing to make decisions about it, and you cannot expect a research community largely captured by seven or eight companies to suddenly develop a conscience because of "norm change." Norms change when power changes.

Consider Acemoglu's own historical account. The postwar compact he describes did not arise because mid-century engineers were more enlightened than today's machine-learning researchers. It arose because a militant labor movement, a New Deal regulatory state, wartime planning institutions, and a credible socialist alternative abroad forced capital into a corner. Henry Ford did not pay five dollars a day out of solidarity. The "good jobs" of Les Trente Glorieuses existed because workers could shut down production, because antitrust was wielded aggressively, and because capital feared something worse if it did not concede. Productivity-enhancing technology followed from this balance of power; it did not create it.

If that reading is right, then Acemoglu's proposed reforms understate what is required. Removing the tax code's bias toward equipment over labor would be useful but marginal. A "measurement framework" for the labor share effect of AI applications is intellectually elegant but politically toothless: any such framework will be gamed by the firms it is meant to discipline, and enforcement will be hostage to the same revolving door he correctly identifies between Big Tech and the universities. Even the climate analogy he reaches for is double-edged. Three decades of carbon measurement frameworks, voluntary norms, and disclosure regimes have produced exactly the kind of incremental progress that is too slow to matter. Real decarbonization, where it has occurred, has come from blunt instruments: feed-in tariffs, outright bans, public investment at scale, and the political defeat of incumbent fossil capital in specific jurisdictions.

What would the AI analogue look like? It would begin with sectoral bargaining. If we want workplace AI deployments to augment rather than displace, the most reliable way to ensure that outcome is to give the workers affected a legally enforceable seat at the table where deployment decisions are made. German codetermination is imperfect, but it has demonstrably slowed the most predatory forms of workplace automation and forced firms to negotiate productivity-sharing arrangements when new technologies are introduced. The U.S. has nothing comparable. A genuine pro-worker AI agenda would build it.

It would also include aggressive structural remedies against the dominant AI firms. Acemoglu notes, almost in passing, that "a handful of tech giants… account for the majority of money spent on AI research." This is not a footnote; it is the heart of the problem. When the same firms set the research agenda, employ the leading academics, supply the compute infrastructure, and capture the rents from deployment, the idea that "norm change" within the research community will redirect them is naïve. Concentrated industries change behavior when they are broken up, regulated as utilities, or subjected to public-option competition. We are long past the point where AT&T-style consent decrees should be on the table for the platform companies.

A serious agenda would treat data as a collectively held resource. Much of what makes modern AI valuable is training data extracted from billions of users who received nothing for it. Treating that data as a commons—governed through data trusts, with enforceable consent and revenue-sharing—would shift the economics of AI development away from surveillance-heavy business models in ways no carbon-style measurement framework can.

And it would invest publicly, at a scale Acemoglu hints at but does not specify. The federal government once funded the development of the internet, GPS, and the transistor. A public AI research agenda comparable in ambition to the postwar NSF and DARPA—oriented explicitly toward augmentation in education, healthcare, and skilled trades—is not utopian. It is what we used to do. The CHIPS-style industrial policy currently being debated for semiconductors should have an AI analogue focused on socially valuable applications.

I anticipate Acemoglu would agree with much of this, and indeed his footnotes gesture in these directions. But the essay's rhetorical center of gravity is on persuading researchers, policymakers, and citizens to see AI differently. That is necessary but insufficient. The deeper lesson of his own historical analysis is that the postwar compact was built on institutions that constrained capital—and those institutions were won through struggle, not consensus. The civil rights movement did not "shift norms" and then secure voting rights; it organized, marched, faced fire hoses, and forced the issue.

The same will be true of AI. We will not get a humane technological trajectory because Stanford computer science departments adopt better ethics curricula, or because IBM declines to sell facial recognition to police departments for a few news cycles. We will get it because gig workers unionize, because copyright holders sue, because regulators in Brussels keep tightening the screws, because antitrust enforcers in Washington remember how to use the tools they have, and because the workers whose jobs are being algorithmically reshaped insist on a voice.

There is one place where I think Acemoglu is too pessimistic, and oddly it is the international dimension. He suggests that even a U.S. democratic awakening might fail because China and other countries will keep developing surveillance and automation AI. But regulatory leadership cascades. The EU's GDPR has reshaped global data practices not because Europe could force compliance everywhere, but because firms operating at scale find it cheaper to adopt the strictest applicable standard. A serious U.S. or EU regime governing facial recognition, workplace surveillance, or algorithmic management would discipline global firms in similar ways. China's domestic surveillance practices are genuinely alarming, but they are not a reason to lower our own standards; they are a reason to raise them, and to make export controls and procurement rules contingent on them.

The essay's final line—"the direction of future AI and the future health of our economy and democracy are in our hands"—is exactly right. But "our hands" must mean something more concrete than the diffuse "we" of policy commentary. It must mean labor unions with bargaining rights over technological change, antitrust agencies with the will to break up platforms, public investment institutions with the budgets to seed alternatives, and democratic publics with enough security and standing to demand all of this. The technology will follow.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The postwar era of shared prosperity was the result of militant labor movements and aggressive regulation that forced capital to concede, not merely a change in technological norms.
  2. 02Meaningful change requires structural labor reforms, such as sectoral bargaining and codetermination, to give workers a legally enforceable seat at the table regarding AI deployment.
  3. 03The concentration of AI research within a few tech giants necessitates aggressive antitrust actions and structural breakups rather than relying on the conscience of the research community.
  4. 04Data should be treated as a collectively held resource governed by trusts to shift the economic incentives away from surveillance-heavy business models.
  5. 05The government must undertake large-scale public investment in AI research for social augmentation to provide a genuine alternative to corporate-driven automation.
  6. 06International regulatory leadership from the U.S. or EU can effectively set global standards for AI, contrary to the fear that competition with China makes domestic regulation futile.
