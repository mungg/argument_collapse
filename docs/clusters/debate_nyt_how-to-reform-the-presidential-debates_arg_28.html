"To make presidential debates more substantive, the format should shift from general policy positions to specific inquiries regarding the constitutional powers, legal limits, and governing choices of the office."

The missing subject in presidential debates is often the presidency itself. Candidates are asked what they think about taxes, immigration, war, schools and the Supreme Court. They are less often asked how, specifically, they would use the office they seek. A more substantive format would focus on powers, limits and governing choices.

For example: “Congress refuses to pass your immigration plan. What executive action, if any, do you take, and where is the legal boundary?” Or: “An ally requests military support for an operation that risks conflict with Russia or Iran. What process do you use before deciding?” Or: “Name one campaign promise you could not accomplish without Congress.” These questions force candidates to distinguish aspiration from authority.

The modern presidency tempts candidates in both parties to promise too much. They speak as if a president can order economic growth, rewrite health care, fix schools, command cultural unity and defeat enemies without tradeoffs. The debate format often encourages this by asking what candidates “will do” rather than what the Constitution, Congress, courts, agencies, states and foreign actors will allow them to do.

A reformed debate should include scenarios. Not trick questions, but governing simulations. A recession begins six months into the term. A Supreme Court vacancy opens during a divided Senate. A cyberattack is traced ambiguously to a foreign power. A hurricane overwhelms state capacity. What information would the candidate seek? Whom would he or she consult? What principles decide the response? What mistakes from past administrations should be avoided?

This format would reveal temperament and knowledge at the same time. It would also make empty promises more visible. If a candidate says every problem will be solved by “leadership,” the moderator can ask what legal authority that leadership uses. If a candidate says he will “bring people together,” the follow-up can ask what happens when the other side refuses.

The moderators should include people who understand governing: perhaps a constitutional scholar, a former budget official, a retired military officer or diplomat, alongside a journalist. The goal is not to create an oral exam for specialists. It is to remind viewers that electing a president is not the same as choosing a national spokesperson.

Substance does not mean more statistics in every answer. It means connecting promises to power, constraints and consequences. A debate that did that would be far more useful than one more exchange of slogans.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Debate questions should force candidates to distinguish between their political aspirations and their actual legal authority under the Constitution.
  2. 02The format should utilize governing simulations and scenarios to reveal a candidate's temperament, decision-making process, and understanding of institutional constraints.
  3. 03Moderator panels should be expanded to include experts in governance, such as constitutional scholars or former officials, to better scrutinize the feasibility of candidate promises.
  4. 04Focusing on the practicalities of the presidency would expose empty campaign rhetoric and the unrealistic promise of solving complex problems through 'leadership' alone.
