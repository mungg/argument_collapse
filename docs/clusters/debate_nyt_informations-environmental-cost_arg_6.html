"We should abandon the expectation of constant, instantaneous information because the psychological and cognitive costs of our impatience are more damaging than the environmental impact of data centers."

I want to defend a position that will sound old-fashioned: yes, we should wean ourselves from the expectation of constant, instantaneous information, not primarily for environmental reasons but because the expectation itself is making us worse.

The data center waste documented this week is the physical signature of a psychological condition. We have built an infrastructure that anticipates our impatience, and in doing so, has trained that impatience deeper into us. Servers idle at 90 percent overcapacity because we cannot bear a half-second delay. Generators rumble in suburban office parks because the thought of our email being briefly unreachable is unendurable. The waste exists because the demand exists, and the demand exists because we have come to experience waiting as a kind of small suffering.

This was not always so. Within living memory, mail took days, phone calls were planned, research required a library visit, and entertainment arrived on a schedule. People were not less informed or less connected in any meaningful sense; they were differently informed, and arguably more deeply so. Information that took effort to acquire was retained; conversations that required appointments were attended to; reading that could not be interrupted by notifications got read.

What we have traded for instantaneity is, among other things, the capacity for sustained attention. The same impulse that demands a sub-second search response demands a steady drip of email, social updates, news alerts, and notifications. The infrastructure that enables this drip is enormous, expensive, and polluting—but its environmental cost is the smaller part of the bill. The larger cost is borne in our cognitive lives, in our relationships, in our institutions of deliberation.

So when the Times asks whether the energy is worth the speed, I would invert the question. Even if the energy were free—even if every data center ran on solar power and emitted nothing—we should still ask whether the speed is worth what it has done to us. A culture that cannot tolerate a three-second delay is not a culture in robust health. The diesel-soaked server farms in Virginia are a symptom; the real disease is the assumption that anything less than now is unacceptable. Slowing down would do us good.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The massive energy waste in data centers is a physical manifestation of a psychological condition where we have come to experience waiting as a form of suffering.
  2. 02The infrastructure designed for speed has conditioned us to be more impatient, eroding our capacity for sustained attention and deep engagement.
  3. 03Historical experience shows that people were not less informed or connected when information required more effort and time to acquire.
  4. 04The true cost of instantaneity is found in the degradation of our cognitive lives, relationships, and institutions of deliberation rather than just environmental pollution.
  5. 05Even if the energy used to power the internet were free and clean, the cultural inability to tolerate minor delays indicates a lack of societal health.
