"While the authors of the lead essay propose valuable policy reforms, their defense of economics fails as a piece of public persuasion because it prioritizes professional self-justification over a genuine reckoning with the discipline's past failures and the lived experiences of the public."

At the heart of “Economics After Neoliberalism” lies a profound and admirable ambition: to rebuild the public legitimacy of a discipline that has, in the eyes of many, lost its moral compass. Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman want to show that economics is not the enemy of a decent society but an indispensable ally. They marshal an impressive arsenal of recent research to demonstrate that the profession’s best minds are grappling with inequality, power, and market failure. Yet for all its erudition and good intentions, the essay fails on its own terms as a piece of public persuasion. It is, in the end, a document written by economists, for economists, about economics. And that is a tragedy.

The problem begins with the central rhetorical move: “neoliberalism is not the consistent application of modern economics, but its primitive, simplistic perversion.” This is a defensive argument, almost a plea. It asks the public to overlook the last forty years of economic policy, which were consistently justified by economic reasoning from prestigious economists, and to trust instead a new set of economic reasonings from equally prestigious economists. It is like a doctor who prescribed a lethal treatment telling the patient, “The medicine was sound, but my colleague administered a naive version. Trust me, I have a new pill.” The public’s skepticism is not irrational; it is a rational response to past failures. The essay’s condescending tone toward the “primitive” old guard only reinforces the impression of an insular guild closing ranks.

What would a genuinely public-facing version of this argument look like? It would start not with a defense of the discipline but with a genuine reckoning. It would admit that economics, as a profession, helped create the conditions of inequality and instability we now face—not because of a few bad apples, but because the discipline’s core assumptions (efficient markets, rational expectations, minimal government) were systematically taught, rewarded, and deployed in policy. It would apologize to the workers whose communities were devastated by trade agreements that economists championed without adequate transition support. It would express humility about the limits of economic knowledge.

The essay also fails to speak in a language that resonates beyond the academy. It mentions “justice-efficiency frontiers” and “macro-prudential contracting” and “incentive compatibility for managers and workers.” These phrases are precise within the discipline, but they are dead on arrival in a town hall meeting or a union organizing drive. The authors recognize the need to “convince our audience,” but they never ask who that audience is or what it needs to hear. The people who have lost faith in economics are not waiting for a more sophisticated model; they are waiting for someone to acknowledge their pain and give them a sense of agency.

Contrast this with the moral clarity of the social movements that are actually reshaping politics today: the Fight for $15, the climate justice movement, the anti-austerity protests in Europe. These movements speak of dignity, rights, and power. They channel anger and hope. The EfIP essay, by comparison, is bloodless and bureaucratic. It offers wage boards when people want a voice; it offers social insurance when people want community; it offers tax reform when people want justice. I am not saying the proposals are bad—they are mostly good. But they are answers to questions that few ordinary people are asking.

If economists truly want to serve inclusive prosperity, they must step out of their professional comfort zone. They must learn to listen before they prescribe, to speak plainly before they model, and to stand in solidarity with those on the receiving end of economic forces before they design institutions to manage them. The lead essay is a fine internal memo for a progressive economics workshop. As a public manifesto, it falls short—and the stakes are too high for that.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The authors' attempt to distance 'true' economics from neoliberalism is a defensive and condescending rhetorical move that fails to address the public's rational skepticism toward the profession.
  2. 02The essay lacks a necessary apology and humble admission that the core assumptions of the economics discipline systematically contributed to current social and economic crises.
  3. 03The use of bloodless, academic jargon prevents the authors from connecting with a broader audience that values moral clarity, dignity, and agency over technical modeling.
  4. 04The proposed policy solutions, while technically sound, are bureaucratic responses that fail to resonate with the language and urgent demands of modern social movements.
  5. 05To regain public legitimacy, economists must move beyond internal professional debates and learn to listen to and stand in solidarity with those harmed by economic forces.
