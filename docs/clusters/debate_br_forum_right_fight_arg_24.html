"While Daniel Richman correctly identifies the tension between federal immigration enforcement and local community policing, his proposal for 'negotiation' is insufficient to protect local law enforcement from federal coercion and fiscal threats."

The most important issue in Daniel Richman's essay is the one he treats most briefly: immigration enforcement. Yet this is precisely where the tension between federal goals and local realities is most acute—and where the stakes of getting the policy wrong are highest.

Richman correctly identifies the "chilling effect" argument: if local police enforce immigration laws, immigrant communities will stop cooperating with law enforcement generally. He cites the International Association of Chiefs of Police on this point, and he notes that the pending CLEAR Act would mandate local immigration enforcement, effectively imposing this chilling effect by federal fiat. His critique is warranted.

But Richman's proposed solution—"the dimensions of this interaction need to be a matter of negotiation, not federal fiat"—fails to grapple with the underlying legal and political dynamics. The CLEAR Act represents a serious legislative threat precisely because it ties immigration enforcement to federal funding. Local departments that refuse to comply would lose substantial sums, forcing a choice between cooperation and fiscal survival. "Negotiation" under these conditions is not negotiation at all; it is coercion dressed in cooperative language.

The policy implications are more complex than Richman suggests. There is a genuine tension between two legitimate goals: effective immigration enforcement and community cooperation with law enforcement. Richman leans heavily toward the latter, arguing that intelligence gains from immigration enforcement would be offset by losses in other areas. This may be correct, but it cannot be assumed. In some communities, the presence of unauthorized immigrants who have committed crimes (not merely violated civil immigration provisions) may represent a genuine public safety threat. The question is not whether immigration enforcement is ever appropriate but where it should be concentrated and who should perform it.

A sensible policy would distinguish between three categories: serious criminal aliens (who should be priorities for removal regardless of enforcement location), immigrants who have committed minor offenses (who might be handled through standard criminal justice processes without immigration-specific interventions), and immigrants who have violated only civil immigration provisions (who should be processed through administrative rather than criminal channels). Local police should be involved in the first category—they already arrest criminals and should hand off immigration violators to federal authorities—but should be explicitly prohibited from immigration-specific enforcement in the other two categories.

This distinction would protect community relationships while preserving federal immigration authority. It would also require clearer federal guidance than currently exists. The Justice Department's post-9/11 interviewing program and the subsequent absconder initiative operated in a legal gray zone that created confusion and resistance. If federal authorities want local cooperation, they need to provide clear legal frameworks that protect local departments from liability while limiting their immigration-related activities to genuine public safety priorities.

Richman's argument that local departments should "exact a toll" from federal authorities in exchange for cooperation is politically astute but practically problematic. The "toll" should be measured in resources, not in authority over immigration policy. The federal government should fund local counterterrorism efforts adequately; it should not cede control of immigration enforcement to localities that have different priorities.
