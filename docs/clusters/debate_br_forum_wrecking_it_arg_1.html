Diversified GPT

"To preserve the Internet's generative nature, we must move beyond the false choice between security and freedom by designing a more forgiving architecture that empowers non-expert users to manage risks without surrendering control to proprietary appliances."

Jonathan Zittrain is right to frame the problem as a problem of preservation, not nostalgia. The question is not whether the Internet of 1988 or 1995 can be frozen in amber. It cannot. The question is whether the qualities that made the Internet and the personal computer so astonishingly fertile can survive the very success they enabled. A network for experts became a network for everyone; a programmable machine for hobbyists became the filing cabinet, bank branch, photo album, classroom, and workplace of ordinary people. The old bargain—freedom in exchange for self-reliance—no longer works when most users cannot possibly know what their machines are doing.

The most important move in Zittrain’s essay is to refuse the easy division between “security” and “freedom.” In practice, insecurity can destroy freedom. A user whose computer is riddled with spyware is not free in any meaningful sense. A person whose broadband connection has been enlisted into a botnet is not exercising autonomy. A small business that cannot trust its own spreadsheets is not enjoying the benefits of an open platform. If the generative PC comes to mean a machine that betrays its owner, then consumers will rationally flee to devices that do less but do it reliably.

Still, I would put more emphasis than Zittrain does on the humble problem of usability. The security community has long behaved as if users were defective components. They click the wrong attachments, ignore warnings, fail to patch, choose weak passwords, and install charming little programs that turn out to be parasites. But these are not moral failings. They are predictable responses to systems that ask impossible questions. “Do you want to allow this ActiveX control?” “This certificate cannot be verified; proceed?” “A program is attempting to access the Internet.” These prompts masquerade as consent while withholding the information necessary for consent.

If we want generativity to survive, we need not only community tools and civic institutions, but better defaults. The genius of wiki reversion is not simply that it permits repair. It lowers the cost of error. That is the model that ought to guide the PC. Ordinary users should be able to try software, undo experiments, recover documents, and see what changed without becoming system administrators. A computer should have a visible history. It should be able to say, in plain terms: “Yesterday this program installed itself, changed your browser home page, and began sending mail.” At present, most people discover such facts only after the damage is done, if ever.

Zittrain’s “Red” and “Green” computer is a promising metaphor because it matches the way people already think. We have work clothes and play clothes, checking accounts and vacation cash, kitchens and garages. We understand boundaries. A Green space for banking, tax records, writing, and family photos, and a Red space for games, experiments, downloads, and unknown tools, could preserve the pleasure of tinkering without making every experiment a wager of one’s digital life. The trick is to make the boundary comprehensible. If moving a file from Red to Green requires priestly knowledge, the idea will fail. If the system can say, “This photo is safe to copy, but this program is not,” then we may have something.

I am also persuaded by Zittrain’s analogy to Wikipedia, though with reservations. Wikipedia works not because people are angels, but because its architecture makes many small acts of repair easy and visible. Vandalism is not eliminated; it is outnumbered. The lesson is not that community spirit alone can secure the network. The lesson is that good tools can convert weak and dispersed goodwill into useful governance. StopBadware, reputation systems for code, shared measurements of crashes and pop-ups, and public warnings about infected sites are all attempts to create the equivalent of page histories and watchlists for software.

But we should be careful about who runs these systems. If code reputation becomes a cartel of large vendors, we will merely have reinvented the appliance in distributed form. A program written by a student in São Paulo or Nairobi should not be treated as presumptively dangerous simply because it is new, obscure, or unsigned by a major corporation. Novelty is the oxygen of generativity. A reputation system must be able to distinguish “unknown” from “bad.” It should warn without forbidding. It should let users rely on communities they trust: universities, open-source projects, consumer groups, employers, friends, or security firms. Pluralism matters as much here as openness.

Zittrain is also right to invite ISPs into the discussion, but this is dangerous territory. There is a difference between filtering known spam and malware at the customer’s request and building networks that inspect, classify, and shape all traffic according to private or governmental priorities. The end-to-end principle was never a suicide pact, but neither is security a blank check. If ISPs are to help, their interventions should be transparent, narrowly tailored, appealable, and optional where possible. A user should be able to know what is blocked and why. Otherwise, the infrastructure of protection will become an infrastructure of control.

The broader point is that openness must be designed for the users we actually have, not the users we wish we had. In the early Internet, the community was small enough and skilled enough that norms could do much of the work. Today’s Internet includes children, grandparents, dissidents, doctors, small merchants, criminals, governments, and organized gangs. A humane architecture must assume both generosity and predation.

The locked-down appliance is attractive because it offers a simple answer: trust Apple, Microsoft, Sony, RIM, or your carrier. That answer will sometimes be good enough. There is nothing dishonorable about wanting a phone that makes calls reliably. But if all our digital life migrates to devices and services whose owners can decide what we may run, say, save, or export, then the public will have traded a messy freedom for a polished dependency.

The task, then, is to make the open world less punishing. Reversion, transparency, data portability, user-readable histories, diverse code reputation systems, and civic security institutions are not romantic add-ons. They are the conditions under which ordinary people can remain creators rather than mere subscribers. Zittrain’s deepest claim is that the Internet’s future depends on whether users experience it as a shared environment. I would add: they will do so only if that environment forgives mistakes. Freedom that requires expertise will not remain broadly distributed. Freedom that can recover from error just might.

Vanilla GPT

"To preserve the Internet's openness, we must move beyond relying on user-driven civic responsibility and instead assign security obligations to the institutional actors best positioned to perform them through usable security defaults and consumer protection laws."

Jonathan Zittrain is right to identify the central danger: the security crisis is not merely that computers are vulnerable, but that our likely responses to vulnerability may destroy the very openness that made networked computing valuable. The temptation to solve insecurity by replacing the personal computer with the appliance is powerful. Consumers do not wake up wanting “generativity.” They want e-mail that works, photos that are not lost, bank accounts that are not emptied, and a phone that still makes calls after they try a new program. If the open platform cannot offer ordinary users a tolerable level of safety, they will rationally choose the closed one.

But the preservation of generativity cannot depend too heavily on turning users into netizens. That is an admirable civic aspiration, and there are domains—Wikipedia is the obvious example—where the analogy works. Yet vandalism in an encyclopedia and malware on a home computer differ in one crucial respect: the costs of participation are radically unequal. A Wikipedia editor can revert a bad edit after seeing it. A computer user may not know that a machine has been compromised, may not understand what process is sending outbound mail, and may be unable to distinguish a legitimate software update from a forged one. Worse, the costs of one user’s inattention are often imposed on others: infected machines send spam, host phishing pages, and participate in denial-of-service attacks. Civic tools can help, but they will not be enough if the basic allocation of responsibility remains so poorly matched to capacity.

The right question, then, is not only how to preserve openness, but how to assign security work to the actors best positioned to perform it while preserving room for unapproved innovation. Today that means taking seriously the roles of operating-system vendors, browser makers, application developers, ISPs, universities, employers, and payment networks—not just end users. The end-to-end principle has served the Internet well, but end-to-end should not become an excuse for dumping all risk onto the least sophisticated endpoint. If a broadband provider can identify a customer machine sending hundreds of thousands of e-mails a day, it should not treat that fact as someone else’s problem. If a browser can warn users away from a site distributing hostile code, it should. If an operating system can make routine work possible without administrator privileges, it should default to that safer condition.

The difficult part is distinguishing protective intervention from gatekeeping. Zittrain’s “least harm to generativity” principle is a useful test, but it needs institutional teeth. Network-level filtering, for instance, may be justified for known spam relays or fast-spreading worms, but it should be transparent, contestable, and narrow. Users should be told when traffic is blocked; technically sophisticated users should have some ability to opt out; and blocking rules should be published at least in general form. Otherwise emergency security measures will become convenient infrastructure for copyright enforcement, political censorship, or commercial discrimination. The same is true of Google’s malware warnings. They are valuable precisely because search engines can change incentives faster than legislatures can. But because they can also crush a small site’s traffic overnight, there must be quick review, clear standards, and a path to correction.

I am also less persuaded that “easy reversion” for PCs can carry as much weight as Zittrain hopes. Version histories and virtual machines are important, and the wiki analogy is illuminating. But malware is not merely a bad edit. A compromised machine can leak passwords, silently alter financial transactions, join a botnet, or encrypt and hide itself before the user even knows there is something to revert. Reversion protects integrity better than confidentiality. Once a tax return, trade secret, or address book has been copied, rolling the machine back does not roll the secret back into the box. Virtualization can reduce damage, but only if users understand the boundary between red and green environments—and experience suggests that users will constantly be asked to move data across that boundary. The hard problem will be making the safe path the easy path.

That suggests a more prosaic agenda than either lockdown or digital civic republicanism: usable security by default. Machines should ship with automatic updating turned on. Applications should be sandboxed where possible, but with documented ways for users to grant additional privileges. Operating systems should make it visible, in ordinary language, when software wants to run at startup, contact the network, alter browser settings, or install additional components. Software installation should resemble informed consent more than a scavenger hunt through a license agreement. The goal should not be to prevent users from taking risks, but to prevent deception and to make risky choices intelligible.

Law has a role here, but it should be aimed less at punishing every author of bad code—many will be unreachable—and more at changing incentives for reachable firms. Deceptive installation practices, undisclosed data collection, and intentionally difficult uninstallation are consumer-protection problems, not just technical nuisances. Vendors that bundle adware or bury material terms in unreadable agreements should face consequences. Companies that hold user data should provide export mechanisms. Platforms that invite developers and users to invest in them should not be able to revoke access arbitrarily once dependence has formed. These interventions do not require government to design the Internet; they require it to police fraud, lock-in, and bait-and-switch behavior.

The best future is not a pure one. We will have open PCs, semi-open phones, closed game consoles, hosted applications, and networks that sometimes intervene. The task is to keep these layers in tension so that none becomes the sole point of control. Generativity will survive not because every device is open, but because enough devices remain open, enough data remains portable, enough networks remain neutral, and enough users can choose experimentation without courting disaster. Zittrain is right to warn that security can become the rationale for a sterile information environment. The answer is not to romanticize insecurity, but to make openness safer than it has been.

Diversified DeepSeek

"The essay argues that the best way to save the generative Internet from security threats is not through corporate lockdown or government regulation, but by empowering a community of 'netizens' with social and technical tools for collective self-defense."

Jonathan Zittrain’s sweeping diagnosis could not be more timely. He has captured the essential paradox of the digital age: the very openness that made the Internet and the personal computer engines of bottom-up innovation is now their greatest vulnerability. And yet, rather than succumb to the twin temptations of lockdown or regulatory bludgeoning, he offers a hopeful third path—one that harnesses the same generative energy to police its own excesses. Zittrain’s call to build “social layer” tools, promote virtual machine architectures, and nurture a culture of engaged netizenship is squarely on the mark. The Internet does not need a central immune system; it needs to strengthen the distributed antibodies that already exist in its communities.

Consider the cautionary tale of the iPhone. When Steve Jobs declared “you don’t want your phone to be like a PC,” he was pandering to a very real fear that Zittrain documents: that mainstream users have been burned by malware, confused by pop-ups, and exhausted by security housekeeping. But Jobs’s vision—a beautiful, sealed appliance—is not the inevitable future. It is a market choice made because the generative alternative is hemorrhaging trust. Zittrain’s proposals, from easy system reversion to virtual Red-Green PCs, aim directly at that trust deficit. If my mother can use a Green PC for her banking and know that nothing her grandchild downloads on the Red side will touch it, the argument for an iPhone-style straitjacket loses much of its force. She gets safety without sacrificing the ability to explore.

The more audacious—and transformative—strand of Zittrain’s argument lifts a page from Wikipedia. The free encyclopedia solved its own vandalism problem not by appointing a handful of censors but by enlisting a broad community of editors and developing simple technical tools: watchlists, rollback privileges, and, later, the Wikiscanner. Those innovations did not rewrite the wiki’s open architecture; they reinforced it. Zittrain wants the same for code. Initiatives like StopBadware and the nascent Herdict toolkit represent exactly the right instinct. A dashboard that tells me how many other users have installed a piece of software, what the self-identified experts think, and whether it has been flagged by my neighbors is a far more generative form of security than a binary block-or-allow decision handed down by a corporate operating system. It turns security from a mysterious paternalism into a collaborative, transparent process—one in which users can, if they wish, see why a particular program is regarded as suspicious and make an informed choice.

Skeptics will and should ask: Will ordinary people actually participate? Zittrain’s answer, rightly, evokes the volunteer fire department. Not everyone will scan packet logs, but a critical minority will—and their behavior, properly aggregated, can guide the rest. Already, millions rate products on Amazon, edit Wikipedia, and flag spam in their email clients. The impulse to contribute to a shared commons is not alien to the human condition; it merely awaits the right tools. The Berkman Center’s Herdict project, which turns routine PC “vital signs”—pop-up counts, crashes—into a collective weather map of Internet health, is a test bed for this proposition. If even a modest fraction of users run it, the resulting data could help distinguish a rapidly spreading botnet from a harmless utility without any central authority having to decide.

Zittrain wisely resists the temptation to demonize commercial security vendors, but he implicitly recognizes that the subscription model of antivirus is a treadmill that cannot keep pace. Botnets now command armies of millions; organized crime treats them as business assets. In a world where Dave Dagon can estimate that one in ten home machines is compromised, waiting for a signature file to be downloaded after a new threat emerges is like trying to extinguish a wildfire with a garden hose. Generative, community-fed measurement can detect anomalies—sudden spikes in network connections, unexpected outbound email—long before a human analyst picks apart a virus sample. That early warning, communicated back through the network, could allow other PCs to quarantine suspicious code preemptively, not retroactively.

The legal framework Zittrain sketches is equally essential. A requirement of data portability would prevent users from being held hostage by any particular web service or appliance. Appliance neutrality principles, grounded in common-law notions of vested reliance, would stop platform vendors from baiting with openness and switching to lockdown once dominance is achieved. Together, they ensure that even as sealed devices proliferate, the horizon of the generative PC remains open as a refuge, a safety valve, and a laboratory. Without these legal guardrails, the gravitational pull toward the safe, non-generative appliance would be unstoppable.

Zittrain’s vision is not a utopian fantasy. It demands that we reject the false choice between a lawless, malware-ridden Wild West and a sterile, Apple-controlled garden. The generative Internet was built by tinkerers, academics, and amateurs acting in good faith. They did not wait for permission from governments or corporations. What they built is now under siege by criminals and, paradoxically, by the very users whose ignorance makes them easy prey. To save it, we must extend that same tradit ion of decentralized, good-faith action to the task of collective self-defense. The tools and norms exist in embryo. What is needed now is the nerve to believe that the network’s users, given a fighting chance, can protect what they have built—and in doing so, become netizens rather than mere consumers.

"To save the generative Internet, we must move beyond Zittrain’s initial proposals and build robust, visible, and pluralistic civic institutions that distribute security responsibility across the user base, much like the governance model of Wikipedia."

The Wikipedia Analogy, and Why It Has to Be Pushed Harder

The most arresting move in Zittrain's essay is the analogy from Wikipedia. Faced with vandalism, copyright infringement, and lies, Wikipedia did not respond by locking its doors. It built community institutions: easy reversion, page histories, watchlists, talk pages, and an evolving normative culture in which a critical mass of editors took responsibility for the encyclopedia's integrity. The result is messy, contested, sometimes wrong, and yet works astonishingly well at scale. Zittrain wants to do for PC security what Wikipedia did for encyclopedia content: build social and technical institutions that distribute responsibility for the platform's health across its users.

I am persuaded by the analogy. I want to argue, though, that Zittrain stops short of its implications. If we take seriously the lesson that distributed community institutions can govern complex, contested resources better than top-down authority, then we should be more ambitious than Herdict and StopBadware suggest. We should be building the institutional plumbing of digital citizenship, and we should be doing it now, while the door is open.

Begin with what Wikipedia actually got right. It is not, despite the romantic narrative, simply that "anyone can edit." Anyone could edit a chalkboard in Times Square; that does not produce an encyclopedia. What Wikipedia got right was the interaction of three things: a low-friction editing interface, a structural commitment to transparency (every change visible, every history navigable), and a community that took ownership of the resource. The first two are technical. The third is sociological, and it is the hardest to manufacture deliberately. Wikipedia's community emerged in part by accident, in part because Jimmy Wales and the early editors made some shrewd cultural choices, and in part because the project had time to incubate before its problems exceeded its volunteers.

The PC security problem does not have that luxury. The botnets are already at scale; the criminal economy is already mature; the population of vulnerable users is already enormous. We cannot afford the decade Wikipedia had to find its footing. So if we want to apply the Wikipedia lesson here, we need to think hard about how to seed and accelerate the community institutions Zittrain rightly identifies as the answer.

Three thoughts on what that might look like.

First, the institutions need to be visible and legitimate, not merely useful. Wikipedia works partly because users see it; they understand they are using something a community built. The StopBadware warning that appears in a Google search result is, by contrast, almost invisible — most users do not know who wrote it or why, and certainly do not see themselves as participants in its construction. If we want the security analogue of Wikipedia, the equivalent of "edit this page" needs to be present in the interface. When my browser warns me that a site is suspicious, there should be a one-click path to seeing why, to contributing my own assessment, and to viewing the deliberations of the community that made the judgment. Without that, we will have built a curated authority service, not a generative civic institution.

Second, the institutions need to be plural. One of Wikipedia's underappreciated features is that there are other Wikipedias — Citizendium, Scholarpedia, Conservapedia, project-specific wikis — competing for editor attention and providing alternatives when Wikipedia gets something wrong. The security space has been dominated by a small handful of antivirus vendors and a small handful of academic projects. Plurality matters because no single institution should be in a position to declare what is "bad code," any more than the Encyclopaedia Britannica should be in a position to declare what counts as knowledge. StopBadware is a good start, but its centralization is a vulnerability. A healthy ecosystem would include several competing or complementary projects with different methodologies, governance structures, and biases. Users could choose, and the choosing itself would be educational.

Third, the institutions need to invest seriously in what I will call "civic onboarding." Wikipedia did not produce its million editors by accident. It produced them by making the entry point unusually shallow: see a typo, fix it, watch your fix become part of an article read by millions, feel the pull of contributing more. Most security tools have no analogous onramp. Herdict, as Zittrain describes it, is admirably designed in this regard — it asks ordinary users to report ordinary observations — but the political and educational work of converting passive Internet users into active netizens has barely begun. This is where Zittrain's call for "artistically and intellectually skilled people of goodwill" lands, and it is the part of his argument that deserves the most amplification. The generative Internet will not be saved by lawyers or by engineers. It will be saved, if at all, by teachers, writers, librarians, and civic organizations that help ordinary people see themselves as stakeholders.

There is a deeper challenge here that Zittrain underplays. Wikipedia governs a resource — encyclopedia content — that does not, in itself, threaten anyone's wallet or safety. When a Wikipedia entry is wrong, the damage is reputational and reversible. When a security judgment is wrong, the damage may be financial, may be permanent, and may attract litigation. The institutional design problem for community-based security is therefore harder than the problem Wikipedia solved. It involves liability, due process, and adversarial actors with resources Wikipedia vandals do not have. We will need not just norms but procedures, not just talk pages but appeals processes, not just bots but auditable governance.

None of this is reason to abandon the Wikipedia analogy. It is reason to take it more seriously than Zittrain does. The right response to the generative Internet's crisis is not to layer community garnishes on top of an architecture still owned by vendors and carriers. It is to build civic institutions of comparable seriousness and durability to the institutions that govern our physical commons — water boards, fire departments, juries — and to embed them in the digital infrastructure as legitimate, contestable, plural, and visible actors. That is a big project. It will take a generation. We should start now, because the alternative Zittrain describes — appliances and tethered services owned by their makers — is not a future the next generation will be able to undo.

  3. 03The movement must prioritize 'civic onboarding' by creating low-barrier entry points that transform passive consumers into active stakeholders and contributors to the network's health.
  4. 04Community-governed security requires more sophisticated institutional 'plumbing' than Wikipedia, including formal procedures for liability, due process, and appeals to handle high-stakes adversarial threats.

"While Jonathan Zittrain correctly identifies the threat that insecurity poses to the generative Internet, his reliance on voluntary community policing and complex technical workarounds is insufficient to combat the industrial-scale organized crime currently exploiting the network."

Jonathan Zittrain has given us an elegant and troubling map of the digital crossroads we face. His history of the generative Internet—from the Morris worm to the botnet economy—shows why the openness that gave us the Apple II and the Web now threatens to undo itself. The diagnosis is bracingly clear. But when he turns to remedies, his prescriptions rely on a hope that I fear is fading even as he writes: that a diffuse community of netizens, armed with clever tools and a civic ethos, can secure the network without sacrificing its experimental soul. I want to share that hope, but the Internet of March 2008 looks far more unruly than the one his social-layer success stories describe. The garden of generativity is not being overrun by mischievous weeds; it is being strip-mined by organized crime, and a neighborhood watch will not be enough.

Zittrain draws inspiration from Wikipedia, where an active community, aided by version histories and bots, keeps vandalism at bay. But Wikipedia is a single, centralized platform with clear norms and a manageable scale of contributors who self-select for a shared mission. The open Internet is nothing like it. A PC connected to the Net faces threats from anonymous actors spread across dozens of jurisdictions, hardened by profit motives rather than adolescent curiosity. The botnets Zittrain rightly fears are not best understood as a tragedy of the commons; they are industrial-scale rackets. The going rate for a distributed denial-of-service attack is, as he notes, tens of thousands of dollars per day. Spam remains wildly profitable. These enterprises do not respond to gentle shaming or the good opinion of one’s peers. They exploit every weakness left open by a system that, by design, asks no one for permission. The kind of community policing that works for a wiki simply does not scale to an environment where the bad actors outnumber the sheriffs by orders of magnitude and have no stake in the community’s health.

The tools Zittrain champions—Herdict, StopBadware, Google warnings—are worthy experiments, but their scope is heartbreakingly small compared to the flood of new malware we read about every week. Even if Herdict provides a dashboard showing pop-ups and crashes per hour, the users most likely to install it are already the most security-conscious. The vast majority of home PCs, which a 2005 study found lacking even basic protection, will remain dark and unmonitored. The StopBadware partnership with Google flagged thousands of hacked sites, but millions more exist elsewhere, and the cleanup burden quickly overwhelmed the researchers. As long as the barrier to entry for writing and spreading bad code remains near zero—and as long as compromised machines can be bought and sold in bulk—these voluntarist measures will only trim the edges of the problem. Zittrain hints at this himself when he observes that antivirus vendors may not be able to withstand the onslaught, yet he still places faith in the emergence of new civic institutions whose membership would necessarily be a fraction of the user base. The asymmetry is too great.

His technical proposals face a similar hurdle: they ask too much of ordinary people. The idea of splitting a PC into Red and Green virtual machines, with a “checkpoint Charlie” for moving clean data, is ingenious. But it assumes a level of engagement and digital literacy that most users do not have and show no sign of acquiring. Indeed, the very frustration that drives people toward locked-down iPhones and Xboxes is precisely the demand that computing just work without constant housekeeping. A PC that requires you to understand that your Red environment is a sandbox you can reset is still, for most consumers, a PC that comes with homework. The flight to appliances is not only about security; it is about simplicity, about guarantees. When Steve Jobs says, “You don’t want your phone to be like a PC,” he is not just being paternalistic; he is reading the market. If the choice remains between a generative PC that demands eternal vigilance and a sterile appliance that prevents the worst disasters, millions will choose the latter—whatever insurance policy the PC theoretically provides.

Zittrain’s legal and policy interventions are more promising, but they tinker around the edges of the central crisis. Data portability requirements would indeed ease lock-in, but they do nothing to make the PC itself more secure. Appliance neutrality principles, borrowed from the Microsoft antitrust case, could prevent platform bait-and-switches, but only after a platform has first become open. That will not save the generative core of the Net if people have already abandoned it for the walled gardens. The deeper problem is that none of these measures directly alters the incentive structure that makes insecure code so lucrative and so easy to distribute. As long as the costs of infection are externalized to the millions whose machines are silently conscripted into botnets, market forces alone will not correct the imbalance. We may need to confront something Zittrain is reluctant to embrace: a modest, carefully targeted regime of intermediary responsibility.

Strict end-to-end neutrality is a fine ideal, but Zittrain concedes that ISPs might screen out viruses at the network level as a stopgap. That concession could be broadened. If ISPs were obligated—perhaps with safe-harbor protections modeled on the Digital Millennium Copyright Act—to offer basic network-level filtering for known malicious traffic, much of the botnet spam and scanning could be dampened without requiring every user to become a security expert. This is not an invitation to turn ISPs into content police, but a recognition that the endpoints have failed and will continue to fail. A limited, transparent, and auditable role for service providers could buy time for the generative tools Zittrain envisions to mature. It may be a calculated retreat from pure openness, but it is far less damaging than the wholesale migration of users to closed appliances, which is already underway.

The generative Internet is worth defending, but it will not be saved by exhorting millions of users to become netizens or by elegant technical Band-Aids. It will be saved—if it can be—by a pragmatic acceptance that the world of 2008 is not the fraternity of 1988. The hacker ethos is dead; the business model for bad code is robust. If we are to preserve any space for the next Apple II, we will likely have to draw firmer lines, make hard choices about limited network filtering, and accept that some of the wildness of the early Net is irretrievable. Zittrain’s essay is the best starting point for that conversation, but his remedies are the beginning of wisdom, not the end.

The Xbox 360 has a homebrew development pathway (XNA); even the locked-down TiVo has spawned a community of modifiers. Generativity is a continuum, and most successful platforms occupy a middle band where curation and openness coexist. Treating Apple's curation as a civilizational fork in the road risks misreading what is actually a fairly old pattern of platform governance.

His "Red PC / Green PC" virtualization proposal is more promising—indeed, products like VMware and Parallels already demonstrate the concept—but Zittrain glosses over the hardest part: the "checkpoint Charlie" between the two. Most malicious payloads now travel inside documents and browser sessions, exactly the data users will want to move between virtual machines. The proposal that worries me most is the invitation for ISPs to filter at the gateway. We are already watching Comcast throttle BitTorrent traffic and various national ISPs filter content under public-safety rationales. Once carriers are competent to inspect packets for viruses, they will be competent—and pressured—to inspect them for copyrighted material, for political content, for whatever the next moral panic demands. Zittrain spends pages worrying about appliancization at the endpoints while proposing exactly the kind of intelligence in the middle that the end-to-end principle was designed to prevent. The remedy concedes the patient.

What Zittrain gets profoundly right is that the cybersecurity problem is also a political problem, that "lockdown" is a choice rather than a fate, and that the social architecture around our machines matters as much as their code. The data-portability and appliance-neutrality proposals, in particular, deserve serious legislative attention. Generativity Is Not Enough: The Case for Serious Security

Reading Zittrain's essay, one is struck by an odd asymmetry. He paints, accurately, a picture of catastrophe: thirty million machines under criminal control, antivirus vendors who admit they may not be able to keep up, patch windows measured in months, an entire economy of badware now monetized through spam and extortion. Then, having walked us up to the edge of this abyss, he offers a response built largely around volunteer community tools, browser-based warning labels, and toolkits that report PC vital signs to a central authority. It is as if he had diagnosed congestive heart failure and prescribed a brisk walk.

The community-tool approach has been the implicit policy of the Internet for fifteen years. The Computer Emergency Response Team, the CVE database, the antivirus industry, the patient work of Bruce Schneier and others to educate the public — these are all expressions of the volunteerist, distributed-responsibility model Zittrain wants to extend. And here is the uncomfortable fact: it has failed. The data Zittrain himself cites is the data of failure. Not failure in the sense of "we are losing some battles"; failure in the sense of "organized crime owns one in ten home machines and the trend is worsening." The community ethos that policed the early Internet did not scale. There is no reason to believe a marginally improved version of it will scale now.

Consider what Zittrain proposes. Herdict will gather telemetry from voluntarily participating users and produce reputation signals for new code. Who installs Herdict? Sophisticated users. Who runs the compromised machines? Unsophisticated users. The population that needs the protection is precisely the population that will not download the tool, and even if they did, they would not know how to interpret its outputs. This is not a quibble. It is the central defect of every voluntary-adoption security scheme of the past decade. SpamAssassin works for technically savvy administrators; spam still arrives in your mother's inbox. PGP exists; almost nobody uses it. Firewalls ship with Windows XP SP2; the 81-percent figure Zittrain quotes tells us how that has gone.

If we take the security problem as seriously as Zittrain says we should, we need to consider tools he is unwilling to consider. Three in particular.

First, mandatory security standards for consumer software. We require seatbelts in cars, child-resistant caps on medication, and electrical safety standards on appliances. The reason consumer operating systems ship in insecure default configurations is that vendors face no liability for the externalities their products impose on the rest of the network. A regulatory floor — minimum default firewall settings, mandatory automatic patching for security updates, a duty to disclose vulnerabilities within a defined window — would impose costs on Microsoft, Apple, and the Linux distributions, but it would also reduce the population of compromised endpoints by orders of magnitude. Zittrain worries about the chilling effect on generativity. I worry about the chilling effect of botnets on the willingness of ordinary users to do anything online at all.

Second, ISP-level responsibility, including the disconnection of compromised machines. Several Australian and Finnish ISPs have begun quarantining infected customers — not filtering content, but notifying users and limiting bandwidth until the infection is cleared. This is not censorship; it is the network analogue of a public health quarantine. The end-to-end principle was never meant to mean that an endpoint pumping out a million spam messages an hour has an inviolable right to do so. Zittrain gestures at this with his call for ISPs to do "virus screening," but he is too tentative. The right model is not screening; it is accountability. If your machine is part of a botnet, your carrier should be empowered, and indeed required, to interrupt your service until it isn't.

Third, identity and authentication at the network layer. Spam, phishing, and most fraud rely on the cheapness of forged identity. Zittrain rightly observes that the hacker ethos has dissolved as the user base has grown, but he draws the wrong inference. The inference is not that we need to rebuild that ethos through Wikipedia-style community institutions; the inference is that anonymity at scale, in a system without reputation, produces the tragedy of the commons we are now living through. This need not mean abolishing pseudonymity — there are cryptographic schemes that allow accountable pseudonymity — but it does mean that the architecture's blithe assumption that every packet is as legitimate as every other has to change.

Zittrain will object that all of this risks generativity. So it does. But generativity is not a terminal value; it is an instrumental value, justified by the goods it produces. If those goods are being eaten alive by a parasitic criminal economy that the generative architecture itself enables, then the calculus has changed. We do not preserve the openness of a city by refusing to have police; we have police precisely so that the openness is bearable. The Internet has been operating as a city without police, on the theory that the residents would behave themselves. The residents have not behaved themselves.

There is also a political point worth making. The longer the legitimate security problem festers, the more drastic the eventual intervention will be. If we will not consider seatbelt-style regulation now, while the problem can still be addressed with measured tools, we are inviting the kind of post-catastrophe legislation that genuinely will wreck the Internet. The Patriot Act is the model here, not the counterexample. A serious, calibrated security policy now is the best protection against an unserious, draconian policy later. Zittrain's hopeful volunteerism, however appealing, will not buy us that protection. It will buy us, at most, the dignity of saying we tried something gentle before the heavy hand arrived.

The real challenge is not preserving the generative PC at all costs. It's ensuring that innovation continues to happen somewhere in the ecosystem, and that users retain meaningful choices about how their devices function. The market is delivering this. We should let it.

"While Zittrain correctly identifies the security threats facing generative systems, his proposed solution of community-based security is impractical because it underestimates the technical risks of malicious code and overestimates the average user's willingness to act as a system administrator."

Jonathan Zittrain’s diagnosis of the current crisis facing the Internet is as timely as it is alarming. He correctly observes that the open, "generative" nature of the personal computer—the very quality that fueled the digital revolution from the Apple II to the widespread adoption of broadband—is now its greatest vulnerability. As organized crime leverages this openness to build massive botnets, consumers are understandably retreating toward the safety of locked-down, "tethered" appliances like the newly introduced iPhone. Yet, while Zittrain's diagnosis is flawless, his proposed cure relies on an overly optimistic faith in the civic capacities of the average Internet user.

Zittrain suggests that we can save the generative PC by migrating the collaborative, community-driven solutions that have worked at the content layer—most notably Wikipedia—down to the technical code layer. He envisions users deploying quick-reversion technologies and crowdsourced security dashboards like Herdict to act as a digital neighborhood watch. However, this analogy fundamentally misjudges the profound asymmetry of risk between text and executable code.

When a vandal defaces a Wikipedia page, the damage is localized, visible, and easily reversed with a few clicks. The cost of error is virtually zero. When a trusting user accidentally executes a malicious payload on their PC, the consequences are immediate and often invisible. Even if a user relies on the "Red/Green" virtual machine division Zittrain proposes, a compromised "Red" machine can still harvest keystrokes, steal financial data, and quietly participate in distributed attacks against critical infrastructure before it is ever rolled back to a safe state. The trial-and-error ethos that makes wikis so successful is simply far too dangerous when applied to the realm of operating systems and network security.

Furthermore, Zittrain’s reliance on "netizens" to collaboratively evaluate the safety of software places an unrealistic cognitive burden on the mainstream public. The computer users of 2008 are no longer the hobbyists of 1979. Today’s broadband subscribers view the PC as a consumer utility—a way to check email, shop, and watch videos. They do not possess the desire, let alone the technical literacy, to monitor data packets or consult crowdsourced dashboards before running an application. The shift toward tethered appliances is not merely a corporate power grab; it is a rational delegation of risk by consumers who desperately want someone else to act as the gatekeeper.

Because users cannot realistically serve as their own system administrators, the transition toward locked-down appliances and Web 2.0 services is likely irreversible. Consequently, the most crucial elements of Zittrain’s essay are not his technical patches, but his legal and regulatory proposals. If we must entrust our digital lives to the walled gardens of hardware manufacturers and web giants, concepts like data portability and "appliance neutrality" become essential safeguards. We must ensure that these platforms allow users to easily extract their personal data and that companies cannot arbitrarily bait-and-switch developers after achieving market dominance.

The era of the purely generative PC may be closing, victim to its own unmanageable security flaws. We cannot reverse this tide by expecting the social dynamics of an online encyclopedia to secure our network infrastructure. Instead, our immediate challenge is to design robust legal frameworks that hold the architects of our new, tethered appliances accountable to the public interest.
