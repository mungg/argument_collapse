"The FCC should pursue bipartisan legislation to update the Communications Act rather than reclassifying the internet as a public utility under Title II."

I find myself in an uncomfortable position: agreeing with the FCC's goal but questioning whether its proposed method will actually achieve it. This is not obstructionism or regulatory fetishism—it is a practical concern about how legal authority actually works in contested terrain.

The FCC has been here before. In 2010, it adopted Open Internet rules that were promptly struck down by the D.C. Circuit Court because the agency lacked sufficient statutory authority. Rather than asking Congress to update the law as the court suggested, the FCC now proposes using Title II reclassification to give itself maximum legal cover. This is a regulatory strategy, not a policy vision.

I have sympathy for this approach—Title II provides the strongest hooks for court review. But we should be honest about what we are doing. We are using an awkward legal mechanism to accomplish what could more elegantly be achieved through legislation. The result will be years of legal challenges, uncertainty for investors, and ongoing regulatory whiplash every time a new FCC chairman takes office.

The better path was bipartisan: work with Congress to update the Communications Act for the digital age, creating rules specifically tailored to broadband that would have durable legal footing. That path remains available, and I wish the FCC had exhausted it beforeDefaulting to utility regulation.

If Title II does become the rule, I hope the FCC uses its discretion to forbear from most burdensome provisions. But forbearance decisions can be reversed. A law specifically prohibiting paid prioritization would be stronger than hoping future commissioners honor informal commitments.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Using Title II reclassification is an awkward legal mechanism that will lead to years of litigation and regulatory uncertainty.
  2. 02Legislative action would provide a more durable legal footing and prevent the 'regulatory whiplash' that occurs when FCC leadership changes.
  3. 03Relying on the FCC's discretion to waive burdensome utility regulations is risky because those decisions can be easily reversed by future commissioners.
  4. 04A law specifically tailored to broadband would more effectively prohibit paid prioritization than the current regulatory strategy.
