"The post-9/11 shift toward a centralized, authoritarian security state must be rejected in favor of an egalitarian model of defense that preserves civil liberties and the rule of law."

Elaine Scarry’s “Citizenship in Emergency” offers a striking reinterpretation of September 11, focusing on the mechanics of power, speed, and consent. While much debate will undoubtedly center on her timeline of the military response to Flight 77, the true power of Scarry’s essay lies in her urgent warning about the domestic consequences of our current security trajectory. By framing the conflict as a choice between a top-down, authoritarian model of defense and a distributed, egalitarian one, Scarry provides a vital framework for understanding the profound threat that the post-9/11 security state poses to American civil liberties.

It has been just over a year since the attacks, and the transformation of the American legal and domestic landscape is staggering. Congress rushed the USA PATRIOT Act into law with virtually no debate, expanding the government’s power to conduct warrantless wiretaps, access library records, and conduct sneak-and-peek searches of private homes. Hundreds of foreign nationals have been detained in secret, held for months without charges, and denied access to counsel. And now, the administration is floating proposals for domestic surveillance networks and turning citizens into informants under programs like Operation TIPS.

Scarry correctly identifies the root of these civil liberties abuses: the enduring myth that “speed” and efficiency require the total centralization of authority. For decades, the military-industrial complex used the threat of incoming nuclear missiles to justify bypassing the Constitution in foreign affairs. Now, that exact same logic is being turned inward. The Bush administration argues that the speed and unpredictability of terrorist threats mean they cannot be bound by the slow, deliberative processes of the Fourth Amendment, the courts, or legislative oversight.

But as Scarry points out, the top-down model failed utterly on 9/11. The rigid, centralized bureaucracy could not adapt to the unprecedented reality of hijacked domestic airliners. It was the distributed network of citizens on Flight 93—gathering information independently, communicating through cell phones, and deliberating together—that successfully countered the threat.

This observation has profound implications for civil liberties. The government is currently using the imperative of "homeland defense" to eliminate the very tools that empower citizens. Scarry notes the chilling proposal by government officials to take over civilian cellular networks in an emergency. This instinct—to silence the public and hoard information—is the hallmark of an authoritarian mindset. If the events of 9/11 proved anything, it is that a well-informed public with open lines of communication is far more resilient than a population kept in the dark and told to simply obey.

The centralization of surveillance and the abridgment of civil liberties do not make us safer; they make us subjects rather than citizens. When the government treats the entire population as potential suspects—subjecting them to mass surveillance and data mining—it destroys the trust and social cohesion necessary for a functioning democracy. Furthermore, centralized intelligence bureaucracies are notoriously prone to groupthink and confirmation bias. By walling off information from public scrutiny, the national security state deprives itself of the distributed intelligence of the citizenry.

Scarry’s call for an egalitarian model of defense is, at its core, a call for transparency and the rule of law. If we are to survive this crisis without losing our democratic soul, we must reject the false premise that security requires the surrender of our rights. We must demand that the government operate in the open, that detentions be subject to judicial review, and that our communications remain free from unwarranted interception.

The heroes of Flight 93 did not ask for a dictator to save them; they relied on themselves, their shared values, and their democratic instincts. We honor their memory not by giving the executive branch a blank check to strip away our civil liberties, but by fiercely defending the constitutional framework they died to protect. As Scarry eloquently reminds us, the protection of the country falls to everyone whose country it is. We must not sit quietly in our seats while our fundamental freedoms are hijacked in the name of security.
