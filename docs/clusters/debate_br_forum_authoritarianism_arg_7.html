"The debate over ideological moderation is a symptom of the Democratic Party's institutional atrophy, and the only way to defeat authoritarianism is to rebuild the party's local organizational roots and civic capacity."

Bonica and Grumbach’s essay is framed as an argument about moderation, but its most important implication concerns party organization. Democrats keep searching for the correct ideological formula because their institutions for knowing, recruiting, training, and sustaining candidates have atrophied. When a party is weak on the ground, it compensates with polling, consultants, television ads, and national messaging. Then it mistakes those substitutes for strategy.

The moderation debate is a symptom of this weakness. If Democrats had dense local organizations, they would not need to ask abstractly whether a candidate should be moderate or progressive. They would know who in a community has trust, what conflicts define local life, which issues are salient, which messengers can persuade, and where turnout can be expanded. They would have a bench of candidates whose identities and commitments were formed through local work, not through last-minute recruitment by national committees. The question would be less “Where should this candidate stand?” and more “Whom does this candidate represent?”

Instead, American parties have become hollow in many places. Campaigns appear, spend, and vanish. Local newspapers have disappeared. Civic associations have weakened. Unions are thinner than they once were. County parties are often underfunded, elderly, and disconnected from younger or working-class residents. Candidate pipelines run through law, business, nonprofits, and personal wealth. Consultants mediate between donors and voters. In this environment, moderation becomes attractive because it is legible to elites: choose the safer message, avoid controversy, raise money, target persuadables. It is a strategy for a party that does not have enough relationships.

Bonica and Grumbach are right that authenticity matters. But authenticity cannot be ordered from headquarters. It emerges from a life embedded in a place or a constituency. A nurse who has fought hospital understaffing can speak about health care differently than a policy entrepreneur. A tenant organizer can speak about rent differently than a consultant. A veteran can speak about service and state power differently than a think-tank fellow. A small-town mayor who fixed a bridge can speak about government competence differently than a senator reciting an infrastructure topline. Recruiting such people requires long-term party work.

Candidate recruitment should therefore be understood as democracy work. The party needs more teachers, nurses, union stewards, electricians, veterans, caregivers, farmers, service workers, small-business owners, and local problem-solvers. But it also needs to support them materially. Working-class candidates cannot self-fund. They may lack flexible jobs, donor networks, campaign experience, or tolerance for online abuse. If Democrats want different candidates, they must build institutions that lower the cost of running: training, childcare support, legal help, digital security, fundraising infrastructure, message coaching that does not erase the candidate’s voice, and protection against harassment.

The authors’ “let a thousand flowers bloom” instinct is right, but flowers need soil. A national party cannot simply declare experimentation and then abandon candidates to a brutal environment. Experimentation should be organized. Democrats should run more contested primaries in safe seats, more local candidates in unwinnable areas, more issue campaigns between elections, and more pilot programs in voter contact. They should measure what works without letting one cycle’s results harden into dogma. They should tolerate regional variation and stop treating every candidate’s deviation from national activist or donor preferences as a crisis.

This also means rebuilding local parties as civic institutions, not just election machines. A county party should help residents navigate public benefits, support striking workers, host candidate forums, train volunteers, register voters at community events, translate political information, and maintain a visible presence when there is no election. Churches, unions, mutual aid groups, veterans’ organizations, parent associations, and immigrant groups often do this better than formal party committees. Democrats should partner with them without trying to control them.

The turnout crisis cannot be solved by presidential campaigns alone. Irregular voters are irregular partly because politics is not woven into their daily lives. A text message in October cannot overcome years of alienation. People vote when someone they trust asks them, when they understand the stakes, when voting feels like part of collective action, and when barriers are manageable. That requires year-round organizing, not just persuasion analytics.

Bonica and Grumbach’s anti-corruption frame could be a powerful organizing tool because it invites local investigation. Where is corruption visible in a community? A utility monopoly? A sheriff’s department? A hospital board? A landlord bloc? A state legislature captured by fossil fuel interests? A school privatization scheme? A police union contract? A procurement scandal? A congressional stock trade? National anti-corruption rhetoric becomes concrete when local organizations identify local abuses and connect them to national patterns.

But here again, organization matters more than message. If Democrats run ads saying “Republicans are corrupt” while their own local officials cut insider deals, voters will see hypocrisy. If they help expose corruption in both parties, support whistleblowers, and back reforms that constrain themselves, voters may begin to believe them. Trust is built by conduct over time.

The party also needs to rethink primaries. Many elites fear primaries because they can produce nominees who are too ideological or damaged. That happens. But coronations produce their own problems: candidates chosen for donor comfort, name recognition, or résumé rather than connection. Competitive primaries can test messages, build volunteer networks, identify talent, and force candidates to speak to real constituencies. The answer is not to abolish elite judgment; parties need vetting. The answer is to make vetting broader and more democratic.

Bonica and Grumbach criticize popularists for seeing voters as fixed consumers. Party hollowing encourages that view. If a party lacks members in any meaningful sense, voters appear only as data points: segments, probabilities, turnout scores, persuasion targets. A stronger party would see voters as participants and potential leaders. It would ask not only what they currently believe, but what they could build together.

There is a paradox here. The United States has strong partisanship but weak parties. Voters identify intensely with national teams, while the organizations that might channel that energy into democratic participation are thin. This combination is dangerous. It produces symbolic polarization without civic capacity. People hate the other side but have few constructive ways to act with their own side beyond voting, donating, posting, or consuming partisan media. Authoritarians thrive in that vacuum because they offer belonging through loyalty to a leader. Democratic parties must offer belonging through shared work.

The moderation debate will not disappear, nor should it. Some candidates should be more moderate than others. Some should be more confrontational. Some districts require cultural reassurance; others reward ideological clarity. But these decisions should arise from rooted political judgment, not national pundit formulas. A living party can make such judgments. A hollow party hires consultants to simulate them.

If Democrats want to defeat authoritarianism, they must become a party people encounter as neighbors, coworkers, organizers, and local leaders, not only as ads and fundraising emails. They must invest in the unglamorous infrastructure of democracy: precinct work, candidate training, local media, unions, community groups, election administration, and year-round service. Anti-corruption, turnout, and authenticity all depend on this foundation.

Bonica and Grumbach are right that the old rules are dead. But new rules will not be discovered in a poll or a model. They will be built by people in organizations. The future of democracy depends less on whether Democrats choose the perfect ideological label than on whether they can become a party with roots deep enough to withstand the storm.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The reliance on polling and national messaging is a poor substitute for dense local organizations that understand community trust and specific local conflicts.
  2. 02Authenticity in candidates cannot be manufactured by national headquarters but must emerge from recruiting individuals deeply embedded in their constituencies, such as nurses, veterans, and laborers.
  3. 03The party must provide material and institutional support—such as childcare, legal help, and training—to enable working-class candidates to run for office.
  4. 04Local parties should function as year-round civic institutions that provide community services and maintain a visible presence rather than acting as mere election-cycle machines.
  5. 05The turnout crisis and the effectiveness of an anti-corruption platform depend on year-round organizing and the identification of local abuses rather than nationalized persuasion analytics.
  6. 06Competitive primaries should be embraced as a tool to test messages and build volunteer networks rather than being avoided by elites in favor of top-down coronations.
  7. 07To counter authoritarianism, the party must offer voters a sense of belonging through shared local work and democratic participation rather than just symbolic national polarization.
