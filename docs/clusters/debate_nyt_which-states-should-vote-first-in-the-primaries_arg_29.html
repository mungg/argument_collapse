"The presidential primary calendar should maintain Iowa and New Hampshire at the start but only if they are immediately followed by more diverse states like Nevada and South Carolina to create a balanced opening screen."

Iowa and New Hampshire are not “representative” in the census sense, and no honest defender should pretend otherwise. They are whiter, older, and more rural than the country. But demographic mirroring is not the only purpose of going first. The first contests are also a test of whether a candidate can answer questions from ordinary voters without a teleprompter, build an organization precinct by precinct, and survive scrutiny before the race becomes a national advertising war.

That is the strongest case for keeping Iowa and New Hampshire near the front. If California, Texas, Florida or New York voted first, the nomination would tilt even more toward celebrity, money and television. Small states give governors, senators and even outsiders a chance to be examined at close range. Jimmy Carter’s rise out of Iowa is the classic example, but the broader point remains: retail politics can humble front-runners and elevate candidates who would otherwise never break through.

Still, Iowa and New Hampshire should not have a permanent monopoly on winnowing the field. Their concerns can distort campaigns: ethanol in Iowa, local anti-tax politics in New Hampshire, and a political culture that is far more white than either party’s national electorate. The current early-state sequence is defensible only because Nevada and South Carolina follow quickly. Nevada brings organized labor, immigrants, Latinos and a service economy into the conversation. South Carolina gives black Democrats and Southern conservatives an early voice.

So the answer is not to abolish Iowa and New Hampshire, but to reduce their mythology. They should be first steps, not kingmakers. Iowa should especially be pressured to make participation easier; caucuses exclude shift workers, parents, the disabled and many people who cannot spend hours in a room on a winter night. New Hampshire’s primary is more accessible, but it too should be followed immediately by states that look less like New England town meeting democracy and more like the country.

The early calendar should remain small, but balanced: Iowa, New Hampshire, Nevada and South Carolina, with strict limits on how long they dominate the news. They are not representative enough alone. Together, and only together, they can form a useful opening screen.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Small states like Iowa and New Hampshire allow for retail politics and close-range scrutiny that prevent the nomination from being decided solely by money and celebrity.
  2. 02The demographic and political limitations of Iowa and New Hampshire can distort national campaigns if they are allowed to act as the sole kingmakers.
  3. 03Including Nevada and South Carolina early in the process ensures that the voices of organized labor, immigrants, and Black voters are heard before the field is winnowed.
  4. 04Iowa specifically needs to reform its caucus system to be more inclusive of voters who cannot participate in lengthy, time-specific events.
