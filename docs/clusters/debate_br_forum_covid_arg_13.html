"While a postmortem of the COVID-19 response is necessary, the critique offered by Macedo and Lee suffers from historical myopia by failing to account for the unprecedented nature of the virus and the organic behavioral shifts of a panicked public."

Stephen Macedo and Frances Lee have issued a provocative and necessary challenge to the intellectual and political classes of the United States: we must look unblinkingly at the totality of our COVID-19 response. Their plea for a comprehensive postmortem is one that any serious student of public policy should welcome. However, in their eagerness to condemn the deployment of non-pharmaceutical interventions (NPIs) and to expose the blind spots of the public health establishment, they fall victim to a profound historical myopia. They construct a narrative that meticulously tallies the costs of pandemic mitigation while conveniently minimizing the terrifying realities of a novel pathogen, the organic behavioral shifts of a panicked populace, and the sheer impossibility of perfect decision-making in the fog of war. In doing so, they substitute the clarity of hindsight for the agonizing uncertainty that characterized the early months of 2020.

To understand the flaws in Macedo and Lee’s retrospective, we must first address their reliance on pre-2020 pandemic planning. The authors place great weight on documents from the World Health Organization and various national health agencies which cautioned against lockdowns, border closures, and prolonged social distancing. They present these documents as a forgotten gospel of public health, ignored by panicked politicians. But this argument fundamentally misunderstands the nature of those pre-pandemic plans. Almost all of them were modeled on influenza—a virus with a short incubation period, where infectiousness largely coincides with symptom onset, and where the epidemiological dynamics were well understood.

SARS-CoV-2 was not influenza. By February 2020, it became clear that this novel coronavirus possessed a terrifying combination of traits: it was highly transmissible, it possessed a significant rate of asymptomatic and pre-symptomatic spread, and it carried a fatality rate substantially higher than the seasonal flu, particularly for the elderly and vulnerable. The pre-existing playbooks were designed for a different enemy. When hospitals in Bergamo, Italy, began running out of oxygen, and when physicians in New York City were forced to treat patients in overflow tents while refrigeration trucks idled outside to hold the dead, adherence to a 2011 UK influenza strategy would have been not just inadequate, but morally indefensible. The collapse of healthcare capacity is a non-linear event; when there are no ICU beds left, mortality spikes not just from COVID-19, but from car accidents, heart attacks, and strokes. The primary goal of the initial lockdowns was not to eradicate the virus—a strawman often erected by lockdown critics—but to flatten the curve to prevent the absolute collapse of the medical system. In that specific, immediate goal, the interventions arguably succeeded.

Furthermore, Macedo and Lee engage in a highly questionable accounting of the economic and social costs of the pandemic, attributing them almost entirely to government mandates. They list unemployment, business failures, delayed medical care, and learning loss as the direct collateral damage of policy. This assumes a counterfactual reality where, in the absence of government mandates, the American public would have gone about its business as usual while a deadly and poorly understood virus ravaged their communities. Economic data from the spring of 2020 tells a different story. In states that delayed locking down, and in countries like Sweden that opted for a lighter touch, consumer spending plummeted, restaurant reservations evaporated, and foot traffic collapsed weeks before any official orders were given. People stayed home because they were terrified of a new disease, not simply because a governor told them to.

The economic contraction was an inevitable consequence of the biological shock. While government mandates undoubtedly formalized and perhaps prolonged the economic deep freeze, placing the entirety of the blame for the 2020 recession and its subsequent societal scarring on NPIs is an analytical error. It ignores the agency of citizens who independently chose to withdraw from public life to protect themselves and their families. We cannot easily disentangle the damage caused by the virus from the damage caused by the response, but to lay all societal ills at the feet of public health officials is an exercise in scapegoating, not sober analysis.

Macedo and Lee also point to the lack of outcome divergence between Republican states that opened early and Democratic states that remained restricted as proof that NPIs failed. But this ecological analysis oversimplifies a complex reality. By the time the partisan divergence in policy became stark in late 2020, the nature of the interventions had changed. The initial, near-universal lockdowns had ended. The subsequent variations in policy were far less extreme—mask mandates versus no mask mandates, capacity limits versus full capacity. Furthermore, the United States features porous state borders. A strict lockdown in one state is inherently compromised if a neighboring state is entirely open. The lack of a unified federal response meant that state-level NPIs were always fighting an uphill battle. Evaluating the theoretical efficacy of NPIs based on their disjointed, politicized, and often poorly complied-with implementation in the American context is like declaring that umbrellas do not work because you tried to use one in a hurricane.

Finally, the authors are highly critical of the communication strategies of public health officials, accusing them of paternalism and a failure to convey uncertainty. On this point, there is merit to their critique. The messaging from institutions like the CDC and figures like Dr. Anthony Fauci was at times contradictory and overly definitive, damaging long-term institutional credibility. However, we must view these missteps through the lens of crisis management. Officials were operating in an environment of unprecedented political polarization, where the sitting President was routinely floating unproven therapeutics and undermining basic public health advice on national television. The impulse to project certainty and unified authority, while ultimately counterproductive, was born of a desperate desire to save lives in a chaotic information environment.

We absolutely need the postmortem that Macedo and Lee demand. We must build a more resilient society that does not rely on blunt-force closures to manage disease. We must invest heavily in indoor air quality, next-generation vaccine platforms, and robust social safety nets. But as we conduct this review, we must extend a measure of grace to those who were forced to make life-and-death decisions with incomplete data in the spring of 2020. The precautionary principle dictated action in the face of an existential, poorly understood threat. We can, and must, learn from the profound collateral damage of our pandemic response, but we should not rewrite history to suggest that the choices facing policymakers were easy, or that the path of minimal intervention would have been devoid of its own catastrophic costs.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Pre-2020 pandemic plans were designed for influenza and were fundamentally inadequate for a novel pathogen characterized by asymptomatic spread and high mortality.
  2. 02The economic and social costs of the pandemic cannot be blamed solely on government mandates, as citizens independently withdrew from public life due to fear of the virus.
  3. 03The lack of outcome divergence between states with different policies is an unreliable metric because state-level interventions were undermined by porous borders and a lack of federal coordination.
  4. 04The initial lockdowns were a necessary and successful application of the precautionary principle intended to prevent the total collapse of the healthcare system.
  5. 05While public health communication was flawed, the impulse to project certainty was a response to an environment of extreme political polarization and misinformation.
