"The primary failure of the COVID-19 response was a matter of political economy, where a professional managerial class designed and defended policies that insulated themselves from risk while shifting the physical and economic burdens of the pandemic onto the working class."

In their compelling retrospective on the COVID-19 pandemic, Stephen Macedo and Frances Lee correctly identify the catastrophic collateral damage of our public health response. However, their analysis, while rich in data regarding school closures and mortality rates, only grazes the surface of the pandemic’s most enduring legacy: the weaponization of public health to engineer the largest upward transfer of wealth and security in modern American history. The true failure of our pandemic response was not merely epistemic or scientific; it was fundamentally a failure of political economy. The policies adopted in the spring of 2020 and maintained for months thereafter were defined, designed, and defended by a professional managerial class that successfully insulated itself from the physical and economic ravages of the virus, actively shifting the burden of both infection and economic ruin onto the working class.

Macedo and Lee briefly acknowledge the "laptop class," noting that socioeconomic status insulated elites from the worst harms. This observation needs to be the central pillar of any pandemic postmortem, not a passing addendum. The phrase "we are all in this together," endlessly repeated by politicians, celebrities, and corporate brands in the early days of the lockdown, was a grotesque fiction. The reality was a stark bifurcation of American society into the exposed and the enclosed.

When stay-at-home orders were issued, millions of knowledge workers retreated to comfortable homes to conduct their jobs over Zoom, continuing to draw full salaries, build equity in rapidly appreciating real estate, and save money on commuting. But the physical world did not stop operating. The logistical infrastructure required to sustain this mass retreat into the virtual realm demanded the continued physical labor of millions of people who were abruptly rebranded as "essential workers." These were the grocery store clerks, the meatpacking plant employees, the Amazon warehouse pickers, the delivery drivers, and the transit workers. They were disproportionately working-class, disproportionately people of color, and disproportionately low-income.

The public health consensus essentially dictated that it was too dangerous for a marketing executive to go into an office, but perfectly acceptable for an Instacart shopper to brave a crowded supermarket to deliver the executive’s groceries. We did not truly lock down society; we simply restricted the movement of the affluent and compelled the working class to act as a human shield, absorbing the biological risk so the professional class could remain safe. The virus, predictably, tore through these essential worker communities, ravaging multi-generational households where social distancing was a spatial impossibility. The staggering racial disparities in early COVID-19 mortality were not a biological mystery; they were the direct result of who was allowed to shelter and who was forced to labor.

Compounding this physical vulnerability was the economic devastation wrought upon those in the non-essential, in-person service sector. Millions of restaurant workers, hospitality staff, retail employees, and salon workers saw their livelihoods evaporate overnight. Macedo and Lee rightly point to the massive expansion of the welfare state—the CARES Act, enhanced unemployment, and stimulus checks—as an unprecedented intervention. But this aid, while vital, was reactive, temporary, and often trapped behind overwhelmed state bureaucracies. It was a Band-Aid on a decapitation.

Meanwhile, the economic policies meant to stabilize the system overwhelmingly favored capital over labor. The Paycheck Protection Program (PPP) was rife with fraud and largely functioned as a massive grant to business owners, with a relatively small percentage of the funds ultimately reaching the workers it was purportedly designed to protect. The Federal Reserve’s aggressive intervention stabilized the stock market and lowered interest rates, sparking a massive boom in asset prices. The wealthy watched their stock portfolios and home values soar to record highs while working-class Americans struggled to navigate broken unemployment portals and faced mounting food insecurity.

This class divide was deeply reflected in the debate over non-pharmaceutical interventions (NPIs) and school closures. The ability to endure a prolonged school closure is a function of privilege. Affluent families could hire private tutors, form "learning pods," or simply rely on a stay-at-home parent or flexible remote-work schedule to guide their children through virtual learning. For single mothers working essential jobs, a closed school was an unmitigated disaster, forcing impossible choices between earning a living and leaving young children unsupervised. The teachers' unions and public health officials who advocated for keeping schools closed well into 2021—long after European nations had safely reopened theirs—were structurally blind to the realities of working-class family life. They operated from a paradigm where the home was a safe, well-resourced sanctuary, an assumption that was profoundly out of touch with the lived reality of millions of Americans.

The suppression of dissent that Macedo and Lee document—the silencing of the Great Barrington Declaration authors and the stigmatization of anyone questioning the collateral costs of lockdowns—was not just a scientific dispute; it was a mechanism of class defense. To question the lockdowns was to question a system that was working exceedingly well for the people in charge of the discourse. The media, the academy, and the public health bureaucracy are overwhelmingly populated by members of the laptop class. Their risk calculus naturally elevated the threat of the virus (which could affect them) over the threat of economic ruin and community decay (which largely affected others).

If we are to have a serious accounting of the COVID-19 years, we cannot limit our critique to the mechanics of epidemiology or the efficacy of masks. We must confront the grim reality that when faced with a generational crisis, the American political and economic system defaulted to its most deeply ingrained habit: protecting capital and the professional class at the absolute expense of the working poor. Until we reckon with the profound selfishness of the "laptop class" response, any preparations we make for the next pandemic will only replicate the same devastating inequalities.
