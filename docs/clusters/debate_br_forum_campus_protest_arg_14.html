"While Robin D. G. Kelley’s critique of the student movement offers valuable intellectual solidarity, his strategic pessimism regarding the university's potential for change risks undermining the movement's ability to use the campus as a primary site for dismantling racial capitalism."

What is so disarming about Robin D. G. Kelley’s “Black Study, Black Struggle” is its willingness to be impolite, even impolitic, within the shared space of the left. Here is a renowned radical historian refusing to simply cheer on the student movement from the sidelines, choosing instead a course of public, loving critique. This is an act of profound intellectual solidarity. Yet in his strategic pessimism about the university as an institution, Kelley may have inadvertently foreclosed on the most radical potential of the current movement: the possibility not just of winning reforms, but of catalyzing the university’s disintegration as a neoliberal enterprise.

Kelley articulates two paths, each leading to a critique of the university, but he ultimately argues that both are doomed to failure. The reformists will never get the university to be a “post-racial haven,” and the revolutionaries wrongly imagine the university as the “leading edge” of social transformation. Both, he says, make demands the university cannot meet. This conclusion is elegant but strangely quietist. It risks consigning student activism on campus to a purely symbolic, pedagogical role—a theater of demystification—while the “real” work of political education and transformation happens “by definition… outside the university.” This is a false spatial binary. The university is not a bubble; it is one of the largest landowners, employers, investors, and purveyors of ideology in American life. A direct strike at the heart of the university’s financial and labor model is a direct strike at the heart of racial capitalism itself, not a rehearsal for it.

Take the radical demands Kelley cites approvingly, particularly those from UNC Chapel Hill. These include a $25 minimum wage for all workers, free childcare, and the retraining and disarming of campus police. If a major public university were forced to adopt even a few of these, it would not simply make the campus “marginally more welcoming.” It would blow a hole through the entire economic logic of the contemporary university. It would redistribute millions of dollars from administration and construction to poor and working-class people, a majority of whom are black and brown. It would decommodify essential social services like child care, creating a beachhead of decommodified life within a sea of market relations. It would fundamentally challenge the carceral state’s reach into daily life by disarming its agents on campus soil. These are not just symbolic acts of demystification; they are a concrete program for prefiguring a socialist society, using the university’s existing resources. If such demands are “unwinnable,” it is only because we have prematurely conceded the fight.

This is where Kelley’s admiration for Harney and Moten’s “undercommons” concept leads to a political dead-end. The undercommons, as Kelley presents it, is a place of refuge, sabotage, and fugitive planning, but pointedly not of contestation, seizure, or institutional transformation. It sneaks in and steals what it can. This is a beautiful metaphor for surviving a hostile institution, but a poor strategy for destroying one. The students at Mizzou, through the brilliant tactic of engaging the football team, did not just “sneak in” and steal; they leveraged the university’s own financial lifeblood—its athletics revenue—to force the resignations of a president and chancellor. That was a seizure of institutional power, a direct contestation on the terrain of political economy. It was far more in the tradition of a labor strike than a fugitive study group. By framing the university’s power as “illusory” and disavowing any contestation on its terms, the undercommons philosophy cedes the very ground where students are effectively building power.

Of course love, study, and struggle are indivisible. Kelley’s recovery of the Freedom Schools and the Mt. Vernon study groups as models of “grounded” intellectual work is the highlight of the essay. But those projects were grounded in movements that also engaged in pitched battle for public resources, land, and labor rights. The study was fuel for the fight, not an alternative to it. The most militant campus organizing today sees the university not just as a fallow field to be fled for the undercommons, or a hostile patriarch to be petitioned for love, but as a terrain of class war to be won. The radical demand to transform the university into a genuinely public, worker-controlled, decolonized institution is one we may never fully realize. But fighting for it on this terrain, with these resources, is one of the most important political educations a young radical can ever get. We shouldn’t be telling students the fight is elsewhere; we should be helping them figure out how to win it right where they stand.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The university is not a separate bubble but a major economic and ideological engine of American life, making a direct strike on its financial model a direct strike on racial capitalism itself.
  2. 02Radical student demands, such as high minimum wages and free childcare, are not merely symbolic but represent a concrete program for redistributing wealth and decommodifying social services using existing institutional resources.
  3. 03The 'undercommons' philosophy of fugitive study and sabotage is an insufficient strategy because it cedes the terrain of institutional power that students have already proven they can successfully contest through tactics like strikes.
  4. 04The historical models of 'grounded' study Kelley cites were fuel for pitched battles over public resources and labor rights, suggesting that intellectual work should empower students to win the class war within the university rather than flee it.
