"While effective altruism correctly prioritizes global need over local luxury, it must evolve from a donor-centered, transactional model into a more democratic approach that respects the agency, dignity, and political aspirations of the communities it seeks to help."

From the standpoint of people in wealthy countries, effective altruism can look like a revolution in moral seriousness. From the standpoint of the poor countries that are often its objects, it can look more ambiguous. Peter Singer’s essay is animated by genuine concern for those whose suffering is too easily ignored. He is right that a child’s life in a malaria-prone village matters no less than a child’s life in San Francisco. But if effective altruism is to be worthy of that principle, it must take the agency, knowledge, and political aspirations of poor communities as seriously as it takes the preferences of donors.

The most attractive feature of Singer’s argument is its refusal of national favoritism. Too much philanthropy follows the donor’s biography: my university, my museum, my city, my disease, my country. For people outside the charmed circle of rich-world attention, this is a deadly bias. If effective altruism redirects money from elite universities to malaria prevention, deworming, direct cash transfers, or basic health care, many people will be better off. That is not a small achievement.

But there is a danger in the question “Where can my donation do the most good?” The person asking it is usually an affluent outsider. The people affected appear as beneficiaries, data points, or “the global poor.” They are rarely presented as political actors with their own analyses of what they need. A bed net may save a life; a cash transfer may help a family invest in a roof, a goat, school fees, or a business. But poor people do not aspire only to be efficiently helped. They aspire to shape the institutions that govern their lives.

Development history is littered with confident outsiders. Some brought useful vaccines, wells, and crops. Others brought schemes that ignored local conditions, undermined existing practices, empowered corrupt intermediaries, or created dependency. Effective altruists distinguish themselves from old-style paternalists by demanding evidence. That is a major improvement. Yet evidence gathered to satisfy donors can still miss what matters to communities. Who decided that the intervention was the right one to test? Who defines the outcome? Who controls the funds? Who is accountable if priorities change?

Take malaria. Insecticide-treated nets are a proven tool. Distributing them widely is good. But lasting malaria control also requires public health systems, trained local workers, reliable supply chains, environmental management, and governments with the capacity to serve remote populations. Those things are harder to fund and harder to measure. If donors favor discrete interventions with clean metrics, they may unintentionally weaken the broader systems on which people depend. The same is true in education, agriculture, and health: a successful project can coexist with institutional fragility.

Singer’s essay does not ignore complexity, but its examples encourage a donor-centered simplicity. “Your $7,500 could save three lives” is morally powerful. It is also rhetorically risky. It can make lifesaving seem like a transaction: donor gives, expert charity delivers, poor child lives. Reality is more relational. The mother who brings a child to a clinic, the community health worker who walks miles, the government official who maintains a program, the local advocate who demands services, and the taxpayers of poor countries themselves are part of the story. Effective altruism should not erase them.

The movement also needs a stronger account of dignity. Direct cash transfers are interesting partly because they resist paternalism: instead of deciding which good poor people should receive, they give money and trust recipients. The early evidence from organizations such as GiveDirectly challenges stereotypes that poor people will waste unconditional aid. This should push effective altruists toward a more general principle: whenever possible, help should increase the choices and power of the people helped.

That principle may lead beyond conventional charity. Migrant remittances, fair labor conditions, access to rich-country markets, reduced corruption, public health financing, women’s education, land rights, and democratic accountability can matter as much as philanthropic programs. Some of these are political, and politics is messy. But poor people live inside political economies, not inside charity evaluations. If effective altruism avoids politics because it is uncertain, it may become excellent at treating symptoms and timid about causes.

None of this is an excuse for the affluent to do nothing. Critics sometimes speak as if exposing paternalism absolves them of responsibility. It does not. If a wealthy person can prevent disease by funding bed nets or can increase a poor household’s options through a cash transfer, she should not withhold help while waiting for perfect justice. The ethical scandal Singer identifies is real: enormous suffering persists while resources are spent on luxuries and prestige projects.

The challenge is to combine urgency with humility. Effective altruists should ask not only “What works?” but “Who says so?” They should fund local organizations, not only international intermediaries. They should support research led by scholars and practitioners from affected countries. They should treat feedback from recipients as evidence, not public relations. They should care about whether programs build local capacity or merely deliver donor-visible outputs. And they should be willing to support advocacy that shifts power, even when the results cannot be predicted with the precision of a bed-net distribution.

Singer’s moral universalism is the right starting point. Every life counts equally. But equal concern requires more than calculating benefits to distant strangers. It requires listening to them, sharing power with them, and respecting their right to define a flourishing future. Effective altruism will do the most good when it is not only effective and altruistic, but also democratic.
