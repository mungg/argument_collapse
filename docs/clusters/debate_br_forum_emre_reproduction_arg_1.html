Vanilla GPT

"While all reproduction is assisted by social and medical systems, a feminist reproductive politics must move beyond simply embracing technology to critically examining the power structures, market forces, and inequalities that govern who controls and pays for that assistance."

Merve Emre is right that “natural” reproduction is neither natural nor autonomous. It is scaffolded by pregnancy tests, sonograms, epidurals, antibiotics, neonatal intensive care units, insurance codes, grandparents, paid and unpaid leave, immigration status, marriage law, and the willingness of other people to recognize one’s family as a family. The fiction that some people simply reproduce while others require “assistance” is a political fiction, and it does real harm. It ranks families by presumed authenticity; it makes queer parents, single parents, disabled parents, trans parents, infertile parents, and adoptive parents explain themselves; it turns the desire for a child into evidence of either selfishness or pathology when that desire does not travel through heterosexual intercourse.

But I hesitate at the suggestion that assisted reproduction should become “the preferred strategy” of a feminist reproductive politics. If all reproduction is assisted, then the central feminist question is not whether we should embrace assistance. We already do. The question is: which forms of assistance, controlled by whom, paid for by whom, and at whose risk?

That question matters because “assistance” is not innocent. It can mean a friend driving you home from an egg retrieval. It can mean a state Medicaid program paying for prenatal care. It can mean a paid doula, a lactation consultant, a union contract, a grandmother’s unpaid labor, or a safe apartment with clean water. It can also mean a clinic selling hope at prices most people cannot afford, a resident learning on a patient’s body without adequate consent, an employer offering egg freezing while leaving workplace timelines intact, or a surrogacy arrangement that transfers bodily risk down class and national hierarchies. To say that all reproduction is assisted should widen our field of vision beyond the clinic, not make the clinic the privileged site of feminist imagination.

The stories Emre tells make this plain. S’s egg freezing is not simply a triumph of technology over biology. It is also a story about a workplace that can absorb the cost of preserving reproductive possibility better than it can imagine reorganizing labor so that workers may have children without penalty. B’s IVF is not simply a story about individual choice. It is a story about debt, contingent academic employment, the absence of universal paid leave, and the peculiar cruelty of a medical system in which one can produce embryos before one can secure the conditions to raise a child. N and K’s experience shows that access to reproductive medicine does not by itself undo heterosexism; the clinic can reproduce the very norms it appears to bypass. The doctor who asks for a “sample” from an imaginary husband is not an incidental comic detail. He is part of the technology.

This is why the opposition between “nature” and “technology” can mislead even when we reject the natural. Reproductive technologies are not merely tools that liberate us from biological destiny. They are institutions, markets, protocols, habits of speech, training regimes, liability calculations, and cultural fantasies. They arrive already shaped by race, class, disability, gender, and nation. A feminist politics cannot be simply pro-technology, any more than it can be pro-nature. It must be pro-accountability.

Consider IVF. It has enabled many people to have children who otherwise could not, and any feminist account that treats this desire with contempt is morally unserious. Yet IVF also dramatizes inequality. In the United States, access depends heavily on geography, insurance, employer, marital status, diagnosis, and wealth. The procedures are physically demanding, emotionally consuming, and uncertain. Success rates vary sharply by age and medical history. Patients often pay not only for retrievals and transfers, but also for medications, genetic testing, storage, donor gametes, legal agreements, and time away from work. Meanwhile, the labor of egg donors and gestational surrogates is often discussed in the language of generosity while being organized through markets that depend on unequal bargaining power. Feminists should defend the legitimacy of assisted reproduction without romanticizing the industries that provide it.

The same is true of artificial wombs, whose history Emre deftly excavates. Ectogenesis has long carried both emancipatory and coercive possibilities. It might reduce the dangers and burdens of pregnancy; it might help extremely premature infants survive; it might expand the ways people form families. But in a country where abortion rights are already fragile, the fantasy of fetal transfer could also become a weapon. If gestation can be imagined as separable from the pregnant person, anti-abortion arguments may shift from “you must remain pregnant” to “you must submit to extraction.” The burden would not disappear; it would change form. A technology that appears to free women from pregnancy could, under conditions of reproductive unfreedom, strengthen the state’s claim over pregnant bodies.

This is not an argument against such technologies. It is an argument against assuming that technological capacity generates political freedom. The history of reproduction in the United States gives us little reason for that confidence. Women of color, poor women, disabled women, incarcerated women, and Indigenous women have been subjected to sterilization abuse, coerced contraception, child removal, punitive welfare policy, and criminal prosecution during pregnancy. At the same time, many have been denied infertility care, prenatal care, safe housing, and the resources necessary to raise children. Dorothy Roberts’s reproductive justice framework is indispensable precisely because it refuses to isolate the right not to have a child from the right to have one and to parent in safety. That framework should also keep us from treating access to IVF or egg freezing as the horizon of reproductive equality.

The “natural” has indeed done ideological damage. It has made pain virtuous, mothers self-sacrificing, breastfeeding compulsory, vaginal birth morally superior, genetic connection mystical, and heterosexual reproduction ordinary in ways that render everyone else derivative. Yet there are reasons some people reach for the language of nature that are not merely reactionary. For some women, especially those who have been overmedicalized, experimented on, dismissed, or harmed by doctors, “natural” can name a demand for bodily autonomy against institutional arrogance. For some Black women facing stark maternal health disparities, the desire for midwives, doulas, and community birth workers may reflect not consumerist Edenism but a rational distrust of hospitals that do not listen to them. For some disabled people, resistance to genetic screening may arise from the knowledge that medicine often confuses disability with a life not worth living.

The task, then, is not to replace reverence for nature with reverence for technology. It is to make reproduction less governed by reverence altogether. People should not have to prove that their reproductive choices are natural, radical, efficient, selfless, or politically exemplary. They should not have to turn their embryos, pregnancies, abortions, miscarriages, adoptions, transitions, or childlessness into public pedagogy. A lesbian couple should not have to reproduce queerly enough. A trans woman should not have to renounce genetic parenthood to validate her womanhood. A single woman should not have to present IVF as either empowerment or tragedy. A woman who wants an epidural, a scheduled cesarean, donor sperm, no children, six children, or a child genetically related to her should not be conscripted into someone else’s symbolism.

What would better assistance look like? Universal health care that includes contraception, abortion, prenatal care, childbirth, infertility treatment, gender-affirming care, and mental health care. Paid parental leave not tied to a particular employer’s generosity. Childcare treated as infrastructure. Legal recognition of diverse families without forcing parents through expensive, humiliating procedures. Strong protections against pregnancy discrimination and anti-LGBT discrimination. Ethical oversight of fertility clinics, egg donation, embryo storage, and surrogacy. Serious attention to maternal mortality, especially the disproportionate deaths of Black women. Support for disabled parents. A foster and adoption system that does not treat poverty as neglect or biology as destiny. These are not glamorous technologies, but they are reproductive technologies in the broadest sense: social arrangements that make life possible.

Emre’s most powerful claim is that reproduction exposes the insufficiency of choice as a political ideal. The people in her essay make choices constantly, but rarely under conditions they would have chosen. They choose between debt and delay, between bodily risk and lost time, between legal vulnerability and medical alienation, between disclosure and loneliness. Feminism should defend their choices, but it should not stop there. It should change the conditions under which those choices become necessary.

All reproduction is assisted. The next step is to insist that assistance be collective, equitable, and noncoercive. Not because technology will save us from nature, and not because nature will save us from technology, but because no one creates or sustains life alone.

  2. 02The focus of reproductive politics should shift from the clinic to broader social infrastructure, such as universal healthcare and paid leave, which are the true 'technologies' that make life possible.
  3. 03Technological advancement does not inherently generate political freedom; without reproductive justice, new technologies like artificial wombs could actually increase state and medical control over pregnant bodies.

  5. 05A truly emancipatory politics should strive to make reproduction less governed by symbolic reverence—whether for nature or technology—allowing individuals to make reproductive choices without having to justify them as politically or morally exemplary.

Vanilla Claude

"While Merve Emre correctly identifies that all reproduction is technologically and socially assisted, her framework risks flattening the power dynamics and market-driven inequalities that determine who receives that assistance and on what terms."

Merve Emre's essay makes a claim that is both modest and audacious: that all reproduction is assisted, and that the appearance of unassistance is itself a kind of privilege. The modest version is undeniable. Prenatal vitamins, ultrasounds, hospital deliveries, epidurals, formula, pediatricians, parental leave, kin networks — none of these is "natural" in any rigorous sense, and the women who do not need to think about them as technologies enjoy a luxury invisible to themselves. The audacious version goes further: that the line between unassisted and assisted reproduction is not a line at all, only a gradient organized by what a culture has agreed to render unmarked. On this point Emre is largely persuasive, and her case is strengthened by the fact that her four narrators — S, B, K and N, micha cárdenas — keep stumbling into versions of "the natural" even as they undergo procedures that should have inoculated them against it. The discourse is sticky precisely because it is not a lifestyle choice; it is, as Emre says, a political problem.

Still, I want to press on a few places where the slogan does too much work. To say that all reproduction is assisted is true at the level of description but risks flattening distinctions that matter for the politics Emre wants to build. There is a difference between assistance that one can refuse and assistance one cannot conceive without, between assistance an insurer will cover and assistance one finances by borrowing from friends, between the assistance offered to a married straight couple at a teaching hospital and the assistance withheld from a fat lesbian whose obstetrician thinks she should not reproduce. Emre obviously knows this — her essay is structured around such gradations. But the rhetorical move from "all reproduction is assisted" to a politics in which "assisted reproduction" becomes "the preferred strategy by which we fight" elides the question of who pays for the assistance and on what terms. The default of the natural is exclusionary, yes; but the default of the technological is, at present, a market.

This matters because the essay's most vivid set piece — S at the unnamed Silicon Valley biotech, whose $20,000 lifetime cap for elective oocyte cryopreservation was won partly through advocacy by an LGBT employee association — is described as a "happy product of Silicon Valley's marriage of capitalist competition and social justice." Emre largely defends S against Suzanne McGee's Guardian piece, and rightly: McGee's contempt landed on the women rather than the firms. But the defense leaves the structural picture uncontested. Egg freezing as a tech perk is not just covered infertility care extended to gay and trans employees; it is also a benefit whose logic dovetails with employer interests in delaying the childbearing of high-performing women. The two things can be true at once. To name only the emancipatory side of the bargain is to reproduce, in miniature, the universalizing move Emre criticizes in feminist manifestos: a technology calibrated to one set of bodies and budgets is offered as a model for reproductive freedom in general.

I think this is why Dorothy Roberts hovers a little uneasily at the end of the essay. Roberts's reproductive justice framework, developed in *Killing the Black Body* and elaborated by SisterSong and others, is not primarily about access to ART. It is about the long American history of forced sterilization, the criminalization of pregnancy among Black and poor women, the prosecution of drug-using mothers, the family-policing functions of the child welfare system, and the disproportionate maternal mortality of Black women — a figure that, by 2018, is roughly three to four times that of white women in the United States. The positive right to have children and raise them in safety is, for the women Roberts writes about, mostly threatened not by the discourse of the natural but by the state and the carceral apparatus that adjoins it. Emre cites Roberts in her closing pages, but the four narratives that organize the essay sit at some distance from Roberts's archive. S, B, K, N, and cárdenas are not interchangeable — cárdenas in particular speaks from inside the violence Roberts describes — but the gravitational center of the essay is the professional woman in a coastal city navigating an under-insured but still navigable system. A politics that wants the technological to catch up to the political will need to be honest about whose technological catching-up is at stake.

The other place I want to push is on the persistence of the biological child as object of desire in nearly every story Emre tells. S has fifteen frozen eggs and a clear path to adoption or donor embryos and yet wonders why she can't "just have the baby." B considers adoption, attends a ten-week foster training, and decides against it because she does not want to rehearse loss. N and K, lectured against biological reproduction, double down on it. cárdenas, the most theoretically radical of the four, banks her sperm and writes movingly about making babies with queer trans bodies. Emre presents this as evidence that bodies are unruly sites for politics — that belief and behavior do not always align — and that this unruliness is itself a kind of anti-naturalist truth. I find this generous and largely right. But it raises a question the essay does not quite answer: if the discourse of the natural is as ideological as Emre argues, why is the wish for genetic continuity, for the child who is "a mixture of us two in human form," so resilient even in those most equipped to see through it? One answer, congenial to Emre, is that ideology runs deep. Another, less congenial, is that the desire for biological kinship is not reducible to ideology, and that an emancipatory politics of reproduction will have to make space for it rather than treat it as residue to be cleared away.

None of this is to argue against the essay's central move. The redescription of "natural" reproduction as merely a form of assistance whose costs are socially absorbed is exactly the kind of conceptual work feminism needs. It is to argue that the slogan, to do the political work Emre wants it to do, has to carry within it the question of who is assisted, by whom, on what terms, and at what cost. Otherwise "all reproduction is assisted" becomes a description of the present rather than a demand on the future.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The claim that all reproduction is assisted elides critical distinctions between assistance that is socially subsidized for the privileged and assistance that is withheld from or prohibitively expensive for marginalized groups.
  2. 02Framing corporate-sponsored reproductive technologies like egg freezing as purely emancipatory ignores how they serve capitalist interests by incentivizing employees to delay childbearing.
  3. 03The essay’s focus on professional women in coastal cities navigating medical systems sits in tension with Dorothy Roberts’s reproductive justice framework, which centers on state-sanctioned violence and the carceral policing of Black and poor mothers.
  4. 04The persistent desire for biological kinship among the essay's subjects suggests that reproductive longing may not be entirely reducible to the 'naturalist' ideology Emre critiques.
  5. 05Without addressing the structural costs and the question of who pays for technology, the slogan 'all reproduction is assisted' remains a description of current market conditions rather than a transformative political demand.
Diversified DeepSeek

"While the lead essay correctly identifies the 'natural' as a site of patriarchal control, its uncritical embrace of reproductive technology risks reducing human life to a matter of technical optimization and ignores the urgent need for ethical limits."

There is a ghost haunting “All Reproduction Is Assisted,” and it is not Shulamith Firestone or Donna Haraway. It is Aldous Huxley. The essay gestures at *Brave New World* only in its historical prelude, as a footnote to the pulp‑era fascination with artificial wombs. But the dystopian anxieties that Huxley gave voice to—about the rationalization of life, the elimination of suffering, the divorce of procreation from love and biology—have never really gone away, and they deserve a more honest hearing than this essay grants them. By dismissing any unease about reproductive technology as “internalized misogyny” or a “dangerous ideology” of the natural, the essay forecloses a conversation that many thoughtful people, including feminists, believe we urgently need to have: not about whether technology may be used, but about what limits, if any, we should place on its development and application.

I want to be clear about what I am not arguing. I do not think that IVF is sinful, that queers and single women should not have access to sperm banks, or that the body is a sacred temple that must not be touched by human artifice. The lead essay is right that the “natural” is often a word that smuggles in patriarchal and heteronormative assumptions. But the solution to a bad concept is not to banish the entire domain of the given; it is to sort out what in our embodiment is a source of constraint we should overcome and what might be a source of value we should learn to honor. The risk of a fully technologized approach to reproduction is that, in the name of liberation, we lose the relational, vulnerable, and unmasterable dimensions of bringing a new human being into existence.

The essay’s own reporting provides plenty of reasons for caution, even if the author does not always underline them. B’s experience of IVF is one of physical punishment—fourteen pounds gained in a week, a racing heart, ovaries “alert to gravity,” the cruel irony of having to sacrifice viable eggs because the body’s response was too strong. She is told that these side effects are “normal,” but it is a normality that feels unsafe. The clinic’s language of optimization leads her through a process of genetic screening that pares twenty‑two eggs down to three “good” embryos. At no point does the essay seriously consider whether there is something ethically troubling about this attrition, this systematic rejection of life that does not meet a chromosomal bar. The author’s tone is one of slightly weary sympathy, not moral interrogation. But the moral questions are inescapable. What does it mean to create a dozen potential lives, only to freeze, discard, or donate to research the eleven you will not implant? What does it do to our sense of human dignity when an embryo’s worth is measured by a DNA report?

The essay’s trans stories are particularly complex, and here the flattening effect of the “all is assistance” framing becomes visible. When a trans woman goes off her estrogen to bank sperm, or a trans man carries a pregnancy, something remarkable is happening: a human being is navigating a body that has been reshaped by hormones and surgery, making use of biological capacities in a way that no one fully controls. That is an experience that calls for humility and care, not for a smug assertion that it is just one more form of “assisted reproduction.” Similarly, when the essay quotes micha cárdenas writing, “I see the sperm under the microscope, each one swimming, with its own intention, each one its own possible life,” the language is Whitmanesque, but the sentiment is not so distant from the wonder that a “natural” parent might feel at a first ultrasound. The difference is not that one wonder is ideological and the other authentic; it is that both arise from an encounter with the strange, uncanny fact of new life—an encounter that technology can facilitate but also, if we are not careful, can reduce to a matter of successful output.

There is a political danger, too, in the unbridled enthusiasm for artificial reproduction. If gestation becomes, in Firestone’s dream, something that can be outsourced to a machine, who will control that machine? The state? A corporation? The history of eugenics in the United States—forced sterilizations, IQ‑based immigration restrictions, the enthusiastic support of early Planned Parenthood for some eugenic measures—teaches us that reproductive technology has always been entangled with projects of racial and class purification. The artificial womb, should it ever arrive, would present an unprecedented tool for social engineering. A government that wants fewer poor babies, fewer disabled babies, fewer babies of a disfavored ethnicity could, in principle, mandate ectogenic gestation with mandatory prenatal screening. The fact that this sounds like a dystopia does not make it impossible; it makes it urgent that we develop ethical brakes and democratic controls now, before the technology outruns our wisdom.

The lead essay ends with a powerful call for reproductive justice, and I share its desire for a world in which no one is shamed for the way they form a family. But a truly just reproductive order would treat the child not as a project of self‑expression or a strategy for social change, but as a person with her own inviolable dignity. That dignity is not respected when she is selected, optimized, or treated as the answer to a longing for a “mixture of us two in human form.” It is respected when she is welcomed as a gift, unanticipated and ungovernable. That language—gift, welcome, mystery—is the language of many religious and humanistic traditions, and it is not simply a mask for patriarchy. It is a reminder that there is more to reproduction than our wills and our technologies. Perhaps the greatest assistance we can give to one another is the assistance to accept our limits, to love the children who actually arrive, and to build a society that cares for them once they are here.

Vanilla MiniMax

"While acknowledging that all reproduction is assisted is a valuable conceptual shift, this recognition alone is insufficient to achieve reproductive justice because it fails to dismantle the material, economic, and legal structures that distribute reproductive technologies unequally."

Emre's essay makes a compelling case for recognizing the inherent technicity of all reproduction, but I want to push back on what seems to be its underlying optimism: that once we acknowledge "all reproduction is assisted," we can begin to close the gap between women of different classes, races, and sexes. The problem is that universalizing assistance doesn't dissolve structural inequality—it may simply make it more legible without changing the fundamental distribution of power.

Consider how Emre's own case studies expose this tension. S, the tech worker with fully covered egg freezing, and B, the lecturer financing IVF through small loans from friends, both undergo "assisted reproduction," yet their experiences are shaped by radically different material conditions. The technology may be the same, but the politics couldn't be more different. Emre gestures toward this problem when she notes that B's brother and sister-in-law were "fully covered by their insurance" while B had to borrow money, but she doesn't fully reckon with what this asymmetry means for her argument. If "assistance" is distributed along class lines, then universalizing the concept doesn't liberate anyone—it just reveals the contours of their captivity more clearly.

This is where I think Emre's analysis needs to go further. She quotes Dorothy Roberts on reproductive justice—a framework that encompasses not just the right not to have children but the right to have them in safe and supportive environments—and suggests that acknowledging the positive right to reproduce "could begin to close the gap" between women. But closing that gap requires more than changing how we talk about reproduction; it requires dismantling the economic and legal structures that make some reproductive choices possible and others merely aspirational. Universal healthcare, paid parental leave, robust protections for LGBTQ+ families—these aren't natural extensions of recognizing that all reproduction is assisted. They're political demands that require organizing against interests that benefit from keeping reproduction unequal.

Emre's narrative approach is effective at conveying the experiential texture of assisted reproduction, but it also carries risks. By presenting multiple individual stories, the essay can inadvertently suggest that the problem is one of visibility or attitude rather than structure. The solution might seem to be more stories, more recognition, more acknowledgment of the "unruliness" of bodies. But the women in Emre's essay aren't struggling because their experiences are invisible; they're struggling because they lack resources, legal protections, and time. S can freeze her eggs, but she still has to choose between career advancement and parenthood on someone else's timeline. B can technically afford IVF, but only by going into debt and navigating a medical system that treats her as an unusual case. N and K can pursue IVF, but only because N's family has money to lend.

There's also something to be questioned in Emre's relationship to Firestone's techno-utopianism. She distances herself from Firestone's enthusiasm while endorsing the general project of expanding reproductive technologies as a path to liberation. But Firestone's warning about technology being justified as "lifesaving" rather than "laborsaving" remains pertinent. The history of reproductive technology is not a history of women gaining control; it's a history of medical institutions gaining control over reproduction. Egg freezing is marketed as a "perk" that benefits workers, but it also allows employers to defer questions about parental leave and workplace flexibility. IVF was developed to help "infertile couples," but it has also generated a reproductive industrial complex that profits from desperation. Surrogacy promises to liberate women from gestation, but it also creates new forms of exploitation in which poor women's bodies gestate children for wealthy intended parents.

Emre gestures toward this problem when she notes that "as was the case with many debates over reproduction, what started out as a critique of capitalism quickly became a critique of women's choices." But she doesn't adequately explain why this keeps happening or how to prevent it. The answer, I suspect, lies not in narrative but in political organization. The egg freezing benefit that S helped design wasn't won because tech workers recognized that all reproduction is assisted; it was won because an LGBT association pushed management to address discrimination that kept gay and trans employees from accessing infertility coverage. The lesson isn't that consciousness precedes politics, but that politics requires collective action on material demands.

My concern is that Emre's focus on language and narrative—on how we talk about reproduction—can inadvertently depoliticize the very structural issues she identifies. Calling conception "assisted" rather than "artificial" or "natural" is a small victory. It might change how some women feel about their choices, and that's not nothing. But it won't change the fact that B has to delay implantation to qualify for paid maternity leave, or that K was told she was "too overweight" and too mentally ill to reproduce, or that trans women of color face violence that renders their reproductive timelines contingent on survival rather than choice.

What I want from a feminist reproductive politics is more than recognition of the assisted nature of all reproduction. I want a politics that takes seriously the relationship between reproductive technologies and the structures that distribute them unequally—capitalism, racism, ableism, transphobia. Emre's essay is valuable for insisting that we think about reproduction more capaciously, but thinking isn't enough. We need a politics that can match the complexity of the situations she describes, one that doesn't mistake the acknowledgment of difference for the achievement of solidarity.

  3. 03The essay's focus on individual choice and terminology risks depoliticizing reproduction by obscuring how medical and corporate institutions use technology to maintain control over women's bodies.
