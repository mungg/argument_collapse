"The distinction between 'natural' and 'assisted' reproduction is a false moral hierarchy that obscures the reality that all reproduction relies on social, legal, and medical support systems."

The lead essay’s most important sentence is also its most disorienting: “All reproduction, even reproduction that appears ‘natural,’ is assisted.” It is disorienting because it asks those of us who have been trained to divide reproduction into two moral categories—ordinary conception and technological intervention—to see that the distinction has always been unstable. Pregnancy tests, ultrasounds, prenatal vitamins, epidurals, incubators, cesarean sections, lactation consultants, paid nannies, unpaid grandmothers, health insurance, marriage law, zoning policy, and workplace flexibility all assist reproduction. Some forms of assistance are medical; others are social, legal, and economic. Some are visible because they require syringes and clinics. Others disappear because they are wrapped in the authority of custom.

This reframing is valuable because debates about reproductive technology too often become debates about whether women are making the “right” choices. Should she freeze her eggs? Should she use IVF? Should she try “naturally” for longer? Should she adopt? Should she breastfeed? Should she have waited? Should she have hurried? The lead essay correctly refuses this tribunal. The better question is not whether individual women, queer couples, trans people, or single parents have chosen wisely. The question is why the conditions under which they choose are so punitive, unequal, expensive, and lonely.

That shift matters politically. American reproductive politics has been dominated by abortion, and for good reason: the right not to be forced to carry a pregnancy is foundational. In 2018, with Roe v. Wade under renewed threat and state legislatures passing increasingly aggressive restrictions, no feminist politics can afford to treat abortion as a side issue. But abortion rights alone cannot carry the full meaning of reproductive freedom. Women of color activists have long argued that reproductive justice must include the right to have children, the right not to have children, and the right to raise children in safe and supportive environments. The lead essay extends that insight into the domain of assisted reproduction. If a wealthy straight couple can receive sympathy for infertility but a single woman, lesbian couple, or trans woman receives suspicion, mockery, or legal uncertainty, then “choice” is a thin word for a deeply stratified reality.

The stories of S, B, N and K, and micha cárdenas show that technology does not simply solve reproductive problems. It reveals them. Egg freezing reveals the workplace’s hostility to caregiving and the class privilege embedded in “family benefits.” IVF reveals the absence of universal health care and paid leave. Queer conception reveals medical systems still organized around presumptive heterosexuality. Trans fertility preservation reveals how often doctors treat trans futures as disposable or unimaginable. Premature infant care reveals that we already rely on machines to sustain reproductive life, even while pretending that only some families are “artificial.”

This is why the essay’s critique of “the natural” is persuasive. “Natural” is not a neutral description. It is often an accusation. It means: your family form requires explanation; mine does not. It means: your child was manufactured; mine was conceived in love. It means: your desire is selfish, desperate, excessive, or politically suspect; mine is simply human. The language of nature allows the privileged to mistake their assistance for independence. A married heterosexual couple who conceives after sex may still rely on inherited wealth, employer-sponsored insurance, a safe neighborhood, a flexible supervisor, paid maternity leave, grandparents nearby, and a hospital neonatal unit. But because none of this looks like a needle entering an ovary, it is not marked as “assisted.”

Still, if all reproduction is assisted, the next question is: assisted by whom, and on what terms? The lead essay ends with a call for the political to catch up to the technological. That is exactly right, but the phrase should be taken literally. We cannot let the market be the institution that decides whose reproduction is supported. Employer-based fertility benefits are better than no benefits for the employees who receive them, and it would be cruel to dismiss what they make possible. But they also dramatize the absurdity of tying reproductive possibility to one’s job. A software engineer may receive $20,000 for egg freezing while a home health aide receives no paid sick days. A lecturer may borrow from friends for IVF while her university celebrates diversity in glossy brochures. A queer couple may spend tens of thousands of dollars to establish a family that law and medicine should have recognized from the beginning.

A feminist reproductive strategy should therefore begin with universal public supports rather than boutique corporate benefits. Universal health care should include fertility care without requiring a heterosexual infertility diagnosis. Insurance coverage should be based on reproductive need, not marital status, sexual orientation, or proof of failed intercourse. Paid parental leave should be guaranteed nationally and should apply to birth, adoption, foster placement, and non-gestational parents. Clinics should be trained and regulated so that queer and trans patients are not treated as anomalies or instructional opportunities. Parentage law should protect intended parents in same-sex and trans families without forcing them through humiliating second-parent adoptions when their children are already theirs in every meaningful sense. Fertility preservation should be offered to trans people before hormone therapy or surgery, not as an afterthought and not with the assumption that they will never want children.

But public support also requires public honesty about risk. To defend assisted reproduction is not to pretend that every technology is liberating. Egg freezing is not a guarantee. IVF can be physically punishing and emotionally devastating. Genetic screening can slide into fantasies of control. Surrogacy can be exploitative when poverty supplies the wombs of the wealthy. The lead essay is at its best when it refuses both technophobia and techno-utopianism. The women it describes are not dupes of capitalism, but neither are they sovereign consumers choosing freely in a frictionless marketplace. They are people improvising under constraint.

That middle position is politically difficult because American public debate prefers cleaner moral categories. Conservatives warn that technology severs reproduction from nature, family, and God. Some feminists warn that technology extends medical patriarchy and capitalist discipline into the body. Libertarians treat technology as consumer freedom. The lead essay asks us to inhabit a less comfortable truth: reproductive technologies can be coercive and emancipatory at once. Egg freezing can be a corporate tool for delaying motherhood and a queer-inclusive benefit won through employee pressure. IVF can expose women to pain, debt, and medical arrogance while also allowing a single person or lesbian couple to become a parent. Sperm banking can intensify dysphoria for a trans woman while preserving a future she was told she could not have.

If all reproduction is assisted, then reproductive politics should not be organized around preserving a mythical natural realm from contamination. It should be organized around justice: fair access, bodily autonomy, informed consent, legal recognition, medical respect, economic support, and the freedom to refuse. The goal is not to make everyone use reproductive technology. It is to remove the hierarchy that makes some reproductive paths seem authentic and others suspect.

The lead essay’s final hope is solidarity across difference. That solidarity cannot come from pretending that all reproductive experiences are the same. They are not. Race, class, disability, sexuality, gender identity, immigration status, and geography shape reproductive life profoundly. But solidarity can begin from the recognition that no one reproduces alone. Every child arrives through networks of bodies, institutions, money, law, medicine, labor, and care. The question is whether those networks will remain hidden privileges for some and punishing obstacles for others—or whether we will build them openly, democratically, and generously.

"While all reproduction is assisted by social and medical systems, a feminist reproductive politics must move beyond simply embracing technology to critically examining the power structures, market forces, and inequalities that govern who controls and pays for that assistance."

Merve Emre is right that “natural” reproduction is neither natural nor autonomous. It is scaffolded by pregnancy tests, sonograms, epidurals, antibiotics, neonatal intensive care units, insurance codes, grandparents, paid and unpaid leave, immigration status, marriage law, and the willingness of other people to recognize one’s family as a family. The fiction that some people simply reproduce while others require “assistance” is a political fiction, and it does real harm. It ranks families by presumed authenticity; it makes queer parents, single parents, disabled parents, trans parents, infertile parents, and adoptive parents explain themselves; it turns the desire for a child into evidence of either selfishness or pathology when that desire does not travel through heterosexual intercourse.

But I hesitate at the suggestion that assisted reproduction should become “the preferred strategy” of a feminist reproductive politics. If all reproduction is assisted, then the central feminist question is not whether we should embrace assistance. We already do. The question is: which forms of assistance, controlled by whom, paid for by whom, and at whose risk?

That question matters because “assistance” is not innocent. It can mean a friend driving you home from an egg retrieval. It can mean a state Medicaid program paying for prenatal care. It can mean a paid doula, a lactation consultant, a union contract, a grandmother’s unpaid labor, or a safe apartment with clean water. It can also mean a clinic selling hope at prices most people cannot afford, a resident learning on a patient’s body without adequate consent, an employer offering egg freezing while leaving workplace timelines intact, or a surrogacy arrangement that transfers bodily risk down class and national hierarchies. To say that all reproduction is assisted should widen our field of vision beyond the clinic, not make the clinic the privileged site of feminist imagination.

The stories Emre tells make this plain. S’s egg freezing is not simply a triumph of technology over biology. It is also a story about a workplace that can absorb the cost of preserving reproductive possibility better than it can imagine reorganizing labor so that workers may have children without penalty. B’s IVF is not simply a story about individual choice. It is a story about debt, contingent academic employment, the absence of universal paid leave, and the peculiar cruelty of a medical system in which one can produce embryos before one can secure the conditions to raise a child. N and K’s experience shows that access to reproductive medicine does not by itself undo heterosexism; the clinic can reproduce the very norms it appears to bypass. The doctor who asks for a “sample” from an imaginary husband is not an incidental comic detail. He is part of the technology.

This is why the opposition between “nature” and “technology” can mislead even when we reject the natural. Reproductive technologies are not merely tools that liberate us from biological destiny. They are institutions, markets, protocols, habits of speech, training regimes, liability calculations, and cultural fantasies. They arrive already shaped by race, class, disability, gender, and nation. A feminist politics cannot be simply pro-technology, any more than it can be pro-nature. It must be pro-accountability.

Consider IVF. It has enabled many people to have children who otherwise could not, and any feminist account that treats this desire with contempt is morally unserious. Yet IVF also dramatizes inequality. In the United States, access depends heavily on geography, insurance, employer, marital status, diagnosis, and wealth. The procedures are physically demanding, emotionally consuming, and uncertain. Success rates vary sharply by age and medical history. Patients often pay not only for retrievals and transfers, but also for medications, genetic testing, storage, donor gametes, legal agreements, and time away from work. Meanwhile, the labor of egg donors and gestational surrogates is often discussed in the language of generosity while being organized through markets that depend on unequal bargaining power. Feminists should defend the legitimacy of assisted reproduction without romanticizing the industries that provide it.

The same is true of artificial wombs, whose history Emre deftly excavates. Ectogenesis has long carried both emancipatory and coercive possibilities. It might reduce the dangers and burdens of pregnancy; it might help extremely premature infants survive; it might expand the ways people form families. But in a country where abortion rights are already fragile, the fantasy of fetal transfer could also become a weapon. If gestation can be imagined as separable from the pregnant person, anti-abortion arguments may shift from “you must remain pregnant” to “you must submit to extraction.” The burden would not disappear; it would change form. A technology that appears to free women from pregnancy could, under conditions of reproductive unfreedom, strengthen the state’s claim over pregnant bodies.

This is not an argument against such technologies. It is an argument against assuming that technological capacity generates political freedom. The history of reproduction in the United States gives us little reason for that confidence. Women of color, poor women, disabled women, incarcerated women, and Indigenous women have been subjected to sterilization abuse, coerced contraception, child removal, punitive welfare policy, and criminal prosecution during pregnancy. At the same time, many have been denied infertility care, prenatal care, safe housing, and the resources necessary to raise children. Dorothy Roberts’s reproductive justice framework is indispensable precisely because it refuses to isolate the right not to have a child from the right to have one and to parent in safety. That framework should also keep us from treating access to IVF or egg freezing as the horizon of reproductive equality.

The “natural” has indeed done ideological damage. It has made pain virtuous, mothers self-sacrificing, breastfeeding compulsory, vaginal birth morally superior, genetic connection mystical, and heterosexual reproduction ordinary in ways that render everyone else derivative. Yet there are reasons some people reach for the language of nature that are not merely reactionary. For some women, especially those who have been overmedicalized, experimented on, dismissed, or harmed by doctors, “natural” can name a demand for bodily autonomy against institutional arrogance. For some Black women facing stark maternal health disparities, the desire for midwives, doulas, and community birth workers may reflect not consumerist Edenism but a rational distrust of hospitals that do not listen to them. For some disabled people, resistance to genetic screening may arise from the knowledge that medicine often confuses disability with a life not worth living.

The task, then, is not to replace reverence for nature with reverence for technology. It is to make reproduction less governed by reverence altogether. People should not have to prove that their reproductive choices are natural, radical, efficient, selfless, or politically exemplary. They should not have to turn their embryos, pregnancies, abortions, miscarriages, adoptions, transitions, or childlessness into public pedagogy. A lesbian couple should not have to reproduce queerly enough. A trans woman should not have to renounce genetic parenthood to validate her womanhood. A single woman should not have to present IVF as either empowerment or tragedy. A woman who wants an epidural, a scheduled cesarean, donor sperm, no children, six children, or a child genetically related to her should not be conscripted into someone else’s symbolism.

What would better assistance look like? Universal health care that includes contraception, abortion, prenatal care, childbirth, infertility treatment, gender-affirming care, and mental health care. Paid parental leave not tied to a particular employer’s generosity. Childcare treated as infrastructure. Legal recognition of diverse families without forcing parents through expensive, humiliating procedures. Strong protections against pregnancy discrimination and anti-LGBT discrimination. Ethical oversight of fertility clinics, egg donation, embryo storage, and surrogacy. Serious attention to maternal mortality, especially the disproportionate deaths of Black women. Support for disabled parents. A foster and adoption system that does not treat poverty as neglect or biology as destiny. These are not glamorous technologies, but they are reproductive technologies in the broadest sense: social arrangements that make life possible.

Emre’s most powerful claim is that reproduction exposes the insufficiency of choice as a political ideal. The people in her essay make choices constantly, but rarely under conditions they would have chosen. They choose between debt and delay, between bodily risk and lost time, between legal vulnerability and medical alienation, between disclosure and loneliness. Feminism should defend their choices, but it should not stop there. It should change the conditions under which those choices become necessary.

All reproduction is assisted. The next step is to insist that assistance be collective, equitable, and noncoercive. Not because technology will save us from nature, and not because nature will save us from technology, but because no one creates or sustains life alone.

"While the lead essay correctly critiques the class and racial privilege inherent in the language of 'natural' reproduction, a truly inclusive reproductive justice framework must expand its focus beyond clinical technologies to address the systemic racial and economic inequalities that govern who is supported in parenting and who is punished for it."

The lead essay begins with artificial wombs and ends with reproductive justice, but its most important political move is to expose the whiteness and class privilege hidden inside the language of “natural” reproduction. The internet world of water births, superfoods, fertility apps, prenatal supplements, and ecstatic breastfeeding videos is not simply silly. It is a consumer culture built on selective innocence. It allows affluent women to imagine reproduction as a return to Eden while other women struggle to survive pregnancy, avoid sterilization, keep custody of their children, or raise them without state violence.

Still, the essay’s own stories largely unfold among people with access to universities, biotech companies, fertility clinics, online communities, and literary or artistic networks. Their struggles are real, and the essay treats them with care. But if feminism needs better reproductive strategies, we must widen the frame even further. In the United States, reproductive assistance has always been distributed through race. Some women are assisted in having children; others are assisted in not having them, or prevented from raising them, or punished for having them at all.

Dorothy Roberts’s work, invoked near the end of the essay, is indispensable here. In Killing the Black Body, Roberts showed that American reproductive politics cannot be understood only through the lens of abortion choice. Black women have faced forced breeding under slavery, coercive sterilization, welfare family caps, punitive child welfare systems, criminalization during pregnancy, and medical neglect. Puerto Rican women were subjected to widespread sterilization. Native women were sterilized by Indian Health Service doctors in the 1970s. Disabled women and incarcerated women have been denied reproductive autonomy. These histories are not ancient. They shape how communities encounter medicine now.

This history complicates any simple celebration of assisted reproductive technology. IVF and egg freezing expand possibilities for some, but reproductive injustice for many women of color is not primarily lack of access to high-tech conception. It is lack of access to safe pregnancy and parenting. Black women in the United States die from pregnancy-related causes at far higher rates than white women. This disparity cuts across income and education, which means it cannot be explained away by poverty alone. Racism in medical treatment, chronic stress, unequal hospitals, and dismissal of Black women’s pain all matter. A reproductive politics that funds egg freezing while failing to keep Black mothers alive would be obscene.

The same is true of environmental racism. The right to have children and raise them in safe environments means little in neighborhoods with lead-contaminated water, toxic air, substandard housing, and underfunded schools. Flint is a reproductive justice issue. Asthma clusters are reproductive justice issues. Police violence is a reproductive justice issue. Deportation is a reproductive justice issue. So is the separation of migrant children from their parents at the border, a cruelty unfolding in our political present. A state that tears children from their families cannot be trusted to define reproductive morality.

The lead essay’s claim that all reproduction is assisted should therefore be read in two directions. Affluent reproduction is assisted by invisible supports: good insurance, safe hospitals, inherited wealth, flexible work, clean neighborhoods, legal recognition, and the presumption of fitness. Poor women’s reproduction is also assisted, but often by systems that surveil and constrain: Medicaid rules, welfare offices, child protective services, immigration enforcement, public hospitals, drug testing, prisons, and courts. Assistance can be benevolent or punitive. It can help a family flourish, or it can mark a family as suspect.

Take the common question directed at poor mothers: why have children if you cannot afford them? This question is rarely asked of affluent parents who rely on tax benefits, employer insurance, public schools, mortgage deductions, unpaid grandparents, and underpaid caregivers. Their dependence is normalized. Poor mothers’ dependence is moralized. When a wealthy woman uses IVF, her perseverance may be celebrated. When a poor woman has a third child, her fertility may be treated as irresponsibility. Nature is not the operative distinction; worthiness is.

This worthiness standard also appears in medical settings. The lead essay’s stories of doctors dismissing queer patients are infuriating. Similar patterns mark the care of women of color. Black women report not being believed about pain. Latina immigrants may face language barriers and fear of deportation. Native women may have limited access to obstetric care. Muslim women may face cultural ignorance. Incarcerated pregnant women may be shackled during labor or denied adequate prenatal care. These are not failures of bedside manner. They are reproductive stratification: some people are encouraged to reproduce and supported when they do; others are discouraged, neglected, or punished.

A better strategy must therefore resist the temptation to make ART the emblem of reproductive futurity. Yes, fertility care should be covered without discrimination. Yes, queer and trans people need access. Yes, egg freezing and IVF can be life-changing. But reproductive justice cannot be built around the clinic alone. It must be built around the conditions in which pregnancy, birth, and childrearing occur.

That means universal health care, but not only universal health care. It means midwifery and doula care for those who want it, especially community-based models that have shown promise in supporting women who distrust hospitals for good reasons. It means confronting implicit bias in obstetrics, while recognizing that bias training alone cannot fix unequal institutions. It means expanding Medicaid and ensuring postpartum care beyond the brief window when many complications emerge. It means paid leave for low-wage workers, not only professional workers with human resources departments. It means ending shackling and improving reproductive care in prisons. It means protecting pregnant workers from dangerous conditions. It means fighting housing segregation, environmental toxins, and school inequality.

It also means defending abortion without allowing abortion to stand in for the whole of reproductive politics. Poor women and women of color need abortion access; restrictions fall hardest on those who cannot travel, take time off work, or pay out of pocket. But they also need the material support to have children if they want them. Too often, policymakers who oppose abortion also oppose welfare, health care, childcare, sex education, contraception, and living wages. Their concern is not life but control. Feminists must not respond by narrowing our own agenda to privacy and choice. Privacy is not enough when families need public goods.

The lead essay’s critique of natural motherhood is also important for race because “natural” has never been applied equally. White middle-class women may be encouraged to experience birth as sacred and breastfeeding as empowerment. Black women have historically been denied the softness of maternal idealization: imagined as breeders under slavery, as laborers who should care for white children, as welfare queens, as irresponsible, as insufficiently feminine or excessively fertile. The same act—having a child—can be read as fulfillment for one woman and pathology for another.

We should also be cautious about global inequalities. In wealthier countries, assisted reproduction sometimes depends on reproductive labor elsewhere: surrogacy in poorer countries, egg markets shaped by economic need, adoption flows from the Global South to the Global North. A feminist politics cannot celebrate the dissolution of biological limits for privileged intended parents while ignoring the women whose bodies and children become available through global inequality. The “natural” is a myth, but so is frictionless cosmopolitan choice.

What would solidarity look like across these differences? It would not ask poor women of color to subordinate their struggles to the anxieties of affluent fertility patients. Nor would it dismiss infertility as a rich woman’s problem; many poor and working-class people experience infertility too and are denied treatment. Solidarity would insist that the same principle applies across contexts: people should be able to avoid pregnancy, pursue pregnancy, give birth safely, and raise children with dignity, without coercion or contempt.

“All reproduction is assisted” should make privilege visible. It should teach those who receive gentle assistance to recognize it as assistance, not virtue. It should teach those who receive punitive assistance to demand care instead of surveillance. And it should push feminism beyond the false choice between abortion rights and fertility access, between technology and social support, between queer family formation and racial justice. The reproductive future we need is not Edenic and not merely high-tech. It is one in which no child’s arrival depends on another family’s abandonment, no mother’s pain is dismissed because of her race, and no person’s fertility is treated as either a public menace or a private luxury.

The Global Womb

Merve Emre's essay is a careful tour through American assisted reproductive technology as it is experienced by relatively privileged American women. I admired its narrative texture and learned from its arguments. I want to register what is missing, because what is missing matters enormously to the politics the essay claims to be doing. The fertility industry the essay describes is global. The American clinic is one node in a transnational supply chain of gametes, embryos, hormones, gestational labor, and reproductive desire. Almost none of that chain appears in Emre's account.

Start with surrogacy. Commercial gestational surrogacy is, as Emre writes, increasingly available to American couples and individuals who can afford it. What she does not say is that the market for it has, over the past fifteen years, become aggressively global. India was, until its 2015–2018 regulatory closures, the largest single destination for international commercial surrogacy, with an industry estimated in the billions of dollars and clinics catering primarily to American, Israeli, and European clients. Thailand and Nepal hosted boom-and-bust markets that produced internationally notorious scandals — the Baby Gammy case in 2014, the post-earthquake evacuations from Nepal in 2015 — before regulatory crackdowns. As of 2018 the principal commercial destinations are Ukraine, Russia, Georgia, Mexico, and increasingly several African countries; clinics in Cambodia and Laos have absorbed displaced demand. The pattern is consistent: surrogacy markets cluster in countries where wages are low enough that the payment is significant for the surrogate, regulation is light enough to permit commerce, and the surrounding political economy generates a population of women willing to gestate strangers' children for money.

The labor conditions of these surrogates have been documented in detail by scholars including Amrita Pande, Kalindi Vora, and Sharmila Rudrappa. Pande's ethnography of Indian surrogacy hostels describes women confined to dormitories for the duration of pregnancy, subjected to managed diets and medical regimes, separated from their own children, and paid sums that, while transformative for them, represent a tiny fraction of what the commissioning parents pay the agency. The pregnancies are routinely managed via maximum-intervention protocols — multiple embryo transfers, scheduled cesareans — driven less by maternal health than by clinic efficiency. The relationship between commissioning parents and surrogates is often structured to minimize affective bonds and to forestall any post-birth claims; surrogates are typically poor, often illiterate in the contract language, and dependent on agencies for both wages and protection.

This is reproductive labor. It is also reproductive assistance. It is, in fact, exactly the kind of "assisted reproduction" that the techno-feminist horizon Emre gestures toward would have to address. The horizon of "more access to assisted reproduction" cannot be articulated honestly without addressing the question: assistance from whom, for whom, at what cost to whom? The egg freezing, IVF, and embryo screening Emre describes do not, in most of her cases, involve another woman's gestational labor. But these technologies coexist, within the same industry, with surrogacy and gamete sourcing arrangements that do. The American clinics that serve B and S and N and K share infrastructure, regulatory environment, and personnel with the agencies that broker Ukrainian surrogates and Spanish egg donors for American clients. The boundary between domestic and international ART is porous in both directions.

Gamete markets show the same pattern at smaller scale. The American egg market is structured by sharply differentiated prices: ova from young, Ivy-educated, white or East Asian donors command premiums of tens of thousands of dollars; ova from donors of color or with less educational capital are cheaper. Sperm banks operate similarly. The pricing structure is, by any reasonable definition, eugenic — not in the strict twentieth-century state sense, but in the consumer-market sense that wealthy buyers can purchase preferred traits and poorer donors can sell their gametes at lower margins. International gamete markets are more starkly stratified. Spanish ova are widely shipped because Spain's regulatory environment permits anonymous donation and high compensation; Eastern European ova are shipped because both the supply and the cultural-cosmetic preferences of Western European and American buyers align. None of this appears in Emre's account, but all of it is part of "assisted reproduction" as it actually exists.

I raise these facts not to oppose ART as such, and not to suggest that S, B, or N and K bear personal responsibility for the global structure of the industry that served them. I raise them because Emre's argument is structured around the claim that "the natural" is the principal obstacle to reproductive justice, and that the embrace of assisted reproduction would open a path toward solidarity. From the perspective of the global supply chain, the opposite seems closer to true. The current configuration of assisted reproduction is producing some of the sharpest reproductive inequalities in human history. A woman in San Francisco can purchase the gametes of an Eastern European donor she will never meet, the gestational services of an Indian or Ukrainian surrogate she will see only on video, and the embryonic screening of a Chicago lab — and assemble the resulting child into a family her American legal system will recognize. The constituent women in the chain — donor, surrogate, sometimes egg-vendor, sometimes wet nurse — have, by design, no comparable access. They will not assemble a child by purchasing gametes from a wealthier woman. They will not engage a surrogate from a more privileged country. The flow of reproductive material and reproductive labor is one-directional, structured by global income inequality, and rationalized through contracts.

The right response is not to ban these arrangements outright. Bans, where they have been tried, have generally pushed the trade to less regulated jurisdictions with worse conditions for the women involved. The right response is the same response that other globalized labor markets demand: real regulation of working conditions, durable rights for the workers, redistribution of the gains, and ultimately the elevation of source-country wages and protections so that the choice to surrogate is a real choice and not a coerced consequence of poverty. This is a labor agenda. It is also, necessarily, a reproductive-justice agenda, because the labor in question is reproductive.

The closing rhetorical move of Emre's essay — "make assisted reproduction the preferred strategy by which we fight" — does not, in this light, sound like a politics of solidarity. It sounds like the export of a particular consumer model. A feminism that wants to be global, rather than provincially American, has to ask whose assistance is assisting whom. The women Emre interviews are working hard, and their stories matter. But so do the women who carry the pregnancies they cannot, donate the eggs they did not have time to freeze in their twenties, and provide the cheap obstetric labor that makes the global price structure work. They have not yet been invited into the conversation. They are the ones the conversation has, so far, been about.

Whose Reproductive Justice?

Merve Emre's essay is rich, careful, and humane. It treats its subjects with attention. It also, in ways that I do not think the author would deny if pressed, takes for its primary frame the reproductive worlds of relatively well-resourced women in the contemporary United States: a Bay Area biotech employee, a university lecturer with a book contract, a queer couple with insurance and family loans, an artist with the cultural and intellectual capital to make bioart out of her own gametes. These are not trivial perspectives. They are, however, a narrow slice of the actual landscape of reproductive politics, and they shape the argument in ways worth naming.

The reproductive justice framework, articulated by Black women activists in the mid-1990s — SisterSong, Loretta Ross, Dorothy Roberts, and others — was built precisely against the tendency Emre's essay tilts toward: the assumption that the central feminist battle around reproduction is the battle to expand individual choice through technology and law. RJ insisted on three linked rights: the right not to have a child, the right to have a child, and the right to parent children in safe and sustainable environments. The third right is the one most easily forgotten in mainstream feminist discourse, and it is the one whose absence most distorts the picture Emre offers.

Consider what the essay does not contain. It does not contain a Black woman whose teenage son was killed by police, raising the question of what it means to bring a Black child into a world structured by anti-Black violence. It does not contain an undocumented woman whose pregnancy was supervised by ICE, or whose child was separated from her at the border in the policy now operative as Emre writes. It does not contain an incarcerated woman shackled during labor, a practice that remains legal in most U.S. states. It does not contain a Native woman whose grandmother was sterilized without consent in an IHS hospital in the 1970s, or whose own children were removed by a state agency under the discredited but lingering logic of Indian boarding schools. It does not contain a woman whose Medicaid does not cover the medications she needs to manage a high-risk pregnancy, or who lives in a county with no obstetric care. It does not contain a woman who works two jobs, neither offering maternity leave at any wage, who is contemplating a third abortion not because she does not want children but because she cannot afford one.

The relevance of these absences is not that Emre should have written a different essay. It is that the rhetorical conclusion she draws — "make assisted reproduction the preferred strategy by which we fight, until the natural no longer looms so large" — does not, in fact, follow from the lives of most women trying to reproduce in this country. For most women, the "natural" is not the central obstacle to reproductive flourishing. The central obstacles are poverty, racism, violence, lack of healthcare, immigration enforcement, mass incarceration, and a welfare state designed to surveil and punish poor mothers, especially poor Black mothers, rather than to support them. Replacing the natural with the assisted does not address these obstacles. It is, at best, orthogonal to them.

I want to be careful here. Emre quotes Roberts approvingly at the end of her essay and signals that she understands the limits of choice-based reproductive politics. The closing paragraphs do good work. But the essay's center of gravity is elsewhere — in the close observation of cryopreservation protocols, IVF retrieval techniques, hormonal dosages, the moral phenomenology of choosing among embryos. These are intimate, important details. They are also, structurally, details of a reproductive landscape that exists only for women who have access to clinics, doctors, insurance, savings, and the cultural authority to demand that their reproductive desires be taken seriously. The injunction to make assisted reproduction the "preferred strategy" speaks within that landscape. From outside it, the injunction can sound peculiar. Preferred over what? When you cannot get a standard well-woman exam, the question of whether to undergo preimplantation genetic screening is not a live one.

There is a deeper issue, too. The reproductive-justice tradition has been suspicious not just of natalist ideology but also of the unmarked progressivism of reproductive technology. The history of contraception, sterilization, and assisted reproduction in the United States is a history of differential application: technologies that liberate some women while disciplining or harming others. The same medical infrastructure that offers IVF to professional women in San Francisco also performs disproportionate hysterectomies on poor Black women, also sterilized 1,400 women in California prisons between 1997 and 2010, also coerced consent from Mexican-American women at LA County–USC Medical Center in the 1970s. To celebrate the assisted, in this context, requires a careful accounting of who is being assisted, by whom, and what is being done in the same hospitals to women whose names rarely appear in the manifestos.

This is why I think the essay's category of "the assisted" needs to be opened up. The "assistance" Black women receive when they walk into a hospital to give birth is, often, the assistance of being ignored. Black women in the U.S. die in childbirth at three to four times the rate of white women, a disparity that has nothing to do with biology and everything to do with how their pain is read, how their warnings are dismissed, how their bodies are touched. The "assistance" that undocumented women receive in pregnancy is, increasingly, the assistance of fear: surveillance at clinics, denial of care, the implicit threat that to seek prenatal services is to risk family separation. Naming all of this as "assistance" is technically correct under Emre's framework — yes, every reproductive act takes place inside a thick mesh of institutional involvement — but it strains the political utility of the term to call medical neglect and immigration enforcement merely different modes of assistance.

A more useful framing might be: all reproduction is conditioned. The conditions vary enormously, and the political project is not primarily to choose between natural and assisted but to transform the conditions so that anyone who wants to parent can do so safely. That project requires more than the redistribution of reproductive technologies. It requires Medicare for All, full immigrant protection, prison and police abolition, real investment in Black maternal health, restoration of tribal sovereignty over Native families, paid leave universal and generous, and a welfare state that supports rather than surveils.

Emre's essay gestures toward this in its final paragraph, and she is right that the political must catch up to the technological. I would put it more strongly: the political project required is large enough that the technological story, fascinating as it is, becomes a relatively small part of it. The horizon is not the universalization of egg freezing. It is the dismantlement of the systems that make so many forms of motherhood — including, and especially, the kinds that have nothing to do with clinics — unlivable.

Merve Emre's essay is the rare piece of writing about reproductive technology that takes seriously both the intimacy and the politics of the procedures it describes. I learned from it, and I want to return the favor with a hard question. Among the technologies Emre catalogs — IUI, IVF, ICSI, oocyte cryopreservation — one is mentioned only in passing but does enormous moral work in the narrative. That is preimplantation genetic screening. B's IVF produces twenty-two eggs, eleven embryos, six PGS-tested embryos, and finally "only three turned out to be free of any chromosomal abnormalities." N and K's last retrieval yields five fertilized eggs, of which "only one was viable." This is the language of the clinic, and Emre adopts it without inflection. From a disability-studies perspective, that inflection — and its absence — is itself the story.

What does it mean for an embryo to be "viable"? Strictly, in PGS, it means screened for aneuploidies — chromosomal abnormalities, of which the best known and most commonly excluded is trisomy 21, Down syndrome. PGS is also increasingly used to screen for single-gene disorders: cystic fibrosis, Huntington's, sickle cell, BRCA mutations, sometimes deafness, sometimes dwarfism. Some clinics test for sex. Some, in countries with looser regulation, test for predispositions that shade into trait selection. The technology is improving rapidly and its use is rapidly expanding. The decisions it underwrites are decisions about what kinds of people will, and will not, come into existence.

The disability-rights critique of selective reproductive technologies is now several decades old and it has not lost its force. Marsha Saxton, Adrienne Asch, Rosemarie Garland-Thomson, Alison Kafer, and many others have argued that the routine prenatal screening regime — which now begins, in many clinics, before implantation rather than at twelve or twenty weeks — communicates a clear social message: that lives with these conditions are lives not worth bringing into being. The message is communicated not through anyone's bigotry but through the architecture of the procedure itself. The default of the testing protocol is to screen out. The default of the counseling is to recommend termination or non-implantation. The default of the funding, in many systems, is to pay for the test but not for the lifelong supports that would make a chosen disabled life livable. This is what Asch called the "expressive" problem: independent of any individual's intention, the practice expresses a social judgment.

Emre's essay does not engage this critique. It treats PGS as a kind of relief, a way of avoiding the "sudden and singular loss" of miscarriage in favor of "gradual attrition — the calculated paring down of her eggs and embryos after tests and screens." That phrase, "calculated paring down," is striking. It is presented as a tender, even feminist alternative to traumatic miscarriage. But it is also a description of selection — of deciding, in advance, which possible children will be permitted to develop. There is a politics there that deserves more than passing.

I do not raise this to moralize against IVF or against the women Emre profiles. I raise it because the essay's overall argument — that "the natural" is an ideological screen and that we should embrace "assisted reproduction [as] the preferred strategy by which we fight" — runs into real trouble at exactly this point. The fantasy of unconstrained reproductive choice that Emre rightly diagnoses includes, in its current technological form, the fantasy of choosing healthy children. And "healthy" is doing tremendous ideological work in that sentence. Healthy is a moving target. In the 1980s, the technology made it possible to screen out trisomies. In the 1990s, to screen out single-gene disorders associated with childhood death. In the 2000s, to screen for adult-onset and reduced-penetrance conditions. By 2018, increasing numbers of clinics test for traits whose disability status is contested even within the affected communities — deafness most famously, but also achondroplasia and others. Each expansion has been described, at the time, as a modest extension of existing practice. Cumulatively, they amount to a quiet eugenic infrastructure built inside the for-profit fertility industry, with little public deliberation.

The standard liberal response is that these are private choices and that the alternative — restricting them — would be coercive and patriarchal. I am sympathetic to that response in many cases. But the disability critique is not, in the main, a call for restriction. It is a call for honest social accounting. If we are building a society in which fewer and fewer children with Down syndrome are born, we should say so, and we should attend to what that means for the people with Down syndrome who do exist and the families who raise them. If the cost of being a person with cystic fibrosis is increasingly being treated as a moral problem to be solved upstream, by not bringing such people into being, then the people downstream — adults living with CF — are entitled to ask what status that confers on their lives.

Emre's essay actually contains a powerful counter to the trajectory I am describing, and she does not develop it. The high-risk obstetrician who tells K and N they "should not reproduce biologically" because of K's depression and weight is recognizable to any reader of the history of eugenics. It is the voice of medical authority telling a couple that their genetic future is not theirs to determine. K and N respond by reproducing anyway, "furious" and "motivated to act without their doctors' blessings." That is a profoundly disability-aware moment: the refusal of medical authority's right to sort who deserves to reproduce. But the same essay does not similarly trouble the moment when PGS is used, by N and K and by B, to sort embryos rather than parents. The sorting has moved one stage earlier and become, somehow, unobjectionable.

What I am asking for is not a different ending to anyone's IVF cycle. I am asking for a more honest reckoning with what the technology, taken at scale, does. The reproductive justice movement has long insisted that the right to parent disabled children with dignity is part of the right to reproduce. That insistence is in tension with the standard PGS protocol, and the tension is not resolved by calling the protocol "assisted."

The deeper philosophical point is this: when Emre argues that all reproduction is assisted and that we should make peace with the assistance, she is right at one level and missing something at another. The assistance reshapes the children who arrive. It does not merely help "the same" children be born under better conditions; it determines, in part, which children are born at all. A feminism that takes this seriously cannot simply celebrate the dethroning of the natural. It has to ask, at every stage, what is being chosen, by whom, in whose interest, with what consequences for the people the technology is now able to refuse.

By 2018, we have seen how corporations use “fertility benefits” to extend their hold on workers’ lives, encouraging delayed childbearing not as a genuine choice but as a way to maximize productive years. When the response to structural inequality is more technology rather than shorter work weeks, universal healthcare, or paid parental leave, we risk legitimizing the very conditions that make child-rearing so difficult in the first place.

The essay acknowledges these structural constraints but ultimately frames the answer as broadening access to assisted reproduction rather than questioning the model of reproduction as a private, technologically mediated endeavor. Yet the desire for a biological child, as the author notes, is not rational; it is shaped by norms that can be as coercive as the natural/artificial binary itself. The fear that a child is “not yours” without a genetic link—a fear that haunts B and drives K and N to undergo repeated transfers—is itself a residue of a culture that equates kinship with biogenetic substance. Expanding IVF coverage without challenging that equation does not dissolve the hierarchy; it merely invites more women to climb its rungs. A truly anti-naturalist politics would not simply dismantle the myth of unassisted reproduction; it would build a world in which the assistance we offer one another—whether through midwife’s hands or petri dish—is guided by solidarity, not profit, and by an expansive vision of kinship that owes nothing to the “natural” but everything to justice.

Merve Emre's essay makes a claim that is both modest and audacious: that all reproduction is assisted, and that the appearance of unassistance is itself a kind of privilege. The modest version is undeniable. Prenatal vitamins, ultrasounds, hospital deliveries, epidurals, formula, pediatricians, parental leave, kin networks — none of these is "natural" in any rigorous sense, and the women who do not need to think about them as technologies enjoy a luxury invisible to themselves. The audacious version goes further: that the line between unassisted and assisted reproduction is not a line at all, only a gradient organized by what a culture has agreed to render unmarked. On this point Emre is largely persuasive, and her case is strengthened by the fact that her four narrators — S, B, K and N, micha cárdenas — keep stumbling into versions of "the natural" even as they undergo procedures that should have inoculated them against it. The discourse is sticky precisely because it is not a lifestyle choice; it is, as Emre says, a political problem.

Still, I want to press on a few places where the slogan does too much work. To say that all reproduction is assisted is true at the level of description but risks flattening distinctions that matter for the politics Emre wants to build. There is a difference between assistance that one can refuse and assistance one cannot conceive without, between assistance an insurer will cover and assistance one finances by borrowing from friends, between the assistance offered to a married straight couple at a teaching hospital and the assistance withheld from a fat lesbian whose obstetrician thinks she should not reproduce. Emre obviously knows this — her essay is structured around such gradations. But the rhetorical move from "all reproduction is assisted" to a politics in which "assisted reproduction" becomes "the preferred strategy by which we fight" elides the question of who pays for the assistance and on what terms. The default of the natural is exclusionary, yes; but the default of the technological is, at present, a market.

This matters because the essay's most vivid set piece — S at the unnamed Silicon Valley biotech, whose $20,000 lifetime cap for elective oocyte cryopreservation was won partly through advocacy by an LGBT employee association — is described as a "happy product of Silicon Valley's marriage of capitalist competition and social justice." Emre largely defends S against Suzanne McGee's Guardian piece, and rightly: McGee's contempt landed on the women rather than the firms. But the defense leaves the structural picture uncontested. Egg freezing as a tech perk is not just covered infertility care extended to gay and trans employees; it is also a benefit whose logic dovetails with employer interests in delaying the childbearing of high-performing women. The two things can be true at once. It is about the long American history of forced sterilization, the criminalization of pregnancy among Black and poor women, the prosecution of drug-using mothers, the family-policing functions of the child welfare system, and the disproportionate maternal mortality of Black women — a figure that, by 2018, is roughly three to four times that of white women in the United States. Emre cites Roberts in her closing pages, but the four narratives that organize the essay sit at some distance from Roberts's archive. S, B, K, N, and cárdenas are not interchangeable — cárdenas in particular speaks from inside the violence Roberts describes — but the gravitational center of the essay is the professional woman in a coastal city navigating an under-insured but still navigable system. A politics that wants the technological to catch up to the political will need to be honest about whose technological catching-up is at stake.

The other place I want to push is on the persistence of the biological child as object of desire in nearly every story Emre tells. S has fifteen frozen eggs and a clear path to adoption or donor embryos and yet wonders why she can't "just have the baby." One answer, congenial to Emre, is that ideology runs deep. Another, less congenial, is that the desire for biological kinship is not reducible to ideology, and that an emancipatory politics of reproduction will have to make space for it rather than treat it as residue to be cleared away.

None of this is to argue against the essay's central move. The redescription of "natural" reproduction as merely a form of assistance whose costs are socially absorbed is exactly the kind of conceptual work feminism needs. It is to argue that the slogan, to do the political work Emre wants it to do, has to carry within it the question of who is assisted, by whom, on what terms, and at what cost. Otherwise "all reproduction is assisted" becomes a description of the present rather than a demand on the future.

When Shulamith Firestone proclaimed that artificial reproduction would liberate women, she was, the lead essay implies, unfairly maligned. Yet there was a reason so many radical feminists of her era—women like Mary Daly and many socialist feminists—recoiled from her technophilia. Their caution was not simply a kneejerk return to nature worship; it was rooted in a dark intuition about the patriarchal and capitalist armature on which reproductive technology would inevitably be hung. “All Reproduction Is Assisted” is a brilliant piece of narrative journalism that, perhaps despite itself, provides all the evidence needed for a more skeptical conclusion: as long as reproduction is embedded in capitalist social relations, technological enhancement will not liberate us but will extend the reach of commodification into the deepest recesses of human life.

Consider S, the biotech employee whose employer covers elective egg freezing. The essay describes this as a “happy product” of competition and social justice. But the happiness is precarious. S’s frozen eggs are an insurance policy, a hedge against professional risk. The company’s message is clear: we will help you postpone fertility so that you can dedicate your most productive years to our quarterly earnings reports. This is not reproductive justice; it is workplace optimization dressed in the language of choice. The same corporate logic that once offered free pizza to keep coders coding all night now offers egg freezing to keep women’s career trajectories uninterrupted. The technology does not loosen the demands of the workplace; it simply shifts the burden onto a different set of bodily practices—daily injections, transvaginal ultrasounds, and the emotional labor of managing the possibility of failure. S’s sigh that the eggs “should be liberating” is the lament of a worker who has been sold a product that doesn’t deliver on its packaging.

The essay’s other stories deepen this critique. B finances her IVF through small loans from friends, a form of informal debt peonage that mirrors the larger student‑loan and medical‑debt crises plaguing younger generations. K and N undergo treatment while N’s brother and sister‑in‑law, presumably heterosexual and more conventionally insured, enjoy full coverage. The state, through insurance mandates that require a diagnosis of infertility defined by intercourse, structures the flow of resources along heteronormative lines, leaving queer people to pay out of pocket or to beg from family. The market for sperm banks, genetic screening, and storage generates profits for a growing biomedical industry, while the emotional costs are borne by individuals. In every account, the reproductive technology is embedded in circuits of money, debt, and differential access. The technology doesn’t float free of capitalism; it is capitalism made flesh.

What the essay celebrates as “assistance” can, from another angle, look like the progressive deskilling and alienation of reproductive work. Firestone’s liberatory vision drew on Marx’s analysis of the division of labor: just as industrial machinery de‑skilled artisanal production, artificial wombs would de‑skill gestation. But Marx also taught us that under capitalism, the machine is not owned by the worker; it is used by the capitalist to extract surplus value and discipline labor. An artificial womb owned by a corporation or a state would not necessarily be a tool of women’s liberation; it could become a site of new forms of exploitation—embryo factories, gestational piecework, a market for commodified wombs that mimics the market for everything else under neoliberalism. The drive to externalize gestation is, in other words, entirely compatible with the further marketization of women’s bodies.

This is not to say that we must reject reproductive technology tout court. A socialist‑feminist politics would embrace technologies of assistance only to the extent that they are yoked to a broader transformation of social reproduction. That means decommodifying care entirely: making child‑rearing a social responsibility, funded by progressive taxation, with robust public services and a guaranteed basic income that makes paid work optional rather than compulsory. In such a context, egg freezing might be a genuine expansion of freedom rather than a career‑management tool. But as long as we live in a world where your access to food, shelter, and healthcare depends on your employment status, reproductive technology will be captured by the employer, the insurer, and the clinic’s bottom line.

The lead essay’s own ending points toward this larger vision when it invokes Dorothy Roberts’s reproductive justice framework: “not just a woman’s right not to have a child, but her right to have children and raise them with dignity in safe, healthy, and supportive environments.” That’s a fine sentence, but it requires a material foundation that the essay’s narratives never quite confront. Raising children with dignity demands more than a successful embryo transfer. It requires a living wage, affordable housing, a non‑discriminatory education system, and a planet that isn’t on fire. Reproductive technology, no matter how sophisticated, cannot deliver any of these things. By focusing so intently on the moment of conception and gestation, the essay risks replicating the very narrowness it seeks to correct: it expands the frame from natural to assisted, but it still keeps the frame trained on the production of the baby rather than on the lifelong labor of care.

Perhaps the most honest moment in the essay is when the author admits, “It is not a question that has a rational answer. Like sexual desire, reproductive desire seems fundamentally irrational.” There is profound political humility in that admission. It suggests that the longing for a child cannot be fully analyzed in terms of interests or rights; it belongs to the realm of the non‑rational, the passionate, the existential. Capitalism has a way of colonizing even that realm, turning it into a market for aspirational consumption. A feminism that wants to be more than a cheerleader for biotechnology must insist that some goods should never be produced as commodities. Caring for the next generation is one of them. The appropriate response to the infertility that the lead essay so vividly documents is not to make ART cheaper or more universally available within the existing market framework. It is to build a society that values care work so highly that the question “Why would you put yourself through this?” is met not with individual anguish but with an answer that is social, collective, and finally unalienated: because the community we live in will embrace whatever child arrives, however she arrives, and will sustain both of you for the whole of your lives.

Emre mentions in passing the 1962 case of Sherri Finkbine, who sought an abortion after learning her child would be born with severe deformities caused by thalidomide, framing this as a tragic restriction of choice. But the essay lacks a disability justice lens to complicate this narrative.

From a disability rights perspective, the routine discarding of embryos with chromosomal variations sends a chilling message about who is deemed fit to exist in our society. The problem with the "natural" is not just that it excludes queer people or those with fertility struggles, as Emre argues; the problem with the medicalized alternative is that it actively seeks to engineer disability out of existence.

When reproduction is moved into the laboratory, the child transforms from a received gift into a curated product. Emre’s subjects speak the language of optimization. S’s doctors monitor her follicles to "retrieve the maximum number of eggs," operating under the imperative that "optimization is key." B wants "gradual attrition—the calculated paring down of her eggs and embryos after tests and screens—not sudden and singular loss." This desire to control the uncontrollable, to mitigate risk through genetic selection, is understandable on an individual level. No one desires suffering for their child. But on a systemic level, this drive toward genetic perfection fundamentally alters the social contract of unconditional care that ideally underpins parenting.

As the philosopher Michael Sandel has argued, the drive for genetic enhancement and selection threatens our "openness to the unbidden." When we meticulously screen and select our embryos, we risk adopting an attitude of mastery over life, viewing children as objects of our design rather than autonomous beings we are tasked to welcome and love regardless of their traits. If parents have invested tens of thousands of dollars and endured immense physical pain to produce a "perfect" embryo, what happens when that child eventually develops a disability, a mental illness, or simply fails to live up to the parental fantasy?

Furthermore, this technological selection reinforces structural inequality. Because ART is so expensive, it allows the wealthy to purchase biological "insurance policies" (as S calls her frozen eggs) and genetically screen their offspring, potentially leading to a bifurcated society where genetic disability becomes increasingly associated with poverty. Emre hopes that making ART widely available could "close the gap between women of different classes, races, and sexes." But universal access to eugenic technologies does not solve the moral problem of eugenics itself.

A truly liberatory reproductive politics must grapple with the fact that human biology is messy, vulnerable, and prone to "defects." While we should absolutely support the rights of queer, trans, and single people to form families, we cannot ignore the ableist foundations of the tools they are forced to use. If "all reproduction is assisted," we must ask what ideologies are embedded in that assistance. We need a reproductive justice framework that doesn't just demand equal access to the IVF clinic, but one that fiercely defends the value of the disabled lives that the IVF clinic is currently designed to eliminate.

"While acknowledging that all reproduction is assisted is a vital paradigm shift, relying on assisted reproductive technologies (ART) as a primary engine for liberation is dangerous because these technologies are currently embedded in a neoliberal, profit-driven healthcare system that prioritizes corporate optimization over genuine reproductive justice."

The lead essay brilliantly dismantles the oppressive ideology of the “natural” in our cultural discourse surrounding reproduction. By tracing the genealogy of the artificial womb from early science fiction to the techno-utopian visions of Shulamith Firestone, and by grounding these theories in the lived realities of individuals navigating fertility clinics today, the essay exposes how the rhetoric of “natural” childbirth and conception serves as a cudgel. It operates as an exclusionary, consumerist logic that obscures the profound physical, emotional, and financial burdens of creating a family. The conclusion drawn—that all reproduction is, in fact, assisted, and that recognizing this could foster solidarity across lines of race, class, sexuality, and gender identity—is a vital paradigm shift.

However, while the essay eloquently maps the structural inequalities that shape access to assisted reproductive technology (ART), it places too much faith in the emancipatory potential of the technologies themselves. The author suggests that acknowledging the positive right to reproduce could make assisted reproduction “the preferred strategy by which we fight” for reproductive justice. But as long as these technologies remain deeply embedded in a neoliberal, profit-driven healthcare system, relying on them as a primary engine for political liberation risks generating new hierarchies rather than dissolving old ones. We must fiercely interrogate not only the biological limits of reproduction, but the economic architecture of the industry that purports to assist it.

This tension is most glaring in the essay’s treatment of S, the biotech employee whose egg freezing is subsidized by her Silicon Valley employer. S views her frozen eggs as a “happy product of Silicon Valley’s marriage of capitalist competition and social justice,” a triumph pushed forward by her company’s LGBT association. While we must celebrate the material benefit this provides to queer employees who have historically been denied care, we cannot ignore the broader, more insidious motives of corporate-sponsored fertility preservation. In 2018, the corporate drive to fund egg freezing is less an act of feminist liberation and more a strategy for extracting maximum labor during a worker’s peak productive years.

By offering elective oocyte cryopreservation, tech giants are not dismantling the structural antagonism between work and family; they are merely privatizing its solution. They shift the burden of reconciling career advancement and motherhood onto the individual’s biological timeline, medicalizing what is fundamentally a social failure. Instead of instituting robust paid family leave, shortening the workweek, or providing on-site childcare—policies that demand structural accommodation for parents—corporations offer a technological workaround that keeps employees tethered to their desks. If we embrace this as a victory, we risk conflating corporate optimization with reproductive freedom.

Furthermore, expanding access to ART without fundamentally altering the market logic of the American healthcare system ignores how deeply commodified human reproduction has become. The fertility industry is currently a multi-billion-dollar enterprise characterized by aggressive marketing and stark stratification. If, as the essay suggests, we are to view all reproduction as assisted, we must ask who profits from this assistance and whose bodies bear its hidden costs. The push for technological intervention often relies on a shadowy gig economy of reproduction. Egg donors are recruited on college campuses and paid premiums for specific racial, physical, or educational traits, turning genetic material into a luxury commodity. Surrogates, both domestic and international, contract their bodily labor to gestate children for those who can afford their fees. Simply universalizing access to the clinic without dismantling the capitalist logic that underpins it risks treating human reproduction as just another site of resource extraction, where the bodies of lower-income women are leveraged to assist the family-building of the wealthy.

The essay also glosses over the ways in which the patriarchal dynamics of the “natural” birthing suite have simply been transferred to the fertility clinic. Firestone’s dream of the artificial womb sought to free women from the physical burdens of gestation. But as the narratives of B, N, and K reveal, engaging with ART is an intensely physical, often agonizing experience. From exhaustive regimens of hormone injections to the terrifying risks of ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome, the burden on the female body is not erased by technology; it is merely heavily medicalized.

The medical establishment, as these stories show, is still rife with the same dismissiveness and normative assumptions that plague traditional reproductive care. The male doctors who ignored B’s retroverted uterus, or who persistently assumed N and K were just friends rather than partners, demonstrate that clinical spaces are deeply saturated with heteronormative and patriarchal biases. Substituting the tyranny of “nature” with the tyranny of a profit-driven medical establishment is not true liberation. It merely changes the architects of women’s alienation from their own bodies.

Dorothy Roberts’s framework of reproductive justice, cited at the end of the lead essay, offers the correct path forward, but we must remember that Roberts’s vision extends far beyond the clinic. Reproductive justice is not inherently techno-utopian; it is fundamentally about the social conditions of parenting. Safe housing, clean water, freedom from state violence, and a non-discriminatory healthcare system are its bedrock. If we elevate assisted reproduction to our “preferred strategy,” we risk focusing on the high-tech creation of children while neglecting the low-tech, everyday structural supports required to keep them alive and thriving. micha cárdenas’s fear of dying before she can raise a child is a profound reminder of this reality. The technology to create a child matters little if the state refuses to protect the parent’s right to survive.

To acknowledge that all reproduction is socially and medically assisted is an essential step in demystifying the biological family. It validates the experiences of queer, trans, and infertile people, and opens the door to expansive, inclusive kinship structures. But we must be careful not to conflate technological intervention with political emancipation. We are writing in a moment where venture capital is flooding the fertility market and digital apps monetize our menstrual data. Our demand must not merely be for equal access to the fertility clinic. True solidarity requires demanding the complete overhaul of the socio-economic system that makes building and sustaining a family a luxury in the first place.
