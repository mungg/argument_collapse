"While the Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) framework provides a vital evidence-based alternative to neoliberalism, its logic should be extended beyond mere policy corrections toward a radical, democratic restructuring of the economic system."

I write this response in a spirit of radical agreement. Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman have provided an intellectual charter for the policy revolution that a growing number of economists, and the public, crave. Their argument that economics, far from being an apology for the status quo, contains the seeds of a fairer and more prosperous society is not merely defensive—it is an invitation to reclaim the discipline’s best traditions. The policy menu they present is at once bold and evidence-based, offering concrete pathways to reduce inequality without sacrificing growth. This is precisely the kind of work that can give progressive politicians the confidence to reject the false choice between “competence” and “compassion.”

Yet I believe the essay underestimates its own radical potential. It frames its proposals as correctives to market failures, as “free lunches” that move us closer to the justice-efficiency frontier. This framing is strategically brilliant because it appeals to efficiency-minded moderates. But it also limits the horizon of imagination. The authors hint at deeper possibilities: they mention “full-blown visions of post-capitalist societies” and note that institutional diversity is “not limited to existing practices.” I want to take that hint seriously.

What if we treated the EfIP proposals not as endpoints but as the first steps toward a genuinely different economic system? Wage boards, for instance, are a way to de-commodify labor, to take the determination of wages partially out of the market and embed it in democratic negotiation. That principle could be extended to other prices: could we have boards that set caps on executive pay, limits on rent increases, or floors on resource prices to account for ecological costs? Once we admit that prices are political constructs, not natural facts, the entire edifice of market-based allocation becomes open to democratic contestation. The authors seem reluctant to follow their own logic this far, perhaps for fear of alarming the mainstream.

Similarly, the proposal to tax multinationals based on sales points toward a broader principle: that capital’s profits are not solely the product of individual effort or risk-taking, but are collectively generated by the social infrastructure of education, law, and public goods. If that is so, then the entire return to capital is, in a sense, a social dividend that can be reclaimed. We might move from taxing profits to socializing ownership—through sovereign wealth funds, cooperative enterprises, or expanded public ownership of essential sectors. The authors’ own acknowledgment that property rights are institutionally contingent opens the door to such ideas, but they do not walk through it.

The essay’s greatest service may be to create a safe space where economists can propose reforms that were, until recently, beyond the pale. But the next generation of inclusive prosperity thinkers should feel empowered to go further. We need not just a set of policy tweaks but a new political economy—one that embraces the possibility of democratizing not just the outcomes of markets but the governance of production itself. The authors have given us a foundation; let us build on it a house that is genuinely post-capitalist, not just post-neoliberal.

  2. 02The principle of taxing collectively generated profits suggests that the returns to capital should be treated as a social dividend, justifying a shift toward socialized ownership and cooperative enterprises.
  3. 03Economists should move beyond fixing market failures to embrace a political economy that democratizes the governance of production itself.
  4. 04The EfIP framework serves as a necessary foundation that allows the next generation of thinkers to imagine genuinely post-capitalist institutional arrangements.
