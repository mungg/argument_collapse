Diversified Claude

"While the case for amnesty based on social membership is correct, it is a limited compromise that fails to challenge the fundamentally unjust premise that states have a moral right to exclude migrants based on the accident of birth."

I read Joseph Carens with the strange feeling of a person who has been arguing for the same position for years and now finds it stated more elegantly than he could state it. The case for amnesty based on the passage of time and the fact of membership is, I think, exactly right. My quarrel with Carens is that he stops too soon.

Carens accepts, "as a premise," that "the state has the right to determine whom it will admit and to apprehend and deport migrants who settle without official authorization." He treats this concession as a methodological move: granting the premise of his opponents in order to show that even from that premise the conclusion he wants follows. Fair enough as a rhetorical strategy. But this premise is doing more work than Carens admits, and I think the work it is doing is bad.

What is the basis of the state's right to exclude? Carens has written elsewhere about this question, and his earlier work pointed toward a much more radical position — that border controls are presumptively unjust, that the freedom of movement that we now confer on citizens within a national territory ought to extend across borders, and that current restrictions are the moral equivalent of feudal privilege. In the present essay he has retreated, accepting the conventional view in order to build a narrower argument. The narrower argument is correct, but the broader argument was more correct.

Consider what we are saying when we say that the United States has a "right" to exclude Miguel Sanchez. We are saying that the accident of his birth in Mexico generates a permanent moral disability — that his country of origin, which he did not choose, gives Americans, who also did not choose theirs, the right to keep him out and condemn him to the wages and political instability of his birthplace. This is, in its formal structure, identical to the principle of hereditary aristocracy. Some are born to privilege; others are not; and the privileged may use force to protect their privilege. Liberal democrats are supposed to reject this principle. We have rejected it as applied to race, sex, religion, parentage. We have not rejected it as applied to nationality. The reason we have not rejected it is not that there is a principled difference but that the consequences of rejecting it would be politically disruptive.

Carens' membership argument, beautiful as it is, depends on a premise that he rejected in his earlier work and that I think we should still reject. If the case for amnesty rests on the migrant's having become a member, what about the migrant who has just arrived? On Carens' framing, she can be deported. The state may apprehend her at the border, send her back to whatever life she fled. The accident of birth, again, determines her fate. The only thing that has changed is the date.

I want to defend amnesty for the long-resident, and I agree with everything Carens says about Miguel Sanchez and Marguerite Grimmond. But I want to be honest about the rest of the picture. The new arrival has the same human interest in a better life that the long-resident has. The reason we feel differently about them is not that her claim is morally weaker but that we have not yet had to see her face. Distance and unfamiliarity do the work that explicit racism used to do. We exclude in advance the people whose individual stories would force us to admit them.

The deeper case for amnesty, then, is that exclusion is generally indefensible and that the long-resident is just the case where indefensibility has become impossible to ignore. Carens hints at this when he writes that "we find it hard to expel them when they are discovered." Yes — and we should ask why we find it hard, and whether the reasons that make it hard in their case do not apply, less visibly, in the case of the migrant we never met.

There is a related worry about Carens' framework. By making time the central criterion, it implicitly endorses the idea that the recent migrant has weaker claims, which gives political cover to harsh enforcement against recent arrivals. If amnesty is restricted to those who have hidden successfully for five or seven years, the enforcement apparatus turns with full force on the new arrival. Detention centers expand. Border deaths multiply. We tell ourselves that this is acceptable because we have a humane policy toward the long-settled. This is the European compromise that has produced both regularization programs and Frontex; both the Catholic compassion of the Italian sanatoria and the drowned bodies off Lampedusa. The price of legalizing the long-settled is, apparently, militarizing the border against everyone else. Carens does not endorse this trade-off, but his argument is consistent with it.

What would a more honest position look like? It would say: settled migrants should be legalized, because everything Carens says is true. It would also say: new arrivals should be admitted in vastly larger numbers, through expanded legal channels, because the right to leave one's country is empty without a corresponding right to enter another. It would say that enforcement should be radically reduced, not because the state has no right to control its borders but because the right is far weaker than we ordinarily assume and the human costs of exercising it are catastrophic. It would say that the long-term goal is a world in which the kind of border-crossing that Miguel Sanchez engaged in is not punished at all, because it is recognized as the ordinary exercise of a basic human freedom.

I write all this as someone who would happily settle for what Carens proposes, today, this year, as a policy outcome. We are very far from the world I have described. We are even far from the world Carens has described. Amnesty on his terms would be an immense moral victory and would relieve immense suffering. I would vote for it tomorrow.

But I do not want us to forget that Carens' argument is a compromise — a compromise he has explicitly made with the assumptions of his opponents in order to win them over. The argument it conceals is more radical and, I think, more true. The borders themselves are the deeper problem. Amnesty is the beginning of the answer, not its end.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The state's right to exclude is morally equivalent to hereditary aristocracy, as it uses the unchosen circumstance of nationality to protect the privilege of those born in wealthy nations.
  2. 02Focusing exclusively on the passage of time as a moral claim for residency risks legitimizing harsh enforcement and militarized borders against new arrivals whose human interests are identical to those of long-term residents.
  3. 03The moral difficulty we feel in deporting long-settled migrants reveals a broader truth that exclusion is generally indefensible, even for those we have not yet met.
  4. 04A truly honest liberal democratic position would advocate for expanded legal channels and a radical reduction in enforcement, viewing migration as the exercise of a basic human freedom rather than a violation to be punished.
  5. 05Amnesty should be viewed as a starting point for reform rather than the final goal, as the ultimate problem lies in the existence of restrictive borders themselves.
