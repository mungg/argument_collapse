"While Dan Breznitz correctly identifies that there are multiple pathways to innovation-based growth beyond the Silicon Valley model, successfully implementing these strategies in the global South requires addressing deep-seated institutional, political, and global structural barriers."

Dan Breznitz’s essay should be read not only as advice to American cities but as a contribution to a much larger development debate. For countries and regions outside the small circle of established technology powers, the Silicon Valley model has long been both alluring and misleading. Governments from Latin America to Africa to Eastern Europe have built technology parks, launched start-up competitions, courted venture capital, and celebrated founders in hoodies. Some of these efforts have produced useful firms. Many have produced more symbolism than transformation. Breznitz is right: innovation-led development cannot be reduced to start-ups chasing exits.

For the global South, his most important message is that there are multiple entry points into the global economy. A country does not need to invent the next iPhone to innovate. It can improve components, specialize in design or production engineering, master assembly at scale, adapt technologies to local conditions, or build service capabilities. This is an encouraging message because the frontier of invention is hard to reach. Advanced economies have incumbent firms, deep capital markets, elite universities, intellectual property portfolios, and military or public research systems that poorer countries cannot quickly replicate. If development required beating Silicon Valley at its own game, most places would have little hope.

The histories of Taiwan, South Korea, China, and parts of India show a different path. Firms and states learned by producing, supplying, adapting, and improving. They entered global value chains in subordinate positions but did not necessarily remain there. Taiwan’s semiconductor foundries, Indian software service firms, Chinese electronics manufacturers, and Korean automakers all developed capabilities over time. They did not begin as pure inventors. They climbed through disciplined learning, export pressure, public support, and links to demanding customers.

Breznitz’s examples of Giant and MediaTek illustrate this beautifully. Giant’s rise from supplier to global bicycle brand depended not on a single invention but on materials knowledge, public research collaboration, production skill, and market understanding. MediaTek’s role in enabling cheaper and more diverse smartphones shows how component innovation can reshape entire industries. These stories matter because they challenge the prestige hierarchy that treats design in California as “innovation” and production in Asia as mere execution. In reality, production sites often solve the hardest problems.

Yet the development implications of Breznitz’s argument are more difficult than they first appear. Global value chains offer entry points, but they also create traps. Many countries assemble garments, electronics, or auto parts for decades without capturing much value or building indigenous capabilities. They remain vulnerable to buyer pressure, wage competition, currency shifts, and relocation. Stage four production can employ many people, but if local firms do not learn, if suppliers remain foreign, if technology is locked away, and if workers have few rights, the result may be dependency rather than development.

The Pearl River Delta is a remarkable case, but it is not easy to copy. Shenzhen’s rise depended on proximity to Hong Kong, massive internal migration, infrastructure investment, special economic zone policies, diaspora capital, state capacity, and China’s enormous domestic market. It also involved labor conditions that democratic societies may not wish to emulate. To say that stage four innovation can produce growth is true. To imply that any region can become Shenzhen by focusing on assembly flexibility would be dangerously optimistic.

The same caution applies to Taiwan. Its success was built by a capable developmental state, public research institutions such as ITRI, education, land reform’s broad social foundation, strategic integration with U.S. markets, and long-term accumulation of engineering talent. Many developing countries have tried to create public research institutes and failed because they lacked bureaucratic capacity, political insulation, or private firms able to absorb knowledge. Institutions cannot be transplanted like machines.

This is not a counsel of despair. It is a reminder that industrial policy is hard. The neoliberal era taught many governments that they should avoid “picking winners” and simply improve the business climate. That advice was too simple and often damaging. But the opposite slogan—“do industrial policy”—is also insufficient. Effective industrial policy requires feedback, discipline, experimentation, and the willingness to stop supporting firms that do not learn. It requires states that are embedded enough in industry to understand it, but autonomous enough not to be captured by it. That balance is rare.

For poorer countries, Breznitz’s framework suggests several practical principles. First, start from existing capabilities, not fantasies. A country with agricultural strength might innovate in food processing, cold chains, irrigation technologies, seeds adapted to local conditions, or bio-based materials. A country with a garment base might move into technical textiles, design, logistics, or machinery maintenance. A country with a large informal repair economy might build formal training and parts supply systems around maintenance and adaptation. The question is not what sounds most futuristic but where learning is already happening.

Second, focus on diffusion as much as frontier innovation. In many countries, the greatest welfare gains come from spreading existing technologies: reliable electricity, clean water, digital payments, basic medical devices, improved logistics, efficient cookstoves, better construction methods, vaccines, and agricultural extension. Silicon Valley ideology often prizes novelty over adoption. But for development, adapting known technologies to local needs can be transformative. Innovation includes making things affordable, durable, repairable, and usable in difficult conditions.

Third, build local linkages. Foreign direct investment can help, but only if it connects to domestic suppliers, workers, universities, and entrepreneurs. Otherwise it becomes an enclave. Governments should negotiate for training, supplier development, technology transfer where possible, and local managerial capacity. They should also support domestic firms that can learn from foreign customers. The goal is not simply to host production but to absorb knowledge.

Fourth, use public procurement creatively. Governments in developing countries buy school meals, buses, medicines, uniforms, construction, energy systems, and digital services. These purchases can help build domestic capabilities if designed carefully. Procurement can create demand for local firms while raising standards. But it must avoid becoming a protected market for low-quality cronies. The public sector should demand performance and improvement.

Fifth, attend to inequality from the beginning. Breznitz’s warning about Israel is relevant far beyond Israel. A high-tech sector can flourish while most of the population remains in low-productivity work. In developing countries with large informal sectors, this danger is acute. A small class of software engineers and founders can become globally connected while rural workers, informal traders, and urban service workers see little benefit. Innovation policy must be linked to education, health, infrastructure, land rights, gender inclusion, and labor protections.

This last point is especially important because the global South is often told to compete through cheap labor. That path can create employment, but it can also lock countries into a race to the bottom. The alternative is not to demand rich-country wages overnight, which would be unrealistic. It is to build productivity and worker power together. Training, technology upgrading, and labor standards should advance in tandem. If innovation raises productivity but workers cannot claim any of the gains, development remains shallow.

The intellectual property regime also deserves more attention than Breznitz gives it. The ability of late industrializers to learn from existing technologies has always depended on access: to machines, designs, scientific knowledge, and reverse engineering. Today, strong patents, trade secrets, digital locks, and platform control can make learning harder. The pandemic has dramatized this issue in debates over vaccine production and intellectual property waivers. If global rules prevent poorer countries from building capabilities in critical technologies, then lectures about innovation ecosystems will ring hollow. A fairer global innovation system would balance rewards for invention with the need for diffusion and local production.

Climate change further raises the stakes. Developing countries need access to clean technologies, adaptation tools, resilient infrastructure, and finance. They cannot be expected to choose between growth and decarbonization. Breznitz’s broader definition of innovation is useful here: local firms can adapt solar systems, mini-grids, irrigation, cooling, building materials, and transit solutions to local conditions. But rich countries must provide finance and technology access. Otherwise green industrial policy in the North may become another form of exclusion, with wealthy countries subsidizing their own firms while poorer countries remain consumers rather than producers.

Breznitz’s essay is ultimately hopeful because it rejects a single ladder of development. There are many ways to learn, many ways to innovate, and many ways to create good jobs. But his examples also show that success requires patience, institutions, and political choices. The global South does not need more start-up pageants modeled on Palo Alto. It needs capable states, broad education, infrastructure, finance for productive firms, bargaining power in global value chains, and room to experiment.

Silicon Valley’s myth is that innovation begins with a founder and ends with an exit. Development teaches a different lesson: innovation begins with collective learning and succeeds when societies retain enough of that learning to improve their own futures.
