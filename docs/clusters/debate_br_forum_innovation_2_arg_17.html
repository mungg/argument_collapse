"While Dan Breznitz correctly identifies the risks of the Silicon Valley model, his critique overcorrects by undervaluing the essential role of radical, stage-one innovation as the primary engine for global technological breakthroughs."

Dan Breznitz has written a powerfully argued essay, and its skepticism about the Silicon Valley model is well-founded in many respects. Yet for all its nuance, the piece overcorrects, risking the creation of a new orthodoxy that undervalues the one thing Silicon Valley undeniably delivers: radical, world-changing novelty. Stage-one innovation—the messy, wasteful, speculative process of turning inventions into products—may not yield inclusive local prosperity on its own, but it is the ultimate source of the breakthroughs that the Japanese, Taiwanese, and Italian firms later refine. Without it, stage two and three are optimizing a shrinking technological frontier.

The essay’s structure, while rich in examples, tends to flatten the very different economic roles these stages play. Yes, Giant’s incremental innovation in carbon fiber bikes created many jobs, but those advances depended on prior radical innovations in materials science, often originating in stage-one environments like U.S. research universities and defense labs. By treating all stages as if they are equally foundational, Breznitz downplays the sequential and asymmetrical nature of the innovation chain. If every country pursued only stage-two or stage-three strategies, who would generate the novel ideas that sustain the whole system? The answer, historically, has been the very high-risk, inequality-exacerbating hubs he criticizes. The global innovation ecosystem requires some regions to specialize in radical novelty, even if that choice produces local disruption.

Moreover, Breznitz’s moral framing—equating inclusive local prosperity with the good, and the Silicon Valley model with widening inequality—obscures the dynamic long-run gains that novelty-based economies can create for broader populations. The smartphone, the internet, mRNA vaccines: all emerged from innovation ecosystems anchored in fundamental research, venture capital, and high-risk entrepreneurship. Their benefits diffused globally, improving welfare for billions, even if the direct employment impacts were concentrated among engineers and investors. A world optimized for stage-three incrementalism might never have produced these leaps. The essay’s own example of MediaTek is telling: MediaTek’s entire business depends on earlier breakthroughs in integrated circuit design, many of which came from the U.S. stage-one ecology. Celebrating the stage-three innovator while disparaging its upstream origin seems like admiring the baker while scorning the farmer.

Breznitz’s portrayal of Israel is a case in point. He is correct that Israel’s high-tech boom widened domestic inequality, but that is at best a partial ledger. Israel’s per capita GDP grew dramatically, and its globally competitive tech sector gave the country strategic weight it would otherwise lack. Moreover, the knowledge and networks built in that sector are now seeding stage-two and stage-three activities within Israel itself, including in cybersecurity and medical devices. The long arc of technological development often starts with concentrated gains before diffusing through broader applications. The same process occurred in the United States during the early industrial era; the Gilded Age tycoons were inequality machines, but their investments eventually built the infrastructure for mass prosperity. To declare the Silicon Valley model a failure because it concentrates initial rewards is ahistorically impatient.

The essay also underplays the catalytic role that active venture capital ecosystems can play in building entirely new industries—industries that later sustain stage-two and stage-three jobs. Tesla’s early venture funding, for example, sparked a revolution in electric vehicles, which has now created thousands of manufacturing and engineering jobs worldwide, many of them precisely the kind of inclusive, high-skill employment Breznitz lauds. Had we heeded a Breznitz-style caution and steered all public support toward tech teens in existing industries, would that energy have been channeled into electric cars? Not obviously. VC-backed moonshots, for all their faults, are capable of jolting systems out of technological stasis.

To be clear, I do not argue that cities should blindly copy Silicon Valley. The essay’s warnings against cookie-cutter science parks and wasted tax incentives are entirely valid. But a sensible innovation policy involves a portfolio. A nation, or even a large region, should nurture some stage-one hubs precisely because they generate the disruptive ideas that other stages refine. For local policymakers, however, the lesson is not “pursue stage-two instead of stage-one,” but rather “understand your place in the global chain and invest accordingly.” Some localities will never have the density of research universities or risk capital to be stage-one leaders, and for them, Breznitz’s advice is invaluable. But for the few that do, abandoning stage-one ambitions in the name of inclusive prosperity would be a tragic waste. The challenge is to make those hubs more inclusive—through housing policy, education, and labor standards—rather than to abandon the very engine of radical novelty.

  3. 03Focusing solely on the local inequality caused by the Silicon Valley model ignores the massive global welfare gains and long-term economic growth generated by radical inventions like mRNA vaccines and the internet.
  4. 04The high-risk venture capital model acts as a necessary catalyst for jolting systems out of stasis and creating entirely new industries that eventually provide stable jobs.
  5. 05Rather than abandoning stage-one ambitions for the sake of inclusivity, policymakers should focus on making high-tech hubs more equitable through better housing, education, and labor policies.
