"The failure of the U.S. mission in Afghanistan stems not from the principles of counterinsurgency itself, but from the over-reliance on military forces to execute a strategy that requires civilian expertise in governance, economics, and law."

Nir Rosen’s critical evaluation of the war in Afghanistan provides a sobering look at the stark realities on the ground as of early 2010. By documenting the harrowing experiences of American troops and their Afghan counterparts, Rosen effectively skewers the hubris of military-driven state-building. However, while his critique of the Pentagon’s misapplication of Counterinsurgency (COIN) is astute, he misidentifies the root cause of the failure. The problem is not that the core principles of COIN are inherently wrong; the problem is that we are attempting to execute a comprehensive political and economic strategy using an almost exclusively military instrument.

Rosen’s essay highlights the absurdity of asking a heavily armed infantry sergeant to act as a civil engineer and a dispute-resolution mediator. The anecdote of Captain Westby trying to solve a water dispute caused by British military bulldozers encapsulates the structural dysfunction of the U.S. effort. The military is trained to "clear" insurgents. They are not trained to "hold" effectively, and they are entirely unqualified to "build." Yet, because of massive bureaucratic imbalances in Washington, the Department of Defense has consumed the entire Afghan mission, marginalizing the civilian agencies that possess the actual expertise needed to stabilize a post-conflict society.

For the past eight years, the United States has chronically underfunded and sidelined the State Department, the United States Agency for International Development (USAID), and the Department of Agriculture in Afghanistan. The Provincial Reconstruction Teams (PRTs) were supposed to be interagency hubs where civilian experts led development efforts, but in reality, they became military-dominated entities where civil affairs officers threw money at short-term, unsustainable projects just to win transient goodwill.

Rosen rightly criticizes the lack of capacity and rampant corruption in the Afghan government. But how did we expect a functional civil service to emerge when our primary interface with the Afghan people is an armored convoy?

A genuine, population-centric stabilization strategy—what the current administration has rhetoricized but failed to resource adequately—requires a massive "civilian surge." To his credit, Richard Holbrooke, the Special Representative for Afghanistan and Pakistan, has recognized this, pushing for an influx of hundreds of agricultural experts, judges, agronomists, and diplomats. But this civilian effort remains a drop in the bucket compared to the 100,000 U.S. troops that will soon occupy the country.

Consider the agricultural sector. Helmand Province, where Rosen’s essay is set, was once the breadbasket of Afghanistan. Today, it is the center of the global heroin trade. The Taliban fund their insurgency by taxing poppy farmers. You cannot shoot your way out of a narcotics-driven insurgency. Eradication by military or police forces, which we have tried, only alienates the farmers and drives them into the arms of the Taliban. The only sustainable solution is to provide farmers with alternative livelihoods—wheat seed, cold storage for pomegranates, access to markets, and rehabilitated irrigation systems (like the one the British inadvertently destroyed in Rosen’s account). This is the work of agronomists and development economists, not infantrymen.

Furthermore, Rosen’s focus on the incompetence of the Afghan National Police (ANP) points directly to a failure of civilian oversight. The militarization of the ANP has been a disaster. We have trained them to be light infantry to fight the Taliban, rather than community police officers to enforce the rule of law. A police force needs a functioning justice system behind it—prosecutors, defense attorneys, and secure prisons. Otherwise, police simply become armed gangs extorting the populace. Building a justice system requires legal scholars and judicial trainers, resources the military cannot provide.

Rosen argues that we cannot create a centralized state because Afghanistan has never had one. This is historically debatable, but even if true, it supports the need for a nuanced, civilian-led approach that empowers local governance and traditional *shuras* (councils), rather than trying to force everything through the corrupt bottleneck of the Karzai administration in Kabul. Civilian diplomats are far better equipped to navigate the complex tribal and ethnic politics of rural Afghanistan than military commanders who rotate out every twelve months.

The Obama administration’s current trajectory is deeply flawed because it puts the cart before the horse. General McChrystal’s troop surge will inevitably increase violence and alienate the population if it is not immediately accompanied, and eventually led, by a civilian strategy that addresses the economic and political grievances fueling the insurgency.

Rosen is right to be deeply skeptical of the military’s ability to win this war. We cannot kill our way to peace, nor can we pave our way to legitimacy with poorly managed military aid funds. But total withdrawal is a false binary. The alternative is a radical demilitarization of our foreign policy. We must rebalance the interagency process, empowering our diplomats and development professionals to lead the stabilization effort, and restrict the military to a supporting role of providing a security umbrella. Until we stop treating a political and developmental crisis as a purely military problem, the tragedies detailed by Rosen will only multiply.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The U.S. military is fundamentally unqualified to perform the 'build' and 'hold' functions of counterinsurgency, such as civil engineering and dispute resolution.
  2. 02Bureaucratic imbalances in Washington have led to the chronic underfunding and marginalization of essential civilian agencies like the State Department and USAID.
  3. 03Sustainable stability in regions like Helmand Province requires agricultural and development experts to provide alternative livelihoods to the poppy trade, rather than military-led eradication.
  4. 04The Afghan National Police have failed because they were trained as light infantry rather than community officers supported by a civilian-led justice system.
  5. 05Civilian diplomats are better equipped than rotating military commanders to navigate the complex tribal and ethnic politics necessary to empower local governance.
  6. 06The current strategy is flawed because it prioritizes a troop surge over the civilian-led political and economic reforms needed to address the root causes of the insurgency.
