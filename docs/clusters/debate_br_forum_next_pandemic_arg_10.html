"The United States' failure during the COVID-19 pandemic was not caused by an over-reliance on the preparedness paradigm, but rather by a lack of actual state capacity, institutional discipline, and the effective execution of existing plans."

Sheila Jasanoff offers a powerful warning against technocratic overconfidence. But her central contrast between preparedness and humility risks drawing the wrong lesson from COVID-19. The United States did not suffer primarily because it was too committed to preparedness, prediction, or control. It suffered because it had too little real preparedness, too little state capacity, too little institutional discipline, and too little willingness to act early on knowledge already available.

It is tempting after failure to indict the paradigm that claimed success. The Global Health Security Index ranked the United States and United Kingdom highly; both then performed badly. Therefore, Jasanoff suggests, the preparedness paradigm itself was flawed. But a bad thermometer does not prove fever is meaningless. The index measured some things and missed others. It overweighted formal capacities and underweighted political dysfunction. That is a serious methodological failure, but it does not show that preparedness is the wrong aspiration. It shows that our concept of preparedness was incomplete.

Indeed, many of the countries that performed better in the first year of the pandemic did not do so because they abandoned preparedness for humility. South Korea, Taiwan, Singapore, New Zealand, Australia, and others varied greatly in regime type and strategy, but their relative success depended on capacities that look very much like preparedness: testing, tracing, border controls, clear communication, quarantine systems, public compliance, and administrative speed. Germany’s early advantage over Britain was not an expression of anti-heroic modesty; it had laboratory capacity, decentralized public health infrastructure, and a chancellor capable of explaining exponential growth. These are practical strengths.

The problem in the United States was not an excess of confidence in plans. The problem was that plans did not bind action. The Obama administration left a pandemic playbook. It was not followed. Federal leaders minimized the threat in January and February 2020, squandered time, sent mixed signals, and turned basic public health measures into partisan identity markers. The CDC test failed and regulatory bottlenecks delayed alternatives. The Strategic National Stockpile was inadequate. Hospitals lacked personal protective equipment. Nursing homes were exposed. Data systems were antiquated. The federal government did not provide consistent national standards. States competed for supplies. Local health departments, weakened by years of underfunding, were asked to perform miracles.

These are not failures of prediction’s ambition. They are failures of execution, investment, and governance.

Jasanoff is right that politics cannot be an afterthought. But preparedness need not presume a fantasy of social unity. A serious preparedness doctrine would treat polarization, federalism, inequality, misinformation, and distrust as operational variables. Military planners, whom Jasanoff criticizes as embodying a command-and-control mindset, are often quite aware that morale, legitimacy, logistics, and local knowledge matter. The better lesson is not to move from preparedness to humility, but to build political sociology into preparedness.

There is a danger in elevating humility too far. In the middle of a fast-moving epidemic, governments must make decisions before all evidence is in. They must sometimes impose restrictions, prioritize scarce resources, and communicate in simple terms. A humility that becomes procedural maximalism can cost lives. In February and March 2020, the United States did not need deeper philosophical reflection to know that explosive spread was possible. It needed rapid testing, masking, cancellation of large indoor gatherings, protection of health workers, and honest communication. Delay was not humility; it was denial.

The same is true of airborne transmission. For too long, many institutions clung to droplet-focused guidance and hygiene theater. Surfaces were scrubbed while ventilation lagged. Here again, the answer was not humility in the abstract but better scientific responsiveness and more precautionary action. The evidence was uncertain, but uncertainty cut both ways. If a respiratory virus might spread through aerosols, improving ventilation and recommending better masks were prudent. In this respect Jasanoff’s own argument supports more decisive preparedness, not less.

Humility is also politically ambiguous. Public officials can invoke humility to justify inaction: “We do not know enough to require masks,” “We do not know enough to close indoor dining,” “We do not know enough to approve rapid tests,” “We do not know enough to tell people the virus is airborne.” During COVID-19, appeals to uncertainty often served as excuses for delay. The ethical demand in a pandemic is not merely to acknowledge uncertainty but to act proportionately under uncertainty. That is a preparedness skill.

One of the most consequential examples concerns rapid antigen testing. The United States was slow to make cheap, frequent at-home testing widely available. Regulatory standards focused heavily on diagnostic accuracy for symptomatic individuals rather than public health usefulness for identifying contagious people quickly. A more adaptive preparedness system would have asked not, “Is this test as sensitive as PCR?” but, “Can repeated low-cost testing reduce transmission while allowing more normal life?” That was a failure of regulatory imagination, but also of preparedness infrastructure. By late 2021, Americans still struggled to obtain affordable rapid tests that were common in parts of Europe. Humility alone would not produce manufacturing capacity or distribution channels.

Jasanoff’s critique of Fineberg’s “crush the curve” wartime rhetoric is understandable. War metaphors can flatten democratic complexity. They can encourage obedience rather than trust. But the alternative cannot be endless consultation during exponential spread. Epidemics punish delay. The window in which to prevent mass death is often short. A democratic society must find ways to act quickly and legitimately. That requires preauthorized tools, clear legal authority, reliable data, and public institutions that have earned confidence before crisis. Again, this is preparedness.

The United States’ vaccine success also complicates Jasanoff’s argument. Operation Warp Speed, whatever one thinks of its branding, was a remarkable case of anticipatory public action: funding multiple candidates, scaling manufacturing before approval, coordinating trials, and accelerating delivery. It was prediction, preparedness, and risk-taking at their best. Without it, the death toll would be much higher. The problem was not that government sought control through technology; it was that technological success was not matched by social delivery. But that argues for integrating vaccination campaigns with community trust, primary care, paid time off, and local outreach—not for demoting preparedness as a guiding ideal.

Jasanoff might reply that this is exactly what she means by technologies of humility. If so, the disagreement may be partly semantic. But terms matter. “Preparedness won’t stop the next pandemic” is a useful provocation only if it does not lead us to undervalue the mundane capacities that actually save lives. Public health agencies need money, staff, laboratories, modern data systems, legal authority, and supply chains. Hospitals need surge plans. Schools need ventilation. Workers need paid leave. Regulators need emergency frameworks. Manufacturers need contracts. Communities need trusted messengers. All of this is preparedness.

What failed before COVID-19 was not the desire to be ready “for any old thing.” It was the substitution of paper readiness for operational readiness. The United States had simulations but not enough masks; plans but not functioning data; experts but not trust; biomedical brilliance but weak public health; federal agencies but fractured authority; wealth but vast insecurity. If we call this overpreparedness, we risk misunderstanding the failure.

The next pandemic could be more lethal, more severe in children, more resistant to vaccines, or more disruptive to essential infrastructure. Humility will be necessary because we will not know its features in advance. But humility should discipline preparedness, not replace it. It should make our plans more realistic, not less ambitious. It should force planners to include inequality, behavior, misinformation, disability, schools, prisons, nursing homes, immigration status, and political conflict. It should teach officials to revise guidance transparently. It should make leaders cautious about triumphalist declarations. But when the alarm sounds, humility must be joined to speed.

The ethical failure of COVID-19 was not only that leaders did not ask who might be hurt if they guessed wrong. It was that they guessed, saw evidence they were wrong, and still failed to build the machinery required to protect people. The dead were not killed by prediction. They were killed by a virus moving through institutions too weak, divided, and underprepared to respond.
