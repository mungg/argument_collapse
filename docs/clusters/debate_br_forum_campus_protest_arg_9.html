"While supporting the push for racial justice on campus, student activists must reject demands for administrative censorship and speech regulation, as these tools ultimately empower university bureaucracies and stifle the intellectual freedom necessary for radical study."

The strongest argument in Robin D. G. Kelley’s essay is that universities are not innocent. They are deeply implicated in hierarchy, wealth, policing, and power. The weakest implication, though perhaps not his intention, is that this critique can sit comfortably alongside many contemporary campus demands. I do not think it can. If we care about black study, political education, and the ruthless criticism of everything existing, we should be more alarmed than Kelley is by the movement’s frequent suspicion of open argument.

There is a real problem of racism on campuses. No serious observer should deny it. Black students at predominantly white institutions often face isolation, condescension, and harassment. Universities have marketed diversity while failing to change the conditions of intellectual and social life. Students are right to protest racist incidents and administrative indifference. They are right to demand more serious attention to black history, slavery, colonialism, and racial inequality. They are right to expose universities that pay workers poorly or invest in morally indefensible enterprises.

But some demands made in the current wave of protest threaten the very intellectual freedom that radical study requires. Calls for speech codes, mandatory ideological training, trigger warnings, and disciplinary action against offensive expression can easily become tools of administrative power. A university bureaucracy empowered to police speech will not remain in the hands of student activists. It will be used against them. The same administrators who issue statements about inclusion can decide that a militant protest creates a hostile environment, that criticism of donors is uncivil, or that antiwar activism makes some students feel unsafe.

Kelley criticizes students for granting the university too much authority over reading choices. That point deserves emphasis. If the curriculum is treated primarily as a mechanism of emotional validation, students may come to expect protection from texts rather than preparation to confront them. This is not a defense of the old canon as sacred. The canon has always been political, and it has excluded too much. But the answer to a narrow curriculum is expansion and argument, not avoidance. Students should read slaveholders, imperialists, racists, liberals, Marxists, nationalists, feminists, conservatives, abolitionists, and reactionaries—not because all are equally right, but because understanding power requires understanding its languages.

The radical study groups Kelley celebrates did not refuse difficult material. They read Marx and Fanon, but also the bourgeois canon they opposed. They sharpened themselves against hostile ideas. There is a lesson here for our moment. A politics organized around safety can become intellectually brittle. It can train students to interpret disagreement as injury and provocation as violence. That habit is bad for scholarship and bad for movements. Movements need people capable of listening strategically, arguing carefully, and distinguishing between an enemy, a potential ally, and a confused bystander.

This does not mean universities should tolerate harassment. Targeted threats, intimidation, and discriminatory treatment are incompatible with education. Nor does it mean that students must politely endure racist abuse in the name of dialogue. But the line between harassment and offensive speech must be drawn with care. A racist epithet shouted at a student is not the same as a controversial argument in a classroom. A threat is not the same as a bad opinion. If we collapse these distinctions, we invite censorship and weaken our ability to name actual coercion.

Kelley’s critique of trauma language is useful here. Trauma is real, but when political disagreement is recoded as harm, institutional remedies tend to become therapeutic and punitive at once: counseling for the wounded, investigation for the offender, training for everyone. This is a poor substitute for education. It also encourages a culture of surveillance. Students begin watching one another for missteps; faculty become cautious; administrators accumulate authority; and genuine political debate retreats into private circles.

The irony is that radical movements have historically depended on liberties they did not always receive: speech, assembly, publication, association, and the ability to disrupt public complacency. Civil rights activists were denounced as agitators. Antiwar protesters were monitored. Black radicals were surveilled and repressed. If today’s activists invite universities to regulate expression in the name of community, they may find tomorrow that their own speech is classified as disorderly, hateful, or unsafe.

A better approach would combine uncompromising anti-racism with uncompromising intellectual freedom. Universities should hire more faculty of color, support black studies, protect students from discrimination, and address the economic structures that make higher education exclusionary. They should also make clear that the answer to offensive ideas is more argument, not administrative prohibition, except in cases of direct harassment or threat. Students should organize teach-ins rather than seek disinvitations, create counter-syllabi rather than demand intellectual shelter, and expose donors and trustees rather than ask deans to manage feelings.

Kelley invokes Baldwin’s idea of love. Love, in this context, cannot mean comfort. It must include the willingness to tell students that they are strong enough to confront ugly ideas, and that their liberation will require more than institutional protection. It must also include telling faculty and administrators that “free speech” cannot be a mask for indifference to racism. The university should be a place where the powerful are challenged, the marginalized are heard, and no idea is immune from criticism.

The danger is not student protest. The danger is that protest will be translated into bureaucracy. A movement for black study should resist that translation. It should demand resources without surrendering freedom, confront racism without empowering censorship, and build forms of solidarity capable of surviving offense, disagreement, and discomfort. If study is to be fugitive, it must remain free.
