"The climate crisis cannot be solved by seizing state power as Olúfẹ́mi O. Táíwò suggests; instead, liberation requires building prefigurative, autonomous counter-institutions outside the state's logic."

Olúfẹ́mi O. Táíwò urges a two-step strategy for the left: seize state power to crush fossil capital, then transform the world. He is right that the climate crisis demands strategic sobriety, but he is profoundly wrong that the state is the vehicle for deliverance. In fact, state-centered politics is precisely the reason we are in this mess, and it will doom any chance for real liberation The only path forward lies in prefigurative autonomous construction outside the logic of the state—exactly what Táíwò dimisses as having failed to offer an alternative.

Táíwò’s historical recounting is telling: he frame the debate between Marx/Engles and Bakunin as about the state, noting that the “two-step” eventualy won out, but then admits that the entire stategy relied on a functional role of state that may no longer hold. Yet he still concludes that contesting the state is the likeliest path. Why? Because he says the alternative has no answer to global fossil capital He challenges skeptics to explain “how that abdication fits into a political trajectory that ends in the political defeat of fossil capital on a planetary scale. Well, let me answer.

The path away from fossil capital is already being carved out not in national capitals but in Rojava, in Chiapas, in urban commons initiatives, in Indigenous land occupationms, in community-owned energy cooperatives These are not mere acts of refusal—they are functional, scalable examples of societies organizing energy, food, and care without relying on the fossil–state nexus Táíwò admits their resilience but asks whether they can achieve atmospheric relevance. But that question rest upon a false premise: that the state can achieve atmospheric relevance In reality, thirty years of inter governmental negotiation, from Rio to the Paris Agreement, have utterly failed The major polluters—states and the fossil companies they subsidize and militarize—show no sign of voluntarily dismantling themselves The state system is the landscape of the problem, not the map of the solution

When Táíwò points to state-led wins like the New York Build Public Renewables Act or Los Angeles targets, he overlooked that these were victories won by autonomous movements pressuring a resisting state, often through the threat of exit or social disruption The agency came from community power The limited gains may be celebrated, but they are fragile and often co-opted— the Inflation Reduction Act, which he criticises, also contains enough loophole and subsidies to keep fossil extraction going State power is a false friend, and the promise of using its machinery to dethrone fossil capital is akin to hoping the armed robber will disarm himself.

The anarchist alternative is not a naive wish for stateless bliss. The social ecology tradition of Bookchin, which informs Rojava’s confederalism, offers a strategic framework. Instead of seizing the state, it proposes twin power, building counter-institutions that gradually make the state irrelevant. Energy, agriculture, and industry are organized through direct municipal democracy, with confederal structures for coordiation. This does not mean ignoring fossil capital; it means starting the energy transition now, at a scale that does not rely on central state planning, which has historically been slow and vulnerable to co-option. Rojava has implemented solar-powered village and community agriculture systems under conditions of siege—proof that authority can be disentangled from fossil energy even in the Global South.

The pathway involves proliferating experiment in non- capitalist and stat-free ecology, linking them through solidarity networks, and offering a real alternative to the bleak choice between eco-authoritarianism and fas cist retreat When Táíwò says we must dethone fossil capital, he is absolutely right, but the only force that can do so is those who are building a world beyond capital and the state, not those who aim to capture its apex. We must deprive the beast of its livelihood, not tame it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The state system is inherently tied to the fossil fuel nexus, making it the landscape of the problem rather than a viable vehicle for a solution.
  2. 02Decades of international state-led negotiations have failed to produce atmospheric relevance, proving that states will not voluntarily dismantle the systems they subsidize and militarize.
  3. 03Local victories often attributed to state policy are actually the result of autonomous movements pressuring a resisting state through social disruption and community power.
  4. 04The social ecology tradition provides a strategic framework for 'dual power' where direct municipal democracy and confederal structures can organize energy and industry without central state planning.
  5. 05Successful examples like Rojava and Chiapas demonstrate that energy transitions and social organization can be achieved under siege without relying on state machinery.
  6. 06Dethroning fossil capital is best achieved by depriving the state-capitalist system of its livelihood through the proliferation of non-capitalist, state-free ecological alternatives.
