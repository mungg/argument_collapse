Diversified DeepSeek

"While Brandon Terry correctly identifies Martin Luther King Jr. as a radical political thinker rather than a passive icon, the true task of modern activists is to translate King’s legacy into new forms of protest and solidarity that address a radically altered media and political landscape."

Brandon Terry’s “MLK Now” is a powerful reminder that the Martin Luther King Jr. worth recovering is not the plaster saint of civic holidays but the unsettling thinker who theorized racism as a complex fusion of fear, ideology, and systemic domination, and who insisted that mass nonviolent civil disobedience was the indispensable tool for democratic renewal. Terry’s essay is honest about King’s failures—especially his sexism—and offers a framework for engaging him as an interlocutor rather than an icon. Yet much of the essay remains a rear-guard action, defending King’s continued relevance against cynics and pessimists. The more pressing task, I think, is to take seriously Terry’s own insight that the morphology of protest must be perpetually rethought, and to ask how King’s intellectual and political legacy can be translated—not simply defended—for a radically altered landscape of media, movement, and power.

Terry’s discussion of King’s theory of racism is particularly useful because it resists the reduction of racial injustice to a single cause. By insisting on the synergy of irrational fear, sociopolitical deformation, and cognitive misrecognition, King offered a model that can inform an intersectional politics without collapsing into the paralyzing view that anti-blackness is an immutable law. That model is already implicit in some of the best contemporary work. For instance, the Movement for Black Lives’ policy platform, released in 2016, addresses not just police violence but also economic justice, education, and environmental racism, reflecting an understanding that racial domination operates across multiple registers. King’s insistence that racism is sustained by ideas that rationalize oppression—not just by individual prejudice—offers a crucial counterweight to the shallow “implicit bias” trainings that have proliferated in the post-Ferguson era, which Terry rightly critiques. Yet King also knew that attacking such ideas required more than consciousness-raising; it required organized, coercive mass action that forced a societal reckoning.

The central challenge Terry identifies is that the practice of civil disobedience has become ritualized. Police departments now routinely negotiate with protest organizers, pre-arrange arrest processing, and manage dissent through a choreography that drains the drama of sacrifice. Street marches, as King himself warned, can be absorbed as “mere transitory drama.” If King’s disruptive power depended on the spectacle of bodies breaking unjust laws and accepting the consequences, then we must ask what new forms of disobedience can recover that disruptive force in an age of social media saturation and state management.

One promising direction is the growing experimentation with economic disruption—strikes, boycotts, and occupations of sites of accumulation—that follow King’s insight that poverty and racism must be attacked together. The Fight for $15 movement, the 2018 wave of teachers’ strikes in states like West Virginia and Oklahoma, and the renewed attention to prison labor strikes have all tapped into King’s vision of nonviolence as a coercive countervailing power, not merely a plea for moral awakening. These movements do not always explicitly invoke King, but they embody his conviction that the poor must unite across racial lines to confront the “gulf between superfluous wealth and abject poverty.” Terry’s reminder that King endorsed a guaranteed income and a full-employment guarantee is timely as those ideas re-enter mainstream debate; yet King’s most radical lesson was that such policies would never be won without sustained, disruptive social movements that operate outside the procedural channels of lobbying and litigation.

Ethically, King’s insistence on love and the refusal of humiliation presents a harder case. Terry wants to rescue a “politics of character” that cultivates virtues such as humility, courage, and magnanimity without sliding into respectability. In our secular, pluralist moment, this can perhaps best be reframed as an ethic of radical solidarity. The demand to see even one’s political enemies as moral equals with whom we share a “garment of destiny” need not depend on the Christian command to love thy neighbor; it can be grounded in the recognition that a just society is impossible unless we build forms of cooperation that transcend enmity. This does not mean forgiving white supremacy or abiding its violence; it means understanding that the ultimate goal is not to vanquish a class of people but to dismantle the systems that deform them and us. That vision is profoundly countercultural in a moment when social media incentivizes the mockery and public shaming Terry decries. But movements that have been most durable—from the civil rights struggle itself to the immigrant rights organizing of the early 2000s—have often understood that building broad, transformative power requires a discipline of non-vengeance, not out of sentimentality, but out of strategic necessity.

Terry’s essay, however, still circles the problem of King’s sexism without fully integrating the critique. The most generative engagement with King today may be precisely at this point of failure. The black church tradition that sustained King’s moral imagination is in decline, and the male charismatic leader model he embodied is increasingly rejected by a new generation of activists who insist on distributed, participatory, and feminist leadership. This is not a betrayal of King’s radicalism; it is its necessary extension. Ella Baker’s notion of “spadework”—the unglamorous, collective building of power from below—provides a counter-tradition that corrects King without discarding his commitment to mass direct action. If we are to learn from King’s mistakes as well as his insights, we must build movements that center the leadership of those who have been most marginalized, including poor women of color, queer and trans people, and disabled people. Such movements would be closer to the universal quality King praised than the ones he actually led.

The most valuable gift Terry offers is the insistence that King remains a profound interlocutor, not a canonical saint. That means we are permitted to argue with him, adapt his ideas, and discard what no longer serves. King’s call to “face the challenge of change” was, after all, directed at ourselves as much as at our adversaries. In a time of rising authoritarianism, climate collapse, and grotesque inequality, we need the clarity, courage, and strategic creativity that animated King’s best thinking. But we need those things in a form that can answer to the world we actually inhabit—a world King glimpsed but could never fully address. That is the unfinished work of MLK now.
