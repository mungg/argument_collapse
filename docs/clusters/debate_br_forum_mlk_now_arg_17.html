"The canonization of Martin Luther King Jr. as a unifying figure of national consensus is a vital civic achievement that should be preserved rather than dismantled in favor of his more radical, disruptive persona."

Brandon M. Terry’s essay "MLK Now" is an intellectually rich, passionately argued call to rediscover the radical, disruptive Martin Luther King, Jr. Terry takes aim at the "consensus" view of King, arguing that by remembering him as a unifier who merely called America to live up to its founding creed, we do violence to his legacy. According to Terry, the integrationist, colorblind King is a "mythic figure" deployed by conservatives as a form of "gaslighting." Terry urges us to instead embrace King the radical—the practitioner of coercive civil disobedience, the critic of capitalism, and the disruptor of public order.

While Terry’s historical excavation of King’s later, more radical thought is accurate, his prescriptive conclusions for 2018 are dangerously misguided. The translation of King from a polarizing activist into a figure of national consensus is not a tragedy; it is one of the most profound civic achievements in American history. By dismissing the "consensus" King, Terry severely underestimates the fragility of our multi-ethnic republic and the vital necessity of unifying national myths.

Terry derides the view of King as a "Christ who sacrificed his life to redeem our nation from its original sin," preferring a King who condemned the United States as the "greatest purveyor of violence in the world." But societies do not cohere around shared critiques; they cohere around shared ideals. The genius of the Civil Rights Movement, and of King’s most iconic rhetoric at the March on Washington, was precisely its ability to graft the demands of black Americans onto the rootstock of the American creed. When King rooted his dream deeply in the American dream, he provided a bridge for the majority of the country to cross over to the side of justice without seeing themselves as a conquered or evil people.

To view the "colorblind" jurisprudence that Terry scorns as mere conservative gaslighting is to rewrite the fundamental moral victory of the mid-century movement. The demand that individuals be judged by the content of their character rather than the color of their skin remains the only viable basis for a pluralistic democracy. As we witness in 2018 the terrifying rise of identity politics on both the right (in the form of white nationalism) and the left, King’s universalist, integrationist vision is more necessary than ever. When we abandon the goal of a colorblind society—even as an aspirational horizon—we surrender the public square to a zero-sum tribalism.

Furthermore, Terry’s endorsement of King's "coercive" civil disobedience—using disruption and intimidation to force a crisis—must be handled with extreme caution in our current political climate. Terry laments that nonviolent resistance has become "ritualized" and robbed of its drama, suggesting a need for more disruptive mass action. But in a highly polarized, deeply fractured 2018, where trust in institutions is at rock bottom, escalating coercive extra-legal protests is a recipe for civic unraveling, not democratic renewal. The rule of law, flawed as it may be, is the ultimate safeguard for minorities. Romanticizing the transgression of public order laws ignores how easily such tactics can be adopted by illiberal forces.

Terry is entirely right to highlight King’s emphasis on the "politics of character" and his warnings against allowing anger to slip into hatred. King’s understanding that vengeance and bitterness destroy the movement from within is a desperately needed rebuke to the vitriolic, call-out culture that dominates modern social media activism. But Terry tries to have it both ways: he wants the unifying, empathetic ethics of *agape* combined with the coercive, radical disruption of the state. In practice, the latter almost invariably consumes the former.

The canonization of King was a necessary alchemy. To integrate a nation divided by centuries of apartheid required elevating King into a civil religion, a unifying prophet of American progress. Cass Sunstein and others whom Terry criticizes are right to view King’s deployment of America's deepest ideals as heroic. Rather than tearing down this monument to find the radical hiding inside, we should recognize that the "mythic figure of consensus" serves a vital, stabilizing function. In an era where the center is barely holding, we need the King who speaks to our highest, shared democratic aspirations, not the King who serves as an avatar for permanent revolution.
