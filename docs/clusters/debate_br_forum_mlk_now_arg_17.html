Diversified Gemini

"While Brandon M. Terry correctly identifies the need to recover Martin Luther King Jr.’s radical intellectual legacy, he underestimates how modern media fragmentation and state management have rendered King’s traditional tactics of mass protest and moral spectacle obsolete."

Brandon M. Terry’s "MLK Now" astutely identifies a central problem in modern progressive politics: the disconnect between the towering moral authority of Martin Luther King, Jr.’s memory and the practical application of his tactical genius. Terry makes a persuasive case for returning to King’s strategic use of nonviolent direct action and his complex understanding of racism. However, while Terry calls for the "morphology of protest" to be "experimentally and imaginatively rethought in light of technological, cultural, [and] political changes," he underestimates just how thoroughly the modern state and media ecosystem have neutralized King’s specific playbook. If we are to apply King's strategic intellect to 2018, we must realize that the era of the mass march is effectively over as a tool of political coercion.

King’s use of civil disobedience relied on a very specific set of historical conditions. As Terry notes, King utilized the "spectacle" of mass, disciplined action to force the country to see ordinary African Americans as active agents of change. The strategy of Birmingham and Selma was essentially a media strategy: to create a crisis that would provoke a brutal response from the authorities (Bull Connor's dogs, Jim Clark's troopers), which would then be broadcast via three centralized national television networks to a relatively unified, moderately liberal white public. The goal was to shock the conscience of the nation and force federal intervention.

In the fragmented, hyper-partisan, and algorithmic media environment of 2018, this mechanism is entirely broken. There is no longer a unified "conscience" to shock. If a modern equivalent of the Birmingham campaign were to occur today, the footage would immediately be sorted into partisan echo chambers. Fox News and right-wing social media networks would frame the protesters as violent rioters, emphasizing disruptions to traffic and property, while liberal networks would broadcast the police brutality. The result would not be a new Civil Rights Act; it would be a calcification of existing partisan divides.

Furthermore, as Terry briefly hints, the state has learned how to manage protest. The "sublime and disruptive force" of civil disobedience has been domesticated. Police departments across the country employ "protest management" strategies, utilizing designated "free speech zones," negotiated arrest protocols, and overwhelming militarized force to prevent the kind of organic, disruptive crisis King sought to create. When millions of people marched peacefully in the 2017 Women's March, or previously against the Iraq War, the state simply allowed them to march, ignored their demands, and waited for them to go home. The dramatic tension of "punishment and sacrifice" that gave King's actions gravitas has been replaced by permitted, weekend rallies that function more as catharsis than coercion.

Therefore, applying King's ideas today means abandoning his most famous tactics. If King's goal was to "attack the evil system" and create a situation so crisis-packed that it opens the door to negotiation, modern activists must find new levers of power. As Terry points out, King was keenly aware of the power of automation and economic disruption. The most effective translation of King’s strategy for 2018 lies not in marching on state capitols, but in the realm of economic non-cooperation and logistical disruption.

We must look to tactics like targeted consumer boycotts, debt strikes, and the disruption of corporate supply chains. The fact that Terry highlights King's late-in-life efforts to organize gang members in Chicago is the most telling detail of the essay. In Chicago, King recognized that symbolic moral appeal was failing against the entrenched, bureaucratic racism of the North. He began searching for localized, structural leverage.

The spirit of King is not found in organizing another permits-secured march down a closed city street. It is found in understanding the precise choke points of modern racial capitalism. Today, that means organizing the workers who power Amazon’s fulfillment centers, blocking the construction of pipelines, and utilizing digital platforms to coordinate mass rent strikes. King teaches us that moral righteousness without coercive leverage is a dead end. To honor him in 2018, we must stop trying to recreate the optics of 1963 and start designing campaigns capable of halting the machinery of the modern economy.
