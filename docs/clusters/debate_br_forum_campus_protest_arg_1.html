"While student activists should pursue radical political education and systemic transformation, they should also strategically utilize institutional reforms and campus resources as necessary infrastructure for long-term struggle."

Robin D. G. Kelley’s essay is most persuasive where it refuses the false choice between the classroom and the street. The great temptation in this moment is to sort student protest into two piles: either serious politics, which supposedly happens only off campus, or therapeutic complaint, which supposedly happens only on campus. Kelley rejects that binary. He asks instead what forms of study make struggle more durable, more imaginative, and less vulnerable to capture by the very institutions it contests. That is the right question.

Still, I would put slightly more weight than Kelley does on the political usefulness of some of the reform demands he treats with suspicion. Diversity, faculty hiring, cultural competency, mental health services, and renamed buildings are not revolution. They will not abolish debt, prisons, police violence, or the racial state. But movements often need partial victories. They need spaces in which people can breathe, convene, read, recover, and recruit. A black studies department, an ethnic studies requirement, a multicultural center, or a funded counseling program can be captured by administrative liberalism; it can also be used against it. The history Kelley invokes shows both processes at once.

The black studies programs born of 1960s insurgency were incorporated into universities, professionalized, and often disciplined by scarcity. Yet they also produced generations of scholars, teachers, organizers, lawyers, artists, journalists, and community workers who carried radical questions into many places. The point is not that institutionalization equals liberation. It does not. The point is that reform can create infrastructure. A classroom can become a study group; a student center can become an organizing base; a curricular requirement can introduce a student to Fanon, Claudia Jones, C. L. R. James, Audre Lorde, or Cedric Robinson for the first time. The university means to manage these encounters. It does not always succeed.

Kelley is right that the language of “belonging” is dangerous when it treats the university as a wounded family rather than a powerful institution. Universities are employers, landlords, investors, police departments, debt machines, prestige factories, and often real estate developers. They are also sites where young people meet one another, argue, love, despair, and form political identities. Students ask to belong because exclusion is not abstract. It shows up in classrooms where they are presumed incompetent, in parties that trade on racist costumes, in monuments that honor enslavers and imperialists, in campus police who treat black students as intruders, and in syllabi that make Europe appear to have invented thought itself.

But belonging should not be the horizon. A university that “includes” black students while paying dining hall workers poverty wages, expanding police power, investing in prisons, or extracting tuition from families already dispossessed by racial capitalism has not been transformed. It has diversified its legitimacy. Kelley’s warning is therefore indispensable: inclusion can become the velvet glove of domination.

The most powerful part of the essay is its insistence on political education. Activism without study is easily exhausted. It becomes reactive, chasing each outrage as if it were unprecedented. Study gives movements memory. It reveals that police killings, campus racism, student debt, adjunct labor, and urban displacement are not separate scandals but connected features of a social order. It also protects movements from mistaking visibility for power. A hashtag can summon people into the street; it cannot by itself answer strategic questions about organization, discipline, coalition, repression, and the state.

Kelley’s critique of trauma language is bracing, but it needs careful handling. Trauma is not merely an administrative category or a neoliberal trap. Black students are not wrong to say they are harmed by repeated exposure to racist violence and institutional contempt. They are naming something real. The danger comes when trauma becomes the whole story, when injury becomes identity, when the demand is reduced to recognition of pain rather than abolition of the conditions producing it. “I am hurt” can open a door. “We are organizing to end what hurts us” must be the next sentence.

The distinction matters because administrators are often eager to meet pain with management. They can hire consultants, issue statements, create listening sessions, and expand counseling while leaving power untouched. They can learn the language of care without practicing justice. A movement that speaks only in the idiom of woundedness risks being answered by bureaucracy. A movement that links wound to wage, curriculum to police, endowment to prison, and campus climate to global capitalism is harder to pacify.

The question, then, is not whether students should demand reform or revolution. They should demand both, but know the difference. Fight for more black faculty, and ask who cleans the buildings in which those faculty teach. Fight for black studies, and make it accountable to communities beyond the campus gates. Fight for mental health services, and connect psychic suffering to debt, racism, gender violence, and precarity. Fight to rename buildings, and ask what forms of wealth built them in the first place.

Kelley’s “love, study, struggle” is not a slogan for withdrawal from campus politics. It is a discipline for deepening them. Love keeps struggle from becoming performance. Study keeps anger from becoming spectacle. Struggle keeps study from becoming career. The best student movements will steal from the university what they can, force concessions where they must, and build forms of collective life that no administrator can grant and no trustee can own.

"Rather than choosing between trusting or rejecting the university, student activists should engage with it as a contradictory institution by pairing immediate material demands with long-term structural and revolutionary goals."

The choice Robin D. G. Kelley presents is not between trusting the university and rejecting it. The university is neither a loving family nor a monolithic enemy. It is a contradictory institution: part employer, part landlord, part credentialing agency, part research center, part police apparatus, part sanctuary, part marketplace, part public good. Student activists understand some of these contradictions because they live them. The practical question is how to act within them without being absorbed by them.

Kelley is right to criticize the sentimental language universities use to describe themselves. “Community,” “family,” “belonging,” and “home” are often marketing terms. They conceal hierarchy. Students do not have equal power with trustees. Adjuncts do not have equal power with provosts. Workers do not have equal power with donors. When a crisis occurs, the family metaphor collapses and the legal department appears. Students are then shocked to discover that the institution promising care is primarily concerned with liability, reputation, and control.

But activists also need strategies that distinguish among different kinds of demands. Some demands are immediately winnable and materially useful: hiring more counselors, funding cultural centers, creating emergency grants, improving reporting procedures, revising retention practices, increasing need-based aid. Some are structural but possible with sustained pressure: faculty hiring lines, ethnic studies requirements, living wage policies, investment transparency, graduate worker recognition, changes in campus policing. Some are horizon demands: free tuition, abolition of prisons, reparative redistribution, democratic control of the university. Movements weaken themselves when they confuse these categories, but they also weaken themselves when they abandon any one of them.

A horizon demand tells the truth about the scale of injustice. A winnable demand improves conditions and builds confidence. A structural demand links the two. The best campus movements will need all three.

Consider the demand for more black faculty. Kelley is correct that representation alone will not transform the university. A black professor can reproduce elitism; a diverse administration can implement austerity. But black faculty can also mentor students, change fields, support organizing, and make it harder for departments to pretend that race is peripheral. The question should not be whether representation is enough. It should be: representation for what purpose, under what conditions, accountable to whom, and connected to what redistribution of power?

The same is true of mental health services. Therapy will not abolish racism, but untreated suffering can destroy students. The proper critique is not “counseling is neoliberal.” It is that counseling without justice is containment. Universities should expand mental health care, especially culturally competent care, while also addressing the policies that produce crisis: debt, isolation, over-policing, sexual violence, food insecurity, and academic exclusion. Care and structural change should be paired, not opposed.

Kelley’s call for independent political education is perhaps the most important recommendation in the essay. Universities are slow, departmentalized, and credential-driven. Movements need spaces where people read across disciplines, connect theory to action, and learn skills not rewarded by grades: facilitation, security, media strategy, coalition-building, historical analysis, conflict resolution, and democratic decision-making. These spaces should be autonomous enough to think freely but resourced enough to endure. Faculty can help without controlling. Departments can provide rooms and funds without owning the agenda. Alumni and community organizations can widen the circle.

At the same time, activists should not disdain institutional knowledge. Budgets, bylaws, accreditation rules, endowment structures, Title VI and Title IX procedures, faculty governance, union contracts, and state funding formulas are not glamorous, but they shape what universities can be forced to do. A movement that understands only moral language can be stalled by procedural language. A movement that learns procedure can turn it into terrain. Fugitive study should include balance sheets.

Administrators, for their part, should stop treating protest as a communications problem. The familiar sequence—incident, outrage, listening session, task force, diversity statement, delayed report—has lost credibility. If universities want to be taken seriously, they should publish timelines, budgets, responsible offices, and measures of accountability. They should say what they will not do and why, rather than hiding behind process. They should invite scrutiny of investments, policing, labor practices, and admissions. They should also protect protest, including disruptive protest, while maintaining clear lines against threats and harassment.

Faculty have obligations too. Too many support student activism in abstract but retreat when students challenge departments, syllabi, or hiring. Others dismiss students as fragile without asking whether their own teaching has become complacent. Faculty should defend academic freedom and rigorous debate, but rigor is not the same as indifference. A rigorous education in 2016 must confront slavery, colonialism, capitalism, policing, gender, sexuality, empire, and environmental crisis as central rather than elective topics.

The most difficult issue is trust. Students often do not trust administrators, for good reasons. Administrators often do not trust students, also sometimes for reasons rooted in fear rather than evidence. Faculty distrust both. Workers are asked to keep the institution running while others debate its soul. Rebuilding trust does not mean restoring the family myth. It means creating enforceable relationships: contracts, transparent decision-making, shared governance, independent grievance processes, and regular public accounting.

Kelley ends with “love, study, struggle.” I would add a fourth term: institution-building. Not because institutions save us, but because movements that refuse to build durable forms often leave behind only memory and burnout. Some institutions will be outside the university; some will be inside; some will move between. The question is not whether the university can love. It cannot. The question is whether people within and around it can build enough power, knowledge, and solidarity to make it answerable to justice.

Kelley’s essay is written with love, and because of that it deserves a serious answer from the students whose language he questions. I agree with him that study matters, that universities are not families, and that diversity without redistribution is a trap. But I worry that his critique of trauma misunderstands what many black students are doing when we speak of pain, safety, and exhaustion. We are not confusing therapy with liberation. We are trying to remain alive and whole long enough to fight.

For students who entered college in the age of Trayvon Martin, Michael Brown, Eric Garner, Tamir Rice, Sandra Bland, Freddie Gray, and Rekia Boyd, the distinction between campus and street has never been stable. The phone in your hand brings death into the dorm room. A police killing happens hundreds of miles away and then repeats on a loop in the dining hall, on Twitter, in class discussion, in the smirk of the student who wants to debate whether the victim “complied.” A professor asks for “both sides.” A classmate says Black Lives Matter is racist. Someone writes a slur on a dorm door. A campus alert describes a suspect as a black male and suddenly every black man on campus is visible in the wrong way.

When students call this traumatic, they are not necessarily making themselves passive. They are naming the conditions under which they are expected to perform excellence. They are saying that the mind is not separate from the body, that learning is not separate from fear, and that the university’s polished language of opportunity hides an everyday demand: be grateful, be exceptional, and be quiet.

Kelley is right that the language of trauma can be absorbed by administrators. A dean can say, “We hear your pain,” and then do nothing. A counseling center can be expanded while campus police continue to stop black students. A diversity training can become an annual ritual of absolution. But every language we use can be absorbed. “Resistance” can become a brand. “Radical” can become a conference theme. “Black study” can become a line in a grant proposal. The danger of co-optation is real, but it cannot be avoided simply by changing vocabulary.

What matters is whether trauma is connected to power. Many students are already making that connection. When they demand mental health resources, they are often also demanding more black counselors, lower tuition, protection for low-income students, accountability for racist harassment, and changes in policing. They know that a panic attack before class is not the same thing as structural racism. They also know that structural racism can produce panic attacks before class. If the analysis cannot hold both truths, it is too thin.

The phrase “safe space” has been mocked so relentlessly that it is hard to recover its meaning. But in black organizing traditions, safety has never meant avoidance of challenge. Churches, beauty shops, kitchens, union halls, bookstores, and living rooms were safe not because everyone agreed, but because people could gather without being immediately exposed to the full force of white surveillance and humiliation. The Mississippi Freedom Schools that Kelley praises had to create conditions in which children could ask dangerous questions. Safety was not the opposite of struggle. It was one of struggle’s preconditions.

Students asking for safe spaces are not always asking to be protected from difficult ideas. Sometimes they are asking for a place where they do not have to begin every conversation by proving racism exists. They are asking for rooms where black students can disagree with one another without performing for white classmates. They are asking for somewhere to grieve before returning to the work. If that is not political, then much of black social life has never been political.

I also want to defend the demand for belonging, though not in the sentimental form universities sell. Black students do not need to be loved by the institution as a parent loves a child. But we do have a right to inhabit the places our labor, tuition, taxes, ancestors, and communities have paid for. Belonging need not mean assimilation. It can mean refusing the lie that the university naturally belongs to others. When students at Missouri call themselves Concerned Student 1950, they are not begging for affection. They are invoking the year black students were first admitted and insisting that the institution’s history is theirs to judge.

Kelley’s call for study is essential. We need more political education, more reading groups, more relationships with workers and communities beyond campus, more analysis of capitalism, empire, prisons, gender, sexuality, and disability. Some student activism is too reactive, too dependent on social media cycles, too quick to elevate individual voices into representatives. Study can deepen the work.

But study must also listen to the knowledge produced by hurt. Pain is not the end of politics, but it is often where politics begins. A student who says, “I cannot breathe here,” is not yet offering a full theory of the university. Still, she is telling the truth. The task is not to correct her by saying trauma is neoliberal. The task is to ask: What is taking your breath? Who benefits? Who else is suffocating? What would it take for all of us to breathe?

Resistance is our heritage, as Kelley writes. So is mourning. So is care. So is the practice of gathering the wounded and sending them back stronger. If black study is to serve black struggle, it must make room for all of these.

"While Robin Kelley’s historical analysis of black radicalism is valuable, his critique of contemporary student activists unfairly dismisses their practical demands and language as neoliberal traps when they are actually necessary tools for survival and institutional navigation."

I read Robin Kelley's essay with admiration and growing unease. The admiration is for the historical depth, the genealogy of black radical study, the careful refusal to dismiss the students whose courage he plainly respects. The unease is harder to name, but it has to do with what feels like a recurring move in contemporary left-of-left commentary: the diagnosis that today's activists are doing it wrong because they have absorbed too much of the neoliberalism they oppose. There is something in this diagnosis that I find both partially correct and rhetorically unfair.

Consider Kelley's treatment of trauma. He grants that trauma is real, that mental health services are urgently needed, then proceeds to suggest that trauma-talk is a kind of theoretical trap—a vocabulary that "tricks" activists into the language of their oppressors. But the activists are not choosing the trauma frame out of theoretical naivete. They are choosing it because it describes something true about their lives, and because, frankly, it is the frame that has cultural traction right now. To say "you should be using the language of oppression, repression, and subjugation instead" is to ask people to perform a kind of voluntary linguistic atavism. It is not obvious that the older vocabulary is more politically efficacious; it has, after all, presided over half a century of political defeat.

Kelley's invocation of Harney and Moten's Undercommons is similarly double-edged. The Undercommons is a beautiful book, and Kelley reads it generously. But there is a problem with holding it up as a model. Harney and Moten counsel a kind of internal exile within the university: "one can only sneak into the university and steal what one can." This is, with respect, a posture available primarily to tenured or near-tenured academics and to graduate students with sufficient cultural capital to pull off the trick. For an undergraduate from a working-class black family, taking on $80,000 in debt to attend Mizzou, the suggestion that the university is best treated as a target for sabotage and fugitivity may not land the same way. Such students cannot afford to be in but not of the institution. They need it to work for them, at least minimally, or they need it to not destroy them financially.

This is why I find the "reformist" demands Kelley dismisses—diversity, inclusion, mental health resources, curricular change—less contemptible than he makes them out to be. They are demands made by people who actually have to navigate the institution as it exists, who do not have the luxury of treating it as a site of pure critique. The fact that these demands are absorbable by the institution is precisely what makes them winnable. And winnable things matter. A black student who can talk to a counselor who understands the specific weight of watching another viral video of police violence is not thereby pacified; she is sustained. Sustenance is a precondition for struggle.

Where Kelley is genuinely illuminating is on the question of multiculturalism's historical trajectory. He is right that the radical anti-racist movements of the 1960s and 1970s were partially defanged by their absorption into a diversity framework that produced black CEOs and black police chiefs without redistributing power or wealth. The mass incarceration of black Americans escalated dramatically during the same decades that saw the rise of black political representation. This is not a coincidence to be explained away; it is the central political puzzle of the post–civil rights era. Kelley is right to make it visible.

But I want to resist the implication that today's students should therefore abandon the diversity/inclusion frame altogether. Walking and chewing gum is a real option. One can fight for more black faculty and for divestment from prisons; one can demand mental health services and freedom schools; one can use the trauma vocabulary in the dean's office and the Fanon vocabulary in the study group. The opposition Kelley draws between reform and revolution is conceptually sharp but practically misleading. Movements throughout history have moved fluidly between the two registers, often making the most progress when they refused to choose.

The students at UCLA whom Kelley celebrates at the essay's end are doing exactly this. They are graduate students at a research university, using its resources, drawing on its faculty—including Kelley himself—while running their own freedom school. They have not abandoned the institution; they have made it useful. That is the actual model, and it is more pragmatic and less pure than Kelley's framing suggests.

"While Robin Kelley correctly identifies that the language of trauma can lead to depoliticized administrative reforms, he underestimates how this vocabulary reflects the actual lived experience of modern activists and must be used as a starting point for structural critique rather than being dismissed as a mere clinical distraction."

Robin Kelley's essay performs a difficult balancing act—honoring the courage of campus activists while critiquing the conceptual frame in which much of their activism unfolds—and on the whole he pulls it off. But I want to press harder on what I take to be the essay's most important move: the distinction between trauma-talk and the older vocabulary of oppression, repression, and subjugation. Kelley is right that something has shifted. The question is how to think about that shift without falling into a generational scold.

When Kelley quotes Naomi Wallace—"Mainstream America is less threatened by the 'trauma' theory because it doesn't place economic justice at its core"—he names a real phenomenon. The university is structurally incapable of granting students reparations, free tuition, or divestment from the prison industry, but it is exquisitely equipped to expand its counseling services and host implicit-bias workshops. Therapeutic accommodation is precisely the form of redress neoliberal institutions can absorb without changing. To frame demands in the language of personal injury is, in a sense, to pre-translate them into the only dialect the administration speaks fluently.

And yet. I think Kelley underestimates the political work that the language of trauma is doing for this generation. Consider what it means, materially, for a black eighteen-year-old to arrive at Yale or Mizzou after spending high school watching cell phone videos of Eric Garner, Tamir Rice, and Walter Scott. The trauma vocabulary is not, for these students, a clinical category imported from the DSM and pressed onto a political situation; it is a description of an actual phenomenology. Their nervous systems are doing something. To insist that they translate this immediately back into the register of structural critique—as if Marx and Fanon offered a more authentic idiom than the one they actually inhabit—risks a kind of theoretical paternalism.

The better question is whether the trauma framework can be a beginning rather than an ending. Kelley acknowledges this in passing ("trauma can be an entrance into activism, it is not in itself a destination"), but I wish he had developed it. Many of the great political traditions he invokes—black feminism especially—were born of theorists who took felt experience seriously as a starting point. Diversified Gemini

"While Robin D. G. Kelley advocates for a radical, subversive retreat into the 'undercommons,' student activists are right to pursue institutional reforms because holding universities accountable to their promises of inclusion is a practical and effective way to redistribute resources and improve marginalized lives."

Robin D. G. Kelley’s "Black Study, Black Struggle" offers a sobering and historically rich critique of contemporary campus activism. He accurately identifies the tension at the heart of the movement: the contradictory desire of students to simultaneously reform the university and reject it as an irredeemable pillar of the neoliberal order. However, in his attempt to steer students toward radical, subversive intellectual traditions, Kelley is far too quick to dismiss the very real value of institutional reform, and he too easily waves away the practical benefits of the "multicultural" measures he derides.

Kelley aligns himself with Stefano Harney and Fred Moten’s concept of the "undercommons," arguing that the university is an inherently hostile space from which one should simply "steal what one can." This romanticization of the guerrilla intellectual is seductive, particularly for those frustrated by the slow pace of administrative change. But it is fundamentally a politics of abdication. By advising students and faculty to view the university merely as a site of counterinsurgency and corporate exploitation, Kelley encourages a retreat from the hard, unglamorous work of institutional governance.

Institutions, for all their flaws, are made of people and policies, both of which can be changed. When students demand greater diversity, inclusion, and cultural-competency training, they are not, as Kelley suggests, being tricked into adopting the language of neoliberalism. They are utilizing the available levers of power to create tangible improvements in their daily lives and the lives of those who will come after them. Funding for cultural centers, the hiring of tenure-track faculty of color, and the establishment of robust mental health resources are not empty gestures of liberal appeasement. They are material victories that redistribute university resources toward marginalized students, providing them with the support necessary to thrive in historically white spaces.

Kelley suggests that the triumph of multiculturalism marked the defeat of a radical anti-racist vision. He laments that demands for inclusion merely fold people into an unjust society. But this critique relies on a false binary: that we must choose between perfect, utopian social transformation and total complicity. The reality is that the presence of black students, faculty, and administrators within the highest echelons of higher education alters the epistemological architecture of these institutions. The curriculum has changed, the scholarship has changed, and the allocation of resources has changed precisely because previous generations of activists demanded inclusion rather than choosing to remain perpetually on the outside looking in.

Furthermore, Kelley’s critique of the "family" metaphor used by universities is overly cynical. Yes, admissions brochures are marketing tools, and universities often fail to live up to their highest ideals. But the promise of a supportive, intellectually nurturing community is not a trick; it is the foundational aspiration of higher education. When students express feelings of betrayal because their lived experience does not match this promise, they are entirely justified. Holding the university accountable to its own stated values is a powerful rhetorical and political strategy. Asking an institution to be better is not a sign of naive dependence; it is an assertion of ownership. These students belong at Yale, at Mizzou, at UCLA, and they have every right to demand that these spaces be made hospitable to them.

Finally, while Kelley is right to point out that the university will never be the primary engine of global social revolution, it remains one of the most powerful engines of social mobility and intellectual development we possess. The students who are currently trying to "simply get through school and join the ranks of professionals" are not a politically apathetic mass to be ignored. They are future doctors, lawyers, teachers, and policymakers who will carry the lessons learned on campus into the wider world. Ensuring that their educational environment is diverse, equitable, and supportive is a crucial step in transforming the broader society.

We should not scoff at students who seek to build a "post-racial haven," even if we recognize the impossibility of fully achieving it within a sea of white supremacy. Pursuing that ideal forces the institution to confront its blind spots and rectify its injustices. A university that takes inclusion seriously, that funds black studies, and that hires black faculty is a better university. Sneaking into the academy to "steal what one can" may sound revolutionary, but staying to rebuild the institution is the work that actually changes the world.
