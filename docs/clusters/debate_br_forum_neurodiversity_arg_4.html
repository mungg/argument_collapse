"The neurodiversity movement should shift from a liberal focus on individual legal accommodations to a labor-centered strategy of collective bargaining and universal design to fundamentally restructure the workplace for all minds."

Robert Chapman’s call for Neurodivergent Power is most compelling when read not as a rejection of accommodations, but as a challenge to their smallness. The workplace is where this challenge becomes clearest. For decades disability law has asked employers to make “reasonable” adjustments for individual workers. Sometimes that works. A person gets written instructions instead of verbal ones, a quieter desk, flexible hours, remote work, a job coach, extra time, or permission to use headphones. These changes can be life-altering. But they leave intact a deeper question: why are so many workplaces built around speed, noise, interruption, surveillance, emotional masking, and constant availability in the first place?

Neurodiversity entered corporate life through a narrow door. Employers discovered that some autistic people were good at pattern recognition, quality control, coding, data analysis, or sustained attention. Consultants then translated neurodiversity into business advantage. This was not entirely cynical. It helped some people get jobs who had been unfairly excluded by interviews, networking rituals, and unwritten social codes. But Chapman is right that the model is selective. It values the autistic worker who can be slotted into a productivity scheme, not the worker who needs the scheme redesigned. It celebrates cognitive difference when profitable and treats it as a performance problem when inconvenient.

A labor perspective helps bridge the gap between liberal accommodation and revolutionary aspiration. The problem is not only that individual rights are too weak, though they often are. The problem is that individual rights individualize conflict. The worker must disclose a diagnosis, request an accommodation, document need, negotiate with management, risk stigma, and perhaps fight retaliation. If denied, they must complain, appeal, or sue—tasks that are especially difficult for people with executive dysfunction, anxiety, trauma, limited money, or low trust in institutions. The legal structure makes the disabled worker the petitioner and management the judge of reasonableness.

Collective bargaining changes the terrain. A union can negotiate predictable scheduling, remote work options, limits on mandatory overtime, sensory standards, breaks, anti-harassment procedures, transparent discipline, workload protections, and just-cause firing rules for everyone. These are neurodiversity issues even when not labeled as such. They reduce the need for humiliating individual exception. They also build solidarity between neurodivergent and neurotypical workers, because many so-called accommodations are simply humane working conditions. Written instructions help autistic workers and new workers. Flexible scheduling helps people with ADHD, parents, caregivers, and people with chronic illness. Quiet spaces help migraine sufferers and anyone trying to concentrate. Limits on surveillance help anxious workers and workers who object to being treated like machines.

Chapman’s essay gestures toward unionizing against neuronormativity, and this may be the most practical version of Neurodivergent Power. It avoids two traps. The first is corporate inclusion without power: the employer decides which neurodivergent traits are useful and which accommodations are tolerable. The second is rhetorical radicalism without a shop-floor strategy. Unions are imperfect, often bureaucratic, and not automatically attentive to disability. Historically, labor movements have sometimes prioritized a standard worker: male, full-time, able-bodied, steadily employed, and available for industrial discipline. Neurodivergent workers do not always fit that image. But that is precisely why disability politics should enter labor organizing and reshape it.

The modern workplace has become more hostile to many minds even as it speaks more fluently about inclusion. Open offices were sold as collaborative but often produce distraction and sensory overload. Digital communication multiplies channels—email, chat, video meetings, project management systems—creating constant task switching. Algorithmic management tracks keystrokes, delivery times, call length, customer ratings, and productivity metrics. Precarious employment makes workers afraid to disclose disabilities. “Culture fit” rewards social fluency and punishes difference. In service work, employees must perform cheerfulness under customer abuse. In warehouses, bodies and attention are timed to the second. In professional work, the laptop dissolves the boundary between job and life.

These conditions harm many workers, but they are especially punishing for neurodivergent people. An ADHD worker may be disciplined for lateness by a scheduling system that changes weekly. An autistic worker may be labeled rude for direct communication. A dyslexic worker may be shamed by software that treats spelling errors as carelessness. A worker with bipolar disorder may be fired after a crisis rather than supported through leave and return. A person with social anxiety may be denied promotion because advancement depends on networking rituals unrelated to competence. None of these problems will be solved by a poster celebrating Neurodiversity Week.

A labor-neurodiversity agenda would begin with universal design at work. Instead of asking, “What exception does this disabled worker need?” it would ask, “How should work be organized so more kinds of people can do it sustainably?” Job descriptions should distinguish essential functions from inherited habits. Hiring should reduce reliance on eye contact, small talk, and rapid verbal performance unless truly necessary. Instructions should be explicit. Meetings should have agendas and notes. Workplaces should offer quiet and collaborative zones. Schedules should be predictable where possible. Leave should be generous and nonpunitive. Performance evaluation should be transparent. Managers should be trained, but training should not substitute for enforceable rules.

The agenda must also include those outside paid employment. Chapman rightly emphasizes that many neurodivergent people are locked out of work entirely. A movement focused only on workplace inclusion will favor those already closest to employability. Labor politics must therefore be joined to welfare politics: disability benefits that do not trap people in poverty, health care not tied to employment, housing as a right, supported employment for those who want it, and no forced work requirements masquerading as empowerment. The dignity of workers and the dignity of nonworkers stand or fall together. If benefits are miserable, employers can exploit disabled workers’ desperation. If employment is the only route to social worth, those who cannot work are degraded.

Here Chapman’s critique of capitalism has bite. Capitalism does not merely exclude neurodivergent people; it defines normal cognition around market needs. The ideal worker is punctual, flexible, emotionally regulated, socially smooth, resilient under stress, able to multitask, and available for self-optimization. This is not “natural.” It is an economic character ideal. Neurodivergent people expose its narrowness because they often cannot or will not perform it. But many neurotypical workers are also crushed by the same ideal. Burnout, anxiety, depression, sleep deprivation, and stress-related illness are not confined to diagnosed minorities. Neurodivergent Power can become broad worker power if it targets the pace and discipline of work itself.

I am less persuaded by Chapman’s turn toward the revolutionary party. Neurodivergent people have reason to be skeptical of any politics that prizes discipline, uniform messaging, long meetings, charismatic speakers, and strategic hardness. Many radical organizations reproduce neuronormativity internally: inaccessible meetings, social cliques, jargon-heavy debate, contempt for inconsistency, and burnout framed as commitment. If the movement needs organization—and it does—it should experiment with forms that fit disabled life: hybrid participation, asynchronous decision-making, plain-language materials, rotating roles, care teams, conflict processes, and respect for fluctuating capacity. The form of organizing must be part of the politics.

Nor should we abandon law. The Americans with Disabilities Act and similar frameworks are limited, but they establish a floor. Unions can raise that floor. Public policy can widen it. Social movements can defend and reinterpret it. The choice is not liberal rights or collective power. Workers need the legal right to accommodation, the contractual power to enforce better conditions, and the political power to transform the economy. In practice these strategies reinforce one another. A union representative can help a worker request accommodations. A legal victory can set standards for bargaining. A strike can make visible needs that courts ignore.

The future of neurodiversity should be built in workplaces, but not as an HR initiative. It should be built through worker committees, disability caucuses in unions, alliances between self-advocates and care workers, campaigns against punitive productivity metrics, and bargaining demands that make work less hostile to human variation. It should learn from teachers resisting school discipline, nurses fighting unsafe staffing, warehouse workers challenging surveillance, graduate workers demanding mental health support, and public employees defending services. These struggles are already about neurodiversity, even when they use other names.

Chapman’s phrase “Neurodivergent Power” will matter only if it becomes institutional power: the power to say no to impossible workloads, to redesign jobs, to protect the slow and the inconsistent, to support those in crisis, to fund life outside employment, and to democratize decisions about technology and management. The neurodiversity movement began by challenging the idea that there is one normal mind. Its next step should be challenging the idea that there is one normal worker. A society that takes neurodiversity seriously will not merely hire a few unusual minds. It will change the tempo of work for everyone.
