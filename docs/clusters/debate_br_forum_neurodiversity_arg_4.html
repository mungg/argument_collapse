Diversified MiniMax

"While Chapman’s critique of 'Liberal Neurodiversity' is valid, a truly liberatory neurodiversity politics must move beyond a unified 'Neurodivergent Power' framework to address the conflicting needs, desires, and lived experiences within the diverse neurodivergent community."

Chapman's essay offers a compelling diagnosis of what he terms "Liberal Neurodiversity," and I find myself largely in agreement with its central critique. The movement has achieved genuine wins—legal protections, shifting social attitudes, spaces for neurodivergent pride—but as Chapman rightly observes, these victories have not reached everyone equally. The autistic person thriving in tech has a very different experience from the autistic person trapped in the school-to-incarceration pipeline. Acknowledging this disparity is essential.

Yet I want to push Chapman's analysis further by interrogating a tension that runs through the neurodiversity movement generally, including in his proposed alternative. Chapman calls for "collective Neurodivergent Power" and suggests that both neurotypicals and neurodivergents are "harmed by our market-oriented system." This framing is strategically useful for coalition-building, but it risks obscuring a fundamental question: who exactly is the subject of neurodiversity politics?

The danger of identity-based political frameworks is that they can flatten internal differences within the category they're meant to represent. "Neurodivergent" encompasses autism, ADHD, dyslexia, dyspraxia, Tourette's, and more—conditions that differ radically in their lived experience, their relationship to medical intervention, and their history of activism. An autistic person who values their neurology as intrinsic to their identity may have very different political needs and desires than someone with ADHD who experiences their condition primarily as a source of suffering and functional limitation. Do both belong to the same political "we"?

Chapman's essay gestures toward intersectionality but largely sidesteps this question. When he lists the concrete demands of his proposed movement—freeing incarcerated neurodivergents, ending homelessness, halting imperialist wars—he assumes a unified neurodivergent subject who would benefit from these changes. But the reality is messier. Some neurodivergent people, regardless of their other identities, may not want to be "freed" from carceral institutions if those institutions provide structures they find necessary for their functioning. Some may resist the ending of certain medical interventions that others view as coercive.

What I'm suggesting is not that Chapman's radical framework is wrong, but that any robust politics of Neurodivergent Power must grapple with the internal diversity of the people it purports to represent. Movements built on identity categories can be powerful organizing tools, but they can also become forms of soft tyranny, pressuring individuals to conform to collective identities that don't fit their experience. The Black Power movement that Chapman invokes as a model was itself critiqued by Black feminists and queer theorists within it for this very reason.

A truly liberatory neurodiversity politics would need to honor neurodivergent diversity in all its complexity—not just the diversity of neurological types, but the diversity of desires, needs, and visions for what a good life looks like. This might mean less emphasis on building a singular "Neurodivergent Power" movement and more emphasis on supporting a ecology of neurodivergent organizing, with space for autistic pride and for autistic critiques of conditions many find genuinely disabling, for ADHD communities seeking accommodation and for ADHD individuals who want treatment without stigma, for neurodiversity as identity and for neurodiversity as material condition requiring support. Chapman's essay is an important intervention; the work now is to ensure that a politics of liberation doesn't reproduce the very normalizing impulses it seeks to resist.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The broad category of 'neurodivergent' flattens significant differences between conditions that vary in their relationship to identity, medical intervention, and functional limitation.
  2. 02Chapman’s radical demands assume a unified political subject, overlooking that some neurodivergent individuals may have conflicting views on institutional structures or medical treatments.
  3. 03Identity-based political movements risk becoming forms of 'soft tyranny' by pressuring individuals to conform to a collective narrative that may not reflect their personal experience.
  4. 04A robust movement should shift from a singular 'Neurodivergent Power' model toward an 'ecology of organizing' that accommodates both identity-based pride and the material need for clinical support or treatment.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The autism population is highly heterogeneous, consisting of individuals with vastly different support needs, communication skills, and life stages that defy a singular representative voice.
  2. 02Deep ideological divides exist between autistic self-advocates and parent-led movements regarding whether autism should be accepted as a natural variation or treated as a disorder requiring a cure.
  3. 03Radical movements risk reproducing existing hierarchies if they are led primarily by educated, verbal activists while failing to meaningfully include those with severe cognitive disabilities or those from marginalized socioeconomic backgrounds.
  4. 04A truly inclusive movement must navigate the complex necessity of proxy representation for minors and those requiring intense support through allyship with parents and caregivers.
  5. 05It is unclear how a macro-level overhaul of economic and carceral systems will simultaneously address the specific, immediate needs of the most multiply marginalized neurodivergent individuals.
