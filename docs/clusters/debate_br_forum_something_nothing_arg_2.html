"The failure of counterinsurgency (COIN) in Afghanistan is not a failure of doctrine or individual soldiering, but a failure of institutional design that assigns tactical units impossible political and social tasks they are not structured to perform."

What Rosen captures better than most strategic commentary is the gap between doctrine and the emotional reality of war. Counterinsurgency manuals speak in calm imperatives: protect the population, understand the environment, use minimum force, build host-nation capacity. On Highway 601 those imperatives must pass through men who have been shot at, who have watched Afghan partners die, who do not trust the police they mentor, who are counting the days until they go home, and who suspect every culvert and compound wall may hide a bomb. The result is not hypocrisy so much as overload.

This is why the essay should be read not only as a critique of COIN but as a critique of institutional design. The United States has assigned tactical units a mission that no tactical unit can perform. A captain is asked to be diplomat, police trainer, development officer, intelligence analyst, anthropologist, mayor, judge, and combat leader. An infantry sergeant is expected to move instantly from “kill the enemy” to “reassure the elder.” A National Guard team mentors Afghan police who lack pay, radios, literacy, equipment, discipline, and sometimes loyalty. Then Washington debates whether “counterinsurgency works,” as if the doctrine itself were the operative unit of history.

Rosen concludes that the American military and policy establishments are incapable of doing COIN because they lack curiosity and empathy. That is too sweeping. Many soldiers and civilians in Afghanistan have shown both. Some have learned languages, returned for multiple tours, built relationships, and risked their lives to protect Afghans. Rosen’s own portrait of Captain Westby is more complicated than his thesis. Westby is exasperated, profane in private, and sometimes patronizing, but he also listens, restrains, negotiates, and tries to keep his Afghan partners alive. The problem is not that Americans are uniquely incurious. It is that the system makes curiosity episodic and empathy optional, while making rotation, force protection, career incentives, and bureaucratic metrics permanent.

Take rotations. A unit arrives, learns its area at great cost, forms relationships, and leaves. Its replacement inherits maps, briefings, and perhaps a few introductions, but not trust. Afghans, who live there permanently, watch foreigners cycle through with new acronyms and promises. Insurgents can wait. Police commanders can manipulate the newcomers. Villagers can hedge. In such an environment, “lessons learned” become a ritual phrase masking structural amnesia. If the United States were serious about counterinsurgency, it would create long-term district teams with repeated deployments to the same area, robust language training, civilian specialists, and continuity across years. Instead it has tried to make general-purpose units perform specialized political labor.

Consider metrics. Military organizations count what they can report: patrols conducted, weapons found, enemies killed or captured, police trained, projects completed, dollars spent. But the decisive variables in Rosen’s story are harder to measure: whether villagers believe the police will steal from them, whether a commander’s cousin is Taliban, whether a mullah regards the district governor as legitimate, whether a family feud is being misread as insurgency. Because these variables are difficult, they are often converted into simplified categories: Taliban, pro-government, neutral; cleared, held, built; trained, partnered, independent. The map becomes cleaner as reality becomes more misunderstood.

Force protection is another contradiction. COIN requires living among the people. Armies want to protect their soldiers. Both impulses are morally serious. No officer wants to tell a family that their son died because headquarters wanted a more authentic village presence. Yet if troops remain in large bases, they cannot protect civilians at night or understand local politics. When they patrol from armored vehicles, they appear alien and frightening. When they dismount, they become vulnerable to IEDs and ambushes. Soldiers respond with suspicion; suspicion produces rough treatment; rough treatment produces resentment; resentment aids insurgents. Doctrine identifies the cycle but cannot abolish the fear behind it.

Rosen also shows the impossibility of mentoring broken institutions from the bottom up. Team Prowler can urge Afghan police to wear body armor, carry ammunition, read maps, and avoid abusing civilians. But the policemen’s behavior reflects higher-level failures: recruitment, pay, logistics, leadership, justice, and political patronage. A policeman who is not paid will extort. A commander appointed through factional ties will prey on rivals. A court that releases criminals for bribes will demoralize honest police and encourage arbitrary punishment. Training a police unit without reforming the system around it is like teaching a man to swim and then dropping him back into sewage.

Still, Rosen’s essay risks fatalism. Institutions can change, though slowly and unevenly. The U.S. military of 2009 is not the military of 2001. It has absorbed painful lessons about civilian casualties, detainee abuse, cultural ignorance, and the limits of firepower. McChrystal’s rules of engagement, whatever their flaws, represent institutional learning. The growth of provincial reconstruction teams, human terrain efforts, agricultural development teams, and embedded training missions reflects an attempt—often clumsy—to adapt. Critics should not deny that adaptation simply because it is incomplete.

The real question is whether the scale of adaptation required exceeds the political time available. Here Rosen is strongest. A true COIN campaign in Afghanistan would require a decade or more of consistent effort, with high-quality civilian participation, long-term language expertise, police and judicial reform, regional diplomacy, and sustained public support at home. The United States is already eight years into the war, economically strained, politically divided, and led by a president who has announced both an escalation and the beginning of a drawdown in 2011. That is not the foundation for a generational campaign. It is a compromise between strategic anxiety and domestic impatience.

If the United States cannot build the institutions required for COIN, it should not pretend that courage at the small-unit level can compensate. Soldiers should not be sent to die for a theory their government is unwilling or unable to implement fully. Nor should Afghan civilians be asked to trust a protection force that may leave before the Taliban decide the contest. Honesty demands matching aims to capabilities.

What would institutional honesty mean? It would mean narrowing missions. If a unit is there to train police, give it the language, time, authority, and civilian legal support to affect police behavior beyond marksmanship. If it is there to secure a population, require persistent presence and accept the casualties and political costs that follow. If the country will not accept those costs, do not call the mission population protection. It would mean reducing reliance on short tours and creating area specialization. It would mean promoting officers for political understanding, not only command aggressiveness. It would mean integrating State Department, USAID, Justice, Agriculture, and intelligence personnel in ways that are more than decorative. And it would mean telling the American public that “success” may look like managed instability, not victory.

Rosen’s soldiers speak in ugly ways, but they are also trapped in an ugly design. The Afghan police “suck,” as one sergeant says, but that fact is not a personality defect; it is the visible symptom of a political order built on corruption and fear. The villagers hedge not because they are ungrateful but because hedging is rational. The Taliban endure not because they are militarily brilliant but because they are embedded in a landscape of grievances and sanctuaries. A doctrine cannot fix these things unless institutions are built to practice it over time.

The lesson of Rosen’s essay is therefore not simply “leave” or “surge.” It is: stop assigning impossible tasks to people at the bottom while evading responsibility at the top. If Washington wants counterinsurgency, it must build a government capable of counterinsurgency—ours first, then Afghanistan’s. If it will not, it should scale down the mission before more captains are asked to solve all the world’s problems with a translator, a radio, and a watermelon.

"The United States should pursue a deliberate military disengagement from Afghanistan because the population-centric counterinsurgency (COIN) strategy is fundamentally unworkable given the absence of a legitimate local partner and the institutional failures of the Afghan state."

Nir Rosen’s essay is most powerful not when it argues against counterinsurgency in the abstract, but when it shows what counterinsurgency becomes in the hands of tired men, frightened policemen, corrupt institutions, and villagers who have learned that every armed actor is dangerous. The details from Highway 601 do more to refute Washington’s Afghanistan debate than another stack of strategy papers. A doctrine that sounds humane in a field manual—protect the population, subordinate military action to politics, understand local grievances—becomes, in practice, a convoy, an interpreter, a handful of Afghan police who cannot read a map, and American soldiers who do not know whether the man in front of them is Taliban, farmer, victim, opportunist, or all four depending on the week.

Rosen is right that the United States is trying to create “something out of nothing.” That phrase may sound too sweeping. Afghanistan is not nothing: it has communities, clerics, markets, tribal and non-tribal networks, armed factions, memories, customs, and forms of authority that predate the American republic. But the “nothing” Rosen identifies is the thing Washington keeps pretending exists: a legitimate national partner capable of converting foreign military success into Afghan political order. That is what is missing. The central state is not merely weak; in many places it is predatory, irrelevant, or alien. The police do not merely need training; they often embody the insecurity from which villagers seek protection. Elections do not merely suffer fraud; they consecrate a political class that many Afghans experience as thieves with international backing.

The counterinsurgency debate in Washington often turns on a misleading question: can COIN work if done correctly? In principle, perhaps. If an outside power possesses deep local knowledge, decades of patience, fluency in local languages, disciplined troops, a legitimate indigenous partner, a functioning justice system, and a population that wants protection from insurgents more than it resents the protector, population-centric counterinsurgency may have a chance. But that list is not a strategy; it is a confession. The United States has almost none of these things in Afghanistan, and where it has fragments, they are not scalable. A good captain such as Westby can apologize to elders, share watermelon with policemen, and try to prevent arbitrary killings. But he rotates out. His replacement relearns the same village from scratch. The elders remain, as does the Taliban shadow structure, as do the police who steal sheep.

The defenders of the new strategy answer that Rosen’s evidence proves not that counterinsurgency is impossible but that it has not yet been tried with adequate resources. More troops, they say, will allow Americans and Afghans to live among the population, secure roads, prevent Taliban intimidation, and create space for governance. But Rosen’s reporting shows why this argument is circular. More troops require more Afghan partners; more Afghan partners require a state capable of recruiting, paying, disciplining, and legitimating them; that state does not exist; therefore the foreign troops must stay longer to build it. Each missing condition becomes a reason for deeper commitment.

The Iraq analogy is especially dangerous. The United States has turned the “Surge” into a parable about willpower: change generals, protect civilians, buy time, and violence declines. But Iraq’s violence had its own trajectory. The Sunni insurgency was exhausted by sectarian defeat and horrified by al Qaeda’s brutality. Shia parties had captured much of the state. Baghdad had been violently sorted. The United States did not create these facts, though it exploited them. Afghanistan offers no comparable moment. The Taliban have not been decisively defeated by a rival Afghan force; they are not generally hated in the way al Qaeda in Iraq came to be hated in Anbar; and the population is dispersed across rural terrain where “protecting the people” would require an impossible density of knowledge and presence.

Nor can the United States define the enemy coherently. Rosen notes that “Taliban” is often a category of convenience. It can mean ideological militants, local fighters, drug networks, angry relatives, paid gunmen, or men resisting abusive police. The broader the category becomes, the more enemies we create; the narrower it becomes, the less our military operations appear relevant to the political disorder we claim to be fixing. A young man with a ringtone can be sent to prison. A farmer whose water supply has been blocked must petition a foreign captain. A village becomes “bad” because insurgents pass through it at night. This is not population protection. It is armed confusion.

The strongest case for remaining in Afghanistan is that leaving may be ugly. The Taliban may take more districts. Afghan women, journalists, teachers, and local officials may be threatened. Men who worked with Americans may be killed. Pakistan may become more unstable. These are not trivial fears. But they should not be transformed into a blank check for a strategy that cannot deliver what it promises. The moral burden of withdrawal is real; so is the moral burden of staying. Every additional year of occupation means raids, air strikes, detainees, empowered warlords, corrupt police, and families forced to choose between collaboration and survival.

Rosen is also right to challenge the assumption that defeating the Taliban is necessary to defeat al Qaeda. The 9/11 attacks were planned by a transnational network that used Afghanistan as one sanctuary, not by the Taliban as a national government pursuing global war. Al Qaeda’s most important sanctuary now lies in Pakistan, where American troops cannot conduct a Helmand-style counterinsurgency. If the core American interest is preventing attacks on the United States, then the relevant tools are intelligence, police cooperation, financial tracking, border diplomacy, limited strikes when necessary, and a foreign policy that reduces rather than multiplies recruits for anti-American violence. None of that requires building a centralized Afghan state at gunpoint.

The United States should therefore begin a deliberate military disengagement, not a theatrical abandonment. It should stop promising Afghans a state we cannot build. It should pursue regional diplomacy that includes Pakistan, Iran, India, Russia, China, and Afghanistan’s neighbors, all of whom have interests in preventing total chaos. It should support negotiations among Afghan factions, including Taliban elements willing to accept local power-sharing arrangements. It should redirect aid away from large contractor-driven projects and toward modest, accountable humanitarian assistance, especially in health, agriculture, and refugee support. It should offer asylum or protection to Afghans most endangered by their association with us. And it should maintain the capacity to disrupt transnational terrorist plots without pretending that every Taliban checkpoint is a threat to New York.

The hardest thing for Washington to admit is that some problems are not soluble by American power. Afghanistan is not a blank slate awaiting our doctrine. It is a country whose people have endured monarchy, communism, jihad, civil war, Taliban rule, invasion, and international reconstruction schemes. They will survive our departure, though not without suffering; they are also suffering through our presence. Rosen’s essay forces us to look at the war as Afghans on Highway 601 might see it: not as a contest between enlightened counterinsurgency and extremism, but as a bewildering succession of armed men demanding loyalty and promising protection they cannot provide. That is not a war we can win. It is a war we should stop enlarging.

Rosen's essay achieves something rare in discussions of American foreign policy: genuine engagement with the realities of military operations as experienced by those who conduct them. His reporting from Helmand province provides insight that strategic documents and official assessments consistently obscure. This is valuable journalism, and its findings deserve attention from anyone seeking to understand why American strategy in Afghanistan has struggled to achieve its objectives.

The essay's central argument—that America is attempting "something from nothing" in Afghanistan—captures a fundamental truth about the limits of military power in deeply divided societies lacking institutional foundations. The assumption that sufficient resources and appropriate strategy can overcome these structural limitations represents a form of magical thinking that Rosen's reporting systematically challenges.

His account of the Afghan security forces reveals dysfunction that official reports consistently minimize. The drug test results, equipment shortages, and popular alienation that Rosen documents suggest not merely implementation failures but fundamental problems with the train-and-equip approach that underpins American exit strategy. If the Afghan police cannot be made competent with current methods, and if alternative methods are not being developed, then the entire framework of transition crumbles.

The comparison with Iraq produces sharp insight. Rosen correctly identifies that the Surge succeeded partly because conditions had already exhausted the insurgency's momentum before American reinforcements arrived. The Sunni Awakening reflected Sunni recognition that they had been defeated and saw American alliance as their best remaining option. This dynamic had no parallel in Afghanistan, where the Taliban had not been decisively defeated and where American operations continued to produce civilian casualties that fed insurgent recruitment.

Rosen's institutional critique of the American military deserves endorsement. The gap between COIN doctrine and practice reflects not individual failures but systemic dysfunction. The military's culture, training, and operational rhythms remain oriented toward conventional warfare despite years of counterinsurgency operations. Rosen's observation that soldiers joke about "doing that COIN shit" captures the distance between official doctrine and actual behavior.

The author's analysis of al-Qaeda's relationship with the Taliban deserves particular attention. If the Taliban's alliance with al-Qaeda reflects "convenience, not ideology," then strategic calculations require adjustment. The original justification for the Afghanistan war—denying al-Qaeda safe haven—has been stretched to include objectives that may not serve American interests and that certainly exceed the original mandate. Rosen's suggestion that American presence strengthens rather than weakens terrorist networks deserves serious engagement.

However, the essay's implicit recommendation of withdrawal requires development that Rosen does not provide. The author never explicitly argues for American departure, perhaps recognizing that such a position remains politically untenable. Yet his analysis points consistently in this direction, creating tension between what he argues and what he implies. A more honest essay would explicitly state its conclusions and defend them directly.

The assumption that terrorist movements are purely reactive to American policy ignores their own ideological commitments and strategic objectives.

Despite these limitations, Rosen's essay represents essential reading for anyone seeking to understand why American strategy in Afghanistan has failed to achieve its objectives. The specific failures he documents—corrupt police, civilian casualties, electoral fraud, institutional dysfunction—are real and require acknowledgment. Whatever policy direction one ultimately favors, this account demands engagement. The comfortable assumption that more resources, better strategy, and sufficient resolve will eventually produce success has no basis in the evidence Rosen presents.

"Nir Rosen’s assessment of the Afghanistan conflict demonstrates that the American strategy is a failure based on a fundamental disconnect between Washington's strategic ambitions and the operational realities on the ground."

After eight years of American involvement in Afghanistan, Nir Rosen has provided what may be the most honest assessment of that conflict's reality. His essay demonstrates why policy discussions in Washington so often seem disconnected from conditions on the ground, and why the gap between strategic ambition and operational reality has grown so dangerously wide.

The core of Rosen's argument is irresistible: the United States military is attempting to build a centralized state in Afghanistan when no such entity has ever existed there, using counterinsurgency methods developed for fundamentally different contexts, while relying on Afghan security forces that remain incapable of standing on their own. The mathematical impossibility of these combined objectives should be apparent to anyone willing to examine them honestly.

Rosen's account of the Afghan police reveals the hollowness at the heart of American strategy. When police commanders cannot read maps, when sixty percent of officers in some districts test positive for narcotics, when police are more feared by civilians than the Taliban themselves, the entire framework of "train and transfer" collapses. The American approach assumes that additional resources, better training, and more mentoring will produce effective security forces. Rosen's reporting suggests this assumption rests on fantasy rather than evidence.

The election fraud documented in the essay deserves particular emphasis. When turnout in the south falls below ten percent and systematic fraud renders the results meaningless, the assumption that legitimate governance can emerge from democratic processes proves false. Rosen is correct that "the failed elections were a message to Afghans that there was no hope of improvement or change." This message, more than any tactical development, will shape Afghanistan's future.

The author's analysis of COIN doctrine's institutional challenges within the American military deserves wide attention. The gap between what the counterinsurgency manual prescribes and what actually happens at the squad level reflects not individual failures but systemic dysfunction. When soldiers joke about "doing that COIN shit" while privately focusing on survival, the doctrine's political factors have no chance of achieving primacy.

The comparison with Iraq is particularly illuminating. Rosen correctly identifies that the Surge succeeded partly because conditions had already exhausted the insurgency's momentum. The Sunnis were defeated; they turned to Americans as the least worst option. No such exhaustion exists in Afghanistan, where the Taliban are not losing and where their nationalist appeal remains intact. Applying Iraq lessons to Afghanistan commits the classic error of assuming that historical analogies trump current conditions.

Perhaps most importantly, Rosen challenges the assumption that defeating the Taliban serves American interests. His argument that al-Qaeda is already established in Pakistan, that the Taliban are primarily nationalist rather than jihadist, and that American presence strengthens rather than weakens terrorist networks deserves serious engagement. The original justification for the Afghanistan war—the need to deny al-Qaeda safe havens—has been progressively stretched to include regime change, nation-building, and counterinsurgency, each additional justification diluting the original rationale.

The essay's implicit conclusion is that American withdrawal remains the only viable option. Rosen never states this directly, perhaps recognizing that such a recommendation remains politically impossible in early 2010. But his analysis points clearly in this direction. If the mission cannot succeed, if the costs outweigh the benefits, and if continued presence only strengthens the forces America seeks to defeat, then the rational choice is to leave.

The remaining question is whether withdrawal can be managed responsibly. Rosen's analysis suggests that a sudden departure would create chaos, but a gradual drawdown tied to verifiable Afghan capacity might create space for political accommodation. The Taliban are not ideologically committed to global jihad; they want foreign forces removed and Islamic governance established. These goals might be achievable through negotiation rather than military victory.

For American policymakers, Rosen's essay should prompt fundamental reassessment. The comfortable assumption that more resources, better strategy, and sufficient resolve will eventually produce success has no basis in evidence. The path forward requires either a dramatically different approach or an honest acknowledgment that the mission cannot be accomplished at acceptable cost.

"Nir Rosen’s essay correctly identifies that the U.S. counterinsurgency strategy in Afghanistan is a failure driven by 'magical thinking' and a fundamental misunderstanding of the country's structural and institutional realities."

Rosen's essay illuminates the dark corners of American strategy in Afghanistan that official discourse prefers to ignore. His on-the-ground reporting reveals the gap between what counterinsurgency doctrine promises and what American forces actually accomplish, between strategic objectives and operational realities. This is valuable journalism, and policymakers would do well to attend to its findings.

The essay's central insight—that America is attempting "something from nothing" in Afghanistan—captures a fundamental truth about the limits of military power in deeply divided societies lacking institutional foundations for centralized governance. The assumption that sufficient resources and correct strategy can overcome these structural limitations represents a form of magical thinking that Rosen's reporting systematically demolishes.

His account of the Afghan police deserves particular attention. Rosen documents a force that cannot read maps, that operates under corruption so pervasive it has become invisible to its participants, that is more feared by civilians than the insurgents it supposedly combats. The drug test results—sixty percent positive in some districts—suggest an institution that has not merely failed but collapsed entirely as a functioning organization. Against this backdrop, McChrystal's plan to expand Afghan security forces to 400,000 personnel appears not merely ambitious but delusional.

The comparison with Iraq proves Rosen's sharpest analytical point. The Surge succeeded not because American strategy was brilliant but because conditions had already exhausted the insurgency's momentum. The Sunni Awakening reflected Sunni recognition that they had been defeated in civil war, not American persuasion. The American military could position itself as a relatively benign actor because Shia militias had demonstrated their willingness to commit atrocities that dwarfed anything the Americans had done. No comparable dynamic exists in Afghanistan, where the Taliban have not been defeated and where American operations regularly produce civilian casualties that feed insurgent propaganda.

Rosen's institutional critique of the American military deserves endorsement. The gap between COIN doctrine and practice reflects not individual failures but systemic dysfunction. The military's culture, training, promotion systems, and operational rhythms remain oriented toward conventional warfare despite a decade of counterinsurgency operations. The bureaucracy that Rosen identifies as "dominated by old-school army thinking" has resisted adaptation not through malice but through institutional inertia that no amount of new manuals or strategic directives can overcome.

The author's analysis of al-Qaeda's relationship with the Taliban deserves careful consideration. If Rosen is correct that this alliance reflects "convenience, not ideology," then the strategic logic of American intervention requires reexamination. The original justification for the Afghanistan war was denying al-Qaeda safe haven, not defeating the Taliban regime. If al-Qaeda is already established in Pakistan, and if the Taliban are primarily concerned with national rather than international objectives, then the costs of American presence may exceed the benefits.

However, Rosen's essay would benefit from greater attention to what withdrawal would actually produce. The implicit assumption that American departure would create space for accommodation between Kabul and the Taliban ignores the Taliban's own strategic objectives and the likely behavior of regional powers in a post-American Afghanistan. Pakistan, with its historical support for Taliban forces and its ongoing security concerns, would likely fill any vacuum created by American withdrawal. Iran might similarly increase its involvement. The result might not be negotiated accommodation but intensified regional competition.

Rosen correctly identifies that "the failed elections were a message to Afghans that there was no hope of improvement or change." This message, combined with continuing civilian casualties from American operations, undermines whatever support for the Karzai government might have existed. But the solution to legitimacy failure is not American withdrawal alone but rather fundamental restructuring of American objectives and methods.

The essay's vision of what "addressing these problems at their roots" might entail remains vague. Rosen mentions American support for Israel and Middle Eastern dictators as grievances that fuel terrorist movements, but does not explain how changing these policies would affect strategic calculations in Afghanistan. The assumption that American retrenchment in the Middle East would reduce terrorist threats to American personnel in Afghanistan deserves explicit argument rather than passing reference.

Despite these limitations, Rosen's essay represents essential reading for anyone seeking to understand why American strategy in Afghanistan has failed to achieve its objectives. The official narratives of progress and momentum cannot survive contact with the realities he documents. Whatever policy direction one ultimately favors, this account demands engagement.
