The Unfair Indictment of Preparedness

Sheila Jasanoff’s indictment of pandemic preparedness is as elegantly argued as it is profoundly mistaken. She contends that the conceptual framework of preparedness—rooted in prediction, metrics, and a can-do militaristic spirit—failed us, and that we must now abandon this hubristic posture for a new ethic of humility, complete with its own institutional machinery. But this misreads the tragedy of the past two years. The fault lies not in the idea of preparedness but in its political sabotage and selective application. The Global Health Security Index, the Obama-era playbook, and the expert advice that urged a rapid, coordinated response were not wrong in principle; they were ignored, undermined, and left to wither under an administration that treated the pandemic as a public relations problem. What we need now is not a retreat into epistemic modesty but a recommitment to the very tools of prediction and planning that, when properly resourced and protected from partisan assault, still offer the best chance of averting catastrophe.

Jasanoff’s central exhibit is the yawning gap between the GHSI’s top rankings and the death tolls in the United States and the United Kingdom. She takes this as proof that the index’s entire conceptual apparatus—its 6 categories, 34 indicators, and 140 questions—was fundamentally flawed, too enamored of “measurable indicators” and blind to politics. But look more closely. The GHSI measured capacities, not outcomes. It correctly identified that the United States had a vast pharmaceutical industry, advanced laboratory networks, and a highly trained public health workforce. Those assets were real. They did not, however, automatically translate into lives saved because a presidential administration refused to activate them in a coherent way, because decades of disinvestment in local public health had hollowed out the institutions that the index’s indicators assumed were functional, and because the index could not—and should not—predict that a sitting president would publicly muse about injecting bleach. These are failures of governance, not of the idea that we should systematically assess our strengths and weaknesses.

The playbook Jasanoff cites suffered the same fate. The Trump administration not only ignored it; senior officials later admitted they didn’t know it existed. The failure was not that the playbook assumed an “ordered democracy dating from a less divisive time.” It was that the country’s leadership did not even try to follow its step-by-step guidance on everything from declaring an emergency to coordinating supply chains. If anything, the playbook underestimated the political malevolence that would greet a pandemic. That is a call to update the political assumptions of our planning, not to jettison the very enterprise. The lesson is to build preparedness plans that are resilient in the face of a polarized public, to depoliticize public health agencies through stronger legal firewalls, and to invest in public communication infrastructure that can compete with viral misinformation. Humility, by contrast, offers no such concrete program. Telling policymakers to “adopt the perspectives of those who are acted upon” is an admirable sentiment, but in the middle of an outbreak, decision-makers need decision tools—dashboards, command structures, and supply chain models—that can operate at speed. They cannot convene a deliberative forum on vulnerability before every mask mandate.

The essay’s most troubling implication is that confidence in science and prediction is itself part of the problem. Jasanoff approvingly quotes Donald Rumsfeld’s typology to suggest that we fixate on “known unknowns” while ignoring deeper “unknown unknowns.” But it was precisely our capacity to convert unknown unknowns into known ones that delivered the fastest vaccine development in human history. The mRNA vaccines were not a product of humility; they were a triumph of prediction, built on decades of modeling viral spike proteins, anticipating the threat of novel coronaviruses, and investing in platform technologies that would be ready when the unknown emerged. That success story is the true face of preparedness, and it vindicates the approach Jasanoff dismisses. If we had simply adopted a posture of humble precaution, endlessly weighing worst-case scenarios before acting, we would never have greenlit Operation Warp Speed, never pre-purchased billions of doses while they were still in trials, and never achieved the immunological miracle that is now the single bright thread in this dark saga.

None of this is to deny that social and political factors matter, or that scientists and planners must listen more carefully to marginalized communities. But those imperatives complement preparedness; they do not replace it. The real path forward is not to discard the project of building national and global capacity for rapid, evidence-driven response. It is to build political coalitions that will defend those capacities, to design institutions that are more resilient to the incompetence or malice of elected leaders, and to invest heavily in the social science that can make our predictions smarter about the human terrain. Humility, in Jasanoff’s rendering, is a mood, not a method. After 740,000 American deaths, we need more method, not more mood.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The discrepancy between high preparedness rankings and poor pandemic outcomes reflects a failure of governance and political activation rather than a failure of the metrics themselves.
  2. 02The Obama-era pandemic playbook failed not because its systematic approach was outdated or hubristic, but because the subsequent administration actively ignored and undermined its guidance.
  3. 03The rapid development of mRNA vaccines serves as a definitive vindication of the predictive and 'can-do' preparedness model that Jasanoff critiques.
  4. 04While social and political factors are important, they should be integrated into existing preparedness methods rather than used as a reason to replace them with a vague 'ethic of humility.'
  5. 05Effective crisis management requires concrete decision tools and command structures that can operate at speed, which a posture of humility cannot provide.
