"Effective altruism is a flawed moral framework because it treats global poverty as a natural disaster to be managed through individual donations rather than a structural injustice that is often perpetuated by the very economic systems givers use to accumulate their wealth."

**Earning to Give, or Laundering a System?**

Peter Singer's essay is a recruiting pitch wrapped in a syllogism. The syllogism: suffering is bad, you can prevent some of it for a few thousand dollars, therefore you ought to. The recruiting pitch: come to Wall Street, become a quant, send the surplus to the Against Malaria Foundation, and you too can be a hero. I want to take the pitch seriously, because I think it is doing real harm to the moral imagination of a generation of bright young people.

Start with the figure of Matt Wage. Singer presents him as a triumph: a philosophy major who turned down Oxford to take an arbitrage job and now donates six figures a year to charity. But what is arbitrage? It is the extraction of small price differentials in financial markets. The most generous defense is that it makes markets more efficient. The less generous, and probably more accurate, description is that it is a zero-sum activity in which clever people armed with capital and infrastructure transfer money from less clever people to themselves. Whatever its merits, it is not the work of a humanitarian. To treat the income as morally clean — as if the only ethical question were what to do with the proceeds — is to accept a strange premise: that the economic system that produced the surplus is beyond moral evaluation, and only the disposal of the surplus is up for debate.

This is not a small philosophical quibble. It is the central political question of our time. The same global economy that allows an American twenty-five-year-old to "save a hundred lives" with his bonus is the economy whose trade rules, tax havens, intellectual property regimes, and capital flows make it cheaper to die of malaria in Lilongwe than to live. Effective altruism treats poverty as if it were a natural disaster — a hurricane that happens to children in distant countries, which we good people must mop up after with our donations. It is not a hurricane. It is a structure, and the structure has beneficiaries. Some of those beneficiaries are now writing checks to GiveWell.

I am not the first to make this point, and Singer would no doubt reply that effective altruists are perfectly free to give to structural causes too. But notice what is conspicuously missing from his essay: any mention of labor unions, of tax justice campaigns, of land reform, of movements to lower the cost of essential medicines, of organizing among the poor themselves. The examples are all of donors and recipients, of givers and gratitude. The poor appear in Singer's argument as objects of rescue, never as agents of their own liberation. This is the oldest pathology of philanthropy, dressed up in the language of evidence-based optimization.

There is also a more subtle ideological move. Singer wants to reorient ethics around the question: what should the privileged individual do with his or her surplus? But the prior question, the one that political philosophy used to take seriously, is why some people have such enormous surpluses while others have none. By making the surplus a starting point rather than a problem, effective altruism converts a question of justice into a question of consumer choice. It treats the structure of wealth as given, and asks only how generously to redistribute its scraps. Lenin called this "bourgeois philanthropy" a century ago, and the term still fits.

I will grant Singer this: the warm-glow critique is real. Americans really do give too little, and what they give they often give thoughtlessly. The Make-A-Wish comparison is, in its own terms, telling. A movement that pressures donors to look at evidence is better than a movement that doesn't. If effective altruism's only effect were to shift marginal philanthropic dollars from sentimental causes to high-impact ones, I would applaud it.

But effective altruism is not just a giving guide. It is, increasingly, a worldview, taught in seminars at elite universities, channeling some of the most idealistic young people of their generation into finance and consulting on the theory that this is what altruism now requires. It tells them not to organize, not to agitate, not to question the source of their salaries, but to optimize. It is, in its quiet way, one of the great conservative ideologies of our time — a doctrine that asks the privileged to be generous so that they need not be just. We should expect more from our ethics, and more from our young.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'earning to give' model ignores the moral cost of how wealth is acquired, treating zero-sum financial activities like arbitrage as ethically neutral sources of humanitarian funding.
  2. 02By focusing exclusively on givers and recipients, the movement strips the poor of their agency and ignores political solutions like labor unions, tax justice, and land reform.
  3. 03Effective altruism converts questions of systemic justice into questions of individual consumer choice, accepting the current distribution of wealth as a given rather than a problem to be solved.
  4. 04The movement functions as a conservative ideology that directs idealistic young people into elite financial sectors, discouraging them from challenging the status quo or organizing for structural change.
