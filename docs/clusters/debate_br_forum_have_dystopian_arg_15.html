"The threat AI poses to democracy and civil liberties is more fundamental than its threat to the labor market, as the preservation of a protected space for dissent and organization is the necessary condition for any humane response to the future of work."

Daron Acemoglu’s essay is framed around two dangers: AI may undermine good jobs, and AI may undermine democracy. I agree with both concerns, but I would reverse the emphasis. The threat to democracy and liberty is not merely one danger among others. It is the condition that determines whether any humane labor-market response is possible. A society that permits ubiquitous surveillance, behavioral manipulation, and opaque algorithmic governance will not retain the civic capacity to bargain over the future of work.

The labor consequences of AI are serious. But people can debate wages, training, taxation, and employment policy only if they possess a protected space for dissent and organization. Surveillance attacks that space. It changes not only what governments and companies know, but how citizens behave. A person who believes every conversation, association, search, location, purchase, and facial expression may be recorded and analyzed is less likely to protest, unionize, leak misconduct, join an unpopular movement, or experiment with dissenting ideas. Democracy cannot survive if citizens internalize the discipline of being watched.

The most troubling feature of AI surveillance is that it merges state and corporate power. Older debates about privacy often imagined government intrusion on one side and private choice on the other. That distinction is now inadequate. Private firms collect data at a scale intelligence agencies once could only dream of. Governments purchase or subpoena that data, contract with vendors, and rely on privately developed tools. Platforms moderate and amplify political speech in ways that shape public life. Employers monitor workers with technologies derived from consumer analytics and security research. The infrastructure of surveillance is hybrid.

China shows the authoritarian version of this system most clearly. Facial recognition, online monitoring, predictive policing, and mass data integration have been used to suppress dissent and control minority populations, especially in Xinjiang. But it would be a mistake for Americans to treat this as a foreign pathology. The United States has its own surveillance architecture: the NSA programs revealed by Edward Snowden; local police use of facial recognition; immigration enforcement aided by databases; private data brokers; platform microtargeting; workplace monitoring; and the routine extraction of personal information through apps and connected devices. The American version is more fragmented and commercialized, but it is dangerous precisely because it can grow without a single dramatic authoritarian decree.

Acemoglu rightly mentions Cambridge Analytica, but the scandal’s lesson is broader than one firm’s misconduct. The deeper problem is that the digital advertising model turns personal vulnerability into a commodity. Platforms profit by predicting and influencing behavior. Political campaigns, corporations, and foreign actors all operate within that incentive structure. Even when no law is broken, the system encourages emotional targeting, outrage, polarization, and informational segregation. AI does not need to create perfect persuasion to damage democracy. It only needs to make manipulation cheap, scalable, and deniable.

This means that “AI for good” is too weak a framework. We do not primarily need benevolent applications sprinkled into a harmful system. We need prohibitions. Some uses of AI should be banned or sharply restricted, just as liberal societies ban certain weapons, searches, and forms of discrimination. A democracy should not allow real-time facial recognition in public spaces except under the narrowest emergency conditions. It should not allow emotion recognition in schools, workplaces, or policing. It should not allow predictive policing systems that reinforce historical patterns of surveillance and then disguise them as neutral risk scores. It should not allow employers to track workers continuously without collective bargaining and strict necessity. It should not allow political campaigns to use intimate behavioral profiles for manipulative microtargeting.

A rights-based AI regime would begin with data minimization. Most data should never be collected in the first place. Consent, as currently practiced, is largely fiction: no ordinary person can read every privacy policy, understand every downstream use, or negotiate with every platform. The burden should be on institutions to justify collection, retention, inference, and sharing. Sensitive biometric data should receive special protection. Location data should not be casually bought and sold. Children’s data should be treated as presumptively off limits for behavioral advertising and algorithmic profiling.

Second, algorithmic systems that affect rights and opportunities should be auditable. If an AI system influences bail, sentencing, hiring, credit, housing, insurance, education, medical treatment, immigration, or access to public benefits, individuals must have notice, explanation, and appeal. Trade secrecy cannot override due process. A person denied a job or benefit by an opaque model is not meaningfully free. The right to contest automated decisions should be as basic as the right to challenge a bureaucratic error.

Third, public procurement should become a democratic choke point. Governments are major buyers of surveillance technology. They should not be able to acquire facial recognition, spyware, predictive policing tools, or social media monitoring systems through obscure contracts. Procurement should require public notice, legislative authorization, civil-rights impact assessments, independent testing, and sunset clauses. Communities most affected by policing and surveillance should have a voice before adoption, not merely after abuse.

Fourth, platform power must be confronted. The largest digital platforms are not simply private companies; they are governors of speech environments. They decide what is amplified, what is buried, what counts as authenticity, what is monetized, and what political advertisers may do. This does not mean the state should dictate content decisions. That would create its own threat to liberty. But democratic law can require transparency about recommendation systems, limit targeted political advertising, mandate researcher access to platform data under privacy-protective conditions, and support interoperability so that users are not trapped in a few privately governed public squares.

Acemoglu’s discussion of research norms is important, but norms alone cannot restrain surveillance capitalism. Some engineers have protested military contracts, facial recognition sales, and unethical data practices. Their courage matters. But the structure of incentives is overwhelming. A researcher who refuses to build a tool may be replaced by another; a company that pauses sales may resume later; a university that adopts an ethics statement may still accept corporate funding. Moral awakening must be translated into law.

There is also a danger in measuring AI’s social value mainly through labor share. A technology can increase labor’s share while still expanding surveillance, discrimination, or state control. Conversely, a privacy-enhancing technology may have ambiguous short-term labor effects but enormous democratic value. The labor question is vital, but civil liberties require their own independent metrics: data exposure, coercion, error rates across groups, chilling effects, contestability, concentration of power, and compatibility with constitutional rights.

The international dimension strengthens the case for democratic limits rather than weakening it. Authoritarian governments will continue developing surveillance AI. Democracies cannot stop them simply by setting a good example. But they can shape global markets. Export controls can limit the sale of spyware, biometric identification systems, and repression-enabling tools to abusive regimes. Sanctions can target companies and officials involved in digital repression. Democratic alliances can establish standards for procurement, data protection, and human rights. Universities and investors can scrutinize partnerships that facilitate surveillance.

Most importantly, democracies must preserve the moral distinction between lawful security and mass control. National security agencies will argue that they need advanced AI to counter terrorism, espionage, cyberattacks, and foreign interference. Some of that is true. But secret capability must be bounded by public law. Warrants, minimization rules, inspectors general, adversarial courts, and legislative oversight are not inconveniences; they are what distinguish democratic intelligence from authoritarian surveillance.

The future of work will be shaped by the future of freedom. Workers cannot bargain if employers can monitor every gesture and predict organizing activity. Citizens cannot demand redistribution if political campaigns can target them with personalized fear and falsehood. Journalists cannot expose abuses if spyware can compromise their phones. Minority groups cannot participate equally if facial recognition and predictive policing place them under permanent suspicion.

Acemoglu is right that AI’s future does not have to be dystopian. But dystopia will not arrive first as a robot uprising or sudden mass unemployment. It will arrive as convenience: faster logins, safer streets, optimized workplaces, personalized feeds, frictionless services, efficient welfare administration. Each tool will be defended as useful. Many will be useful. That is why the line must be drawn politically, before surveillance becomes the taken-for-granted condition of modern life.

A democratic AI agenda should start from a simple premise: there are parts of human life that should not be optimized by institutions seeking profit or control. Privacy is not nostalgia. Anonymity is not suspicious. Dissent is not a data problem. If AI is to serve democracy, democracy must first forbid AI from colonizing the spaces where free citizens are formed.

  2. 02The modern surveillance architecture is a dangerous hybrid of state and corporate power that commodifies personal vulnerability and enables manipulation through digital advertising models.
  3. 03A rights-based AI regime must move beyond 'AI for good' frameworks to include strict prohibitions on harmful applications like real-time facial recognition and emotion recognition.
