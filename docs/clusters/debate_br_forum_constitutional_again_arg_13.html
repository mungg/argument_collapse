Diversified Gemini

"Progressives should reject the call to 're-constitutionalize' their political agenda because framing policy preferences as constitutional mandates legitimizes judicial supremacy and shifts the terrain of struggle away from democratic majoritarianism toward a legalistic framework that historically favors conservative elites."

Joseph Fishkin, William E. Forbath, Andrea Scoseria Katz, Aziz Rana, and Mark Tushnet have issued a provocative, deeply historical, and passionately argued call for progressives to reclaim the Constitution. They urge the left to stop viewing constitutional law as a pristine, apolitical domain managed exclusively by courts, and to instead embrace a “democracy-of-opportunity” tradition. In this vision, the Constitution is not merely a set of negative liberties but a mandate for an egalitarian political economy. While the authors’ historical account of this lost tradition is compelling, their strategic prescription—that progressives should counter conservative juristocracy by making their own aggressive, substantive constitutional claims—is a dangerous trap.

The central problem with “re-constitutionalizing” progressive politics is that it inherently legitimizes the very institutional mechanisms and modes of argument that conservatives have used, and will continue to use, to thwart democratic majorities. By arguing that the Constitution *compels* certain economic arrangements, progressives would be endorsing the premise that the Constitution is the ultimate arbiter of political economy. But the Constitution is notoriously difficult to amend, and its interpretation in our system is heavily, perhaps inevitably, mediated by the judiciary. Even if progressives make these arguments primarily in the political sphere, framing policy preferences as constitutional mandates fundamentally shifts the terrain of debate away from democratic majoritarianism and toward legalistic essentialism.

Consider the history of the Supreme Court, which the authors acknowledge is currently an “archconservative” body. With the brief exception of the Warren Court—a historical anomaly that has heavily distorted liberal legal thinking—the Supreme Court has functioned primarily as a reactionary institution. From the protection of slavery in *Dred Scott* to the invalidation of civil rights legislation in the late nineteenth century, from the *Lochner* era’s hostility to labor to today’s weaponized First Amendment, the Court’s overarching historical role has been to protect entrenched capital and traditional hierarchies against democratic encroachment. When progressives argue that the Constitution mandates progressive policies, they are playing a game on a board designed by and for their opponents.

The authors point to the Affordable Care Act (ACA), arguing that liberals failed by merely defending the ACA as constitutionally *permissible* rather than constitutionally *required*. But what happens if we accept the premise that the Constitution dictates specific forms of social insurance? If a progressive Congress passes universal healthcare and declares it constitutionally required to prevent oligarchy, they are establishing a framework where economic policy is a matter of fundamental constitutional right. If a subsequent conservative Congress is elected and repeals that healthcare program, progressives would presumably argue that the repeal is unconstitutional. This asks the judiciary to step in and override the elected branches on the core matters of taxing and spending. It asks courts to act as super-legislatures. This is precisely the anti-democratic overreach that progressives rightly condemn when conservatives engage in it.

The left’s critique of the current Supreme Court should not be that the justices have the *wrong* vision of constitutional political economy, but that they have *any* judicially enforceable vision of constitutional political economy at all. The conservative weaponization of the First Amendment in cases like *Citizens United* or *Janus* is abhorrent not just because it favors capital over labor, but because it invents constitutional barriers to the people’s ability to govern themselves through their elected representatives. The antidote to *Lochnerism*—whether the 1905 version or the 2022 version—is not left-wing *Lochnerism*. The antidote is democratic deference.

Furthermore, the authors’ strategy risks alienating the very voters progressives need to build a durable majority. Most Americans do not view healthcare, labor rights, or antitrust enforcement through the arcane lens of constitutional theory. They view them in material terms: higher wages, lower costs, and fairer markets. When political debates are transformed into constitutional debates, the discourse inevitably becomes elite-driven. It empowers lawyers, law professors, and judges at the expense of organizers, workers, and everyday citizens. The labor movement of the 1930s did not win the Wagner Act merely because they had a better constitutional theory of the Commerce Clause; they won it because millions of workers went on strike and created an untenable crisis for industrial capital. The power was in the streets and the factories, not in constitutional treatises.

The authors rightly note that conservatives never accepted the post-war liberal consensus that separated law from politics and economics from both. Conservatives built a formidable movement to install judges who would read a libertarian political economy into the Constitution. But the conservative success in the courts is largely due to the structural advantages the right enjoys in the American political system—specifically, the malapportionment of the Senate and the Electoral College, which allows a conservative minority to consistently capture the judiciary. Reclaiming the rhetoric of constitutionalism does nothing to alter this structural imbalance.

If progressives want to overcome the conservative juristocracy, they must focus on structural democratic reforms, not constitutional re-interpretation. This means prioritizing the abolition of the filibuster, the expansion of voting rights, and, crucially, the structural reform of the federal judiciary itself. Ideas such as court-packing, jurisdiction stripping, and term limits for Supreme Court justices are far more appropriate responses to a rogue judiciary than attempting to persuade that same judiciary, or the public, of a novel progressive constitutionalism.

We must also recognize that the Constitution, as a document, is deeply flawed and profoundly undemocratic. It was drafted by propertied elites who were terrified of the populist, redistributive impulses of the state legislatures under the Articles of Confederation. The entire system of checks and balances, bicameralism, and judicial review was designed to slow down democratic action and protect minority rights—where "minority" primarily meant the wealthy minority. To try and squeeze a radical, egalitarian political economy out of a document designed to protect property and constrain mass democracy is an exercise in futility.

The "democracy-of-opportunity" tradition the authors celebrate was essentially a series of attempts by popular movements to overcome the Constitution’s structural biases. When Jacksonians or New Dealers succeeded, they did so by asserting the primacy of democratic politics over constitutional formalism. The lesson we should draw from the New Deal is not that Franklin Roosevelt had a better constitutional theory, but that he was willing to threaten the Court with structural destruction (the court-packing plan) if it did not back down and allow the democratic branches to govern.

In the areas the authors highlight—the First Amendment, race and political economy, and antitrust—the most effective progressive strategy is straightforward legislative action combined with a forceful demand that courts stand down. We do not need to argue that campaign finance reform is constitutionally required; we only need to assert that the Constitution leaves the regulation of elections to the legislature. We do not need to argue that the Constitution mandates a wealth tax; we only need to point out that the Sixteenth Amendment and the broad taxing power grant Congress the authority to levy it.

By demanding that progressive legislation be evaluated as a constitutional imperative, the authors inadvertently raise the stakes of every policy debate to an existential constitutional crisis. In a deeply polarized nation, where trust in institutions is at historic lows, turning every major economic dispute into a battle over the fundamental meaning of the Constitution is a recipe for further democratic erosion.

Progressives should indeed reject the idea that economic questions are best left to technocrats, and they must certainly fight for a more egalitarian political economy. But they should do so unapologetically in the name of democracy, majoritarianism, and the material well-being of the working class. They should argue for their policies because they are right, because they are necessary, and because the majority of the American people want them. They do not need to seek permission, or rhetorical cover, from an eighteenth-century text.
