"Progressives must reclaim constitutional interpretation from the conservative judiciary by articulating a substantive vision of 'constitutional political economy' that treats egalitarian reforms as democratic necessities rather than mere policy options."

The lead essay’s most important claim is also its simplest: if progressives do not argue about the Constitution, the Constitution will not disappear from politics. It will simply be interpreted by others. In practice, that means it will be interpreted by a Supreme Court majority that has shown remarkable creativity in turning constitutional law into a shield for concentrated wealth, corporate power, state obstruction, and minority rule. The answer cannot be for progressives to retreat into policy talk and hope courts stay away. Courts will not stay away. They have already arrived.

The call to “make progressive politics constitutional again” is therefore not a matter of rhetorical ornament. It is a matter of democratic self-defense. The Court’s conservative majority does not merely enforce neutral limits. It advances a constitutional political economy—one in which property rights, corporate speech, state sovereignty, and religious exemption are treated as foundational, while social citizenship, labor power, multiracial democracy, and egalitarian participation are treated as optional, suspect, or invisible. Progressives need a constitutional politics equal to the struggle.

The essay is especially persuasive in recovering the “democracy-of-opportunity” tradition. Too often, constitutional debate is presented as a contest between originalism and living constitutionalism, as though progressives must choose between historical memory and modern needs. But American constitutional history is not the property of the right. The Constitution has been invoked not only to restrain government but also to justify public action against domination: the Reconstruction amendments, the antimonopoly tradition, the New Deal defense of labor rights and social insurance, the civil rights movement’s demand for full citizenship. These were not merely policy movements that happened to operate in constitutional space. They were constitutional movements because they asked what kind of republic the Constitution was meant to sustain.

That question is unavoidable today. A republic cannot endure if economic power is so concentrated that it can buy political power, discipline workers, shape the information environment, and block collective action. Formal political equality means little when one citizen can write a check that alters the entire terrain of an election and another cannot afford a day off work to vote, attend a meeting, or care for a sick child. The Constitution does not specify every detail of campaign finance, labor law, health insurance, or corporate governance. But it does presuppose a political community capable of self-rule. The lead essay is right to insist that economic structure is not external to constitutional democracy. It is one of its conditions.

The First Amendment is the clearest example. In popular mythology, free speech is about dissidents, pamphleteers, journalists, union organizers, religious minorities, and unpopular speakers resisting censorship. That tradition remains vital. But the modern Court has increasingly transformed the First Amendment into a deregulatory tool. Campaign finance restrictions become censorship. Public-sector union fees become compelled speech. Disclosure and professional regulation become suspect burdens. Corporate expenditures are treated as the speech of “disfavored speakers,” while the democratic interest in preventing plutocratic domination is treated as illegitimate.

This is not neutrality. It is a substantive theory of power. It imagines the political arena as a marketplace in which unequal resources may be freely converted into unequal influence, and it treats democratic attempts to alter that conversion as constitutionally dangerous. The result is a First Amendment that protects the strong against collective regulation more reliably than it protects the weak against private domination. To call this “weaponization” is not hyperbole. It describes a doctrinal pattern.

A progressive constitutional response should not be anti-speech. On the contrary, it should be more genuinely pro-speech than the Court’s current doctrine. A campaign finance system that enables more candidates to run, gives small donors greater influence, and reduces dependence on wealthy patrons expands political speech in any meaningful democratic sense. A labor law regime that protects organizing, picketing, striking, and solidarity action expands the speech and association of working people. The right’s great success has been to identify “speech” with the power of already powerful actors to spend, resist regulation, or opt out. Progressives should reclaim speech as a practice of democratic participation.

The same is true of race and political economy. One of the lead essay’s strengths is its refusal to confine constitutional racial justice to antidiscrimination doctrine. That doctrine matters, but it is too narrow to capture the constitutional legacy of racial subordination. Segregated schools, racialized labor markets, exclusion from New Deal programs, predatory lending, environmental injustice, mass incarceration, and unequal health access are not separate from constitutional democracy. They shape who has real freedom, who can participate, who can build wealth, who is exposed to state violence, and whose communities flourish.

The Court’s decisions in NFIB v. Sebelius and Shelby County v. Holder illustrate the point. In both, the Court presented itself as defending constitutional structure: state sovereignty, federalism, equal sovereignty among states. But structure is never innocent. Allowing states to reject Medicaid expansion predictably left millions without coverage, heavily concentrated in the South and disproportionately affecting Black and poor people. Striking down the Voting Rights Act’s coverage formula predictably enabled new voting restrictions in jurisdictions with histories of racial exclusion. In both cases, abstract solicitude for states limited Congress’s ability to secure equal citizenship for people.

Progressives should be explicit about this inversion. Federalism has democratic value when it enables participation, experimentation, and local self-government. But when state power is used to entrench racial hierarchy, restrict voting, deny health care, suppress labor, or shield oligarchic interests, federal constitutional authority must be available to secure republican freedom. Reconstruction established that principle at terrible cost. The Court has often forgotten it. Political movements cannot afford to.

The lead essay’s third field—antitrust, corporate power, and political economy—may be the most promising because public opinion has begun to shift. Across ideological lines, many Americans sense that concentrated corporate power has become intolerable: in technology, agriculture, pharmaceuticals, finance, hospitals, meatpacking, and labor markets. The old consumer-welfare antitrust framework seems inadequate to a world in which low prices can coexist with surveillance, wage suppression, supplier coercion, political lobbying, and private governance by dominant firms.

Here the constitutional argument is not that the Sherman Act has a single mandated meaning written into Article I. It is that a constitutional republic needs laws preventing private concentrations of power from becoming rival sovereigns. This was obvious to earlier generations who feared trusts, monopolies, and chartered privileges. It should be obvious again in an age when large corporations can structure markets, employment, speech platforms, supply chains, and politics. Antimonopoly is not nostalgia. It is republicanism applied to modern economic life.

Still, the lead essay’s project faces two dangers. The first is abstraction. “Constitutional political economy” can sound remote from everyday politics unless attached to concrete institutions and campaigns. The authors gesture in the right direction: public financing, the PRO Act, voting rights legislation, wealth taxation, antitrust reform, corporate governance. But the constitutional argument must be translated into organizing language. Workers do not need to hear only that unions are consistent with the First Amendment; they need to hear that the Constitution belongs to them when they organize against arbitrary power. Patients do not need only a theory of federal spending; they need to hear that equal citizenship includes the material security necessary to live as free people.

The second danger is court-centrism in reverse. The essay rightly rejects the idea that constitutional meaning belongs only to judges. But progressive constitutional politics must not become a strategy aimed primarily at persuading courts. The current Court is not waiting to be convinced by better Brandeisian history. Constitutional arguments matter because they shape public legitimacy, legislative courage, administrative action, and movement identity. They can also prepare the ground for future judicial change. But if the measure of success is immediate Supreme Court acceptance, disappointment is guaranteed.

That is why constitutional politics must be paired with institutional reform. The filibuster, malapportioned Senate, Electoral College, partisan gerrymandering, voter suppression, and life-tenured Court all distort democratic authority. A democracy-of-opportunity tradition cannot flourish while antidemocratic veto points make legislative majorities nearly powerless. Progressives should not choose between substantive constitutional argument and procedural democracy reform. They need both. A constitutional movement that says Congress has a duty to protect voting rights must also be willing to change Senate rules that prevent Congress from fulfilling that duty. A movement that says courts have overreached must consider jurisdictional limits, ethics rules, term limits, or Court expansion.

The lead essay’s deepest contribution is to recover a lost confidence. For too long, many liberals have spoken as though the Constitution is mainly a constraint on progressive ambition. They defend programs as permissible, not as necessary; acceptable, not as expressions of democratic principle. Conservatives have rarely been so modest. They have built institutions, trained lawyers, mobilized voters, and told a constitutional story in which their political goals are linked to freedom, tradition, and limited government. Progressives need not imitate their substance, but they should learn from their seriousness.

The Constitution is not self-executing. It is a terrain of struggle. It has sanctioned slavery and enabled emancipation; protected property and authorized reconstruction; been invoked against labor and for labor; narrowed democracy and expanded it. To enter that struggle is not to worship the Constitution or deny its failures. It is to insist that democratic majorities, social movements, and elected representatives have as much authority to interpret our constitutional commitments as judges do.

A progressive constitutional politics should say plainly: oligarchy is incompatible with republican government; racial hierarchy is incompatible with equal citizenship; worker domination is incompatible with freedom of association; mass exclusion from health, housing, education, and political participation is incompatible with democracy. These claims will not win every case tomorrow. But without them, progressives enter the central constitutional conflicts of our time already disarmed.
