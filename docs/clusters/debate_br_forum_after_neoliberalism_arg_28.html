"The essay "Economics After Neoliberalism" is a rhetorical rebranding exercise that fails to truly break from neoliberalism because it ignores the discipline's historical complicity in those ideas and maintains a technocratic, anti-democratic approach to policy."

The essay “Economics After Neoliberalism” is a masterful piece of rhetorical repositioning. It attempts to distance the economics profession from the disastrous outcomes of the last forty years by arguing that “neoliberalism” is a perversion, not an application, of economic science. The authors then present a suite of policy proposals that, they claim, demonstrate how economics can serve inclusive prosperity. As an intellectual historian of neoliberalism, I find this move both fascinating and deeply troubling.

The problem is not the content of the proposals themselves—many are reasonable and evidence-based—but the historical amnesia that underpins the entire argument. The authors treat “neoliberalism” as an external virus that infected an otherwise healthy body of economic knowledge. But the genealogy of the ideas they now denounce is far more entangled with the mainstream of the discipline than they admit. Milton Friedman, Gary Becker, Robert Lucas, and their disciples were not marginal figures peddling a “primitive, simplistic perversion” of economics; they dominated the profession’s most prestigious departments, journals, and the Nobel Prize roster for decades. Their work was, and remains, central to the discipline’s self-understanding. The claim that modern economics is, and always was, rife with caveats and market failures is historically dubious. The public face of economics was not some accidental distortion propagated by conservative think tanks; it was cultivated by leading economists who actively built the Mont Pelerin Society, wrote popular columns, and advised governments.

It is true that empirical economics has become more sophisticated and more skeptical of simple market fundamentalism since the 1990s. But it is also true that this shift occurred largely in response to external shocks—the Latin American debt crisis, the Asian financial crisis, the 2008 crash—and the political movements they triggered. Economics did not self-correct from within; it was dragged, kicking and screaming, toward a grudging acknowledgment of reality. And even now, as the authors themselves note, many economists remain wary of “providing ammunition to barbarians.” The instinct to protect the brand of market rationality dies hard.

Moreover, the essay’s framing re-enacts a classic technocratic move: it takes a fundamentally political set of questions—who gets what, who bears risk, who has power—and recasts them as technical problems to be solved by experts. The inclusive prosperity project is, after all, a collection of policy briefs written by academic economists. There is no role envisioned here for democratic deliberation, for social movements, for the messy work of building political consent. The authors gesture toward democratic values, but their model of change is the seminar room, not the street. They want to replace one set of expert prescriptions with another, more enlightened one. This is not a departure from neoliberalism’s anti-democratic tendencies; it is their soft reincarnation.

What would a genuine break with neoliberal economics look like? It would mean first acknowledging the discipline’s own profound complicity in the injustices of the past half-century—not dismissing it as the work of a few “primitive” thinkers, but confronting the ways in which Chicago School economics, public choice theory, and efficient markets hypothesis were, for a time, mainstream economics. It would mean democratizing economic knowledge, making it accessible and contestable by ordinary people, rather than hoarding it as a guild secret to be deployed by benevolent philosopher-kings. It would mean ceding some of the economist’s authority to other ways of knowing—the lived experience of workers, the expertise of community organizers, the moral reasoning of philosophers.

The authors write that “economists have an indispensable role to play.” That may be true, but it is a role that must be redefined in humility and partnership, not in the vanguardist spirit that pervades this essay. Until then, “Economics After Neoliberalism” looks less like a break with the past and more like a rebranding exercise by a profession desperate to regain its waning public legitimacy.
