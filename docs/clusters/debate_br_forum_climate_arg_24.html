"While political struggle against fossil capital is necessary, a state-centric 'two-step' strategy is insufficient because it underestimates the deep structural and cultural integration of fossil fuels into the material fabric of daily life."

The climate crisis is not primarily a technical problem awaiting a political solution. It is the expression of a civilizational choice—a decision, made and remade billions of times daily, to organize human life around the consumption of fossil fuels rather than alternatives. Understanding this distinction matters for how we think about political strategy.

Táíwò's "two-step" framework treats fossil capital as the obstacle to climate action, a barrier that can be removed through political struggle. There is much truth in this. The fossil fuel industry has deployed its resources systematically to block climate policy, fund climate denial, and capture regulatory processes. These are documented facts, not conspiracy theories. But treating fossil capital as the sole or even primary obstacle risks underestimating the depth of fossil fuel integration into everyday life.

Consider the transportation sector. Electric vehicles are expanding, but they remain a small fraction of the global vehicle fleet, and their emissions benefits depend on the electricity source. More fundamentally, car-dependent urban planning—itself a product of decades of public policy, private investment, and cultural assumption—makes electric vehicles a partial solution at best. The same pattern repeats across sectors: industrial processes designed around fossil fuel inputs, buildings heated by natural gas, agricultural systems dependent on synthetic fertilizers made from fossil fuels. The energy transition is not simply a matter of replacing one energy source with another; it requires restructuring the material fabric of daily life.

This is not an argument against political strategy. It is an argument for a political strategy adequate to the depth of change required. Táíwò is right that electoral and legislative politics matter. But politics alone cannot restructure urban form, redesign industrial processes, or transform agricultural systems. These are technical changes, yes, but they are also cultural and social changes that must be embraced by populations if they are to be sustained.

The movements Táíwò discusses—Extinction Rebellion, the Sunrise Movement, pipeline saboteurs—represent different approaches to building the political power necessary for change. But they also represent different theories of social change. Extinction Rebellion emphasizes moral suasion, seeking to shift public consciousness through dramatic action. The Sunrise Movement focuses on electoral politics, seeking to elect representatives who will pass climate legislation. Andreas Malm's pipeline sabotage represents a theory of economic disruption, seeking to make fossil fuel extraction unviable.

These approaches need not be mutually exclusive, but they reflect different assessments of where power actually lies. If power lies in public opinion, then consciousness-raising makes sense. If power lies in elected officials, then electoral politics makes sense. If power lies in the operations of the energy system itself, then direct action makes sense. The "two-step" framework assumes that power lies in the state, or can be brought there through struggle. This may be true, but it requires demonstration rather than assertion.

What evidence exists for the claim that state power can be successfully aligned against fossil capital? Táíwò points to energy democracy movements and the Fossil Fuel Non-Proliferation Treaty. These are promising developments, but they remain marginal to the actual operations of the global energy system. Fossil fuels still provide over 80 percent of global energy. The major emitters—the United States, China, India, the European Union—have failed to enact policies consistent with their Paris commitments. The trend lines point toward continued warming, not stabilization.

This is not an argument for despair or paralysis. It is an argument for realism about what state-centric politics can achieve and for building complementary forms of power outside the state. Community resilience, mutual aid networks, alternative economic institutions—these cannot substitute for political action, but they can create social capacity for change and provide models for what a transformed society might look like. The climate crisis demands that we build power at every scale, through every viable means. The "two-step" framework, if it leads the left to neglect these other forms of power in favor of state-centric strategies, may prove inadequate to the task.
