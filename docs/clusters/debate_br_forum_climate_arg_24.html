"While Olúfẹ́mi O. Táíwò’s 'two-step' strategy offers a pragmatic approach to the climate crisis, it is fundamentally limited by a conservative reliance on state-centric institutions that ignores the potential of emerging non-state political forms."

The most radical thing about Táíwò's essay is its fundamental conservatism. Not conservative in the partisan sense—he clearly identifies with the left and its transformative ambitions—but conservative in the philosophical sense: a willingness to work within inherited categories and established institutions rather than imagining genuinely new forms of political organization.

The "two-step" strategy, despite its historical framing, accepts the basic terms of contemporary political debate: the state as the primary arena of political action, elections as the mechanism for selecting rulers, policy as the instrument of social change. These categories are not natural facts but historical products, shaped by centuries of struggle and institutional development. They represent one possible answer to the question of how to organize collective life, not the only answer.

Táíwò briefly acknowledges alternative forms—Zapatista autonomy, Rojava's democraticconfederalism—before dismissing them as insufficient to the scale of the climate challenge. But this dismissal relies on a premise that deserves examination: the assumption that only state-level action can achieve atmospheric relevance. This premise is itself a product of a particular historical moment, when states commanded the resources and deployed the technologies that made them the dominant actors in world politics.

The twenty-first century is challenging this dominance in ways that Táíwò's analysis underweights. The rise of asset managers like BlackRock and Vanguard represents a shift of economic power away from both states and productive capital toward financial intermediaries. Cities and regions are asserting autonomous climate agendas that diverge from national policies. Social movements are building transnational networks that operate independently of state structures. The emergence of genuinely global public problems—climate change, pandemic disease, financial contagion—exposes the limitations of state-centric solutions.

This does not mean that states are irrelevant. They remain the primary repositories of coercive power and the principal architects of the legal frameworks within which other actors operate. But it does mean that effective climate politics cannot be reduced to state politics. We need to build power at every scale and through every available means.

Táíwò is right that the climate crisis demands strategic clarity. But clarity requires us to see the full range of possibilities, not merely the familiar ones. A politics adequate to the climate crisis must be bold enough to imagine alternatives to the existing order while pragmatic enough to work within it. It must be internationalist in scope without waiting for international consensus. It must be democratic in aspiration without assuming that existing democratic institutions are adequate to the task. It must be humble about the limits of any single strategy, including the "two-step" approach that Táíwò defends.

The climate crisis is the greatest challenge humanity has ever faced. Meeting it requires all our creativity, all our solidarity, and all our courage. The left has contributed important analyses and strategies, of which Táíwò's essay is a notable example. But we should be wary of premature closure, of declaring victory for one approach before we have fully explored others. The future is not yet written, and its shape will depend on what we dare to imagine.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The essay's focus on the state as the primary arena of action accepts historical categories of power rather than imagining new forms of political organization.
  2. 02The dismissal of autonomous movements like the Zapatistas or Rojava underestimates the ability of non-state actors to achieve atmospheric relevance in the twenty-first century.
  3. 03The rise of global financial intermediaries and transnational social networks suggests that economic and political power is shifting away from the traditional dominance of the nation-state.
  4. 04Effective climate politics must build power across multiple scales—including cities, regions, and international networks—rather than reducing the struggle to a contest for state policy.
  5. 05Relying on existing democratic institutions is insufficient because those institutions may not be adequate to the unprecedented scale and nature of the climate crisis.
