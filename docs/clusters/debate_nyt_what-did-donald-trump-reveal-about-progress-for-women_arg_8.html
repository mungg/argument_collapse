"The public reaction to Donald Trump’s comments reveals that while society has learned to perform public outrage against sexism, it has not yet achieved the structural transformation necessary to end the systemic protection of powerful men."

The reaction to Trump’s comments should make us skeptical of measuring progress by outrage. Outrage is easy. Consequences are harder.

Yes, the tape produced a remarkable public backlash. Politicians fled, editorial pages condemned him, and many voters recoiled. But the same week also showed how quickly a society can metabolize misogyny and move on. Trump called the remarks “locker room talk,” issued a brief apology, pivoted to attacking the Clintons, and dared his party to abandon him. Many did not. For a significant portion of the electorate, a man boasting about grabbing women without consent became one more item in the partisan ledger.

That is not a sign that nothing has changed. In 2016, a woman is the Democratic nominee for president, women are a majority of college graduates, and open sexual harassment is less socially acceptable than it once was. But symbolic progress can coexist with deep habits of permission. A woman can run for the highest office in the country while her opponent’s contempt for women is treated as a campaign “problem” to be managed rather than a window into character. Female advancement has not ended the old bargain by which powerful men expect forgiveness for behavior that would destroy less powerful people.

The most revealing sentence in the tape was not the vulgar one. It was: “When you’re a star, they let you do it.” That sentence describes an entire social order. It assumes that women’s boundaries are negotiable when a man has fame, money, or authority. It also shifts responsibility onto women: they “let” him. Anyone familiar with harassment knows how false that can be. People comply, freeze, laugh nervously, stay silent, or avoid confrontation for reasons that have nothing to do with consent.

The public response also showed the limitations of partisan feminism. Some Republicans condemned Trump only after calculating that he might damage their races. Some Democrats, meanwhile, spoke as though sexism were a problem located solely in the opposing party. That is too convenient. The structures that protect abusive men are not confined to one campaign. They exist in offices, restaurants, universities, media companies, churches, police departments, and political families of both parties.

So what would real progress look like? It would mean not needing a leaked tape before women are believed. It would mean stronger workplace protections, less retaliation against complainants, better sex education about consent, and political parties willing to reject misogyny even when it costs them power. It would mean judging male candidates not only by whether they can perform remorse under pressure but by whether they have treated women as equals when no camera was rolling.

Trump revealed that America has learned to denounce certain forms of sexism in public. He also revealed that denunciation is not the same as transformation. The scandal will matter only if it changes what voters, parties, employers, and men permit after the news cycle fades.
