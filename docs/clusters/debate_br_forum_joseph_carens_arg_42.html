Diversified DeepSeek

"Granting a statutory right to amnesty based on length of residence is a misguided policy that would incentivize more illegal immigration and repeat the failures of past reforms."

I read Joseph Carens’s essay with a growing sense of unease. His prose is elegant, his sympathies are clearly in the right place, and yet the argument feels like a sophisticated rationalization for ignoring one of the most painful lessons of recent American history: that amnesty without real enforcement is a recipe for a permanent, ever-growing illegal population. In 1986, we tried exactly the approach Carens now recommends—a legalization for those who could show long-term residence—and it failed spectacularly. Unless we are willing to learn from that failure, we will be condemned to repeat it, with even more devastating consequences.

The Immigration Reform and Control Act of 1986 (IRCA) was sold on the promise that it would “wipe the slate clean” and put an end to illegal immigration. It legalized nearly 2.7 million people, most of them agricultural workers and those who had lived continuously in the U.S. since before 1982. In exchange, it mandated employer sanctions, increased border enforcement, and created a special agricultural worker program. Yet by the early 2000s, the unauthorized population had not shrunk; it had ballooned to over 10 million. Why? Because employer sanctions were never seriously enforced, border security was uneven, and the very act of legalization sent a powerful signal to would-be migrants around the world: if you can just get here and stay long enough, eventually the law will relent.

Carens addresses this history not at all. He mentions IRCA only in passing, if at all, and he dismisses the incentive effect as speculative. But it is not speculative; it is the lived reality of millions of families in Mexico, Central America, and beyond who have made the calculated decision to come illegally because they believe, with good reason, that the United States does not have the will to deport them once they are settled. The smuggling networks explicitly market this: “Come now, and after a few years you’ll be legal.” Carens’s proposal would supercharge that message by codifying it into an automatic statutory right. No longer would amnesty be a discretionary act of Congress, subject to political winds; it would be an entitlement, vested after a fixed period. The result would be a huge increase in migration pressure, as millions rationally decide to risk a few years in the shadows for a guaranteed path to permanent residence.

Carens argues that the state still has the right to enforce border controls and to deport within the early years. But there is the rub. If we cannot enforce the law now against 11 million people, how will we enforce it more effectively when the incentive to come is even greater? Carens seems to imagine a world in which the state aggressively deports recent arrivals while passively allowing long-term residents to transition to legality. That is a fantasy. A state that lacks the political will or capacity to deport long-term residents will inevitably be even less effective against new arrivals, who are harder to identify and easier to hide. The practical effect of a five-year amnesty rule would not be a cleaned-up immigration system; it would be a continuously revolving door, with millions entering illegally, waiting out the clock, and then legalizing, while enforcement resources are diverted to the hopeless task of catching everyone within the window.

The comparison to statutes of limitations, again, is deceptive. The state’s power to prosecute crime does not evaporate after five years for all offenses; it does so only for those where the legislature has judged that the costs of pursuing cold cases outweigh the benefits. And crucially, statutes of limitations do not apply to ongoing conspiracies or continuing crimes. Illegal presence is ongoing; every day it persists, the violation renews. The state’s interest in stopping that violation does not diminish over time; if anything, it may increase as the migrant’s ties deepen and the disruption of deportation becomes greater. The logic works in the opposite direction from what Carens claims: precisely because the harm of deportation grows over time, the state has a strong incentive to act early, not late. But if it fails to do so, it is not thereby ethically barred from acting at all. We do not say that a fugitive who evades capture for decades thereby acquires a right to remain free; we say the state should have caught him sooner.

I am not advocating mass roundups or cruelty. I am advocating realism. The way to prevent the human tragedies Carens so movingly describes is not to promise amnesty, but to create a system where illegal immigration no longer pays. That means mandatory E-Verify for all employers, with stiff penalties; it means a secure border fence and sufficient agents; it means cracking down on visa overstayers with an entry-exit tracking system; and it means a temporary worker program that is genuinely responsive to labor needs without creating a permanent illegal underclass. Once those measures are credibly in place, we can talk about a one-time amnesty for the existing population. But to do amnesty first, as Carens proposes, is to get the sequence exactly backwards. It is to repeat the mistake of 1986 on a grander scale, and to ensure that in another twenty years, we will be having this same debate all over again, with an even larger population of people living in the shadows. That is not compassion; it is neglect.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Immigration Reform and Control Act of 1986 proved that providing legalization without effective enforcement leads to a massive increase in the unauthorized population.
  2. 02Codifying amnesty as an automatic entitlement would create a powerful incentive for prospective migrants to enter illegally and wait out the clock for legal status.
  3. 03A state that lacks the political will to deport long-term residents is unlikely to possess the capacity to effectively enforce laws against new arrivals.
  4. 04The analogy to statutes of limitations is flawed because illegal presence is a continuing violation rather than a past offense that has concluded.
  5. 05The state should prioritize creating a system where illegal immigration is no longer viable through workplace and border enforcement before considering any form of amnesty.
  6. 06True compassion involves preventing future human tragedies by establishing a credible, enforceable immigration system rather than rewarding lawbreaking.
