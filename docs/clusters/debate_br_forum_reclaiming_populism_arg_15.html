"The American left should prioritize long-term institutional building over the discursive reclamation of populism, as the latter's episodic and personality-driven nature fails to create the durable power necessary for sustained progressive governance."

Let me offer a dissent from the framing that structures this forum. The effort to reclaim populism as a framework for left politics is understandable given the failures of establishment centrism, but it rests on a misdiagnosis of what has gone wrong and a misapprehension of what ails the American left. The problem is not that populism has been unfairly stigmatized; the problem is that the American left has not adequately grappled with the institutional prerequisites for successful progressive politics. In this response, I want to argue that the left's weakness is structural rather than discursive, and that the path forward requires a reorientation from populist appeals to institutional building.

To understand why I think this matters, consider what populism actually is, as a political form. Populism involves an appeal to "the people" as a unified political agent against enemies—whether those enemies are defined as elites, outsiders, or corrupt institutions. It is, by definition, a politics of the masses rather than of organized groups with specific interests and institutional presence. This is both its strength and its weakness. Its strength is that it can mobilize people who have been marginalized from conventional politics, that it can articulate a critique that resonates with broad audiences, and that it can generate energy and excitement in ways that institutionally grounded politics often cannot. Its weakness is that it tends to be episodic,人格-driven, and structurally unstable. Populist movements rise and fall with their leaders and their moments; they do not build the durable institutional infrastructure that is necessary for sustained progressive governance.

The Sanders campaign is the most obvious recent example. Whatever its successes in mobilizing new voters and shifting political discourse, it did not build lasting institutions. The volunteers who worked for Sanders will not automatically translate into the organized constituency that can sustain progressive politics across election cycles. The enthusiasm that Sanders generated will not, by itself, produce the organizational density—the local party structures, the community organizations, the unions, the issue advocacy groups—that progressive politics requires. This is not a criticism of Sanders but an observation about the structural limits of populism as a political form.

Now consider the alternative: a politics that prioritizes institutional building over populist mobilization. The CTU's trajectory offers one model. The union spent years building internal capacity—developing leaders, organizing members, constructing relationships with community groups—before it was in a position to win the strikes and electoral victories that have made it a model for progressive politics. This is not a politics of the mass meeting or the charismatic leader; it is a politics of the slow accumulation of organizational power. It is, in the terminology that Getachew uses, less populist than it is institutional.

I want to be careful here. I am not arguing that populism and institutional politics are mutually exclusive. The most successful social movements have combined elements of both: the mass mobilization that creates political opportunity and the institutional building that converts opportunity into durable change. But the emphasis matters, especially for a left that has historically been stronger at mobilization than at institution-building. The risk of "reclaiming populism" is that it reinforces existing tendencies toward episodic, personality-driven politics at the expense of the patient, unglamorous work of building organizations that can last.

The COVID-19 pandemic has revealed both the need for and the limits of populist politics. On one hand, the crisis has generated a wave of mutual aid organizing and grassroots mobilization that has been genuinely inspiring. On the other hand, this mobilization has not easily translated into the institutional capacity to demand and win systemic change. The workers who are most exposed to the virus—the grocery clerks, the warehouse workers, the delivery drivers—are largely unorganized, lacking the voice and power that would allow them to demand the protections they need. Populist appeals to "the people" will not solve this problem. What is needed is the slower, more difficult work of building organizations that can represent these workers and aggregate their interests over time.

This does not mean that discourse and framing do not matter. The essays in this forum are right that the left cannot cede populist rhetoric to the right, that the term "populism" has been weaponized in ways that serve establishment interests, and that a revitalized left needs a compelling account of who "the people" are and what they want. But discourse is an instrument for organizing, not a substitute for it. The left will not win by reclaiming a word; it will win by building the organizational power that can translate values into policy and policy into lived reality. The pandemic has not changed this fundamental truth. If anything, it has made it clearer.
