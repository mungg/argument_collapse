"To ensure surrogacy is ethical and safe, the legal and regulatory framework must prioritize the long-term interests and protections of the child over the desires of the adults involved."

Any ethical surrogacy system must begin with a question too often treated as secondary: what will protect the child who is born? The adults may have lawyers, doctors and contracts. The child has none of those things at conception, and yet he or she will live longest with the consequences.

The first child-centered rule should be legal certainty from birth. If the intended parents have followed the law, they should be the legal parents immediately, and they should be unable to refuse that status because the child is disabled, because twins are born, or because their marriage collapses during the pregnancy. Recent controversies abroad have shown how quickly compassion can evaporate when a child does not match expectations. The law should make clear that surrogacy creates obligations, not a consumer option to accept or reject a finished product.

Second, every agreement should include contingency planning. What happens if the intended parents die before birth? What if one intended parent disappears? What if the baby needs months of neonatal intensive care? A guardian designation, insurance coverage and a funded reserve should be required before embryo transfer. These rules may sound bureaucratic, but they are less cruel than improvising after a crisis.

Third, children born through surrogacy should have access to accurate information about their origins. This does not mean every surrogate must become a social parent. It does mean that medical history, genetic information and identifying records should be preserved. As with adoption and sperm donation, secrecy often serves adult discomfort more than children’s long-term interests.

Child protection also supports limits on medical practice. Transferring multiple embryos to improve success rates may please impatient adults, but it increases the risk of prematurity and disability. Nonmedical sex selection and casual use of selective reduction should be discouraged or barred. The aim is not to police every reproductive decision, but to prevent children from bearing avoidable risks created by a commercial process.

Of course, protecting children also requires protecting surrogates. A woman who receives good medical care, independent counseling, legal advice and freedom from coercion is more likely to have a healthy pregnancy. Protecting intended parents matters too, because stable parentage is central to a child’s security.

Surrogacy should not be governed as a mere contract for services. It is the deliberate creation of a human being within a web of adult promises. Ethical law should make those promises durable, transparent and enforceable on behalf of the child.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The law must establish immediate and irrevocable legal parentage from birth to prevent intended parents from abandoning children who do not meet their expectations.
  2. 02Every surrogacy agreement should require mandatory contingency planning, including guardian designations and insurance, to protect the child in the event of a crisis.
  3. 03Children born through surrogacy should have a guaranteed right to access accurate medical, genetic, and identifying information regarding their origins.
  4. 04Medical practices in surrogacy, such as multiple embryo transfers and nonmedical sex selection, should be strictly limited to prevent children from bearing avoidable health risks.
  5. 05Protecting the surrogate's health and autonomy is essential because her well-being is directly linked to the safety and security of the child.
