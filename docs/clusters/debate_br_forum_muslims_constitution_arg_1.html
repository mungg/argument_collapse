"While the Trump administration's policies intensified anti-Muslim prejudice, they are rooted in a long-standing, bipartisan security infrastructure that treats Muslim belonging as provisional and requires constant, performative proof of loyalty."

The most painful sentence in Akbar and Theoharis’s essay is not about surveillance, courts, or war. It is the question about Khizr Khan: why did it take a Muslim family’s sacrifice for them to be celebrated? That question lingers because many Muslim Americans know the answer before it is asked. We have been trained to understand that our belonging is provisional. We may be neighbors, classmates, doctors, cab drivers, teachers, soldiers, lawyers, store owners, and children born in New Jersey or Michigan or Texas. But when the country becomes frightened, we are asked to introduce ourselves again.

This repeated reintroduction is exhausting. After every attack somewhere in the world, Muslims are asked to condemn. After every arrest, Muslims are asked whether our mosques are doing enough. After every political controversy, Muslims are asked whether Islam is compatible with democracy, women’s rights, free speech, gay rights, modernity, America. The questions are posed as if they are new. They are not. They are rituals of suspicion, and participation is compulsory.

The travel ban intensified this ritual but did not create it. The executive order made airports into scenes of open humiliation: families waiting for relatives who did not come through the doors, lawyers sitting on floors drafting habeas petitions, translators searching for frightened travelers, protesters chanting outside glass walls. But for many Muslims, the airport had long been a place where citizenship thinned. You learn to leave extra time. You learn to unlock your phone or wonder if you must. You learn that a beard, a name, a stamp in a passport, a place of birth, or a visit to family can become an invitation to questioning. You learn to smile carefully.

The lead essay’s account of surveillance captures something that ordinary policy language misses: surveillance is not only information gathering; it is atmosphere. It changes how people breathe. When a community suspects that informants may be present, conversations shift. A young person who wants to talk about Syria or Palestine or police violence may stay quiet. A convert may be viewed with unfair suspicion because people have heard too many stories of informants. An imam may avoid controversial subjects not because he lacks courage but because he must protect a congregation. Parents may warn their children not to post, not to argue, not to attend that meeting, not to say that phrase. A political identity becomes a risk assessment.

This is why Countering Violent Extremism programs have been so damaging. Their language is soft: partnership, resilience, engagement, prevention. But the premise is hard. Muslim communities are treated as spaces where danger incubates and where signs must be detected before they become crimes. Religious practice, adolescent alienation, political anger, mental health struggles, and moral outrage at war are folded into a grid of possible radicalization. The result is not safety. It is mistrust.

Defenders of CVE often say that communities asked for resources and that some Muslim organizations participated. That is true. Muslim communities are not monolithic, and they make difficult choices under pressure. If a city offers youth programs, mental health funding, or dialogue with officials, some leaders may accept because the needs are real. But a program does not become non-stigmatizing simply because some of the stigmatized participate. Poor communities have long accepted police-sponsored programs, school resource officers, or gang-prevention grants while also experiencing over-policing. Consent under conditions of unequal power is complicated.

Akbar and Theoharis are also right to place Muslim experiences within broader histories of racial control. This is not always easy to discuss because American Muslims are racially diverse: black Muslims whose families have been here for centuries, Arab immigrants and their children, South Asian professionals, refugees from Somalia or Iraq, white converts, Latino Muslims, Iranians, Bosnians, Turks, West Africans, and many others. Some are wealthy; many are not. Some are citizens; others are undocumented or on temporary visas. Anti-Muslim policy touches them differently.

But the war on terror has racialized Islam in a particular way. The imagined terrorist has a body, an accent, a beard, a gender, an origin story. Sikhs have been attacked by people who think they are Muslims. Arab Christians have been profiled. South Asians of different religions have learned that brownness can be read as Muslim threat. Black Muslims experience anti-Muslim suspicion layered on top of anti-black policing. Somali communities face counterterrorism scrutiny, refugee politics, and ordinary racism simultaneously. The state may say it targets conduct, but the social field on which suspicion operates is racial.

That is why alliances with Black Lives Matter and immigrant-rights movements are not optional. They are not fashionable add-ons. They are analytically necessary and politically vital. Stop-and-frisk, gang databases, deportation raids, FBI visits, no-fly lists, school surveillance, prison isolation, border interrogations—these are not identical practices, but they share logics. They treat certain communities as problems to be managed before any individual wrong has been proven. They create categories of people who must live transparently before the state while the state remains opaque to them.

Still, I would add one caution to the lead essay. In criticizing the “good Muslim/bad Muslim” binary, we should not create another binary between radical critique and compromised community life. Many Muslims respond to suspicion through patriotism, interfaith work, military service, law enforcement careers, or public declarations of loyalty. Some do so strategically; some sincerely; often both. They should not be dismissed as dupes of empire. A refugee who flies an American flag because this country gave her safety is not responsible for the Pentagon. A Muslim police officer who believes he can serve justly is not reducible to the security state. A Gold Star family’s grief should not be treated only as evidence of national pathology.

The point is not to judge how Muslims survive conditional belonging. The point is to end the conditions. Some will wave the Constitution; some will quote the Qur’an; some will march against war; some will enlist; some will build local mutual aid; some will litigate; some will write poetry; some will keep their heads down. A movement spacious enough to defend Muslim life must make room for all of these without allowing the state to decide which ones count as acceptable.

The question of foreign policy cannot be separated from this. Many American Muslims have relatives in countries bombed, occupied, sanctioned, or destabilized by the United States. When public officials say “we are fighting them over there so we do not fight them here,” Muslims hear the geography of kinship turned into a battlefield. When drone strikes kill civilians, the grief travels through diaspora communities. When refugees are described as threats after fleeing wars in which the United States has played a role, cruelty is compounded by denial.

Yet Muslims are also not merely victims of American policy. They have political opinions about Assad, ISIS, Saudi Arabia, Iran, Egypt, Pakistan, Palestine, Kashmir, drones, dictators, sectarianism, feminism, secularism, and religious authority. These debates are vibrant and sometimes fierce. Surveillance flattens them. It makes internal community disagreement risky because a position may be overheard as extremism. One of the great harms of anti-Muslim policing is intellectual: it narrows the space in which Muslims can argue with one another about the world.

What should be demanded now? First, the travel ban must be defeated not only because it is badly drafted but because religious exclusion is intolerable. Second, watch lists must be made subject to meaningful due process. Third, CVE programs that single out Muslims should be rejected, and resources for youth, mental health, and anti-violence work should be provided without intelligence strings. Fourth, material support laws must be narrowed so that charity, speech, and political association are not chilled. Fifth, mosques and Muslim student groups should not be mapped or infiltrated absent individualized suspicion of crime. Sixth, opposition to anti-Muslim hate must include opposition to wars that render Muslim lives abroad invisible.

Finally, non-Muslim allies must resist the urge to defend Muslims only by saying “they are just like us.” Sometimes that phrase is meant kindly. But Muslims do not need to be just like anyone else to have rights. Some Muslims are secular and some are devout; some are feminist and some patriarchal; some are liberal and some conservative; some are queer; some are homophobic; some are generous; some are ordinary and flawed. A community’s humanity cannot depend on its public relations department.

The airport protests suggested that many Americans are ready to say no to the most blatant forms of exclusion. The next task is harder: to say no when exclusion wears the language of expertise, when suspicion is hidden in databases, when informants call themselves friends, when courts defer, when bombs fall far away, when the person targeted is not a perfect symbol. Akbar and Theoharis ask us to look beyond Trump. That is the right demand. The ban is a crisis, but the deeper crisis is the notion that Muslims must be managed into belonging. We do not need management. We need freedom.

"The widespread protests against Trump’s travel ban must evolve into a broader movement to dismantle the pre-existing, bipartisan national security infrastructure that treats Muslim identity and political dissent as inherently suspicious."

Amna Akbar and Jeanne Theoharis are right to insist that the airport protests, heartening as they were, cannot be the end of the story. The executive order that stranded travelers, separated families, and turned green-card holders into supplicants was shocking not because it came from nowhere, but because it made visible a structure that many Americans had learned not to see. Trump did not invent the idea that Muslim lives are presumptively suspicious. He inherited a vast architecture of war, policing, surveillance, immigration control, informant recruitment, watch lists, and judicial deference. His innovation is not conceptual but atmospheric: he has stripped away the liberal euphemisms.

That stripping away is dangerous, but it is also clarifying. For years, American officials have spoken in the language of balance: security and liberty, vigilance and tolerance, hard power and pluralism. Yet the balancing has almost always occurred on terrain already defined by the state. The Muslim student association, the mosque, the charity, the airport line, the religious study circle, the antiwar conversation, the family WhatsApp thread, the remittance, the trip to visit relatives—these have all been made available to official scrutiny in ways that would have been recognized as intolerable had they been directed at many other communities. “If you have nothing to hide” became the governing civic theology. But what surveillance hides is its own damage: the self-censorship, the broken friendships, the suspicion introduced into sacred spaces, the fear that political opinion will be translated into evidence.

The lead essay’s most important contribution is its refusal of the “good Muslim/bad Muslim” bargain. That bargain has become so routine that even many opponents of the travel ban reproduce it without noticing. We defend the Syrian child, the elderly grandmother, the interpreter who helped U.S. troops, the physician at the Cleveland Clinic, the Gold Star father. These defenses are not wrong. The individual stories are real; the people deserve protection. But the pattern is morally narrow. It says, in effect, that Muslims may be admitted into American sympathy if they are useful, grateful, suffering, secular enough, patriotic enough, or dead in an American uniform.

A democracy cannot allocate rights that way. The Constitution does not protect only the agreeable worshipper, the apolitical immigrant, or the soldier’s family. It protects the dissenter, the conservative believer, the angry critic of American foreign policy, the person who refuses to sing for his supper. If Muslims must continuously perform innocence in order to avoid collective punishment, then innocence has become a disciplinary demand rather than a principle of justice.

This matters especially because the state’s definition of danger has been unstable and expansive. “Material support” law is a central example. The public imagines material support as guns, cash, or training camps. But the statute, especially after its expansion in the Patriot Act era and the Supreme Court’s approval in Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project, can reach speech, advice, association, and coordinated advocacy, even when the advocacy is for lawful or humanitarian ends. When such broad legal categories are combined with informants who are paid or pressured to generate cases, the line between preventing violence and manufacturing plots can become dangerously thin.

The familiar answer is that terrorism is exceptional. But exceptional to what? The United States has long built domestic legal regimes around racialized fear: slave patrols and fugitive slave laws, Chinese exclusion, Palmer raids, Japanese American incarceration, COINTELPRO, the war on drugs, gang databases, mass deportation. Each regime was defended as necessary, practical, and limited. Each expanded because courts deferred, politicians competed to appear tough, and the targeted communities lacked sufficient power to force accountability. The national security state repeats this history while claiming to stand outside it.

This is why the comparison to the war on drugs is not rhetorical ornament. It is analytic. Both regimes rely on preemption and suspicion. Both make informants central. Both incentivize prosecutors to use leverage—long sentences, immigration consequences, secret or inaccessible evidence—to secure pleas. Both produce public rituals in which dangerous classes are identified and incapacitated. And both depend on the public’s willingness to believe that the people swept up probably did something wrong. The Muslim “terror suspect” and the black or Latino “drug suspect” occupy different legal categories but often serve the same political function: they make state violence appear protective.

The lead essay is also right to connect domestic policy to U.S. action abroad. Americans often discuss terrorism as if it were a meteorological phenomenon: storms gather over there, and we must protect ourselves here. But the United States is not a bystander in Iraq, Afghanistan, Pakistan, Yemen, Somalia, Libya, Syria, or elsewhere. It has invaded, occupied, bombed, armed proxies, supported authoritarian allies, and conducted drone strikes with civilian casualties and uncertain legal accountability. None of this justifies attacks on civilians. But refusing to justify terrorism is not the same as refusing to analyze the conditions in which political violence grows. A country cannot rain bombs on Muslim-majority societies and then treat Muslim grief, anger, or criticism as evidence of extremism.

Countering Violent Extremism programs crystallize the problem. Presented as community partnership, CVE turns teachers, social workers, religious leaders, and youth mentors into quasi-intelligence actors. It invites Muslim communities to prove their loyalty by monitoring themselves. It medicalizes dissent by treating religious intensity, alienation, political grievance, or concern for foreign suffering as possible precursors to violence. And because the program has overwhelmingly focused on Muslims, it ratifies the very association between Islam and terrorism that officials deny making.

The obvious rejoinder is that the government must prevent violence. Of course it must. But prevention cannot mean abandoning ordinary standards of evidence, equality, and restraint. If a person plans a bombing, prosecute the plan. If a person buys weapons for an attack, stop him. If a network funds violence, investigate it. The issue is not whether the state may act against crime. The issue is whether whole communities may be treated as incubators of crime, whether political and religious life may be mapped as a threat environment, and whether constitutional protections evaporate when prosecutors say “terrorism.”

The post-9/11 consensus has evaded that question by making Muslims prove that they are on “our” side. Bill Clinton’s convention line—Muslims who love freedom and hate terror should stay and help—was widely received as inclusion. It was inclusion, but conditional inclusion. No one tells Methodists that they may remain if they hate the Ku Klux Klan, or white evangelicals that their belonging depends on helping the FBI defeat militias. The asymmetry is the point. Muslims are welcomed as assistants in a war directed at people thought to resemble them.

What should follow from Akbar and Theoharis’s critique? Not merely better messaging and not only litigation against Trump’s executive order. We need a politics that demands the dismantling of the machinery beneath the ban. That means transparent and contestable watch lists; an end to coercive informant recruitment; serious limits on undercover operations that create plots rather than interrupt them; narrowing material support laws; rejecting CVE programs that single out Muslim communities; challenging secret evidence; ending prolonged solitary confinement and special administrative measures absent extraordinary proof; and insisting that foreign policy be part of the civil liberties debate.

It also means changing how we argue in public. The airport protests were beautiful because people acted before waiting for permission from courts or parties. But if the rallying cry becomes only “let in the innocent,” the movement will fail the first time the government points to someone less sympathetic. Rights are tested at the margins. A person may be devout, angry, alienated, foreign-born, politically radical, and still entitled to due process. A person may criticize U.S. wars and still belong here. A person may refuse patriotic theater and still be protected.

Trump’s travel ban has awakened many Americans to the fragility of constitutional promises. The danger now is nostalgia: the belief that if courts block this order and officials speak again in polished tones, normality will have returned. But normality is the problem. The ban is a door opened by a state that has long been building the hallway. If we want a country not defined by bans and registrations, we must stop asking Muslims to prove that they deserve ordinary freedom. We must ask instead why ordinary freedom was ever made conditional.

Vanilla GPT

"While the Trump administration's Muslim ban is uniquely cruel, it is the logical extension of a long-standing, bipartisan legal and bureaucratic architecture that must be dismantled rather than merely returned to its pre-Trump status quo."

Akbar and Theoharis are right to insist that the Muslim ban cannot be treated as an aberration that fell from the sky on January 20. The order’s cruelty and constitutional infirmity are urgent, but its plausibility rests on a much older architecture: secret lists, broad immigration powers, material support statutes, informant networks, watchlisting, expansive surveillance, and a political culture in which Muslims are routinely asked to prove that they are not the enemy. If the response to Trump is only to say “this is not who we are,” we will fail to see how much of this moment has been made by policies defended in more respectable language by both parties.

The central achievement of the essay is to shift the question from Trump’s animus to the conditions that made his program administratively and politically possible. That matters because the travel ban is not simply a symbolic insult. It operates through institutions that already know how to sort, detain, interrogate, and exclude Muslims and people perceived to be Muslim. It is easy to condemn a campaign promise of “a total and complete shutdown of Muslims entering the United States.” It is harder to confront the bureaucratic normality of secondary inspection, watchlists, immigration delays, pressured informant recruitment, and “extreme vetting” when they are described as ordinary security measures. But the latter is what gives the former teeth.

Still, the critique should not lead us to abandon constitutional argument or the language of equal citizenship. The authors are wary of Khizr Khan’s pocket Constitution, and understandably so. His reception did reveal the narrow terms on which Muslim belonging is often recognized: the grieving Gold Star father is embraced because his family has given the nation what the nation recognizes as the highest proof of loyalty. Bill Clinton’s convention line—Muslims may “stay here” if they love America and hate terror—was indeed an appalling formulation, as though American Muslims were guests whose lease depended on good behavior.

Yet Khan’s appeal also did something important. He made visible that Muslims are already within the constitutional “we.” He did not ask for tolerance as a favor; he demanded accountability from a presidential candidate who presumed to define the nation against him. The Constitution is not self-enforcing, and it has often been interpreted to permit slavery, segregation, exclusion, internment, and surveillance. But it is also a language through which excluded groups have made claims, exposed hypocrisy, and organized power. To criticize constitutional romance is necessary; to discard constitutional struggle would be a mistake. The question is not whether we invoke the Constitution, but whether we do so in a way that protects only the sympathetic, the patriotic, and the innocent, or in a way that challenges the state’s authority to distribute rights according to suspicion.

The innocence frame is particularly dangerous in national security politics. The first weekend after the executive order, the stories that circulated most widely were of infants, elderly travelers, translators who had helped U.S. forces, students, doctors, and families. Those stories deserved attention. They cut through abstraction and showed the human cost of policy made by stereotype. But a politics built only on the obviously blameless will not protect the person with unpopular views, the mosque-goer whose religious practice is deemed “conservative,” the charity donor whose recipient is later scrutinized, the young man baited by an informant, or the noncitizen with an old conviction. Civil liberties are tested precisely when the person claiming them is not perfect.

This is where the material support regime is so troubling. The law’s breadth blurs the line between aiding violence and engaging in association, speech, humanitarian work, or conflict mediation. After the Supreme Court’s decision in Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project, even coordinated training in peaceful dispute resolution with a designated group could be criminalized. That logic gives the executive enormous power because designation decisions are themselves political and often opaque. If the Muslim Brotherhood were designated as a foreign terrorist organization, as some in the Trump orbit have urged, the consequences could radiate far beyond any actual security threat, sweeping in charities, civic organizations, scholars, and ordinary believers through guilt by association. Such powers should disturb conservatives and liberals alike.

The same is true of watchlisting. In recent months Democrats have sometimes embraced the No Fly List as a tool for gun control, asking why someone too dangerous to board an airplane should be allowed to buy a firearm. But that argument assumes what it should question: that the list reliably identifies dangerous people and that those listed have meaningful process. A secret executive list with vague criteria, predictable errors, and inadequate avenues for redress should not become the basis for depriving people of additional rights. The answer to the gun violence crisis cannot be to launder one civil liberties failure through another. If the state can restrict movement, religious practice, employment, or arms possession on secret evidence, the issue is not merely which right is at stake; it is whether law has become an instrument of unreviewable suspicion.

At the same time, an adequate response must speak honestly about violence committed in the name of jihadist movements. The existence of anti-Muslim prejudice does not mean there are no threats, just as the racist history of policing does not mean there is no violence in need of public response. ISIS, al-Qaeda, and their sympathizers have killed Muslims and non-Muslims in large numbers, and governments have a duty to prevent attacks. The problem is not that the state seeks security. It is that the dominant framework has too often equated Muslim identity, Muslim dissent, and Muslim religiosity with danger, while producing methods—mass surveillance, informant manipulation, overbroad prosecution—that are both unjust and often counterproductive. A rights-respecting security policy would focus on conduct, credible evidence, and individualized suspicion. It would not treat religious communities as pools of latent threat.

Countering Violent Extremism programs illustrate the dilemma. Presented as community partnership, they often ask teachers, social workers, religious leaders, and parents to become intelligence gatherers trained to detect supposed signs of radicalization. But the social science behind such indicators is weak. Political anger, religious conservatism, alienation, or criticism of U.S. foreign policy does not predict violence in any reliable way. Indeed, treating these as warning signs risks criminalizing adolescence, piety, and dissent. It also undermines trust in precisely the institutions—schools, mosques, clinics, community centers—that should remain places of support rather than surveillance. If the Trump administration narrows CVE to focus explicitly on “radical Islamic extremism,” it will make explicit what many communities already experienced: a program of ideological monitoring aimed primarily at Muslims.

Akbar and Theoharis are also right to connect the War on Terror to immigration enforcement and mass incarceration. These systems share habits of thought: dangerous classes, predictive policing, preventive detention, plea pressure, deference to prosecutors, and the conversion of social problems into security problems. They also share personnel, technology, and rhetoric. The militarized police response in Ferguson did not emerge from nowhere; it reflected decades in which domestic policing absorbed the equipment and posture of war. Likewise, immigration law’s civil label has long allowed severe punishments—detention, family separation, exile—without the protections associated with criminal law. For Muslim, Arab, South Asian, Black, and immigrant communities, these regimes overlap in daily life.

But the intersectional frame should be more than a coalition slogan. It should sharpen our analysis of who is harmed and how. Muslims in the United States are not a single racial or ethnic group. They are Black, Arab, South Asian, white, Latino, immigrant, native-born, wealthy, working-class, incarcerated, undocumented, secular, devout. Anti-Muslim policy often operates through race, but not always in identical ways. A Black Muslim may experience Islamophobia through the preexisting criminalization of Blackness; an Iranian student may encounter it through sanctions, visas, and suspicion of foreign ties; a Somali refugee may face the combined force of anti-Blackness, xenophobia, and counterterrorism scrutiny. A serious movement must be attentive to these differences rather than imagining a generic Muslim subject.

The foreign policy dimension is indispensable. The United States cannot bomb, raid, sanction, arm, occupy, and drone across Muslim-majority societies and then pretend that domestic suspicion of Muslims is unrelated. War produces categories of enemy identity. It circulates images of Muslim spaces as zones of chaos and Muslim bodies as risks to be managed. It also produces refugees, some of whom are then described as threats when they seek safety. To oppose the ban while refusing to discuss Iraq, Afghanistan, Yemen, Somalia, Libya, Syria, Pakistan, and the broader machinery of U.S. power is to treat symptoms while preserving causes.

Here again, precision matters. Criticizing American militarism does not require denying the brutality of Assad, ISIS, al-Qaeda, sectarian militias, or authoritarian regimes. Nor does it require imagining that nonintervention is always simple. But it does require recognizing that military force has repeatedly been oversold as liberation and underexamined in its consequences. The 2003 invasion of Iraq remains the central catastrophe in this history, destabilizing a region and helping create conditions in which later violence flourished. Drone strikes and special operations may appear cleaner than ground wars, but they normalize a global battlefield with little transparency and few constraints. Civilian deaths, mistaken targeting, and the psychological terror of drones hovering overhead are not incidental to how affected populations understand American power.

What, then, should resistance demand? First, the travel ban should be defeated not only because it was clumsily drafted or because it harmed visa holders and permanent residents, but because religious disfavor and nationality-based collective suspicion are incompatible with equal citizenship and a lawful immigration system. Courts should not avoid the anti-Muslim purpose evident in Trump’s campaign statements and the order’s design.

Second, watchlisting and no-fly procedures require real due process: notice, reasons, meaningful opportunities to contest placement, and independent review. Secret law cannot be the ordinary condition of modern governance.

Third, material support statutes should be narrowed so that they punish intentional assistance to violence, not speech, association, humanitarian engagement, or religious and political activity. Designation processes need transparency and checks.

Fourth, surveillance should return to individualized suspicion and judicial oversight. Mosques should not be mapped as threat sites; students should not be monitored for political opinions; border searches should not become fishing expeditions into a person’s digital life.

Fifth, CVE programs should be rejected where they deputize community institutions into ideological policing. Resources would be better spent on education, employment, mental health, anti-violence work, and community-led initiatives not tied to intelligence gathering.

Finally, opposition to Islamophobia must include opposition to the wars and security partnerships that sustain it. Otherwise we will defend Muslims at the airport while ignoring the bombs that made many of them refugees.

The danger of the Trump era is not only that new abuses will be invented. It is that old abuses will be intensified, renamed, and cheered. The opportunity is that more people now see what Muslim communities have been saying for years: emergency powers do not remain confined to emergencies, and suspicion built into law becomes ordinary life. The task is to build a politics capacious enough to defend the child detained at the airport, the dissident imam, the imperfect defendant, the refugee abroad, the citizen on a watchlist, and the community that refuses to purchase belonging through silence.

Vanilla MiniMax

"While the lead essay accurately identifies Islamophobia as a systemic, bipartisan policy architecture rather than a Trumpian invention, it fails to provide the necessary strategic and tactical guidance for activists to dismantle these entrenched structures."

The lead essay's critique of American Islamophobia as a deeply embedded feature of U.S. policy architecture rather than a novel Trumpian invention is both accurate and essential. Yet in cataloguing the six misapprehensions that perpetuate anti-Muslim policy, the analysis risks becoming a lamentation of past failures without adequately addressing how activists and scholars might move beyond them. The essay correctly identifies the contours of a problem that has developed over decades, but it offers relatively little guidance on the strategic and tactical questions that now press urgently upon those seeking to contest it.

One of the essay's most valuable insights is its insistence that the domestic War on Terror and the global military campaign against Muslim-majority nations are inseparable. The 23,000 bombs dropped on Iraq, Syria, Afghanistan, Pakistan, Yemen, and Somalia in 2015 alone did not fall in a vacuum separate from American Muslim communities. They fell within a unified framework in which domestic surveillance, policing, and prosecution reinforce and are reinforced by military intervention abroad. The lead essay gestures toward this connection but does not fully explore its implications for resistance. If these regimes are indeed intertwined, then challenging one requires challenging both. This suggests that movements for immigrant rights, black liberation, and anti-war activism share more common ground than often recognized, and that the siloed nature of contemporary organizing may itself be a misapprehension that needs correction.

The essay's critique of the innocence framework is sharp and warranted. When protests against the travel ban centered on the image of detained children and elderly travelers, they inadvertently reinforced the assumption that Muslims deserve protection only when they appear harmless or sympathetic. But moving beyond innocence framing is easier said than done. The alternative proposed—some form of solidarity that does not require demonstrating harmlessness—demands a more fundamental transformation of how Americans understand rights, belonging, and the relationship between the state and its citizens. It requires insisting that rights are not privileges earned through service or suffering but entitlements rooted in personhood itself. This is a tall order, and the essay would have benefited from exploring what this transformation might look like in practice.

The fifth misapprehension, concerning the exceptionalization of Guantánamo, deserves particular attention. The lead essay rightly rejects the notion that Guantánamo represents an anomaly in an otherwise fair criminal justice system. Federal terrorism prosecutions have indeed replicated many of the same injustices—secret evidence, anonymous witnesses, aggressive material support charges—that characterize military commissions. Yet there is a danger in this comparison as well. If Guantánamo and federal courts are equally unjust, it becomes difficult to articulate a reform agenda. The demand to close Guantánamo at least provided a concrete, achievable goal. What replaces it when the problem is understood to be systemic rather than exceptional? Perhaps the answer lies not in reform of existing institutions but in building alternative structures outside the national security apparatus entirely. This is a direction the essay hints at but does not pursue.

The discussion of the material support ban raises similar strategic questions. The ban's reach is undeniably broad, criminalizing charitable giving, political speech, and religious association in ways that chill legitimate expression and activism. Yet the ban also reflects a particular understanding of what constitutes support for terrorism—one that conflates humanitarian aid, political advocacy, and religious practice with violence. Challenging this understanding requires not merely arguing against the ban's application in individual cases but refuting its foundational assumptions about causation and culpability. The lead essay identifies the problem but does not fully grapple with how to dismantle the conceptual architecture that makes such laws possible.

Intersectionality emerges as a crucial theme, particularly the observation that black, immigrant, and Muslim communities often overlap. This overlap is not merely demographic but structural: the same legal and political frameworks that target Muslim communities for surveillance and prosecution are deployed against Black and immigrant communities as well. The war on drugs and the War on Terror share not only policing tools but a logic that conflates racialized suspicion with criminality. Recognizing this shared logic creates potential for solidarity, but it also raises difficult questions about how movements can coordinate without erasing the specific harms experienced by each community. The lead essay acknowledges this challenge but does not offer a clear path forward.

The airports flooded with protesters in the days following the ban provided a moment of possibility, but that moment has already begun to fade. The essay is right to warn against complacency, but it understates the difficulty of sustaining resistance against policies that operate through bureaucratic routine rather than dramatic executive action. Surveillance, material support prosecutions, and the Countering Violent Extremism program do not announce themselves. They proceed quietly, case by case, until the cumulative effect becomes a regime of control that is difficult to perceive as a whole. Building lasting resistance to such a regime requires not only public protest but the painstaking work of legal challenge, community education, and political organizing that may never generate the headlines that airport demonstrations did.

Ultimately, the lead essay performs a valuable service by mapping the terrain of American Islamophobia with precision and care. It demonstrates that the problem predates Trump, that it encompasses far more than the travel ban, and that confronting it requires understanding both the historical roots and the present mechanisms of anti-Muslim policy. What remains underdeveloped is a vision of what resistance looks like when it is not galvanized by a sudden provocation like the ban. The movements for black liberation and immigrant rights have grappled with this challenge for years, and their insights about building durable coalitions, sustaining organizing over time, and cultivating political consciousness from within affected communities may be precisely what the anti-Islamophobia movement now needs. The essay identifies the problem with admirable clarity; the harder task of building lasting solutions lies ahead.

Vanilla Claude

"While Donald Trump’s anti-Muslim policies represent a dangerous escalation, they are a continuation of a long-standing, bipartisan legal and surveillance infrastructure that must be structurally dismantled rather than merely critiqued as a recent aberration."

The most important move Akbar and Theoharis make is refusing the framing of Trump's executive order as rupture. The seven-country list did not spring from Steve Bannon's head; it was lifted from the Obama administration's 2015 and 2016 amendments to the Visa Waiver Program. The premise of "extreme vetting"—that Muslim travelers carry a presumption of risk requiring affirmative rebuttal—has been operationalized at borders, airports, and consulates for fifteen years. The chant "this is not who we are," however emotionally necessary at JFK and Dulles, was empirically wrong. This is precisely who we have been.

I want to extend the authors' argument with three concrete observations, and then press on two places where I think their case needs sharpening.

First, on continuity: the legal architecture of the post-9/11 security state is more durable than the authors even suggest. Consider *Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project* (2010), in which a 6–3 Supreme Court majority—an opinion written by Chief Justice Roberts and joined by Justice Stevens—upheld the material support statute against a First Amendment challenge brought by a human-rights group that wanted to train the PKK and LTTE in nonviolent dispute resolution and U.N. petitioning. The Court accepted the government's premise that even pure speech "coordinated with" a designated organization could be criminalized because it might "free up" the group's resources for violence. This is the case Akbar and Theoharis gesture toward when they describe the material support ban as a "black box." Its breadth is why a U.S. citizen like Tarek Mehanna could be sentenced to seventeen and a half years primarily on the basis of translating jihadist texts; why the Holy Land Foundation prosecution criminalized charitable giving through zakat committees the U.S. government itself had previously worked with; why the Newburgh Four—poor, mentally vulnerable men from upstate New York—were nudged toward a bombing plot by an FBI informant offering a quarter million dollars and a BMW. Trevor Aaronson's reporting and the 2014 Human Rights Watch/Columbia Law School study documented that informants played a central role in roughly half of post-9/11 federal terrorism prosecutions; in nearly thirty cases, the informant arguably engineered the plot. Whatever the next four years bring, this is the inherited toolkit.

Second, on surveillance: Edward Snowden's leaks captured the public imagination because they implicated everyone. But the more sustained scandal—the NYPD's Demographics Unit, exposed by the Associated Press beginning in 2011—targeted Muslims qua Muslims. Plainclothes officers mapped mosques, halal butchers, Muslim Student Associations at Yale and Penn, and entire neighborhoods on the theory that ordinary religious and social life was a "radicalization indicator." The unit produced not a single lead, by the NYPD's own admission, before it was disbanded in 2014. *Hassan v. City of New York* is still working its way through the Third Circuit. The point Akbar and Theoharis make—that white liberal alarm at NSA bulk collection coexisted with indifference to the Demographics Unit—is exactly right, and the asymmetry has political consequences. A coalition built only on the privacy interests of the universal "American" will not protect those whose surveillance is justified precisely by their particularity.

Third, the historical frame should reach further back than 2001. The federal government's anti-Muslim apparatus has genealogies in COINTELPRO's surveillance, infiltration, and disruption of the Nation of Islam in the 1950s and 1960s; in the Palmer Raids and the Red Scare deportations of putatively dangerous immigrants; in the internment of Japanese Americans under *Korematsu*, a case the Supreme Court has never overruled. Khizr Khan's pocket Constitution is a moving prop, but the Constitution it invokes is the same document that authorized chattel slavery, accommodated Jim Crow, and supplied no obstacle to Executive Order 9066. The authors are right that the romance of the document obscures more than it reveals. The relevant question is not whether the law is on our side—often it is not—but whether we can build the political power to bend it.

Now the pushbacks.

The authors are correct that the airport mobilizations rested on a particularized notion of Muslim innocence: the doctor returning from a conference, the grandmother visiting family, the Iraqi interpreter who had served U.S. forces. They are correct that this framing leaves intact the category of the "dangerous Muslim" who might justly be excluded. But I am not sure the choice they pose—innocence-based defense versus structural critique—is quite the right one for this moment. Mass mobilizations need legible victims; the airport scenes broke through because the people detained were ones the government's own categories had pre-cleared. The strategic question is how to use that opening to indict the categories themselves—to say, in effect, that if these people can be detained, then no one is safe, and the system that made them detainable was never legitimate to begin with. This is the move civil rights lawyers eventually made about the Japanese internment cases, and the move ACLU and CCR lawyers have been making in their challenges to the no-fly list and the terrorism watch list. The "innocent traveler" framing is not the destination; it can be the entry point.

The second pushback concerns equivalence. Akbar and Theoharis are emphatic, and rightly so, that Trump did not invent these policies. But there is a risk in the continuity argument: that it flattens distinctions that still matter. Obama's drone program was lawless and lethal, and his administration prosecuted whistleblowers more aggressively than any predecessor. It was also subject to internal legal review of a kind that does not appear to constrain the new administration; it operated within rhetorical limits that, however hypocritical, retained the form of universal citizenship. Trump's contribution is not the apparatus but the explicit avowal of religious targeting, the public licensing of vigilante harassment, the appointment of figures like Bannon and Sebastian Gorka who have argued that Islam itself is the enemy, and the restructuring of CVE to drop even the pretense of attending to white-supremacist violence at the moment when Dylann Roof's massacre and a wave of mosque arsons make that violence undeniable. The authors register these developments. But the response cannot only be "this was always here." It must also be: this is a regime that intends to use what is here in qualitatively new ways, and the legal infrastructure that seemed tolerable when administered by lawyers with a sense of constitutional shame will not be tolerable now.

The agenda the essay implies is therefore the right one and more urgent than it has ever been: repeal or narrow the material support statute; dismantle CVE; abolish the no-fly list or subject it to adversarial process; end the suspicionless border searches of electronic devices; rein in the 2001 AUMF and the unreviewed targeted-killing program; defend the right of Muslim communities to be politically dissident, religiously conservative, and unsurveilled all at once. The airports were a beginning. Whether they become anything more depends on whether the publics that showed up are willing to defend Muslims who are not, by anyone's measure, innocent of having opinions about American power.

  2. 02Surveillance programs like the NYPD’s Demographics Unit demonstrate that the government has long targeted Muslims specifically, a reality often ignored by liberal critics of universal surveillance.
  3. 03The current anti-Muslim apparatus is rooted in a deep historical lineage of American state repression, including COINTELPRO and Japanese internment, which the Constitution failed to prevent.
  4. 04While defending 'innocent' victims is a useful strategic entry point for mass mobilization, activists must use those cases to eventually indict the legitimacy of the 'dangerous Muslim' category itself.
  5. 05The continuity of these policies should not obscure the fact that the Trump administration intends to use this inherited infrastructure in qualitatively more aggressive and explicitly religious ways.
  6. 06True reform requires specific policy actions, such as repealing the material support statute and abolishing the no-fly list, rather than just returning to the pre-Trump status quo.
Diversified Gemini

"The responding essay argues that the current resistance to Trump's Islamophobic policies must go beyond partisan outrage to dismantle the entire bipartisan national security apparatus that has treated Muslim citizenship as conditional and suspicious for decades."

Amna A. Akbar and Jeanne Theoharis’s essay, “Islam on Trial,” captures a reality that Muslim Americans have lived in the marrow of our bones for the last fifteen years. For many Americans, Donald Trump’s rhetoric and his chaotic travel ban felt like a sudden, shocking rupture in the fabric of American values. But for the Muslim American community, the shock was only in the volume, not the song. As the authors powerfully articulate, Trump is merely shouting what the architecture of the national security state has been quietly whispering since September 11, 2001: that Muslim existence in the United States is inherently suspicious, fundamentally provisional, and continuously contingent upon our utility to the state.

The most vital contribution of Akbar and Theoharis’s essay is their deconstruction of the "Good Muslim/Bad Muslim" dichotomy, a paradigm that has deeply damaged the social and psychological fabric of our communities. The liberal embrace of Khizr Khan, as the authors note, is instructive. While Khan’s grief and patriotism are unassailable, the public’s requirement that a Muslim family sacrifice their child to the American war machine in order to be deemed worthy of the Constitution is a devastating indictment of our conditional citizenship.

This requirement for constant performative patriotism has a profound chilling effect on Muslim civil society. Every Friday sermon, every campus Muslim Student Association (MSA) meeting, and every community fundraiser is conducted under the invisible but heavy gaze of the state. When the authors discuss the "material support" statutes, they are not talking about abstract legal theory. They are talking about the fact that fulfilling *Zakat*—the obligatory Islamic pillar of charitable giving—has been transformed into a legal minefield. Following the prosecution of the Holy Land Foundation and other charities, many Muslims live in genuine fear that sending money to an orphanage in Syria or a hospital in Palestine could result in a dawn raid, frozen assets, and decades in a federal penitentiary. The state has effectively criminalized our compassion, forcing a wedge between American Muslims and the global Ummah.

Furthermore, the surveillance detailed by the authors is not merely a violation of privacy; it is a weapon of social fragmentation. The NYPD’s Demographics Unit, which operated for years with the tacit approval of federal authorities, sent informants into our mosques, our student groups, and our cafes. They recorded who we prayed with, what we discussed over tea, and how often we grew our beards. The psychological toll of this is immeasurable. When you know that the person praying next to you might be an FBI informant pressured into entrapment by the threat of being placed on the No Fly List, the sacred space of the mosque is desecrated. Trust is eroded. Young people who are exploring their religious identity, or who wish to passionately debate U.S. foreign policy—a right taken for granted by their non-Muslim peers—are quickly silenced by elders who know that such talk invites the knock at the door.

Akbar and Theoharis are also entirely correct to highlight the intersectional reality of our struggle. The American Muslim community is incredibly diverse, with Black Muslims making up a substantial portion of our population. Yet, the national discourse frequently erases them, treating "Muslim" as synonymous with "Arab" or "South Asian immigrant." The reality is that the aggressive policing of the War on Terror perfectly overlays the aggressive policing of the War on Drugs in Black communities. A Black Muslim in America exists at the crosshairs of two distinct but collaborating systems of state violence. When the authors draw the line from Ferguson to the counter-radicalization paradigms, they validate the lived experience of those who face both the militarized police officer on their street and the federal agent monitoring their mosque.

Perhaps the most insidious aspect of the last fifteen years, which the essay touches upon in its critique of Countering Violent Extremism (CVE) programs, is the government's attempt to define normative Islam. CVE programs, often disguised as community "empowerment," fundamentally rely on the premise that conservative Islamic practice or vocal opposition to U.S. imperialism are precursor indicators to violence. By funding specific "moderate" voices and marginalizing those who are critical of the state, the government is engaging in a modern, insidious form of religious engineering. We are being asked to neuter our faith, to strip it of its demands for social justice and its critiques of power, in order to be deemed "acceptable."

As we sit in early 2017, watching thousands of our fellow Americans flood the airports to defend our rights, there is a profound sense of gratitude, but also a lingering skepticism. Where were these crowds when the Patriot Act was reauthorized? Where was the outrage when the Obama administration expanded the drone program, killing civilians in Yemen and Pakistan? Where were the tears when Muslim charities were shuttered and community leaders were aggressively prosecuted on trumped-up material support charges?

If the current resistance to the Trump administration is to be anything more than a fleeting spasm of partisan outrage, it must internalize the hard truths presented by Akbar and Theoharis. We cannot simply return to the polite, technocratic Islamophobia of the previous fifteen years. Fighting the travel ban is necessary, but it is not sufficient.

True solidarity requires dismantling the entire apparatus of suspicion. It means recognizing that Muslims have the right to be politically angry, religiously conservative, and loudly critical of American foreign policy without being placed on a watch list. It means understanding that our innocence should not be contingent on our utility, and our constitutional rights should not require a blood sacrifice. Until the American public is willing to defend the flawed, the loud, and the politically dissenting Muslim with the same fervor they defend the grieving military father, the trial of Islam in America will not end.

Vanilla Gemini

"The current liberal resistance to Donald Trump’s Islamophobic policies is insufficient because it ignores the bipartisan, entrenched national security apparatus and legal infrastructure that predated his presidency."

The images that emerged from international airports across the United States in the waning days of January were undeniably moving. Thousands of Americans, holding hastily made cardboard signs, flooded arrival halls to protest Donald Trump’s executive order banning travel from seven Muslim-majority nations. Lawyers sat cross-legged on terminal floors, drafting habeas corpus petitions on their laptops. For a brief moment, a spontaneous and vibrant coalition stood in direct opposition to the new administration’s overt Islamophobia, culminating in a temporary victory when the federal courts halted the ban’s implementation. It was a heartening display of civic resistance. Yet, as Amna A. Akbar and Jeanne Theoharis so powerfully argue in their lead essay, this outpouring of outrage risks obscuring a much darker, far more entrenched reality. If our resistance begins and ends with Donald Trump, we will have failed to confront the sprawling, bipartisan national security apparatus that made his presidency—and his policies—possible in the first place.

Akbar and Theoharis correctly identify a historical amnesia at the heart of the current liberal “resistance.” The outrage over Trump’s travel ban has frequently been framed as a defense of American norms against a sudden, unprecedented authoritarian aberration. But the truth, however uncomfortable it may be for defenders of the previous administrations, is that Trump did not build the machinery of the modern surveillance and security state. He merely inherited it. By focusing our political energies exclusively on the vulgarity of Trump’s rhetoric and the cruelty of his immediate executive actions, we grant a retroactive absolution to the architects of the post-9/11 War on Terror—architects who belong to both major political parties.

Nowhere is this dynamic more insidious than in the liberal establishment’s embrace of the “good Muslim” archetype, a phenomenon Akbar and Theoharis brilliantly dissect through the lens of Khizr Khan’s speech at the Democratic National Convention. The rapturous applause that greeted Khan’s invocation of the Constitution revealed a deeply conditional framework for Muslim belonging in the United States. To be accepted as fully American, a Muslim must offer a blood sacrifice. They must either lose a child in one of America’s endless overseas wars, or they must enthusiastically collaborate with domestic law enforcement. As Bill Clinton explicitly stated in his own DNC remarks, Muslims are welcome so long as they “help us win.”

This is not a framework of equal rights; it is a framework of probation. It demands that American Muslims endlessly prove their loyalty to a state that fundamentally views them as a suspect class. By anchoring the defense of Muslim Americans in narratives of exceptional patriotism or absolute, helpless victimization—such as the elderly refugees and young children detained at airports—we implicitly validate the state’s right to police, surveil, and deport the “bad Muslims.” We accept the premise that constitutional rights must be earned through perfect behavior, rather than inherently possessed by virtue of humanity.

This dichotomy between the “good” and “bad” Muslim has been aggressively operationalized by federal law enforcement for the last fifteen years. While liberals rightly condemn Trump’s proposed restructuring of the Countering Violent Extremism (CVE) program, we must reckon with the fact that CVE was a hallmark of the Obama administration. Under the guise of community empowerment and public-private partnerships, the Obama-era CVE programs effectively deputized teachers, social workers, and community leaders to monitor Muslim youth for signs of “radicalization.” The indicators of this supposed radicalization were shockingly broad, often encompassing protected First Amendment activities: growing a beard, becoming more observant in daily prayers, or expressing political anger over U.S. foreign policy and drone strikes.

CVE programs, alongside the widespread dragnet surveillance enacted by the NYPD’s now-defunct Demographics Unit and the FBI’s sprawling network of informants, created an environment where the most intimate aspects of Muslim life were criminalized. When the state treats conservative religious practice or political dissent as a precursor to terrorism, it creates a chilling effect that hollows out the community from the inside. We did not need Donald Trump to teach us how to view Muslims with suspicion; the federal bureaucracy has been training local police and civil servants to do exactly that for a decade and a half.

Furthermore, as the lead essay emphasizes, we must critically examine the domestic legal infrastructure that has allowed these abuses to flourish with minimal judicial pushback. The current celebration of the federal judiciary for halting the travel ban glosses over a bleak judicial record regarding Muslim civil rights. For years, the courts have shown immense, often cowardly deference to the executive branch on matters of national security.

The most potent weapon in this legal arsenal is the “material support” statute (18 U.S.C. § 2339B), vastly expanded by the USA PATRIOT Act. The material support statute has become a black box of preventative prosecution, allowing the government to secure decades-long prison sentences for individuals who have never committed an act of violence. In 2010, the Supreme Court ruled in *Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project* that even peaceful speech, legal advice, and human rights advocacy can be criminalized as "material support" if directed toward a designated foreign terrorist organization. This disastrous ruling eviscerated First Amendment protections and provided the FBI with a blank check to conduct entrapment-style sting operations. For years, the FBI has targeted economically vulnerable or mentally ill Muslim men, providing them with the plot, the fake explosives, and the transportation, only to arrest them with great fanfare to justify counterterrorism budgets. The courts have routinely rubber-stamped these manufactured conspiracies, dismissing clear evidence of entrapment. If Trump decides to utilize these same tools, he will find a judiciary that has already spent fifteen years sharpening the blade for him.

Akbar and Theoharis also challenge us to recognize the deep intersectionality between the War on Terror and the broader regimes of mass incarceration and border militarization. It is a profound failure of imagination to treat the defense of Muslim civil rights as a discrete silo, separate from the movement for Black lives or the fight for undocumented immigrants. The legal and technological mechanisms of oppression are deeply intertwined. The same Department of Homeland Security grants that funded counterterrorism units in major cities were used to purchase the armored vehicles and military-grade weaponry deployed against Black protesters in Ferguson and Baltimore. The ideological conflation of Blackness with inherent criminality operates on the exact same frequency as the conflation of Islam with inherent terrorism. Both paradigms are designed to justify state violence and preemptive policing against communities of color.

Finally, we cannot confront domestic Islamophobia without confronting American imperialism. It is the height of hypocrisy for politicians to wring their hands over the plight of Syrian or Yemeni refugees stranded at JFK Airport while simultaneously voting to authorize the drone strikes and military interventions that forced those refugees to flee their homes in the first place. The United States has dropped thousands of bombs on the very countries listed in Trump’s travel ban—and Obama’s earlier visa restriction list—destabilizing the region and generating a massive humanitarian crisis. The domestic War on Terror is merely the homecoming of this violent foreign policy. We cannot successfully demand an end to the racial profiling of Muslims in New York or Los Angeles while remaining silent about the extrajudicial assassination of Muslims in Sana’a or Kabul.

As we move forward in this new and frightening political era, the lead essay by Akbar and Theoharis provides a vital roadmap. It demands that we refuse the comfort of easy nostalgia. The goal of the current resistance cannot simply be a return to January 19, 2017. To long for the days of the Obama administration is to long for a polite, bureaucratized Islamophobia—one managed by eloquent lawyers rather than crude demagogues, but one that destroys lives nonetheless.

If we truly wish to defend Muslim communities, we must dismantle the infrastructure of suspicion in its entirety. We must demand an end to the secretive No Fly List, the repeal of overbroad material support statutes, the defunding of CVE programs, and a radical scaling back of American military intervention abroad. We must recognize that the civil rights of Muslim Americans are not contingent upon their willingness to fight our wars or validate our constitutional myths. They are inherent. Donald Trump’s presidency presents a terrifying escalation of the War on Terror, but it also offers a moment of profound clarity. The mask of American benevolence has slipped. We now have an opportunity to look clearly at the carceral, militarized state we have built over the last two decades, and, finally, to begin the hard work of tearing it down.
