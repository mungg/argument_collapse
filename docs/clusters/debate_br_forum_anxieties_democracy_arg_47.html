"The current crisis of democratic dysfunction is primarily driven by the absence of a unifying external threat, which previously disciplined internal partisan divisions and forced a consensus on democratic fundamentals."

The most illuminating moment in Katznelson's essay comes when he quotes Michel Albert's observation that the end of Soviet communism meant democracy no longer had external enemies—only internal debates about its proper form. This point deserves far more development than Katznelson provides. When democracy faced communism, the stakes were existential. Democratic citizens could unite against a common threat, subordinating internal disagreements to survival. Today, without external enemy, internal divisions become primary. This may explain the paradoxical combination of relative peace with democratic dysfunction.

The Cold War provided what political scientists call a "rally round the flag" effect. American democracy's most successful periods—World War II, the early Cold War, the 1990s—coincided with external threats that focused political attention and enabled bipartisan cooperation. The post-Cold War era, by contrast, has seen partisan polarization steadily increase. This isn't coincidental. Democrats and Republicans can no longer agree on basic facts, let alone policies, because there's no external framework establishing shared premises.

Katznelson might argue that this makes democratic resilience more important, not less. But perhaps we should question whether democracy's current form was ever sustainable without external discipline. The New Deal succeeded partly because conservatives and liberals shared a consensus against fascism. That consensus has dissolved. Today's Republican Party explicitly rejects the regulatory state that FDR built. Without external threat or internal consensus, democracy has become a contest over fundamentals rather than variations on shared premises.

This analysis suggests a darker prognosis than Katznelson acknowledges. He believes democracy can adapt through institutional reform and normative commitment. But adaptation requires some baseline agreement about what democracy is for. When one major party questions whether climate change is real, whether healthcare is a right, whether voting should be easy, there's no institutional mechanism that can bridge these divides. The filibuster, the Electoral College, and campaign finance are symptoms, not causes.

What would genuine renewal require? At minimum, a reconstituted consensus about democratic fundamentals—shared acceptance that government should regulate markets for common benefit, that all citizens deserve political voice, that policy disagreements should be resolved through normal politics rather than permanent minority rule. Katznelson doesn't tell us how such a consensus might emerge. Perhaps he doesn't know. The Cold War built one through threat. We have no equivalent today.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The historical success of democratic projects like the New Deal was predicated on a shared existential opposition to external enemies like fascism and communism.
  2. 02Without the 'rally round the flag' effect provided by the Cold War, internal political divisions have become primary, leading to a breakdown in the shared premises required for bipartisan cooperation.
  3. 03Institutional symptoms of decay, such as polarization and gridlock, cannot be solved by reform alone because there is no longer a baseline agreement on what the government is for.
  4. 04The lack of a modern equivalent to the existential threats of the 20th century makes the reconstitution of a democratic consensus unlikely, suggesting a darker prognosis for democracy than Katznelson acknowledges.
