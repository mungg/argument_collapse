"The contemporary left must move beyond mere ideological analysis and adopt the formative institutional practices of religious communities to build durable, thick forms of political belonging."

The most provocative feature of Mie Inouye’s essay is not its critique of deference politics or its defense of conflict, important as both are. It is the claim that the contemporary left has something to learn from Mormonism. For many readers, especially those who have suffered under conservative religious authority, this will sound perverse. Yet Inouye’s point is not that the left should imitate Mormon theology or ecclesiastical hierarchy. It is that solidarity requires formation, and religious communities are among the few remaining institutions in American life that still know how to form people.

That fact should unsettle secular progressives. Many leftists have inherited habits from Protestantism—confession, testimony, excommunication, moralized language, suspicion of impurity—while rejecting the institutional disciplines that made religious community durable: regular gathering, intergenerational obligation, care for the sick, shared meals, music, ritual, and a calendar that organizes time. We have kept the sermon and lost the congregation.

Inouye’s account of growing up Mormon in the Boston area conveys the power of such formation. She learned to attend meetings with people she did not like, to participate in mutual aid, to encounter class and ideological difference, and to endure boredom. That last item should not be dismissed. Boredom is one of the foundations of durable association. A society trained by markets and screens to seek constant stimulation will struggle to build democratic organizations, because much of democracy is tedious. Someone must reserve the room, count the votes, call the absent member, bring the food, read the minutes, and sit through the report from the committee that has not done its work.

Religious communities also understand that belonging precedes agreement. In many congregations, people are bound together not because they have arrived at matching political analyses but because they share practices. They sing, kneel, study, mourn, celebrate, and serve. Over time, these practices create obligations that can survive disagreement. In a political culture increasingly sorted by education, geography, lifestyle, and media consumption, that is no small achievement.

Of course, the achievement is morally ambiguous. Inouye is clear about this. Mormonism helped her Japanese American family survive after internment, but it also helped assimilate them into an American order structured by whiteness, capitalism, heteronormativity, and obedience. The giant pink sugar cookie given to her father is a perfect symbol: sweet, welcoming, culturally specific, and politically loaded. It said, “You belong,” but also, “This is what belonging tastes like.”

That ambivalence is not unique to Mormonism. The Black church has been a seedbed of liberation struggles and a site of patriarchy and respectability politics. Catholic parishes have sustained immigrant communities while enforcing hierarchy and sexual conservatism. Synagogues, mosques, temples, and evangelical churches have all provided mutual aid, identity, and discipline while also drawing boundaries between the saved and the suspect, the respectable and the deviant, the family and the outsider. Religion binds, and binding can protect or dominate.

The question for the left is whether it can create thick forms of belonging without reproducing the coercive features that have so often accompanied them. This is harder than it sounds. Thin belonging is easy: a mailing list, a slogan, a march, a hashtag, a donation. Thick belonging requires claims on time and conduct. It asks people to show up when they are tired, give money when no crisis is trending, forgive one another without forgetting harm, and submit personal preference to collective need. Modern people often want the fruits of thick community without its demands.

Inouye’s “social endurance” is therefore a spiritual as well as political category. It names a willingness to remain available to transformation through repeated contact. Religious traditions have long known that transformation rarely occurs through argument alone. It occurs through practice. You become the kind of person who prays by praying, the kind who serves by serving, the kind who forgives by attempting forgiveness before you feel forgiving. Likewise, one becomes a comrade not by achieving the correct opinion about solidarity but by doing the unromantic work of solidarity.

The left has sometimes tried to generate this through ideology alone. If people can be persuaded that capitalism is exploitative, racism structural, gender hierarchical, and empire violent, they will act together. But belief does not reliably produce conduct. Many people believe true things and remain isolated, frightened, vain, or passive. Others hold confused beliefs but behave with remarkable loyalty and courage because they are embedded in communities that expect such behavior. Formation is not reducible to analysis.

This does not mean analysis is unimportant. Religious formation can produce reactionary solidarity as easily as liberatory solidarity. Inouye’s Uncle Gerald embodies the contradiction: generous rural doctor, community servant, patriarch, Trump-adjacent libertarian, COVID victim. He had endurance and obligation, but they were organized around a world that made certain vulnerabilities unspeakable. He could care for poor patients while rejecting “victim mentality.” He could risk himself for a friend while refusing public health wisdom. Community without emancipatory analysis can become a school for sacrificial reaction.

The task, then, is to combine the left’s critical intelligence with religion’s formative intelligence. We need organizations that can say both “the world is structured by domination” and “someone has to bring soup to Maria after surgery.” We need political education and potlucks, campaigns and childcare, internal democracy and ritual. We need songs, not because songs are decorative, but because people remember what they sing together. We need holidays of movement memory: not as branding exercises, but as ways of locating ourselves in a lineage of struggle. We need practices for welcoming children and elders, because no movement that consists only of young adults can endure.

We also need a healthier relationship to commitment. Many secular progressive spaces are simultaneously demanding and noncommittal. They demand exact language, instant responsiveness, and public moral clarity, but they offer weak structures of belonging in return. Religious communities often do the opposite: they tolerate eccentricity or ideological confusion if one keeps showing up and contributing to the life of the body. There is wisdom there. A movement that expels everyone who speaks clumsily will become small and anxious. A movement that never disciplines harmful behavior will become unsafe and unjust. The balance can only be learned through durable practice.

Inouye is right to resist permanence as an ideal. The Mormon “eternal family” depends on exclusions that democratic solidarity cannot accept. Political organizations should be revisable; people should be able to leave; no human institution should claim eternal authority. But if the left rejects permanence, it must still honor duration. A campaign may end. An organization may dissolve. But people need some continuity of shared life if they are to survive defeats. Religion understands defeat. It gives people practices for grief, repentance, waiting, and recommitment. The left, by contrast, often swings between euphoria and despair.

The aftermath of the 2020 uprisings illustrates the problem. Millions entered the streets and felt, perhaps for the first time, collective power. But many had no durable community to receive them afterward. Social media supplied judgment faster than formation. Nonprofits supplied campaigns but not always belonging. Universities supplied language but not courage. When repression, backlash, confusion, and internal conflict arrived, many drifted away. They had experienced a political revival without joining a church.

The metaphor is imperfect, but useful. A liberatory politics does not need dogma in the religious sense. It does need disciplines of attention, obligation, memory, and repair. It needs to make demands on people without claiming ownership of their souls. It needs to cultivate endurance without sanctifying suffering. It needs rituals that bind without suppressing dissent.

Inouye’s essay invites secular radicals to ask an uncomfortable question: what kinds of people do our organizations produce? Not just what policies do they endorse, what statements do they issue, or what theories do they cite, but what habits do they instill? Do they make people braver, kinder, more strategic, more honest, more capable of disagreement? Do they connect the lonely, feed the hungry, protect the vulnerable, and discipline the powerful? Do they help people stay?

If not, we should be humble before institutions we may otherwise reject. Mormonism, like many religious traditions, has often used solidarity for assimilation and control. But it has also preserved knowledge about how human beings become obligated to one another over time. The left does not need to become religious. It does need to become less naïve about the social forms that sustain faith, sacrifice, and collective action. Without those forms, “solidarity forever” is only a song. With them, solidarity might become a way of life.

"Solidarity should be understood not as a state of perfect agreement or shared identity, but as a practiced craft of 'social endurance'—the capacity to remain in structured, difficult relationships with people one did not choose in order to build durable power."

Mie Inouye’s essay is most persuasive when it shifts the question of solidarity away from the fantasy of perfect agreement and toward the habits that make disagreement survivable. Much contemporary political argument assumes that coalitions fail because they lack the correct theory: too much race, not enough class; too much class, not enough race; too much moralism, not enough material interest; too much economism, not enough moral imagination. These arguments matter. But Inouye is right that they often leave out a more mundane and more decisive question: who will still be in the room after the third bad meeting?

That question is not glamorous, but it is political. The great weakness of many contemporary movements is not that people lack opinions, moral commitments, or even courage. The summer of 2020 showed that millions of people were willing to march, risk arrest, donate money, and change their language. The problem was that relatively few people were embedded in organizations capable of converting that moral shock into durable power. Many people had a movement experience without acquiring a movement home. They returned to workplaces, campuses, families, and social media feeds where politics was intense but organizational life was thin.

Inouye’s phrase “social endurance” names a capacity that previous generations often acquired in churches, unions, fraternal associations, ethnic clubs, neighborhood organizations, political machines, and extended families. These institutions were never innocent. Many were racist, patriarchal, homophobic, corrupt, or authoritarian. But they trained people to do something that liberal individualism and digital life both discourage: remain in structured relationship with people one did not choose.

That training matters because solidarity is not simply a feeling of warmth toward the oppressed. Nor is it identical to enlightened self-interest. It is a practice of binding one’s fate, at least partially, to others. Binding is unpleasant. It means being outvoted. It means doing work one did not design. It means hearing criticism that is badly phrased but partly true. It means discovering that one’s comrades have bad taste, bad manners, bad theories, or bad habits. It means, as Inouye says, showing up even when one does not like the other people in the room.

The Mormon material in the essay is valuable precisely because it resists sentimentalization. Inouye does not present the church as a model to be copied. Her account of Mormonism is double-edged: it gave her family belonging after internment, but it also helped assimilate them into a racial and economic order that demanded conformity. It created endurance, but in part through hierarchy, discipline, and exclusion. This is the dilemma facing the left. The institutions that have historically produced commitment have often done so by narrowing the range of permissible conflict. They teach people to stay by making exit costly. A democratic politics cannot simply reproduce that structure.

Still, there is no way around the need for formation. The left often speaks as if political subjects arrive fully made: the worker knows their interest; the racialized subject knows domination; the ally knows they must defer; the organizer merely activates what is already there. But political life is more developmental than that. People learn what they want by acting with others. They discover what they fear, what they resent, what they can tolerate, and what they are willing to risk. Bob Lee’s question in American Revolution 2—“What do you want?”—is powerful because it assumes that the answer is not automatically available. It must be drawn out, tested, and connected to the wants of others.

That is why Inouye’s critique of deference politics is so important. Deference may look like humility, but often it is a way to avoid the harder work of relationship. If a relatively privileged participant simply adopts the language and demands of someone else, they may temporarily reduce tension. But they have not explained why they are there. They have not offered themselves as a political person with stakes, fears, interests, and contradictions. Their unspoken motives remain available to reappear later as resentment, withdrawal, or sabotage.

The alternative is not for the privileged to dominate the conversation in the name of authenticity. Inouye is careful on this point. Standpoint theory contains an indispensable truth: social location shapes perception. People subject to policing, eviction, medical neglect, or workplace exploitation know things that others do not know, or know them with a clarity others lack. But this insight should deepen inquiry, not end it. It should make us ask more questions, not fewer. What do you see from where you stand? What can I not see from where I stand? What do we each misunderstand about our own situation? What can we learn only by acting together?

The challenge is to institutionalize those questions. Social endurance cannot remain a mood or an exhortation. It requires forms. Meetings need agendas, roles, follow-up, food, child care, translation, transportation, and norms of disagreement. Organizations need political education that is neither indoctrination nor endless graduate seminar. They need procedures for leadership development and leadership removal. They need rituals of welcome that do not become purity tests. They need ways to distinguish between conflict that clarifies and conflict that merely consumes.

Here the old institutions still have something to teach. Churches know that repetition matters. Unions know that grievances can become political education. Twelve-step groups know the power of testimony bound by form. Tenant unions know that one person’s leak, rent hike, or eviction notice can become a collective demand. Even good classrooms know that people speak more honestly when the structure is clear enough to hold uncertainty.

But social endurance also requires limits. Some people should leave some rooms. Abuse, bigotry, manipulation, and chronic bad faith cannot be transfigured by patience alone. There is a difference between tolerating annoyance and tolerating domination. Democratic solidarity has to preserve the possibility of exit even as it cultivates the discipline of return. Otherwise endurance becomes submission, and the lesson of Mormonism’s darker side is forgotten.

Inouye’s essay suggests that solidarity is less like discovering a preexisting identity than like acquiring a craft. Crafts require apprenticeship, repetition, correction, and time. They also require materials that resist us. The fantasy of frictionless politics is therefore anti-political. It imagines that the right people, with the right analysis, will move together without strain. But any coalition capable of changing the world will include people whose presence is difficult for one another. The difficulty is not incidental to solidarity; it is the medium in which solidarity is made.

This does not mean that conflict is automatically productive. Many left spaces have mastered the art of making conflict both moralized and inconsequential. People fight over language while avoiding decisions; they personalize strategic disagreement; they confuse intensity with seriousness. Social endurance should not mean more meetings for their own sake. It should mean building organizations where conflict is tied to action, where action produces new knowledge, and where new knowledge reshapes the organization.

Perhaps the most moving line in Inouye’s essay is her answer to Henry, revised through the memory of Uncle Gerald: she organizes because the same system that killed George Floyd killed her uncle, “however more softly and however more self-inflicted.” That formulation does not erase differences. It does not claim that a rural doctor who treated himself with hydroxychloroquine suffered the same violence as a Black man murdered by police. Instead it makes a political connection across unequal injuries. It says that domination does not touch everyone in the same way, but it can still implicate us in a common fight.

That is the kind of solidarity we need: not unity, not innocence, not permanent harmony, but disciplined exposure to one another in pursuit of shared power. The left does not need more people who enjoy meetings. It needs more people who can endure them long enough to become useful.

"While social endurance and movement-building are essential for solidarity, true transformation requires translating that energy into durable political institutions and governing power."

Mie Inouye’s essay rightly emphasizes the social and emotional work required for solidarity, but it says less about the institutional form that can translate solidarity into governing power. Movements, meetings, and coalitions matter. Yet in a democracy, durable transformation also requires parties, elections, legislation, administration, and the unglamorous work of implementation. If solidarity is to survive beyond moments of protest, it must become capable of governing.

This is not an argument against movements. The abolition of slavery, labor protections, women’s suffrage, civil rights, environmental regulation, LGBTQ rights, and many redistributive policies were driven by movements that disrupted ordinary politics. But movements win lasting change when they alter institutions. They need vehicles that can contest budgets, appoint officials, write rules, enforce rights, and survive backlash. In the United States, that means engaging the state, and usually the party system, however frustrating and compromised it is.

Inouye focuses on the summer of 2020 as a moment of extraordinary interracial protest that did not become a sustained multiracial movement against policing and mass incarceration. One reason is the absence of durable organizations. Another is the difficulty of converting protest energy into a political program capable of winning majorities and governing cities. “Defund the police” expressed a real insight: policing had become the default response to social problems better addressed through housing, health care, education, and employment. But as an electoral slogan and governing agenda, it faced predictable challenges: public fear of crime, municipal budget complexity, union resistance, media backlash, and disagreement within Black and brown communities themselves.

A stronger organizational left might have handled these challenges better. It could have developed local platforms distinguishing immediate cuts to abusive units, non-police crisis response, violence interruption, housing investment, and long-term decarceration. It could have trained candidates, supported elected officials under attack, negotiated with unions, and communicated with residents worried about both police violence and neighborhood violence. Instead, in many places, the energy of 2020 met fragmented nonprofits, activist networks, progressive legislators with limited power, and Democratic Party officials eager to manage symbolism while avoiding structural change.

This is where political parties matter. Parties are among the few institutions designed to aggregate interests across difference. They are maddening because they include people who disagree, compromise, and seek power. But those are also their virtues. A party can connect a tenant’s rent, a teacher’s contract, a climate investment, a criminal justice reform, and a tax policy into a governing agenda. It can make tradeoffs explicit. It can discipline politicians, develop leaders, and give voters a way to reward or punish coalitions. Without party-like structures, movements often either remain outside power or are absorbed by politicians who face little accountability.

The United States has weak parties in some respects and rigid two-party competition in others. The Democratic Party is a coalition of labor, professionals, racial minorities, urban voters, suburban moderates, social movements, donors, and elected officials whose interests often diverge. The Republican Party, especially in the Trump era, has fused plutocratic economics, cultural backlash, authoritarian temptation, and intense partisan identity, though it too contains internal tensions. For left organizers, the Democratic coalition is both the obvious terrain and a source of constant disappointment. But there is no shortcut around it at national scale.

Inouye’s critique of deference politics applies to party politics as well. Democratic politicians often engage in symbolic deference: adopting activist language, celebrating diversity, issuing statements, and appointing task forces while leaving power relations intact. Conversely, some activists treat electoral compromise as betrayal, avoiding responsibility for building majorities. Both habits are evasive. Real solidarity would require constituencies to enter coalition with clear demands and a willingness to negotiate without disappearing.

Labor offers the clearest path. Unions are schools of social endurance because they bind people who may not share ideology but do share a workplace and a contract. They also connect civil society to electoral politics. When unions are strong, they can pressure parties materially, not just morally. The decline of union density has weakened the social basis for egalitarian politics and left the Democratic Party more dependent on affluent professionals and donors. Reviving labor is therefore not merely an economic project; it is a democratic one.

Recent organizing among teachers, nurses, logistics workers, service workers, graduate students, and autoworkers suggests possibilities. These struggles are multiracial and often connect wages to broader public goods. Teachers have argued that their working conditions are students’ learning conditions. Nurses have connected staffing ratios to patient safety. Such campaigns can produce the kind of solidarity Inouye describes: not sentimental unity, but conflictual cooperation rooted in shared stakes. They also generate political demands: labor law reform, public funding, health care policy, industrial strategy.

But labor alone is not enough. The left needs tenant organizations, climate groups, racial justice organizations, immigrant rights networks, disability justice groups, reproductive freedom advocates, and rural institutions linked through electoral strategy. Otherwise each fights alone, and politicians can placate them separately. The question is how to build a coalition broad enough to govern without suppressing internal disagreement.

Here Inouye’s emphasis on conflict is indispensable. Governing coalitions must fight internally over priorities. Should a city fund mental health crisis teams before expanding affordable housing? Should climate legislation accept permitting reform to accelerate transmission lines? Should prosecutors be replaced, constrained, or pressured? Should universal programs be prioritized over targeted reparative ones? These are not distractions from solidarity. They are what solidarity looks like when it approaches power.

The danger is that internal conflict becomes identity theater or procedural paralysis. Party and movement institutions need rules for decision: conventions, votes, platforms, primaries, endorsements, member assemblies, caucuses, and accountable leadership. Many activists distrust such mechanisms because they can be manipulated. They can. But the alternative is often informal power. Democracy requires procedures, not because procedures guarantee justice, but because they make conflict legible and decisions contestable.

Epistemic humility also has a governing dimension. Policymakers must learn from those affected by policy, but they must also make judgments under uncertainty. A housing policy may help some tenants and hurt others. A climate project may reduce emissions while disturbing local land. A criminal justice reform may reduce incarceration but raise difficult questions about violence. Listening is necessary, but governing cannot mean waiting for perfect consensus. Solidarity in power requires accepting responsibility for imperfect decisions and building mechanisms to revise them.

Inouye’s Mormon example helps clarify what political institutions must and must not do. Mormonism formed people through thick belonging, but its unity depended on hierarchy and exclusion. Parties and unions can develop similar pathologies: loyalty tests, suppression of dissent, protection of abusive leaders, hostility to outsiders. The answer is not to reject durable institutions but to democratize them. Members must be able to challenge leadership, minorities must be able to organize internally, and exit must remain possible. “Solidarity forever” should mean commitment to a struggle, not unconditional obedience to an organization.

The left also needs patriotism of a certain kind, though the word will make some radicals uneasy. To govern a country, one must speak to people who understand themselves as members of it. This does not require denying U.S. crimes—slavery, Indigenous dispossession, exclusion, empire, internment, Jim Crow, and more. It requires framing repair as a common democratic task. The Freedom Budget and the Poor People’s Campaign did this: they articulated material redistribution as fulfillment of democratic promise, not merely rejection of the nation. A multiracial working-class coalition needs a language capacious enough for anger and attachment.

The right has exploited the human desire for belonging by offering nationalism without equality. The left often responds with critique without belonging. Inouye’s essay suggests why that is insufficient. People need meetings, songs, rituals, candidates, institutions, and shared victories. They need to feel that politics is not only an emergency but a home. Parties at their best can provide some of that: a place where local struggles recognize one another as part of a larger project.

Of course, electoral politics can domesticate movements. Politicians may ask activists to mute demands for the sake of electability; donors may block redistribution; party leaders may prefer symbolic inclusion to structural reform. Movements must therefore retain independent power. The relationship should be tense. But tension is not failure. It is the normal condition of democratic solidarity.

If I would amend Inouye’s argument, I would say: social endurance must become political endurance. Not in opposition to her point, but as its extension. We need people who can stay in meetings, and organizations that can stay in campaigns, and coalitions that can stay in power long enough to change law and administration. We need the patience to govern without losing the capacity to protest.

The next uprising will come from circumstances we cannot fully predict: another police murder, economic crisis, climate disaster, attack on democracy, war, or something not yet visible. The question is whether there will be institutions ready to receive that energy and turn it into durable power. Solidarity now means building them before the streets fill again.

"While social endurance is a necessary virtue for collective action, it must be structured by disciplined democratic organization and material struggle to prevent it from becoming a tool of domination or a fetish of interpersonal process."

Mie Inouye’s essay makes a powerful case for one of the least glamorous virtues in politics: showing up. Against a culture that treats disagreement as contamination and exit as self-care, she reminds us that durable collective action requires the ability to remain in relationship with people who annoy, misunderstand, or even wound us. This is especially important on a left that often imagines solidarity either as perfect moral alignment or as the automatic expression of shared material interest. Neither fantasy survives much contact with actual organizing. People enter movements for mixed reasons. They misunderstand their own interests. They bring with them the very hierarchies they hope to abolish. If they cannot stay in the room long enough to be changed, no amount of correct analysis will save them.

Still, I worry that “social endurance” names a necessary condition of solidarity but not yet a sufficient one. Endurance can be radical, as Inouye argues, when it keeps us available to transformation. But endurance can also be a discipline of domination. Many people—especially women, people of color, queer and trans people, workers, immigrants, and the poor—have been trained all their lives to endure: to sit through humiliating meetings, to keep families and workplaces functioning, to absorb insult for the sake of peace, to translate themselves for the benefit of others, to return again and again to institutions that do not love them back. The political question is therefore not only how to cultivate endurance where neoliberal exit has weakened it. It is also how to distinguish emancipatory endurance from the old demand that the injured be patient.

That distinction cannot be made at the level of temperament alone. It depends on power. A white professional learning to remain in a multiracial tenants’ union after being criticized is practicing one kind of endurance. A Black tenant being asked to remain in the same union while white members repeatedly dismiss police harassment as a “secondary issue” is practicing another. Both may need to stay; both may need to be changed. But the costs are not symmetrical, and a theory of solidarity must be able to say so without collapsing into the deference politics Inouye rightly criticizes.

This is where I would put more emphasis on organization than on sociality. Meetings do not transform us simply because we attend them. Conflict does not educate simply because it occurs. The same room can produce courage, resentment, conformity, scapegoating, or burnout depending on its rules, leadership, culture, and connection to action. Endurance becomes politically generative when it is attached to practices that make conflict answerable to a common project: democratic decision-making, clear expectations, political education, leadership development, accountability procedures, and campaigns with stakes beyond the meeting itself. Without these, “staying in the room” can become a fetish of process, a way to mistake proximity for power.

Inouye’s own examples suggest as much. Bob Lee’s organizing in Chicago was not merely an exercise in presence, touch, or interpersonal confrontation. It was embedded in a disciplined political organization with an analysis, a program, and a strategy. Lee did not ask “What do you want?” as a therapeutic prompt. He asked it in the context of a campaign over housing, policing, poverty, and community control. He was eliciting needs so they could be organized into demands and action. That is why the question had force. In many contemporary spaces, by contrast, “What do you want?” floats free of any mechanism for collective power. People are invited to disclose pain, narrate identity, and perform vulnerability, but not necessarily to build the capacity to win anything. Under those conditions, endurance becomes exhausting because nothing changes except the emotional weather in the room.

The same point applies to the summer of 2020. Inouye is right that millions of people felt, however inchoately, that George Floyd’s murder was connected to unemployment, sickness, isolation, state abandonment, and the violence of everyday life under racial capitalism. She is also right that those connections were often left unorganized, displaced into allyship or deference. But the failure was not simply that people lacked the social endurance to fight through disagreement. It was also that the United States had too few mass organizations capable of receiving that insurgent energy and converting it into durable power. Protesters encountered nonprofits, ad hoc formations, social media influencers, local abolitionist groups, unions of uneven strength, electoral campaigns, and Democratic officials eager to metabolize revolt into reform. Some places produced serious organizing. Many did not. The weakness was infrastructural.

This matters because the people most in need of solidarity cannot be asked to wait for the privileged to become transformed through repeated exposure. The clock of political education runs slower than the clock of eviction, deportation, incarceration, climate disaster, medical debt, and police violence. Of course people change through struggle; indeed, they often change only through struggle. But organizations must be capable of acting before everyone has reached clarity about why they are there. A union does not wait for every member to develop a perfect account of racial capitalism before filing a grievance or striking. A tenants’ association does not postpone a rent campaign until every participant has resolved their inherited prejudices. Sometimes the discipline of a common fight precedes the moral and epistemic transformation Inouye seeks.

This is why material interest should not be demoted too quickly. Inouye is persuasive that shared interests do not appear automatically, transparently, or equally across social divisions. But material struggle remains one of the best schools for the kind of solidarity she describes. When workers strike, when tenants withhold rent, when parents fight school closures, when patients demand public health resources, people discover not only what they have in common but also what they do not. They learn who can take risks, who cannot, who is trusted, who is ignored, whose suffering has been normalized, whose anger is treated as a problem. Material campaigns force these questions because they require decisions: What are we demanding? Who is the target? What risks will we take? Who bears them? What counts as a win? In that sense, material interest is not an alternative to political education. It is one of its conditions.

Inouye’s critique of deference politics is especially welcome because deference often masquerades as humility while evading responsibility. To say “I will follow the lead of the most affected” can mean: I recognize that my perspective is partial and that others know things I do not. But it can also mean: I would rather not think, argue, or be accountable for the consequences of my choices. The first attitude is essential; the second is corrosive. Movements need people who can listen without disappearing, speak without dominating, and change their minds without outsourcing judgment. “Epistemic humility” captures part of this ethic.

Yet humility, too, has class inflections. Professional-managerial spaces often reward elaborate performances of self-interrogation while leaving underlying relations untouched. People become fluent in naming their positionality but less capable of taking direction, doing unglamorous work, accepting discipline, or losing a vote. In such settings, humility can become another form of self-display. The antidote is not less reflection, but reflection subordinated to collective work. The test of whether I have learned from others is not whether I can redescribe myself more sensitively. It is whether I act differently when the organization needs canvassers, strike support, childcare, data entry, jail support, meeting facilitation, or money.

I also want to complicate the analogy to religious endurance. Inouye is admirably clear-eyed about Mormonism’s exclusions and hierarchies, and she does not romanticize the church. But the example reveals a hard truth: institutions that reliably produce social endurance often do so by making exit costly, by sacralizing obligation, and by embedding people in thick webs of dependency and surveillance. Modern left organizations usually cannot and should not replicate those mechanisms. They cannot promise eternal family, salvation, or a total world. They cannot command the same authority over marriage, childhood, ritual, and daily life. So the challenge is sharper than “how do we get people to keep showing up?” It is: how do democratic movements create enough obligation to sustain sacrifice without reproducing coercive belonging?

Part of the answer is victory. People endure more when endurance is rewarded not only with moral meaning but with concrete evidence that collective action can alter their lives. The labor movement, at its best, has understood this. A union meeting may be boring, factional, and full of people one would not choose as friends. But if the meeting is connected to a contract campaign that raises wages, protects a coworker, or changes staffing ratios, people learn that attendance matters. Solidarity is then neither sentiment nor mere stamina. It is a practice confirmed by power.

Another part of the answer is permission to leave. This may seem contrary to Inouye’s emphasis, but democratic endurance requires boundaries. Some conflicts are transformative; others are degrading. Some organizations are imperfect vehicles for collective struggle; others are abusive, unserious, or captured by cliques. If we tell people that leaving is always neoliberal fragility, we risk trapping them in miniature versions of the institutions we oppose. The right norm is not “always stay” but “do not leave merely because you are uncomfortable, criticized, outvoted, or bored.” That is a demanding standard. It asks us to examine whether our exit protects our conscience or merely our ego. But it also preserves the possibility that departure can be an act of judgment rather than consumer preference.

The deepest insight in Inouye’s essay is that solidarity is not found; it is made. It is made among people who do not begin with identical interests, identical wounds, or identical dreams. It is made through campaigns, arguments, meals, votes, disappointments, study, repetition, and the strange intimacy of shared tasks. We should not expect unity, and we should be suspicious of organizations that demand it too quickly. But neither should we settle for togetherness as such. The point of staying in the room is to become capable of acting outside it.

So yes: solidarity now requires endurance. But endurance must be organized, bounded, and aimed at power. It must ask more of the privileged than patience and more of the oppressed than continued forbearance. It must be tied to material struggles urgent enough to clarify disagreement and institutions sturdy enough to survive it. If we can build those, then the meeting may become what Inouye hopes it can be: not a refuge from politics, not a performance of virtue, but a place where people learn how to fight for one another without pretending they are the same.

The Romance of the Meeting

I want to be the curmudgeon in this forum. Mie Inouye's essay is graceful and self-aware, and I have learned things from it. But I think it suffers from a peculiar disorder common to a certain kind of left writing in our moment: the romance of the meeting. The premise that what ails the left is our inability to sit through one another's company, and that the cure is a cultivated capacity to do so, mistakes a symptom for a cause.

Most political change does not happen because people sat in rooms with each other and were transformed. It happens because organized blocs, possessing material leverage, force concessions from power. The meeting is one of the places where that bloc gets coordinated. It is not the place where the bloc is constituted, and it is certainly not the place where the bloc wins. The eight-hour day was not won by Bob Lee–style relational organizing in church basements, however much such organizing contributed. It was won by strikes — illegal, often violent, sustained over years — that imposed economic costs on employers until the law changed. The Civil Rights Act of 1964 was not the byproduct of interracial sensitivity workshops. It was the byproduct of a Black freedom movement that made the South ungovernable in front of television cameras until Lyndon Johnson decided he could no longer afford the spectacle.

I am not pretending that movements arise without organization, or that organizations exist without meetings. Of course they don't. But the contemporary left's obsession with the affective texture of coalitional life — who feels seen, who feels alienated, who can endure whom — has slowly displaced the question of how concentrated power gets challenged. Inouye, to her credit, gestures at this displacement. She quotes Piven and Cloward; she acknowledges that organizing exists to make use of socially structured protest opportunities. But the essay then proceeds to spend most of its argumentative energy on the interior life of the meeting, and very little on the question of what the meeting is supposed to produce.

Consider the example she returns to most often: the summer of 2020. Inouye describes her students' experiences in the streets, the felt connections among unemployment, isolation, and police violence, the missed opportunity to forge those connections into something durable. She concludes that what failed was the discursive work — that "forging those connections explicitly would have involved conflict, risk, and time," and that we defaulted to allyship and deference instead. I think this diagnosis is partial at best. What failed in 2020 was not primarily our capacity to talk to each other about our reasons for being in the streets. What failed was the absence of organizational vehicles capable of converting mass insurgency into sustained pressure on specific institutions — police budgets, prosecutor offices, prison contracts, state legislatures. The protesters who showed up in June were largely unaffiliated with mass-membership organizations because such organizations no longer exist at the scale required. The energy dissipated not because people couldn't endure each other but because there was nowhere for the energy to go.

This is a structural failure, not a discursive one. And here is my deeper worry about the genre of writing Inouye's essay represents: it tends to flatter the intellectual class by suggesting that the problems of the movement are problems that intellectuals are uniquely qualified to solve. If the bottleneck is endurance, humility, openness to transformation, then those of us who write essays and teach courses are doing the most important work — we are the ones modeling the right disposition. This is, to put it kindly, convenient. It is also wrong. The bottleneck is institutional capacity, and intellectuals can contribute to that only marginally, mostly by amplifying the work of people whose names rarely appear in essays.

Let me put it more sharply. The recent revival of labor militancy — Starbucks Workers United, the Amazon Labor Union's victory at JFK8, the UAW's "stand-up strike" beginning this very week against the Big Three automakers, the Hollywood writers' strike now in its fifth month, the threatened UPS strike that won historic gains in August — has happened largely without any of the discourse about endurance and humility that Inouye's essay treats as central. It has happened because organizers identified pressure points, built majorities, picked fights, and won them. Where there has been internal conflict over race and gender and ideology — and there has been plenty — it has been processed within campaigns that already had concrete targets and timelines. The targets and timelines made the conflict productive; the conflict did not produce the targets and timelines.

I would say something similar about the most successful electoral insurgencies of the recent left — the Working Families Party at its best, certain DSA campaigns, the Sunrise Movement's pressure on the Inflation Reduction Act. These succeeded to the extent that they identified specific demands, built coalitions around them, and applied disciplined pressure. They failed, where they failed, mostly because the pressure was insufficient, not because the coalition lacked humility.

What would I want from a theory of solidarity, then? Honestly, less theory and more case studies. I want close studies of the conditions under which interracial working-class coalitions have actually formed and held — the 1199 hospital workers' campaigns, the SEIU justice for janitors campaign in Los Angeles, the New York Taxi Workers Alliance, the Coalition of Immokalee Workers. I want close studies of the conditions under which they have fallen apart, and what could have held them. I want less Jodi Dean and Nathan DuFord and more Bill Fletcher, Jane McAlevey, Stephen Lerner, Ai-jen Poo. I want fewer essays and more campaigns.

This is not a counsel of anti-intellectualism. Theory matters. But I think the left has, for some time now, been overproducing a particular kind of theory — affective, discursive, attentive to interiority — and underproducing the kind of strategic analysis that actually helps organizers make decisions. The unspoken assumption of much current left writing is that the obstacles to solidarity are obstacles in our heads, and that if we could only think and feel and relate differently, the movements would follow. The historical evidence does not bear this out. Movements follow material conditions, organizational capacities, and strategic choices. Heads and hearts adjust to those, more often than they shape them.

So my response to Inouye is to demur, gently. The Bob Lee footage is wonderful. The Henry conversation is honest and instructive. The Uncle Gerald story is moving. But the lesson I take from these vignettes is not that we need to cultivate endurance. It is that we need to build the kinds of organizations Bob Lee built, the kind of organization that could give Henry's life and Uncle Gerald's life and the author's life a common political horizon. That building is hard, slow, mostly invisible work, and it does not lend itself easily to essays. Which may be precisely why we keep writing essays about something else.

Solidarity as Improvisation

Mie Inouye's essay does something that the genre of solidarity writing rarely does: it admits that the author does not entirely know why she organizes. This admission is the essay's most generative move, and I want to develop it in a different direction than she does. Where Inouye lands on "social endurance" as the under-theorized basis of solidarity, I want to suggest a complementary concept that I think captures something her examples actually demonstrate: improvisation.

Consider what Bob Lee is doing in the Uptown footage. He has come into a room that he does not control, full of people whose dispositions he cannot predict. He does not have a script. He has a goal — disrupting the Model Cities meeting — but the path from this room to that goal runs through micro-decisions about which body to approach, when to touch, when to question, when to insist. The hand on Roger's hair is not a planned intervention; it is a response to a particular sixteen-year-old whose bravado needed to be tempered by tenderness. The lifting of the young mother to her feet is not in a curriculum; it is a read of the room in a particular moment, by an organizer attuned to who needs to speak and what their speaking might do.

This is improvisation in the sense that jazz musicians and pickup basketball players and skilled clinicians mean it: not the absence of structure but the capacity to act effectively within structure when the structure itself does not specify what to do. Improvisation requires deep knowledge of the form, intimate familiarity with one's collaborators, and a tolerance for risk. It cannot be reduced to a method, but it can be cultivated through practice.

I want to suggest that solidarity, at the level of the meeting and the campaign, is fundamentally improvisational. The theories that try to ground solidarity in shared interest, shared morality, shared ideology, or shared identity all run aground on the same rock: the people in the room never quite share any of these things in the way the theory requires. What they share, at most, is an occasion — a particular fight, a particular building, a particular threat — and a willingness to act together within that occasion. The character of their solidarity is worked out in real time, through bodies, words, gestures, and small decisions that no advance theory could fully specify.

This reframing has consequences. First, it shifts the question from "what is the basis of solidarity?" to "what conditions allow improvisational solidarity to flourish?" These are different questions. The first invites us to look for foundations; the second invites us to look for the supportive infrastructure of practice. The improvising jazz musician relies on a deeply internalized vocabulary, on hours of practice with the same players, on a venue, an audience, an instrument, a tradition. The improvising organizer relies on the same kinds of supports: trained skills, repeated experience, a base, an opponent, a context.

Second, it makes sense of why Inouye's "endurance" matters without making endurance the master virtue. Endurance is one of the supportive conditions for improvisation, because improvisation requires sustained co-presence — you cannot read a partner's gestures if you have not spent enough time with them to know how they move. But endurance is not what makes the music. The music is made in the moment, by people who have done the work to be ready when the moment arrives.

Third, it suggests that the most important pedagogical work of left intellectuals is not to specify the basis of solidarity in advance but to study and disseminate the practices that have produced effective solidarity in particular cases. This is closer to a jazz school than a philosophy department. We learn by watching Bob Lee, listening to old organizers talk about how they handled hard meetings, reading the campaign accounts of UNITE HERE local 11 or the New York taxi drivers or Bayard Rustin's March on Washington logistics. We learn by trying things, failing, and trying again with people who can give us notes.

Inouye's essay actually models this kind of pedagogy. The Bob Lee footage works in her classroom not because it illustrates a theory but because it shows a master at work, and students can see things in it that no theory would have prepared them to see — Lee's hands, his timing, his willingness to move into discomfort. This is what improvisational learning looks like. It is also, I think, why her students struggle to imagine themselves in Lee's place. The capacity to organize like that does not come from reading essays about organizing. It comes from doing it, badly at first, with mentors who can show you what you missed.

There is a deeper philosophical point here, drawing on the pragmatist tradition. John Dewey, in The Public and Its Problems, argued that publics are not pre-given but emerge in response to particular harms — they are constituted through the recognition of shared consequences. Hannah Arendt, in The Human Condition, made a related claim about action: that it is irreducibly initiatory, that it cannot be predicted from prior conditions, and that political life consists in the bringing-into-existence of relationships and possibilities that did not exist before. Neither thinker would have had much patience for the contemporary search for foundations of solidarity. Both would have insisted that solidarity is what happens when people respond to a situation together, in ways they could not have specified in advance.

This pragmatist/Arendtian frame helps clarify what is right and what is overstated in Inouye's account. What is right is the insistence that we cannot know in advance what a meeting will do to us — that solidarity is an open-ended process, that transformation is its hallmark. What is overstated is the suggestion that endurance per se is the key virtue. Endurance matters as a condition for improvisation, but the cardinal virtue of the improviser is something else: responsiveness. The capacity to read the room, to feel where the energy is, to know when to push and when to wait, to recognize the moment when a previously silent person needs to speak.

Responsiveness is harder to teach than endurance. It is also more dangerous to romanticize, because it can shade into the cult of the charismatic organizer who reads everyone perfectly and brooks no dissent. The best improvisers are not soloists; they are deeply attentive ensemble players, and the best organizing traditions have ensemble structures — collective leadership, accountability practices, debriefs, mentoring chains — that distribute responsiveness across many people rather than concentrating it in one.

So my suggested amendment to Inouye is: keep the endurance, but add the improvisation. And take seriously what jazz teaches us about how improvisers are made. They are made through practice, through ensemble, through tradition, through proximity to masters, through the freedom to fail. They are not made through manifestos. The left's failure of solidarity in the post-2020 moment is not, in this view, a failure of theory or even of endurance. It is a failure of practice infrastructure — of the schools, the apprenticeships, the campaigns, and the elders that would have made our improvisers ready when the music started.

This is what I think Bob Lee's hands tell us. They tell us that there is a craft here, that it can be learned, and that it cannot be learned from books. The most useful thing essays like Inouye's — and like this one — can do is to point at the craft, point at the masters, and say: go find them, go practice, and come back when you have something to play.

"While social endurance is essential for solidarity, it is not a natural resource but an organizational achievement that must be cultivated through intentional structures, discipline, and emotional infrastructure."

Mie Inouye is right that solidarity requires the capacity to stay in rooms where we are annoyed, challenged, and disappointed. But as an organizer, I want to add a caution: endurance is not a natural resource we can simply call upon. It is produced—or depleted—by the design of organizations. Bad meetings do not merely reveal insufficient commitment. They manufacture burnout.

This matters because the exhortation to “keep showing up” can land very differently depending on who hears it. For some, it is a necessary corrective to consumer politics: stop treating organizations like brands, stop leaving whenever you feel uncomfortable, stop confusing disagreement with harm. For others, especially those who have been asked all their lives to absorb disrespect, overwork, racism, sexism, transphobia, ableism, or class contempt, “keep showing up” can sound like another demand to endure the intolerable.

Inouye knows this. Her distinction between democratic solidarity and the coercive unity of Mormon family life is crucial. Still, the practical question remains: how do we cultivate social endurance without romanticizing suffering?

The answer begins with recognizing that conflict is not automatically educational. Some conflict clarifies stakes, surfaces hidden assumptions, and deepens trust. Other conflict teaches people that the organization is unsafe, unserious, or not worth their time. The same disagreement can be productive or destructive depending on context. A heated debate in a union meeting before a strike vote may strengthen the campaign if members trust the process. A similar debate in a loosely organized activist group with no agenda, no facilitator, and no decision rule may drive people away.

Endurance depends on containers. A container is the set of structures that allows people to take risks without falling into chaos: agendas, time limits, facilitation, decision rules, membership expectations, grievance processes, political education, social time, and clarity about the organization’s purpose. These sound bureaucratic. They are actually emotional infrastructure. People can tolerate more disagreement when they know how decisions will be made, how long the meeting will last, what behavior is unacceptable, and what happens next.

Many left organizations underinvest in this infrastructure because they associate structure with hierarchy. The result is not freedom but informal domination. The loudest, most confident, most educated, most available, or most socially connected people set the tone. New members cannot tell whether they are joining a campaign, a friend group, a therapy circle, or a battlefield. Conflict then becomes personal because the organization has not given it a political form.

Bob Lee’s organizing in American Revolution 2 is powerful not only because he asks, “What do you want?” but because he brings discipline into the room. He does not invite endless self-expression. He draws testimony toward action. When the young man wants to fight the police, Lee tells him there must be discipline first. That word matters. Without discipline, pain becomes spectacle or adventurism. With discipline, pain can become power.

Discipline should not mean militarized obedience. It should mean shared commitments that make trust possible. If we agree not to surprise one another with public denunciations before private conversation, that is discipline. If we agree that people directly affected by an issue should have special weight in shaping demands, that is discipline. If we agree to start and end on time because some members have child care constraints or hourly jobs, that is discipline. If we agree not to make decisions in the bar after the meeting, that is discipline. These practices are not secondary to solidarity. They are solidarity in administrative form.

The critique of deference politics also needs organizational grounding. Inouye is persuasive that deference can let privileged people avoid conflict and responsibility. But what replaces it? If the answer is simply “everyone speak honestly from their own stake,” the result may favor those trained to narrate themselves fluently. Professional-class participants are often very good at turning self-reflection into authority. Working-class members, immigrants, people with less formal education, or those burned by institutions may not immediately answer “What do you want?” in polished political language. Without careful structure, the move beyond deference can become a return to familiar inequalities.

A better approach is leadership development. People do not enter organizations equally ready to speak, facilitate, analyze, negotiate, or take risks. Good organizing identifies potential leaders and helps them grow. It asks quiet members to take on bounded tasks. It pairs new people with experienced members. It makes space for testimony but does not leave people trapped in testimony. It teaches members how budgets work, how landlords operate, how labor law constrains action, how city councils hide power, how police departments manipulate fear. It turns lived experience into strategic capacity.

This is where Inouye’s “epistemic humility” becomes practical. Humility is not just a personal virtue; it can be built into process. Before deciding on a demand, map who is affected and who is missing. Before assuming a conflict is about personality, ask what material pressures shape it. Before adopting language from national discourse, ask whether members use it and what it clarifies. Before declaring consensus, check whether disagreement has been silenced by fatigue, jargon, or fear of being labeled. These are humble practices.

At the same time, organizations must protect members from endless process. One reason people leave movements is that meetings consume time without producing action. A person working two jobs cannot attend a four-hour discussion about whether the group should issue a statement on an issue unrelated to its campaign. Parents cannot repeatedly arrange child care for meetings that end with no decisions. Low-income members cannot subsidize the emotional education of affluent comrades forever. Social endurance is easier when the organization respects people’s lives.

Respect also means boundaries around harm. In some left spaces, any conflict is treated as potentially transformative, and members—often women, queer people, people of color, or younger organizers—are asked to keep engaging with those who demean or manipulate them. That is not solidarity. The capacity to stay must be paired with the right to say: this behavior violates our norms; this person needs to step back; this conflict requires mediation; this organization cannot hold this situation. Exit is sometimes wisdom.

But the opposite mistake is equally common: treating discomfort as harm, disagreement as violence, or criticism as disposability. Organizations need vocabularies that distinguish awkwardness, offense, conflict, abuse, and danger. These categories overlap, but they are not identical. If every bad feeling triggers maximal response, members learn to hide disagreement until it explodes. If no bad feeling matters, members learn that power protects itself. The work is to build intermediate responses: clarification, apology, repair, coaching, caucusing, temporary separation, formal accountability, and, when necessary, removal.

Inouye’s Mormon example shows why this is difficult. High-demand communities often produce endurance through shame and obligation. Many activists fleeing such backgrounds rightly resist anything that smells like coercion. Yet low-demand politics rarely survives. The challenge is to make commitments explicit and revisable. Instead of relying on guilt—“good comrades show up”—organizations can say: members of this committee attend twice a month, complete assigned tasks, contribute dues if able, and participate in political education. If you cannot do that now, there are other roles. Clear expectations are kinder than moral fog.

The summer of 2020 revealed both the hunger for solidarity and the absence of containers. People poured into the streets with grief, rage, loneliness, and hope. Some found organizations. Many found only protest calendars, Instagram infographics, donation links, and sudden workplace diversity conversations. She focuses on Lee’s physicality, his insistence that people say what they want, his refusal to let them remain passive. He was connecting a young white woman’s brother being stabbed by a cop to the Panthers’ larger analysis of the state’s repressive role. His question “What do you want?” was not an endless listening session; it was a step toward building demands that could be fought for together. Social endurance, without the scaffolding of a shared political-economic analysis and a concrete horizon of demands, will exhaust itself.

Inouye’s own family story is deeply moving and illustrates the contradictory pulls of religion, race, and class. Organizing such a person would require not just meeting him where he is with patience, but also offering him a concrete alternative that addresses his material precarity as a rural doctor and a member of a largely working-class community. I am not arguing that material interest is everything or that moral transformation is irrelevant. Social endurance must be tied to a practice of winning something. Otherwise, it becomes a spiritual discipline for the PMC, a way to feel virtuous while the fundamental structures remain untouched. Inouye has given the left a beautiful meditation on what it means to keep showing up. Now we need a strategy for what to do once we’re in the room—and why it’s worth the heartbreak.

That is a tragedy of inadequate political education, not just individual stubbornness. It would need to give people a sense of historical agency, a way of understanding themselves as part of a long lineage of resistance. What the Organizers Already Know

I read Mie Inouye's essay as a professional organizer of twenty years, and I want to say first that there is a great deal in it I recognize. The scene with Bob Lee in Uptown is canonical in the trainings I have led, and the description of how Lee uses physical proximity, touch, and the demand that the young mother speak — these are skills I have tried to teach and have watched dozens of organizers learn. The honest exchange with Henry at the fundraiser is the kind of moment any of us who have worked the doors will recognize. The essay's instincts about organizing as a relational, embodied, transformative practice are right.

But I want to enter a friendly objection. Much of what Inouye presents as a theoretical intervention — "social endurance," "epistemic humility," the cultivation of the capacity to stay in the room — is already the explicit pedagogical content of most serious organizing traditions in the United States. We have known this for a long time. The reason this knowledge has not produced the multiracial working-class movements we want is not that we lack a theory. It is that the institutional vehicles for transmitting and practicing this knowledge have been hollowed out, and the political economy has shifted in ways that no amount of better disposition can overcome.

Take "epistemic humility." Every IAF training I have ever attended begins with the relational meeting: an hour-long one-on-one in which the organizer's job is to ask questions and listen, to suspend their own analysis, to surface the leader's self-interest in their own words. Marshall Ganz's public narrative methodology, taught to thousands of organizers through the New Organizing Institute and now through Leading Change Network, formalizes this same humility into the "story of self, us, and now." McAlevey's structure-based organizing, derived from her time with 1199, insists on charting workplaces, identifying organic leaders, and recognizing that the organizer is not the most important person in the room. The Midwest Academy curriculum, going back to Heather Booth in the 1970s, teaches that the campaign begins with what the constituency wants, not with what the organizer thinks they should want. The PICO/Faith in Action network has built its entire ecclesiology around the discipline of listening across difference.

I do not list these to score points. I list them because Inouye's essay reads, in places, as though the practical alternative to deference politics has not yet been invented. It has been, repeatedly, and it works. What we are missing is not the curriculum. We are missing the conditions under which the curriculum gets implemented at scale.

Consider what has changed materially. In 1970 union density was about 27 percent. Today it is around 10 percent and under 7 percent in the private sector. That collapse alone has destroyed the most important site at which working-class people of different races, religions, and politics were once forced to engage each other in sustained, conflictual cooperation — the union local. The decline of mainline religious participation, of fraternal organizations, of bowling leagues (Putnam was not wrong, just incomplete) has stripped out the secondary institutions. The professionalization of nonprofits, with their grant cycles and their executive directors and their staff-to-member ratios, has substituted advocacy for organizing in too many places. The geographic sorting of Americans by class and political tribe has thinned the natural occasions for cross-difference encounter. And the algorithmic mediation of political life through screens has created the illusion of engagement without the friction that produces actual relationships.

Against this backdrop, "social endurance" is not a virtue we lack but a capacity that no longer has anywhere to be exercised. The Mormon ward Inouye describes is one of the very few American institutions that still works the way thick institutions used to work. Of course she found in it a model. So would I. So does every honest organizer who has watched what the LDS Church, or for that matter the Catholic parish in a working-class neighborhood, or a strong local of UNITE HERE, can still do.

Here is what I would add to Inouye's argument, then, from the organizer's seat. First: stop treating discourse interventions as a substitute for institution-building. Critiques of deference politics, however accurate, will not produce the multiracial working-class movement we need. Only sustained, well-resourced, long-haul investment in dues-based membership organizations will. The labor movement is having a real moment — Starbucks, Amazon, UAW, UPS, the actors and writers — and the most important thing the left can do right now is pour bodies and resources into supporting those campaigns, not into the discourse.

Second: take seriously the asymmetry between organizing the working class and organizing the PMC. Inouye, following Clemons, considers the possibility that the PMC is "the most accessible base" right now and wonders how to work with its contradictions. With respect, this is a defeatist framing. The PMC is accessible because it shows up to protests; it is not accessible because it does the long, boring, accountable work of building base power. PMC members will donate, march, sign petitions, and post — they will rarely commit to two years of weekly meetings to win a single contract. The traditional working class is harder to reach but easier to hold once reached, because the stakes are clearer and the cultural script of mutual obligation is, in many communities, still alive. Investing in PMC mobilization at the expense of working-class organizing is a strategic error we have made for forty years.

Third: do not romanticize conflict, but do not flinch from it. The best organizers I know practice what we sometimes call "tension without rupture" — the discipline of raising hard truths inside relationships strong enough to hold them. This is not the same as conflict-avoidance, and it is not the same as conflict-seeking. It is a craft, and it is teachable, but it requires sustained relationships, which requires institutions, which brings us back to point one.

Finally: respect the political economy of organizers themselves. The reason organizing pedagogy has gotten thinner over the past decade is partly that the job has gotten worse. Organizers are underpaid, overworked, often working on yearly grant cycles that punish long-term investment, frequently caught between national strategy memos and local realities. The intellectual class writing about solidarity rarely talks to the people doing the work week in and week out, and when it does, it tends to extract anecdotes rather than transmit lessons. Inouye, to her credit, has done some of the listening. I wish more of her colleagues would.

None of this is meant as a brush-off. Inouye's essay is one of the more useful pieces of left writing I have read this year, precisely because it takes the embodied, relational, awkward character of organizing seriously. But the lesson I draw from it is different from the one she seems to draw. We do not need a new theory of solidarity. We need to put the theory we already have to work, in institutions worth being part of, on campaigns worth winning. Endurance will follow. It always has.

But institutions shape our capacities, and the current institutional landscape of the left—largely informal networks, social media–mediated groups, short-term campaigns—is not designed to foster endurance. The Mormon Church succeeded in instilling social endurance because it was a total institution that structured its members’ lives from childhood. It had buildings, a professional clergy (of sorts), a dense calendar of rituals, and a theology that made endurance a path to salvation. The secular left has nothing comparable. Our meetings are held in borrowed spaces, our organizations often run on shoestring budgets with volunteer labor, and our members are pulled in a hundred directions by the demands of precarious work and family life. Asking individuals to just “endure” in such a context is like telling someone to swim against a current without a boat. We need to build the boat: more robust, membership-based organizations that provide not just a place to meet but material support, mutual aid, and a sense of collective identity that can hold people through the hard times.

The Black Panther Party, the Young Lords, and the Young Patriots were not just gatherings; they were organizations with offices, programs, and a public presence. The ideological work of forging a shared vision was embedded in these day-to-day material practices. Today’s left has some echoes of this: community fridges, bail funds, union drives. I find myself, after reading Mie Inouye's essay several times, in a position of partial agreement and partial unease. The agreement is easy to state: Inouye is right that organizing is a transformative practice, right that deference politics has produced fragile coalitions, right that something like epistemic humility is preferable to either confident moralism or evasive deference, right that the Mormon ward of her childhood and the Rainbow Coalition of 1969 both have things to teach us about what it takes to hold differently situated people together over time. The unease is harder to articulate, but it has something to do with what the essay does not quite ask: what is endurance for?

Inouye's account suggests that endurance is its own reward, or close to it. Through endurance we become "less racist, less classist, less transphobic, less judgmental, more flexible, more knowledgeable, and, ultimately, more useful to the project of building the world we want to share." This list is appealing, but notice the gradient: from the personal-ethical (less racist, less judgmental) to the politically functional (more useful to the project). The essay's center of gravity is on the personal-ethical end. The political project remains underspecified, a horizon toward which the cultivated dispositions are presumably moving, but whose actual content the essay does not fill in.

I want to suggest that this asymmetry is not accidental. It reflects a particular intellectual moment in which the left's confidence about what we want has, paradoxically, declined even as our discursive sophistication about how we ought to relate to each other has grown. We have a great deal to say about the ethics of coalition, the dangers of deference, the value of conflict, the importance of staying in the room. We have less to say, with collective conviction, about what we are trying to build. The result is a body of writing — and I include Inouye's essay in this, although it is one of the more thoughtful examples — in which the dispositional questions have crowded out the substantive ones.

This is not a criticism of any individual author. It is an observation about a genre. And I think the genre's flourishing reflects, in part, a defensive crouch. After the high hopes of 2020, after the demobilization that followed, after the rise of authoritarian movements on the right that have outmaneuvered the left in arena after arena, after the disappointments of the Biden administration on the issues the left cares about most, there is something protective in turning to the interior questions of how we should relate to each other. These questions are tractable in a way the larger strategic questions are not. We can recommend humility; we cannot easily recommend a path to power.

But I think this defensive crouch is the wrong response to the situation, and I want to use my space here to suggest what a more confident posture might look like.

First, the left should be willing to specify what it wants. Not in the sense of a utopian blueprint — that has its own pathologies — but in the sense of a near-term political horizon detailed enough that people can recognize themselves in it. What is the political economy we are fighting for? A Nordic-style social democracy? A solidarity economy? A democratized version of the U.S. welfare state? Some configuration of public ownership, sectoral bargaining, robust commons? These are real choices with real implications, and the left's reluctance to choose among them has costs. Inouye's essay invokes "a shared vision of a just society" as the binder of coalition, but the essay itself does not try to specify the vision. I am not asking Inouye to do this single-handedly; I am observing that the genre of writing she is contributing to rarely tries.

Second, the left should be willing to identify its actual political opponents with the same specificity it brings to its identification of allies. Inouye's essay names "neoliberal capitalism" several times, citing Clemons and DuFord. This is fine as far as it goes, but "neoliberal capitalism" is an abstraction that does not name the actually existing institutions, individuals, and political coalitions that constitute the opposition. The fossil fuel industry, the private equity sector, the carceral apparatus, the union-busting law firms, the donor networks of the Republican Party, the financialized real estate complex, the technology monopolies — these are the actual opponents, and they need to be named, mapped, and confronted as such. A movement that only knows itself through its internal dispositions will not develop the strategic clarity to fight specific opponents on specific terrain.

Third, the left should rebuild its institutional capacity at every level — labor, electoral, community, communications, legal. This is the unglamorous work that most of the people I admire most are doing, often without much attention from the discursive left. The unionization wave currently unfolding, the strongest in fifty years, is the most important thing happening in American politics, and its outcome will shape what is politically possible for a generation. The state-level electoral work that has slowly turned places like Georgia and Arizona competitive has been done by organizers most readers of this forum have never heard of. The tenant union infrastructure being built in cities across the country, the bail funds that have professionalized into durable institutions, the climate movement's pivot from protest to policy implementation — these are the seedbeds of whatever the left will become.

Fourth, the left should make peace with its own ideological pluralism without retreating from the demand that pluralism take constructive form. Inouye and DuFord are right that solidarity organizations cannot demand unity, that conflict is constitutive, that disagreement is not a sign of failure. But this is compatible with — indeed, requires — the development of disciplined practices for processing disagreement constructively rather than letting it fester or splinter. The Chinese left's old practice of "criticism/self-criticism," for all its abuse in the Maoist era, contains an important kernel: that organizations need procedures for the surfacing and resolution of internal conflict that are neither suppressive nor explosive. The contemporary left has, by and large, not developed these procedures, and our organizations suffer for it.

Finally, the left should reclaim a confident relationship to hope. I do not mean naive optimism. I mean the kind of hope that has historically animated serious freedom movements — the conviction that the world can be different, that we can change it, that the work is worth doing even when the odds are long. The dispositional turn in left writing has tended toward a kind of meditative ambivalence — neither despair nor confidence, but a careful holding-open of possibilities. This register has its place, but it does not, by itself, mobilize. People do not endure in struggle because they have been trained in epistemic humility. They endure because they believe the struggle can be won.

This brings me back to Inouye's essay and what I admire most in it: the story of Uncle Gerald. The essay's most powerful moment is the recognition that "the same shit that killed George Floyd killed my Uncle Gerald, however more softly and however more self-inflicted, and because I, too, want my family to survive." This is not a meditation; it is a claim. It names a specific harm — racial capitalism, intersected with the pandemic — and a specific aspiration — survival, for one's own people and across difference. If the essay had built outward from this claim into a more developed political program, it would have been a different essay, and perhaps a more useful one. The materials are there.

What comes after endurance, I want to say, is not more endurance. It is the actual project that endurance has been preparing us for: building the institutions, naming the opponents, specifying the demands, contesting power, winning, and consolidating the wins. Inouye's essay is a contribution to the preparation. I hope the next one is a contribution to the project.

It must be both, which means that the work of building solidarity is not only the work of cultivating individual endurance but also the work of designing institutions that make endurance possible.

The PMC’s “shared vision” often shatters the moment material interests—such as taxation, housing density, or educational tracking—are threatened.

If we want to build lasting solidarity, we must stop asking people to endure discomfort for the sake of abstract transformation. We must organize around demands that materially unite the working class, forcing the PMC to either align with working-class interests or abandon the coalition. Solidarity is forged in the crucible of class struggle, not in the crucible of the committee meeting.

Mie Inouye's essay has generated thoughtful responses from scholars and activists across the political spectrum. What strikes me, reading the essay again, is how thoroughly it operates within a particular paradigm of American left thought—one that emphasizes organizing, consciousness, and culture over and against electoral politics, state power, and the hard work of winning concrete victories. I want to offer a friendly challenge to this paradigm while recognizing the genuine insights it contains.

The paradigm Inouye operates within has deep roots in American radical history. From the Socialist Party of Eugene Debs through the Communist Party of the 1930s and 1940s, through the New Left of the 1960s and 1970s, American radicals have often emphasized the importance of building countercultural institutions, cultivating revolutionary consciousness, and creating alternative social relations over and against the conquest of state power. This emphasis has produced important contributions: the civil rights movement, the feminist movement, the LGBTQ+ movement, the environmental movement. Inouye's essay is squarely within this tradition. Her emphasis on "social endurance," on "showing up" to meetings, on cultivating conflict tolerance and epistemic humility—these are all practices of countercultural formation rather than conquest of power. The essay says relatively little about elections, legislation, state policy, or the strategic questions that radicals must face if they hope to transform society rather than merely critique it.

This is a significant limitation. In the United States, the state remains the primary locus of coercive power and the primary vehicle for social transformation. Healthcare, housing, education, criminal justice, immigration, environmental regulation—these are all state functions. Movements that cannot influence the state, that cannot win elections, that cannot shape legislation and policy, will not transform society regardless of how robust their internal cultures are or how much conflict they can productively hold.

I am not arguing that organizing and culture are unimportant. They are essential. But they must be connected to political strategies capable of contesting power. The civil rights movement succeeded not only because it built powerful institutions and cultivated committed activists; it also because it won Supreme Court cases, passed legislation, and elected officials. The labor movement succeeded not only because it organized shops and built solidarity; it also because it won political power sufficient to pass the Wagner Act and later the Great Society programs.

Inouye might respond that her essay is about solidarity, not strategy, and that solidarity is a prerequisite for the kind of mass politics that can win state power. This is fair. Solidarity is indeed a prerequisite for effective political action. But solidarity must be directed toward political ends. When solidarity organizations cannot connect to broader political strategies, they often become ends in themselves—communities of mutual affirmation that provide meaning and connection but cannot influence the world beyond their borders.

The challenge for the left is to combine the insights of essays like Inouye's—about the importance of solidarity, conflict tolerance, and organizational culture—with a robust engagement with state power. We need organizations that can build solidarity across difference and also win elections. We need cultures that can hold internal conflict and also develop coherent political strategies. We need movements that can sustain struggle through difficult periods and also translate that struggle into concrete political victories.

This is a harder challenge than either pure organizing or pure electoralism alone. It requires building the kind of mass organizations that can hold diverse constituencies, develop political analysis, and contest power at multiple levels. Such organizations are rare today. Building them is the central task for anyone who takes seriously the project of transforming American society. Essays like Inouye's can contribute to that project, but only if they are read as part of a broader political vision that takes seriously the question of power.

Vanilla Gemini

"While Mie Inouye correctly identifies 'social endurance' and the embrace of conflict as essential to solidarity, these virtues cannot be sustained by voluntary ideological commitment alone; they require structural institutions that tie the material survival of participants together."

Mie Inouye’s “Solidarity Now” offers a vital, penetrating diagnosis of the fragile state of contemporary left organizing. By zeroing in on “deference politics”—the increasingly common practice wherein relatively privileged individuals reflexively defer to those perceived as more marginalized—Inouye captures a dynamic that has paralyzed coalition-building. As she rightly observes, deference is often less about genuine solidarity than it is about conflict avoidance. It is a mechanism through which the professional-managerial class (PMC) can insulate itself from accusations of privilege while simultaneously dodging the messy, uncomfortable work of true political engagement. In place of this brittle dynamic, Inouye proposes “social endurance”: the capacity to stay in the room, endure friction, and allow oneself to be transformed by the collective struggle.

Inouye’s intervention is brilliant, particularly in her willingness to rescue the concept of conflict from the dustbin of toxic behaviors. She correctly identifies that neoliberalism atomizes us not merely by making us selfish, but by encouraging us to retreat from friction. Yet, while Inouye’s prescription of social endurance and epistemic humility is exactly the right moral and psychological framework for solidarity, her essay leaves a crucial structural question unanswered. She draws her model of social endurance from her upbringing in the Mormon Church, an institution defined by its totalizing worldview and immense material resources. In doing so, she inadvertently highlights the exact missing ingredient in voluntary, democratic left organizations: a structural mechanism that makes exit costlier than staying.

To understand the limits of social endurance as a purely voluntary ethos, we must look closely at the institution that taught Inouye this virtue. The Mormon Church is highly effective at forging social endurance among disparate people, but it does not achieve this through epistemic humility or a shared commitment to democratic transformation. It achieves it through a combination of profound theological stakes—the promise of eternal family and the threat of spiritual ruin—and a massive, highly organized network of material support. For Inouye’s family in the aftermath of internment, the church provided not just a spiritual home, but a lifeline of social and professional integration that allowed them to survive the brutality of racial capitalism.

When you are bound to an institution by the very survival of your family, or by the belief in your eternal soul, you will sit through three hours of agonizingly dull or politically infuriating Sunday meetings. You will endure the presence of people you do not like, and you will learn to cooperate with them. The barriers to exiting the Mormon Church are extraordinarily high, encompassing the potential loss of one’s family, community, and safety net.

By contrast, the barriers to exiting a neighborhood abolitionist working group or a local chapter of the Democratic Socialists of America are practically nonexistent. Modern left organizing spaces are overwhelmingly voluntary associations. When the PMC activist encounters “unrealistic conflict” or simply grows tired of the endless ideological purity tests, they do not have to stay in the room. They can simply close out of the Zoom meeting, turn off their phone, and retreat to the comforts of their class position. Without a structural binding agent, social endurance remains a personal virtue—a hobby for the politically committed—rather than a reliable basis for a mass movement.

This brings us back to Henry, the working-class organizer who confronts Inouye at the beginning of her essay. Henry organizes, he explains, because of “the material changes my family needs to see to our lives in order to survive.” Inouye feels chastened by this, admitting that she organizes largely for “the meetings” and the long-tail hope of generating revolutionary possibilities. She then attempts to bridge the gap between Henry’s materialist motivations and her own ideological ones by positing that a shared vision of a just society—and the endurance to fight for it together—might be enough to bind them.

But Henry is right, and his motivation holds the key to the very endurance Inouye seeks. The reason Henry will not leave the room when the meeting gets contentious is that he cannot afford to. His family’s survival depends on winning the struggle. The PMC activist, however, can afford to leave. If solidarity is to transcend the transactional fragility of deference politics, it cannot rely merely on the PMC cultivating a tolerance for discomfort. It must involve tying the material fate of the PMC to the material fate of the working class.

Fred Moten’s quote, which Inouye highlights, points precisely in this direction: “I don’t need your help. I just need you to recognize that this shit is killing you, too, however much more softly.” The genius of the original Rainbow Coalition was not just that Bob Lee physically touched people and forced them to articulate their desires, though that organizing craft is essential. The genius was that the Black Panthers, the Young Lords, and the Young Patriots recognized a shared material enemy in the Chicago police, the slumlords, and the city’s political machine. They did not defer to one another out of a sense of liberal guilt; they fought alongside one another because their literal survival in their respective neighborhoods depended on a unified front against the state.

If we want to build the kind of social endurance Inouye experienced in the Mormon Church within a democratic, secular left, we must build institutions that provide immediate, tangible material benefits and exact real costs for abandonment. Historically, the labor union has been the primary vehicle for this kind of solidarity under capitalism. A union hall is one of the few places in American life where a progressive, college-educated barista might be forced into a room with a socially conservative, working-class shift leader. They do not have to like each other. They do not have to defer to one another’s lived experiences. But if they want a living wage and health insurance, they have to endure one another. The binding agent is the collective bargaining agreement. If one walks away, they both lose their leverage. The friction between them is unavoidable, but it is rendered productive by the shared necessity of defeating the boss.

Beyond the workplace, building institutional stickiness means creating mutual aid networks, tenant unions, and cooperative housing structures that actually sustain people’s lives. If a political organization is paying into a strike fund that keeps your electricity on, or if a tenant union is the only thing standing between your family and eviction, you will endure a great deal of interpersonal conflict to keep that organization intact. You will learn to bite your tongue when a comrade says something clumsy, and you will learn to push back constructively when a strategy is failing. Epistemic humility ceases to be an academic exercise in standpoint theory and becomes a practical necessity for keeping the heat on.

Inouye is absolutely correct that the left must abandon the paralyzing theater of deference politics. Treating individuals as perfectly transparent avatars of their demographic categories is a recipe for stagnation. Her call to embrace conflict as a site of mutual transformation is one of the most vital lessons organizers can learn today. But we cannot expect neoliberal subjects, trained their entire lives to curate their experiences and avoid discomfort, to suddenly choose social endurance purely out of ideological commitment.

To heed Inouye’s call for "Solidarity Now," we must fuse her insight about social endurance with Henry’s urgent mandate for material survival. We must build political homes that are as vital to our survival as the Mormon Church was to her family’s, but fundamentally rooted in democratic equality rather than top-down hierarchy. Only when our organizations become the literal mechanisms of our shared survival will we find the strength to stay in the room when the hour grows late and the arguments grow fierce. Only then will we look across the table at someone we profoundly disagree with, recognize that our lives depend on them, and get down to the hard work of building a new world together.

  2. 02Deference politics and voluntary associations allow the professional-managerial class to retreat from political friction because their material survival is not tied to the success of the movement.
  3. 03True solidarity requires moving beyond personal virtues like 'epistemic humility' to create 'institutional stickiness' through labor unions, tenant unions, and mutual aid networks.
  4. 04Conflict only becomes productive and transformative when participants are forced to stay in the room because they share a common material enemy and a mutual need for collective resources.
  5. 05To build a mass movement, the left must fuse Inouye’s psychological framework of endurance with a materialist strategy that makes political organizations essential to the daily survival of their members.
