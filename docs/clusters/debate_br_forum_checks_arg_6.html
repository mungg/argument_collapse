"While the American system of checks and balances is currently dysfunctional, the solution lies in reforming political culture, norms, and statutory rules rather than pursuing a radical and potentially dangerous constitutional overhaul."

Lisa L. Miller’s searing indictment of American checks and balances is both timely and, in its broad strokes, convincingly argued. She is right that the byzantine veto points embedded in our constitutional architecture have often served to thwart popular majorities and entrench elite privilege. The litany of failures—on health care, labor protections, immigration reform—is familiar and damning. And she is certainly correct that merely brandishing the rhetoric of checks and balances against the Trump administration’s abuses offers little substantive resistance. But in her rush to constitutional revision, Miller underplays a more immediate and less dramatic remedy: reforming the political culture and party system that operate within that architecture. The crisis is not only institutional; it is profoundly cultural and procedural. Before we gamble on a top-to-bottom constitutional overhaul, we should devote our energies to rebuilding the political norms, electoral incentives, and deliberative capacities that made the system work tolerably well—or at least less dysfunctionally—in earlier eras.

Miller’s historical examples of successful reform—the New Deal and Great Society—are instructive. She presents them as triumphs of majoritarianism over veto points. But they were also products of unusually broad consensus forged through robust deliberation, cross-party cooperation, and civic mobilization that extended far beyond the Democratic Party base. Social Security passed with substantial Republican support. The Civil Rights Act of 1964 overcame a Senate filibuster thanks to an alliance of northern Democrats and Republicans. These legislative achievements are not simply stories of one party ramming through its agenda; they required building supermajority coalitions in a system that demanded them. The structural veto points Miller decries were thus not suspended; they were overcome through painstaking compromise and bipartisan leadership. The question is why that capacity for consensus has atrophied so dramatically.

The answer lies less in the number of checkpoints and more in the collapse of the institutional and cultural conditions that once incentivized cross-partisan dealmaking. The rise of primary-driven polarization, gerrymandered safe seats, the transformation of media, and the nationalization of politics have created a Congress in which the electoral rewards for obstruction far outweigh those for governing. The filibuster, once a rarely used tool of last resort, has become a routine supermajority requirement because minority parties face no political cost for wielding it. The courts have become more assertive in overturning legislation in part because judicial appointments have become total-war battlegrounds, with a generation of ideologues on the bench. These are problems of political behavior and norm erosion, not immutable constitutional features. After all, the filibuster is not in the Constitution; it can be eliminated by a simple Senate rules change. The size of the House is adjustable by statute. Many of the most crippling veto points are thus legislative, not constitutional in origin, and can be altered without the extraordinary effort of a constitutional convention or ratification.

Miller’s call for a constitutional amendment empowering citizens’ assemblies might be inspirational, but it is a nonstarter in the current political climate and risks channeling energy away from more achievable reforms. Ending the filibuster, expanding the House, restoring nonpartisan redistricting, and implementing public campaign financing could go a long way toward reducing the choke points while remaining within the realm of ordinary legislation. These changes would recalibrate the balance between majorities and minorities without jettisoning the entire framework. They would also avoid the perils of unleashing an unfettered majoritarianism that, in a deeply polarized age, could swing violently from election to election, destabilizing the rule of law and minority rights. Miller herself acknowledges that Trump’s executive activism is a form of bypassing Congress precisely because his agenda lacks broad support. But a weakened Congress, stripped of its deliberative functions, would make the presidency even more dominant, regardless of who occupies the White House. Far from constraining authoritarian impulses, an enfeebled legislature might amplify them.

Moreover, Miller’s vilification of “elites” can sometimes elide the necessary role that expertise and institutional memory play in sound governance. The problem is not elite influence per se, but the type of elite influence—whether it serves narrow corporate interests or broader public goods. The New Deal coalition included plenty of elite reformers, academics, and administrators who channeled mass discontent into durable policy. Rebuilding that kind of alliance between popular movements and knowledgeable publics is a discursive and organizational challenge, not merely an institutional one. It requires restoring trust in expertise, science, and the very idea of public-interest governance, all of which have been systematically undermined by the same forces that broke the political culture.

Thus, while I share Miller’s impatience with the pious invocations of checks and balances as an all-purpose savior, I worry that her focus on constitutional architecture risks backfiring. It may convince those already frustrated with the system that only radical, disruptive change can work—while leaving the existing mechanisms for incremental reform untouched. The history of American democracy is a story of periodic adjustments that, cumulatively, transformed the system without abolishing it. That path is still open. By rebuilding a politics of compromise, reviving the civic infrastructure that sustains democratic deliberation, and pursuing targeted institutional fixes, we can unstick the gears of government without abandoning the machine. The alternative—a leap into constitutional redesign—may not only fail to cure the disease but could, in the current climate, empower the very forces of authoritarian populism it seeks to defeat.

Diversified DeepSeek

"While Miller correctly identifies that the American system of checks and balances enables elite minorities to block popular policies, the solution lies in achievable, incremental statutory and procedural reforms rather than a politically impossible and potentially destabilizing overhaul of the Constitution."

Lisa L. Miller has performed a valuable service by laying bare the gap between the mythology of checks and balances and their actual functioning. Her central claim—that the many choke points in American government have been exploited by powerful minorities to block broadly popular policies—is empirically robust and strategically essential. Where she goes astray is in the sweep of her proposed remedy. A constitutional amendment bonanza, including citizens’ assemblies and the reduction of the Senate to a delaying body, is not only politically impossible in the current climate; it is also likely to generate a reaction that sets back the very causes she champions. Rather than reaching for the stars, those of us who share her diagnosis should focus on a concrete, incremental reform agenda that can actually be enacted and that builds momentum for deeper change over time. The path to reviving democratic responsiveness runs not through a constitutional convention but through the Senate chamber, the gerrymandered districts, and the campaign finance regime.

Start with the most glaring veto point that is entirely within our grasp: the Senate filibuster. It is a procedural rule, not a constitutional mandate, that requires a supermajority of 60 votes to advance most legislation. Its effect, as Miller notes, is to allow Senators representing a fraction of the American population to kill bills supported by majorities of both the public and the House. Abolishing or substantially reforming the filibuster would instantly make the legislative process more responsive to electoral outcomes. It would also force Senators to take public responsibility for their positions rather than hiding behind procedural obstruction. This single change, achievable by a simple majority vote at the start of a new Congress, would do more to break the veto point logjam than a dozen constitutional amendments that will never see the light of day. The fact that the filibuster is not enshrined in the Constitution should remind us that many of the worst dysfunctions of the American system are statutory or rule-based in nature, not constitutional in origin.

Miller’s proposal to amend the Constitution to reduce Senate power is, in effect, a backdoor attempt to achieve the same end. But constitutional amendment requires a two-thirds vote in both houses—or a convention called by two-thirds of the states—followed by ratification by three-quarters of the states. That means small states would have to voluntarily relinquish their disproportionate influence, a political impossibility. Why waste precious energy on such a quixotic quest when the same result can be partially achieved by ending the filibuster? The same logic applies to many of her other proposals. Expanding the House of Representatives can be done by ordinary statute, and it would mitigate, though not eliminate, the distortions of the Electoral College and the imbalance of power between large and small states. Stronger national voting rights legislation, including automatic registration and an end to felony disenfranchisement, would empower the very majorities Miller wants to unleash without touching the Constitution’s text. These are fights that can be won in the near term, and they build a broader coalition of voters with a stake in further reform.

The incrementalist approach also has the advantage of being more democratic in its own way. Major constitutional change, in the current environment, would almost certainly be driven by elite legal scholars, national advocacy groups, and billionaire-funded initiatives rather than by genuine grassroots deliberation. The constitutional convention process is especially vulnerable to capture by well-funded interests, as the experience of state-level ballot initiatives has shown. Incremental statutory reforms, on the other hand, can be debated in Congress, tested in states, and adjusted in response to public feedback. They respect the principle of institutional continuity that even a flawed system can command, thereby reducing the risk of a destabilizing backlash. Miller herself concedes that Trump taps into a sense that institutions are broken. A revolutionary constitutional overhaul could easily be demagogued as an attempt by the “deep state” or coastal elites to rig the rules permanently, and that narrative could empower the very authoritarian forces she opposes.

Moreover, incremental reform is not a surrender to gradualism devoid of vision; it is a strategy of political realism that multiplies opportunities for success. The history of American democratic expansion has always unfolded through a series of discrete, hard-fought victories, each of which shifted the political terrain for the next. Women’s suffrage, which Miller cites as a forty-two-year struggle, was itself a piecemeal process, achieved first in individual states before the Nineteenth Amendment. The civil rights movement secured the Civil Rights Act and Voting Rights Act through conventional legislative means, not through a constitutional revolution, though it drew on the moral authority of the Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments. The labor victories of the 1930s were embedded in the National Labor Relations Act, a statute that could later be weakened, yes, but also expanded. The lesson is that durable progress requires political majorities that are built, tested, and sustained over time, not a one-time constitutional redesign that hopes to lock in progressive outcomes forever.

Miller’s call for mass mobilization is essential, but it needs a tangible, short-term target. Mobilizing people around the abstractions of constitutional amendment—or even around a citizen assembly proposal that sounds like a gimmick to many—will be far harder than mobilizing them around the concrete demand to end the filibuster so that Congress can finally pass a $15 minimum wage, paid family leave, or a public option. Winning one or two of these long-blocked policies would demonstrate that the system can be made to work, restoring faith in democratic politics and building an appetite for more. That is the virtuous cycle that could eventually create the conditions for deeper constitutional change, if it remains necessary.

We are in a race against the authoritarian tendencies Miller rightly fears. We don’t have the luxury of a decade of constitutional pamphleteering and ratification drives. We have a Congress that, even under divided government, can be pressured to change its rules and pass transformative legislation if public demand is sufficiently intense and organized. That is where energy should be directed. The dead end of checks and balances can be opened with the keys already in our possession, if only we have the strategic sense to use them.

  4. 04Historical progress in American democracy, such as the labor and civil rights movements, has traditionally been achieved through discrete legislative victories rather than total constitutional redesigns.
  5. 05Mass mobilization is more effective when focused on concrete, short-term policy goals—like a minimum wage increase—that demonstrate the system's capacity to function for ordinary people.
