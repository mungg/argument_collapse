"The central premise that a vast new domestic intelligence apparatus is necessary should be questioned, as the actual domestic threat may not justify the permanent expansion of surveillance infrastructure."

I want to come at Richman's essay from a different angle than most of the responses it is likely to attract. Rather than dispute his policy prescriptions, I want to question one of the empirical premises on which they rest: that effective domestic counterterrorism in the United States requires the construction of a vast new domestic intelligence apparatus, federal or otherwise.

Richman writes as if this premise is self-evident — indeed, he says explicitly that "the need for a national domestic-intelligence network focused on threats of terrorist attack ought not be in dispute." But let us actually look at what we know, three years and three months after September 11, about the domestic threat picture.

The 9/11 hijackers were a foreign cell, infiltrated into the United States, supported by external networks, and detected — to the extent they were detected at all — through foreign-intelligence channels rather than domestic ones. The principal failures of 9/11, as documented in the Commission report, were failures of analysis at the CIA, of communication between the CIA and FBI, of the FBI's internal handling of field-office leads, and of immigration controls at ports of entry. None of these failures would have been addressed by a denser network of local-police intelligence reporting.

Since 9/11, the actual domestic terrorism cases brought by the Justice Department have been a remarkable mixed bag. The Lackawanna Six. The Portland Seven. The Northern Virginia Paintball cases. José Padilla. Iyman Faris. The Buffalo case. In several of these, the underlying conduct was attenuated — attendance at training camps abroad in some cases years before 9/11, "material support" theories applied to behavior far from any concrete plot. The few cases of actual operational planning inside the United States have been few enough to count on one hand. The Inspector General's report on post-9/11 detentions found that of the more than 700 individuals detained on immigration charges as part of the terrorism investigation, essentially none were ultimately tied to terrorism. We have built a vast investigative apparatus and the catch has been, by counterterrorism standards, modest.

There are two ways to read this. One is the way the administration reads it: the absence of a second attack is evidence that the apparatus is working, and we should build more of it. The other is that the threat picture was always more limited than the post-9/11 mobilization assumed, and that we are now building a permanent domestic surveillance infrastructure substantially in excess of any plausible threat. We do not know which reading is correct, and we may not know for some time. But the second reading deserves more weight than Richman gives it.

Why does this matter for Richman's essay? Because his entire framework presupposes that a national domestic intelligence network is necessary, and asks only how to structure it. If we relax that premise, different policy options come into view. The question is no longer "how do we make federal-local intelligence cooperation more accountable?" but "what is the minimum domestic intelligence footprint compatible with reasonable security?" That is a different question, and it points toward a more modest architecture.

Consider what a modest architecture might look like. It would focus federal counterterrorism intelligence on the foreign-domestic seam — international travel, finance, communications with overseas operatives — where the U.S. government has comparative advantage and the threat picture is best understood. It would rely on traditional criminal-investigative tools for domestic plotters, on the theory that anyone serious enough to plan an attack will leave the kind of evidentiary trail that criminal investigation can follow. It would treat local police primarily as recipients of warnings about specific threats rather than as nodes in a continuous intelligence network. It would explicitly disclaim the project of building general intelligence files on populations of interest, in favor of targeted, predicated investigations.

Such an architecture is not radical. It is essentially the architecture we had before 9/11, minus the specific failures the Commission documented. The Phoenix memo problem and the Moussaoui problem were not failures of insufficient domestic intelligence; they were failures of analytic culture and bureaucratic information flow inside an agency that already had vast investigative authorities. Fix those, and the case for a major expansion in domestic intelligence collection becomes thinner.

Richman might respond that I am underestimating the threat, and that the absence of a second attack does not mean a third is not coming. Fair enough. But the burden of proof for permanent expansions of state surveillance authority should sit with the proponents, not with the skeptics. The post-9/11 default — assume the worst, build everything, sort out the constitutional implications later — is not a sustainable mode of policymaking in a democracy. At some point, three years on, we should be asking what we have actually learned about the threat, and whether the institutional response is proportioned to it.

The reason this matters for Richman's specific prescriptions is that his federalism framework implicitly normalizes the expansion. If our policy question is "how do we structure the new domestic intelligence network?" we have already accepted that there is going to be one. The bigger question — should there be one, and at what scale? — disappears from view. Local-police involvement gets folded into the architecture without anyone ever quite deciding whether the architecture itself is wise.

I will allow that some expansion of intelligence capacity is reasonable after 9/11. I am not arguing for the status quo ante. But I am arguing that "necessity" claims in this area need to be tested, not asserted. Richman's essay would be stronger if it took seriously the possibility that the right answer involves a smaller domestic intelligence enterprise than the post-9/11 political class assumes — and that the right fight, in that case, is to keep the apparatus from growing, not to manage its growth with cleverer federalism.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The failures leading to the September 11 attacks were primarily analytical and communicative within federal agencies rather than a result of insufficient local police intelligence.
  2. 02The modest results of post-9/11 domestic terrorism prosecutions suggest that the actual threat level may be more limited than the current mobilization assumes.
  3. 03A more modest security architecture focusing on foreign-domestic seams and traditional criminal investigation is a viable alternative to a continuous national intelligence network.
  4. 04The burden of proof for expanding state surveillance should rest with proponents, yet the current federalism framework prematurely accepts the necessity of such an expansion.
  5. 05Integrating local police into a national intelligence network risks normalizing a permanent surveillance state without first determining if the scale of the response is proportional to the threat.
