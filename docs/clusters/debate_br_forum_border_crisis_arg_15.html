Diversified Gemini

"While Lea Ypi correctly identifies the hypocrisy of current immigration regimes, her prescription of dismantling the liberal democratic state in favor of a transnational class struggle is a reductive and impractical approach that ignores the agency of migrants and the necessity of institutional reform."

Lea Ypi’s diagnosis of the modern border crisis correctly identifies the hypocrisy of contemporary immigration regimes, where passports are for sale to the global elite while insurmountable bureaucratic walls are built for the global poor. Her critique of "golden visas" and the commodification of citizenship is sharp, necessary, and morally unassailable. However, in moving from critique to prescription, Ypi falls into the trap of revolutionary reductionism. By reducing the entirety of the migration debate to a symptom of global capitalist exploitation and demanding a total overhaul of the liberal democratic order, she discards the practical, institutional mechanisms that have actually improved the lives of millions of migrants.

Ypi’s central claim is that migration is essentially a class conflict, and that attempting to manage it through the existing framework of the liberal state is a fool's errand. She dismisses the social democratic attempt to balance welfare provisions with managed migration as mere capitulation to right-wing hegemony. In her view, the liberal democratic state is little more than an engine for entrenching property asymmetries and empowering oligarchs.

This is a profound misreading of both the history of liberal democracy and the actual desires of the migrants she seeks to defend.

First, let us examine the empirical reality of migration. Ypi claims that migration is "rarely the realization of free choice; it is more often the consequence of power relations in which liberal democracies play a decisive role." While it is undeniably true that conflict, economic desperation, and climate pressures force many to move, it is overwhelmingly reductive to strip all migrants of their agency, casting them merely as tragic victims of neoliberalism. Millions of people migrate legally and irregularly every year because they actively desire the opportunities, the legal protections, and the standard of living that liberal democracies provide. They are not seeking to overthrow the capitalist state; they are seeking to participate in it.

When a software engineer from Bangalore moves to Silicon Valley, or a construction worker from Jalisco moves to Texas, they are engaging in a deeply rational pursuit of upward mobility. The remittances they send home—which, as Ypi herself notes, dwarf global foreign aid—are a direct result of the wage differentials created by the global market. To frame this entire dynamic merely as "a war waged on the most vulnerable" ignores the massive, demonstrable improvements in living standards that migration has facilitated for countless families over the last half-century.

Moreover, Ypi’s dismissal of the liberal democratic state ignores the fact that it remains the most effective engine for integrating diverse populations in human history. The Enlightenment values Ypi praises—freedom, equality, and solidarity—are not self-executing. They require robust legal institutions, independent judiciaries, and constitutional protections to become reality. It is precisely within the framework of the liberal state that migrants have successfully fought for and secured civil rights, labor protections, and pathways to citizenship.

When Ypi urges progressives to abandon "membership" and the nation-state in favor of building a transnational working-class alliance, she is advising them to abandon the only political terrain where real power can be wielded. Trade unions and labor rights are enforced by state agencies; anti-discrimination laws are upheld by national courts; welfare benefits are distributed by government bureaucracies. A global, borderless proletariat cannot pass legislation.

The real task for progressives in late 2025 is not to dismantle the liberal state, but to make it work better. This means engaging in the hard, unglamorous work of institutional reform. We need to massively expand legal pathways for both high-skilled and low-skilled workers, recognizing that our economies desperately need their labor. We must decouple work visas from specific employers to prevent the exploitation that arises when a migrant's legal status is tied to a single boss. We should invest heavily in processing capacities to end the agonizing, years-long backlog for asylum seekers. And yes, as Ypi argues, we must end the grotesque practice of selling citizenship to billionaires.

But to achieve any of this, progressives must win elections. And they will not win elections if they adopt Ypi’s stance that national borders are inherently illegitimate and that the concerns of domestic citizens regarding cultural change and social cohesion are merely false consciousness generated by the capitalist class. The electorate in Western democracies is asking for a system of managed, orderly migration that respects the rule of law while remaining compassionate and economically dynamic. When progressives refuse to offer a coherent vision for border management—opting instead for rhetoric about tearing down the global capitalist order—they do not raise the class consciousness of the masses. They simply drive the median voter into the arms of the populist right.

The liberal democratic project is undeniably flawed, and globalization has produced severe inequities. But the solution to the border crisis is not to blow up the system in the name of a theoretical global solidarity. The solution is rigorous, practical reform that harnesses the economic dynamism of migration, protects the rights of workers through robust domestic laws, and maintains the democratic consent of the host population. Nathan the Wise did not ask us to destroy our institutions; he asked us to make them worthy of our highest ideals. That is the hard work of liberal governance, and we cannot afford to abandon it for the comforts of revolutionary theory.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Framing migration solely as a consequence of capitalist exploitation strips migrants of their agency and ignores the fact that many move to actively participate in and benefit from the opportunities liberal democracies provide.
  2. 02The liberal democratic state, despite its flaws, remains the most effective framework for protecting migrant rights and integrating diverse populations through established legal and constitutional mechanisms.
  3. 03Abandoning the concept of national membership for a borderless proletariat ignores the reality that labor rights and welfare benefits can only be enforced and distributed by state-level institutions.
  4. 04Progressives must focus on practical reforms—such as expanding legal pathways and ending the sale of citizenship—rather than revolutionary theory, in order to win the democratic consent necessary to improve migration systems.
  5. 05Dismissing the concerns of domestic citizens regarding social cohesion as mere 'false consciousness' politically alienates the median voter and cedes the migration debate to the populist right.
