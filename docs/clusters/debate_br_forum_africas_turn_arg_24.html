"The current economic growth in Africa is a temporary illusion driven by a global commodity cycle rather than a sustainable development miracle, and it risks reinforcing a destructive resource-dependent model."

Edward Miguel asks if it is Africa’s turn to be the world’s next development miracle. As we stand in the spring of 2008, watching oil hover near record highs and copper prices soaring, it is easy to be seduced by the GDP growth numbers. But a closer examination reveals a deeply troubling reality: the current "boom" is largely an illusion, a temporary sugar high driven by an unprecedented global commodity super-cycle. Rather than a structural economic transformation, we are witnessing the latest iteration of the resource curse, one that may leave Africa worse off when the cycle inevitably turns.

Miguel concedes that "industrial transformation is not driving Africa’s growth" and that the manufacturing sector remains stagnant. This is not a minor footnote; it is the fatal flaw in the Afro-optimist narrative. No region in modern history has achieved sustained, broad-based prosperity without a transition to manufacturing and industrialization. Asia’s rise, from Japan to South Korea to China, was built on factories, skill acquisition, and value-added exports. Africa, by contrast, is currently de-industrializing. The flood of cheap manufactured goods from China is destroying local industries—from textiles in Kano to furniture makers in Nairobi—while Africa exports unrefined crude oil, raw copper, and unprocessed timber in return. This is the classic colonial economic model, simply with a new metropole.

The influx of Chinese investment, which Miguel describes with ambivalence, is actively reinforcing this primary-resource dependence. Chinese state-backed infrastructure projects in Angola, Sudan, and the Democratic Republic of Congo are marvels of engineering, but they are designed almost exclusively to extract resources and transport them to the coast. These are not investments in Africa’s productive capacity; they are arteries for extraction. Furthermore, the "no-strings-attached" nature of this capital provides a massive lifeline to autocratic regimes. In places like Sudan and Angola, petrodollars are entirely bypassing the population, enriching tiny political elites and funding internal repression.

Even in more democratic countries, sudden resource wealth distorts the economy. It drives up local currencies (the "Dutch Disease"), making agricultural and manufacturing exports uncompetitive, which pushes more people out of formal employment and into the precarious informal sector.

Miguel proposes a clever technocratic fix: Rapid Conflict Prevention Support (RCPS), a sort of drought insurance for poor countries. But this ignores the political economy of resource-dependent states. The violence in the Niger Delta or the civil wars in Congo are not simply driven by "sudden economic shocks" like droughts; they are vicious battles for control over immense resource rents. Providing foreign aid as "insurance" will do nothing to solve the fundamental structural problem: when a state’s primary source of revenue comes from a hole in the ground rather than from taxing its citizens, the government has no incentive to be accountable to its people.

We should not mistake a global commodity bubble for an African development miracle. Until African nations build the institutions necessary to capture resource rents, invest them in human capital, and develop competitive manufacturing sectors, the current growth rates will remain a statistical mirage, enriching the elites and foreign investors while leaving the structural poverty of the continent untouched.
