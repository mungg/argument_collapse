"The failure of the COVID-19 response was not a lack of humility or prediction, but rather an institutional reliance on rigid, centralized blueprints that are fundamentally unsuited for managing complex adaptive systems."

In her essay “Public Policy after Pandemic,” Sheila Jasanoff draws a sharp contrast between the “heroic, can-do posture” of pandemic preparedness and the deliberative, historically informed “technologies of humility.” She argues that our reliance on prediction and control left us blind to the complex realities of COVID-19, and that we must instead embrace precaution and an ethical acknowledgment of our own ignorance. While Jasanoff powerfully dismantles the hubris of the pre-pandemic planning establishment, she traps us in a false binary. Both the mechanistic blueprints of preparedness and the slow, deliberative ethics of precaution fail for the exact same reason: they are fundamentally unsuited to managing complex adaptive systems.

The tragedy of the COVID-19 response was not simply that our predictions were wrong or that our experts lacked humility. The tragedy was that our institutional architecture was designed for a complicated world, rather than a complex one. In systems theory, a complicated problem—like sending a rocket to the moon—can be solved with enough data, blueprints, and top-down engineering. A complex problem—like a novel virus spreading through highly networked, behaviorally unpredictable human populations—cannot. Complex systems are characterized by non-linearity, feedback loops, and emergent phenomena. They defy perfect prediction, but they also defy the kind of front-loaded, precautionary deliberation that Jasanoff advocates.

Consider the failure of the Global Health Security Index (GHSI) and the U.S. pandemic playbook. As Jasanoff rightly notes, these documents treated the impending pandemic as a logistical puzzle to be solved by unified command and massive resource mobilization. But their deeper flaw was their rigidity. They assumed that a pandemic would unfold in a linear, predictable sequence. When the CDC insisted on developing its own proprietary diagnostic test in early 2020, and the FDA strictly forbade independent academic and clinical labs from deploying their own tests, they were following the centralized, command-and-control logic of the playbook. The result was a catastrophic blind spot that allowed the virus to seed itself across the country undetected for weeks.

This was not a failure of prediction; it was a failure of institutional agility. The system lacked the capacity to learn and adapt in real time. But would Jasanoff’s “technologies of humility” have fared any better in those crucial early weeks? Jasanoff describes these technologies as institutional mechanisms for incorporating memory, experience, and concerns for justice. While these are vital components of a healthy democracy, they are woefully mismatched to the speed of exponential viral growth. If your response to a fast-moving, shape-shifting threat is to convene citizen assemblies to map out the historical vulnerabilities of marginalized groups, the virus will have already mutated and burned through the population before the first committee report is drafted.

The alternative to mechanistic prediction is not deliberative precaution; it is resilience. A resilient system does not try to perfectly predict the future, nor does it freeze in the face of uncertainty. Instead, it accepts that shocks are inevitable and builds slack, redundancy, and decentralized adaptability into its very structure.

The global supply chain crisis that has defined much of 2021 perfectly illustrates this dynamic. Decades of hyper-optimized, just-in-time manufacturing eliminated all redundancy in the name of efficiency. When the pandemic hit, the lack of slack meant that a disruption in one part of the world immediately cascaded into a global shortage of personal protective equipment, ventilators, and eventually, basic consumer goods. A resilient approach would not have required a crystal ball to predict the exact nature of the disruption, nor would it have required a profound ethical humility. It simply would have required designing supply networks with buffered inventories and distributed manufacturing capabilities.

Similarly, a resilient public health strategy does not rely on a Harvey Fineberg-style unified command. Complex environments are best navigated by decentralized networks that can run parallel experiments, share data horizontally, and scale successful interventions rapidly. Taiwan’s early success in the pandemic was not driven by heavy-handed military mobilization, but by rapid, iterative integration of travel and healthcare data, crowdsourced mask-tracking apps developed by civic hackers, and immediate feedback loops between the state and the public. They did not try to crush the curve with a single, massive blow; they danced with the virus, constantly adjusting their footing based on real-time information.

As we face the new, highly mutated Omicron variant in late 2021, the limitations of both prediction and precaution are starkly evident. We cannot predict exactly how much this variant will evade our current vaccines, nor can we predict its precise trajectory. At the same time, retreating into a purely precautionary stance—shutting down borders entirely, locking down societies before the data is clear—imposes massive, often unsustainable costs on the very marginalized communities Jasanoff wishes to protect.

We need systems that can pivot. We need genomic surveillance networks that continuously sequence viral samples. We need rapid antigen testing that is cheap, ubiquitous, and decentralized, allowing individuals to make dynamic risk assessments in their daily lives. We need a regulatory apparatus that can approve updated vaccine formulations in a matter of weeks, not months.

Jasanoff is profoundly right that we must abandon the “Promethean dreams” of total control. But acknowledging the limits of our knowledge should not lead us to unheroic self-abnegation. It should lead us to design institutions that are as adaptive, networked, and responsive as the biological threats we face. We cannot predict the complex futures that await us, and we cannot debate them into submission. We can only build systems that are agile enough to survive them.
