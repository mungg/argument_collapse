Diversified DeepSeek

"The primary obstacle to gender equality is not a flaw in care feminism or mothers who 'care too much,' but rather the persistent endurance of male privilege and the failure of men to share the burdens of caregiving."

In her powerful and unsettling essay, Nancy Hirschmann has named a problem that feminists have been loath to talk about: the way in which the culture of care, even when wrapped in progressive language, can reinforce women’s subordination. She points her finger at mothers who lie, mothers who demand special treatment for their children, and mothers who trade on the prestige of mothering to evade the demands of the wider community. Yet one group escapes her scrutiny by a conspicuous margin: the men. It is men who have the incentive to preserve a world in which women do the caring, and it is men who refuse to change. The real force behind the perversion of care is not a theoretical flaw in care feminism but the stubborn, aggressive endurance of male privilege.

Let us look again at the anecdotes that frame Hirschmann’s argument. Carla Wagner’s parents—not her mother alone—asked the judge if their daughter could summer in Paris. Susan’s mother wrote a lie, but what of Susan’s father? Anthony’s mother berated the professor, but where was Anthony’s father in this defense of his son’s future? The pattern is clear, and it is a pattern Hirschmann does not name: the dirty work of shielding children from consequence, the emotional labor of advocating for the family, is overwhelmingly carried out by women, while men stand in the background, benefiting from the advocacy without doing the advocating, or they are absent altogether. The same families in which mothers are “caring too much” often feature fathers who are caring too little—and getting away with it. Hirschmann’s argument risks giving those men yet another pass: if women are the ones who are supposed to care less, then the burden of solving the problem once again falls on women, and men need do nothing.

Hirschmann knows that the division of care work is unequal, but her proposed remedy—women should gain economic clout to force men’s hand—is an admission of defeat dressed up as strategy. It assumes, first, that men will only change when they are coerced by women’s bargaining power, and second, that such power is a reliable tool. Yet decades of evidence show that even when women work full-time and earn equivalent salaries, they continue to shoulder the bulk of the housework and childcare. The power of a paycheck does not automatically translate into power within a marriage. Marriage is not a simple labor market; it is a thicket of love, habit, and psychological legacy, and men historically have proven themselves remarkably adept at remaining in the comfortable role of “helper” rather than equal partner. A woman who withholds care as a tactic to get her husband to do more may simply find herself exhausted by the conflict, or worse, discover that her husband is willing to tolerate a lower standard of domestic order than she is. The result is not equality but a grubby, resentful stalemate.

The real target of feminist energy should be men and the culture that shapes them. We need policies that force men to care, not ones that gently invite them. Countries such as Sweden and Iceland have shown that when parental leave includes a substantial, non-transferable portion for fathers, men take it. They take it not because they have suddenly seen the ethical light but because the alternative is to lose the time with their child—and the income. Over time, these policies change norms; they make paternity leave a normal part of a man’s biography, not a mark of insufficient career ambition. The problem in the United States has never been that men refuse to take the leave that is offered; it is that we have never built a system that dares to require them to.

Hirschmann worries that state policies let men off the hook, but the right kind of state intervention would put men on the hook. Paid parental leave reserved for fathers, combined with a radical expansion of affordable childcare and protections for part-time workers, would begin to rewire the expectations that both men and women bring to the workplace and the home. It would also, crucially, challenge the employer culture that penalizes men who care. As long as the “ideal worker” is a man with no visible care responsibilities, women will be marked as deviant the moment they become mothers, and men who want to be involved fathers will be marginalized as well. A feminism that targets only women’s choices leaves that ideal worker intact.

Hirschmann’s sorrow over her students’ dreams of staying home is understandable, but her students are also responding to a world in which men, as a class, have not changed enough. They see their mothers and older sisters exhausted from the second shift, and they reasonably conclude that if one parent must stay home, they might as well be the one who is culturally prepared and less likely to face career penalties for doing so. The answer to that dilemma is not to shame them for caring too much; it is to make it impossible for men to continue caring too little. Let us turn our righteous anger where it belongs: not at mothers who are doing the best they can in a broken system, but at the fathers, the bosses, and the political establishment that have, for generations, banked on women’s care as a free resource. Women have cared enough; it is time for men to start.
