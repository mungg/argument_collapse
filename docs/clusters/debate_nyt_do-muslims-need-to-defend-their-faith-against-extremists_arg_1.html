The question itself contains a trap. Asking whether Muslims must defend their faith presumes a collective ownership of the violence committed by the Islamic State, Boko Haram, or the Khorasan group — a presumption no one applies to Christians when a Lord's Resistance Army operates in Uganda, or to Buddhists when monks incite pogroms against the Rohingya in Myanmar. The 1.6 billion Muslims who had nothing to do with the beheading of Alan Henning do not owe the rest of us an apology, and the demand that they perform one, ritually, after every atrocity is itself a small act of bigotry. And yet the more interesting question — not whether Muslims must defend their faith to non-Muslims, but whether they should contest its meaning among themselves — has an obvious answer: of course, and they already are. In September, more than 120 Muslim scholars, including muftis from Egypt, Nigeria, and Bosnia, published an open letter to Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi systematically dismantling the Islamic State's theology point by point — its takfir of other Muslims, its enslavement of Yazidis, its claim to a caliphate, its destruction of shrines. The #NotInMyName campaign by young British Muslims and the unequivocal condemnations from Al-Azhar, the Organization of Islamic Cooperation, and figures from Hamza Yusuf to Yusuf al-Qaradawi (who disagree on almost everything else) show that the intra-Muslim argument is happening at full volume. Westerners simply aren't listening.

Where Bill Maher and Sam Harris go wrong is not in saying that ideas have consequences — they do, and a tradition that contains the Kharijites also contains the resources to refute them — but in flattening a vast civilization into a single ideological bloc. Where Ben Affleck goes wrong is in suggesting that theology is irrelevant, that ISIS is purely a product of Western intervention and Sykes-Picot. Both moves are evasions. ISIS's recruiters do cite scripture, and the response cannot only be sociology; it must also be exegesis. That work belongs primarily to Muslims because they are the ones with the standing, the languages, and the audience to do it persuasively.

So the obligation, such as it is, runs in two directions. Muslim scholars and communities have every reason to wage the theological argument against the extremists, and they are. Non-Muslims have an obligation to stop treating every Muslim cab driver and cardiologist as a suspect required to prove a negative. Confusing those two obligations — demanding apologies instead of amplifying refutations — is how a debate about extremism becomes a vehicle for the prejudice it claims to oppose.

The militants themselves weaponize this dynamic: they claim to be the sole authentic voice of Islam, daring the world to equate the faith with their barbarism. In this context, some argue that ordinary Muslims have an urgent duty to refute the extremists’ theological claims, to prove that “true” Islam is peaceful. But this framing is a trap. It accepts the premise that Islam’s legitimacy hinges on a public relations campaign, and it places a burden of perpetual apology on a diverse global community of 1.6 billion people who never consented to be represented by murderers.

What, then, should Muslims do? Not to satisfy non-Muslim critics, but for their own integrity, they must do what any community does when its moral vocabulary is hijacked: they must reclaim it. This is not “defense” in the sense of answering to an external jury. It is an internal struggle—a *jihad* in its truest ethical sense—over meaning and identity. Across the world, Muslim scholars, activists, and ordinary believers are already doing this. This work is essential, not to prove respectability, but to protect vulnerable young people from a death cult that offers a sense of belonging and purpose to the alienated.

No, Muslims do not need to defend their faith, as if it were a defendant before a hostile court. They do need, as all people of conscience do, to confront evil that speaks in their name. That confrontation is not a performance for the approval of the anxious; it is an act of self-preservation and moral clarity. The proper answer to extremism is not perpetual apology but a confident, forthright insistence that the hijackers do not own the house they have invaded.
