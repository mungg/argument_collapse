"While Muslims should not be held collectively responsible for the crimes of extremists, they have a unique and necessary role in countering radicalism by challenging its theological and political claims from within the faith."

Muslims should not be asked to “defend Islam” in the sense of standing in the dock for crimes they did not commit. A Muslim shopkeeper in Queens, a student in Birmingham, or a teacher in Jakarta bears no more personal responsibility for the Islamic State than a Christian in Ohio bears for the Lord’s Resistance Army or for anti-abortion terrorism. Collective suspicion is unjust, and it also helps extremists, who thrive on the claim that the West is at war with Islam itself.

But Muslims do have a particular role to play in resisting extremism, precisely because the argument is being waged in Islamic language. The Islamic State does not merely commit atrocities; it advertises them as religious duties. It recruits alienated young people by presenting itself as the authentic voice of Islam, dismissing other Muslims as hypocrites, apostates or cowards. That claim must be answered by people with religious credibility: parents, imams, scholars, teachers, youth workers and ordinary believers who can say, from within the tradition, that murder of civilians, enslavement, sectarian slaughter and rule by terror are not marks of piety.

This is already happening more often than many critics admit. Muslim scholars across the world have condemned the Islamic State. Sunni and Shiite authorities have denounced its brutality. Muslim communities in Europe and North America have cooperated in efforts to prevent recruitment, often while also warning governments that crude surveillance and stigmatization can backfire. The problem is not silence so much as audibility: condemnations by Muslims rarely receive the same attention as crimes committed in Islam’s name.

Still, condemnation alone is not enough. Extremism feeds on theology, but also on politics, humiliation, war, dictatorship, sectarianism and social failure. The Islamic State grew out of the catastrophe of Iraq, the Syrian civil war, the repression of Sunnis, and the collapse of state authority. Young recruits in Western countries may be drawn by rage, adventure, identity, grievance or the promise of belonging. A serious Muslim response must therefore be moral, intellectual and civic: teaching religious literacy; challenging conspiracy theories and sectarian hatred; creating institutions where young people can argue, belong and be heard; and refusing to romanticize violence even when Muslims are victims elsewhere.

Non-Muslims also have responsibilities. It is lazy to treat 1.6 billion people as if they were a single suspect community. It is also historically ignorant. Most victims of jihadist violence are Muslims, and many of the people fighting the Islamic State today are Muslims. If public debate insists that “Islam” itself is the problem, it pushes moderate and devout Muslims into an impossible position: either accept a smear against their faith or appear evasive. Better to distinguish between a religion with diverse texts, histories and schools, and modern political movements that weaponize selected ideas for power.

So the answer is yes and no. Muslims need not apologize for being Muslim, and they should reject demands for ritual denunciation after every outrage. But Muslims, like members of any tradition, must contest those who corrupt their language and symbols. The most effective answer to extremism will come when Muslim communities can oppose it confidently from within, while the wider society refuses to confuse that struggle with an indictment of Islam itself.

"While Muslims are not collectively responsible for the actions of extremists and do not owe the world an apology, they have a unique and necessary role in contesting the theological interpretations of their faith from within."

The question itself contains a trap. Asking whether Muslims must defend their faith presumes a collective ownership of the violence committed by the Islamic State, Boko Haram, or the Khorasan group — a presumption no one applies to Christians when a Lord's Resistance Army operates in Uganda, or to Buddhists when monks incite pogroms against the Rohingya in Myanmar. The 1.6 billion Muslims who had nothing to do with the beheading of Alan Henning do not owe the rest of us an apology, and the demand that they perform one, ritually, after every atrocity is itself a small act of bigotry. Reza Aslan made this point bluntly on CNN last month, and he was right.

And yet the more interesting question — not whether Muslims must defend their faith to non-Muslims, but whether they should contest its meaning among themselves — has an obvious answer: of course, and they already are. In September, more than 120 Muslim scholars, including muftis from Egypt, Nigeria, and Bosnia, published an open letter to Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi systematically dismantling the Islamic State's theology point by point — its takfir of other Muslims, its enslavement of Yazidis, its claim to a caliphate, its destruction of shrines. The #NotInMyName campaign by young British Muslims and the unequivocal condemnations from Al-Azhar, the Organization of Islamic Cooperation, and figures from Hamza Yusuf to Yusuf al-Qaradawi (who disagree on almost everything else) show that the intra-Muslim argument is happening at full volume. Westerners simply aren't listening.

Where Bill Maher and Sam Harris go wrong is not in saying that ideas have consequences — they do, and a tradition that contains the Kharijites also contains the resources to refute them — but in flattening a vast civilization into a single ideological bloc. Where Ben Affleck goes wrong is in suggesting that theology is irrelevant, that ISIS is purely a product of Western intervention and Sykes-Picot. Both moves are evasions. ISIS's recruiters do cite scripture, and the response cannot only be sociology; it must also be exegesis. That work belongs primarily to Muslims because they are the ones with the standing, the languages, and the audience to do it persuasively.

So the obligation, such as it is, runs in two directions. Muslim scholars and communities have every reason to wage the theological argument against the extremists, and they are. Non-Muslims have an obligation to stop treating every Muslim cab driver and cardiologist as a suspect required to prove a negative. Confusing those two obligations — demanding apologies instead of amplifying refutations — is how a debate about extremism becomes a vehicle for the prejudice it claims to oppose.

"Muslims should not be required to defend their faith to satisfy external critics or double standards, but they should internally reclaim their religious identity to protect their community and moral integrity from extremist hijacking."

The demand that Muslims defend their faith against extremists has become a ritualized feature of public debate, but it is a demand that is as tiresome as it is misguided. It presumes that Islam, as a monolithic entity, stands in the dock, and that its adherents are collectively responsible for the crimes of a fringe. No other religious community is systematically asked to account for the violence of its most distorted adherents in the same way—few demanded that Christians “defend” Christianity after Anders Breivik’s massacre in Norway, or that Buddhists explain away the ethnic cleansing in Myanmar. The question itself, then, is a product of a double standard that implicitly frames Islam as uniquely suspect.

Yet the question persists because the Islamic State’s medieval savagery—broadcast in high definition—and the stream of disaffected youths from London to Sydney who flock to its banner create a visceral anxiety. The militants themselves weaponize this dynamic: they claim to be the sole authentic voice of Islam, daring the world to equate the faith with their barbarism. In this context, some argue that ordinary Muslims have an urgent duty to refute the extremists’ theological claims, to prove that “true” Islam is peaceful. But this framing is a trap. It accepts the premise that Islam’s legitimacy hinges on a public relations campaign, and it places a burden of perpetual apology on a diverse global community of 1.6 billion people who never consented to be represented by murderers.

What, then, should Muslims do? Not to satisfy non-Muslim critics, but for their own integrity, they must do what any community does when its moral vocabulary is hijacked: they must reclaim it. This is not “defense” in the sense of answering to an external jury. It is an internal struggle—a *jihad* in its truest ethical sense—over meaning and identity. Across the world, Muslim scholars, activists, and ordinary believers are already doing this. They point out that the Islamic State’s caliphate is a grotesque parody that violates centuries of Islamic jurisprudence on the conduct of war, the protection of innocents, and the prohibition of coercion in faith. They argue, from within the tradition, that the extremists are not just un-Islamic but anti-Islamic. This work is essential, not to prove respectability, but to protect vulnerable young people from a death cult that offers a sense of belonging and purpose to the alienated.

The most powerful rebuttal to extremism, however, is lived. When Muslim communities openly practice their faith with the mundane decency that characterizes the vast majority—running schools, healing the sick, participating in democratic life—they undermine the narrative of a clash of civilizations far more effectively than any reactive statement. The insistent call for Muslims to “condemn” and “defend” often misses the quiet fact that the most dignified response to a hijacking is not to negotiate with the hostage-takers but to make their claimed mandate laughably irrelevant.

No, Muslims do not need to defend their faith, as if it were a defendant before a hostile court. They do need, as all people of conscience do, to confront evil that speaks in their name. That confrontation is not a performance for the approval of the anxious; it is an act of self-preservation and moral clarity. The proper answer to extremism is not perpetual apology but a confident, forthright insistence that the hijackers do not own the house they have invaded.

The brutal ascent of the Islamic State in Iraq and Syria has cast a long, dark shadow over the global Muslim community. As masked militants broadcast horrific acts of violence under the black flag of faith, a disturbing narrative is gaining traction in the West: the suggestion that Islam itself is inherently violent. Recent high-profile cultural debates have only amplified this tension, putting 1.6 billion Muslims on trial for the atrocities of a radical fringe. In this fraught environment, we must distinguish between the necessary theological refutation of extremism and the unjust demand that every Muslim act as a spokesperson against terror.

Do Muslims need to defend their faith? In the theological realm, yes. Islamic scholars and clerics possess a unique and urgent responsibility to dismantle the ideological foundations of groups like the Islamic State. And they are already doing so. Last month, over 120 prominent Islamic scholars from around the world issued an open letter to ISIS leader Abu Bakr al-Baghdadi, meticulously using Quranic texts and classical jurisprudence to dismantle his group's claims to a "caliphate." Meanwhile, grassroots movements like the #NotInMyName campaign launched by young British Muslims demonstrate a widespread, vocal rejection of terror.

The broader public rarely demands that ordinary Christians collectively apologize for the atrocities committed by the Lord’s Resistance Army in central Africa, nor are all Buddhists asked to answer for the violent persecution of Muslims in Myanmar. Expecting a Muslim teacher in Chicago or a shopkeeper in London to constantly prove their humanity in the face of ISIS’s barbarism is not only unfair, but it alienates the very communities that Western democracies need to embrace. We must remember that the primary victims of the Islamic State's brutality—those being slaughtered, displaced, and oppressed in the Middle East—are overwhelmingly Muslims themselves.

Moving forward, the response to extremism must be a shared endeavor. Muslim communities must remain vigilant, actively working to inoculate disaffected youth against the siren song of radicalization. This means creating spaces for open dialogue about political grievances and ensuring that religious education is robust enough to withstand extremist distortions.

However, the non-Muslim world must also fulfill its role. Instead of amplifying the voices of a violent minority and asking why the peaceful majority is silent, society and the media must start listening to the millions of Muslims who are already speaking out. Defeating the ideological virus of extremism requires solidarity. If we allow terrorists to successfully hijack the definition of Islam and dictate how the West views ordinary Muslims, we grant the extremists the very legitimacy they desperately crave.
