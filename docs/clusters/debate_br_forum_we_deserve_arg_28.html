"While Morozov’s critique of AI’s teleological origins is insightful, his proposed alternative of 'ecological reason' is politically and practically unfeasible because it fails to account for the necessity of scale, the genuine value users find in efficiency, and the structural power of the current global order."

Morozov's diagnosis of AI's teleological origins is largely compelling, but his proposed alternatives raise troubling questions about scale and political feasibility. The language of "ecological reason" sounds emancipatory, yet we must ask: can this approach actually govern the infrastructure of a global digital economy? The Allende and Cybersyn examples are instructive precisely because they failed—not through lack of vision but through the impossibility of sustaining that vision under economic and military pressure from without. Morozov acknowledges these failures but treats them as evidence that we haven't tried hard enough rather than as evidence that the project may be structurally impossible in the current global order.

The core problem is that Morozov underestimates how much the "efficiency lobby" he critiques has become synonymous with human flourishing in the contemporary imagination. When billions of people around the world voluntarily surrender their time and attention to platforms optimized for engagement metrics, we cannot simply dismiss this as false consciousness or ideological manipulation. People find genuine value in these systems, even as they recognize their flaws. A vision of "ecological reason" that requires citizens to fundamentally reorient their relationship to technology—to become Stone Age wanderers in a digital landscape—seems more like an aesthetic preference than a democratic mandate.

Moreover, Morozov's critique of the Perceptron's military origins applies equally to his own proposed alternatives. The MIT Media Lab, which he discusses, was indeed co-opted from playful beginnings into corporate functionality. But what about the many eolithic experiments that never found institutional homes at all? The history of countercultural technology is littered with promising prototypes that remained prototypes precisely because scaling them required exactly the institutional resources and market logic Morozov wishes to escape. The tension between the eolithic and the scalable may not be a contingent failure of implementation but a logical contradiction.

I also worry that Morozov's framework, for all its sophistication, underweights the genuine successes of goal-oriented AI. The language learning application he describes using is genuinely useful—not merely because it optimizes for efficiency, but because it helps real people achieve real goals they care about. The question is not whether instrumental reason is bad, but whether it should be the only framework. Morozov sometimes suggests that any teleological framing is inherently suspect, yet human beings routinely set and pursue goals without this being dystopian. The problem is not goal-oriented behavior but goals imposed by distant institutions on unwilling subjects.

What would a genuinely democratic AI agenda look like? It would need to do more than theorize about alternative paths; it would need to build coalitions capable of sustaining alternative institutions against market pressures. This requires boring work—organizing, lobbying, building public alternatives—that Morozov's philosophical framing does not adequately address. The Allende government had the political will but lacked the economic and military resilience to survive. Today's advocates of public AI face similar challenges, compounded by the fact that AI infrastructure is even more capital-intensive than earlier computing systems.

In the end, Morozov offers a valuable provocation: we should not accept that instrumental reason is the only possible framework for thinking about technology and intelligence. But he underestimates the degree to which goal-oriented systems can be made more democratic through familiar mechanisms—transparency requirements, labor rights, public options, antitrust enforcement—that do not require us to reinvent the entire relationship between citizens and computational systems. The question is whether we can make the efficiency lobby more accountable rather than whether we can abolish it.

  2. 02Morozov underestimates the extent to which the 'efficiency lobby' provides genuine value and flourishing for billions of users, making his 'ecological reason' seem more like an aesthetic preference than a democratic mandate.
  3. 03There is a logical contradiction between the idiosyncratic nature of 'eolithic' experimentation and the institutional resources required to scale technology for a global population.
  4. 04Goal-oriented or instrumental AI is not inherently dystopian; the problem lies in institutional imposition rather than the pursuit of goals themselves.
  5. 05A truly democratic AI agenda requires the 'boring work' of building political coalitions and public alternatives rather than a radical philosophical reorientation of human intelligence.
  6. 06Existing democratic mechanisms like antitrust enforcement and transparency requirements are more realistic paths to accountability than abolishing the framework of instrumental reason.
