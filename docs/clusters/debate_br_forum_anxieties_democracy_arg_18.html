"While institutional and procedural reforms are necessary to address the crisis of democracy, they will remain ineffective unless accompanied by a revitalization of the civic culture and social habits that make representative government workable."

Ira Katznelson’s essay rightly emphasizes institutions, but democracy’s present anxiety also arises from culture: the habits, media environments, and social relations that make representative institutions either workable or impossible. Congress is polarized because the country is polarized, but the country is polarized partly because the institutions that once organized conflict into negotiable forms have weakened. Democracy needs procedures; it also needs citizens capable of living with one another.

The phrase “civic virtue” can sound nostalgic or moralizing, and it has often been used to scold the powerless. Still, some civic dispositions are indispensable. Citizens must be willing to lose elections without treating the winners as illegitimate. They must distinguish opponents from enemies. They must accept that compromise is not always corruption. They must trust some sources of information beyond their own camp. They must care about the future beyond the next news cycle. These habits cannot be enacted by statute.

Our media environment makes them harder to sustain. In the mid-twentieth century, Americans received news through a limited number of newspapers and broadcast networks. That system had serious flaws: gatekeepers were often white, male, centrist, and deferential to power. But it did create some shared factual baselines. Today’s fragmented media gives citizens more voices and more access, which is a democratic gain. It also allows people to inhabit self-confirming worlds. Cable news, talk radio, partisan websites, viral social media, and algorithmic sorting reward outrage and identity affirmation.

This transformation affects representatives. A member of Congress who compromises can be denounced instantly by activists, donors, and media personalities. A misleading clip travels farther than a careful explanation. Primary electorates, already more ideological than general electorates, are reinforced by information systems that punish deviation. Legislators do not simply deliberate poorly because they lack virtue; they operate in an attention economy that makes deliberation look like weakness.

Social trust has also declined. Americans bowl alone, as Robert Putnam famously put it, but the problem is broader than association membership. Many citizens distrust government, corporations, unions, universities, newspapers, religious institutions, and one another. Some distrust is earned. The Iraq War, the financial crisis, clerical abuse scandals, racialized policing, corporate fraud, and political hypocrisy have all damaged authority. But generalized distrust is corrosive. If every institution is presumed rigged, democratic persuasion becomes nearly impossible.

Katznelson asks whether democracy can live without a singular public interest. It can, but not without some sense of common fate. Pluralism is not enough if groups believe they are merely trapped together in a zero-sum struggle. Economic inequality worsens this by separating citizens into different neighborhoods, schools, risks, and life chances. Racial segregation and class isolation mean that many Americans rarely encounter those unlike themselves except through stereotypes. Democracy becomes abstract when daily life is socially sorted.

Civic education is therefore not a sentimental add-on. Many Americans know little about constitutional structure, federalism, parties, courts, or the legislative process. More importantly, they have few opportunities to practice democratic disagreement. Schools should teach not only facts about government but habits of argument: how to evaluate evidence, interpret opposing views charitably, speak in public, and change one’s mind. Student governments and debates are not trivial if they teach that procedure can channel conflict.

National service deserves renewed consideration. Military service now touches a small portion of society, often from particular regions and families. A broader system of civilian service—voluntary but strongly encouraged through educational benefits—could bring young Americans across lines of class, race, religion, and geography to work on infrastructure, elder care, conservation, tutoring, and disaster response. Service is not a cure for polarization, but shared work can build civic muscle that online argument cannot.

Local democracy also matters. National politics is too distant and symbolic to bear the whole burden of citizenship. School boards, city councils, neighborhood associations, libraries, churches, unions, and civic groups give people experience with concrete problems and imperfect allies. Yet local institutions can also be exclusionary or captured. The goal should be more inclusive local participation, not romantic localism. Participatory budgeting, community policing boards, and local planning forums can help citizens see government as something they do rather than something done to them.

Parties, too, must become civic institutions again. They now function heavily as fundraising machines and ideological brands. At their best, parties aggregate interests, recruit candidates, educate voters, and broker compromise. Reviving local party organizations might sound old-fashioned, but democracy needs mediating institutions between isolated individuals and national spectacle. Without mediation, politics becomes a direct emotional relationship between citizens and mass media figures.

Katznelson is correct to warn against plebiscitary and populist shortcuts. But the cure is not only procedural reform in Congress. A legislature cannot deliberate well for a society that has forgotten how to deliberate at all. Nor can it compromise if citizens regard compromise as betrayal before negotiations begin.

The repair of democracy will therefore be slow. Campaign finance rules, voting rights protections, and legislative reforms are necessary. But so are schools, associations, public media, integrated neighborhoods, service opportunities, and leaders who refuse to profit from contempt. The Capitol dome can be restored on a schedule. Civic trust cannot. It must be rebuilt through repeated experiences of fair dealing across difference.

Forster gave democracy two cheers because it admits variety and permits criticism. Today we might add a quieter requirement: it asks us to share a country with people who anger, puzzle, and sometimes frighten us. Institutions make that possible, but only if citizens are willing to try.
