"The federal government's attempt to compel local police into civil immigration enforcement threatens to destroy the community trust essential for effective domestic intelligence gathering."

Daniel Richman’s essay “The Right Fight” correctly identifies the crucial tension at the heart of domestic intelligence gathering: the need for local police to maintain the trust of immigrant communities while simultaneously assisting federal counterterrorism efforts. He astutely observes that if local police are viewed as agents of deportation, they will lose the ability to gather the very information that might prevent a terrorist attack. However, Richman treats the issue of immigration enforcement as just one variable in a broader negotiation between local and federal authorities, believing that the pushback from local police chiefs will naturally temper federal demands. In doing so, he severely underestimates the destructive power of the current federal push to merge local policing with civil immigration enforcement, and he overestimates the ability of local resistance to withstand federal legislative pressure.

The battle over whether local police should enforce federal civil immigration laws is not a peripheral dispute; it is the absolute crux of the domestic intelligence debate. If the firewall between local law enforcement and federal immigration enforcement falls, the entire foundation of community policing—and by extension, the local intelligence network Richman champions—will collapse.

For decades, the standard practice in nearly all major American cities has been to strictly separate local policing from immigration status. This was not born out of a liberal political agenda, but out of cold, hard operational necessity. As the International Association of Chiefs of Police has repeatedly stated, local police rely on the cooperation of victims and witnesses to maintain public safety. If a battered undocumented woman, or an illegal immigrant who witnesses a murder, believes that calling 911 will result in their deportation, they will not call. The resulting silence creates zones of impunity where criminals can operate freely, victimizing both legal and illegal residents alike.

Post-9/11, the Department of Justice under Attorney General John Ashcroft has aggressively sought to dismantle this firewall. By entering the names of civil immigration violators into the National Crime Information Center (NCIC) database and encouraging local police to arrest them, the federal government is attempting to deputize 700,000 local officers into a massive deportation force.

Richman notes that local police have largely resisted these efforts, and he views this resistance as evidence that his model of "institutional friction" is working. But he fails to account for the sledgehammer that the federal government is currently preparing to swing: the Clear Law Enforcement for Criminal Alien Removal (CLEAR) Act.

The CLEAR Act, currently pending in Congress, would mandate that state and local police enforce federal immigration laws, explicitly threatening to strip vital federal funding—including State Criminal Alien Assistance Program (SCAAP) funds—from any jurisdiction that refuses to comply. This is not a negotiation; it is an ultimatum. If the CLEAR Act or similar legislation passes, the local police chiefs whom Richman relies upon to defend community interests will have their hands tied. Mayors facing bankrupt city budgets will be forced to capitulate, ordering their officers to check the immigration status of individuals during routine traffic stops and noise complaints.

Once this happens, the "optimistic story" Richman outlines evaporates. The Arab, Muslim, and broader immigrant communities will instantly, and rationally, view local police not as peacekeepers, but as an existential threat to their families and livelihoods. The dialogue that Richman points to—police chiefs meeting with Arab-American chamber of commerce leaders and guarding mosques—will be seen as a cynical trap. You cannot build a relationship of trust with a community while simultaneously wielding the power to tear their families apart over visa overstays.

Richman argues that the costs of aggressive federal immigration enforcement "need to be recognized by both the executive and legislative branches," and that the dimensions of federal-local interaction "need to be a matter of negotiation, not federal fiat." But why would the federal government negotiate when it has the power to compel? The political rewards for politicians in Washington to appear "tough on borders" and "tough on terror" are immense, and the costs of ruined community relations in Dearborn or Brooklyn are entirely borne by local officials.

Therefore, we cannot rely on the structural friction of federalism to naturally resolve this conflict. The protection of the local intelligence network requires a hard, statutory firewall. Congress must explicitly reject legislation like the CLEAR Act and instead codify the principle that federal homeland security and law enforcement grants cannot be conditioned on local participation in civil immigration enforcement.

If we truly believe, as Richman does, that the local beat cop is essential to uncovering the next terrorist plot, we must aggressively protect that cop’s ability to talk to the most vulnerable members of our society. Allowing the War on Terror to become a pretext for a mass federalization of immigration enforcement will not make us safer; it will blind our local police, silence crucial informants, and drive the very communities we need to engage deep into the shadows. The fight for civil liberties and effective intelligence is, right now, the fight to keep local police out of the immigration business.
