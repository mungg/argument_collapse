"While offshore balancing is the correct strategic direction, the United States must pursue a more radical and consistent policy of military disengagement than Stephen Walt proposes to truly avoid the pitfalls of imperial overreach."

Stephen Walt’s call for offshore balancing is a welcome antidote to the imperial overreach of the Bush years. His diagnosis of the ways American primacy provokes fear, resistance, and anti-Americanism is largely correct, and his insistence that the United States husband its power rather than squander it in preventive wars is a lesson the nation has paid for in Iraq with blood and treasure. And yet, for all its wisdom, Walt’s offshore balancing remains too timid. He still clings to a forward military presence in Asia, retains NATO as a shell, and imagines interventions to contain China or to halt genocide when conditions allow. If we take the logic of his argument seriously—that American power is most effective when it is least provocative, and that local powers will balance on their own unless we remove their incentives to do so—the conclusion must be a far more sweeping disengagement.

Walt’s mistake is to preserve too many of the reflexes of global leadership. He would keep “a significant military presence in Asia,” primarily air and naval forces, to reassure allies and lay a foundation for containing China. But that same logic of playing hard-to-get that he applies so lucidly to the Persian Gulf applies equally to East Asia. Japan, South Korea, and Australia are wealthy, technically advanced democracies fully capable of defending themselves and offsetting Chinese ambitions without permanent American garrisons. If they sometimes fear abandonment, that fear is healthy: it spurs them to do more for their own security and to make their relationships with the United States truly reciprocal. The Bush administration has already begun to rearrange forces globally; offshore balancing should complete that process by withdrawing virtually all forward-based American troops in Asia, leaving only naval stations that can be quickly surged in an emergency, not a permanent tripwire.

The same logic demands a final, honest reckoning with NATO. Walt’s proposal—small training contingents but no leading security role—is a half-measure that perpetuates the illusion of an alliance that has outlived its purpose. Europe faces no plausible conventional military threat. The modest security challenges it might confront, from Balkan instability to terrorism, are manageable with European resources. Keeping the transatlantic security architecture on life support distorts European defense budgets and encourages free-riding while simultaneously giving the United States a stake in squabbles that do not touch its vital interests. The United States should declare that the mutual defense clause of Article V is no longer operative in the post-Soviet environment and that European security is a European responsibility. Such candor would be a shock, but it would be a productive one, much as the Nixon Doctrine encouraged regional self-reliance in Asia.

Walt also leaves the door ajar for humanitarian interventions, even as he acknowledges that offshore balancing would make them easier. This is a dangerous temptation. The recent history of such interventions—from Somalia to Kosovo—shows that even well-intentioned missions drag the United States into messy nation-building that inflames nationalism and drains resources without clear strategic benefit. The genocide in Darfur, heartbreaking as it is, does not threaten the security of the United States. We have no obligation, and usually no capacity, to fix all the world’s horrors. Offshore balancing should be understood as a strategy of strategic restraint, not a justification for wars of altruism that bluntly instrumentalize American power. Walt’s own argument that nationalism defeats outside occupations applies just as forcefully to peacekeeping and humanitarian occupation as it does to democracy-by-gun.

Even more, offshore balancing as Walt presents it underestimates the economic drain of a global presence. Maintaining air and naval supremacy across the world’s oceans, even without large ground forces, still requires staggering defense budgets that compete with domestic investment and undercut the long-term vitality of American power. A genuine strategy of offshore balancing would draw down military spending significantly and reinvest those resources in education, infrastructure, and energy independence, which are ultimately the true foundations of national strength. Walt notes the importance of economic vitality but does not press the case far enough. The post-9/11 surge in defense spending has produced a military that can invade and occupy a medium-sized country but cannot sustain its own technological edge without an economy that grows faster than its competitors. A leaner global posture, focused on the core mission of defending the Americas and sea lanes, would be both more sustainable and less provocative.

Finally, Walt’s discussion of Israel, while courageous, still assumes that American leverage can and should be used to engineer a comprehensive peace. Offshore balancing, however, suggests that the United States should not be the guarantor of every regional peace. If the Palestinians and Israelis cannot reach a settlement, the cost falls primarily on them. The United States can and should step back from the role of permanent mediator and banker. Yes, we should make clear that settlement expansion is not something we underwrite, but we should not replace one form of intimate entanglement with another. The Middle East will remain turbulent regardless of American diplomacy; our interest is simply that no hostile hegemon controls the Gulf’s oil. That can be secured from over the horizon, as Walt himself suggests, and it need not require resolving the Palestinian question.

Offshore balancing is the best available grand strategy, but it must be pursued with the remorseless logic its own premises demand. Half-measures will keep the United States enmeshed in regional rivalries that breed resentment and drain resources. The world’s only superpower can afford to do less, precisely because it is so strong. The hardest lesson of primacy is that restraint is not passivity but the ultimate expression of confidence. If Americans want their power to attract rather than repel, they must do more than reduce their footprint. They must bring their troops home, shed obsolete alliances, and trust that a nation so blessed by geography and wealth will remain secure not by policing the globe but by minding its own strength.
