Diversified DeepSeek

"While Sypnowich correctly identifies the failures of liberal 'opportunity talk,' her proposed focus on 'equal flourishing' remains a form of top-down social engineering that fails to challenge the underlying capitalist structures of wage labor and production."

Christine Sypnowich’s essay is a welcome intervention against the impoverished egalitarianism of the liberal mainstream, and her call to center human flourishing echoes the best of the socialist tradition. Yet for all its apparent radicalism, her argument ultimately remains trapped within the very capitalist logic it purports to transcend. By focusing on distribution of “flourishing” and retaining categories of contribution, talent, and even a modified market, Sypnowich offers what is essentially a social-democratic vision dressed in radical language. A genuinely socialist conception of equality demands not the equalization of flourishing within a framework of wage labor and state-managed incentives, but the abolition of the conditions that force us to measure human worth by productivity in the first place. We need to go beyond both liberal opportunity and Sypnowich’s managerial outcome egalitarianism toward a society of free association and the decommodification of life.

Sypnowich repeatedly invokes Marx, but she selectively reads him. She quotes the famous communist dictum, “from each according to his ability, to each according to his needs”—yet she interprets it as a principle of fair contribution and community solidarity, glossing over its revolutionary implications. For Marx, this principle was meant for the higher phase of communist society, after the abolition of the division of labor, the antithesis between mental and physical labor, and the subordination of individuals to the production process. In that society, labor would have become life’s prime want, not a means to an end, and the very idea of measuring contribution—of holding people responsible for how much they produce—would be obsolete. Sypnowich, by contrast, still talks of “enabling people to make the most of their potential,” of “contribution” as a source of disadvantage if lacking, and of “incentives” to take up valuable pursuits. This is the language of social engineering, not of liberation.

The problem is that Sypnowich’s “equal flourishing” remains distributive: it assumes that the state, or perhaps a democratic community, will determine what counts as flourishing, how to measure it, and how to allocate resources to achieve it equally. This is a recipe for a new form of bureaucratic domination. Even if the process is democratic, the very act of defining and administering a metric of flourishing for an entire population will inevitably codify certain norms and marginalize others. Who decides? Sypnowich says we should have “legitimate, democratic debate,” but history shows that under capitalism, and indeed under state socialism, such debates are heavily skewed by the interests of the ruling strata—whether capitalists, managers, or party officials. Without a fundamental transformation of the relations of production, democratic decision-making is hollow.

A truly socialist approach would aim not at equal access to a pre-defined basket of “flourishing,” but at creating the material and social conditions under which every person can freely develop their creative capacities. This requires the abolition of private property in the means of production and the end of wage labor. Workers’ time would no longer be commanded by an employer; automated production, where possible, would liberate humans from the realm of necessity, as Marx envisioned. The measure of wealth would no longer be labor time but disposable time. In such a society, the very question of equalizing outcomes becomes moot, because production is for use, not for exchange, and individuals are free to pursue their own manifold activities without being slotted into a hierarchy of “more or less desirable” social positions that Sypnowich’s meritocracy critique presupposes.

Sypnowich’s critique of opportunity talk often revolves around the damage that capitalism inflicts on people’s ability to choose well. But she does not call for the overthrow of capitalism; she calls for a “socialism” that seems to consist of universal provision, a robust welfare state, and a cultural project to elevate tastes. This leaves intact the basic economic structure—the market, the firm, the wage relation—while merely buffering its worst effects and nudging people toward approved activities. It is reminiscent of what Nancy Fraser has called the “progressive neoliberal” settlement, albeit with a humanistic gloss. The result could well be a world where everyone is equally monitored and managed, a capitalist state with a smiling face.

What would a more radical egalitarianism look like? It would start from the demand for a universal basic income or basic services not as an opportunity-equalizing device but as a fundamental right, decoupled from work and behavior. It would champion the reduction of the working week and the expansion of the commons—public wealth held in common and governed democratically, from land to intellectual property. It would foster cooperative enterprises and community self-provisioning, rather than relying on state redistribution of market incomes. And it would recognize that the very notions of “talent” and “merit” are ideological constructs that serve to justify hierarchy, not natural facts to be accommodated.

Sypnowich is right that liberal egalitarianism’s focus on opportunity is insufficient, and her emphasis on community and solidarity is important. But without a commitment to dismantling capitalism at its roots, her vision risks becoming a top-down project that perpetuates alienation under a new name. The goal should not be that everyone “flourishes” according to some official standard, but that everyone possesses the real freedom to shape their own activity in cooperation with others, unburdened by the compulsion to sell their time or conform to state-sanctioned life plans. That is the true heart of the socialist idea, and it lies beyond equal flourishing.
