"The solution to political corruption lies not in technocratic campaign finance reforms or constitutional nostalgia for the Framers' intent, but in mass grassroots mobilization and the radical expansion of voting rights to counter organized capital."

Lawrence Lessig’s “Democracy After Citizens United” frames the problem of money in politics as a failure of institutional design, a deviation from the Founders' intent that Congress be dependent "upon the People alone." He proposes clever, technocratic solutions like democracy vouchers to realign this dependency. But in his focus on the mechanics of campaign finance and the jurisprudence of the First Amendment, Lessig misses the deeper, systemic reality of American power. The corruption he describes is not an aberration to be fixed by tweaking the funding formulas; it is the logical, working outcome of a political system designed from its inception to protect property over people. To truly address this, we need less constitutional nostalgia and more grassroots mobilization.

Let us start with Lessig’s reliance on the Framers. He repeatedly quotes Federalist No. 57, arguing that the Founders intended representatives to have a "habitual recollection of their dependence on the people." But who were "the people" in 1787? They were white, male, property owners. The framers explicitly designed a system that excluded the vast majority of the population—women, enslaved people, Native Americans, and unpropertied men—from the franchise. They created a Senate insulated from popular vote, an Electoral College to check the passions of the masses, and a judiciary appointed for life. The dependency the Framers designed was a dependency on the wealthy elite.

When Lessig argues that *Citizens United* disrupted a system meant to serve the public interest, he is romanticizing a past that never existed for most Americans. The "institutional corruption" of Congress prioritizing the affluent over the working class is not a recent development caused by PACs and independent expenditures; it is the historical baseline of the Republic. Money has always governed Washington. The only difference today is the brazenness with which it operates and the legal sanction it has received from the Roberts Court.

Therefore, the solution cannot merely be structural campaign finance reform. Lessig’s endorsement of the Fair Elections Now Act and small-dollar vouchers is fine as far as it goes. If enacted, it would certainly lower the barrier to entry for candidates who aren't independently wealthy or connected to corporate networks. But it is a mistake to believe that changing the way candidates raise money will fundamentally alter the balance of power in a society characterized by staggering economic inequality.

If we give every voter a $50 democracy voucher, we are still operating on a playing field where a billionaire can spend $50 million. The "iceberg theory" Lessig cites guarantees that even fully publicly funded politicians will govern in fear of the unlimited independent expenditures unleashed by *Citizens United*. We cannot outspend organized capital. Attempting to match corporate money with public vouchers is a defensive strategy that accepts the premise that money is the primary currency of political power.

The only historical counterweight to organized money is organized people. The moments in American history where the government has been forced to act against the interests of corporate power—the abolition of slavery, the establishment of the weekend and the eight-hour workday, the civil rights movement, the creation of Medicare and Social Security—did not occur because reformers found the perfect campaign finance mechanism. They occurred because millions of people mobilized, protested, struck, and disrupted the status quo until the political cost of inaction became higher than the financial rewards of the donor class.

If we want to break the dependency corruption in Congress, progressives need to stop obsessing over the Supreme Court's interpretation of the First Amendment and start focusing on the actual voters who have been marginalized by this system. We are facing a crisis of participation. Turnout in midterm elections is abysmal, and the electorate skews older, wealthier, and whiter than the nation as a whole. This is exactly how the donor class wants it. When the electorate is small and disengaged, television advertisements funded by dark money have maximum impact.

The true remedy for *Citizens United* is a radical expansion of democracy itself. Instead of just fighting for campaign finance reform, we must fight for automatic voter registration, the abolition of restrictive voter ID laws, the restoration of voting rights to the formerly incarcerated, and the establishment of Election Day as a national holiday. We must rebuild the power of labor unions, which for decades served as the primary financial and organizational counterweight to corporate influence before being systematically dismantled by the very political class Lessig hopes to reform.

In the 2008 election, Barack Obama did not defeat the establishment solely by raising money; he built a massive, grassroots organizing apparatus that activated millions of new voters. That ground game overwhelmed the traditional power structures. When a politician knows that they are backed by a highly organized, highly mobilized constituency that will knock on doors, make phone calls, and turn out at the polls regardless of the weather, the threat of an Exxon or Google independent expenditure loses its teeth.

Lessig is right that the system is corrupt. But the way to cure institutional corruption is not just to insulate the politicians from the money; it is to overwhelm the money with the power of the people. Vouchers and matching funds are tools, but they are not a movement. Until we focus our energy on mass democratic mobilization and dismantling the barriers to the ballot box, Congress will remain dependent on the few, just as it has been since the beginning.

  3. 03Technocratic fixes like democracy vouchers are insufficient because they cannot match the scale of organized capital or eliminate the 'iceberg' effect of unlimited independent expenditures.
  4. 04Historical progress against corporate power has always been achieved through mass social movements and disruption rather than changes to funding formulas.
  5. 05The most effective way to neutralize the influence of money is to increase political participation through reforms like automatic voter registration and rebuilding labor unions.
  6. 06A highly organized and mobilized constituency provides a more powerful shield for politicians against corporate threats than any campaign finance regulation.
