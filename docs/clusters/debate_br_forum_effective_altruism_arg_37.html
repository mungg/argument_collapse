"While effective altruism’s emphasis on rigor is valuable, the framework oversimplifies the complexities of international development by overestimating the certainty of impact metrics and neglecting systemic political solutions."

As someone who has worked in international development for fifteen years, I want to offer a practitioner's perspective on effective altruism. Peter Singer's framework is intellectually appealing, but it does not always translate smoothly into the messy realities of aid work, and some of its implications are more complicated than the theory suggests.

Let me start with what effective altruism gets right. The emphasis on evidence and effectiveness is genuinely valuable. Too much charitable giving is based on sentiment rather than impact, and the culture of rigor that Singer promotes is a welcome correction. GiveWell's research on charity effectiveness has been particularly valuable, forcing organizations like mine to think more carefully about outcomes.

But the framework's confidence in our ability to measure effectiveness can be misleading. Singer presents a world in which we can know, with reasonable precision, which interventions work and which don't. The reality is more uncertain. The evidence base for many development interventions is weak, and studies frequently reach conflicting conclusions. Bed nets for malaria prevention have strong evidence, but many other popular interventions have much thinner support. Effective altruists who assume that some causes are objectively superior to others may be overconfident about our knowledge.

There is also the problem of generalizability. A malaria net might be highly effective in one context but ineffective in another due to differences in transmission patterns, local health systems, or community practices. What works in a randomized controlled trial may not work when scaled up in complex real-world settings. The effective altruism movement sometimes treats evidence from controlled studies as definitive, when in fact implementation matters enormously.

The Matt Wage example raises another concern. Singer presents Wall Street arbitrage as a straightforwardly useful way to generate donations. But arbitrage trading does not create value in the way that productive business activity does; it profits by identifying price discrepancies in markets. One might question whether this is an ethically neutral means of generating funds for charity, or whether it has effects on market efficiency, resource allocation, or economic inequality that should factor into the moral calculation.

More broadly, I worry that effective altruism's focus on individual donations distracts from the political dimensions of development. The reason billions of people live in extreme poverty is not primarily a failure of charitable giving but a failure of political will—by developed countries, by corrupt governments in developing nations, by international institutions. The most effective intervention for reducing global poverty might be political advocacy, not malaria nets.

Singer might respond that effective altruists can choose to support political causes. But the framework's emphasis on measurable outcomes favors interventions like bed nets over harder-to-measure activities like advocacy. This bias may lead effective altruists to neglect precisely the levers that could produce systemic change.

None of this is meant to dismiss effective altruism entirely. Singer is right that we should think carefully about how to help others, and his movement has done valuable work in promoting evidence-based philanthropy. But practitioners know that the world is more complicated than the theory suggests. Humility about our knowledge and openness to multiple approaches are essential.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The evidence base for many development interventions is often weak or conflicting, making the movement's confidence in 'objectively superior' causes potentially overconfident.
  2. 02Success in controlled trials does not guarantee effectiveness in real-world settings where local health systems and community practices vary significantly.
  3. 03The 'earning to give' model, exemplified by Wall Street arbitrage, ignores the potential negative ethical and economic consequences of how that wealth is generated.
  4. 04The movement’s preference for easily measurable outcomes creates a bias toward direct aid like bed nets while neglecting harder-to-measure political advocacy that could address the root causes of poverty.
  5. 05Effective altruism fails to sufficiently account for the political dimensions and institutional failures that are the primary drivers of global inequality.
