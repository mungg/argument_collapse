"The American left should abandon the project of reclaiming the language of 'freedom' because the concept is inherently and inextricably rooted in settler colonialism and the production of racialized unfreedom."

Beyond Reclamation: Why Black Radicalism Has Already Moved On

Aziz Rana's essay performs a familiar gesture in left-liberal American thought: it acknowledges that the language of freedom in the United States is structured by settler conquest and racial subjugation, then concludes that this language must nonetheless be reclaimed, refurbished, and put to emancipatory work. The reasoning is pragmatic — freedom is too powerful a word to surrender. But the gesture rests on a confidence that the word can be cleansed of its historical sediment, and the Black radical tradition has, for some time now, expressed real doubt about that confidence. Rana cites King, Hamer, the Freedom Budget, the Movement for Black Lives. He does not cite Saidiya Hartman, who has shown how the supposed transition from slavery to freedom remade rather than dissolved the structures of unfreedom. He does not cite Frank Wilderson or Jared Sexton, whose work argues that anti-Blackness is not a contingent feature of American liberty but its constitutive shadow. He does not cite Ruth Wilson Gilmore, whose abolitionist geography insists on building something rather than reclaiming something.

This is not a quibble about citations. It points to a deeper problem with Rana's frame. He treats the settler/imperial corruption of American freedom as a historical accident that produced exclusionary outcomes, against which a more inclusive freedom might be politically constructed. But for a substantial current of Black radical thought, the corruption is not accidental. The "free" subject of American political discourse was constituted in and through the unfreedom of others — not just because settlers happened to dispossess Natives and enslave Africans on the way to their own liberty, but because the very category of the free citizen required, conceptually and materially, the production of unfree non-citizens against whom freedom could be measured and felt.

If this is right — and the historical and theoretical case is strong — then Rana's project of "reclaiming" freedom faces a problem he does not adequately confront. You cannot reclaim a concept whose internal structure depends on the production of the unfree. You can only repurpose its language while building something different underneath. The Black radical tradition has increasingly understood this, which is why so much of its most generative recent work uses different vocabulary: abolition, fugitivity, marronage, the undercommons, refusal. These are not synonyms for freedom-as-inclusion-and-solidarity. They name something more radical: a politics that does not seek admission to the house of American liberty but proposes to dismantle the architecture entirely.

Consider the abolitionist current within contemporary movements against policing. Rana mentions "racial justice protests against policing following George Floyd's murder" as an example of potent left invocations of freedom. But "abolish the police" is not a freedom claim in the sense Rana means. It is not a demand to be included in a more democratic policing institution. It is a demand to end policing as a mode of social control and replace it with different infrastructures of care, mutual aid, and conflict resolution. The activists most associated with this current — Mariame Kaba, Patrisse Cullors, Derecka Purnell — have generally been careful to distinguish their project from a reformist freedom politics that would make policing more accountable or more democratic. They are not trying to extend the franchise of liberty. They are trying to build a different world.

The same is true of much contemporary work on reparations. The most rigorous arguments — those of William Darity and Kirsten Mullen, for instance — do not frame reparations as a fulfillment of American freedom's promise to Black people. They frame reparations as the partial repair of a debt produced by centuries of plunder, a debt that is in principle unpayable and that places the moral relationship between the American state and Black descendants of slavery on different terms than the relationship of liberty-bearing citizenship. This is not freedom-talk. It is something else.

Rana might respond that such framings can be folded into his solidaristic vision: the inclusive freedom he wants would, of course, include reparative justice, abolitionist transformation, and the rest. But this folding is precisely the problem. To translate every radical project into the vocabulary of freedom is to refuse to learn from the people who have stopped using that vocabulary because they found it inadequate to what they were trying to say. It is to insist that the master's tools must be reclaimed, when those tools — to recall Audre Lorde — will never dismantle the master's house.

There is also a strategic point. Rana acknowledges that "the most potent left invocations of freedom overwhelmingly involve activists within marginalized communities who are referencing actual direct, immediate, and ongoing threats." He treats this as a partial success of the left freedom project. I read it differently. Those invocations are potent because they are local, specific, and tied to material struggles — against a police killing, against a state abortion ban, against a school-board attack on trans kids. They do not generalize easily into a national freedom narrative, and the attempt to generalize them risks abstracting them from the conditions that make them resonant. The Movement for Black Lives's policy platform, for example, is far more granular than any "freedom for all" framework can capture, and its power lies precisely in that granularity.

What, then, would I recommend instead? Three things.

First, take seriously the possibility that the left does not need a single master-signifier to organize its politics. The conservative movement won its dominance not because it had a better word than "freedom," but because it built durable infrastructure — donor networks, think tanks, judicial pipelines, media — capable of advancing many different policy demands under a shifting array of rhetorical banners. The left could do worse than imitate the infrastructure while letting a hundred vocabularies bloom: abolition here, dignity there, solidarity in another context, care in another.

Second, be honest that the Black radical tradition has produced political vocabularies that are not reducible to liberalism's freedom-talk, and that the white left's persistent attempts to translate these vocabularies back into the language of American liberty have often functioned to defang them. Rana's essay does this politely. The defanging is still defanging.

Third, recognize that Rana's institutional program — voting rights, labor law reform, demilitarization, decriminalized migration — is largely correct and largely separable from his freedom-framing. We can build those institutions whether or not we call the result "freedom." Indeed, calling the result "freedom" may be precisely what allows it to be co-opted, watered down, and absorbed back into the same liberal-imperial frame that Rana otherwise wants to escape. The American experience of "freedom" includes, after all, the Cold War weaponization of the word, the Freedom Fries he mentions, Operation Enduring Freedom, and the long history of empire abroad and prison expansion at home being justified in liberty's name. A movement that wins the institutions but loses the word may end up further ahead than a movement that wins the word but loses the institutions.

Rana wants both. I am skeptical the word can be won. But I am with him on the institutions, and that may be enough.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Black radical tradition has developed more generative and accurate vocabularies—such as abolition, fugitivity, and repair—that cannot be reduced to or translated into traditional 'freedom-talk.'
  2. 02The historical structure of American liberty is not accidentally exclusionary but conceptually dependent on the existence of an unfree 'other' against whom the free citizen is measured.
  3. 03Attempting to fold radical movements like police abolition into a universal freedom narrative defangs their specific material demands and ignores their goal of dismantling rather than joining the existing political architecture.
  4. 04The left does not require a single 'master-signifier' like freedom to be successful; it should instead focus on building durable infrastructure and allowing diverse political vocabularies to coexist.
  5. 05The institutional reforms proposed by the lead essay, such as labor and voting rights, are valuable on their own merits and do not require the potentially co-optable framing of 'freedom' to be achieved.
Diversified GPT

"While freedom is a powerful political concept, the left should prioritize democracy, equality, and solidarity as its central organizing terms because the American definition of freedom is dangerously unstable and prone to being used as a justification for domination."

Aziz Rana argues that the left should not abandon freedom to the right. I understand the appeal. Freedom is among the most powerful words in political life, and oppressed movements have used it with extraordinary force. The Black freedom struggle, women’s liberation, gay liberation, anticolonial liberation, labor’s fight against wage slavery—all testify that the language can be emancipatory. Still, I remain unconvinced that freedom should be the left’s central organizing term. Its American meanings are not merely contested; they are dangerously unstable. Democracy, equality, and solidarity may serve us better.

Rana’s own history helps explain why. In the United States, freedom has so often meant the power to dominate that the word carries a recurring ambiguity. Freedom for slaveholders meant property in human beings. Freedom for settlers meant land cleared of Indigenous claims. Freedom for employers meant open shops and injunctions against strikes. Freedom for segregationists meant local control. Freedom for men meant patriarchal authority. Freedom for gun absolutists means living under the threat of mass violence. Freedom for corporations means deregulation. Freedom for fossil fuel interests means the right to destabilize the climate. Freedom for anti-vaccine activists can mean exposing others to preventable disease. The pattern is not incidental.

Of course, every political ideal can be abused. Democracy can become majoritarian tyranny; equality can become enforced sameness; solidarity can become conformity. But freedom is unusually susceptible to appropriation because it asks, freedom from what, freedom for whom, freedom to do what? Without an answer, the word often defaults to the perspective of the person who feels constrained. The landlord feels unfree when tenants gain rights. The employer feels unfree when workers organize. The polluter feels unfree when regulated. The parent feels unfree when a school recognizes a child’s identity against the parent’s wishes. The armed man feels unfree when asked to leave his gun at home. Politics becomes a competition of injured sovereignties.

Rana tries to solve this by defining freedom as inclusive and solidaristic self-rule. That is a noble definition, but it may be too philosophically demanding to do the work he wants. If we must constantly explain that freedom does not mean doing what one wants, does not mean market choice, does not mean property dominion, does not mean freedom from democratic obligation, does not mean national impunity, and does not mean the power to exclude, perhaps the term is not our best vehicle. A politics that needs a seminar attached to its core word may struggle against the right’s simpler cry: leave me alone.

This is not an argument for purism. Movements should use freedom when it resonates. “Freedom to marry” was effective because it named a concrete exclusion. “Reproductive freedom” is powerful because state coercion is immediate and bodily. “Freedom from police violence” is intelligible because domination is visible. But as an overarching framework, freedom may pull the left toward defensive politics: freedom from bans, freedom from censorship, freedom from discrimination, freedom from bosses, freedom from debt. These are vital, yet they may not articulate the positive common world we need.

Democracy might. Democracy asks not “how can I be unconstrained?” but “how shall we govern ourselves?” It immediately implies equality of voice, public justification, institutions, participation, and accountability. It can include rights, because democracy requires free speech, association, bodily security, and minority protection. It can include social provision, because people cannot participate as equals under conditions of desperation. It can include workplace reform, because authoritarian workplaces train citizens in obedience. It can include anti-imperialism, because one people cannot democratically rule another. Democracy does much of the work Rana asks freedom to do, with less conceptual baggage.

Equality is equally indispensable. The central defect of American freedom has been its compatibility with hierarchy. Equality makes that harder. It asks whether some people are positioned above others in law, wealth, status, or power. It exposes the false universalism of freedom claims made from dominant positions. A billionaire and a warehouse worker may both dislike constraints; equality asks whether their complaints deserve the same weight. A state and an occupied people may both invoke security; equality asks whose life is being treated as disposable. Freedom without equality can legitimate domination. Equality without freedom can become oppressive, but the left is not in much danger of forgetting liberty in a country saturated with libertarian rhetoric.

Solidarity names another missing element. Rana rightly emphasizes that people must experience collective agency. Solidarity says plainly that our fates are linked. It does not begin from the sovereign individual but from relationship. It can be sentimental if left vague, but in organizing it is practical: I will take a risk for you because your fight affects mine. Solidarity can cross borders more naturally than national freedom, which often turns possessive. It is harder, though not impossible, to turn solidarity into a demand for domination.

The objection is obvious: Americans love freedom. Why choose less resonant words? But resonance is not fixed. Political language changes through conflict. Conservatives spent decades making “liberal” into an insult and “freedom” into a market brand. The left can spend decades making democracy and equality feel as American as the Fourth of July. In fact, many people already respond to them. The defense of democracy after January 6, the outrage over voter suppression, the support for unions, the anger at billionaires, the rejection of abortion bans in ballot measures—these are not only freedom sentiments. They are democratic and egalitarian sentiments.

Rana’s institution-building program remains persuasive even if we shift the vocabulary. Unions are democratic institutions. Voting rights are democratic rights. Demilitarization is democratic accountability. Immigrant legalization is equal membership. Public goods are solidaristic infrastructure. Reproductive justice is bodily equality and democratic citizenship. The same agenda may be more coherent if organized around democracy rather than freedom.

Still, I do not propose banning the word. That would be impossible and undesirable. Freedom is too deeply rooted in struggles against slavery, colonialism, patriarchy, and repression. But the left should use it adjectivally and concretely: reproductive freedom, academic freedom, freedom from hunger, freedom from eviction, freedom to organize, freedom from occupation, freedom from debt peonage. Each phrase specifies the domination at issue. What I doubt is the usefulness of “freedom” standing alone as the master term.

The right benefits when freedom is abstract because abstraction allows projection. Everyone can imagine their favorite constraint as tyranny. The left should force specificity. Who is constrained? By whom? Through what institution? With what alternatives? On whose labor does this freedom depend? What inequalities does it preserve? These questions lead us to democracy, equality, and solidarity.

Rana’s warning against fatalism is important. A politics that gives up on emancipation gives up on politics itself. But perhaps the path forward is not to reclaim freedom so much as to discipline it—to subordinate it to democratic equality. Freedom is valuable when it names release from domination and participation in common life. It is dangerous when it names escape from obligation. The left should say so. And it should not be afraid to let other words lead.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The term 'freedom' is uniquely susceptible to right-wing appropriation because, without specific qualifiers, it defaults to the perspective of the individual who feels constrained, often at the expense of others.
  2. 02Defining freedom as 'inclusive self-rule' is too philosophically complex for effective political messaging compared to the right's simpler appeal to individual autonomy.
  3. 03The concept of democracy is a more effective framework than freedom because it inherently implies equality, participation, and collective accountability without the same conceptual baggage.
  4. 04Equality and solidarity are necessary to discipline the concept of freedom, as they expose the hierarchies and interdependencies that abstract notions of liberty often ignore.
  5. 05The left should use the language of freedom only in concrete, adjectival forms—such as 'reproductive freedom' or 'freedom from debt'—rather than allowing it to stand alone as an abstract master term.
  6. 06The institutional reforms proposed by the lead essay, such as union building and voting rights, are more coherently framed as projects of democratic and egalitarian renewal than as 'reclaiming freedom.'
