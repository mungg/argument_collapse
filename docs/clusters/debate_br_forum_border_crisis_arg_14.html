"While Ypi’s class-based analysis of migration is theoretically sound, her refusal to accept the practical necessity of border management risks consigning the left to permanent political irrelevance and ceding power to the far right."

Lea Ypi’s essay is a formidable synthesis of insights from critical theory, dependency economics, and labour history. Her central argument—that the migration crisis is a crisis of class, not culture, and that progressive parties betray their mission when they echo anti-immigration rhetoric—is a necessary corrective to a public debate poisoned by scapegoating. Yet the essay flinches at the hard practical questions that any left government would face in office. For all its analytical rigour, it offers a strategy that is more likely to consign the left to permanent opposition than to build a durable political bloc capable of winning and wielding power. The tension is not between principle and pragmatism, as Ypi sometimes implies, but between two different ways of understanding what principles demand in a deeply imperfect world.

Consider the real-world constraints that social democratic parties have confronted in the past decade. In Germany, Sahra Wagenknecht’s insistence that mass immigration undermines the welfare state is not a perverse invention of bad-faith actors. It reflects a genuine dilemma. The German welfare system, like those in other Northern European countries, rests on a high degree of social trust and a shared sense of reciprocal obligation, built up over generations through institutions that are national in scope. When a large number of newcomers arrive in a short time—as happened during the 2015–16 refugee crisis and its aftermath—these institutions come under strain. Schools, hospitals, and housing markets in certain areas are suddenly overwhelmed. Native workers who already feel the squeeze of stagnant wages and precarious employment see their access to public services diminish. This does not justify xenophobia, but it is a material fact that a responsible left party cannot simply dismiss as false consciousness. Ypi argues that the real problem is underfunding and austerity, not immigration. That is partly true, but it ignores the time lag: you can redistribute resources only after you have built the political will to do so, and building that will is made harder when large-scale immigration, in the absence of sufficient investment, fuels a zero-sum perception.

The European electoral landscape of 2024–2025 bears this out. In France, the far-right Rassemblement National continues to make inroads into working-class constituencies by linking immigration to the cost of living and insecurity. Marine Le Pen’s party has skilfully framed itself as the defender of the welfare state against a globalised elite that uses migrants to undercut wages. The French left, fragmented and often dismissive of these cultural anxieties, has not found a compelling answer. In Sweden, the Social Democrats have struggled to hold together a coalition that includes liberal urbanites and traditional working-class voters, with immigration a persistent wedge issue. When Danish social democrats adopted a tougher line on asylum and integration, they did so not out of moral failure but out of a cold assessment that their welfare model could not otherwise survive public confidence. Ypi’s response—that the left loses when it plays the nativist game—is only half the story. The Danish Social Democrats have managed to implement a restrictive immigration policy while still pursuing a relatively robust welfare agenda and keeping the far right at bay. Their approach is not a capitulation to racism but a strategic decision to remove immigration as a mobilising issue for the hard right, thereby clearing the ground for a left economic agenda.

Ypi is correct that “tough words” can validate xenophobia, but she underestimates the degree to which voters distinguish between different kinds of tough policies. There is a world of difference between Donald Trump’s chain-dragging deportation videos and a policy that caps numbers, prioritises family reunification and asylum seekers, and invests heavily in integration. The latter can be defended on progressive grounds: a managed inflow allows receiving communities to plan services, ensures labour standards are not eroded, and reduces the chaos that smugglers like “Ben the Lame” exploit. Ypi romanticises the figure of the smuggler as a worker doing a job, but the reality is that smuggling networks are brutal, exploitative, and feed off the desperation created by a broken system. A well-regulated legal migration framework would strip these networks of their power far more effectively than open-border advocacy ever could.

Furthermore, Ypi’s class-based analysis, while powerful, is incomplete. Culture and identity are not merely tools of elite manipulation; they are genuine sources of belonging that people hold dear, and they shape the willingness to share resources. This is not a reactionary concession but an anthropological reality. The welfare state did not emerge from abstract class solidarity alone; it grew out of national solidarity, often forged in opposition to external enemies or colonial others. The challenge for the left is not to dismiss the national dimension as a false consciousness but to recast it in inclusive, civic terms. That requires time, patient institution-building, and a frank acknowledgment that rapid demographic change can unsettle even the most egalitarian communities. Ypi’s vision of transnational class organisation, while inspiring, has rarely succeeded on a sustained basis. The “Make Amazon Pay” campaign she cites is a valuable gesture, but it is a thin reed on which to hang a global political strategy. Amazon workers in Germany and the UK may share interests, but they vote in different elections, pay into different tax systems, and rely on different states for health care and pensions. The state remains the primary locus of redistributive capacity, and ignoring that fact does not make it go away.

The tragedy of the current moment is that the far right has captured the language of protection while much of the left has retreated into a cosmopolitanism that speaks only to a minority. Ypi’s call to “challenge dominant exclusionary discourses, even at the price of a short-term loss” is noble, but it is also a luxury that people living on the sharp end of austerity cannot afford. A short-term loss for a political movement can mean a decade of right-wing rule, with all the damage to public services, workers’ rights, and climate policy that entails. The left’s first duty is to prevent that outcome. That means building a broad electoral coalition that can include people who are anxious about immigration but still support progressive taxation, public investment, and strong labour rights. It means offering a narrative that acknowledges the legitimacy of borders as a tool of democratic self-governance while insisting that those borders must be governed humanely and with a deep awareness of global responsibility.

This is not an abandonment of principle but a recalibration. It requires, as Ypi herself suggests, linking migration to the wider crisis of neoliberalism. But that link must be made not by dismissing the concerns of host communities, but by showing how a well-managed migration system, combined with massive investment in public services and a crackdown on exploitative employers, can benefit both newcomers and settled residents. It means championing the rights of migrants already here, regularising the undocumented, and opposing the grotesque sale of citizenship to oligarchs, while also asserting that a democratic state must be able to decide who enters its territory. There is no contradiction in saying that borders should exist and that they should be governed by justice. The real courage is to hold both thoughts at once, and to build the political majorities that can turn them into reality.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The strain that rapid migration places on national welfare institutions and public services is a material reality that cannot be dismissed as mere false consciousness or elite manipulation.
  2. 02Adopting restrictive but managed immigration policies, as seen in Denmark, can be a strategic necessity to neutralize the far right and secure public support for a progressive economic agenda.
  3. 03A well-regulated legal migration framework is a more humane and effective way to undermine exploitative smuggling networks than open-border advocacy.
  4. 04National identity and cultural belonging are genuine sources of social trust required for redistributive welfare states, and they cannot be easily replaced by abstract transnational class solidarity.
  5. 05The state remains the primary locus of redistributive power, meaning the left must prioritize building winning electoral coalitions within national borders to prevent the damage of long-term right-wing rule.
  6. 06Progressives should champion a 'middle path' that asserts the democratic right to control borders while ensuring they are governed humanely and linked to massive investment in public infrastructure.
