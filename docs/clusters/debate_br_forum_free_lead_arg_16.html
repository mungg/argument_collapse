"The corruption of the American political system is caused by the excessive power and scope of the federal government rather than the campaign finance system itself."

Governor Buddy Roemer articulates a frustration shared by many Americans across the political spectrum: Washington is out of touch, beholden to special interests, and completely detached from the economic realities facing ordinary citizens. His critique of the Wall Street bailouts—where taxpayers were forced to subsidize the reckless gambles of the financial elite—is entirely justified. However, Roemer misdiagnoses the fundamental disease afflicting our republic. He believes that money corrupting politics is the root problem, when in fact, the influx of money into politics is merely a symptom of a government that has grown far too powerful.

Roemer laments that the tax code is “thousands and thousands of unreadable pages” and that Congress crafts an “insanely complicated system of special favors” to collect campaign contributions. He points to this and concludes we need to overhaul how campaigns are funded. But this logic is entirely backward. If Congress did not have the arbitrary power to pick winners and losers in the marketplace, to hand out billions in subsidies, and to rewrite the regulatory landscape at a whim, special interests would have no reason to spend millions lobbying and funding campaigns.

Consider the reality of rent-seeking. When the federal government injects itself into every corner of the economy—from healthcare to energy to financial services—businesses are forced to engage in politics simply as a matter of self-defense. If a single line in a regulatory bill can wipe out your industry or double your profit margins, investing millions in campaign contributions is not just rational; it is a fiduciary duty to your shareholders. You cannot build a massive honeypot of federal power and then be surprised when the bears show up to gorge themselves.

The solution to the crony capitalism Roemer rightfully despises is not to micromanage the First Amendment or to cap individual donations at an arbitrary $100. The solution is to radically shrink the size, scope, and arbitrary power of the federal government. If Congress had no authority to bail out Wall Street, Wall Street would not waste its money buying Congress.

Furthermore, Roemer’s proposed campaign finance reforms are deeply problematic from a constitutional and practical perspective. He argues for individual contribution limits of $100 and praises the idea of "democracy vouchers" funded by taxpayers. But restricting political contributions restricts political speech. In a modern mass democracy, disseminating a message requires resources. By strictly limiting how much individuals or organizations can spend to amplify their views, we do not level the playing field; we merely shift power to the incumbent political class and the mainstream media, who face no such restrictions on their ability to influence the public narrative.

Roemer’s democracy voucher proposal also violates his own stated principle that "no system should force any citizen to support political speech that he or she doesn’t believe in." Because money is fungible, allowing taxpayers to direct public funds to politicians means that the tax base as a whole is subsidizing political campaigns. I should not be forced, through my tax dollars, to underwrite the campaign of a politician whose values I find abhorrent, regardless of the bureaucratic mechanism used to distribute the funds.

Ultimately, Roemer is asking the wrong question. He asks how we can "fix" the campaign finance system so that politicians are free to lead. We should instead be asking how we can constrain the power of politicians so that the American people are free to live and do business without needing to buy permission from Washington. As long as the government has the power to give you everything you want and take everything you have, the money will find its way to the politicians. To get the money out of politics, we must first get the politics out of the economy.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Special interests only invest in lobbying and campaign contributions because the government has the arbitrary power to pick winners and losers in the marketplace.
  2. 02Businesses are forced into rent-seeking and political spending as a matter of self-defense when federal regulations can fundamentally alter their industry's viability.
  3. 03The only effective way to eliminate crony capitalism is to radically shrink the size and authority of the federal government so there is no 'honeypot' for special interests to pursue.
  4. 04Strict contribution limits infringe upon the First Amendment and unfairly empower incumbents and the media by restricting the ability of others to disseminate their messages.
  5. 05Taxpayer-funded democracy vouchers violate the principle of not forcing citizens to subsidize political speech they find abhorrent.
  6. 06To remove money from politics, the government must be stripped of its power to intervene in and control the economy.
