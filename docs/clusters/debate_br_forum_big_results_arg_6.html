"While behavioral economics offers useful micro-level interventions, its focus on small-scale nudges and individual decision-making distracts from and fails to address the macro-structural forces that are the true causes of global poverty."

Rachel Glennerster and Michael Kremer offer a deeply seductive vision of international development in their essay, "Small Changes, Big Results." At a time when the development community is exhausted by the grand, competing narratives of the past—the heavy-handed structural adjustment programs of the 1980s and 1990s, or the utopian, trillion-dollar "Big Push" models advocated by figures like Jeffrey Sachs—the promise that a few cents’ worth of chlorine or a bag of lentils could transform lives is undeniably appealing. It is the ultimate technocratic dream: poverty alleviated not through messy political upheaval or massive wealth redistribution, but through clever administrative tweaks and a better understanding of human cognition. However, we must be deeply skeptical of this paradigm. By focusing so intensely on the micro-behaviors of the poor, the behavioral economics revolution risks blinding us to the macro-structural forces that actually create and sustain poverty.

The core premise of the behavioral approach is that the poor, much like middle-class Americans failing to save for retirement, make sub-optimal decisions due to cognitive biases, procrastination, and lack of information. Glennerster and Kremer point to the failure of households to adopt cheap point-of-use chlorination or to keep children in school, suggesting that small incentives—a conditional cash transfer or a free school uniform—can overcome these behavioral hurdles. But this diagnosis fundamentally misdiagnoses the disease. Poverty is not a series of cognitive errors waiting to be corrected by a well-designed nudge. Poverty is a profound lack of power, resources, and institutional support.

Consider the authors’ focus on education. They celebrate the fact that a free school uniform, costing a mere six dollars, can increase school attendance. This is undoubtedly true, and in the short term, beneficial to those children. But why is a six-dollar uniform the barrier to entry in the first place? It is because the structural economic conditions in these countries have left families with absolutely no disposable income, and because states have failed to provide truly free, accessible public education. More importantly, getting a child into a classroom is only a fraction of the battle. If that child is sitting in a crumbling building with fifty other students, taught by an under-qualified teacher who frequently fails to show up to work, and if that child graduates into an economy with zero formal employment opportunities, what has the uniform actually achieved? The RCT framework measures the easily quantifiable metric—attendance—while ignoring the structural dead-end that awaits the student.

The same critique applies to the health interventions celebrated in the essay. Offering rural Rajasthani mothers a set of plates to complete their child’s immunization schedule is presented as a triumph of behavioral design, raising immunization rates from 5 percent to 38 percent. But a society where baseline immunization is 5 percent is a society experiencing catastrophic state failure. It is a society where the public health infrastructure has been systematically starved of resources, perhaps due to historical debt burdens, unfair trade regimes, or entrenched domestic corruption. Giving out lentils and plates does absolutely nothing to build a resilient, functioning ministry of health. In fact, it allows governments and international donors to bypass the hard, unglamorous work of state-building in favor of quick, measurable, NGO-driven fixes.

Furthermore, the history of global development simply does not support the idea that "small changes" yield the kind of big results that actually lift nations out of poverty. If we look at the countries that have successfully eradicated mass poverty over the last fifty years—South Korea, Taiwan, and more recently, China—they did not do so by running randomized controlled trials on mosquito nets or chlorine dispensers. They achieved monumental poverty reduction through aggressive industrial policy, land reform, massive investments in physical infrastructure, and forced integration into the global trading system. They transformed their economies at a structural level. The behavioral economics approach, by contrast, operates entirely at the margins. It assumes the structural status quo is immutable and asks only how we can make life slightly more bearable for those trapped within it.

There is also an inherent danger in the way behavioral economics frames the poor. By constantly highlighting their "irrationality" and "procrastination," the discipline subtly shifts the blame for poverty onto the poor themselves. If a community suffers from rampant waterborne illness, the structuralist sees a failure of the state to build water treatment plants and lay plumbing—infrastructure that citizens in the developed world take for granted as a basic right. The behavioral economist, however, sees a failure of the local residents to properly dose their water with chlorine. This shift in perspective is profoundly ideological, even if it masks itself in the objective language of randomized trials. It absolves the global economic system and local elites of their responsibility to provide basic public goods.

None of this is to say that RCTs are entirely useless or that we should stop providing mosquito nets. Harm reduction is valuable, and saving a child from diarrhea is a moral good regardless of the method. But we must be honest about the limitations of this approach. Glennerster and Kremer argue that the experimental approach forces economists to "engage development problems where they play out." But they are only engaging with a highly restricted subset of development problems—the ones small enough to fit inside a controlled experiment.

As we look toward the future of development policy in 2011, we must resist the temptation to let "small changes" become the entirety of our ambition. The poor do not just need clever incentives to navigate broken systems; they need those systems to be fixed. They need democratic accountability, functional state institutions, equitable global trade, and genuine economic opportunity. Until we are willing to tackle these massive, messy, un-randomizable structural issues, the behavioral revolution will remain nothing more than a highly efficient way of applying band-aids to deep, systemic wounds.

  3. 03Small-scale incentives like giving out lentils for immunizations allow governments and donors to bypass the essential, long-term work of building resilient state health infrastructures.
  4. 04Historical evidence from countries that successfully eradicated mass poverty shows that transformation requires aggressive industrial policy and structural economic shifts rather than marginal behavioral adjustments.
  5. 05The behavioral framework shifts the blame for poverty onto the poor by framing their survival strategies as 'irrationality' while absolving global and local elites of their responsibility to provide basic public goods.

Diversified Gemini

"While behavioral economics and randomized controlled trials offer valuable micro-level insights, they distract from the macroeconomic structural transformations and growth-oriented policies necessary to truly eradicate poverty."

"Small Changes, Big Results" by Rachel Glennerster and Michael Kremer is a quintessential document of the new consensus in development economics as of early 2011. Driven by the methodological purity of the randomized controlled trial (RCT) and the insights of behavioral economics, the authors argue that poverty alleviation is best achieved through micro-interventions that correct human cognitive biases. While their findings on procrastination, price sensitivity, and peer effects are undoubtedly fascinating, their approach suffers from a severe macroeconomic myopia. By training the entire intellectual apparatus of development economics on the micro-decisions of the poor, the authors distract from the fundamental macroeconomic engines of wealth creation. Nations do not escape poverty through chlorine dispensers and school uniforms; they escape poverty through structural transformation and economic growth.

The authors celebrate the fact that conditional cash transfers in Mexico (PROGRESA) increased secondary school enrollment by nearly 15 percentage points, or that small incentives can drastically alter healthcare-seeking behavior. These are positive outcomes, but they operate entirely within the realm of social policy and human capital accumulation. The implicit assumption is that if we can just get enough children educated and healthy, development will naturally follow. However, human capital is only one side of the economic equation. If an economy does not generate productive jobs to absorb these newly educated, healthy young people, the result is not broad-based prosperity, but rather educated unemployment, wage stagnation, and potentially severe political instability.

Let us look at the empirical reality of the last fifty years. The most spectacular poverty reduction in human history has occurred in East Asia, most notably in China, where hundreds of millions of people have been lifted out of extreme poverty since the late 1970s. Did the Chinese government achieve this by running RCTs to figure out the optimal behavioral nudge to get farmers to wash their hands? No. They achieved it through massive, state-directed investments in physical infrastructure—roads, ports, and power grids—coupled with export-oriented industrialization, special economic zones, and aggressive integration into global supply chains. They moved millions of workers out of low-productivity subsistence agriculture into high-productivity manufacturing.

This kind of macro-structural transformation is entirely absent from the worldview presented by Glennerster and Kremer. The word "infrastructure" does not appear in their essay. The concepts of trade, industrial policy, credit markets, and capital accumulation are nowhere to be found. Instead, the authors deride the field for promoting "big-think fads." But the "big think" of industrialization and macroeconomic growth is not a fad; it is the only historically proven method for a society to transition from poverty to wealth.

The danger of the RCT revolution is not that its findings are false, but that its methodology dictates the agenda, crowding out macro-level research and investment. Because an RCT requires a specific type of intervention—one that can be easily randomized across a large sample of individuals or villages—it inherently limits the scope of development policy to the micro level. You can randomize the distribution of de-worming pills across Kenyan schools. You cannot randomize the construction of a national highway system, the negotiation of a bilateral trade agreement, or the establishment of a central bank. As a result, the brightest minds in economics and the bulk of philanthropic funding are increasingly diverted away from the difficult, messy, un-randomizable questions of macroeconomic growth, and toward optimizing the distribution of lentils.

Furthermore, the authors’ assertion that small barriers (like a 30-cent fee for chlorine) prevent rational investments reveals a misunderstanding of what poverty actually is. If a family cannot afford a 30-cent fee to save their child from life-threatening diarrhea, the problem is not a behavioral quirk or a lack of understanding of the benefits. The problem is an absolute, crushing lack of income. Providing the chlorine for free is a necessary humanitarian band-aid, but it does absolutely nothing to raise the family’s income so they can afford the 30 cents tomorrow. Only macroeconomic growth, driven by job creation and productivity gains, can provide that sustained income.

Even the authors' example of the call centers in India inadvertently proves the macro point. They note that when recruiters for white-collar jobs went to villages outside Delhi, families started sending girls to school more and improving their nutrition. The authors use this to show that information about economic returns changes behavior. But the true driver of this change wasn't the "information"—it was the fact that the macroeconomic structure of India was changing. The call center jobs actually had to exist for the behavior to change. That requires foreign direct investment, telecommunications infrastructure, and a growing service sector.

Glennerster and Kremer offer a highly optimized approach to managing poverty, making life at the bottom of the income distribution slightly healthier and slightly more educated. But we must not mistake the management of poverty for the eradication of it. To truly lift the developing world out of poverty, we must return our gaze to the macro economy, focusing on the large-scale investments and structural transformations that create real wealth. The "small changes" of behavioral economics may yield big results in academic journals, but only big changes in the real economy yield prosperity.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Human capital accumulation through health and education interventions is ineffective if the macroeconomy fails to generate productive jobs for the newly educated population.
  2. 02Historical evidence from East Asia demonstrates that massive investments in physical infrastructure and industrialization, rather than behavioral nudges, are the primary drivers of poverty reduction.
  3. 03The methodological requirements of randomized controlled trials inherently limit the scope of development policy by favoring small-scale, easily randomized interventions over essential but un-randomizable macroeconomic reforms.
  4. 04The extreme price sensitivity observed in the poor is a symptom of a crushing lack of income that can only be solved by productivity gains and job creation, not by humanitarian 'band-aids' like free distribution.
  5. 05The success of micro-interventions often depends on pre-existing macroeconomic shifts, such as the emergence of new industrial sectors, which provide the actual incentives for behavioral change.
