"While the right to be hostile and engage in disruptive protest must be protected against administrative repression, a healthy university community also requires the development of democratic relationships and civic infrastructure to address the genuine pain and alienation that rights alone cannot resolve."

Alex Gourevitch is right that universities have confused discomfort with danger, and that this confusion has licensed repression. But a campus cannot live by rights alone. The right to be hostile may be necessary; it is not sufficient.

The recent protest crisis has revealed two failures at once. The first is institutional: administrators, donors, trustees, and politicians have used safety, civility, property, and civil rights language to suppress dissent, especially dissent over Israel’s war in Gaza. The second is communal: many students no longer trust that those on the other side of a political conflict recognize their humanity. The first failure requires civil liberties. The second requires practices of democratic relationship that civil liberties cannot by themselves provide.

Gourevitch’s essay is most convincing when he argues that subjective feelings of unsafety cannot determine the limits of protest. If students can shut down demonstrations by saying they feel harmed, every movement will be vulnerable to the most anxious or powerful audience. Pro-Palestinian protests can be suppressed because Jewish students feel threatened. Pro-Israel events can be suppressed because Palestinian students feel threatened. Anti-abortion displays can be suppressed because women feel attacked. Police abolition events can be suppressed because officers feel demonized. There is no stable freedom down that road.

A university must therefore draw a line between distress and danger. It should punish threats, violence, vandalism, targeted harassment, and discriminatory exclusion. It should not punish the mere expression of political hostility. It should not call police because chants are upsetting or because a protest makes a quad unpleasant to cross. Gourevitch’s warning is indispensable.

Yet his frame leaves an important question underdeveloped: what should campus communities do with the pain that does not justify suppression but is real nonetheless?

Consider a Jewish student who hears classmates celebrate “resistance” immediately after the murder of Israeli civilians. Consider a Palestinian student who hears classmates defend the bombing of Gaza as necessary or dismiss thousands of dead children as human shields. Consider an Israeli student whose family members were killed or kidnapped. Consider a Muslim student who is treated as a terrorist sympathizer for wearing a keffiyeh. Consider a student who walks through an encampment and sees not a democratic assembly but a crowd in which she believes her grief has no place.

Not all of these experiences are legally actionable. Many should not be. But a university that responds only, “You are not objectively threatened, so endure it,” will not build a healthy democratic culture. It may protect liberty while allowing alienation to harden into hatred.

This is where both administrators and activists have failed. Administrators have offered bureaucracy: bias forms, disciplinary codes, police, task forces, statements, and compliance procedures. Activists have often offered certainty: our side is liberation, your side is oppression; our grief is political, yours is propaganda. Neither response helps people inhabit a shared institution amid moral catastrophe.

The alternative is not civility in the shallow sense. Calls for civility often function as demands that the aggrieved speak softly enough for the comfortable to ignore them. The Gaza protests were not wrong because they were angry. Anger is an appropriate response to mass death, occupation, terrorism, bombardment, dispossession, antisemitism, Islamophobia, and state violence. A university that cannot hear anger cannot hear the world.

But democratic hostility should still be answerable to democratic responsibility. Protesters have a right to use slogans others find offensive. They also have a responsibility to ask what their slogans communicate to those who hear them through histories of persecution. Jewish students who seek protection from antisemitism have a right to be taken seriously. They also have a responsibility not to equate Palestinian advocacy with hatred of Jews. Administrators have a responsibility to protect access and safety. They also have a responsibility not to transform political pressure into disciplinary panic.

A better campus response would have three layers.

The first layer is rights. Universities should adopt strong protest protections: access to commons, clear rules, no viewpoint discrimination, no police except in genuine emergencies, no severe discipline for nonviolent protest, and individualized due process. They should resist federal coercion, whether it comes in the language of antisemitism, anti-DEI politics, or national security.

The second layer is equality. Universities should investigate actual harassment and discrimination against Jews, Israelis, Palestinians, Arabs, Muslims, and others under the same standards. They should protect students from doxxing, threats, exclusion, and retaliation. They should be explicit that neither Zionist nor anti-Zionist identity, neither Palestinian nationalism nor Jewish peoplehood, places a student outside the community.

The third layer is encounter. This is the missing one. Universities should create spaces that are not disciplinary hearings or public relations events but sustained forums for difficult speech: facilitated dialogues, jointly taught courses, historical workshops, mourning spaces, and structured debates where students must listen as well as accuse. Participation should not be a precondition for protest rights; compelled dialogue is usually bad dialogue. But institutions should invest in the civic infrastructure that makes conflict more intelligible.

Some will dismiss this as naïve. Dialogue did not stop bombs from falling on Gaza. Dialogue did not free hostages. Dialogue did not prevent antisemitic attacks or anti-Palestinian racism. True. But universities cannot directly solve war. What they can do is form people capable of political judgment under conditions of anguish. That requires more than punishing misconduct and more than defending abstract liberty. It requires teaching students how to remain in the presence of those whose convictions they abhor.

Gourevitch worries that appeals to vulnerability empower authorities. He is right. But vulnerability is not only a tool of repression. It is also a fact of human life, especially in a campus community containing people with intimate ties to the violence being debated. The task is to acknowledge vulnerability without making it sovereign. Feelings should matter morally without ruling legally.

This distinction could change the tone of campus conflict. A student might say: “Your protest does not violate my rights, but it wounds me, and I want you to understand why.” Another might answer: “Your wound does not give you a veto, but I will listen, and I ask you to listen to mine.” That exchange will not always happen. Sometimes anger will overwhelm listening. Sometimes one side will exploit the language of pain. Sometimes the only appropriate response to speech is counter-speech. But universities should make such exchanges more possible, not less.

The crackdown has done the opposite. Police raids teach students that force settles conflict. Congressional hearings teach them that political theater matters more than truth. Donor pressure teaches them that wealth governs speech. Expulsions teach them that dissent is risky when it embarrasses the institution. Deportation threats teach noncitizens that their rights are conditional. These are anti-educational lessons.

A university worthy of the name would teach different lessons: that freedom includes the freedom to offend; that safety means protection from violence and exclusion, not insulation from anguish; that political opponents are not automatically enemies; that some speech can be lawful and still morally wrong; that the answer to bad protest is often better protest; and that shared life requires both courage and restraint.

Gourevitch gives us the courage. We should add the restraint—not the restraint of administrative censorship, but the restraint of people who understand that winning a political argument is not the same as building a livable world. The right to be hostile must be defended. So must the possibility that, after the chanting stops, members of a university can still face one another as human beings.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Subjective feelings of distress or unsafety should never be used as a legal or administrative basis to suppress political speech or protest.
  2. 02Universities must maintain a strict distinction between objective danger, which warrants punishment, and political hostility, which must be tolerated.
  3. 03The current crisis reflects a communal failure where students no longer trust in the shared humanity of their political opponents.
  4. 04Administrative reliance on bureaucracy, police, and disciplinary codes fails to help students inhabit a shared institution during times of moral catastrophe.
  5. 05A robust campus response requires three layers: the protection of civil liberties, the equal enforcement of anti-discrimination standards, and the creation of spaces for sustained, difficult encounter and dialogue.
  6. 06Acknowledging human vulnerability is morally necessary for a livable community, even if those feelings should not be granted sovereign power to veto the rights of others.
  7. 07The recent crackdowns have been anti-educational by teaching students that force, wealth, and political theater are the primary arbiters of conflict.
