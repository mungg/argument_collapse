"While the right to protest must be defended against government and administrative overreach, the university must also protect the right of all students to belong by distinguishing between protected political dissent and patterns of group-targeted intimidation that create a truly hostile environment."

Alex Gourevitch offers a bracing defense of protest, and much of it is necessary. The police raids were often excessive. The Trump administration’s threats against universities are menacing. The disappearance and attempted deportation of a lawful permanent resident protest leader should alarm anyone who cares about civil liberty. Universities have behaved with cowardice, opacity, and an eagerness to appease donors and politicians. On those points, Gourevitch is right.

But his essay also asks Jewish students, including many who experienced the last eighteen months as a profound rupture in their trust in campus life, to accept too thin an account of what happened. In his effort to defend protest against repression, he sometimes treats the hostile environment claims as little more than subjective discomfort elevated into censorship. That is too simple. Some of what Jewish students described was not merely the pain of hearing political views they rejected. It was the experience of being made to wonder whether their classmates saw them as presumptively complicit in murder unless they performed the right kind of disavowal.

That distinction matters.

A campus can and must tolerate criticism of Israel. It must tolerate anti-Zionism. It must tolerate arguments that the war in Gaza is genocidal, that U.S. military aid is immoral, that universities should divest, and that Zionism itself is a form of colonial domination. Those claims are political claims, and many Jews hold some of them. They cannot be banned as antisemitic simply because they disturb many other Jews.

But campus protest has not always stopped there. Some students have reported being told to leave spaces because they were “Zionists.” Some have seen posters of kidnapped Israelis ripped down with contempt. Some have heard chants that seemed not only to condemn a state but to celebrate the murder of civilians. Some have encountered slogans that, whatever their intended meaning, reasonably evoke the elimination of the one Jewish-majority state in the world. Some have watched student organizations issue statements immediately after October 7 that framed the slaughter of Israeli civilians as legitimate resistance or as an inevitable act for which Israel alone bore responsibility. Some have seen “globalize the intifada” treated as a harmless poetic slogan even though, to many Jews, it calls to mind buses, cafés, and families blown apart.

Gourevitch acknowledges that he heard antisemitic statements and grotesque celebrations of violence. But he then moves quickly to say that none amounted to imminent threats directed at specific individuals. That may be true as a First Amendment standard. It may be enough to resist police intervention. It is not enough to describe the moral problem facing universities.

The university is not only a state actor constrained by constitutional doctrine, nor only a public square in which citizens must endure one another. It is also a residential and educational institution with obligations to cultivate equal membership. Students eat, sleep, study, pray, socialize, and build friendships there. If a Jewish student hears classmates celebrate the killing of people who could have been her cousins, and then sees the same classmates insist that Zionists are unwelcome in progressive spaces, her concern is not merely that she has encountered “hostility to political views.” It is that an identity central to her life has been translated into a mark of moral contamination.

Gourevitch worries that such claims convert discomfort into actionable harm. Sometimes they do. But there is another danger: converting group-targeted intimidation into mere disagreement because it uses the vocabulary of politics.

The hardest cases arise because Jewish identity, Zionism, Israel, and U.S. foreign policy overlap without being identical. Administrators and activists alike often fail to respect those distinctions. Pro-Israel groups sometimes brand nearly all anti-Zionist speech antisemitic. Anti-Zionist activists sometimes pretend that Zionism is merely a policy preference, like a view on tax rates, rather than an identity-laden commitment for many Jews shaped by centuries of persecution and the memory of genocide. Each side accuses the other of bad faith, and sometimes both are right.

Gourevitch is correct that subjective fear cannot be the sole standard. Yet the alternative cannot be an exclusively criminal-law-like threshold of individualized imminent threat. Hostile environments are often created through patterns: repeated targeting, exclusionary social norms, epithets, vandalism, and public rituals of humiliation. A noose on a Black student’s door need not be accompanied by a named plan of assault to be threatening. A swastika on a Jewish student’s dorm room door need not include a written death threat to warrant serious action. Context makes symbols and words actionable.

The question, then, is whether campus anti-Israel protest as such created such an environment. The answer should be no. But particular conduct within or around protests sometimes may have contributed to one. Universities need the courage to say both things.

That courage has been lacking. Instead of careful distinctions, we have seen maximalist claims from all directions. Some politicians and donors want to define antisemitism so broadly that Palestinian advocacy becomes nearly impossible. Some activists want to define antisemitism so narrowly that only explicit Nazi ideology counts. Neither approach is adequate.

A better framework would begin with several principles.

First, political criticism of Israel, including anti-Zionism and support for divestment, must be protected. Students must be free to accuse states and institutions of grave crimes.

Second, protest may be disruptive, but access matters. No student should be prevented from entering a library, classroom, dormitory, dining hall, religious center, or campus event because of identity or presumed politics. “Zionists off campus” is not equivalent to “divest from Israel.” The former can easily become an exclusionary demand aimed at members of the university community.

Third, universities should distinguish slogans from targeted conduct but should not pretend slogans are meaningless. “From the river to the sea” may be used by some as a call for equal rights and by others as a call for elimination. “Globalize the intifada” may be defended as anti-occupation rhetoric and heard as a celebration of violence. The answer is usually not punishment. But universities should encourage, and sometimes require in official contexts, serious explanation and counterspeech. Ambiguity does not automatically justify discipline, but neither does it absolve speakers of responsibility for foreseeable meanings.

Fourth, discipline must be individualized and proportionate. Gourevitch is right that mass punishment is unjust. If a student threatens another student, punish that student. If a student organization excludes Jews, Israelis, Palestinians, Muslims, or Arabs from events on discriminatory grounds, investigate that organization. Do not send riot police into a peaceful encampment because administrators dislike its message or fear congressional hearings.

Fifth, the same solicitude shown to Jewish students must be shown to Palestinian, Arab, and Muslim students. This has not happened. Palestinian students have watched relatives killed, homes destroyed, and whole communities demonized, while many universities issued sterile statements or no statements at all. Muslim and Arab students have faced doxxing, surveillance, racist abuse, and suspicion. If Jewish vulnerability is real, so is theirs. A university that recognizes only one set of fears is not protecting civil rights; it is distributing institutional empathy according to political pressure.

The Trump administration’s intervention makes all of this worse. A government that threatens universities with massive funding cuts while demanding ideological restructuring is not a trustworthy guardian of Jewish safety. It is exploiting Jewish fear to discipline higher education. Jews should be especially wary of rulers who claim to protect us by expanding state power over dissent. History gives us little reason to trust such bargains.

Still, opposition to Trump’s coercion should not require minimizing antisemitism on campus. The most durable defense of protest will come from a movement that can say, without defensiveness: we oppose the war; we defend Palestinian life; we reject antisemitism; we will not harass Jewish students; we will not celebrate murdered civilians; we will not allow donors or politicians to equate our cause with bigotry; and we will hold our own accountable when they cross the line.

Gourevitch’s “right to be hostile” captures something important about democratic life. But hostility has objects, histories, and audiences. When hostility toward a state slides into hostility toward students associated with that state by ancestry, religion, nationality, or communal attachment, the university must respond—not always with punishment, and rarely with police, but with clarity.

The right to protest is real. So is the right to belong. The challenge is not to sacrifice the first to a therapeutic notion of safety, as many administrators have done. But neither should we define the second so narrowly that only broken bones or explicit death threats count. A university worthy of the name must be able to protect fierce political protest while refusing to let any group of students become the campus’s acceptable enemy.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The standard for administrative intervention should not be limited to imminent physical threats, as repeated targeting, exclusionary social norms, and rituals of humiliation can constitute actionable harassment.
  2. 02Hostility toward a state becomes a violation of campus belonging when it translates a student's central identity into a mark of moral contamination or results in their exclusion from campus spaces.
  3. 03Universities must apply their standards for safety and belonging equitably, providing the same solicitude to Palestinian, Arab, and Muslim students that is currently offered to Jewish students.
  4. 04Discipline for campus conduct should be individualized and proportionate rather than relying on mass punishment or the deployment of police against peaceful encampments.
  5. 05The Trump administration’s use of federal funding threats to restructure university speech codes is an exploitation of Jewish fear that ultimately undermines civil liberties for everyone.
