"While national idolatry is dangerous, education should focus on democratic citizenship within a specific polity rather than cosmopolitan world citizenship because political responsibility and democratic reform require a shared institutional identity."

Martha Nussbaum has written a powerful warning against idolatrous nationalism. No decent democrat should be indifferent to that warning. The twentieth century has supplied enough evidence that nations can become objects of worship, and that the worship of collective identity can license cruelty on a terrifying scale. Her invocation of Tagore is apt: to make the nation a god is to curse it.

But Nussbaum’s argument moves too quickly from a sound moral caution to an unsound educational conclusion. It does not follow from the danger of national idolatry that schools should teach children that they are “above all citizens of the world” rather than citizens of a particular democratic polity. The alternative to cosmopolitanism need not be chauvinism. There is such a thing as democratic patriotism, and it is not merely a concession to weakness. It is a necessary condition of political responsibility.

The central difficulty is this: citizenship is not only a moral identity. It is an institutional role. Americans vote in American elections, serve on American juries, pay American taxes, argue over American laws, and inherit responsibility for American public institutions. They may and should care about India, Nigeria, Norway, and Bolivia. They should learn much more about them than they presently do. But they cannot legislate for them in the way they legislate for themselves. They are accountable for the conduct of their own government in a distinctive way. To teach them that national citizenship is morally secondary may weaken precisely the habits by which democratic reform occurs.

Nussbaum recognizes that we may give special attention to our “own sphere,” but she treats this special attention as a practical concession justified from the universal point of view. That formulation is elegant, but it understates the moral significance of shared political membership. Fellow citizens are not simply nearby persons whom we happen to be well placed to help. They are co-authors of a coercive order. We make law together; we impose burdens on one another; we decide, collectively, how schools will be financed, whether prisons will be built, how taxes will be levied, what rights will be protected. These relations generate obligations that are not reducible to generic duties to humanity.

Consider the civil rights movement. Its power did not come only from an appeal to universal human dignity, though that appeal was indispensable. It also came from a claim on America—a claim that the nation had betrayed its own promises. Frederick Douglass, Abraham Lincoln, W. E. B. Du Bois, Martin Luther King Jr.: all in different ways used the language of American ideals against American practices. King’s “promissory note” was not a cosmopolitan metaphor. It was a specifically national indictment, and its force depended on the thought that citizens may demand of their country fidelity to its professed creed.

The same is true of labor movements, women’s suffrage, and struggles for immigrant rights. Reformers have often needed more than universal morality; they have needed a public language capable of moving fellow citizens who share institutions and history. “This is what justice requires” is a noble sentence. “This is what our Constitution and our deepest promises require of us” is sometimes politically indispensable.

Nussbaum worries that patriotism slides into jingoism. It can. But cosmopolitanism has its own temptations. One is evasion. The person who declares herself a citizen of the world may feel morally enlarged while avoiding the humbler duties of local politics: school board meetings, tax fights, neighborhood conflicts, party organizing, jury service. Another is abstraction. Humanity is easier to love at a distance than neighbors are up close. The world citizen may speak movingly of distant suffering while neglecting the obligations created by the institutions that protect her own freedom.

There is also a democratic concern. Cosmopolitan moral language is often most comfortable to the highly educated, mobile, and professionally secure. It comes easily to those who attend international conferences, read several literatures, and move among universities. But many citizens experience national belonging not as an idol but as one of the few forms of dignity available to them in a society increasingly stratified by education and wealth. To tell them that this attachment is morally suspect may confirm their suspicion that cosmopolitanism is a creed of elites who enjoy the benefits of national stability while disdaining the sentiments that sustain it.

This does not mean schools should teach complacent nationalism. Quite the contrary. A serious patriotic education must include slavery, conquest, exclusion, anti-immigrant violence, the treatment of Native peoples, the internment of Japanese Americans, McCarthyism, Vietnam, and the persistent gap between American ideals and American realities. It must teach that dissent can be patriotic, that loyalty to constitutional principles may require opposition to government policy, and that love of country is not obedience to the state.

But it should also teach gratitude. Gratitude is not worship. It is possible to be grateful for constitutional government, for the Bill of Rights, for traditions of voluntary association, for public libraries, for the expansion of suffrage, for the defeat of fascism, for the courage of reformers, without believing that Americans are morally superior beings. A country is not a god; neither is it merely an administrative unit. It is a historical project into which citizens are born or naturalized and for which they bear special responsibility.

Nussbaum asks why the values that bind citizens across race, class, gender, and religion should “lose steam” at the national boundary. They should not lose all force. But they may change form. The duty not to torture, murder, enslave, or starve human beings is universal. The duty to provide public schools, maintain courts, fund social insurance, and deliberate as equals is institutionally specific. A foreign child has the same human worth as an American child. But American citizens have special responsibility for American public schools because those schools are their collective creation and their political charge.

The best answer, then, is not patriotism instead of cosmopolitanism, but a patriotism disciplined by cosmopolitan morality. Students should learn that their country is one country among others, that its interests do not exhaust justice, and that foreigners are not lesser persons. They should learn world history, geography, languages, comparative religion, and global ecology. They should learn that American consumption and policy have consequences abroad. On these matters Nussbaum is entirely persuasive.

Where she is less persuasive is in opposing “world citizenship” to “democratic/national citizenship” as the central focus of education. The central focus should be democratic citizenship understood in a morally universal frame. Children should first learn how to take responsibility for the institutions through which they can act, while also learning that the moral standards by which they judge those institutions are not parochial. This is not Bande Mataram. It is not worship of the motherland. It is the cultivation of citizens capable of saying both “my country is mine to serve” and “my country is not the measure of all things.”

Nikhil’s sentence remains the right one: worship Right, not country. But one may serve Right by serving a country well. Indeed, for democratic citizens, there may be no other way to begin.

  4. 04A cosmopolitan identity can lead to political evasion and abstraction, allowing individuals to ignore the humble, local duties of self-governance.
  5. 05Framing national identity as morally suspect risks alienating non-elite citizens for whom national belonging is a primary source of dignity and stability.

"While Martha Nussbaum correctly warns against the dangers of idolatrous nationalism, her proposal to make world citizenship the central ideal of education is mistaken because the survival of a constitutional republic depends on the specific civic loyalty and habits of its citizens."

Martha Nussbaum’s essay is learned, humane, and, in its political recommendations, mistaken. She is right that nationalism can become a form of idolatry. She is wrong to suppose that patriotic education is therefore morally suspect or that American schools should make “world citizenship” their central ideal.

The first duty of American schools is to form American citizens. This is not because Americans are better than other people. It is because the United States is a constitutional republic whose survival depends on the knowledge, habits, and loyalty of its citizens. A republic does not run on abstractions. It requires people willing to vote, obey laws they dislike, serve on juries, pay taxes, defend constitutional liberties, and accept the results of elections. These habits are not generated by a generalized love of humanity. They are generated by attachment to a particular political inheritance.

Nussbaum repeatedly contrasts patriotism with “Right,” as though love of country must compete with moral principle. But the American patriotic tradition, at its best, is precisely a loyalty to principles: constitutional government, ordered liberty, equality before the law, freedom of religion and speech, representative institutions, and the dignity of the individual. These principles are not merely tribal emblems. They are universal in aspiration, but they have been given durable form in a particular nation. To love America rightly is not to worship soil or blood. It is to cherish a regime of liberty.

Of course students should learn about other countries. They should know geography, world history, foreign languages, and the global consequences of American policy. Ignorance of the world is a serious defect in American education. But Nussbaum’s proposal goes beyond correcting ignorance. She wants children to think of themselves “above all” as citizens of the world. That phrase has a noble ring, but it lacks political substance. There is no world constitution to which they owe civic allegiance, no world electorate in which they participate, no world court with democratic legitimacy comparable to our own institutions, no world legislature accountable to them. “World citizenship” is a metaphor. American citizenship is a fact.

The metaphor can even be dangerous. It may encourage students to think that loyalty to their own country is a childish prejudice to be overcome by the enlightened. That is a poor lesson for a democratic people, especially at a time when confidence in public institutions is already fragile. The United States is absorbing large numbers of immigrants from every part of the globe. If schools do not teach a common civic identity, what will bind these future citizens together? Ethnic pride? Consumer culture? Litigation? Television? A thin cosmopolitanism will not do the job.

The American experiment has always depended on transforming strangers into fellow citizens. That process requires more than respect for rights. It requires affection for a common story. Immigrants do not become Americans simply by residing within our borders. They become Americans by learning the language of our public life, the meaning of the Constitution, the sacrifices of those who preserved the Union, the struggles that expanded liberty, and the responsibilities of self-government. A curriculum that treats the nation as morally arbitrary may weaken the very framework that makes pluralism possible.

Nussbaum asks why our duties should stop at the border. They should not. We have duties of charity, justice, and prudence toward foreigners. We should not pollute their air, exploit their labor, or ignore famine and massacre. But duties differ. I have obligations to my family that I do not have to all families, though all persons possess equal human worth. I have obligations to my neighbors that differ from my obligations to people in distant cities. Likewise, citizens have special obligations to one another because they share a political compact. That compact is not morally arbitrary. It is the means by which liberty is secured.

The recent horrors in Rwanda and Bosnia remind us not that patriotism is obsolete but that decent patriotism is precious. When national or ethnic identity is severed from law, religion from tolerance, and grievance from restraint, barbarism follows. The lesson for Americans is not to dilute our civic loyalties but to deepen them—to teach why constitutionalism, federalism, religious liberty, and equal citizenship matter.

Nussbaum fears that patriotism is close to jingoism. It can be. But the cure for bad patriotism is good patriotism. Teach children to love their country honestly: its achievements without concealing its sins, its principles without pretending they have always been honored, its heroes without denying their flaws. Teach them that dissent may be patriotic and that America is judged by standards higher than power. But do not teach them that their primary allegiance is to an abstraction called humanity. Human beings live under laws, speak languages, inherit institutions, and defend freedoms in particular places.

“To worship my country as a god is to bring a curse upon it.” Yes. But to refuse one’s country a rightful love is to deprive it of the devotion it needs to remain free.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The primary duty of schools is to form citizens who are prepared to sustain the practical demands of a republic, such as voting and obeying laws, which require attachment to a particular political inheritance rather than a generalized love of humanity.
  2. 02American patriotism is not a rejection of universal moral principles but a loyalty to those very principles—such as liberty and equality—as they are uniquely realized and protected within a specific national framework.
  3. 03The concept of 'world citizenship' lacks political substance and democratic legitimacy because there is no global constitution, legislature, or electorate to which a student can actually owe civic allegiance.
  4. 04A common national identity is essential for social cohesion in a pluralistic society, especially for integrating immigrants into a shared story of self-government.
  5. 05Special obligations to fellow citizens are not morally arbitrary but are the necessary result of a shared political compact that secures liberty.
  6. 06The solution to the dangers of jingoism is not to abandon patriotism for cosmopolitanism, but to teach a 'good patriotism' that honestly acknowledges national flaws while remaining devoted to constitutional ideals.
