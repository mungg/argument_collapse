"While amnesty for irregular migrants is morally justified, it should be conditioned on demonstrated civic and cultural integration rather than being granted automatically based on the mere passage of time."

Joseph Carens’s essay is an eloquent plea to recognize that time erodes the state’s right to deport irregular migrants. I agree with much of his moral reasoning, but I part ways on a critical point: the definition of “social membership.” Carens treats it almost as a natural fact—if you live somewhere long enough, you become a member. This neglects the cultural and civic dimensions of membership that are essential to a functioning liberal democracy. I believe that amnesty should be granted, but it should not be automatic. It should be conditioned on demonstrated integration: language acquisition, civic knowledge, and commitment to the liberal values that make amnesty possible in the first place.

Carens’s own examples inadvertently support this distinction. Marguerite Grimmond had been raised in Scotland from early childhood; she presumably spoke English, attended local schools, and absorbed the norms and habits of her community without distinction from her neighbors. Hiu Lui Ng acquired an education in the U.S., worked, and married an American. These are individuals who, by any measure, had integrated—not merely resided. But Carens’s proposed rule would extend the same right to stay to anyone who has simply been present for five years, regardless of whether they have made any effort to join the society in a meaningful way. A migrant who spends five years in a linguistic and cultural enclave, with no attempt to learn English or engage with the broader community, has not become a “member” in any thick sense. The mere passage of time does not create a shared civic identity.

Why does this matter? Because legal status is not just about the migrant’s rights; it is also about the kind of society we wish to build. A liberal democracy requires a baseline of cultural cohesion and mutual understanding among its citizens. This is not ethnic nationalism, but civic nationalism—the idea that membership entails a willingness to participate in a common political project. When we grant amnesty, we are offering a path to full citizenship. That offer should be reciprocated by a willingness to learn the language of the country, to understand its laws and history, and to embrace the principles of democracy, tolerance, and individual rights. These are not onerous demands; they are precisely what immigrants have been expected to do throughout American history.

Carens dismisses the idea of a wider range of criteria as too prone to discriminatory application. But that is a problem of administration, not of principle. European countries, as Carens notes, have often attached integration requirements to regularization programs. France, for example, tied legalization to a “republican integration contract” obliging migrants to attend language classes and civic instruction. These programs are not without their flaws, but they embody an important idea: that residency implies a two-way street. The state grants rights, and the migrant undertakes responsibilities. Carens’s automatic model, by contrast, reduces membership to a passive accumulation of days, draining the concept of civic meaning.

One might object that integration requirements are a form of coercion, penalizing those who, because of poverty or isolation, cannot easily access language classes. This is a genuine concern, which is why any such requirements should be coupled with publicly funded education programs. If we are serious about integrating migrants, we should invest in adult education, community outreach, and support services. That would be a far more constructive use of state resources than the current enforcement-and-detention regime. And it would give migrants a real opportunity to meet the requirements, rather than setting them up for failure.

There is also the question of democratic legitimacy. A civic integration requirement can help build public support for amnesty by addressing the legitimate fear that mass legalization without assimilation will lead to social fragmentation. Polls consistently show that Americans are more favorable toward legalization if it is tied to learning English and other integration measures. Carens is right that some opposition to amnesty is rooted in nativism, but not all of it. A segment of the public is genuinely concerned about the erosion of a common public culture. A well-designed integration requirement speaks to that concern without giving in to anti-immigrant prejudice.

Carens’s moral logic remains powerful. He is correct that it is wrong to deport people who have built lives here. But he is incorrect to think that time alone, absent integration, constitutes membership. The liberal democratic state is not a hotel; it is a community of citizens bound by shared values. We should offer a generous amnesty to those who have demonstrated, through their actions, that they wish to join that community. And we should help them do so. An amnesty that rewards mere presence, without any expectation of cultural and civic integration, would be a disservice to both migrants and the society that receives them. Let us build an amnesty that is not only compassionate but also wise.
