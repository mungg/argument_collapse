"Jonathan Zittrain’s legal prescriptions for preserving the internet's openness are practically unenforceable and would likely stifle the very innovation and 'generativity' he aims to protect."

Jonathan Zittrain’s “Protecting the Internet Without Wrecking It” provides an excellent sociological and technical diagnosis of the malware crisis driving consumers toward closed platforms. However, when Zittrain transitions from diagnosis to legal prescription, his argument falters. In his attempt to use the law to artificially sustain the "generativity" of the internet, he proposes regulatory frameworks—specifically "appliance neutrality" and legally mandated "data portability"—that are legally tortured, practically unenforceable, and likely to stifle the very innovation he seeks to protect.

Zittrain’s concept of "appliance neutrality" is particularly problematic. He argues that if a company like Microsoft makes an Xbox and opens it even slightly to third-party developers, the law should prevent Microsoft from later restricting that access. He attempts to ground this in common law doctrines like "adverse possession" and "prescriptive easements," arguing that "certain consistent behaviors can give rise to obligations."

This is a dangerous misapplication of property law to software ecosystems. An easement applies to physical land where a right-of-way becomes essential. A digital platform is an actively maintained, constantly updating software environment. If the law dictates that allowing a third-party application on your platform creates a permanent legal obligation to support that application forever, the rational response from any hardware manufacturer is absolute lockdown from day one. Why would Apple or Microsoft ever experiment with a limited beta program for outside developers if changing their mind later invites an antitrust lawsuit or regulatory fine? Zittrain’s proposed rule would instantly kill the exact kind of tentative generativity he hopes to encourage.

Similarly, Zittrain proposes that the law should mandate "data portability policies" or even create a legal "right of data portability" to prevent users from being locked into tethered appliances or Web 2.0 services. On its face, allowing users to extract their personal photos or documents is appealing. But translating this into a legal mandate opens a regulatory nightmare.

What constitutes portable data in a complex, dynamic web application? If a user wants to port their Facebook profile, does that include the comments their friends made? If they want to port their Xbox Live gaming profile, how does the government mandate a standardized format that Nintendo or Sony can read? Data formats are deeply tied to the proprietary architecture of the services themselves. Forcing companies to design their databases according to government-mandated interoperability standards will slow development to a crawl. The market, not the law, is the better arbiter here. If a web service refuses to let users download their data, competitors will emerge offering export features as a competitive advantage.

Zittrain’s suggestions regarding copyright and patent law are slightly more grounded. Clarifying the statute of limitations for software patents so that it does not restart upon every single use of the code would indeed provide some necessary breathing room for open-source developers. But these are minor tweaks that do not address the central botnet crisis that forms the premise of his essay.

Ultimately, Zittrain is trying to use the blunt instrument of the law to enforce a specific architectural philosophy (generativity) that the consumer market is actively rejecting for valid security reasons. He admits that familiar legal tools like banning harmful code will not work because jurisdictional issues make it impossible to track sophisticated wrongdoers. Yet he fails to realize that enforcing "appliance neutrality" on tech companies will be just as futile and economically destructive.

We must accept that the legal system is ill-equipped to micromanage the architectural balance of computing platforms. If the open PC is to survive, it must do so by evolving better technical defenses, not by lobbying Congress to outlaw the closed appliances that consumers are currently flocking to.
