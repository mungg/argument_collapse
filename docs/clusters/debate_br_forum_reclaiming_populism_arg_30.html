Diversified Gemini

"To build a successful populist movement, the left must move beyond the cultural preoccupations of the professional-managerial class and return to a majoritarian, class-first strategy centered on universal material demands."

Adom Getachew’s reflection on the need to reclaim populism arrives at a bitter moment for the American left. With the effective end of the Bernie Sanders campaign in early April and the coalescing of the Democratic establishment around Joe Biden, the immediate hopes for a national left-populist project have been dashed. Yet, as Getachew notes, the underlying conditions that gave rise to this project—staggering inequality, a hollowed-out middle class, and now, the cataclysmic economic and health impacts of the COVID-19 pandemic—remain. If the left is to learn from the defeats of Sanders in the US and Jeremy Corbyn in the UK, it must engage in a ruthless autopsy of why left populism failed to consolidate a winning coalition. The hard truth is that the left’s attempt to construct a multiracial, working-class populism has been continually derailed by an overreliance on the professional-managerial class and a retreat into boutique cultural issues that alienate the very working-class base it seeks to mobilize.

The essence of a successful populist project is a clear, unyielding line drawn between the many and the few. Historically, left populism has drawn this line along class boundaries: the working masses versus the capitalist elite. The power of Sanders’s 2016 run lay precisely in his laser focus on universal, material demands—Medicare for All, a living wage, free public college—that resonated across racial and geographic divides. It was a message that understood class as the primary contradiction in American life, recognizing that while racism and sexism are profound evils, they are inextricably linked to and weaponized by a system of economic exploitation.

However, in the years between 2016 and 2020, the progressive movement allowed itself to be increasingly captured by the cultural preoccupations of its highly educated, urban, professional-class wing. Instead of doubling down on a majoritarian, class-based appeal, left-wing institutions, media, and even the Sanders campaign itself began to adopt the specialized language and hyper-fragmented identity politics of the academy and the nonprofit sector.

Getachew’s essay reflects this shift when she speaks of building a “multiracial working class alignment” but frames it alongside the need to combat "the persistence of white supremacy." While anti-racism is a non-negotiable principle for any left project, the political strategy of foregrounding racial divisions often serves to undermine the construction of a unified popular subject. When the left speaks to voters primarily as members of distinct racial, ethnic, or gender cohorts rather than as workers sharing common material struggles, it inadvertently reinforces the atomization of the working class. It allows the neoliberal center—exemplified by the Biden campaign—to co-opt the language of diversity and inclusion without conceding an inch on economic redistribution. We see this constantly: corporations and centrist politicians enthusiastically adopt anti-racist rhetoric and performative diversity, while ruthlessly crushing union drives and opposing universal healthcare.

A true left populism cannot be built on an additive model of identity politics, where a coalition is stitched together through targeted micro-appeals to different demographic groups. This was the fundamental error of the Warren campaign, which had "a plan for that" for every conceivable subgroup but lacked a unifying, visceral narrative of class conflict. Sanders largely avoided this trap, but his campaign was nevertheless weighed down by a broader progressive ecosystem that too often projected moral superiority and cultural disdain toward working-class voters who did not share their progressive cultural sensibilities.

The COVID-19 pandemic perfectly illustrates the absolute necessity of a class-first populism. The virus does not care about the culture wars. The primary divide exposed by the pandemic is a brutal class divide: between the "essential workers" (delivery drivers, grocery clerks, warehouse workers, nurses) who must risk their lives to earn a wage, and the professional class who can safely telecommute. The fact that a disproportionate number of these essential workers are Black and Latino is a vital element of this reality, but their exploitation is fundamentally rooted in their class position. The solution is not means-tested programs or race-specific policies, but massive, universal interventions: a federal jobs guarantee, universal basic income, universal healthcare divorced from employment, and robust protections for labor organizing.

To reclaim populism, the left must stop apologizing for focusing on class. It must aggressively court the millions of working-class voters—of all races—who have checked out of the political system entirely because neither party offers them material relief. It must also recognize that a working-class majority will not look or sound like a graduate school seminar. It will be culturally diverse, yes, but it will also hold contradictory, sometimes conservative, social views. A populist movement must be willing to meet these workers where they are, uniting them around their shared economic interests rather than imposing a purity test on every social issue.

The defeats of Morales, Corbyn, and Sanders are heavy blows. But the objective conditions for a left-populist resurgence are stronger than ever as unemployment skyrockets to Great Depression levels. If the left can shed the baggage of professional-class moralism, reject the neoliberal trap of fragmented identity politics, and forge an uncompromising, majoritarian movement based on material working-class demands, the populist moment can still be won.
