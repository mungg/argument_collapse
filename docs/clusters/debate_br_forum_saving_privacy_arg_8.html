"Reed Hundt’s proposal for a digital bill of rights is too modest because it focuses on government surveillance and due process while failing to address the underlying commercial infrastructure built on the extraction and monetization of personal life."

Reed Hundt’s “Saving Privacy” rightly identifies an alliance between big government and big information. But his remedy remains too modest because it treats the crisis chiefly as a problem of surveillance and due process. The deeper problem is that the dominant institutions of the digital economy are built to extract, store, analyze, and monetize personal life. Government surveillance is alarming, but it feeds on a commercial infrastructure that was already normalized before the NSA appeared in the headlines.

The bargain of Internet 2.0 was never as innocent as “free services in exchange for ads.” Americans did not knowingly negotiate the terms by which their friendships, searches, movements, purchases, photographs, reading habits, and political affinities would become raw material for behavioral prediction. The bargain was imposed through design, network effects, unreadable terms of service, and the absence of meaningful alternatives. Google, Facebook, Amazon, Yahoo, mobile carriers, data brokers, app developers, and advertising networks have not merely collected information; they have reorganized social life so that participation increasingly requires exposure.

That is why Hundt’s language of “customer is king” is inadequate. We are not kings in our relationship to dominant platforms. We are subjects, workers, consumers, audiences, and products all at once. A Facebook user cannot bargain over surveillance terms in any ordinary sense. A smartphone owner cannot realistically inspect the data practices of every application. A parent cannot withdraw a child from the data economy without withdrawing from ordinary modern life. Markets do not discipline these practices because the harms are diffuse, delayed, hidden, and collective.

Snowden’s revelations shocked the public because they showed the government exploiting this architecture. But one lesson should be that data retained for one purpose will eventually be used for another. Commercial databases become intelligence resources. Location histories become law enforcement tools. Social graphs become maps of association. Search records become windows into conscience. The distinction between corporate and state surveillance matters, but it is porous. The state can compel, purchase, request, or quietly receive what companies collect. The most privacy-protective database is the one that does not exist.

For that reason, a digital bill of rights should begin not with encryption, but with data minimization. Companies should collect only what is necessary to provide the service requested, retain it only as long as necessary, and use it only for purposes reasonably understood by the user. Sensitive categories—location, health information, children’s data, political and religious association—should receive heightened protection. Secondary use should require more than a ceremonial click. In many contexts it should be prohibited outright.

We also need to confront the advertising model. Surveillance advertising has become so familiar that it seems like the natural economic foundation of the Web. It is not. Contextual advertising, subscriptions, cooperatives, public funding, and paid services are all possible, though each has limits. The question is not whether advertising may exist, but whether the entire digital public sphere should be financed by watching people as closely as possible. If a business model requires pervasive monitoring, perhaps the business model—not privacy—should yield.

Hundt is right to praise encryption and to warn against government backdoors. Strong encryption is essential. Tor and related tools can protect dissidents, journalists, whistleblowers, abuse victims, and ordinary citizens who do not want every act of reading and association logged. But privacy tools cannot be the main solution because they shift the burden onto individuals. The people most vulnerable to surveillance are often the least able to master complex tools: the poor, immigrants, children, the elderly, political minorities, workers subject to employer monitoring. A society that tells everyone to install better software has already conceded too much.

Nor should Bitcoin be romanticized as a template for democratic renewal. The block chain is ingenious, and decentralized systems may prove useful in many settings. But Bitcoin’s early association with speculation, theft, volatility, and the Silk Road is not an accident wholly external to the design. Pseudonymous, irreversible transactions solve some problems while creating others. They may empower individuals; they may also empower fraudsters, tax evaders, and those with the technical sophistication to exploit less sophisticated users. “Trustless” systems do not eliminate trust. They relocate it—to code, exchanges, developers, miners, and security practices that most people cannot evaluate.

The proper role of law is therefore larger than Hundt suggests. We need privacy rights against the state, but also against firms. We need warrants for government access, but also limits on what companies may gather before the warrant is ever requested. We need transparency reports, but also enforceable duties. We need penalties not only when data are stolen, but when they are hoarded recklessly. We need rights of access, correction, deletion, and portability, so that individuals can leave platforms without losing their social existence. We need anti-discrimination rules for algorithmic decisions that affect credit, employment, housing, insurance, and education. Due process must apply not only when the state accuses, but when opaque systems classify.

Antitrust should be part of privacy policy as well. The concentration of digital life in a handful of firms makes surveillance more profitable and resistance more difficult. When a platform becomes the place where one’s friends, customers, colleagues, or readers already are, “choice” is compromised. Breaking up or regulating dominant intermediaries may do more for privacy than another round of consent notices. Interoperability requirements could allow people to communicate across services without surrendering to one company’s terms. Public or nonprofit digital infrastructure could offer alternatives to extractive platforms.

Finally, workers deserve attention. Employers increasingly monitor email, keystrokes, location, productivity, and social media. The workplace is where many people experience surveillance most directly and with the least freedom to refuse. A digital bill of rights that ignores labor will protect mainly the already powerful. Privacy should include the right not to be continuously measured as a condition of earning a living.

Hundt asks who rules: individuals, companies, or the state. But “individual sovereignty” can become a comforting fiction if the structures surrounding individuals remain unchanged. Real privacy requires collective limits on both corporate extraction and government access. It requires saying that some information should not be collected, some inferences should not be drawn, and some markets should not exist. Encryption can help citizens hide. Law should also reduce the need to hide.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The distinction between corporate and state surveillance is porous, as data collected for commercial purposes inevitably becomes a resource for government intelligence and law enforcement.
  2. 02Individual sovereignty and market choice are illusions in a digital economy where dominant platforms impose surveillance through design, network effects, and unreadable terms of service.
  3. 03A digital bill of rights must prioritize data minimization—collecting only what is necessary for a service—rather than just focusing on encryption and transparency.
  4. 04The advertising-driven business model that finances the digital public sphere through pervasive monitoring must be challenged and replaced with alternative economic foundations.
  5. 05Relying on privacy tools like encryption and Tor is insufficient because it unfairly shifts the burden of protection onto individuals, particularly the most vulnerable populations.
  6. 06Privacy policy must incorporate antitrust measures and interoperability requirements to break the concentration of power that makes surveillance profitable and resistance difficult.
  7. 07Meaningful privacy requires collective legal limits on both corporate data extraction and algorithmic classification, including protections for workers against employer monitoring.
