Diversified Gemini

"The influence of money in politics is a rational response to the excessive power of the modern administrative state, meaning the only way to reduce that influence is to shrink the size and scope of government rather than regulating campaign finance."

Lawrence Lessig’s *Democracy After Citizens United* offers a compelling narrative: a noble Congress, filled with “insanely hard-working, decent souls,” has been tragically hijacked by an insidious “economy of influence.” According to Lessig, this “dependency corruption” forces politicians to betray the public interest in favor of wealthy donors. But while Lessig is busy inventing new categories of corruption and pining for a bygone era of pure representative dependency, he entirely misses the fundamental reason why so much money flows into Washington in the first place. The problem is not that money is corrupting our politicians; the problem is that our politicians have so much power over the economy that spending money to influence them is the only rational behavior for anyone with capital at stake.

If we want to understand the flood of campaign cash, we must look not at campaign finance laws, but at the size, scope, and arbitrary power of the modern administrative state. Lessig casually mentions two examples of alleged corruption: “when ‘free-market’ Republicans vote to support milk subsidies or sugar tariffs, or when ‘pro-consumer’ Democrats vote to exempt used-car dealers from consumer financial-protection legislation.” He uses these as proof that the system is broken by money. But a more rigorous economic analysis, rooted in public choice theory, asks a different question: why does the federal government possess the power to prop up the price of milk, set sugar tariffs, or micromanage the financing of a used Honda Civic?

When a government assumes the power to pick winners and losers in the marketplace, to hand out subsidies, to erect protectionist tariffs, and to impose crushing regulatory burdens on competitors, it creates immense economic value—or economic peril—with the stroke of a pen. In economics, the pursuit of these government-granted privileges is known as "rent-seeking." If an industry stands to lose a billion dollars because of a proposed regulatory change, or stands to gain a billion dollars from a targeted tax loophole, spending ten million dollars on campaign contributions and independent expenditures is not a sign of democratic failure; it is a highly rational, defensive investment.

Lessig argues that we need to “design institutions that avoid these conflicting dependencies.” But he is looking at the wrong institution. He wants to redesign the system of campaign funding, ignoring the fact that as long as the prize—the coercive power of the state—remains vast and lucrative, money will always find a way to influence the outcome. If you dam a river, the water doesn't disappear; it simply finds another path. We have spent the last forty years passing increasingly complex campaign finance regulations, from the post-Watergate reforms to McCain-Feingold. What has been the result? The money hasn’t vanished; it has simply migrated from direct contributions to political parties, to 527 organizations, and now, post-*Citizens United*, to independent expenditures.

Lessig is horrified by *Citizens United* because it removes a barrier to this flow of money. He warns of the "iceberg theory," where the mere threat of a million-dollar ad buy by Exxon or Google will force politicians to alter their votes. But consider why Exxon or Google feel compelled to make those threats. Exxon is operating in an environment where Congress is constantly threatening to impose carbon taxes, cap-and-trade systems, or massive regulatory penalties that could wipe out their business model. Google is facing the looming specter of antitrust actions, net neutrality regulations, and privacy mandates that could fundamentally alter the architecture of the internet. When the government has a gun pointed at your head, you do not sit quietly; you spend whatever it takes to ensure the person holding the gun is friendly to your interests.

Lessig writes that we must prevent "the deviation of an institution from its proper or intended focus." But what is the intended focus of Congress today? It is certainly not the limited, enumerated powers envisioned by the Framers Lessig so fondly cites. The modern Congress operates as a colossal clearinghouse for the redistribution of wealth, presiding over a multi-trillion-dollar budget and delegating immense authority to an alphabet soup of unaccountable regulatory agencies. It is this concentration of power that breeds what Lessig calls institutional corruption.

The solution to the problem Lessig identifies is not to establish a system of "democracy vouchers" or small-dollar matching funds. Such schemes merely force taxpayers to subsidize the political campaigns of people they may vehemently disagree with, without doing anything to reduce the underlying incentive for rent-seeking. As long as the government continues to subsidize milk and regulate used-car loans, dairy farmers and car dealers will find ways to coordinate their political influence, regardless of how campaigns are financed.

If we genuinely wish to drive money out of politics, we must take the politics out of money. We must pursue a radical reduction in the size and scope of the federal government. If Congress had no power to grant sugar tariffs, the sugar lobby would cease to exist. If the tax code were a flat rate devoid of loopholes, the army of tax lobbyists occupying K Street would vanish overnight. If the regulatory state were restrained, corporations would return their focus to innovating and serving consumers, rather than investing in political risk management.

*Citizens United* was not the creation of a new, corrupted political reality; it was the Supreme Court simply recognizing the reality that the First Amendment does not allow the government to criminalize political speech, even when that speech is loud and well-funded. Lessig’s crusade against the decision is a distraction. He is attempting to treat the symptom while actively ignoring the disease. Until we are willing to confront the bloated, omnipotent nature of the state, complaints about the influence of money in politics will remain nothing more than the naive lamentations of those who fail to understand human nature and basic economics. The only way to starve the economy of influence is to shrink the economy of government.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'dependency corruption' identified by Lessig is actually the economic phenomenon of rent-seeking, where individuals and corporations must spend money to protect themselves from or benefit from the government's vast regulatory and distributive powers.
  2. 02Campaign finance regulations are ineffective because as long as the state holds immense coercive power, political spending will simply migrate to different, less regulated channels.
  3. 03Lessig’s focus on the 'economy of influence' ignores the fact that the modern Congress has strayed from its limited, enumerated powers to become a clearinghouse for wealth redistribution, which naturally attracts special interest spending.
  4. 04Proposed solutions like democracy vouchers or small-dollar matching funds fail to address the underlying incentives for rent-seeking and unfairly force taxpayers to subsidize political speech they may oppose.
  5. 05The Citizens United decision correctly upholds First Amendment protections for political speech in a reality where government actions pose significant economic risks to private entities.
