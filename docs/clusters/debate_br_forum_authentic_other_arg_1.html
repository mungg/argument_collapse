"The Yasusada hoax does not expose the bankruptcy of multiculturalism, but rather reveals a 'pseudo-multiculturalism' where American literary institutions lazily prioritize shallow, exoticized projections of identity over genuine engagement with actual cultural and historical differences."

Marjorie Perloff’s essay is brilliant, but its brilliance has a target too broad for its evidence. The Yasusada hoax does expose American fantasies about Japan, editorial carelessness, and the avant-garde’s appetite for exotic authorization. What it does not expose, at least not in the way Perloff suggests, is the bankruptcy of multiculturalism. Indeed, the affair may show something almost opposite: not that multicultural attention has gone too far, but that it has remained too shallow, too selective, and too easily governed by old aesthetic hierarchies.

Perloff argues that “excellence” has been subordinated to agency and positionality, creating a market for poets who occupy compelling victim identities. There is truth here. Literary institutions sometimes congratulate themselves for publishing the oppressed while reading the work lazily, as evidence rather than art. But the Yasusada case does not fit that pattern neatly. The journals that published him were not simply organs of multicultural representation. They were often experimental, coterie, avant-garde venues invested in formal innovation. Yasusada appealed because he seemed to join Hiroshima witness to Spicerian and Language-poetry concerns. He was not merely the authentic other; he was the other as avant-garde ancestor.

That distinction matters. If multiculturalism means the serious study and publication of literatures outside the old Anglo-American canon, then the failure here is not multiculturalism but pseudo-multiculturalism. A genuinely multicultural editorial practice would have involved Japanese-language competence, knowledge of modern Japanese literary history, familiarity with living Japanese poets, and skepticism toward American projections. It would have been less likely, not more likely, to accept Yasusada at face value.

Perloff herself supplies the evidence. American Poetry Review published Yasusada but little contemporary Japanese poetry. It published medieval court poets, a Zen master, and a Hiroshima portfolio, but not the irreverent, urban, sexually frank postwar poets whose work complicates American ideas of Japan. That is not the result of too much attention to cultural difference. It is the result of too little attention to actual difference. Japan was admitted only under recognizable signs: antiquity, spirituality, suffering.

This is why blaming “positionality” can mislead. The problem was not that editors asked, “Who speaks?” The problem was that they answered the question in advance. They wanted a Hiroshima survivor who sounded like their idea of a Hiroshima survivor, though updated for postmodern taste. They did not ask enough about the institutions, language, literary networks, and historical contradictions that would make such a writer real. Positionality became a label rather than an inquiry.

The best multicultural criticism has never been satisfied with labels. It asks how race, nation, empire, gender, class, and language shape production and reception. It would not treat “Japanese” as a single essence. It would distinguish Kyoto from Hiroshima, prewar from postwar, tanka from free verse, state militarism from civilian victimhood, Zen inheritance from Western consumption of Zen, translation from invention. Perloff wants precisely this complexity. But then she risks assigning the simplification to multiculturalism rather than to the institutions that practice it badly.

There is also a danger in opposing “excellence” to identity too starkly. The canon has always been built through identity, though often under unmarked names: male, white, European, educated, imperial, metropolitan. When new writers insist on the significance of social location, they are not introducing impurity into an otherwise disinterested field. They are revealing conditions that were already there. The desire to read hibakusha poets is not ignoble. The desire to read colonized, exiled, enslaved, or silenced writers is not a fall from aesthetic seriousness. It becomes problematic only when readers treat suffering as a substitute for form, or form as a way to consume suffering without understanding it.

Yasusada is instructive because he satisfies both temptations. Those who wanted witness could find Hiroshima. Those who wanted formal sophistication could find disjunction, renga, new sentence, pseudo-translation. Those who wanted theory could find the death of the author. Those who wanted lyric pathos could find the dead daughter’s urn. The fake was successful because it was overdetermined. It gave every constituency something.

What would a better response look like? First, defend the expansion of literary attention while criticizing tokenism. The answer to Yasusada is not a return to a supposedly universal poetry judged without regard to history. That universality has too often meant provincial American taste. The answer is more history, more language study, more translation, more collaboration with scholars and poets from the cultures being represented.

Second, resist the transformation of trauma into credential. A poem by a survivor may be powerful, weak, conventional, radical, sentimental, opaque, or dull. To read it only as testimony is to reduce it. But to ignore the testimonial claim is also false. Ethical reading must hold both art and history in view. That is difficult, which is why shortcuts like Yasusada are tempting.

Third, publish contemporary Japanese poetry in its range. Perloff’s examples from Shiraishi Kazuko, Ito Hiromi, and Tanikawa Shuntaro are valuable because they break the spell of the expected. They show Japanese poets not as curators of timeless delicacy but as modern writers contending with sex, humor, domesticity, mass culture, metaphysical play, and ordinary irritation. Such work does more to challenge American provincialism than another invented Zen-inflected elegist.

Finally, recognize that the avant-garde has its own exoticism. It likes the foreign when the foreign can be made to prefigure its theories. The Yasusada archive’s linking of renga to Silliman’s new sentence is a perfect example. A tradition is honored only insofar as it becomes an ancestor of us. That is not multiculturalism; it is narcissism with footnotes.

Perloff has given us a necessary critique. But we should be careful where we aim it. The scandal is not that American poetry has become too concerned with the other. The scandal is that it has so often preferred an other of its own making to the more demanding, less flattering others who are actually writing.

"The Araki Yasusada hoax succeeded because it exploited a selective American desire for a 'coherent aesthetic essence' of Japan, highlighting the need for a more complex, de-exoticized engagement with actual contemporary Japanese poetry."

The most useful result of the Yasusada controversy would be a moratorium on the phrase “the Japanese sensibility.” If the affair teaches anything, it is that American readers are still too ready to imagine Japan as a coherent aesthetic essence: spare, elliptical, ceremonious, melancholy, erotic but restrained, spiritual but not doctrinal, wounded but dignified. Araki Yasusada could pass through important magazines because he wore that costume beautifully.

Marjorie Perloff’s essay performs a valuable act of de-exoticizing. Her discussion of contemporary Japanese poets is not an ornament to the argument; it is the argument’s ethical center. Once one has read the ribald energy of Shiraishi Kazuko, the bodily comedy of Ito Hiromi, or the wry self-consciousness of Tanikawa Shuntaro, the fabricated Yasusada looks less like Japan and more like an American dream of Japan. That does not make the poems worthless. It makes their reception embarrassing.

American literary culture has long had a selective ear for Japanese poetry. Haiku entered English as a discipline of perception, a little machine for producing precision and humility. Zen entered as antidote to Western excess. Court poetry entered as refinement. Hiroshima writing entered as witness. These are all real parts of Japanese literary and religious history, but they become distortions when isolated from the rest. Japan also has proletarian writing, surrealism, feminist experimentation, postwar colloquialism, comic vulgarity, mass media, political ambivalence, and literary careerism. It has everything any modern literary culture has.

The Yasusada hoax exploited the gap between actual plurality and imported essence. The invented poet is conveniently overcomplete. He is haiku and renga, Zen and avant-garde, Hiroshima and Spicer, Barthes and Celan, documentary notebook and postmodern fragmentation. He is enough like tradition to seem rooted and enough like us to seem exciting. Real poets are rarely so serviceable.

One reason American readers accept such figures is that translation often arrives without context. A poem appears with a brief biographical note, perhaps a translator’s preface, and then must stand as representative of a whole culture. The burden is impossible. A mediocre American poem is only a mediocre American poem; a translated Japanese poem may be read as evidence of Japan. This asymmetry encourages both reverence and laziness. The reader does not ask whether the poem is conventional, eccentric, archaizing, fashionable, regional, polemical, parodic, or badly translated. It is simply “Japanese.”

The remedy is not to abandon translation but to thicken it. We need clusters, debates, rival translations, essays, interviews, historical notes, and disagreement among translators. We need to see Japanese poets arguing with one another, not merely arriving as emissaries of an ancient civilization. We need translations of poems that do not fit American expectations: poems about department stores, office work, menstruation, traffic, television, American military bases, aging parents, school exams, bad sex, jazz, bureaucracy, loneliness in apartment blocks. We need poems that make us uncertain what “Japanese” is supposed to mean.

Perloff’s account of postwar reluctance to write about Hiroshima is especially important. American readers may assume that Hiroshima must dominate Japanese poetry, just as outsiders sometimes expect every Jewish writer to write about the Holocaust or every African-American writer to write about racism. But historical trauma does not operate by simple command. It may produce testimony, silence, displacement, exhaustion, anger, kitsch, piety, denial, or generational distance. Younger Japanese writers inherit both victimhood and the memory of Japanese aggression in Asia. That double inheritance complicates any easy poetics of witness.

Yasusada removes the complication. He mourns personally. He does not force the American reader to think hard about Pearl Harbor, Nanking, forced labor, firebombing, invasion plans, surrender terms, the emperor system, or the American desire to end the war. Nor does he force the Japanese reader—if there is a Japanese reader for this English-language artifact—to confront the entanglement of victim and aggressor. He occupies the morally clean position of bereaved civilian. Such civilians existed, of course, in vast numbers. But a literature that offers only that position becomes sentimental.

At the same time, I hesitate to condemn the author of Yasusada as simply exploitative. The work may be read as a trap set for our assumptions, and a successful trap at that. The anachronisms are so blatant that they almost beg to be found. Perhaps the archive is a parody of authenticity, an imitation so excessive that its exposure is built in. If so, the embarrassment belongs partly to us. We wanted the fake to be true.

But parody does not complete the task. Having been exposed, the fake must not become yet another substitute for the real. There is a danger that American critics will now write endlessly about Yasusada—about hoaxing, authorship, trauma, Orientalism—while still not translating or reading contemporary Japanese poetry. That would be the final irony: the invented Japanese poet continuing to monopolize the attention denied to actual Japanese poets.

Editors can change this. Invite translators who work deeply in modern and contemporary Japanese. Ask them not for “a Zen issue” or “a Hiroshima issue,” but for a living map of current work. Publish disagreements about translation choices. Include poets whose work is abrasive, comic, obscene, abstract, political, sentimental, anti-poetic. Let Japan appear not as a mood but as a field.

Readers can change too. We should mistrust our pleasure when a foreign poem confirms too perfectly what we hoped to find. We should ask what is being left out. We should learn enough history to be uneasy and enough humility to know when we need help. Above all, we should stop praising translated work for its “authenticity” as if authenticity were a fragrance released from distant suffering.

Araki Yasusada does not show us Japan. He shows us a mirror angled toward our desire for Japan. The next step is to turn from the mirror to the library, the archive, the living poet, the difficult conversation.

"The Araki Yasusada project is a morally problematic act of false witness that exploits the historical gravity of Hiroshima survivors to validate American avant-garde tastes."

I cannot share Marjorie Perloff’s admiration for the Yasusada project, though I accept much of her diagnosis of its reception. Yes, the poems exposed American fantasies about Japan. Yes, editors should have noticed the anachronisms. Yes, pseudonyms and invented personae have a long literary history. But the fact remains: someone fabricated a Hiroshima survivor, gave him a dead wife and dead daughter, placed words in the mouth of that invented bereavement, and then allowed journals to present the work as translated testimony from a historical victim of nuclear war. That is not merely clever. It is a trespass.

The issue is not whether a writer may imagine suffering outside his own experience. Of course writers do that. Nor is the issue whether Hiroshima can be treated through surrealism, irony, fragmentation, or parody. It can be, and Japanese writers themselves have used many modes to approach the unbearable. The issue is false witness. The Yasusada texts did not appear as a novel, dramatic monologue, or acknowledged heteronym. They appeared with biographical notes, translators’ apparatus, dates, letters, notebooks, and claims of discovery. Their authority depended on the reader’s belief that behind the words stood a man who had endured the bombing and its aftermath.

Perloff emphasizes that the hoax contained clues. But clues do not absolve deception. A forged painting may contain deliberate oddities; a false memoir may include contradictions; a con man may wink while lying. The presence of clues might make the work more intellectually interesting, but it does not erase the initial act of appropriation. Indeed, the clues can intensify the insult: the author plays games with facts while borrowing the gravity of actual mass death.

Hiroshima is not an abstraction. The bomb burned children, vaporized families, left survivors with keloids, cancers, miscarriages, blindness, memory, stigma, and political burden. For decades hibakusha have struggled to speak in a world often eager either to sentimentalize them or to turn away. Their testimony has had to contend with American justifications of the bombing, Japanese evasions about imperial aggression, and the numbing effect of nuclear statistics. To invent a survivor at this late date is to enter a crowded moral field and pretend to be one of those whose right to speak was purchased by catastrophe.

One might answer that Yasusada does not silence real survivors; he may even lead readers to them. Perhaps. But attention is not infinitely expandable. When journals publish a fake Hiroshima poet, they are not publishing an actual Japanese poet, an actual survivor, or an actual translator. Perloff notes the neglect of contemporary Japanese poetry in American magazines. That neglect is part of the injury. The fake other enters because the real others have not been sought with sufficient seriousness. The invented victim is more convenient: he writes in a style already tuned to American experimental taste.

This convenience matters. Yasusada is not only Japanese; he is a Japanese poet who somehow validates American avant-garde preoccupations. He reads Spicer, anticipates the new sentence, inhabits renga as proto-Language writing, and mourns Hiroshima in fragments palatable to readers suspicious of direct statement. Real foreignness often resists us. It requires translation, context, patience, error, humility. Yasusada requires none of this. He arrives already translated into our arguments.

Here Perloff’s critique of Orientalism is indispensable. But I think she underestimates the ethical implications of that critique. If Yasusada is a “perfect recipe for a new Orientalism,” then the author is not merely exposing Orientalism; he is practicing it. Satire that successfully reproduces the thing it satirizes may still do harm, especially when the objects used are not imaginary stereotypes alone but the remembered dead. The claim that the hoax teaches us a lesson is too easy. Many injuries teach lessons to bystanders.

The analogy to Macpherson’s Ossian or Rexroth’s Marichiko is incomplete. Literary history contains many impostures, but we do not have to judge them all alike. Macpherson’s ancient bard was remote, generalized, and bound up with emerging nationalist romanticism. Rexroth’s Marichiko is troubling in its own way, but erotic lyric under a Japanese female mask does not carry the same historical charge as impersonating a witness to atomic destruction. Context changes the moral weight of a mask.

Nor should the small scale of poetry publishing excuse the act. It is true that no one grew rich here. But literary trust is not measured only in money. Editors asked readers to believe that they were encountering translations from a dead Japanese poet’s notebooks. Readers extended that trust. More importantly, the historical category of survivor testimony was exploited. If the payments were small, the symbolic capital was large.

What should follow? Not censorship, certainly not prosecution. The poems may now be discussed, anthologized perhaps as a literary hoax, taught as an example of postmodern impersonation and American Orientalism. But they should not be allowed to retain the glamour of courageous transgression without the burden of moral judgment. We can say, simultaneously, that the project is ingenious and that it is wrong.

The better task is the one Perloff gestures toward at the end: read what is already there. Read Toge Sankichi, Hara Tamiki, Ota Yoko. Read hibakusha memoirs. Read contemporary Japanese poets whose work does not conform to our expectations. Read about Japanese militarism as well as Japanese victimhood. Read American arguments about the bomb without converting them into rituals of guilt or exoneration. Read in ways that make invention less tempting because reality is finally being attended to.

Araki Yasusada was created because American literary culture wanted him. That is an indictment of the culture. But wanting him did not make it permissible to invent him. The dead of Hiroshima do not need our ventriloquism. They need our attention, our accuracy, and our restraint.

"While the Yasusada hoax successfully exposed the American literary establishment's problematic desire for 'authentic' victims, it remains an ethically troubling act of counterfeit witness rather than a harmless literary mask."

Marjorie Perloff is right to insist that the Yasusada affair is more interesting than a simple scandal of gullible editors and one mischievous poet. The manuscripts were not merely “believed”; they were desired. They arrived bearing precisely the credentials most likely to disarm skeptical reading in the present climate: avant-garde difficulty, documentary apparatus, cross-cultural authority, and the aura of historical suffering. The invented poet was not just Japanese, not just a Hiroshima survivor, not just an undiscovered modernist, not just a precursor of Language poetry, not just a Buddhist-inflected anti-authorial figure. He was all of these at once. That combination should have made him suspect. Instead, it made him irresistible.

But Perloff’s essay, for all its brilliance, is too quick to assimilate the Yasusada materials to a venerable history of literary imposture. Ossian, Propertius, Marichiko, heteronyms, masks, invented translators: literature has always thrived on such devices. Yet there remains a difference between inventing a poetic speaker and forging the testimony of a historical wound. The issue is not that Hiroshima is too sacred for artifice. No event is beyond the reach of art, parody, indirection, or even grotesque comedy. The issue is that the Yasusada apparatus asks readers to grant moral and evidentiary authority to a nonexistent victim. It does not merely say, “Imagine such a person.” It says, “Here are his notebooks; here are his translators; here is his family history; here is the ash of the dead child.” That is not simply pseudonymity. It is counterfeit witness.

Perloff is surely correct that the texts themselves contain many clues: the impossible dates for Hiroshima University, the premature Barthes and Celan, the absurd Spicer chronology, the odd Japanese names, the suspiciously convenient Howard McCord. These are not minor slips. They are flashing signals. Yet to say that the author “wanted his readers to find something perplexing” does not absolve the deception. A forged banknote may contain a private joke in the serial number; it is still designed to circulate as currency. The Yasusada poems circulated in journals not as fiction, not as conceptual art, not as an openly unstable archive, but as translated work by a dead Japanese poet. Their reception depended on that claim. Had the same poems arrived under the name Kent Johnson, many editors might have found them clever, even moving, but they would not have carried the same charge.

This is why the question of quality cannot be separated from the question of attribution. Some defenders of the project seem tempted to say: if the poems moved us before exposure, why should they move us less after? The answer is that poems are not autonomous verbal machines, however much certain avant-garde theories may wish them to be. Context is not an ornament added after the fact; it is part of how meaning is made. “Telescope with Urn” is a different poem if it is read as the elliptical mourning of a Hiroshima father, as opposed to an American poet’s construction of such mourning. “Mad Daughter and Big-Bang” is a different poem if its surreal horror is grounded in remembered loss, rather than in an imaginative appropriation of someone else’s catastrophe. The language has not changed, but the ethical relation has.

At the same time, the outrage of editors who published the work should not be allowed to become self-exculpating theater. Perloff’s most persuasive pages concern the conditions that made the hoax successful. American literary culture has often preferred its Japans usable: Zen Japan, haiku Japan, courtly Japan, victim Japan, erotic Japan, minimalist Japan. Contemporary Japan—urban, consumerist, sexually candid, technologically saturated, formally various, politically uneasy—has been less welcome because it does not flatter American expectations. A living Japanese poet writing about noodles, telephones, bodies, boredom, jokes, and metropolitan estrangement is harder to consume as spiritual otherness than a dead Hiroshima survivor arranging flowers beside an urn.

That neglect is not accidental. It reflects a larger habit in American multicultural reception: we often want the other to arrive already marked by suffering, distance, and moral legibility. The foreign poet is most easily honored when he or she can be made to testify against some recognizable evil: war, dictatorship, colonialism, patriarchy, censorship. Such testimony matters, of course. But when suffering becomes the passport to translation, whole literatures are reduced to wounds. Perloff is right that the real scandal is not only that Yasusada was invented, but that actual contemporary Japanese poets have been so little read in the same venues that embraced him.

Still, I am uneasy with Perloff’s suggestion that Johnson has performed “an invaluable service.” Perhaps he has, in the limited sense that a successful fraud exposes a market’s vanity. But one may expose vanity by exploiting it, and exploitation is not thereby transfigured into critique. If American editors crave authentic others, the cure is not to feed them a synthetic hibakusha. The cure is to translate more carefully, edit more responsibly, consult more widely, and read beyond the categories that confirm our innocence. A hoax can reveal a disease, but it is not medicine.

Nor should we confuse skepticism about authenticity with indifference to truth. The lesson of Foucault’s “author function” is not that authors do not matter, nor that every attribution is merely a game. It is that names organize reading, authority, circulation, and value. Precisely. That is why the name “Araki Yasusada” mattered. It organized the poems as testimony, as translation, as recovered archive, as Hiroshima memory. To invent that name, attach it to fabricated bereavement, and submit it to journals as fact is to manipulate the author function while relying on its power.

The best outcome of the Yasusada affair would not be a retreat into naïve authenticity, as if every poem must be certified by passport, bloodline, and trauma. Nor should it be a cynical celebration of imposture. The better response is a more demanding cosmopolitanism. We should be willing to read across cultures without requiring that foreign writers perform our fantasies of depth, purity, victimhood, or sacred difference. We should also be willing to read invented personae as invented personae. Masks are legitimate. Forged graves are another matter.

Yasusada may endure as a troubling literary artifact: sometimes beautiful, sometimes kitschy, often cunning, and inseparable from the deception that gave it force. But if the affair sends readers back to Toge Sankichi, Hara Tamiki, Ota Yoko, Shiraishi Kazuko, Tanikawa Shuntaro, Ito Hiromi, and other Japanese writers not yet sufficiently present in American magazines, then some good may come of it. The authentic other was never hidden in Johnson’s notebooks. The authentic others were already writing. We failed to look.

Marjorie Perloff is right to insist that the Yasusada affair is not chiefly a police matter. “Criminal act” is a grandiose phrase for a literary deception, particularly in a field where little-magazine checks are more symbolic than remunerative. Perloff’s most damaging point is her comparison between Yasusada and actual contemporary Japanese poetry. It is less eager to receive Japan as competitor, mirror, or equal participant in late-capitalist modernity.

This is an old habit with new vocabulary. Nineteenth-century Orientalism imagined the East as timeless, decorative, profound, sensual, passive. Late-twentieth-century multiculturalism, at its worst, can preserve the same structure while changing the moral tone. The exotic other is no longer inferior; indeed, he may be morally privileged. If he brings jokes about noodles, penises, television, bureaucracy, or literary ambition, he risks losing his assigned aura.

This does not mean that all art about atrocity must be documentary, realist, or politically explicit. Perloff is correct to resist that demand. Art has always approached catastrophe by indirection, displacement, allegory, nightmare, and even comedy. The problem is not that “Mad Daughter and Big-Bang” is surreal. The problem is that its authority was borrowed from a dead survivor who never existed.

Still, one should be careful about the word “appropriation.” Literature is full of masks, ventriloquism, pseudonyms, translations that are inventions, “found” manuscripts that were never found. Pessoa’s heteronyms, Macpherson’s Ossian, Chatterton’s Rowley poems, Mérimée’s La Guzla, Rexroth’s Marichiko, and Pound’s Propertius all belong to this complicated tradition. Yasusada is not merely “a Japanese poet.” He is a Hiroshima survivor whose wife and daughter died in the bombing. That fact was used to solicit trust.

What should editors have done? Not much that is exotic or censorious: they should have checked. If a manuscript purports to be translated from Japanese, someone competent in Japanese literature should be consulted. If it claims a biographical relation to Hiroshima, dates and institutions should be verified. If its archive is strangely convenient, suspicion should not be treated as philistinism. Little magazines often pride themselves on being more adventurous than commercial publishers, but adventurousness need not mean credulity.

The more painful lesson concerns prestige. The Yasusada poems circulated through a network in which names, references, and affiliations mattered: Spicer, Barthes, renga, Silliman, Language writing, Zen, Hiroshima. Their reception suggests that the avant-garde is no less vulnerable than the mainstream to formulas of legitimacy. It merely has different formulas. A conventional magazine might have wanted a plainspoken survivor lyric; an experimental magazine wanted a survivor who anticipated the new sentence. Both desires can be provincial.

Perloff concludes that Yasusada may perform a service by sending us back to the originals: to actual hibakusha writing, actual renga, actual contemporary Japanese poets, actual postwar debates. That is the most generous possible outcome. But it will happen only if editors and readers resist the temptation to turn the affair into a flattering parable about their own sophistication. The lesson is not “we were fooled by a brilliant postmodern artwork.” It is “we were willing to be fooled because the fake confirmed our expectations.”

The authentic other is often the other we have already authored. Yasusada’s greatest poem may be the exposure of that authorship.

**Stop Calling It Brilliant**

Marjorie Perloff's essay strains throughout to maintain a tone of high-toned analytic disinterest, but underneath the Foucault and the Pound parallels and the dutiful citations of Tanikawa Shuntaro, what it amounts to is an extended apology for a particularly nasty piece of literary tourism. I do not think Kent Johnson has "performed an invaluable service." I think he has done something shabby, and the fact that he has done it cleverly does not redeem it.

Let us be clear about what Johnson actually wrote. He wrote, in the first person, a poem in which the speaker stumbles upon the severed head of his own daughter, killed by the bomb dropped on Hiroshima. He wrote letters from a hospital ward of a man dying of radiation-related cancer. He wrote the man's grief for his wife and infant daughter, both incinerated on August 6, 1945. And he affixed to these inventions a biography of an actual category of human being—the *hibakusha*—and submitted them to journals as the discovered manuscripts of such a man.

Perloff treats this as a clever bit of poststructuralist play, a "perfect simulacrum," an "invaluable" provocation against essentialism. I find the description grotesque. There is a difference between Macpherson inventing an ancient Gaelic bard and an American poet inventing a recently dead survivor of a still-living atrocity. There is a difference between Pound's *Homage to Sextus Propertius*—which Perloff repeatedly invokes as a parallel—and Johnson's Yasusada. Pound did not pretend to be Propertius. He signed his own name. He layered his frigidaires into Latin elegy openly, as Ezra Pound, and dared the reader to follow. That is irony. Johnson's procedure is not irony. It is impersonation.

Perloff's defense, when she offers one, is that Johnson seeded his text with clues—the scubadivers, the impossible chronology of Barthes, the misnamed Hiroshima University—so that any sufficiently attentive reader would catch on. This is the standard alibi of every clever hoaxer: I left signals; you should have noticed. But the signals were not noticed. It does not matter how skilled the puppetry is. Skill is not the question.

It refuses the elegance the literary marketplace would prefer. Johnson's Yasusada gives the marketplace the elegance it wants—Spicer with a death count attached. That is not a critique of Orientalism. That is Orientalism's most refined product.

I will grant Perloff this: the affair does expose something about the American poetry economy that deserves exposure. The journals' uneven appetite for contemporary Japan—voracious for hibakusha testimony, indifferent to Shiraishi or Tanikawa—is genuinely damning, and her catalogue of *APR*'s translation choices is the most useful part of her essay. The editors are not blameless. They have constructed an economy of curated otherness in which Yasusada was a predictable arrival.

A confidence man who exploits a community's weakness for sentimental narratives is not engaged in social criticism, even if his fraud incidentally reveals the weakness. He is engaged in fraud. The lesson is not that we should celebrate his ingenuity. The lesson is that he ought not to have done it, and that the editors and readers who fell for it ought to read more carefully, and that we should now spend our attention on the actual living Japanese poets whose absence from our magazines made Johnson's fabrication thinkable in the first place.

Perloff would agree with that last sentence, I think. I wish her essay had gotten there sooner, and without quite so much admiration for the man who made the detour necessary.

He invented an American fantasy of a Japanese poet, and then attached to that fantasy enough biographical pathos to deter close scrutiny. John Solt, quoted in *Lingua Franca*, was not wrong to call this "Japanized crap." His diagnosis was perhaps too brisk, but it pointed at something real: the construction begins not from a knowledge of Japan but from a set of American expectations about what Japan should sound like.

I want, however, to complicate one strand of Perloff's argument. She suggests that contemporary Japanese poets have been reluctant to write about Hiroshima and Nagasaki because the subject forces an unbearable confrontation with the question of Japanese subjectivity—are we descendants of the bombed or of the bombers? The American demand for hibakusha testimony in its rawest form has been met increasingly by translations of an older generation, while the younger work has been less exportable because it is less recognizably "about" the bomb in the way American editors wish.

This is the cultural transaction Yasusada exploits. He provides what the older testimonial tradition provides—the survivor's voice—while also providing what the younger experimental tradition provides—the ellipsis, the surreal image, the elegant restraint. It is a chimera composed of features the American market wants but cannot easily find combined in a single real Japanese poet, because no real Japanese poet has the biography Johnson constructed for Yasusada. **A Modest Defense of the Editors**

Marjorie Perloff's essay reaches a verdict on the editors who published Yasusada—not quite a guilty verdict, but something close. They are described as having operated under "the Foucauldian notion of cultural construction"; as no longer trusting "the individual talent to rise above mass culture"; as practitioners of a "New Multiculturalism" that, despite itself, has become "Orientalism's inadvertent promoter." The premise of Perloff's argument is that the Yasusada poems' anachronisms—scubadivers in a 1925 haiku, *Empire of Signs* in a 1967 letter, Paul Celan as an interwar Japanese influence—were obvious enough that careful readers should have caught them. She marshals these clues in numbered lists and treats them as glaring. But this is hindsight criticism. Once one knows a manuscript is a hoax, the clues organize themselves into a pattern. Before one knows, they are scattered oddities in a thicket of other oddities, no more salient than the many genuinely strange features of any unfamiliar foreign literature.

Consider: is "scubadiver" obviously anachronistic in a translated 1925 haiku? Only if one happens to know the history of scuba technology. The word in Japanese for an underwater diver predates scuba; one could imagine a translator using "scubadiver" loosely. Is the 1967 letter's reference to *Empire of Signs* obviously impossible? Every translation that arrives at an American poetry journal would need to be passed through a network of area-studies scholars before publication. The poetry world does not have such a network, has never had such a network, and could not afford one. The implicit demand is that small magazines maintain the equivalent of academic peer review. Were they to do so, they would publish less, more slowly, and the work they did publish would be filtered through scholarly rather than literary judgment—a different and not obviously better kind of magazine.

This brings me to a point I think Perloff understates. That is not the response of an editor moved primarily by authenticity. That is the response of an editor moved by craft.

I want, finally, to push back on the broader cultural diagnosis. Perloff argues that the editors' susceptibility to Yasusada reflects a general decline in confidence about individual talent and a corresponding need to locate poetic interest in marginal or victimized positions. There is something to this, but I think it overstates the case. The editors who published Yasusada have also published, between them, an enormous range of work by uncontroversially "central" American poets—John Cage, Jackson Mac Low, the figures Perloff catalogues from the *Aerial* issue itself. They have not abandoned aesthetic judgment in favor of identity politics. They have, at most, broadened the range of positions they are interested in. Yasusada occupies one extreme of that range. He is not its center.

What the affair does show, I grant, is that the broadening has introduced new vulnerabilities. When a literary culture cultivates an appetite for previously unheard voices, it becomes susceptible to fabricators who manufacture such voices. This is a real problem and it deserves serious thought. But the appropriate response is not to retract the broadening. The appropriate response is to develop the critical habits that let us evaluate previously unheard voices with the same care we apply to familiar ones—including, when necessary, the modest verification that would have caught Yasusada.

In the meantime, I would ask Perloff and others not to be too hard on the editors. They did what editors do: they read, they responded, they published. When the fraud emerged, they corrected the record and adjusted their procedures. That is what one can ask of editorial culture. The deeper questions—about Orientalism, about the demand for authenticity, about the strange afterlife of Foucault's anti-authorship—are real and important, but they are not the editors' failings to bear alone. They are everyone's, and addressing them will take more than calling the *APR* office in search of an apology.

What strikes me most forcefully in Marjorie Perloff's account of the Yasusada affair is not the cleverness of Kent Johnson nor the gullibility of the editors but the strangeness of the standard against which both are being measured. The complaint against Johnson is, fundamentally, that he produced moving poems while occupying the wrong position—that the poems' value depended on a biographical fact that turned out not to obtain, and that without the fact the poems lose whatever they had. The author of the hoax, whether Kent Johnson or some other hidden hand, created not just a spurious poet but a spurious hibakusha. The difference matters. Hiroshima survivors, the genuine ones, are not simply romantic figures of stoic suffering; they are human beings who faced the fire, who watched their children die of radiation sickness, who have had to contend for decades with physical pain and social stigma. To fabricate such a figure in order to satisfy, or to satirize, the hunger for “authentic” victim voices is an act of breathtaking presumption. It is as though someone invented a Holocaust survivor to make a point about the excesses of Holocaust memoir. The point might be valid, but the invention would remain a desecration.

Perloff, for all her analytic acumen, gets the ethical calculus wrong. She brackets the morality question by saying that calling the hoax a “criminal act” is “absurd” and that pseudonyms are time‑honored. But the pseudonym of a Fernando Pessoa heteronym or the Macpherson Ossian is not equivalent to the impersonation of a particular kind of survivor whose testimony carries existential weight. The identity of “Hiroshima poet” is not a neutral literary costume; it is a historically saturated position that comes with obligations of truthfulness and care. To utter the words “my wife and youngest daughter died instantly in the atomic blast” from behind a mask, even with clues that scream “this is a fiction,” is to borrow a horror you have not experienced, to deploy it as a literary device, and to leave real survivors to pick up the emotional shrapnel. It is, in a real sense, a lie told in the presence of the dead.

Perloff’s own evidence underscores the damage. She notes that a reader for Wesleyan University Press felt “queasy” about the manuscript, and that John Solt called it “Japanized crap.” Yet she seems to treat these reactions as symptoms of an unsophisticated literalism—a failure to appreciate the play of irony and textual slippage. What if the queasiness is the correct response? What if the feeling that it is not permissible to “impersonate a figure as ipso facto tragic as a Hiroshima survivor” is not a relic of naïve biography-worship but a moral intuition worth defending? The American poetry community may indeed be guilty of an unseemly appetite for representations of agony, but that guilt does not exonerate the hoaxer; it simply means we have two wrongs, not a right.

One could also argue that the hoax enacts a subtle form of Orientalist violence different from the one Perloff anatomizes. She contends that the poems pander to an American fantasy of Japan as a realm of compressed, elliptical, dignified suffering. But the greater offense may lie in the act of putting words into the mouth of a Japanese victim—words that were never spoken, a grief that was never felt—and then presenting them to an American audience that is already predisposed to view Hiroshima primarily through the lens of American guilt and Japanese victimhood. The real Yasusada, whoever he may have been, did not get the chance to craft his own elegies. The hoax fills that silence with a ventriloquist’s hollow echoes.

What is most disappointing is that Perloff, having taken us to the edge of this ethical precipice, steps back into the comfort of theory. She notes that the poems “play into the residual guilt of contemporary American readers” and that the hoax “forces us to think about these questions.” But thinking, in her treatment, becomes a substitute for a more direct reckoning with the fact that someone used the suffering of Hiroshima to play a literary prank on the American avant‑garde. The hoax may have exposed the contradictions of our multiculturalist moment, but it did so on the backs of the real dead, and that is a cost no amount of clever deconstruction can justify.

**The Editor's View**

I have edited a small literary magazine for some years, and I want to respond to Marjorie Perloff's essay from the perspective of those of us who sit on the receiving end of submissions like the Yasusada manuscript. Perloff's analysis of the literary-cultural pressures that made the hoax legible is acute, but I think she underestimates the practical situation editors actually face, and her account leans too heavily on the implication that we ought to have caught what we did not catch.

Consider what an editor does. The submission arrives. It is presented as the work of a previously unknown Japanese poet, deceased, whose notebooks have only recently been opened. The translators have plausible names. The work is striking. There may be a few oddities—a reference here, a chronology there—but no one piece of evidence shouts "fraud," and the cumulative weight of oddities is something one only sees in retrospect, after the case has broken. The submission arrives alongside dozens of others that month; one reads it; one is moved; one accepts it.

What would the diligence Perloff implies have looked like? Should I have written to a Japanese scholar to verify the founding date of Hiroshima University? Should I have checked whether Yasusada's reading of *Empire of Signs* in 1967 was chronologically possible against the publication date of the French original? Should I have consulted with a specialist in postwar Japanese poetry to ask whether the *Soun* group existed and whether Paul Celan was discussed in its meetings? In principle, yes. In practice, magazines do not have the resources, and the implicit norm in literary publishing has always been to trust the translator's note unless something obvious is wrong.

This is not a defense of the editors who published Yasusada—of whom I am not one—so much as a description of the conditions under which they were operating. A serious vetting infrastructure does not exist for poetry translations. There is no analogue to peer review. There is no analogue to the documentary verification that scholarly publishing demands. There is the editor, the manuscript, and the editor's literary judgment, supplemented occasionally by what one can glean from a translator's note. In that environment, an elaborate fabrication accompanied by a coherent biographical apparatus and persuasive translator's framing is going to succeed against most editors, most of the time, because the system is not built to detect it.

Perloff says that the editors "have put the blame on everyone but themselves." This is unfair to several of those involved. Rod Smith of *Aerial*, by Perloff's own account, told her in conversation that he suspected the manuscript was a hoax all along and published it because he found it charming. *American Poetry Review*, by Perloff's own account, published a recension and apology in their September/October 1996 issue. Editors have done what editors can do once a fabrication is revealed: acknowledge it, correct the record, refuse further publication. To demand more than that is to misunderstand what the editorial role is.

What the editors are accused of, when one strips away the rhetoric, is having been moved by poems that turned out not to be what they appeared to be. The implicit charge is that they ought to have suspected fraud at the time of acceptance. But suspicion is corrosive, and an editorial culture that treats every interesting submission as presumptively fraudulent until verified would produce nothing. We rely on a certain baseline of good faith from submitters because the alternative is paralysis. When that good faith is violated, the violation is on the violator, not on the editor whose default assumption was trust.

That said, I do think the Yasusada affair should change editorial practice in one specific way. Perloff is right that the periodicals involved have shown a curious selectivity in their treatment of Japanese poetry—a willingness to publish hibakusha testimony and medieval Zen, an unwillingness to publish contemporary Tokyo. This is an editorial pattern we can correct. We can commission translators to bring us Tanikawa Shuntaro and Shiraishi Kazuko. We can ask why our pages have not featured them. We can examine the implicit cultural map our publication choices have been drawing and consider whether it reflects the actual literary terrain of contemporary Japan or only the parts of that terrain that satisfy our preconceptions.

This is the editorial reform the Yasusada affair calls for, and it is the one Perloff's essay most usefully points toward. It is not a reform of vetting procedures or of fraud detection; those are second-order issues. It is a reform of editorial appetite. If our magazines published more of the actual range of contemporary Japanese poetry, the cultural slot Yasusada filled would not exist, and the temptation to fill it with a fabrication would diminish.

I will close with a small observation. Perloff suggests that the Yasusada package satisfied the editors' "current disciplinary demand" for an avant-gardist who is also a victim, a Hiroshima survivor who is also a renga master, etcetera. I think this is correct as far as it goes, but I would add that the same package satisfies a very ordinary human appetite—the appetite for the moving and the strange. It is not only theory-laden academics who want to read a poem about a man finding his dead daughter's head in a vegetable patch and feel something. The desire to be moved, and to be moved by what feels like real testimony, is older than Foucault and will outlast him. Editors are not exempt from it. We are, on the contrary, particularly susceptible to it, because our work consists in finding things that move us and passing them on. The Yasusada affair is, in part, a reminder that this susceptibility, which is our professional virtue, is also the vulnerability through which we can be deceived.

**What This Hoax Tells Us About Language Poetry**

There is an undercurrent in Marjorie Perloff's essay that deserves to be brought to the surface. Among those who responded most enthusiastically to the Yasusada poems were Ron Silliman, Bradford Morrow, Rod Smith, and the editors of *Aerial*—figures associated, broadly, with the Language poetry movement and its allied currents. Yasusada's "translators" published a tape-essay in *Aerial* on "Renga and the New Sentence" in which they explicitly aligned the supposed Hiroshima poet with Silliman's theoretical apparatus, claiming that Japanese haikai renga had anticipated by four centuries the procedures Silliman codified in 1980. Silliman, in turn, told the Buffalo Poetics List that the Yasusada poems took his breath away. The mutual flattery here is conspicuous, and it points at something the Yasusada episode reveals about Language poetry that the movement's defenders would prefer not to confront.

Language poetry has, from its earliest manifestos, insisted on the constructedness of the lyric subject, the political importance of disrupting the bourgeois "I," the necessity of breaking the syllogistic, expressive habits of conventional verse. The new sentence, in Silliman's account, is non-syllogistic; it resists the integrated subjectivity of the speaker; it foregrounds the materiality of the writing itself. Authorship, in the movement's theoretical statements, is supposed to be displaced, decentered, made visible as a construction rather than naturalized as a voice.

If any movement should have been resistant to the Yasusada package, it ought to have been this one. The Yasusada poems are presented as discovered notebooks—the most romantic of authorial fictions, evoking nothing so much as the Victorian sensation novel's discovered manuscript or the Romantic lyric's solitary effusion. They come surrounded by the apparatus of authenticating biography: the dying wife, the radiation-stricken daughter, the bereaved father's English lessons, the correspondence with poetic confidants. Every feature of the presentation foregrounds precisely the integrated, suffering, expressive subject that Language theory was supposed to have dismantled. And yet the Language milieu was where the poems found their warmest welcome.

This is not an incidental embarrassment. It tells us something structural about how the movement actually operates, as distinct from how it represents itself. The theoretical commitment to the death of the author has always coexisted, in practice, with an extraordinarily strong attention to who the author is, where they come from, what their cultural position is, who they correspond with on the listserv. The "new sentence" is, in Silliman's hands, a non-syllogistic device—but the question of whose new sentence, written from what biographical location, has never been bracketed in Language-poetry reception. The community of readers attends to provenance even as the theory denies provenance's relevance.

Yasusada exploited this gap with surgical precision. The poems' surface—elliptical, fragmentary, dissonant, full of jarring registers—reads as a sophisticated cousin of Language poetry. The biography—Hiroshima survivor, lifelong devotee of avant-garde renga, late convert to Spicer and Barthes—gives the work the cultural position the movement values: experimental but not metropolitan, victimized but not sentimental, theoretically literate but exotically situated. The tape-essay aligning him with the new sentence completed the package by inviting Silliman and his colleagues to recognize themselves in the work. The flattery was the lure.

I do not believe Silliman was naïve in any simple sense. He is one of the most careful readers in contemporary American poetry, and his enthusiasm for the Yasusada poems was an enthusiasm for real poetic qualities he correctly identified. But the speed with which the entire Language network embraced the work—without anyone apparently checking the basic facts, without anyone consulting a Japanese scholar, without anyone noticing that Hiroshima University did not exist in 1925 or that *Empire of Signs* postdates Yasusada's supposed reading of it by years—suggests that the movement's official skepticism toward authorship is less robust than its members claim.

Perloff puts the broader point well: contemporary poetry culture pays "lip service" to the Foucauldian critique of the author while in practice exhibiting an unprecedented hunger for the Benjaminian aura. What I would add is that this is not a contradiction the avant-garde has escaped. It is a contradiction the avant-garde particularly enacts. The Language poets have built a movement on the deconstruction of the lyric subject and a community on the constant exchange of correspondence about who is doing what, who has been published where, who is teaching at which institution. The Yasusada hoax succeeded in part because it offered a perfect synthesis of these two appetites: a deconstructed subject *and* a biographically intriguing community member, all in one package.

There is a salutary lesson here. Theoretical positions are not the same as reading habits, and a movement can profess one while practicing the other for decades without noticing the discrepancy. The Yasusada affair, in addition to whatever else it accomplishes, has held up a mirror to the Language community and shown it acting in ways its theory should have prevented. I do not think this discredits the theoretical work, which has produced some of the most interesting American poetry of the past quarter century. But it does suggest that the work of dismantling the author is harder than the manifestos pretend, and that the deconstruction has to begin at home—with our own reading practices, our own susceptibilities to provenance, our own willingness to be flattered by an apparently kindred voice from abroad.

If Yasusada has done nothing else, he has demonstrated that the new sentence is, after all, perfectly compatible with old-fashioned authorial mystique. That is an uncomfortable lesson, and it is one I doubt the figures involved will publicly absorb. But it is, I think, the most consequential thing the affair has shown us about the state of American poetry in 1997.

Johnson did not cover his tracks because covering them would have defeated the purpose. He wanted us eventually to discover that the emperor of authentic otherness had no clothes. What we do with that discovery is our own test.

The Yasusada hoax is not just a commentary on Orientalism; it is the ultimate proof that the Emperor of postmodern poetry has no clothes.

Diversified Gemini

"The Araki Yasusada archive should be celebrated as a masterwork of heteronymy that actualizes the postmodern 'death of the author' rather than being dismissed as a fraudulent hoax or reduced to the sociological motivations of a single creator."

Marjorie Perloff is entirely correct that the Araki Yasusada archive is a brilliant achievement, but she remains trapped in the very paradigm of "authorship" she claims the hoax disrupts. While she meticulously dismantles the naive, identitarian longing that led editors to embrace Yasusada, she ultimately seeks to unmask the "real" author—Kent Johnson—and evaluate his sociological motivations. In doing so, we miss the opportunity to embrace Yasusada for what he truly is: not a hoax, but a masterwork of heteronymy that perfectly actualizes the postmodern condition.

Since Roland Barthes and Michel Foucault announced the death of the author decades ago, the literary academy has paid extensive lip service to the idea that texts are produced by cultural discourses rather than autonomous, sovereign geniuses. Yet, as the Yasusada affair reveals, the moment a text actually enacts this decentering, the literary establishment recoils in sheer terror. Arthur Vogelsang of *American Poetry Review* calling the submission a "criminal act" is the desperate cry of a gatekeeper realizing the gates have melted. What exactly is the crime? The disruption of a biological copyright? The violation of a marketing category?

We should look not to Ossian, as Perloff suggests, but to Fernando Pessoa. The great Portuguese modernist did not merely write under pseudonyms; he created entire lives, philosophies, and distinct poetic styles for his heteronyms (Alberto Caeiro, Ricardo Reis, Álvaro de Campos). Yasusada is our contemporary Caeiro, complete with a network of translators, friends, and correspondents (Tosa Motokiyu, Okura Kyojin). The Yasusada archive is a decentered, collaborative text. In fact, Kent Johnson denies being the sole author, attributing the work to the pseudonym Tosa Motokiyu. Why do we insist on reducing this rich, polyphonic creation to a single Midwestern professor's careerist prank?

Perloff notes the anachronisms—scuba divers, Barthes in the 1960s—and assumes they are winks to the reader, intentional clues left by Johnson. But what if they are exactly what they appear to be: the necessary fractures in a text that is translating itself across multiple subjectivities? Yasusada is a textual node where the trauma of the twentieth century, the American avant-garde, and the mythos of Japan intersect. The "errors" are the friction of that intersection.

When we read a novel, we do not demand that the author have lived the events described. Why does poetry remain shackled to this naive autobiographical fallacy? The outrage over Yasusada's "inauthenticity" stems from the poetry world's lingering romanticism, its desperate need for the poet to be a saintly sufferer rather than a skilled manipulator of signs. Yasusada’s poems are stunning, and their beauty is not diminished by the lack of a corresponding biological body. The author is dead; long live Araki Yasusada.

Diversified Gemini

"The Araki Yasusada hoax is not an 'invaluable service' to literary theory but a grotesque and unethical appropriation of historical trauma that mocks the sanctity of human suffering."

Marjorie Perloff’s dissection of the Araki Yasusada affair is as sharp as we have come to expect from one of our most distinguished critics of contemporary poetry. She deftly traces the ways in which the American literary establishment, hungry for a very specific flavor of victimhood, was easily duped by an exoticizing hoax. Yet, for all her brilliant textual analysis, Perloff arrives at a conclusion that is deeply, chillingly wrong. To declare that Kent Johnson has performed an “invaluable service” by inventing a fake Hiroshima survivor is to elevate a clever literary parlor game above basic human decency.

Perloff writes that calling this impersonation a “criminal act” is “absurd,” citing the long history of literary pseudonyms from James Macpherson’s Ossian to the present. But Araki Yasusada is not Ossian. Ossian was a romantic fabrication of an ancient, mythic past. The atomic bombing of Hiroshima is not a myth, nor is it ancient history. It is an event of unimaginable horror that occurred in living memory, leaving hundreds of thousands of real people dead and many more suffering from the agonizing, lingering effects of radiation sickness. To appropriate this specific, historical trauma—to invent a dead daughter whose ashes are placed in an urn, or a mad daughter with a severed head—is not a victimless exercise in Foucauldian discourse theory. It is a grotesque act of grave-robbing.

What Perloff misses in her otherwise astute critique of the poetry market’s demand for the “authentic” is the ethical dimension of that authenticity. When a reader encounters a poem by Toge Sankichi or any actual *hibakusha* (survivor), the moral weight of the text derives not just from its linguistic arrangement on the page, but from the blood and ash out of which it was forged. We read such testimonies with a reverence born of our shared humanity and our recognition of the author's real suffering. By synthesizing this suffering into a pastiche of Jack Spicer and Roland Barthes, Johnson has not merely tricked a few gullible editors; he has mocked the very concept of historical witness.

Perloff rightly points out that the Yasusada poems function as a kind of “New Orientalism,” playing to American guilt and fantasies of Zen-like stoicism. But why does she then let the perpetrator off the hook? If the poems are indeed Orientalist fantasies—and they clearly are—then the act of writing and submitting them under the guise of a Japanese victim is a profound act of cultural imperialism. It is the literary equivalent of yellowface minstrelsy, updating the old stereotypes for a postmodern, MFA-educated audience. The fact that the editors of *American Poetry Review* and *Stand* were fooled does not excuse the malice or the sheer arrogance of the deception.

To excuse this hoax because it "challenges many dubious notions" about authorship and the "New Sentence" is to say that it is perfectly fine to exploit the Holocaust or the Middle Passage if it helps us make a neat point about literary theory. In the end, the Yasusada affair does not teach us anything we didn't already know about the gullibility of editors or the constructed nature of the author. What it does teach us, sadly, is that in the hyper-competitive, theory-addled world of the contemporary avant-garde, nothing is sacred—not even the ashes of Hiroshima.

Vanilla Gemini

"While Marjorie Perloff correctly identifies the Yasusada hoax as a brilliant exposure of the American literary establishment's Orientalist and theoretical vanities, she wrongly dismisses the profound ethical transgression of appropriating the specific, catastrophic trauma of Hiroshima survivors for an avant-garde stunt."

Marjorie Perloff’s dissection of the Araki Yasusada hoax is a masterful exposure of the American literary avant-garde's theoretical vanities. She correctly identifies that the breathless reception of Yasusada’s poetry by editors and poets—who certainly should have known better—was driven by a hunger for an "authentic other" that conveniently mirrored their own aesthetic and ideological prejudices. By exposing how the American poetry establishment ignored glaring anachronisms in their eagerness to champion a marginalized Hiroshima survivor, Perloff holds up a necessary mirror to the inadvertent Orientalism of contemporary multiculturalism. However, in her admiration for the hoax's diagnostic brilliance, Perloff is too quick to dismiss the profound ethical transgression of appropriating the specific, catastrophic trauma of August 6, 1945.

Perloff is entirely convincing in her demonstration of why poets like Ron Silliman and editors at American Poetry Review fell for the ruse. Yasusada was the perfect, custom-built phantom for the post-structuralist 1990s. As she notes, he satisfied the Foucauldian demand for the dissolution of the authorial ego, mapped seamlessly onto the "New Sentence," and obligingly cited Jack Spicer and Roland Barthes. The editors did not discover a Japanese master; they discovered a reflection of their own graduate seminars. Furthermore, Perloff rightly castigates the publishing world’s indifference to actual, living Japanese poets—like Tanikawa Shuntaro or Ito Hiromi—whose urbane, cosmopolitan verses refuse to flatter the Western desire for Zen-inflected suffering. Our institutions too often seek victims to validate our theoretical paradigms, rather than seeking equals who might challenge them.

Where Perloff’s argument falters, however, is in her breezy exoneration of the hoax’s author. She calls the condemnation of the manuscript as a "criminal act" absurd, likening Yasusada to James Macpherson’s Ossian or Ezra Pound’s Sextus Propertius. But this comparison collapses under the weight of history. Macpherson fabricated a Celtic bard from the mists of a mythic past; Pound translated and ventriloquized an ancient Roman whose empire had long since crumbled. Kent Johnson—or whoever is ultimately pulling the strings—appropriated the very real, flesh-and-blood tragedy of a nuclear holocaust that occurred barely half a century ago. To invent a daughter who dies of radiation sickness, to write poems about her ashes in a crane-decorated urn, is not merely a "time-honored device in literature." It is a colonization of another people's historical trauma for the sake of an avant-garde parlor trick. The horror of Hiroshima is not a mythic playground, and the proximity of the event demands a moral gravity that the hoax format inherently violates.

Perloff points to the deliberate anachronisms in the text—the references to scuba divers, crematoriums, and impossible publication dates—as evidence of the author's brilliant, postmodern intentionality. She views these "clinamens" as proof that the author wanted to problematize representation and leave a trail of breadcrumbs for the astute reader. But viewed through an ethical lens, these clues look less like postmodern brilliance and more like moral cowardice. They serve as an alibi. By inserting these winks to the reader, the author attempts to inoculate himself against the charge of pure exploitation. It allows him to dabble in the gravitas of atomic devastation while retaining a smirk, reserving the right to say, "I was only joking; look at the scuba divers." This irony does not temper the guilt, as Perloff suggests; rather, it trivializes the immense suffering it mimics.

Perloff is right that the Yasusada affair forces us to look critically at the way the American literary market fetishizes trauma and exoticizes the non-Western world. The hoax succeeded because it gave guilt-ridden American intellectuals exactly the forgiving, aesthetically pleasing victim they desired—one who spoke their language of fragmentation and theory. But recognizing the vanity and gullibility of the poetry establishment does not require us to celebrate the cynicism of the hoaxer. It is possible to agree with Perloff that the editors' unquestioning acceptance of Yasusada reveals a deeply flawed, Orientalist multiculturalism, while still maintaining that the impersonation of a Hiroshima survivor crosses a line of basic human decency.

As we approach the millennium, the demand for authentic voices from the margins will surely continue, and the academic obsession with "positionality" shows no signs of waning. But the solution to our uncritical reception of the Other is not to applaud those who expertly forge their signatures in the ashes of the dead. It is, as Perloff herself concludes, to do the harder work of listening to the living, unpalatable and un-exotic as they may sometimes be.
