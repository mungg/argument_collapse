"The Yasusada hoax does not expose the bankruptcy of multiculturalism, but rather reveals a 'pseudo-multiculturalism' where American literary institutions lazily prioritize shallow, exoticized projections of identity over genuine engagement with actual cultural and historical differences."

Marjorie Perloff’s essay is brilliant, but its brilliance has a target too broad for its evidence. The Yasusada hoax does expose American fantasies about Japan, editorial carelessness, and the avant-garde’s appetite for exotic authorization. What it does not expose, at least not in the way Perloff suggests, is the bankruptcy of multiculturalism. Indeed, the affair may show something almost opposite: not that multicultural attention has gone too far, but that it has remained too shallow, too selective, and too easily governed by old aesthetic hierarchies.

Perloff argues that “excellence” has been subordinated to agency and positionality, creating a market for poets who occupy compelling victim identities. There is truth here. Literary institutions sometimes congratulate themselves for publishing the oppressed while reading the work lazily, as evidence rather than art. But the Yasusada case does not fit that pattern neatly. The journals that published him were not simply organs of multicultural representation. They were often experimental, coterie, avant-garde venues invested in formal innovation. Yasusada appealed because he seemed to join Hiroshima witness to Spicerian and Language-poetry concerns. He was not merely the authentic other; he was the other as avant-garde ancestor.

That distinction matters. If multiculturalism means the serious study and publication of literatures outside the old Anglo-American canon, then the failure here is not multiculturalism but pseudo-multiculturalism. A genuinely multicultural editorial practice would have involved Japanese-language competence, knowledge of modern Japanese literary history, familiarity with living Japanese poets, and skepticism toward American projections. It would have been less likely, not more likely, to accept Yasusada at face value.

Perloff herself supplies the evidence. American Poetry Review published Yasusada but little contemporary Japanese poetry. It published medieval court poets, a Zen master, and a Hiroshima portfolio, but not the irreverent, urban, sexually frank postwar poets whose work complicates American ideas of Japan. That is not the result of too much attention to cultural difference. It is the result of too little attention to actual difference. Japan was admitted only under recognizable signs: antiquity, spirituality, suffering.

This is why blaming “positionality” can mislead. The problem was not that editors asked, “Who speaks?” The problem was that they answered the question in advance. They wanted a Hiroshima survivor who sounded like their idea of a Hiroshima survivor, though updated for postmodern taste. They did not ask enough about the institutions, language, literary networks, and historical contradictions that would make such a writer real. Positionality became a label rather than an inquiry.

The best multicultural criticism has never been satisfied with labels. It asks how race, nation, empire, gender, class, and language shape production and reception. It would not treat “Japanese” as a single essence. It would distinguish Kyoto from Hiroshima, prewar from postwar, tanka from free verse, state militarism from civilian victimhood, Zen inheritance from Western consumption of Zen, translation from invention. Perloff wants precisely this complexity. But then she risks assigning the simplification to multiculturalism rather than to the institutions that practice it badly.

There is also a danger in opposing “excellence” to identity too starkly. The canon has always been built through identity, though often under unmarked names: male, white, European, educated, imperial, metropolitan. When new writers insist on the significance of social location, they are not introducing impurity into an otherwise disinterested field. They are revealing conditions that were already there. The desire to read hibakusha poets is not ignoble. The desire to read colonized, exiled, enslaved, or silenced writers is not a fall from aesthetic seriousness. It becomes problematic only when readers treat suffering as a substitute for form, or form as a way to consume suffering without understanding it.

Yasusada is instructive because he satisfies both temptations. Those who wanted witness could find Hiroshima. Those who wanted formal sophistication could find disjunction, renga, new sentence, pseudo-translation. Those who wanted theory could find the death of the author. Those who wanted lyric pathos could find the dead daughter’s urn. The fake was successful because it was overdetermined. It gave every constituency something.

What would a better response look like? First, defend the expansion of literary attention while criticizing tokenism. The answer to Yasusada is not a return to a supposedly universal poetry judged without regard to history. That universality has too often meant provincial American taste. The answer is more history, more language study, more translation, more collaboration with scholars and poets from the cultures being represented.

Second, resist the transformation of trauma into credential. A poem by a survivor may be powerful, weak, conventional, radical, sentimental, opaque, or dull. To read it only as testimony is to reduce it. But to ignore the testimonial claim is also false. Ethical reading must hold both art and history in view. That is difficult, which is why shortcuts like Yasusada are tempting.

Third, publish contemporary Japanese poetry in its range. Perloff’s examples from Shiraishi Kazuko, Ito Hiromi, and Tanikawa Shuntaro are valuable because they break the spell of the expected. They show Japanese poets not as curators of timeless delicacy but as modern writers contending with sex, humor, domesticity, mass culture, metaphysical play, and ordinary irritation. Such work does more to challenge American provincialism than another invented Zen-inflected elegist.

Finally, recognize that the avant-garde has its own exoticism. It likes the foreign when the foreign can be made to prefigure its theories. The Yasusada archive’s linking of renga to Silliman’s new sentence is a perfect example. A tradition is honored only insofar as it becomes an ancestor of us. That is not multiculturalism; it is narcissism with footnotes.

Perloff has given us a necessary critique. But we should be careful where we aim it. The scandal is not that American poetry has become too concerned with the other. The scandal is that it has so often preferred an other of its own making to the more demanding, less flattering others who are actually writing.

"The Araki Yasusada hoax succeeded because it exploited a selective American desire for a 'coherent aesthetic essence' of Japan, highlighting the need for a more complex, de-exoticized engagement with actual contemporary Japanese poetry."

The most useful result of the Yasusada controversy would be a moratorium on the phrase “the Japanese sensibility.” If the affair teaches anything, it is that American readers are still too ready to imagine Japan as a coherent aesthetic essence: spare, elliptical, ceremonious, melancholy, erotic but restrained, spiritual but not doctrinal, wounded but dignified. Araki Yasusada could pass through important magazines because he wore that costume beautifully.

Marjorie Perloff’s essay performs a valuable act of de-exoticizing. Her discussion of contemporary Japanese poets is not an ornament to the argument; it is the argument’s ethical center. Once one has read the ribald energy of Shiraishi Kazuko, the bodily comedy of Ito Hiromi, or the wry self-consciousness of Tanikawa Shuntaro, the fabricated Yasusada looks less like Japan and more like an American dream of Japan. That does not make the poems worthless. It makes their reception embarrassing.

American literary culture has long had a selective ear for Japanese poetry. Haiku entered English as a discipline of perception, a little machine for producing precision and humility. Zen entered as antidote to Western excess. Court poetry entered as refinement. Hiroshima writing entered as witness. These are all real parts of Japanese literary and religious history, but they become distortions when isolated from the rest. Japan also has proletarian writing, surrealism, feminist experimentation, postwar colloquialism, comic vulgarity, mass media, political ambivalence, and literary careerism. It has everything any modern literary culture has.

The Yasusada hoax exploited the gap between actual plurality and imported essence. The invented poet is conveniently overcomplete. He is haiku and renga, Zen and avant-garde, Hiroshima and Spicer, Barthes and Celan, documentary notebook and postmodern fragmentation. He is enough like tradition to seem rooted and enough like us to seem exciting. Real poets are rarely so serviceable.

One reason American readers accept such figures is that translation often arrives without context. A poem appears with a brief biographical note, perhaps a translator’s preface, and then must stand as representative of a whole culture. The burden is impossible. A mediocre American poem is only a mediocre American poem; a translated Japanese poem may be read as evidence of Japan. This asymmetry encourages both reverence and laziness. The reader does not ask whether the poem is conventional, eccentric, archaizing, fashionable, regional, polemical, parodic, or badly translated. It is simply “Japanese.”

The remedy is not to abandon translation but to thicken it. We need clusters, debates, rival translations, essays, interviews, historical notes, and disagreement among translators. We need to see Japanese poets arguing with one another, not merely arriving as emissaries of an ancient civilization. We need translations of poems that do not fit American expectations: poems about department stores, office work, menstruation, traffic, television, American military bases, aging parents, school exams, bad sex, jazz, bureaucracy, loneliness in apartment blocks. We need poems that make us uncertain what “Japanese” is supposed to mean.

Perloff’s account of postwar reluctance to write about Hiroshima is especially important. American readers may assume that Hiroshima must dominate Japanese poetry, just as outsiders sometimes expect every Jewish writer to write about the Holocaust or every African-American writer to write about racism. But historical trauma does not operate by simple command. It may produce testimony, silence, displacement, exhaustion, anger, kitsch, piety, denial, or generational distance. Younger Japanese writers inherit both victimhood and the memory of Japanese aggression in Asia. That double inheritance complicates any easy poetics of witness.

Yasusada removes the complication. He mourns personally. He does not force the American reader to think hard about Pearl Harbor, Nanking, forced labor, firebombing, invasion plans, surrender terms, the emperor system, or the American desire to end the war. Nor does he force the Japanese reader—if there is a Japanese reader for this English-language artifact—to confront the entanglement of victim and aggressor. He occupies the morally clean position of bereaved civilian. Such civilians existed, of course, in vast numbers. But a literature that offers only that position becomes sentimental.

At the same time, I hesitate to condemn the author of Yasusada as simply exploitative. The work may be read as a trap set for our assumptions, and a successful trap at that. The anachronisms are so blatant that they almost beg to be found. Perhaps the archive is a parody of authenticity, an imitation so excessive that its exposure is built in. If so, the embarrassment belongs partly to us. We wanted the fake to be true.

But parody does not complete the task. Having been exposed, the fake must not become yet another substitute for the real. There is a danger that American critics will now write endlessly about Yasusada—about hoaxing, authorship, trauma, Orientalism—while still not translating or reading contemporary Japanese poetry. That would be the final irony: the invented Japanese poet continuing to monopolize the attention denied to actual Japanese poets.

Editors can change this. Invite translators who work deeply in modern and contemporary Japanese. Ask them not for “a Zen issue” or “a Hiroshima issue,” but for a living map of current work. Publish disagreements about translation choices. Include poets whose work is abrasive, comic, obscene, abstract, political, sentimental, anti-poetic. Let Japan appear not as a mood but as a field.

Readers can change too. We should mistrust our pleasure when a foreign poem confirms too perfectly what we hoped to find. We should ask what is being left out. We should learn enough history to be uneasy and enough humility to know when we need help. Above all, we should stop praising translated work for its “authenticity” as if authenticity were a fragrance released from distant suffering.

Araki Yasusada does not show us Japan. He shows us a mirror angled toward our desire for Japan. The next step is to turn from the mirror to the library, the archive, the living poet, the difficult conversation.

"While the Yasusada hoax successfully exposed the American literary establishment's problematic desire for 'authentic' victims, it remains an ethically troubling act of counterfeit witness rather than a harmless literary mask."

Marjorie Perloff is right to insist that the Yasusada affair is more interesting than a simple scandal of gullible editors and one mischievous poet. The manuscripts were not merely “believed”; they were desired. They arrived bearing precisely the credentials most likely to disarm skeptical reading in the present climate: avant-garde difficulty, documentary apparatus, cross-cultural authority, and the aura of historical suffering. The invented poet was not just Japanese, not just a Hiroshima survivor, not just an undiscovered modernist, not just a precursor of Language poetry, not just a Buddhist-inflected anti-authorial figure. He was all of these at once. That combination should have made him suspect. Instead, it made him irresistible.

But Perloff’s essay, for all its brilliance, is too quick to assimilate the Yasusada materials to a venerable history of literary imposture. Ossian, Propertius, Marichiko, heteronyms, masks, invented translators: literature has always thrived on such devices. Yet there remains a difference between inventing a poetic speaker and forging the testimony of a historical wound. The issue is not that Hiroshima is too sacred for artifice. No event is beyond the reach of art, parody, indirection, or even grotesque comedy. The issue is that the Yasusada apparatus asks readers to grant moral and evidentiary authority to a nonexistent victim. It does not merely say, “Imagine such a person.” It says, “Here are his notebooks; here are his translators; here is his family history; here is the ash of the dead child.” That is not simply pseudonymity. It is counterfeit witness.

Perloff is surely correct that the texts themselves contain many clues: the impossible dates for Hiroshima University, the premature Barthes and Celan, the absurd Spicer chronology, the odd Japanese names, the suspiciously convenient Howard McCord. These are not minor slips. They are flashing signals. Yet to say that the author “wanted his readers to find something perplexing” does not absolve the deception. A forged banknote may contain a private joke in the serial number; it is still designed to circulate as currency. The Yasusada poems circulated in journals not as fiction, not as conceptual art, not as an openly unstable archive, but as translated work by a dead Japanese poet. Their reception depended on that claim. Had the same poems arrived under the name Kent Johnson, many editors might have found them clever, even moving, but they would not have carried the same charge.

This is why the question of quality cannot be separated from the question of attribution. Some defenders of the project seem tempted to say: if the poems moved us before exposure, why should they move us less after? The answer is that poems are not autonomous verbal machines, however much certain avant-garde theories may wish them to be. Context is not an ornament added after the fact; it is part of how meaning is made. “Telescope with Urn” is a different poem if it is read as the elliptical mourning of a Hiroshima father, as opposed to an American poet’s construction of such mourning. “Mad Daughter and Big-Bang” is a different poem if its surreal horror is grounded in remembered loss, rather than in an imaginative appropriation of someone else’s catastrophe. The language has not changed, but the ethical relation has.

At the same time, the outrage of editors who published the work should not be allowed to become self-exculpating theater. Perloff’s most persuasive pages concern the conditions that made the hoax successful. American literary culture has often preferred its Japans usable: Zen Japan, haiku Japan, courtly Japan, victim Japan, erotic Japan, minimalist Japan. Contemporary Japan—urban, consumerist, sexually candid, technologically saturated, formally various, politically uneasy—has been less welcome because it does not flatter American expectations. A living Japanese poet writing about noodles, telephones, bodies, boredom, jokes, and metropolitan estrangement is harder to consume as spiritual otherness than a dead Hiroshima survivor arranging flowers beside an urn.

That neglect is not accidental. It reflects a larger habit in American multicultural reception: we often want the other to arrive already marked by suffering, distance, and moral legibility. The foreign poet is most easily honored when he or she can be made to testify against some recognizable evil: war, dictatorship, colonialism, patriarchy, censorship. Such testimony matters, of course. But when suffering becomes the passport to translation, whole literatures are reduced to wounds. Perloff is right that the real scandal is not only that Yasusada was invented, but that actual contemporary Japanese poets have been so little read in the same venues that embraced him.

Still, I am uneasy with Perloff’s suggestion that Johnson has performed “an invaluable service.” Perhaps he has, in the limited sense that a successful fraud exposes a market’s vanity. But one may expose vanity by exploiting it, and exploitation is not thereby transfigured into critique. If American editors crave authentic others, the cure is not to feed them a synthetic hibakusha. The cure is to translate more carefully, edit more responsibly, consult more widely, and read beyond the categories that confirm our innocence. A hoax can reveal a disease, but it is not medicine.

Nor should we confuse skepticism about authenticity with indifference to truth. The lesson of Foucault’s “author function” is not that authors do not matter, nor that every attribution is merely a game. It is that names organize reading, authority, circulation, and value. Precisely. That is why the name “Araki Yasusada” mattered. It organized the poems as testimony, as translation, as recovered archive, as Hiroshima memory. To invent that name, attach it to fabricated bereavement, and submit it to journals as fact is to manipulate the author function while relying on its power.

The best outcome of the Yasusada affair would not be a retreat into naïve authenticity, as if every poem must be certified by passport, bloodline, and trauma. Nor should it be a cynical celebration of imposture. The better response is a more demanding cosmopolitanism. We should be willing to read across cultures without requiring that foreign writers perform our fantasies of depth, purity, victimhood, or sacred difference. We should also be willing to read invented personae as invented personae. Masks are legitimate. Forged graves are another matter.

Yasusada may endure as a troubling literary artifact: sometimes beautiful, sometimes kitschy, often cunning, and inseparable from the deception that gave it force. But if the affair sends readers back to Toge Sankichi, Hara Tamiki, Ota Yoko, Shiraishi Kazuko, Tanikawa Shuntaro, Ito Hiromi, and other Japanese writers not yet sufficiently present in American magazines, then some good may come of it. The authentic other was never hidden in Johnson’s notebooks. The authentic others were already writing. We failed to look.

"The uncritical reception of the Araki Yasusada hoax reveals that Western literary circles prefer a projected, aestheticized version of the 'authentic other' over the complex reality of contemporary Japanese culture."

Marjorie Perloff is right to insist that the Yasusada affair is not chiefly a police matter. “Criminal act” is a grandiose phrase for a literary deception, particularly in a field where little-magazine checks are more symbolic than remunerative. But she is equally right that the affair is not harmless. The most interesting scandal here is not that a poet may have invented a poet. It is that so many sophisticated readers recognized, in this fabricated Hiroshima survivor, exactly the kind of foreignness they were already prepared to admire.

That is the force of Perloff’s essay. The “hoax” worked not because it was seamless but because its seams were attractive. Hiroshima University before its founding, Barthes’s Empire of Signs before publication, Paul Celan before Celan, Jack Spicer as a Hiroshima avant-garde influence, “scubadivers” in a 1925 haiku: these are not small mistakes hidden in archival dust. They are glaring anachronisms. Yet they did not trouble many of the journals that published the work. Why? Because the desired figure was too powerful: the wounded Japanese witness who is also, miraculously, a precursor of American postmodernism.

Yasusada arrived bearing all the right credentials. He was marginal, traumatized, non-Western, archival, fragmentary, Buddhist-adjacent, and avant-garde. He had lost wife and child at Hiroshima but did not accuse the American reader too directly. He suffered in a way that could be contemplated aesthetically. His poems were oblique enough to flatter experimental taste, documentary enough to flatter historical conscience, and “Japanese” enough to satisfy an old Orientalist appetite for reticence, ritual, and luminous incompletion. The result is less a fraud imposed upon innocent editors than a collaboration between maker and audience.

Perloff’s most damaging point is her comparison between Yasusada and actual contemporary Japanese poetry. American poetry magazines have shown themselves ready to publish medieval court women, Zen masters, and hibakusha testimony, but largely uninterested in living Japanese poets writing in colloquial, irreverent, urban, sexual, postwar idioms. This selectiveness is revealing. The West will receive Japan as antiquity, as spiritual corrective, or as victim. It is less eager to receive Japan as competitor, mirror, or equal participant in late-capitalist modernity.

This is an old habit with new vocabulary. Nineteenth-century Orientalism imagined the East as timeless, decorative, profound, sensual, passive. Late-twentieth-century multiculturalism, at its worst, can preserve the same structure while changing the moral tone. The exotic other is no longer inferior; indeed, he may be morally privileged. But he remains an object of Western projection. The Japanese poet must bring us either Zen emptiness or atomic sorrow. If he brings jokes about noodles, penises, television, bureaucracy, or literary ambition, he risks losing his assigned aura.

Yasusada’s invented biography solves a problem for American readers: it makes the experimental poem ethically weighty without making it politically difficult. The Hiroshima background guarantees seriousness. But the poems do not require sustained inquiry into Japanese militarism, American strategic calculations, civilian bombing, imperial war, racism, memory, defeat, occupation, or nuclear policy. The reader may feel sorrow without being forced into thought. That is why the elegiac indirection is so effective. It is Hiroshima cleansed of argument.

This does not mean that all art about atrocity must be documentary, realist, or politically explicit. Perloff is correct to resist that demand. Toge Sankichi’s direct witness poems and Johnson-Yasusada’s surreal elegies are different projects; one cannot simply legislate one mode as morally permissible and another as forbidden. Art has always approached catastrophe by indirection, displacement, allegory, nightmare, and even comedy. The problem is not that “Mad Daughter and Big-Bang” is surreal. The problem is that its authority was borrowed from a dead survivor who never existed.

Still, one should be careful about the word “appropriation.” Literature is full of masks, ventriloquism, pseudonyms, translations that are inventions, “found” manuscripts that were never found. Pessoa’s heteronyms, Macpherson’s Ossian, Chatterton’s Rowley poems, Mérimée’s La Guzla, Rexroth’s Marichiko, and Pound’s Propertius all belong to this complicated tradition. But traditions do not absolve judgment. A mask made from a generalized type is one thing; a mask made from the face of historical suffering is another. Yasusada is not merely “a Japanese poet.” He is a Hiroshima survivor whose wife and daughter died in the bombing. That fact was used to solicit trust.

What should editors have done? Not much that is exotic or censorious: they should have checked. If a manuscript purports to be translated from Japanese, someone competent in Japanese literature should be consulted. If it claims a biographical relation to Hiroshima, dates and institutions should be verified. If its archive is strangely convenient, suspicion should not be treated as philistinism. Little magazines often pride themselves on being more adventurous than commercial publishers, but adventurousness need not mean credulity.

The more painful lesson concerns prestige. The Yasusada poems circulated through a network in which names, references, and affiliations mattered: Spicer, Barthes, renga, Silliman, Language writing, Zen, Hiroshima. Their reception suggests that the avant-garde is no less vulnerable than the mainstream to formulas of legitimacy. It merely has different formulas. A conventional magazine might have wanted a plainspoken survivor lyric; an experimental magazine wanted a survivor who anticipated the new sentence. Both desires can be provincial.

Perloff concludes that Yasusada may perform a service by sending us back to the originals: to actual hibakusha writing, actual renga, actual contemporary Japanese poets, actual postwar debates. That is the most generous possible outcome. But it will happen only if editors and readers resist the temptation to turn the affair into a flattering parable about their own sophistication. The lesson is not “we were fooled by a brilliant postmodern artwork.” It is “we were willing to be fooled because the fake confirmed our expectations.”

The authentic other is often the other we have already authored. Yasusada’s greatest poem may be the exposure of that authorship.

**A Japanese Reader's Note**

I write as someone who has spent his working life inside contemporary Japanese poetry, and I want to offer a perspective somewhat different from the framing Marjorie Perloff gives the Yasusada case. Perloff's essay is admirable in many respects—she has read more contemporary Japanese poets than nearly any of the American editors involved in this affair, and her insistence that Tanikawa Shuntaro, Shiraishi Kazuko, and Ito Hiromi exist and ought to be translated is one I share. But I want to register what the Yasusada hoax looks like from the side of the culture being impersonated, because that perspective is largely missing from the American debate.

First, the elementary errors. Perloff has catalogued some of them—"Araki" used as a given name, "Nomura" used as a first name, "Motokiyo" misspelled as "Motokiyu," "Ogyu" as "Ojiu," a Hiroshima University that did not yet exist, a Paul Celan who was a child in the years he was supposedly being read by the *Soun* group. To this list I would add other things that would strike any Japanese reader as wrong immediately. The casual deployment of "fucking" as intensifier in a putative 1930s renga is not merely anachronistic in English translation; there is no register in Japanese of that era that corresponds to it. The notion that prewar avant-garde renga groups would have engaged in the kind of programmatic theorizing Yasusada's "translators" attribute to them—the talk of "flat linking," "mosaic linking," "linking through paragram"—reflects a contemporary American Language-poetry sensibility imposed retroactively onto a tradition that did not work that way.

Above all, the Japanese poetic field of the 1920s and 30s did not look like Yasusada's. It was dominated by debates between modernism and proletarian poetry, between Anzai Fuyue and Kitagawa Fuyuhiko and the *Shi to shiron* circle on one side and the Marxists on the other, with figures like Hagiwara Sakutaro and Miyazawa Kenji occupying their distinctive ground. A serious avant-garde Hiroshima poet of the period would have a relationship to those debates. Yasusada has no relationship to them. He floats free of his ostensible context, available only to influences—Spicer, Barthes, Celan—that locate him precisely where the contemporary American reader would like him to be.

This is the deepest problem, and it is the one Perloff sees most clearly. Johnson did not invent a Japanese poet. He invented an American fantasy of a Japanese poet, and then attached to that fantasy enough biographical pathos to deter close scrutiny. John Solt, quoted in *Lingua Franca*, was not wrong to call this "Japanized crap." His diagnosis was perhaps too brisk, but it pointed at something real: the construction begins not from a knowledge of Japan but from a set of American expectations about what Japan should sound like.

I want, however, to complicate one strand of Perloff's argument. She suggests that contemporary Japanese poets have been reluctant to write about Hiroshima and Nagasaki because the subject forces an unbearable confrontation with the question of Japanese subjectivity—are we descendants of the bombed or of the bombers? My friend Nagahata Akitoshi, whom Perloff quotes, puts this elegantly. But the situation is more layered than even his careful formulation suggests. There has in fact been a continuous, if often quieter, engagement with the *genbaku* (atomic bomb) experience in Japanese poetry across the postwar period, including in work by poets too young to have witnessed it. What has changed is the rhetorical mode. Direct testimony, in the manner of Toge Sankichi, gave way to oblique, often allegorical treatments in figures like Ishigaki Rin or Ibaragi Noriko. The American demand for hibakusha testimony in its rawest form has been met increasingly by translations of an older generation, while the younger work has been less exportable because it is less recognizably "about" the bomb in the way American editors wish.

This is the cultural transaction Yasusada exploits. He provides what the older testimonial tradition provides—the survivor's voice—while also providing what the younger experimental tradition provides—the ellipsis, the surreal image, the elegant restraint. It is a chimera composed of features the American market wants but cannot easily find combined in a single real Japanese poet, because no real Japanese poet has the biography Johnson constructed for Yasusada. That is the diagnostic value of the fabrication, and Perloff is right to insist on it.

Where I differ from her is in the moral weighing. Perloff is too quick, I think, to dismiss the editorial reaction as panicked overreaction. Calling the submission a "criminal act" may be overheated, but the queasiness the Wesleyan reader felt was not foolish. It registered something important: that to impersonate, in the first person, a victim of mass atrocity is not the same kind of literary gesture as to write under a pseudonym. The pseudonym hides the author's identity. The impersonation appropriates someone else's. The line between them is not absolute, but it is real, and it is worth defending.

That said, I do not want this affair to become a referendum on Kent Johnson alone. The more serious project—the one Perloff begins and that I hope others will continue—is to ask why American poetry journals find a fictional Hiroshima survivor easier to publish than a living, working Tokyo or Osaka poet. That is the question with which Japanese readers of this debate will mainly be left. The answer will not be flattering to American editorial culture, and it will not be remedied by withdrawing Yasusada's check.

**The Authenticity Trap**

What strikes me most forcefully in Marjorie Perloff's account of the Yasusada affair is not the cleverness of Kent Johnson nor the gullibility of the editors but the strangeness of the standard against which both are being measured. The complaint against Johnson is, fundamentally, that he produced moving poems while occupying the wrong position—that the poems' value depended on a biographical fact that turned out not to obtain, and that without the fact the poems lose whatever they had. The complaint against the editors is that they failed to verify the fact before publishing. Both complaints rest on the same premise: that the position of the author is constitutive of the work's worth.

This premise is now so naturalized in literary culture that even its critics tend to argue within it rather than against it. Perloff comes closer than most to questioning it. She notes the irony that a literary culture officially committed to the Foucauldian critique of the author has produced an unprecedented hunger for biographical authentication, and she suggests, rightly, that the two phenomena are connected: that the dismissal of "excellence" as essentialist creates a vacuum into which positionality rushes as the new criterion of merit. But she does not, I think, quite take the measure of how peculiar the resulting situation is.

Consider what the editors of *American Poetry Review* are effectively saying when they demand the return of Johnson's payment. They are saying: the poems we published, which we found good enough to publish under the impression they were written by a hibakusha, are not in fact good enough to publish under the impression they were written by an Illinois professor. The poems have not changed. Their language, imagery, line breaks, ironies, surreal turns—all of these are identical to what they were when the journal accepted them. What has changed is a fact about the author. And that fact, apparently, is determinative of whether the work merits publication.

If this is the criterion, then the work was never being evaluated as poetry. It was being evaluated as testimony, with poetic features as a kind of aesthetic surcharge. That is an honorable thing to publish, but it is not what the editors of *APR* or *Conjunctions* or *Grand Street* present themselves as publishing. They present themselves as publishing literature. The Yasusada affair has shown, perhaps inadvertently, that for at least some kinds of submission they are not in fact doing so. They are publishing biographical credentials with poems attached.

Perloff herself acknowledges that the hoax’s clues were “surely not unintentional,” but she stops short of fully crediting the critical work the Yasusada texts perform. She finds the “New Sentence” genealogy dubious because it appropriates a Japanese form without acknowledging Language poetry’s own cultural specificity. However, the fakery of the renga essay in *Aerial*—with its suspiciously fluent quotation of Earl Miner and its claim that Spicer influenced Yasusada in the 1930s—is so over-the-top that it reads as a reflexive joke on the American avant-garde’s habit of claiming Eastern precedents to bolster its innovations. The editors may have been complicit in the game, not just dupes. Perloff’s insistence that they should have consulted “bona fide Japanese poets” assumes that the work’s value hinges on its ethnographic accuracy, rather than on its intervention in American poetry politics.

The real scandal of the Yasusada affair is not that a white poet pretended to be a Japanese survivor, but that the literary community’s protocols of authentication are so selectively applied. As Perloff reminds us, Rexroth’s *Marichiko* was an admitted fabrication, yet it remains in print and is widely taught. The outcry over Yasusada, including calls of “criminal act,” reveals a new anxiety about cultural appropriation at a time when postcolonial theory has made that anxiety palpable. But the hoax also exposes the limits of that anxiety: it is easier to denounce a fraudulent position than to examine the structures that make the position of the victim so valuable. Perloff’s own conclusion, that we need to “look searchingly and critically at what is always already there,” is precisely the activity the Yasusada “notebooks” provoke. They force us to notice how much we project onto the image of a Hiroshima poet, and how our standards of authenticity are themselves constructed by a tradition that is far from innocent. The hoax, in other words, is not the problem; it is a diagnostic tool. By mistaking the symptom for the disease, Perloff ends up longing for a genuine encounter with the Other that her own logic shows to be impossible.

"The Araki Yasusada hoax succeeded because it exploited a contemporary literary culture that prioritizes the performance of multicultural diversity over genuine, effortful cross-cultural engagement."

I approach the Yasusada affair from the perspective of literary history, and I find in it a remarkable repetition of patterns established two centuries ago. James McPherson's Ossian poems, published in the 1760s, were accepted as translations of ancient Gaelic poetry for decades before being definitively exposed as fabrications. They succeeded because they met the needs of a particular cultural moment: the Scottish Enlightenment required a national poet comparable to Homer, and McPherson supplied one. The hoax worked because the culture wanted it to work.

The same pattern recurs throughout literary history. Samuel Johnson fabricated the life of Richard Smith to provide a suitable author for a set of political verses. Mark Twain invented a German philosopher to satirize German pedantry. Diderot wrote as if he were a naive woman reflecting on his own Encyclopédie. The list is long and its lesson is clear: literary hoaxes succeed when they satisfy desires that the culture does not wish to examine.

Kent Johnson's Yasusada fits this tradition. The contemporary literary culture desires multicultural diversity but has limited mechanisms for achieving it. Real cross-cultural exchange is difficult: it requires knowledge of languages, extended contact with living traditions, tolerance for difference. Johnson's invented poet provided an easier path: diversity without scholarship, authenticity without verification, engagement without effort.

The difference between Johnson and his predecessors is the context of multiculturalism. McPherson was creating a national tradition; his fraud served the construction of Scottish identity. Johnson was deconstructing multicultural desire; his fraud exposed the hollowness of the diversity being sought. Whether he intended this deconstruction is unclear — he has not spoken candidly about his motives — but the effect is the same.

Consider what happened after the exposure. Editors expressed outrage at having been deceived. The APR editors called the hoax a "criminal act" and published an apology to their readers. Wesleyan University Press dropped its plan to publish a volume of Yasusada poems. These responses are revealing: the editors were not grateful for having their assumptions challenged; they were indignant at having been made to look foolish.

This is the typical response to hoaxes that target cultural pieties. The exposed fraud becomes an occasion for reaffirming the values that the fraud exploited. The editors were not Multiculuralists who happened to be fooled; they were people who performed multicultural concern for career reasons, and they were angry at having that performance disrupted. Johnson had made visible the gap between their stated values and their actual practices.

In this reading, the hoax was a success even before its exposure. By allowing the literary establishment to publish "Japanese" poetry without doing any actual work, Johnson revealed how little genuine multicultural engagement means to the people who claim to champion it. His subsequent refusal to admit the hoax clearly — his suggestion that Tosa Motokiyu is a pseudonym for yet another, safely dead poet — extends the provocation. The establishment wanted a confession and an apology; Johnson offered only ambiguity.

I do not mean to romanticize Johnson or his hoax. The invention of a hibakusha survivor is ethically troubling in ways that the invention of an ancient Scottish bard is not. But the historical framing helps us see what is at stake: not just literary fraud but the credibility of multicultural commitments in a literary culture that talks a great deal and learns very little.

"The literary community's outrage over the Araki Yasusada hoax reveals a hypocritical reliance on biographical authenticity that contradicts the avant-garde principle of judging a text based on its own aesthetic merits."

If the controversy over Araki Yasusada teaches us anything, it is that the biographical imperative is astonishingly difficult to dislodge, even among those who profess to have bid it farewell. The editors who published the Yasusada poems and the critics who now denounce the hoax share a single assumption that ought to be questioned: that the value of these works is inextricably tied to the identity and history of their purported author. What if we simply refused that assumption? What if we judged the poems themselves as poems—texts whose origin matters no more than the geological provenance of marble matters to the quality of a sculpture? On that basis, the Yasusada affair would look less like a scandal and more like a dispiriting demonstration of bad faith.

Marjorie Perloff’s lead essay ably traces the “clues” that, according to her, make the hoax obvious. She catalogs the anachronisms with quasi‑Poundian glee, and she charges the editors of Conjunctions, Grand Street, and APR with an inexcusable failure to consult actual Japanese specialists. But notice the underlying logic: the editors should have known the poems were fake, she implies, because the biographical details didn’t add up. That is, the poems’ right to exist in those pages depends on the correct background check of the name attached to them. This is a startling position for a critic of Perloff’s sophistication, who in other contexts has been a champion of the “text as such.” If Ron Silliman read “Telescope with Urn” and found it compelling—its rhythms, its silences, its startling juxtaposition of the cosmic and the intimate—why should it matter to his judgment that Araki Yasusada never touched a real pen? Silliman’s aesthetic response, as Perloff reports it, was immediate and visceral: the poem “takes my breath away,” it kept him awake. That is an authentic transaction between reader and text, and no amount of genetic criticism can retroactively annul it.

The counterargument, of course, is that the poems derived much of their force from the assumed context of a Hiroshima survivor’s witness. But this is both empirically questionable and theoretically incoherent. Empirically, many readers—and Silliman evidently one of them—were drawn to the syntactical strangeness, the odd lineation, the queer mingling of haiku‑like compression with demotic sexual frankness. Those are formal qualities, and they survive the unmasking. Theoretically, if we accept the poststructuralist axiom that meaning is produced in the act of reading, not injected by the author’s biography, then the “Hiroshima” frame is merely one possible horizon of interpretation a reader may bring to the text. To insist that the poems become worthless once the frame is revealed as fabricated is to revert to the very intentionalist dogma that the avant‑garde has spent decades dismantling. It is to say, in effect, that a poem is good only if the suffering behind it is certified authentic—a kind of literary fair‑trade labeling.

The editors’ supposed naïveté is also less scandalous than it appears. Rod Smith of Aerial, Perloff notes in a footnote, suspected the manuscript was a hoax all along but found it “charming and apropos” and decided to publish it. This is hardly the reaction of a gullible dupe; it is the decision of an editor who values textual play over forensic verification. If the poems were “apropos”—that is, if they fit the aesthetic and dialogic context of the journal—then Smith’s choice was editorially defensible. The subsequent outcry, with talk of “criminal act,” has the flavor of an institutional face‑saving operation: caught enjoying a literary fiction without the proper authorial pedigree, the gatekeepers have to perform outrage to reassert their authority.

None of this is to dismiss the genuine ethical qualms surrounding the invention of a Hiroshima survivor persona. That discussion, however, belongs to a different register—one of social responsibility and historical trauma—and it does not automatically trump the aesthetic autonomy of the work. What the Yasusada case reveals is a widespread, largely unacknowledged literary hypocrisy. We pay lip service to the death of the author, but we are not prepared to live with the consequences when a death turns out to have been faked as well. The hoax is not a criminal act; it is a mirror held up to a community that cannot decide whether it believes in texts or in saints.

"The enthusiastic reception of the Araki Yasusada hoax reveals a shameful tendency in the contemporary literary establishment to prioritize the performance of multicultural authenticity and victimhood over critical vigilance and historical accuracy."

Marjorie Perloff has done something invaluable with her dissection of the Yasusada affair: she has forced us to ask whether we read poems or read positions. The question is not whether Kent Johnson's invented poet constitutes a technical "hoax" — clearly it does — but whether the literary community's enthusiastic reception of Yasusada reveals something shameful about contemporary poetry politics. I believe it does, and Perloff's essay is courageously honest in exposing this shame.

Consider the sequence of events: a manuscript arrives with Japanese names, references to Hiroshima, claims of avant-garde affiliations with American poets. Editors at prestigious journals — Conjunctions, American Poetry Review, Grand Street, Aerial — respond with near-unanimous enthusiasm. These are not naive readers. Bradford Morrow is an Ezra Pound scholar. Ron Silliman is one of the most sophisticated poet-critics working in American letters. Yet none of them bothered to check the most basic factual claims. Hiroshima University did not exist until 1949. Barthes's Empire of Signs was not published until 1970. Celan began publishing in 1952. These are not obscure facts; a moment's research would have exposed the fraud.

Why the failure? Perloff's answer is compelling: the literary establishment has developed an appetite for multicultural authenticity that overrides ordinary critical vigilance. When a manuscript promises to deliver an "authentic" Hiroshima survivor — a tragic figure who combines avant-garde sophistication with victimhood — who would want to be the skeptic? Who would want to question whether this rare and valuable voice might actually be real? The desire for the authentic Other proved stronger than the obligation to verify.

The tragedy is compounded by the actual richness of contemporary Japanese poetry, which Perloff carefully documents. Tanikawa Shuntaro, Ito Hiromi, Shiraishi Kazuko — these poets are producing brilliant, often hilarious, always technically sophisticated work. Yet American journals show no interest. Why? Because postmodern Japan is too similar to postmodern America. It won't permit the comfortable fantasy of an exotic, spiritual, other-worldly Orient. The real Japan is too close to home.

Kent Johnson understood this dynamic perfectly. His Yasusada is not a realistic simulation of a Japanese poet; it is a perfect simulation of the American literary establishment's fantasy of a Japanese poet. The "mistakes" in the manuscript — the scubadivers in haiku, the anachronistic terminology — are not failures of craft but intentional clues. Johnson was offering his readers a mirror: here is what you want to see when you look at Japan. The editors who praised these poems were praising their own projections, their own comfortable Orientalism.

The question then becomes: what is the appropriate response? Perloff is right that calling this a "criminal act" misses the mark. Pseudonymity has a long and honorable history, from Ossian to the West-East Divan. But there is something different at stake here. Johnson did not simply publish under a pseudonym; he invented an entire cultural identity and presented it as real. He created a victim whose tragedy could be appropriated for literary cachet. The Hiroshihara survivor is not a persona like Pound's Propertius; the Hiroshima bombing is not a literary tradition to be playfully translated.

Perhaps Johnson's best defense is that he exposed his readers' prejudices more effectively than any critical essay could. Perhaps the hoax was a kind of guerrilla warfare against multicultural condescension. But this defense only works if we believe that exposing prejudice is more important than respecting the specificity of historical suffering. I am not convinced it is. The bomb that destroyed Hiroshima killed real people with real names. Inventing a poet who survived that bomb and then presenting his work as authentic reduces those deaths to literary raw material. That seems to me a genuine moral failure, whatever the satirical intentions.

"While Marjorie Perloff correctly identifies the Yasusada hoax as a brilliant exposure of the American literary establishment's Orientalist and theoretical vanities, she wrongly dismisses the profound ethical transgression of appropriating the specific, catastrophic trauma of Hiroshima survivors for an avant-garde stunt."

Marjorie Perloff’s dissection of the Araki Yasusada hoax is a masterful exposure of the American literary avant-garde's theoretical vanities. She correctly identifies that the breathless reception of Yasusada’s poetry by editors and poets—who certainly should have known better—was driven by a hunger for an "authentic other" that conveniently mirrored their own aesthetic and ideological prejudices. By exposing how the American poetry establishment ignored glaring anachronisms in their eagerness to champion a marginalized Hiroshima survivor, Perloff holds up a necessary mirror to the inadvertent Orientalism of contemporary multiculturalism. However, in her admiration for the hoax's diagnostic brilliance, Perloff is too quick to dismiss the profound ethical transgression of appropriating the specific, catastrophic trauma of August 6, 1945.

Perloff is entirely convincing in her demonstration of why poets like Ron Silliman and editors at American Poetry Review fell for the ruse. Yasusada was the perfect, custom-built phantom for the post-structuralist 1990s. As she notes, he satisfied the Foucauldian demand for the dissolution of the authorial ego, mapped seamlessly onto the "New Sentence," and obligingly cited Jack Spicer and Roland Barthes. The editors did not discover a Japanese master; they discovered a reflection of their own graduate seminars. Furthermore, Perloff rightly castigates the publishing world’s indifference to actual, living Japanese poets—like Tanikawa Shuntaro or Ito Hiromi—whose urbane, cosmopolitan verses refuse to flatter the Western desire for Zen-inflected suffering. Our institutions too often seek victims to validate our theoretical paradigms, rather than seeking equals who might challenge them.

Where Perloff’s argument falters, however, is in her breezy exoneration of the hoax’s author. She calls the condemnation of the manuscript as a "criminal act" absurd, likening Yasusada to James Macpherson’s Ossian or Ezra Pound’s Sextus Propertius. But this comparison collapses under the weight of history. Macpherson fabricated a Celtic bard from the mists of a mythic past; Pound translated and ventriloquized an ancient Roman whose empire had long since crumbled. Kent Johnson—or whoever is ultimately pulling the strings—appropriated the very real, flesh-and-blood tragedy of a nuclear holocaust that occurred barely half a century ago. To invent a daughter who dies of radiation sickness, to write poems about her ashes in a crane-decorated urn, is not merely a "time-honored device in literature." It is a colonization of another people's historical trauma for the sake of an avant-garde parlor trick. The horror of Hiroshima is not a mythic playground, and the proximity of the event demands a moral gravity that the hoax format inherently violates.

Perloff points to the deliberate anachronisms in the text—the references to scuba divers, crematoriums, and impossible publication dates—as evidence of the author's brilliant, postmodern intentionality. She views these "clinamens" as proof that the author wanted to problematize representation and leave a trail of breadcrumbs for the astute reader. But viewed through an ethical lens, these clues look less like postmodern brilliance and more like moral cowardice. They serve as an alibi. By inserting these winks to the reader, the author attempts to inoculate himself against the charge of pure exploitation. It allows him to dabble in the gravitas of atomic devastation while retaining a smirk, reserving the right to say, "I was only joking; look at the scuba divers." This irony does not temper the guilt, as Perloff suggests; rather, it trivializes the immense suffering it mimics.

Perloff is right that the Yasusada affair forces us to look critically at the way the American literary market fetishizes trauma and exoticizes the non-Western world. The hoax succeeded because it gave guilt-ridden American intellectuals exactly the forgiving, aesthetically pleasing victim they desired—one who spoke their language of fragmentation and theory. But recognizing the vanity and gullibility of the poetry establishment does not require us to celebrate the cynicism of the hoaxer. It is possible to agree with Perloff that the editors' unquestioning acceptance of Yasusada reveals a deeply flawed, Orientalist multiculturalism, while still maintaining that the impersonation of a Hiroshima survivor crosses a line of basic human decency.

As we approach the millennium, the demand for authentic voices from the margins will surely continue, and the academic obsession with "positionality" shows no signs of waning. But the solution to our uncritical reception of the Other is not to applaud those who expertly forge their signatures in the ashes of the dead. It is, as Perloff herself concludes, to do the harder work of listening to the living, unpalatable and un-exotic as they may sometimes be.
