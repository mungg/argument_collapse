Sub-arguments
  1. 01Political parties serve unique democratic functions, such as organizing government and enhancing accountability, that distinguish them from profit-driven corporations and narrow interest groups.
  2. 02The Citizens United decision and existing regulations unfairly disadvantage parties by restricting their ability to coordinate with candidates while allowing corporations to spend freely.
  3. 03Contrary to the view that party loyalty is a form of corruption, effective party unity actually provides a necessary bulwark against the influence of special interest contributions and lobbying.
  4. 04Reform efforts should include allowing unlimited coordinated expenditures for small-dollar donations and providing public financing directly to party organizations.
  5. 05Encouraging 'soft money' for non-campaign activities like voter registration and political education allows parties to perform long-term institutional roles that mitigate public passivity.
Diversified Gemini

"To combat the 'dependency corruption' identified by Lessig, reformers should focus on strengthening centralized political parties as a buffer against private wealth rather than pursuing individualized solutions like democracy vouchers."

In his essay, Lawrence Lessig eloquently diagnoses the sickness of “institutional corruption” afflicting the American Congress. His critique of the Supreme Court’s narrow, *quid pro quo* definition of corruption in *Citizens United* is legally sound and morally urgent. However, while Lessig is right about the diagnosis, his search for a cure looks in the wrong direction. By focusing entirely on the financial relationship between individual candidates and individual donors, Lessig reinforces the very dynamic that is destroying our politics: the rampant individualism of the modern American politician. If we want to insulate our representatives from the "dependency corruption" of wealthy donors and independent expenditures, we must look to the one institution historically capable of acting as a buffer between private wealth and public policy: strong, centralized political parties.

Lessig’s proposed solutions—small-dollar public matching funds and "democracy vouchers"—are built on an atomized view of democracy. He envisions a system where each individual representative is directly and independently tethered to the voters in their district via fifty-dollar checks. But this hyper-individualized model of politics is precisely what has made members of Congress so vulnerable to the influence Lessig decries. When candidates must build their own personal campaign organizations, hire their own consultants, and raise their own war chests, they become solitary entrepreneurs. And in the face of the "iceberg" threat of a multi-million-dollar corporate independent expenditure, the solitary entrepreneur is defenseless.

Consider Lessig’s example of Google threatening to spend a million dollars against a candidate. If a representative is a lone actor reliant on $50 vouchers, that threat is existential. It will almost certainly alter their behavior. But what if that representative were strongly backed by a robust national political party? If a corporation threatens a loyal party member, a strong party can retaliate, not just with money, but with legislative discipline across the board. The party can act as a shield, absorbing the financial blow and diffusing the threat across the collective strength of its caucus.

Unfortunately, for the past four decades, American political reform has been obsessed with weakening political parties. In the name of "cleaning up" politics, reformers have systematically dismantled the party apparatus. The post-Watergate reforms established individual contribution limits that drove candidates to become relentless, full-time fundraisers. The Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act (McCain-Feingold) of 2002—the very law partially struck down in *Citizens United*—banned "soft money" to political parties. While this was hailed as a great victory against corruption, political scientists at the time warned of the inevitable consequence: the money would not disappear; it would simply flow away from the accountable, transparent structure of the political parties and into the shadowy, unaccountable realm of outside interest groups.

That prediction has come true with a vengeance. By starving the parties of funds, we have outsourced the vital functions of democracy—voter mobilization, issue advocacy, and candidate defense—to single-issue advocacy groups, 527s, and now corporate-backed independent expenditure campaigns. Lessig worries about candidates becoming dependent on funders rather than voters. But the worst kind of dependency is a dependency on ideological extremists and narrow corporate interests. Historically, broad-based political parties have had to build coalitions. They must moderate their extreme factions to win general elections. A political party wants to win a majority; an independent expenditure group wants to secure a specific tax loophole or ideological purity test.

European parliamentary democracies offer a stark contrast to our current crisis. In many of these systems, campaign finance is heavily regulated, but the money that is spent is channeled almost entirely through the political parties, not individual candidates. The parties develop platforms, and voters choose the party that best aligns with their interests. Individual candidates do not spend six hours a day in call centers begging for money, because their election depends on the party's success. This system has its own flaws, but it effectively neutralizes the "dependency corruption" Lessig describes at the individual level. A lobbyist cannot easily threaten to destroy a single parliamentarian because the parliamentarian’s political survival is tied to the party machinery.

The ruling in *Citizens United* is undoubtedly a disaster, but it is a disaster largely because our political parties are too weak to manage the fallout. If we want to restore trust in Congress and break the cycle of dependency corruption, we should abandon the fantasy of a Congress composed of 535 independent, voucher-funded free agents. Such a Congress would be chaotic, undisciplined, and completely at the mercy of organized outside money.

Instead, we need a counter-reformation in campaign finance. We should lift the limits on contributions to political parties, provided those contributions are subject to immediate, absolute transparency. We should allow parties to coordinate freely with their candidates, enabling them to direct funds to defend members who are under attack by independent corporate expenditures. By strengthening the parties, we create an institution large enough, and powerful enough, to stand up to the immense aggregations of wealth that *Citizens United* has unleashed into the political ecosystem. Lessig’s goal of a Congress dependent upon the People alone is admirable, but in a world of unlimited corporate spending, the People need an organization to fight on their behalf. That organization should be the political party.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Lessig’s proposed small-dollar vouchers reinforce a hyper-individualized model of politics that leaves solitary representatives defenseless against massive corporate independent expenditures.
  2. 02Strong political parties can act as a shield for representatives, using collective legislative discipline and resources to absorb and diffuse financial threats from outside interest groups.
  3. 03Past reforms that weakened parties, such as the ban on 'soft money,' inadvertently drove political spending into the hands of unaccountable, narrow-interest outside groups.
  4. 04Broad-based political parties are naturally incentivized to build moderate coalitions and win majorities, whereas independent expenditure groups prioritize specific loopholes or ideological purity.
  5. 05Lifting contribution limits on political parties and allowing them to coordinate freely with candidates would create an institutional force powerful enough to stand up to the aggregations of wealth unleashed by Citizens United.
