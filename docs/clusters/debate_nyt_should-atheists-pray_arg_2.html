Prayer Without a Listener Is Just Talking to Yourself

The current vogue for "atheist churches" reveals something embarrassing about the New Atheist movement: having spent a decade insisting that religion is intellectually bankrupt, its adherents now find themselves lonely on Sunday mornings. The Sunday Assembly in London, and its imitators springing up in Baton Rouge and New York, are a tacit admission that Dawkins and Hitchens told only half the story. Religion isn't just a set of factual claims about the universe; it's a technology of community, ritual, and consolation. Fine. But prayer is a bridge too far.

Singing together makes obvious secular sense — humans have sung in groups since long before monotheism. Sermons are just lectures with better pacing. Potlucks are potlucks. These can be lifted out of the religious context without contradiction. Prayer cannot. Prayer is, by definition, address: it presupposes a listener. To pray to no one is not a ritual; it's a performance of belief one does not hold. It is the spiritual equivalent of writing letters to Santa as an adult — sweet, perhaps, but fundamentally confused.

Defenders will say prayer can be "reframed" as meditation, gratitude practice, or focused intention. But we already have words for those things, and they don't require the theological vocabulary. Meditation is meditation. Journaling is journaling. Calling these activities "prayer" is either rhetorical sleight of hand or nostalgia for a worldview one has supposedly outgrown. If a former Catholic finds comfort in lighting a candle and reciting words into the void, I won't begrudge them the comfort — but they should be honest that they're soothing a habit, not engaging in a coherent practice.

There's also a strategic point. The intellectual credibility of unbelief depends on its willingness to do without the consolations of belief. Atheists who borrow prayer are vulnerable to the obvious rejoinder: if the rituals are so valuable, perhaps the underlying claims deserve a second look. Better to build genuinely new secular practices — civic rituals, philosophical discussion groups, mutual-aid networks — than to wear religion's hand-me-downs and pretend they fit.

Gather, by all means. Sing. Listen to a good talk. But keep your hands unfolded and your eyes open. Honest unbelief is its own discipline, and it doesn't need to whisper to a god it has already dismissed.

"Atheists should not engage in prayer because the practice inherently requires a divine recipient, and adopting the ritual without the belief lacks intellectual and spiritual integrity."

Atheists should not pray. They should gather, sing, mourn, meditate, celebrate, forgive, repent and serve. But they should not call these activities prayer unless they are willing to smuggle into their practice the very premise they deny.

Prayer is not merely a pleasant inward feeling. It is not just taking a deep breath before a difficult conversation or feeling grateful at sunrise. Historically and linguistically, prayer is addressed. It has a “thou.” Even when wordless, it presumes that reality includes a hearer, or at least a sacred presence before whom one stands. To remove that address and keep the term is like staging a wedding with no spouse, no promise and no union, then saying the important thing was the flowers.

This does not mean atheists lack depth. On the contrary, one of the most admirable features of serious atheism is its refusal to pretend. Atheists often insist that comfort is not a sufficient reason for belief. They should apply that same rigor to ritual. If no one is listening, then petitionary prayer is false. If no one is there to receive thanksgiving, then thanksgiving should be directed toward the people, institutions and natural conditions that actually made one’s life possible.

There are many alternatives. Meditation can cultivate attention without metaphysical confusion. Ethical reflection can examine conscience. Public assemblies can build solidarity. Music can stir courage. Eulogies can honor the dead. Silence can make room for grief. None of these requires the vocabulary of prayer.

Believers, too, should resist reducing prayer to therapy. Prayer may bring psychological benefits, but that is not its essence. For Jews, Christians, Muslims and many others, prayer is an encounter with God: praise, confession, lament, intercession and surrender. It is not primarily a self-improvement technique. It can be dry, difficult, even apparently unanswered. Its meaning depends on its truth.

The fashion for atheist church services reflects a real social problem. Secular modernity can be lonely. People miss singing with others; they miss rites of passage; they miss a moral community larger than consumer preference. But borrowing the outer form of religion while denying its inner claim risks producing a thin imitation of both religion and unbelief.

Atheists should build institutions worthy of their convictions. Let them have assemblies, songs, lectures and acts of service. Let them cultivate gratitude and awe. But let prayer remain what it is: speech, silence or longing directed toward God. Integrity is better than imitation.

A Believer's View: Please Don't

Let me speak as a practicing Christian who finds the spectacle of "atheist churches" oddly touching and the proposal of atheist prayer oddly insulting — to both sides.

Prayer, in the tradition I inhabit, is not a self-help exercise. It is the soul's response to being addressed first. When I pray, I am not generating a mood or rehearsing my values; I am answering a Person who, I believe, has spoken. Take away that conviction and what remains is not prayer but its hollow shell — the posture without the encounter. To call that "prayer" is to misunderstand the word so thoroughly that the misunderstanding becomes its own kind of statement.

I don't say this to gatekeep. If atheists want quiet reflection, gratitude journals, group singing, ethical exhortation — wonderful. Humanity has always needed these, and the secular world's recent rediscovery that community and ritual matter is a welcome correction to a century of liberal individualism. But there are perfectly good secular names for these activities: meditation, contemplation, civic ceremony, mutual encouragement. Borrowing the word "prayer" muddies what each tradition actually claims.

It also does atheists a disservice. The intellectually serious atheist position — the one I disagree with but respect — holds that the universe is finally indifferent, that we are alone in a meaningful sense, and that meaning must be constructed by us rather than received from above. That is a hard view to live by, and its dignity lies in its refusal of consolations it considers false. When atheists fold their hands and address the ceiling, they trade away that dignity for a feeling. Pascal would have smiled; Nietzsche would have wept.

What I would say to my unbelieving neighbors is this: take ritual seriously, take community seriously, take silence seriously. These are human goods, not religious property. But if you find yourself reaching, in the silence, for Someone — pay attention to that reaching. Don't paper it over with the word "prayer" used in scare quotes. Either follow it where it leads, or be honest that you've decided it leads nowhere. The middle path — atheist prayer — strikes me as the worst of both worlds: too much religion for the skeptic, too little for the seeker.

  3. 03Adopting prayer undermines the dignity of the atheist position, which is rooted in the honest acceptance of a universe without divine consolation.
  4. 04Atheist prayer represents a compromise that fails to satisfy either the requirements of skeptical logic or the needs of genuine spiritual seeking.

"Atheists should not engage in prayer because it is an intellectually dishonest practice that relies on supernatural connotations incompatible with a secular worldview."

Atheists have no business praying. The word “prayer” is not a neutral term we can casually repurpose; it inherently involves addressing a transcendent being or power. For an atheist to adopt the posture and language of prayer is not only intellectually dishonest but also ethically dubious. It blurs the distinction between reason and superstition at a time when clear thinking is desperately needed. The recent atheist “church” services, with their borrowed rituals, illustrate a misguided nostalgia for religious form without content. Collective singing, inspirational talks, and moments of silence can all be valuable, but let’s call those what they are: meditation, reflection, or shared contemplation. To call them “prayer” is to smuggle in the connotation of a listener who isn’t there, and that leads to a muddled worldview.

If I bow my head and “pray” for a sick friend, what am I doing? If there’s no one to hear, it’s merely a wish. But framing it as prayer may give me a false sense of having done something effectual when I should be offering tangible help. The psychological benefits that proponents claim for prayer are well-attested for meditation and mindfulness, which carry no supernatural baggage. So there is simply no need to co-opt a loaded religious term. Moreover, using “prayer” can be offensive to believers who see it as a sacred act of communion with God; it trivializes their practice. Atheism is a positive worldview that embraces naturalism and evidence. We should be creating robust secular alternatives to religious rituals, not retreading them with half-hearted theistic language.

The point of prayer for believers is to seek divine guidance, forgiveness, or intervention. For atheists to mimic this is to engage in a category error. We might as well “baptize” babies into the church of reason using water and words. It’s an unnecessary concession to supernatural thinking. Atheists should build a vocabulary of self-reflection that honestly owns its secular nature. In short, atheists should not pray—they should think, meditate, and act, but leave prayer to those who believe.

The notion of atheists congregating for worship strikes most believers as a category error, and they're largely right. What these gatherings call "prayer" or "sermons" in a secular context is essentially cosplay — borrowing the theatrical apparatus of religion without its substance. The Louisiana and New York gatherings, often dubbed "atheist churches" by their organizers, are essentially community meetups with a self-consciously religious aesthetic. The singing, the readings, the formal structure — all of this is deliberate theater.

This matters because it reveals an underlying confusion. When secular humanists say they want the "community" of church without the "superstition," they misunderstand what makes religious community actually work. Believers gather not merely for social contact but because they share genuine conviction about transcendent realities. The rituals gain their power from collective belief in their efficacy. Remove the metaphysics, and you have a sophisticated simulation — pleasant enough, perhaps, but hollow at its core.

The more honest approach for secularists is to build genuinely secular institutions: humanist community centers, philosophical societies, neighborhood associations with explicit naturalistic missions. These can provide authentic community without the pretense of pretending that borrowed liturgical forms mean what they meant in their original context.

If atheists genuinely wish to pray — that is, to address some greater power with the hope of being heard or affected — they are no longer atheists in any coherent sense. And if they merely wish to commune with each other while using religious vocabulary loosely, they should be honest about what they're doing rather than suggesting that the categories of belief and disbelief are interchangeable performance options.

"Atheists should not pray because prayer is a fundamentally relational act directed toward a Creator, and attempting to perform it without belief renders the ritual meaningless."

The phenomenon of atheists borrowing the trappings of religious life—Sunday assemblies, communal singing, and now the question of prayer—is fascinating, but it reveals a deep misunderstanding of what prayer actually is.

For the believer, prayer is fundamentally relational. It is not merely a meditative exercise designed to lower blood pressure, nor is it a therapeutic mechanism for venting one’s anxieties. It is a conversation with the Creator of the universe. It is predicated on the reality of an "Other" who knows us, loves us, and listens to us.

When an atheist attempts to pray, what exactly are they doing? If they are simply engaging in introspection, counting their blessings, or sitting in silence, they are practicing mindfulness. There is nothing wrong with mindfulness—it is a healthy and commendable human activity. But it is not prayer. To rebrand solitary cognitive exercises as "prayer" empties the word of its entire meaning.

This trend among nonbelievers points to a glaring spiritual hunger within modern secularism. Human beings are inherently religious creatures. We are hardwired to seek the transcendent, to desire communion with something greater than ourselves, and to participate in rituals that bind us together in shared reverence. The fact that atheists are now constructing "churches" and wondering if they should pray suggests that a purely materialist worldview—one that insists we are nothing but atoms and void—is emotionally and spiritually insufficient.

You cannot strip the theology out of a religious ritual and expect it to retain its soul. An atheist gathering to "pray" is like picking up a disconnected telephone and carrying on a conversation; it might help you clarify your own thoughts, but you are still just talking to yourself. Instead of borrowing the empty forms of faith, perhaps the secular community should ask why these forms remain so irresistibly attractive to the human heart in the first place.

Atheists should not pray, at least not if the word is to retain its ordinary meaning. They should meditate, sing, reflect, mourn, celebrate and organize. They may even do all these things in buildings that look suspiciously like churches. But prayer is not merely a mood or a technique. Prayer is address.

For Jews, Christians, Muslims and many other believers, prayer is not reducible to mental hygiene. It is not just self-soothing, group bonding or a poetic form of journaling. It is a response to a living reality beyond the self. Sometimes it is petition; sometimes thanksgiving; sometimes confession; sometimes silence. But even silence is silence before God. To remove the addressee and keep the vocabulary is to change the act into something else.

That does not make the something else worthless. Atheists are right to notice that human beings need ritual. The modern secular world is often thin in precisely the places where religion is thick: births, deaths, meals, guilt, reconciliation, awe. If nonbelievers want to gather weekly, sing songs, hear moral exhortation and serve their neighbors, good. Many churches could use the competition.

But honesty is a virtue for believers and unbelievers alike. The atheist who says “I pray” may mean “I concentrate my hopes.” The believer usually means “I speak to God.” Confusing the two encourages the modern habit of treating religion as a box of useful emotions detached from any truth claims. Yet believers do not pray because prayer is useful, though it may be. They pray because they believe God is real and worthy of praise.

This is also a warning to believers. If our own account of prayer is no deeper than “it makes me feel better” or “maybe it will get me what I want,” we should not be surprised when atheists borrow the practice and discard the doctrine. Prayer is not a celestial vending machine. It is communion, obedience, repentance and trust.

There is one exception. An atheist who prays, “God, if you are there, help me seek the truth,” is not playacting. That is an honest prayer of uncertainty. But a settled atheist need not pray. Better to speak plainly: reflect, resolve, remember. Those are noble acts. They are not prayer.

"Atheists should refrain from praying because adopting religious rituals undermines the integrity of secularism and contradicts the fundamental principles of atheism."

Atheists should resist the temptation to pray. Not because prayer is ridiculous — for believers it can be profound — but because an atheist prayer too easily becomes make-believe with therapeutic benefits.

The new “atheist church” phenomenon is understandable. Many secular people want what churches provide: community, music, childcare, moral seriousness, a reason to get out of bed on Sunday and stand next to neighbors. But borrowing religious forms is not cost-free. Rituals teach. Postures teach. Repeated language teaches. Prayer, in particular, teaches that the proper response to fear and dependence is to address an unseen power. That is exactly the lesson atheism rejects.

Some will answer that atheist prayer can simply be redefined as reflection or aspiration. But why call that prayer? We already have words for silence, meditation, remembrance, gratitude, resolve and public commitment. To blur them together under religious vocabulary suggests a lack of confidence in secular life. It also risks patronizing religion, treating believers’ most intimate practice as a useful shell once the embarrassing belief has been removed.

The social problem facing atheists in America is not that they lack enough churchlike trappings. It is that many citizens still do not trust them to be moral, communal or patriotic without God. The answer is not to say, “Look, we pray too.” The answer is to demonstrate that responsibility does not require supernatural surveillance. Feed the hungry, visit the sick, tutor children, build institutions, argue honestly. These are better advertisements for unbelief than a godless invocation.

Nor should we romanticize prayer’s effects. People have prayed before wars, after wars, for victory, for vengeance, for healing, for rain, for parking spaces. Prayer can console, but it can also flatter helplessness or sanctify tribal desire. Its benefits are real only when joined to self-criticism and action — and those are available without prayer.

Atheists should absolutely create ceremonies. We need funerals that do not lie, weddings that do not invoke a deity, baby welcomings, communal songs, ethical exhortations and moments of shared silence. A mature secular culture will have rituals of its own. But it should not call them prayer merely to borrow religion’s emotional capital. The dignity of atheism lies in facing the world without pretending someone else is listening.
