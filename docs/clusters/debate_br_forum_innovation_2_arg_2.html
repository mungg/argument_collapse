"While Dan Breznitz correctly argues that local prosperity depends on diversifying innovation beyond the Silicon Valley model, true inclusive growth requires coupling these technical strategies with robust labor protections and democratic accountability."

Dan Breznitz’s essay lands with special force because it challenges not just a policy mistake but an entire civic fantasy. For a generation, mayors, governors, university presidents, philanthropies, and consultants have repeated some version of the same promise: if a place can attract venture capital, build an incubator, lure a few “anchor” tech firms, and brand itself as entrepreneurial, prosperity will follow. That promise has been politically seductive because it appears to reconcile growth with modernity. No one wants to be against innovation. No one wants to tell young people that the future is elsewhere. But Breznitz is right that the Silicon Valley template has too often functioned as a mirage. It produces ribbon cuttings, glossy renderings, and a handful of spectacular winners, while doing far less to generate durable, broadly shared prosperity.

The most important contribution of Breznitz’s argument is his insistence that innovation is not synonymous with invention. This distinction sounds academic, but it is the hinge on which the whole debate turns. Invention is the dramatic moment: the new device, the breakthrough molecule, the clever algorithm, the founding myth in a garage. Innovation is usually less photogenic. It is the reworking of production, the redesign of a component, the better process, the cheaper and more reliable version, the capacity to make something at scale, the accumulated know-how of technicians, engineers, line workers, suppliers, and users. The first kind of activity attracts magazine covers. The second kind builds regions.

That is why the examples of Shimano, Giant, Taiwan’s semiconductor ecosystem, and the Italian design-production districts matter. They show that many of the richest opportunities for local development lie downstream or adjacent to the celebrated moment of invention. A community that can design prototypes, engineer production, improve components, or assemble complex products with flexibility is not merely doing “manufacturing” in the old, dismissive sense. It is accumulating knowledge. It is building capabilities that are difficult to copy precisely because they reside in networks of firms, schools, suppliers, routines, and workers. Such capabilities can support jobs across a wider skills spectrum than the typical venture-backed software company.

This is especially important in the United States, where the loss of production capacity has often been treated as an unfortunate but inevitable byproduct of moving “up the value chain.” For decades many business leaders argued that the United States could keep the design, marketing, and finance while sending production elsewhere. But production is not inert. It is a site of learning. When firms stop making things, they often lose the ability to improve them. The Schwinn story in Breznitz’s essay captures this beautifully and painfully. Outsourcing production did not simply reduce costs; over time it eroded the company’s knowledge base. The same dynamic has haunted other sectors, from consumer electronics to pharmaceuticals to semiconductors. We are now rediscovering, in the midst of a pandemic and supply chain disruptions, that productive capacity is not a switch that can be flipped back on after decades of neglect.

Still, if Breznitz’s framework is to become a practical guide for inclusive prosperity, it needs to be joined to an equally serious account of labor power and social institutions. Innovation in stages two, three, and four may create more jobs at more skill levels than venture-backed stage one start-ups, but that does not automatically make those jobs good jobs. A region can become an advanced production hub and still generate precarious employment, long hours, weak bargaining power, and unequal rewards. The mere presence of manufacturing or design capability does not guarantee inclusion. China’s Pearl River Delta, which Breznitz presents as a center of stage four innovation, has unquestionably generated astonishing productive capacity and employment. But the quality of that employment, the rights of migrant workers, and the distribution of gains remain crucial questions. A strategy that creates millions of jobs but leaves workers with little voice should not be our model of inclusive prosperity without qualification.

The missing term, then, is institution-building not only for firms but for workers. Breznitz is right to emphasize public and semi-public goods: specialized education, shared production facilities, industry networks, and collaborative spaces. But inclusive local prosperity also requires wage standards, sectoral training systems tied to career ladders, labor representation, portable benefits, and public mechanisms for ensuring that subsidies produce public returns. If local governments help firms develop capabilities, those firms should accept obligations: to hire locally, to train workers, to share productivity gains, to remain rooted, and to maintain decent labor standards throughout their supply chains.

This matters because the Silicon Valley model is not only an economic model; it is a governance model. It socializes risk and privatizes upside. Public science, public universities, infrastructure, immigration systems, defense spending, and procurement have all helped make American technology firms possible. Yet when the payoff comes, it is often captured by founders, early employees, and investors. The broader community is left with housing costs, congestion, tax avoidance, and inequality. A better innovation policy cannot simply shift attention from apps to components or from venture capital to production engineering. It must change the bargain.

Breznitz’s “tech teens” are promising in this respect. Firms that have survived the earliest stage, know their markets, and want to grow rather than flip may be better partners for local development than speculative start-ups. They are often too small for public markets, too established to attract the romance of seed capital, and too practical to interest politicians chasing the next unicorn. Yet they may be exactly the firms capable of anchoring local supply chains and providing stable employment. Operation Warp Speed’s support for SiO2 Materials Science is an instructive example because it combined public money, technical assistance, and domestic production requirements. It did not merely hope that private incentives would align with public need; it structured the relationship.

That approach should be generalized. Public procurement is one of the most powerful innovation tools governments possess. If communities and national governments want domestic capacity in medical supplies, clean energy equipment, transit vehicles, water systems, building materials, or semiconductor-related technologies, they can create demand while requiring local capability-building. Grants and tax credits are weaker tools if they do not come with coordination, standards, and accountability. The aim should not be autarky. It should be resilience, learning, and bargaining power within global production networks.

Breznitz is also right that communities must choose. This may be the hardest political lesson. Every region cannot become a biotech hub, a software capital, a semiconductor center, a clean energy manufacturing cluster, and a design district all at once. Local leaders love broad strategies because they avoid conflict and maximize the number of stakeholders who can see themselves in the plan. But real capability-building requires focus. It requires saying no to some opportunities in order to build depth in others. It requires understanding existing assets honestly: the local workforce, supplier base, universities, infrastructure, and industrial history. Cleveland should not imitate San Jose. Nor should rural Alabama imitate Boston. The question is not “How do we become the next Silicon Valley?” but “What can we learn to do unusually well, and how can that learning support a decent life for the people who live here?”

At the same time, local strategy cannot substitute for national policy. A city or region cannot on its own fix currency misalignment, trade rules, antitrust failures, health care costs, financial short-termism, or the chronic underfunding of vocational education. The United States has often asked localities to solve problems created by national and global systems. That is unfair and inefficient. If we want many roads out of Cobalt, we need federal investment in infrastructure, research, community colleges, clean energy, and industrial finance. We need antitrust enforcement so dominant firms cannot simply absorb or crush local innovators. We need patient capital that does not demand an exit in five years. And we need a tax system that rewards productive investment more than financial engineering.

The lesson of Cobalt is not that boomtowns should have planned better. It is that extraction without capability leaves little behind. The lesson of Toronto is that some places convert a boom into institutions that endure. The central question is whether today’s communities can convert public investment, private enterprise, and local knowledge into capabilities that remain after the headlines fade.

Breznitz’s essay gives us a vocabulary for that task. It frees innovation from the narrow mythology of venture capital and start-up disruption. It reminds us that the mundane work of improvement is often the source of broad prosperity. But to complete the argument, we must add democratic accountability and worker power. Innovation can be inclusive, but only if inclusion is designed into the system. Otherwise we may escape the Silicon Valley myth only to build more sophisticated versions of the same unequal economy.

"While Dan Breznitz correctly identifies the need to diversify innovation beyond the Silicon Valley model, his framework fails to address the fundamental role of power and democratic control in determining whether innovation leads to shared prosperity or increased inequality."

Dan Breznitz asks what Silicon Valley gets wrong about innovation. His answer is persuasive as far as it goes: Silicon Valley narrows innovation to start-ups, venture capital, novelty, and exit, while ignoring the many forms of learning and improvement that occur across production. But there is another thing Silicon Valley gets wrong, and it is not only Silicon Valley’s mistake. It is the belief that innovation can be discussed apart from power.

Who owns the firm? Who controls investment? Who captures the gains from productivity? Who decides whether a plant stays open, whether jobs are outsourced, whether workers are trained or discarded, whether public subsidies create public obligations? These questions are not secondary to innovation policy. They determine whether innovation produces shared prosperity or merely a more technologically sophisticated form of inequality.

Breznitz’s essay is valuable because it challenges “techno fetishism.” Yet at times it risks replacing the fetish of novelty with the fetish of capability. We are told that communities need specialized skills, shared assets, public-private networks, and a clear understanding of where they fit in global production. That is true. But many communities have had plenty of capable workers and viable firms and still suffered decline because capital had the power to move, disinvest, merge, strip assets, or demand concessions. The problem was not a lack of innovative capacity alone. It was a lack of democratic control over economic life.

Consider the American manufacturing story. There is a familiar narrative in which the United States lost production because it failed to appreciate incremental innovation and allowed firms to offshore the learning that comes from making things. That narrative is correct, but incomplete. Firms did not offshore because they misunderstood innovation. They offshored because they could increase profits, weaken labor, arbitrage wages and regulations, and satisfy financial markets. Executives were not always making a national economic mistake from their own perspective. They were making a class-interested decision. The costs were borne by workers, towns, and future productive capacity.

This is why the Schwinn example is so powerful but also why it should be read politically. Schwinn lost manufacturing knowledge and eventually collapsed. But before that collapse, decisions to outsource and offshore reflected a broader corporate environment in which production workers had become costs to be minimized rather than stakeholders in a shared enterprise. The same pattern ran through steel, electronics, machine tools, textiles, and many other sectors. Innovation policy that does not confront corporate governance will be forever trying to rebuild what financialized capitalism keeps tearing down.

Breznitz’s preferred examples—Taiwan, Japan, Italy, parts of China—also depend on institutional arrangements that cannot be reduced to technical choices about innovation stages. Taiwan’s success was not simply a matter of selecting stage three opportunities. It involved a developmental state, public research institutes, land reform’s egalitarian legacy, education, disciplined finance, and a geopolitical context in which the United States supported East Asian industrialization during the Cold War. Italy’s industrial districts rested on dense social networks, craft traditions, small-firm cooperation, and in some regions strong local civic institutions. Japan and Germany built manufacturing strength alongside systems of vocational training, patient finance, and worker consultation, though each with its own inequalities and exclusions. These are power arrangements. They structure whose interests count.

In the United States, by contrast, local development policy often proceeds as if firms are clients and workers are inputs. Cities ask what companies need: tax abatements, land, infrastructure, training funds, regulatory flexibility. Workers are promised that benefits will trickle down through jobs. But when public money is involved, workers and communities should be at the table from the beginning. A public-private partnership that excludes labor is not a partnership; it is a subsidy channel.

What would it mean to put power at the center of innovation policy? First, public support should come with enforceable conditions. If a firm receives grants, loans, procurement contracts, tax breaks, or access to publicly funded research, it should meet standards for wages, benefits, training, domestic or local investment where appropriate, and neutrality toward union organizing. If public policy helps create value, the public should share in the upside, whether through equity stakes, royalties, profit-sharing, or reinvestment obligations. The public sector should not merely de-risk private investment while private owners capture the returns.

Second, workers should be treated as sources of knowledge, not just beneficiaries. Much incremental innovation comes from the shop floor: workers noticing defects, improving processes, adapting tools, reducing waste, and solving coordination problems. Yet workplaces organized around surveillance, speedup, and fear suppress this knowledge. Unions, works councils, labor-management committees, and cooperative governance can make innovation more democratic and more effective. The idea that worker voice slows innovation is a prejudice of managerial ideology. In many sectors, the people closest to production understand its possibilities best.

Third, local innovation ecosystems should include institutions of worker ownership and community wealth-building. Not every firm can or should be a cooperative. But employee ownership trusts, municipal enterprises, community land trusts, public banks, and cooperative suppliers can help anchor wealth locally. One of Breznitz’s criticisms of Israel’s VC model is that foreign investors captured much of the gain from exits. The same concern applies wherever ownership is absentee. If the owners of a region’s most dynamic firms live elsewhere, sell quickly, or answer mainly to distant capital markets, local embeddedness will be fragile. Ownership matters.

Fourth, antitrust policy must be part of innovation policy. Dominant firms can present themselves as innovation champions while using their market power to squeeze suppliers, acquire potential competitors, dictate terms to workers, and capture data. A small manufacturer or tech teen cannot thrive in an ecosystem where a platform giant can copy its product, bury its visibility, or force predatory contract terms. The Silicon Valley model has produced not only start-ups but also monopolistic gatekeepers. If we want diverse regional innovation, we need markets that are actually open.

Fifth, we must be careful about celebrating “inclusive prosperity” in places where inclusion is partial. The Pearl River Delta’s manufacturing system has generated enormous employment and productive sophistication. But many of its workers are migrants who have faced harsh conditions, limited rights, and social exclusion. That does not mean there is nothing to learn from Shenzhen. It means job creation alone is not sufficient. A development model can be innovative and exploitative at the same time. The standard should be not only how many jobs are created, but what kind of power workers have within them.

The same point applies to the green transition. Decarbonization will require massive innovation across all stages: invention, design, components, production, installation, maintenance, and system integration. But unless we build labor standards into climate policy, we may create a low-wage clean economy that reproduces the injustices of the fossil economy. Solar panels, batteries, heat pumps, transmission lines, electric buses, and building retrofits can support millions of good jobs. Or they can become another arena of subcontracting, union avoidance, and regional inequality. The difference will be political.

Breznitz is right that communities must not chase the mirage of becoming the next Silicon Valley. But they should also avoid becoming supplicants to any innovation elite, whether venture capitalists, advanced manufacturers, global brands, or local growth coalitions. Too often “public-private partnership” means public officials and business leaders deciding a region’s future while workers are invited only to applaud. A democratic innovation strategy would begin with a broader question: what do the people who live here need, and how can our productive capacities serve those needs?

That question might lead to export-oriented clusters in some places. In others, it might lead to public housing retrofits, care work, food systems, transit maintenance, flood protection, or medical supply production. Some of these activities will not look like innovation in the glamorous sense. But they can involve real learning, technical improvement, and institution-building. They can also meet urgent social needs.

Silicon Valley’s deepest error is not simply that it confuses invention with innovation. It is that it confuses private accumulation with progress. A better model must democratize the economy that innovation transforms. Without worker power, public ownership stakes, strong standards, and community control, innovation will keep doing what it has too often done: making society richer while leaving many people with less security, less voice, and less claim on the future.

  2. 02Public support for innovation should be contingent on enforceable conditions, such as labor standards, local investment obligations, and public equity stakes in the value created.
  3. 03True inclusive prosperity requires treating workers as active sources of innovative knowledge and empowering them through unions, works councils, and cooperative governance.
  4. 04Local economic stability depends on anchoring wealth through institutions of worker ownership and community wealth-building to prevent the extraction of profits by absentee capital.
  5. 05Antitrust policy is a necessary component of innovation strategy to prevent monopolistic gatekeepers from stifling the growth of diverse regional firms.
  6. 06The success of international innovation hubs like Taiwan or Italy is rooted in specific political and institutional power arrangements rather than just technical choices about production stages.

"While Dan Breznitz correctly identifies a broader range of innovation models, his framework remains flawed because it prioritizes global market competitiveness over the foundational social and democratic needs of a community."

Dan Breznitz wants to rescue innovation from Silicon Valley. That is a worthy project. But perhaps we should also rescue prosperity from innovation.

The lead essay argues that communities have been misled by a narrow model: venture-backed start-ups, high-tech novelty, financial exits, and the dream of becoming the next Silicon Valley. In its place Breznitz offers a broader understanding of innovation across multiple stages of production. Cities and regions, he argues, can build inclusive prosperity by finding the right place in global value chains: design, prototyping, component improvement, production engineering, assembly, and continuous upgrading. This is a much better account of how economies actually work. It honors forms of skill and learning that the start-up world ignores.

And yet the essay still accepts a premise that deserves more scrutiny: that local well-being should be organized around competitive innovation-led growth. What if that premise is part of the problem?

The places Breznitz describes are constantly asked to adapt to global demand. They must identify niches, build capacities, upgrade skills, and remain alert to shifting markets. There is no permanent security. A community survives by becoming useful to global production networks. This may be realistic, but it is also a bleak foundation for democratic life. It means that the dignity of a town depends on whether it can find a defensible market position. Those that succeed become case studies. Those that fail become Cobalt.

Of course communities need economic activity. People need jobs, tax bases, and opportunities. But the language of innovation can crowd out other ways of thinking about what places are for. A town is not a firm. A city is not a product. A region is not a portfolio. The purpose of local life is not to maximize competitiveness but to allow people to live decently together: to care for children and elders, educate the young, repair homes, maintain parks, provide food, support art, protect health, and sustain relationships. Much of this work is not export-oriented. Much of it does not scale. Much of it is not “innovative” except in the ordinary human sense of solving problems with the resources at hand.

The pandemic should have made this obvious. When COVID-19 arrived, the workers most essential to daily survival were not primarily venture-backed engineers or advanced manufacturing specialists. They were nurses, home health aides, teachers, grocery clerks, delivery drivers, farmworkers, cleaners, public health staff, child care workers, and neighbors checking on neighbors. Some relied on sophisticated technologies, but the core of their work was care, maintenance, and social reproduction. These sectors are often low-paid not because they lack social value, but because our economy rewards market power more than human need.

An inclusive prosperity agenda that begins with innovation risks missing this. It may create good jobs in certain industries while leaving care workers underpaid, renters insecure, bus systems unreliable, and public health departments understaffed. A region could become a world-class hub for battery components and still fail its elderly residents. It could build a thriving design cluster and still have homeless encampments under the freeway. It could produce export success and still leave single parents without child care.

Breznitz might reply that innovation-based growth supplies the tax base for these social goods. Sometimes it does. But in the United States, growth often fails to translate into collective provision. Silicon Valley itself is the proof. Immense wealth exists alongside housing crisis, long commutes, food insecurity, and public dysfunction. The problem is not only the wrong kind of innovation; it is the weakness of social claims on private wealth. Without strong taxation, public institutions, labor power, and democratic planning, even successful local growth can bypass basic needs.

There is also an ecological question. The essay is written in the shadow of COVID-19, but also in the age of climate crisis. Growth through endless production, upgrading, consumption, and global competition cannot be assumed benign. Some innovation is essential for decarbonization and adaptation. But many innovations accelerate throughput, planned obsolescence, extraction, and waste. A cheaper smartphone, a faster delivery system, a more flexible manufacturing process—these may increase convenience and profits while deepening environmental burdens. The question should not be “How can this community innovate?” but “What kinds of economic activity are compatible with a livable planet and a decent society?”

Bicycles, one of Breznitz’s central examples, are interesting in this respect. Better bikes can support low-carbon mobility and public health. But a bicycle economy oriented around constant product differentiation, carbon fiber upgrades, and globalized consumption is not the same as an economy oriented around safe streets, repair shops, affordable transportation, and everyday use. The social value of the bicycle lies less in the sophistication of its components than in whether people can safely ride to school, work, and the grocery store. That requires urban design, public investment, maintenance, and political choices about cars. The innovation that matters most may be a protected bike lane, not a lighter frame.

The same distinction applies elsewhere. Food systems need less glamorous innovation and more regional resilience, soil health, fair wages, and reduced waste. Housing needs new materials and construction methods, yes, but even more it needs land policy, tenant protections, public housing, and an end to exclusionary zoning. Health care needs medical innovation, but also primary care, staffing, trust, and universal access. Education needs technology only insofar as it supports teachers and students; it does not need another wave of platforms promising disruption. In each case, the central issue is not whether innovation occurs but whether human needs govern it.

A better frame might be the “foundational economy”: the sectors that make everyday life possible. These include utilities, care, housing, food distribution, health, education, transit, sanitation, and local public administration. They are often less mobile than high-tech firms and less glamorous than export clusters. They also employ large numbers of people across skill levels. Improving them can create jobs, reduce inequality, and make places more livable. Some improvements will involve technology and process innovation. Others will involve higher staffing ratios, better wages, public ownership, or simpler rules. The point is to begin from needs rather than from markets.

This does not mean rejecting Breznitz’s argument. On the contrary, his broader definition of innovation can be useful if turned toward maintenance and care. Continuous improvement is not only for bicycle gears and semiconductors. It is also for elder care scheduling, school meals, heat-wave response, building retrofits, bus reliability, water systems, and public libraries. But once we move in this direction, the distinction between innovation policy and social policy begins to dissolve. The goal is not local competitiveness; it is collective capability.

Communities should still build on their strengths. A region with machine tool expertise should not ignore it. A city with medical device firms should support them. A rural area with agricultural knowledge should develop it. But these strategies should be subordinate to a broader democratic plan for well-being. Too often economic development agencies ask how to attract and grow firms, then assume the public will benefit. They should ask first what residents need: affordable homes, clean air, good schools, dignified care, reliable mobility, meaningful work, cultural life, and security. Firms and innovation strategies should be evaluated by how well they serve those ends.

This also changes how we think about “left behind” places. The phrase implies that every community must catch up to a growth frontier defined elsewhere. But some places may not want to become hubs. They may want stability, beauty, affordability, ecological restoration, and care for an aging population. That is not failure. It is only failure in an economic imagination that recognizes value mainly when it is tradable, scalable, and innovative.

The Silicon Valley myth is damaging because it tells communities to chase a narrow dream. Breznitz offers a wider and more realistic set of dreams. But perhaps the deeper freedom is to dream less competitively. A good society would not force every town to justify itself through global market success. It would guarantee the material foundations of life and then allow places to cultivate their own forms of usefulness, creativity, and belonging.

Innovation has a role in that society. But it should be a servant, not a god.
