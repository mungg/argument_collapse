"While Africa's recent economic growth is promising, its long-term success depends on building impartial political institutions that can convert growth into state legitimacy and prevent the winner-take-all nature of elections from sparking violence."

Edward Miguel is right to resist both Afro-pessimism and development triumphalism. The most compelling feature of his essay is not the claim that Africa is “turning,” but the insistence that the turn, if real, remains politically fragile. The image of Busia’s paved road is therefore apt: a road is a development achievement, but also a reminder that prosperity depends on public order, state capacity, and trust. When the highway closes because of election violence, the economy’s hidden political foundations become visible.

The question is whether Africa’s recent growth represents a durable transformation or a favorable episode. Miguel gives us reasons to think it could become durable: democratization, new technologies, higher commodity prices, Chinese investment, and the end of several terrible wars. But his own evidence suggests that the key variable is not growth itself. It is whether African states can convert growth into legitimacy.

This is where the Kenya example matters most. Kenya’s crisis was not caused by absolute destitution. It occurred after several years of strong growth, rising investment, expanding media freedom, and visible middle-class confidence. That should warn us against a simple “growth brings peace” story. Growth can create new expectations faster than political institutions can handle them. It can raise the stakes of controlling the presidency. It can make land more valuable, intensify old grievances, and provide politicians with more spoils to distribute. Kenya did not explode because nothing had improved. It exploded partly because enough had improved that losing access to power seemed intolerable.

Miguel’s essay implicitly recognizes this, but I would push the point further. Democracy, in the minimal sense of multiparty elections, is not sufficient. In ethnically divided societies with centralized presidencies, elections can become censuses of communal power. If winning the presidency means winning the state, and if the state controls land allocation, police protection, civil-service jobs, contracts, and development funds, then electoral defeat can look like collective exclusion. Under those circumstances, democracy can discipline rulers only if the rules are credible and the state is not treated as private property.

That is why the institutional agenda must be more specific than “more democracy.” Africa’s promising countries need independent electoral commissions, professional courts, credible local government, parliamentary oversight, and police forces that are not partisan instruments. They also need constitutional arrangements that reduce the winner-take-all character of politics. Kenya’s power-sharing bargain may have prevented further bloodshed, but power-sharing after a stolen election is a dangerous precedent if it teaches incumbents and opposition leaders alike that violence is a route to office. The deeper task is to make the next election count without making it existential.

Miguel is also persuasive in arguing that foreign aid has not been the main driver of Africa’s recent growth. The timing does not fit. Aid was high during decline and lower during stagnation. But one should distinguish between aid as a growth engine and aid as a stabilizer. A fire department does not produce the economy, but a city without one is a bad place to build factories. In that sense Miguel’s proposal for “rapid conflict prevention support” deserves serious attention. If rainfall shocks and commodity collapses increase the probability of civil war, then well-timed assistance could be more valuable than routine project aid.

Still, RCPS would face a severe political problem: how to deliver emergency support to fragile governments without strengthening the very rulers whose predation fuels instability. Botswana’s drought program worked not only because money arrived during droughts, but because Botswana had a relatively capable and legitimate state. Transplanting that model to Chad, Sudan, or the Democratic Republic of Congo would be far harder. A drought-relief program administered through a predatory army or a ruling-party machine might purchase temporary calm while entrenching injustice.

The answer is not to abandon the idea, but to design it with political constraints in mind. Some support should flow through public works, food-for-work programs, school feeding, and transparent local mechanisms, with independent monitoring and rapid publication of spending. It should also be tied to conflict-sensitive goals: keeping young men employed during harvest failures, maintaining food prices, preventing displacement, and protecting pastoral mobility where drought turns herders and farmers into competitors. The worst time to invent such programs is after the killing starts.

Miguel’s discussion of China is also balanced. China’s demand has lifted commodity prices and helped finance roads, mines, and telecommunications. Western commentators who denounce Chinese investment sometimes forget that Western firms and governments have hardly been models of virtue in Africa. Europe and America subsidize farmers, protect their markets, and have long tolerated dictators when convenient. But China’s no-questions-asked approach can undermine the democratic gains Miguel celebrates. If rulers can obtain loans, arms, and diplomatic cover without accountability, citizens lose leverage. The issue is not whether Chinese money is “good” or “bad,” but whether African publics can force all investors—Chinese, Western, Indian, South African, and domestic—to obey decent labor, environmental, and transparency standards.

The largest missing piece in the current boom is manufacturing. Commodity-led growth can improve incomes, but it rarely generates enough jobs for Africa’s rapidly growing cities. A genuine development miracle will require African firms to compete not only in coffee, oil, copper, and flowers, but in labor-intensive production. Yet African manufacturers face high transport costs, unreliable electricity, small domestic markets, weak credit systems, and competition from Asia. Roads like the one to Busia matter because they lower the cost of everything else. So do ports, power grids, customs reform, and regional trade.

Is it Africa’s turn? Perhaps. But turns are not allocated by history. They are made by institutions, bargains, technologies, and luck. The encouraging fact is that many African societies now have more open politics, more educated citizens, more connected markets, and fewer wars than they did a decade ago. The sobering fact is that these gains can be reversed quickly.

The right conclusion is neither celebration nor despair. It is urgency. If African governments and their partners use the current expansion to build impartial institutions, insure citizens against shocks, deepen regional markets, and invest in agriculture, health, and infrastructure, the boom may become a transformation. If they treat high commodity prices as a windfall to be consumed and elections as battles for ownership of the state, Busia’s road will remain a symbol not of arrival but of possibility interrupted.
