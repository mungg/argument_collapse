"The primary threat to representative democracy is the collapse of a shared information ecosystem, which prevents the informed public and factual consensus necessary for legislative deliberation to function."

Of all the forces undermining representative democracy, the one that Ira Katznelson’s essay most neglects is the transformation of the information ecosystem. He notes that a free press is a component of liberal democracy and that voters need “opportunities to gain enlightened policy understanding,” but he treats these as baseline assumptions rather than as sites of acute failure. In reality, the very possibility of the “informed public” that democratic theory requires has been shattered by the fragmentation of media, the collapse of economic models for serious journalism, and the rise of algorithmically curated echo chambers. The anxieties Katznelson catalogs are, in large part, symptoms of a polity that no longer shares a common set of facts.

Consider the landscape in August 2015. Trust in mass media has fallen to historic lows, especially among partisans. The Fairness Doctrine has been dead for decades, replaced by a cable news environment where two distinct realities are constructed for left and right. The Pew Research Center documents that consistent conservatives and consistent liberals inhabit separate news universes, with barely an overlap in the sources they trust. On social media, Facebook’s algorithms and Twitter’s trending topics amplify the most inflammatory content because it generates engagement. The result is not just polarization but a kind of epistemic fragmentation: different groups not only disagree about values but cannot agree on basic descriptions of events—whether the economy is recovering, whether climate change is happening, whether vaccines are safe. Under such conditions, the “physics of consent” in a legislature becomes impossible, because there is no shared world of fact on which deliberation can be built.

Katznelson’s ideal of legislative debate “openly acknowledging and respecting … the inevitable differences of opinion” assumes that those differences are over principles and policies, not over the fundamental nature of reality. But when one side believes that President Obama was born in Kenya and the other believes that the banking system was deliberately structured to impoverish the working class, the legislative process cannot function as a site of reasoned adjudication. It becomes a theater for performing loyalty to these divergent worldviews. The delegitimizing of expertise—the notion that government statistics, scientific consensus, and judicial findings are just another form of partisan spin—further erodes any basis for resolving disputes through evidence.

The historical comparison to the 1930s is again instructive, though in an uncomfortable way. The interwar democracies were crippled not just by economic crisis and institutional gridlock but by propaganda machines—radio, state-controlled newspapers, and party-owned media—that fragmented the public sphere. Weimar Germany was famously awash in pamphlets, mass rallies, and competing narratives that undermined any sense of common truth. The United States is not Weimar, but the structural parallels are worrying: we have replaced Goebbels’s radio with Breitbart and Daily Kos as sites of partisan identity formation, and we have all but abandoned the shared rituals—the trusted evening news anchor, the major metro newspaper—that once provided a common factual floor.

Fixing this goes beyond campaign-finance reform or procedural changes in Congress. It requires a deep investment in public-interest journalism, rebuilt around nonprofit models, public media, and educational institutions that can provide reliable information insulated from the market’s demand for outrage. It requires civic education that teaches not just the branches of government but media literacy: how to evaluate sources, how to recognize propaganda, how to engage across difference without surrendering one’s core commitments. It also requires a cultural shift among elites who have cynically exploited the information chaos for short-term gain. Katznelson is right that democracy needs “background agreement,” but the most elementary form of that agreement is a commitment to a shared reality. Without it, no legislature, however well-designed, can be dignified, because the very act of reasoning together becomes impossible. Restoring our information commons is the urgent, unglamorous work that must undergird any democratic renewal.
