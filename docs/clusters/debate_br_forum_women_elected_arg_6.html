"To achieve true gender parity in U.S. politics, the focus must shift from individual women's ambition to structural reforms within political parties and the electoral system."

Jennifer Piscopo is right to insist that the centennial of the Nineteenth Amendment should not be treated as a victory lap. The vote was necessary, but it was never sufficient. A democracy in which women are a majority of voters but only a quarter of Congress has not solved the problem of political citizenship; it has merely moved the problem from the polling place to the candidate pipeline, the party meeting, the donor call, and the ballot.

The most important move in Piscopo’s essay is to shift the conversation from women’s “ambition” to the structure of opportunity. American politics has become comfortable with a strangely therapeutic account of women’s underrepresentation: women need to be encouraged, trained, persuaded, inspired. There is truth in this. Organizations such as EMILY’s List, Emerge, Higher Heights, She Should Run, and many others have plainly mattered. The record number of women candidates in 2018 did not appear by magic. It was built by networks of recruitment, money, and mutual support.

But the ambition story is incomplete and, at its worst, misleading. It asks why women do not step forward often enough without asking why men are so often invited, forgiven, funded, and presumed qualified. If women who win tend to have stronger credentials than men who win, then the system is not merely failing to “encourage” women. It is overvaluing ordinary men and undervaluing exceptional women. That is not a confidence gap. It is a power gap.

Piscopo’s comparative examples are especially useful because they puncture American exceptionalism. The United States often treats its constitutional arrangements as if they are the natural form of democracy rather than one historically contingent design among many. Other democracies have asked a different question: not simply whether the state refrains from excluding women, but whether democratic institutions are organized so that women actually share power. That question has produced quotas, parity laws, party rules, public financing incentives, and legal penalties for political violence against women. Not all of these measures can be imported wholesale into the United States, but the underlying principle can be: exclusion that persists after formal barriers fall is still exclusion.

The strongest near-term path in the United States is through political parties. A federal gender quota for congressional candidates would collide with our candidate-centered system, single-member districts, constitutional doctrine, and the decentralized nature of ballot access. It would also provoke a political backlash that might swallow the reform before it could be tested. But parties already regulate themselves in countless ways. They decide who receives early endorsements, donor introductions, staff support, debate access, voter files, committee assignments, and leadership opportunities. They cannot pretend to be neutral spectators.

The Democratic Party, in particular, has no excuse for weak action. It already embraces gender balance in convention delegations and internal committees. It celebrates descriptive representation rhetorically. It relies heavily on women voters, especially Black women. Yet in competitive primaries, local party actors still frequently ask women—particularly women of color—to “wait their turn.” That phrase is one of the most revealing in politics. It sounds polite, but it protects incumbency, hierarchy, and informal male entitlement. Parties could adopt rules requiring gender-balanced shortlists for open seats where party leaders make endorsements. They could require state and county committees to report the gender and racial composition of recruited candidates, endorsed candidates, and recipients of party funds. They could commit that in races without an incumbent, party resources will not flow to a candidate slate that reproduces old patterns of exclusion.

Republicans should not be let off the hook either. Women’s underrepresentation is not only a Democratic problem, even if Democratic women have recently driven most of the gains. In fact, the gender gap between the parties has itself become a representational problem. A two-party democracy in which one party elects many more women than the other does not produce parity; it produces polarization around gender equality. Republican women such as Elise Stefanik have noticed the recruitment gap in their own party and tried to address it. Conservative women voters deserve more than being represented mostly by men. If the Republican Party does not cultivate women candidates, it narrows both its talent pool and its democratic legitimacy.

Still, party rules alone will not be enough. The United States should also expand public financing. Money is not the only barrier to office, but it is a barrier that magnifies every other one. Candidates without wealthy networks spend more time dialing for dollars and less time meeting voters. Women, working-class candidates, and candidates of color are less likely to be plugged into donor circles that treat political giving as routine. Small-donor matching systems, democracy vouchers, and grants for qualifying candidates could make running for office less dependent on elite fundraising. Such reforms would not be “women-only,” but they would disproportionately help candidates who lack access to old boys’ networks.

We should also take seriously Piscopo’s point about harassment and political violence. The abuse directed at women in politics—especially women of color—is not simply incivility. It is a mechanism of exclusion. Online threats, sexualized attacks, racist harassment, and disproportionate scrutiny of appearance and family life all raise the cost of candidacy. A system that tells women to run while leaving them to absorb organized intimidation is not neutral. Legal remedies must be carefully designed to protect speech, but parties, platforms, law enforcement agencies, and news organizations can do far more than they do now. Threats should be treated as threats. Coordinated harassment should not be dismissed as the weather of public life.

The hard question is whether the United States can embrace a positive conception of political rights. Piscopo argues that we should. I agree, but I would frame the claim in institutional rather than purely philosophical terms. Americans may resist the language of group rights, quotas, or state-guaranteed candidacy. Yet they already accept that democracy requires active maintenance. We have districting rules, campaign finance rules, ballot access rules, voting rights protections, anti-discrimination laws, and public institutions designed to make political participation possible. The question is not whether government shapes representation. It already does. The question is whose representation the rules facilitate.

This is why “merit” arguments against positive action often ring hollow. No existing system simply selects the best candidates. It selects candidates through a maze of incumbency advantages, fundraising networks, media narratives, party favors, family flexibility, racial stereotypes, gender stereotypes, and sheer luck. Men who benefit from these arrangements are rarely asked to prove that their presence is the result of pure merit. Women are asked to justify reforms that would only partially correct distortions already embedded in the system.

But supporters of parity should be careful about one thing: women’s representation should not be defended primarily on the promise that women will govern better, cleaner, or more compassionately. Women are not civic disinfectant. They can be hawks or doves, reformers or machine politicians, egalitarians or reactionaries. They can disappoint. They can disagree profoundly with one another. The reason women should hold power is not that they are morally superior. It is that they are equal citizens. Any policy benefits that follow are important, but they are not the foundation of the claim.

That foundation is democratic legitimacy. A legislature that persistently overrepresents men is not merely demographically imperfect. It reflects a long history of deciding that men are the normal political actors and women are the exceptions. The point of positive action is not to install token women into an unchanged system, nor to romanticize women’s leadership, but to make political equality real.

The Nineteenth Amendment removed a formal exclusion. It did not dismantle the habits, institutions, and assumptions built around that exclusion. One hundred years later, the United States should stop acting surprised that a political system designed by and for men still produces male-dominated institutions. If we want different results, we need different rules.
