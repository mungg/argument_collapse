"To ensure both support for survivors and fairness for the accused, colleges must dismantle the current biased adjudication system and restore constitutional due process protections."

The catastrophic Rolling Stone story is not an aberration; it is the predictable outcome of a campus sexual assault apparatus that has sacrificed due process on the altar of political expediency. Since the Education Department’s 2011 “Dear Colleague” letter, colleges have been told to adjudicate these cases using the lowest standard of proof—preponderance of the evidence—and to limit accused students’ ability to challenge their accusers. The result has been a rash of proceedings that are secretive, one-sided, and utterly incapable of separating genuine assault from regretted encounters or outright falsehoods. The way to treat women supportively while ensuring fairness for the accused is to dismantle this kangaroo court system and re-inject constitutional values.

The standard of proof must come first. Preponderance of the evidence—a mere 50.01 percent certainty—is too low for a finding that can brand a young man a rapist for life. A clear and convincing evidence standard, common in civil fraud, would still be lower than the criminal threshold but would insist on a genuine high probability of guilt. This is not about disbelieving women; it is about recognizing that the stakes are enormous for both sides.

Equally important is the right to confront one’s accuser. The current practice of allowing only indirect questioning, or none at all, violates the most basic principle of justice. Traumatic though cross-examination may be, it is the greatest engine of truth. A fair process would allow the respondent’s attorney to cross-examine the complainant in a respectful, controlled manner, with the adjudicator ruling on relevance. Survivors can still be supported with advocates by their side; the proceeding need not be brutal to be effective.

Beyond procedural fixes, the entire premise of campus felony adjudication is flawed. Sexual assault is a crime, and if a complainant wants serious consequences—expulsion, public branding—she should be required to report the incident to the police. Genuine victims deserve a real investigation by detectives who can subpoena evidence, not a campus panel that relies on rumor and text messages. For those who do not wish to go to the police, colleges can offer mediation, counseling, and accommodations, but they should not be empowered to issue life-altering punishments based on a closed-door process.

This is not a call to return to the days when campus rape was swept under the rug. A victim-supportive environment means having compassionate advocates, confidential resources, and swift safety measures. But compassion does not require convicting the innocent. The best way to honor the real victims of sexual violence is to create a system whose decisions are so scrupulously fair that no one can dismiss them as the product of hysteria or bias. Rolling Stone should be our wake-up call: it’s time to restore justice to campus justice. Fairness and sensitivity are not enemies, but they cannot coexist in a system that treats one side’s rights as an afterthought.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The standard of proof for campus sexual assault cases should be raised to 'clear and convincing evidence' to reflect the life-altering stakes of the accusation.
  2. 02Accused students must be granted the right to cross-examine their accusers through legal counsel to ensure the truthfulness of the testimony.
  3. 03Serious felony allegations should be handled by the criminal justice system rather than campus panels that lack the investigative tools and authority of the police.
  4. 04Colleges should focus on providing compassionate support services and safety accommodations rather than conducting secretive, quasi-judicial proceedings.
  5. 05A scrupulously fair and transparent process is necessary to maintain the credibility of findings and truly honor victims of sexual violence.
