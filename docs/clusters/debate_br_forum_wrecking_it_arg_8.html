"To preserve the Internet's generative nature, we must move beyond the false choice between security and freedom by designing a more forgiving architecture that empowers non-expert users to manage risks without surrendering control to proprietary appliances."

Jonathan Zittrain is right to frame the problem as a problem of preservation, not nostalgia. The question is not whether the Internet of 1988 or 1995 can be frozen in amber. It cannot. The question is whether the qualities that made the Internet and the personal computer so astonishingly fertile can survive the very success they enabled. A network for experts became a network for everyone; a programmable machine for hobbyists became the filing cabinet, bank branch, photo album, classroom, and workplace of ordinary people. The old bargain—freedom in exchange for self-reliance—no longer works when most users cannot possibly know what their machines are doing.

The most important move in Zittrain’s essay is to refuse the easy division between “security” and “freedom.” In practice, insecurity can destroy freedom. A user whose computer is riddled with spyware is not free in any meaningful sense. A person whose broadband connection has been enlisted into a botnet is not exercising autonomy. A small business that cannot trust its own spreadsheets is not enjoying the benefits of an open platform. If the generative PC comes to mean a machine that betrays its owner, then consumers will rationally flee to devices that do less but do it reliably.

Still, I would put more emphasis than Zittrain does on the humble problem of usability. The security community has long behaved as if users were defective components. They click the wrong attachments, ignore warnings, fail to patch, choose weak passwords, and install charming little programs that turn out to be parasites. But these are not moral failings. They are predictable responses to systems that ask impossible questions. “Do you want to allow this ActiveX control?” “This certificate cannot be verified; proceed?” “A program is attempting to access the Internet.” These prompts masquerade as consent while withholding the information necessary for consent.

If we want generativity to survive, we need not only community tools and civic institutions, but better defaults. The genius of wiki reversion is not simply that it permits repair. It lowers the cost of error. That is the model that ought to guide the PC. Ordinary users should be able to try software, undo experiments, recover documents, and see what changed without becoming system administrators. A computer should have a visible history. It should be able to say, in plain terms: “Yesterday this program installed itself, changed your browser home page, and began sending mail.” At present, most people discover such facts only after the damage is done, if ever.

Zittrain’s “Red” and “Green” computer is a promising metaphor because it matches the way people already think. We have work clothes and play clothes, checking accounts and vacation cash, kitchens and garages. We understand boundaries. A Green space for banking, tax records, writing, and family photos, and a Red space for games, experiments, downloads, and unknown tools, could preserve the pleasure of tinkering without making every experiment a wager of one’s digital life. The trick is to make the boundary comprehensible. If moving a file from Red to Green requires priestly knowledge, the idea will fail. If the system can say, “This photo is safe to copy, but this program is not,” then we may have something.

I am also persuaded by Zittrain’s analogy to Wikipedia, though with reservations. Wikipedia works not because people are angels, but because its architecture makes many small acts of repair easy and visible. Vandalism is not eliminated; it is outnumbered. The lesson is not that community spirit alone can secure the network. The lesson is that good tools can convert weak and dispersed goodwill into useful governance. StopBadware, reputation systems for code, shared measurements of crashes and pop-ups, and public warnings about infected sites are all attempts to create the equivalent of page histories and watchlists for software.

But we should be careful about who runs these systems. If code reputation becomes a cartel of large vendors, we will merely have reinvented the appliance in distributed form. A program written by a student in São Paulo or Nairobi should not be treated as presumptively dangerous simply because it is new, obscure, or unsigned by a major corporation. Novelty is the oxygen of generativity. A reputation system must be able to distinguish “unknown” from “bad.” It should warn without forbidding. It should let users rely on communities they trust: universities, open-source projects, consumer groups, employers, friends, or security firms. Pluralism matters as much here as openness.

Zittrain is also right to invite ISPs into the discussion, but this is dangerous territory. There is a difference between filtering known spam and malware at the customer’s request and building networks that inspect, classify, and shape all traffic according to private or governmental priorities. The end-to-end principle was never a suicide pact, but neither is security a blank check. If ISPs are to help, their interventions should be transparent, narrowly tailored, appealable, and optional where possible. A user should be able to know what is blocked and why. Otherwise, the infrastructure of protection will become an infrastructure of control.

The broader point is that openness must be designed for the users we actually have, not the users we wish we had. In the early Internet, the community was small enough and skilled enough that norms could do much of the work. Today’s Internet includes children, grandparents, dissidents, doctors, small merchants, criminals, governments, and organized gangs. A humane architecture must assume both generosity and predation.

The locked-down appliance is attractive because it offers a simple answer: trust Apple, Microsoft, Sony, RIM, or your carrier. That answer will sometimes be good enough. There is nothing dishonorable about wanting a phone that makes calls reliably. But if all our digital life migrates to devices and services whose owners can decide what we may run, say, save, or export, then the public will have traded a messy freedom for a polished dependency.

The task, then, is to make the open world less punishing. Reversion, transparency, data portability, user-readable histories, diverse code reputation systems, and civic security institutions are not romantic add-ons. They are the conditions under which ordinary people can remain creators rather than mere subscribers. Zittrain’s deepest claim is that the Internet’s future depends on whether users experience it as a shared environment. I would add: they will do so only if that environment forgives mistakes. Freedom that requires expertise will not remain broadly distributed. Freedom that can recover from error just might.
