Diversified Gemini

"Joseph Carens’s case for amnesty based on the passage of time is fundamentally flawed because it undermines the rule of law and violates the principle of democratic consent."

Joseph Carens argues that the passage of time diminishes the state’s moral right to enforce its immigration laws, eventually transforming an irregular migrant’s illegal presence into a moral entitlement to legal residency and citizenship. This is an emotionally appealing argument, anchored in sympathetic narratives of individuals who have built lives in the shadows. But Carens’s case for amnesty rests on a profound misunderstanding of the rule of law and the nature of democratic consent, ultimately proposing a framework that rewards the successful evasion of law while punishing those who abide by it.

The fundamental flaw in Carens’s argument is his treatment of illegal immigration as a mere administrative oversight, akin to a traffic ticket. He draws a false equivalence between exceeding the speed limit and permanently settling in a country without authorization. A traffic violation is a momentary lapse; illegal settlement is a continuous, daily violation of the nation’s sovereignty and its democratically enacted laws. By asserting that this continuous violation eventually neutralizes the state’s right to enforce its borders, Carens introduces a perverse incentive structure into our legal system: if you break the law long enough and successfully evade detection, the law ceases to apply to you.

This logic is corrosive to the rule of law. Democratic societies function on the premise that laws are binding on all individuals within their jurisdiction. When we grant amnesty based on the mere passage of time, we signal that the law is merely a suggestion for those persistent enough to ignore it. Carens waves away the unfairness this poses to those waiting in the legal immigration queue by claiming that for many, there is no queue. But the absence of a legal avenue for every person who wishes to immigrate does not invalidate the rules we do have. Millions of people around the world respect U.S. and European immigration laws, often waiting years or decades for a chance to migrate legally, or accepting that they cannot. An amnesty tells these law-abiding individuals that they were fools to respect our laws.

Furthermore, Carens’s analogy to statutes of limitations fails under legal scrutiny. A statute of limitations applies to a discrete act committed in the past. If someone commits a robbery and evades capture for twenty years, the state may be barred from prosecuting the original act. But illegal settlement is not a discrete act; it is a continuing offense. Every day an unauthorized migrant remains in the country, they are actively violating the law. There is no statute of limitations on a crime that is still in progress.

Carens also leans heavily on the concept of "social membership," suggesting that simply living and working in a place makes one a member of that society, entitled to the rights of that membership. But in a liberal democracy, membership is a political concept rooted in mutual consent. The migrant has chosen the host country, but the host country, through its laws, has explicitly withheld its consent to the migrant’s presence. To suggest that a migrant can unilaterally impose membership on a political community by defying its laws strips the community of its right to self-determination.

We can feel deep sympathy for individuals like Miguel Sanchez or Hiu Lui Ng without concluding that their illegal acts entitle them to legal status. The tragedy of their vulnerability to deportation is real, but it is a vulnerability born of their own choices to bypass the legal framework of the society they sought to join. A nation that cannot decide who enters and remains within its borders ceases to be a sovereign democratic state. We must enforce our laws consistently, not dissolve them simply because they have been broken for a long time.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Illegal settlement is a continuous violation of national sovereignty rather than a discrete administrative oversight like a traffic ticket.
  2. 02Granting amnesty based on the duration of a violation creates a perverse incentive that rewards those who successfully evade the law while penalizing those who follow legal procedures.
  3. 03The legal concept of statutes of limitations is inapplicable to unauthorized residence because the offense is ongoing rather than a completed past act.
  4. 04Social membership in a liberal democracy must be rooted in mutual consent between the individual and the political community, not unilaterally imposed by the migrant through the defiance of laws.
  5. 05A sovereign democratic state must maintain the right to determine who enters and remains within its borders to ensure the consistent application of its laws.
Diversified Gemini

"The passage of time does not grant irregular migrants a moral right to residency because social membership cannot be ethically derived from a unilateral, non-consensual trespass against a political community's sovereignty."

In "The Case for Amnesty," Joseph Carens presents a seemingly straightforward moral equation: the longer an irregular migrant resides in a country, the stronger their moral claim to stay, eventually crossing a threshold where deportation becomes unjust. While this argument is intuitively appealing, it relies on a profound philosophical asymmetry regarding the nature of time and consent. Carens asks the state to honor the passage of time, but he ignores the fact that this time was stolen, not granted.

Carens bases his argument on the idea of "social membership" developing organically over time. He correctly notes that people put down roots, form relationships, and become part of a community. But he glosses over the crucial fact that these roots were planted against the explicit, democratically codified will of the host society.

In any other ethical framework, we do not allow an individual to generate moral claims against another party through unilateral, non-consensual action. If I pitch a tent in my neighbor’s backyard without their permission, the fact that I have lived there for five years, cultivated a garden, and befriended their dog does not generate a moral right for me to permanently appropriate their land. The passage of time does not sanitize the initial lack of consent; it compounds the trespass.

Carens attempts to circumvent this by suggesting that immigration violations are victimless, administrative matters. But the political community is the victim when its sovereign right to self-determination is bypassed. By entering and remaining illegally, the migrant forces the host society into a relationship it explicitly rejected.

When Carens argues that the state's right to deport "erodes" with time, he is effectively saying that defiance of the law eventually nullifies the law. This creates a deeply unstable moral framework where rights are not derived from mutual agreement or democratic process, but from endurance and the successful evasion of authority.

Furthermore, Carens’s threshold of five to seven years is entirely arbitrary, as he himself admits. But if the moral weight of social membership is so profound that it overrides the state's sovereignty, why does it magically crystallize at year five? A child brought at age two has deep social membership by age four. By reducing human attachment to a crude numerical timeline, Carens reveals the artificiality of his construct.

We can recognize the tragic human consequences of deportation without conceding that the migrant has somehow *earned* a moral right to stay simply by outlasting the enforcement mechanisms of the state. Time spent in violation of a community's consent cannot, by itself, forge a moral demand for membership in that community.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The roots and social ties formed by irregular migrants are established against the explicit, democratically codified will of the host society.
  2. 02In ethical frameworks, individuals are not permitted to generate moral claims against others through persistent, non-consensual actions.
  3. 03The political community is a victim of irregular migration because its sovereign right to self-determination is bypassed.
  4. 04Allowing the state's right to deport to erode over time suggests that successful defiance and evasion of the law eventually nullify the law itself.
  5. 05The arbitrary nature of a five-to-seven-year threshold for amnesty reveals that social membership is an artificial construct rather than a profound moral absolute.
  6. 06One can acknowledge the tragic human consequences of deportation without conceding that a moral right to stay has been earned through the successful evasion of authority.
