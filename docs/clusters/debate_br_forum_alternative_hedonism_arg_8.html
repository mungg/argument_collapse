"The transition to a post-carbon society must be framed through feminist political economy, where the redistribution of time is the essential prerequisite for replacing commodity dependence with the pleasures of social reproduction and self-cultivation."

**The Time We Don't Have: Alternative Hedonism as Feminist Political Economy**

Kate Soper's lead essay is the best short statement I have read of why the climate transition cannot succeed as a politics of austerity. Her core move — refusing the assumption that consumerist affluence is the standard against which postcarbon life will look diminished — opens a door that environmental politics has kept stubbornly closed. I want to push that door wider by reading alternative hedonism through a frame Soper touches on but does not develop: feminist political economy and the politics of time.

Begin with what Soper calls the "work-dominated, time-scarce, junk-ridden 'affluence'" of contemporary life. The phrase is precise. The defining experience of the affluent worker today is not abundance but harriedness. We outsource cooking because we have no time to cook; we outsource childcare because we have no time to parent; we outsource cleaning, laundry, eldercare, dog-walking, even friendship (in the form of paid therapy) because we have no time for any of it. The market then sells us back, at a markup, the relationships and capacities our work has stolen from us. Hochschild called this the "commercialization of intimate life" twenty years ago, and it has only deepened. The fast-food meal, the gig-delivered groceries, the Amazon impulse purchase at 11 p.m. — these are not expressions of decadent desire. They are coping mechanisms of people whose discretionary time has been confiscated by waged labor and the unwaged labor of social reproduction that piles on top of it.

Once you see this, Soper's argument acquires a sharper edge. The pleasures she catalogues — cooking from scratch, mending clothes, growing vegetables, walking children to school, lingering in the street, learning an instrument — are not whims. They are the activities of social reproduction and self-cultivation that capitalism has progressively stripped out of life and either commodified or eliminated. They are, overwhelmingly, activities historically performed by women, undervalued precisely because they were unwaged, and now invisible enough that we no longer recognize their loss as loss. To recover them as pleasures is to reverse one of the longest movements of capitalist modernity: the conversion of self-provisioning into commodity dependence.

This is why I think the feminist dimension of alternative hedonism is not optional or supplementary. It is the conceptual key. The thing that has to be redistributed is not, in the first instance, stuff. It is time — and not time as an abstract quantity but time understood through the lens of who currently has it, who currently spends it on whom, and what activities have been priced out of the lives of the time-poor.

Consider the four-day week, the central practical demand of Soper's program. In a conventional framing, it is a labor demand: workers should get more leisure. But this framing already absorbs the patriarchal assumption that what happens outside paid work is "leisure" — golf, hobbies, rest. For most adults, especially most women, the hours outside paid work are not leisure but a second shift of cooking, cleaning, caring, scheduling, emotional labor, school logistics, and the management of household supply chains. A four-day week distributed equally between partners, combined with serious public investment in childcare and eldercare and with cultural permission for men to actually do the care work, would do more for the lived condition of women than any number of representation campaigns. It would also, incidentally, make Soper's other proposals possible, because the slower pleasures she invokes — cooking, walking, conviviality — all presuppose discretionary time that is presently monopolized by paid and unpaid labor.

Lida Maxwell, quoted in the lead essay, warns that uncritical celebrations of "desire" risk reinscribing the white, heteronormative subject of consumer society. She is right, and the warning bites. The historical archive of anti-consumerism contains more than its share of moralism aimed at women's pleasures specifically: the fashion-conscious woman as the symbol of frivolity, the suburban housewife as the avatar of mass culture, shopping itself as a feminized vice. Soper is too sophisticated to fall into this trap explicitly, but the trap is set by the genre, and her essay does not fully disarm it. An alternative hedonism worth having will have to specify that the pleasures of fashion, ornament, sexual expression, and self-fashioning are not the enemy. The enemy is the system that makes those pleasures available only through the planet-destroying churn of fast fashion and the body-shaming surveillance of social-media advertising.

There is a constructive path here. The slow-fashion movement, the rise of dressmaking and mending communities, the resurgence of swap economies and "shrub" libraries — these are not the death of style but its democratization. Conviviality and ornament are perfectly compatible. The Black feminist tradition, in particular, has long insisted that pleasure, adornment, and beauty are not bourgeois indulgences but resources of survival and resistance, and any postconsumerist politics that cannot make room for that insistence will fail.

This leads me to my second point of expansion. Soper's structure of feeling is rooted, I think, in a register that is still recognizably European, white, and a bit melancholic — the lost world of unhurried streets and artisanal bakers. There is nothing wrong with that register, but it is not the only one available. Across the Global South, and within diasporic communities in the North, alternative hedonisms already exist in robust, lived form. The extended family meal that goes on for hours; the open courtyard cooled by neighbors' chatter; the markets that double as social centers; the cooperative childcare of multigenerational households; the ritual calendar of festivals and saints' days — these are not nostalgic fragments but ongoing practices, eroded in many places by exactly the work-and-spend dynamic Soper criticizes. A genuinely global alternative hedonism would treat these as central exhibits, not honorable mentions.

Nemonte Nenquimo's words, which Soper quotes, point in this direction. The Waorani critique of extractivist civilization is not just about ecology; it is about the spiritual poverty of a culture that cannot sit still. Indigenous and feminist epistemologies converge on a striking point: the most exhausted thing in the affluent world is the affluent worker, and the most enviable resource in many "underdeveloped" places is precisely the social fabric that the affluent world has burned for fuel.

What follows for politics? Three things. First, the demand for time — shorter hours, public holidays, paid leave, predictable schedules — should be foregrounded as the climate demand, because nothing else in Soper's program is possible without it. Second, the infrastructure of social reproduction — childcare, eldercare, healthcare, schools, parks, libraries, transit — should be understood as climate infrastructure, because it is what permits low-carbon, high-conviviality life. Third, alternative hedonism should explicitly disavow any romance of the simple life that erases gendered labor; the goal is not to send anyone back to the kitchen but to make the kitchen, the street, and the workplace each less totalizing than they have become.

Soper has given us a frame strong enough to carry these expansions. The task now is to fill it in with the texture of lives actually lived — and to insist that the politics of pleasure is, before anything else, a politics of time.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Modern consumerist affluence is actually a state of time-scarcity where the market profits by selling back the intimate services and relationships that overwork has forced individuals to outsource.
  2. 02The 'alternative' pleasures proposed—such as cooking, mending, and caretaking—are historically undervalued forms of social reproduction that must be reclaimed from capitalist commodification.
  3. 03A shorter workweek is a vital feminist demand because it addresses the 'second shift' of unpaid domestic labor that currently prevents women from accessing leisure and slower forms of consumption.
  4. 04To avoid the historical trap of moralizing against feminized pleasures like fashion and adornment, alternative hedonism must focus on democratizing style through slow-fashion and community-based swap economies.
  5. 05A global vision of alternative hedonism should move beyond European nostalgia by centering existing practices of conviviality and social fabric found in Indigenous and Global South cultures.
  6. 06Climate infrastructure must be redefined to include the institutions of social reproduction, such as childcare and public parks, which facilitate a low-carbon, high-conviviality lifestyle.
