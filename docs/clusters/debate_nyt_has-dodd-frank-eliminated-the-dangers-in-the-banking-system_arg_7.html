Diversified Claude

"Dodd-Frank has failed to eliminate the dangers in the banking system because its dense, technocratic complexity relies on the same fallible regulatory judgment that failed during the 2008 crisis."

Both camps in this debate are missing the point. The question is not really whether Dodd-Frank is sufficient or whether the banks should be broken up. The question is whether our entire approach to financial regulation—dense, technocratic, discretionary—is capable of containing the risks of modern finance. I am increasingly convinced it is not.

Dodd-Frank is roughly 2,300 pages of legislation that has spawned, by various counts, more than 22,000 pages of regulations, with rule-making still incomplete more than five years after passage. The Volcker Rule alone runs nearly a thousand pages because its prohibition on proprietary trading must somehow be reconciled with permitted market-making, hedging, and underwriting. Compliance costs at the largest banks run into the billions annually. And yet, when regulators evaluated the living wills last week, they concluded that five of the eight biggest banks still cannot demonstrate they could fail safely.

This is the central paradox: the more complex our regulatory apparatus becomes, the more it resembles the complex institutions it tries to control, and the more it relies on the same regulatory judgment that failed catastrophically in 2007-08. Alan Greenspan, Tim Geithner, Ben Bernanke—these were not incompetent people. They were highly intelligent regulators who missed the crisis because the system they were watching had grown beyond comprehension. Dodd-Frank's response was to make the system more complicated, not less.

A better approach would be radically simpler rules. Anat Admati and Martin Hellwig have argued persuasively for equity capital requirements of 20-30 percent of total assets—not risk-weighted assets, which invite gaming, but raw assets. A bank funded with that much equity simply cannot fail in a way that threatens the system, because shareholders absorb losses before creditors panic. Such a rule would fit on a single page. It would not require armies of supervisors interpreting derivative exposures or living-will scenarios.

Sanders is right that too-big-to-fail remains an unsolved problem, but his break-up remedy is a blunt instrument. Clinton is right that Dodd-Frank made progress, but her defense underestimates how brittle that progress is and how dependent it remains on regulators continuing to do their jobs well during a boom—precisely when political pressure is greatest to ease up.

The honest answer to the debate question is: no, Dodd-Frank has not eliminated the dangers, and no plausible amendment to it will. We need fewer rules but much stronger ones, applied mechanically rather than discretionarily. That is a conversation neither presidential candidate is having.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The massive scale of Dodd-Frank regulations and compliance costs has not prevented major banks from failing to demonstrate they can go through bankruptcy safely.
  2. 02The complexity of current financial regulations makes the system impossible to manage and invites the same oversight failures seen in 2007-08.
  3. 03A more effective solution would be a radically simpler rule, such as high non-risk-weighted equity capital requirements, which would absorb losses without requiring discretionary supervision.
  4. 04Dodd-Frank's progress is inherently brittle because it depends on regulators resisting political pressure to ease oversight during economic booms.
