"The strategic focus on candidate ideology is misplaced because the nationalization of elections is driven by a transformed media environment that requires Democrats to prioritize a coordinated national narrative over individual candidate positioning."

The Bonica and Grumbach essay represents the best thinking in contemporary political science on Democratic electoral strategy. I want to push their argument in one direction they only partially develop: the role of media and information environments in shaping what is politically possible.

The authors correctly note that candidate ideology matters less than it once did and that elections are nationalized referendums. But they do not fully explore why this is true. The answer lies in the transformation of American media. When voters consumed local newspapers and local television, they encountered candidate-specific information. Now they consume national media—cable news, social media, podcasts—that frames everything through a national lens.

This creates both constraints and opportunities. The constraint is that local candidates cannot easily escape national partisan tides. The opportunity is that national frames can be changed. If Democrats can shift the national narrative in their direction, down-ballot candidates benefit automatically.

This suggests that the strategic emphasis on candidate positioning—whether moderate or progressive—is somewhat misplaced. The real leverage point is the national narrative. Which party is seen as representing the future? Which party is seen as having solutions to the problems voters care about? These questions cannot be answered at the candidate level; they require coordinated communication strategy at the party level.

The anti-corruption frame the authors propose is promising precisely because it can travel across media environments. Corruption is a compelling narrative that works on cable news, social media, and conversation alike. It offers a villain (the corrupt insider) and a hero (the reformer) that voters can immediately understand.

But executing this strategy requires something Democrats currently lack: a coordinated communications infrastructure that can sustain a narrative across media environments. This means investing in surrogates, commentators, and messengers who can amplify the chosen frame. It means disciplined messaging that does not allow the opposition to set terms.

The authors are right that experimentation is needed. I would add: experimentation at the narrative level, not just the policy level. Democrats have spent too much time debating which policies to emphasize and too little time building the capacity to make a narrative dominant.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The decline of local media and the rise of nationalized digital platforms prevent local candidates from escaping national partisan tides through individual moderation.
  2. 02National media environments offer a strategic opportunity to benefit down-ballot candidates automatically if the party can successfully shift the overarching national narrative.
  3. 03An anti-corruption frame is particularly effective because its narrative structure of 'villains' and 'heroes' translates easily across modern, fragmented media platforms.
  4. 04To succeed, Democrats must move beyond policy debates and invest in a robust communications infrastructure capable of sustaining and amplifying a dominant national message.
