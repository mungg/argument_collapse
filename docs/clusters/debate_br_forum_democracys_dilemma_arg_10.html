"Farrell and Schneier’s framework for protecting democracy is flawed because it prioritizes technocratic stability and the management of information over the radical, disruptive contestation that is essential for true democratic renewal."

Farrell and Schneier’s “Democracy’s Dilemma” is an impressive synthesis of political science and information security. They rightly diagnose the inadequacy of the Millian free‑speech model in an era of automated propaganda, and they propose to replace static equilibrium theories with a dynamical one. But their account remains trapped within a liberal problematic that prizes stability and manages disruption through technical fixes. Democracy is not primarily a system to be stabilized; it is a space of contestation in which the very terms of political life are at stake. By focusing on “attack surfaces” and “negative feedback loops,” the authors risk depoliticizing the conflicts that are democracy’s lifeblood. A truly robust democratic response would not seek to dampen dissonance but to radicalize it, turning informational chaos into an engine for deeper democratic transformation.

The lead essay’s central metaphor—Democracy’s Dilemma as a mirror of the Dictator’s Dilemma—is illuminating but misleading. Autocrats face a tradeoff because they fear information will reveal their illegitimacy and enable collective action. Democracies, by contrast, are supposed to be built on the ongoing revelation of illegitimacy: citizens elect representatives to contest existing arrangements, and they protect the right to organize against unjust laws. The “dilemma” for democrats is not how to allow just enough information to keep the economy humming while preventing revolution; it is how to ensure that those with power cannot shut down the challenges that threaten their dominance. From this perspective, the problems Farrell and Schneier identify—targeted disinformation, flooding, the erosion of shared knowledge—are not symmetrical threats to democracy but asymmetrical weapons wielded by the powerful to preserve their hegemony.

Take the examples. The FCC net neutrality comments were flooded with fake pro‑repeal comments by telecom lobbyists. This was not an accident of an open system; it was a deliberate tactic by corporations to counterfeit popular support and neutralize genuine public outrage. The solution is not to authenticate everyone, which would merely shift power to those who can afford to organize authenticated campaigns, but to dismantle the regulatory capture that makes such fraud effective. Similarly, the Trump administration’s pursuit of a citizenship question on the census is not an “information attack” from outside; it is a domestic political strategy to reduce the representation of noncitizens and shift resources away from immigrant communities. The conflict is over who counts as a member of the demos, and that is a quintessentially political struggle, not an informational one. To frame it as a vulnerability requiring better “common political knowledge” is to pretend that there is a neutral, technical baseline when in fact the census has always been a tool of racial and political ordering.

The authors valorize the creative power of democratic disagreement, citing Nancy Rosenblum and the civil rights movement. Yet their proposed feedback loops aim to contain disagreement within bounds that preserve the “dynamic equilibrium.” What if the equilibrium itself is the problem? Jim Crow was an equilibrium; so, arguably, is the current neoliberal order that generates massive inequality and disenfranchisement while channeling dissent into manageable electoral cycles. The civil rights movement didn’t just add new voices to an existing consensus; it shattered the old consensus and rewrote the rules. That kind of rupture requires not carefully calibrated feedback loops but massive, disruptive mobilization that may well exacerbate distrust and polarization. The Black Lives Matter and #MeToo movements have used social media to challenge established knowledge and institutions, often in ways that Farrell and Schneier might class as “destabilizing.” That destabilization, however, is necessary for democratic renewal.

The danger of the information security lens is that it treats all destabilization as similarly threatening. Russian interference in 2016 sought to deepen divisions, yes, but it did so by feeding on preexisting fractures. The correct response is not to try to return to a pre‑lapsarian unity—which never truly existed—but to address the material conditions that make so many Americans receptive to anti‑system narratives: economic precarity, racial resentment, and political corruption. Until we tackle those, any informational fix will be a Maginot Line.

A more promising democratic project would draw on the agonistic tradition of political theory, associated with thinkers like Chantal Mouffe. In this view, democracy is constituted by conflict between adversaries who respect each other’s right to contest, but who profoundly disagree about the common good. The challenge is to transform antagonistic enemies into agonistic adversaries who fight fiercely but accept democratic rules. The erosion of that transformation is what we are witnessing, as Republicans increasingly treat Democrats as illegitimate, and vice versa. Rebuilding it requires a politics that gives people meaningful stakes and collective identities, not technocratic management of the information sphere.

Farrell and Schneier are right that we need new disciplinary alliances. But instead of pairing political scientists with information security experts, we should be connecting social movements, labor unions, community organizers, and independent media makers with the developers of decentralized, open‑source technologies. Democracy’s resilience lies not in its procedural integrity alone but in the organized power of ordinary people to demand change. The Internet can facilitate that power, but only if we fight to keep it out of the hands of the corporations and state actors that want to tame it. The goal is not to secure the democratic system as it is, but to make it more democratic.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The authors' focus on 'attack surfaces' and 'feedback loops' risks depoliticizing fundamental conflicts and treating political struggles as mere technical vulnerabilities.
  2. 02The 'Democracy’s Dilemma' metaphor incorrectly suggests a symmetry between autocracy and democracy, failing to recognize that democracy is built on the ongoing revelation of illegitimacy rather than its suppression.
  3. 03The informational problems identified, such as the FCC comment flooding, are actually asymmetrical weapons used by powerful corporate and state actors to preserve hegemony rather than neutral system failures.
  4. 04Proposed solutions like authentication and institutional reform ignore the fact that the 'equilibrium' being protected may itself be an unjust order that requires rupture rather than stabilization.
  5. 05The information security lens mistakenly treats all forms of destabilization as threats, potentially delegitimizing necessary social movements that challenge established power structures.
  6. 06True democratic resilience requires addressing the underlying material conditions of inequality and corruption rather than implementing technocratic fixes to the information sphere.
  7. 07A robust democratic project should focus on empowering social movements and decentralized technologies to facilitate collective action rather than pairing political science with security expertise to manage dissent.
