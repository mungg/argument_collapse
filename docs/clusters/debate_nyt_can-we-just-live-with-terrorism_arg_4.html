Diversified Claude

"Societies should reject the rhetoric of "living with terrorism" because it represents a preemptive resignation that absolves the political class of its responsibility to address fixable policy failures."

The phrase "live with terrorism" should make us deeply uncomfortable, and the discomfort is appropriate. Manuel Valls deserved the criticism he received, not because he was wrong about the difficulty of preventing every attack, but because the language of accommodation is itself a form of surrender.

Words matter in politics. When a head of government tells citizens they must "live with" something, he sets the terms of public expectation. He defines the horizon of the possible. Telling the French people, three days after 84 of their compatriots were mowed down at a national celebration, that this is the new normal — that is not stoicism, it is preemptive resignation. It tells the families of the dead that their losses are part of the price of doing business as a modern European nation.

There is nothing inevitable about the current wave of jihadist violence in Europe. It has identifiable causes: the collapse of Syria and Iraq, the rise of ISIS as a state-sponsor of attacks abroad, the radicalization of marginalized young men in the banlieues, porous external borders, intelligence services that share too little, prisons that function as jihadist seminaries, and an integration model that has failed in measurable ways. Each of these is a policy failure, and each could in principle be addressed. To shrug and say "we must live with it" is to absolve the political class of responsibility for fixing things that are fixable.

Compare the response to other catastrophic risks. We do not tell parents that they must "live with" school shootings, or air travelers that they must "live with" plane crashes. We pour resources into prevention, we investigate causes, we change procedures, and we expect improvement. Terrorism deserves the same seriousness — not as an excuse for emergency powers without end, but as a problem to be solved through patient, intelligent statecraft.

Yes, freedoms must be protected. The choice is not between liberty and security in some simple zero-sum way. But the answer to a security failure is to fix the failure, not to redefine it as a permanent condition of life. Valls essentially announced that the French state has given up trying to keep its citizens safe at public gatherings. That is an extraordinary admission, and the public was right to recoil from it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The language of accommodation functions as a form of surrender that defines terrorism as an acceptable 'new normal' for citizens.
  2. 02The current wave of violence is not an inevitability but rather the result of specific, identifiable policy failures in areas like intelligence, integration, and border security.
  3. 03Treating terrorism as a permanent condition of life ignores the fact that other catastrophic risks are met with rigorous prevention and demands for improvement rather than passive acceptance.
  4. 04The state's primary duty is to fix security failures rather than redefining them as an unavoidable part of the modern experience.
Diversified MiniMax

"Societies should not accept "living with terrorism" as a governing philosophy because it represents an abdication of the state's primary responsibility to protect its citizens."

"Living with terrorism" sounds like resignation. It sounds like a prime minister who has run out of ideas and is asking a grieving nation to lower its expectations. In the immediate aftermath of Nice—84 dead, including children, lying on the Promenade des Anglais—that is simply not good enough.

The problem with accepting "living with terrorism" as policy is that it mistakes a description of reality for a prescription for action. Yes, we are currently living with terrorism; we have no choice. But that is a condition to be overcome, not a permanent state to be embraced. When Manuel Valls says France must learn to live with attacks, he is implicitly conceding that the state's primary function—protecting its citizens from violence—is beyond reach. That is a dangerous concession.

We need to ask harder questions. Why does France face this persistent threat? What intelligence failures preceded Nice? What systemic vulnerabilities were exploited? These questions point toward actionable remedies: better coordination between French domestic intelligence and their European partners, more robust border controls within the Schengen zone, greater attention to the ideological ecosystems that produce lone-wolf attackers, and sustained investment in the kind of community policing that can identify threats before they materialize.

To "live with" terrorism as a governing philosophy means accepting that our security apparatus is essentially reactive—playing defense after the attack has already occurred. That is not a strategy; it is an abdication of responsibility. The families of Nice deserve a government that promises more than endurance.

This does not require abandoning our values or descending into authoritarianism. But it does require refusing to normalize the abnormal, demanding better from our institutions, and maintaining the political will to confront an enemy that operates across borders and ideologies.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Accepting terrorism as a permanent state mistakes a current reality for an unchangeable condition that cannot be overcome.
  2. 02The philosophy of "living with" terrorism suggests that the state's security apparatus is merely reactive rather than proactive.
  3. 03Governments should focus on actionable remedies such as intelligence coordination, border controls, and community policing rather than resignation.
  4. 04Refusing to normalize terrorism does not require a descent into authoritarianism but rather a demand for institutional accountability and political will.
Diversified Gemini

"Society should not accept "living with terrorism" because it is a fatalistic framing that ignores the state's responsibility to address the domestic social failures that cause radicalization."

When Manuel Valls tells the French people they must "live with terrorism," he employs a fatalistic framing that intentionally obscures the state's own failures. Treating terrorism as if it were a natural disaster—an unpredictable hurricane or earthquake that society must simply weather—is highly convenient for politicians. It shifts the focus away from the structural and systemic domestic issues that are manufacturing radicalization right in their own backyards.

We should not accept "living with terrorism" because doing so means accepting the broken social fabric that produces it. The terrorists striking France and Belgium are, by and large, homegrown. They are citizens of Europe. To pretend this is purely an external threat imported from the deserts of Syria is to ignore the glaring reality of Europe’s marginalized communities.

For decades, France has relegated its immigrant and Muslim populations to the *banlieues*—neglected, poverty-stricken suburbs that encircle major cities. These areas suffer from astronomical youth unemployment, crumbling infrastructure, and rampant police discrimination. Young men growing up in these environments are told they are French, yet they are systematically denied the promises of *Liberté, égalité, fraternité*. They face discrimination in the job market based on their names and addresses, and they are routinely profiled by the state.

This deep sense of alienation and humiliation creates fertile ground for radical recruiters. The Islamic State offers these marginalized young men a twisted sense of purpose, belonging, and empowerment that their own society has denied them.

Refusing to "live with terrorism" doesn't mean imposing draconian security measures; it means doing the hard, generational work of true social integration. It requires massive economic investment in the *banlieues*, comprehensive educational reform, and a ruthless cracking down on institutional discrimination in housing and employment. It requires a rethinking of *laïcité* (secularism) so that it functions as a neutral space for all, rather than a weapon used to aggressively police the visibility of Muslim citizens.

As long as European nations continue to produce a permanent, resentful underclass, they will continue to produce terrorism. We do not have to live with this violence, but to cure it, we must be willing to look in the mirror and fix the deeply broken societies we have built.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Treating terrorism as an unavoidable natural disaster allows politicians to deflect blame from their own systemic policy failures.
  2. 02The threat is primarily homegrown, stemming from the marginalization and alienation of citizens within Europe's own borders.
  3. 03The systemic neglect and discrimination faced by residents in the banlieues create a sense of humiliation that makes young men vulnerable to radical recruitment.
  4. 04Ending terrorism requires proactive social and economic integration, including investment in infrastructure and the reform of discriminatory secularist policies.
  5. 05Terrorism will persist as long as European nations continue to maintain a permanent, resentful underclass through institutional exclusion.
