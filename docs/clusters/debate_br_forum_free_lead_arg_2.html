"Governor Roemer’s proposed campaign finance reforms are logically inconsistent and practically insufficient because they attempt to solve systemic corruption while adhering to a restrictive constitutional framework that protects the very spending he seeks to curb."

**The First Amendment Problem Roemer Cannot Wish Away**

Governor Roemer's speech is most persuasive when it is most concrete: the Research and Experimentation tax credit perpetually almost-permanent, the Primary Dealer Credit Facility lending Goldman Sachs hundreds of billions at near-zero rates, the New Democrat Coalition retreating to the Eastern Shore with the lobbyists they were supposed to be regulating. These are vivid and damning. They do the work of a thousand abstractions about "the system."

It is less persuasive—indeed, it is somewhat incoherent—when it tries to translate that anger into constitutional doctrine. I want to focus on that incoherence, because I think it reveals a problem that runs through virtually all modern campaign finance reform proposals, including the ones I am sympathetic to.

Roemer wants to "craft a system of independence for Congress within the boundaries given to us by the Court." Good. He then offers four principles. The first is that "the system should not silence anyone or any view." This, he says, is "the kernel of truth in the *Citizens United* court decision." The second is that "no system should force any citizen to support political speech that he or she doesn't believe in." Together, these principles are doing far more work than Roemer acknowledges.

Principle one rules out aggregate spending caps. Principle two rules out tax-funded public financing of the conventional sort. Roemer admits this: he says his principles "would veto most of the reform proposals of the past 30 years," including McCain-Feingold and the Fair Elections Now Act. So we are left with a narrow corridor: voluntary small-donor systems funded by voucher mechanisms in which each citizen redirects a sliver of his or her own taxes. Even here, there is a problem—if my taxes generate the voucher, then in some sense I am still being "forced" to participate in campaign finance, even if I choose where my voucher goes. The principle either swallows the voucher or it doesn't, and Roemer never tells us why it doesn't.

This matters because the Court's First Amendment jurisprudence is not a stable container into which we can pour any reform. *Buckley v. Valeo* (1976) drew a famously brittle line between contributions, which can be limited, and expenditures, which generally cannot. *Citizens United* (2010) extended free-expenditure rights to corporations. *SpeechNow.org v. FEC* (D.C. Cir. 2010) effectively created the modern Super PAC by holding that contribution limits cannot constitutionally apply to groups that make only independent expenditures. The trajectory is unmistakable: the Court is reading the First Amendment to protect more and more political spending from regulation. Roemer's "voucher plus individual contributions" model may survive, but anything that looks like a serious constraint on big money outside of voluntary participation will not.

This is the deep dilemma. The honest options are roughly three:

First, the libertarian option: accept that *Buckley* and *Citizens United* are correct readings of the First Amendment, and concentrate on disclosure and anti-corruption measures. This was Justice Kennedy's view in *Citizens United*: the cure for bad speech is more speech and full transparency, not regulation. The trouble is that disclosure has been steadily eroded by the rise of 501(c)(4) "dark money" vehicles, and the Court has not been particularly aggressive in defending disclosure either.

Second, the constitutional amendment option: amend the First Amendment, or add a clause specifying that political spending may be regulated. This is honest but politically distant. No amendment has passed in forty years, and the threshold—two-thirds of both houses, three-fourths of state legislatures—is formidable.

Third, the public financing option: build a parallel system of public funds robust enough to render private spending optional rather than essential. This is what the Fair Elections Now Act gestures toward, and what Roemer rules out by his second principle.

Roemer has chosen, in effect, a fourth option: voluntary individual virtue plus modest voucher experiments. I respect the consistency, but I do not think it scales. As long as candidates who reject the voucher system can outraise candidates who accept it, the system will collapse under its own incentive structure. We saw this in 2008, when Barack Obama became the first major-party nominee since the post-Watergate reforms to decline public general-election funding, precisely because the available funds were dwarfed by what he could raise privately. Any system that depends on candidate opt-in is one transformative fundraiser away from irrelevance.

There is one more concern. Roemer says, with feeling, that "elections are the time for clearly labeled debate. They are not the time for the ugliest and least responsible political speech of our culture to drown out any hope for political dialogue." I agree as a matter of taste. But "clearly labeled" and "responsible" are not constitutional categories in this country. The First Amendment protects the ugly and the irresponsible alongside the elevated and the careful. Reformers who want to clean up political discourse will discover, again and again, that they are bumping against a constitutional culture that protects speech they detest.

The way out is harder than Roemer admits. He is right to start the conversation. But the conversation has to include the Constitution itself, not as a fixed boundary but as something the political branches can, with enormous effort, eventually move. Otherwise we are arguing about the furniture in a burning room.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Roemer’s principles create a narrow and incoherent policy space by simultaneously ruling out aggregate spending caps and traditional tax-funded public financing.
  2. 02The Supreme Court’s First Amendment trajectory increasingly protects political spending, making any reform that lacks serious constraints on 'big money' ineffective.
  3. 03A voluntary voucher system is destined to fail because it cannot compete with the incentive structure of private fundraising, as demonstrated by candidates who opt out of public funding to outraise their opponents.
  4. 04Roemer’s desire to eliminate 'irresponsible' political speech conflicts with a constitutional culture that protects ugly or anonymous speech just as much as elevated debate.
  5. 05Meaningful reform requires addressing the Constitution itself as a malleable target for change rather than treating current judicial doctrine as an unchangeable boundary.

"While Governor Roemer correctly identifies that systemic fundraising corruption prevents Congress from governing, his proposed solutions are too modest and personal to address the structural and constitutional roots of the problem."

**The Diagnosis Is Right, the Prescription Is Too Small**

Governor Roemer deserves credit for naming the disease so plainly: a Congress whose every waking calculation runs through the donor class is not a Congress capable of governing. His account of the Research and Experimentation tax credit—permanent in effect but temporary by design, precisely so it can be auctioned again and again—captures what political scientists have started calling the "extortion" model of fundraising. Members do not need to be bribed; they need only to keep the spigot open. On this, Roemer is exactly right, and his willingness to say it from inside the system, as a former Congressman, governor, and bank president, lends the indictment a weight that activists cannot supply.

But his cure is far too modest for the disease he diagnoses. A self-imposed $100 cap and a PAC-free pledge are fine personal ethics. They are not a system. A presidential campaign that raises money exclusively in $100 increments is a feel-good thought experiment; it does not bind the next candidate, and it does nothing about the actual river of money flowing through Super PACs and 501(c)(4)s in the wake of *Citizens United* and *SpeechNow*. Roemer himself acknowledges the 130 percent surge in independent expenditures in 2010. His four principles, taken seriously, leave that surge untouched.

Consider the "democracy voucher" idea he gestures toward—small public allocations that citizens direct to candidates who agree to accept only small donations. Bruce Ackerman and Ian Ayres proposed something like this nearly a decade ago in *Voting With Dollars*. Lawrence Lessig has been refining variants of it since. It is a serious idea, and I am for it. But Roemer treats it almost as an afterthought, sandwiched between general principles, when in fact it is the only structural element of his program that could conceivably scale. He should be running on the voucher, not on his personal donation limit.

Second, Roemer's insistence on working "within the boundaries given to us by the Court" is prudent but ultimately inadequate. *Citizens United* and *Buckley v. Valeo* together construct a constitutional architecture in which money is speech, corporations are speakers, and any aggregate limit on political spending is suspect. Within that architecture, the most reformers can do is expand the pool of small donors and hope to dilute the big ones. That is worth doing. But to pretend it will be sufficient is to misread the Court's trajectory. A constitutional amendment clarifying that Congress may regulate election spending is not a fringe position; it is the logical endpoint of the diagnosis Roemer has offered.

Third, Roemer's framing of the financial crisis as a story of corrupted regulation is powerful but incomplete. Yes, Wall Street bought deregulation. Yes, Glass-Steagall's repeal mattered. But the deeper problem is regulatory capture in the technical sense Stigler described forty years ago: agencies staffed by the regulated, revolving doors that turn faster than legislative cycles, and an epistemic monoculture in which Treasury, the Fed, and Goldman Sachs share the same alumni directory. Capping donations will not unwind that. We need post-employment restrictions with real teeth, a strengthened Office of Financial Research independent of the banks it studies, and—Roemer hints at this but does not push—a return to size and activity limits on systemically important institutions. The Dodd-Frank bill he rightly criticizes failed not only because Wall Street bought concessions but because Congress lacks the expert capacity to write the rules its lobbyists won't.

Fourth, a friendly worry about Roemer's tone. He writes as though the public, freed from the corrupting influence of money, would naturally converge on sensible governance. I doubt it. The Tea Party rallies and the union halls are both full of small donors, and they do not agree on what "good governance" looks like. Money is one corruption; tribal polarization is another, and the two interact in ways that small-donor democracy may exacerbate rather than soothe. The most prolific small donors in both parties tend to be the most ideologically extreme. A purer democracy of $100 checks could be a more polarized one. Reformers should think hard about this before promising that getting the money out will let reason back in.

None of this is to dismiss Roemer. A presidential candidate willing to make money in politics the issue—not a side issue, not a clause in a platform, but the issue—is genuinely valuable, especially given how thoroughly both parties have learned to monetize their offices. He is unlikely to win his party's nomination. He may not even make a debate stage. But the test of his campaign should not be electoral. The test is whether he forces his rivals, and the press, to take this question seriously. On that score, the speech is a start.

The diagnosis is right. The prescription needs more medicine.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Individual pledges to cap donations and avoid PACs are personal ethical choices that fail to address the massive flow of independent expenditures enabled by Citizens United.
  2. 02Structural reforms like 'democracy vouchers' should be the central focus of the platform rather than an afterthought, as they are the only proposals capable of scaling to a national level.
  3. 03Working strictly within current Supreme Court boundaries is insufficient, necessitating a constitutional amendment to allow for the actual regulation of election spending.
  4. 04Capping donations does not address the deeper issue of regulatory capture, which requires technical solutions like post-employment restrictions and increased expert capacity within government agencies.
  5. 05The assumption that removing big money will naturally lead to sensible governance ignores the fact that small-donor democracy often fuels ideological polarization.

"While Governor Roemer correctly identifies that campaign fundraising has corrupted congressional governance, his proposed reforms are too timid and libertarian to achieve the structural change necessary to fix American democracy."

**Roemer Is Half a Reformer**

Governor Roemer's speech is bracing, and on the central point—that a Congress that spends most of its time fundraising cannot govern—he is unanswerable. I admire his decision to cap contributions at $100 in his own campaign. I admire that he made the same pledge against PAC money during his Congressional service, which is more than almost any of his colleagues can say. The man has walked the walk.

But I want to register a complaint from his left: Roemer has stopped halfway. He sees the problem clearly, then loses his nerve at the constitutional door, and the result is a reform program that the donor class can live with.

Start with his principles. "The system should not silence anyone or any view." Fine. "No system should force any citizen to support political speech that he or she doesn't believe in." This sounds reasonable until you realize what it forecloses. Most robust public financing systems—the kinds that actually work in places like Arizona, Maine, and Connecticut—rely on general tax revenues. Roemer's second principle would seem to make these unconstitutional in spirit, on the theory that some taxpayer somewhere disagrees with some candidate funded. By that logic, public schools are illegitimate because pacifists pay for ROTC programs and creationists pay for biology textbooks. This is not how a democracy works. Citizens are routinely "forced" to support things they disagree with; that is the meaning of majority rule constrained by rights. Treating campaign finance as uniquely sacred is a libertarian premise smuggled in as common sense.

The premise then does real work. Roemer says his four principles "would veto" both McCain-Feingold's restrictions on corporate spending and the Fair Elections Now Act, which provides matching funds to small-donor candidates. In a single paragraph he eliminates the two most serious legislative reform vehicles of the past decade. What is left? A voluntary voucher system that depends on candidates opting in. A self-imposed donation cap that depends on the individual ethics of individual candidates. These are not structural reforms; they are personal pledges scaled up to a slogan.

Second, Roemer accepts *Citizens United* with too little resistance. He calls it potentially harmful, then promises to work "within the boundaries given to us by the Court." But the Court is not a force of nature. It is a body of nine appointed lawyers whose constitutional interpretations can be challenged, narrowed, and—through the amendment process—overturned. There is a serious movement for a constitutional amendment clarifying that money is not speech and corporations are not persons for purposes of campaign finance. Roemer should be leading it. Instead he treats *Buckley v. Valeo*—a 1976 decision that has done more damage to American democracy than perhaps any other case of the modern era—as settled law beyond reform.

Third, Roemer's diagnosis of Wall Street is sharp but his remedy is timid. He correctly notes that the largest banks are bigger than before the crisis, that Dodd-Frank failed to break them up, and that Glass-Steagall's repeal helped get us here. He should then call for the obvious things: a hard cap on bank size, a modern Glass-Steagall (the Brown-Kaufman amendment, which would have done this, failed narrowly in 2010), a financial transactions tax to discourage the kind of high-frequency churn that adds nothing to the real economy, and the prosecution of the executives whose firms peddled mortgage-backed securities they privately mocked. Instead he treats campaign finance reform as the master key—fix the money in politics, and these other reforms will become possible. But this gets the causality only half right. The financial sector's political power is real, but so is its economic power, and the latter does not dissolve when the former is constrained. We have to attack both.

Fourth, Roemer is silent on the question of who actually does politics in America. The donor class is roughly 0.5 percent of the population, overwhelmingly white, overwhelmingly male, overwhelmingly affluent, and disproportionately concentrated in a few zip codes in Manhattan, San Francisco, and the wealthy suburbs of Washington. This is not just a problem of corruption; it is a problem of representation. A Congress that hears constantly from this slice of America and rarely from the other 99.5 percent will reflect that imbalance in its instincts, not just its votes. Roemer's voucher idea begins to address this, but only if it is mandatory and universal, not optional and small.

What I would want from Roemer is the courage to follow his own logic. If money in politics is the master problem—and I think he is largely right that it is—then we need a constitutional amendment, robust public financing as the default rather than the exception, real limits on independent expenditures, and structural reforms to the financial sector that reduce its political weight in the first place. We need, in short, more than personal pledges.

Roemer has the diagnosis. What he needs is the nerve.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Roemer’s principle that citizens shouldn't fund speech they disagree with wrongly treats campaign finance as a uniquely sacred category, effectively delegitimizing the robust public financing systems used in successful democracies.
  2. 02By rejecting major legislative vehicles like McCain-Feingold and the Fair Elections Now Act, Roemer reduces structural reform to a series of optional personal pledges and voluntary vouchers.
  3. 03Roemer mistakenly treats damaging Supreme Court precedents like Buckley v. Valeo and Citizens United as settled law rather than advocating for a constitutional amendment to overturn them.
  4. 04Treating campaign finance as the sole 'master key' ignores the need for direct economic reforms, such as hard caps on bank size and a financial transactions tax, to curb the inherent political power of the financial sector.
  5. 05The reform program fails to adequately address the representational imbalance caused by a donor class that is demographically unrepresentative of the broader American population.
