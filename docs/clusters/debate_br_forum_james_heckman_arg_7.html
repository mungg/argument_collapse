"While early childhood intervention is a necessary strategy for social mobility, it must be paired with the strengthening of economic institutions and labor protections to ensure that developed skills actually lead to secure, high-quality jobs."

James Heckman’s essay is a necessary correction to a great deal of lazy thinking about inequality. If children reach school already unequal in language, health, attention, and emotional security, then policies that begin at college admissions or job training arrive very late. If perseverance and self-regulation matter alongside cognitive ability, then test-score reform is too narrow. If small, intensive early interventions have produced durable gains, then the United States should invest more seriously in the first years of life.

But a mobility agenda built primarily around skill formation is incomplete. Inequality is not only a problem of insufficient skills among the poor. It is also a problem of the rewards, risks, and power relations generated by the economy into which those skills are deployed. A child may become more capable because of early intervention and still enter a labor market with stagnant wages, weak unions, eroding benefits, unstable schedules, and limited routes upward for workers without a four-year college degree. Human capital matters, but it is not the whole economy.

This point is especially important in 2012. The country is still recovering from the Great Recession. Millions of Americans are unemployed or underemployed not because they suddenly lost non-cognitive skills, but because demand collapsed, households deleveraged, construction cratered, and employers stopped hiring. Young workers graduating into this labor market face scarring effects that no preschool program can fully prevent. A society that tells them the real problem occurred when they were three years old is missing something obvious.

Over the longer run, wage inequality has risen partly because education and skills command higher returns. Heckman emphasizes this, and he is right that dropping out of high school is increasingly damaging. But wage inequality has also been shaped by institutions: the decline of unions, the fall in the real value of the minimum wage over long periods, changes in corporate governance, trade shocks, deindustrialization, immigration policy, deregulation, and the fissuring of employment relationships through subcontracting and temporary work. These factors influence whether skills are rewarded broadly or captured narrowly.

Consider two workers with similar capacities. One enters a mid-twentieth-century manufacturing firm with internal job ladders, union representation, health benefits, and a pension. Another enters a contemporary low-wage service job with unpredictable scheduling, little training, no union, and high turnover. Their “non-cognitive skills” may be identical. Their life chances will not be. Institutions convert skills into security. When institutions weaken, even hardworking people struggle.

This is not an argument against early childhood investment. It is an argument against asking early childhood investment to do too much. Skill formation can improve the supply side of the labor market. It cannot by itself ensure that the labor market produces enough good jobs. A society of better-prepared children is still unjust if too many of them grow into adults competing for poverty-wage work.

The word “predistribution” is useful, but Heckman defines it too narrowly. Predistribution should mean shaping market outcomes before taxes and transfers, not only shaping children before school. Early childhood programs are one form of predistribution. So are labor laws, wage floors, collective bargaining rules, public employment, infrastructure investment, and regulations that prevent employers from shifting risks onto workers. If we care about mobility, we should intervene both before children enter school and before workers receive paychecks.

There is also a feedback loop between labor markets and families. Heckman rightly emphasizes parenting quality, but parenting quality is affected by job quality. A parent with a stable schedule can attend school meetings, cook dinner, supervise homework, and establish bedtime routines. A parent with unpredictable shifts cannot do these things as easily. A father with decent wages is more marriageable and more likely to remain connected to his children. A mother with paid sick leave is less likely to lose her job when a child has asthma. Thus labor policy is family policy, and family policy is child development policy.

Housing policy belongs in the same conversation. Children develop in neighborhoods as well as families. Exposure to violence, lead, eviction, and frequent moves undermines attention and emotional regulation. If early childhood programs teach self-control during the day while children return at night to chaos produced by housing insecurity, the programs are swimming upstream. Vouchers, affordable housing, and efforts to reduce concentrated poverty may not look like “skill policy,” but they help create the stable environments in which skills form.

Heckman’s treatment of immigration also deserves caution. He notes that unskilled migration increases the proportion of low-skilled workers, contributing to inequality and reducing productivity growth. That may be partly true in a narrow accounting sense, but immigrants are not merely skill units. They are workers, parents, entrepreneurs, and future citizens. Their children often make substantial educational gains. A humane mobility agenda should invest in immigrant children early, improve English-language learning, regularize the status of families where possible, and enforce labor standards so that employers cannot use vulnerable workers to undercut wages. The answer to low skill is not resentment of migrants but inclusion and institution-building.

Where Heckman is strongest is in demolishing the idea that equal opportunity can be achieved by opening a gate at age eighteen and inviting everyone to run. By then, the race has been structured by years of unequal development. But we should be equally skeptical of the idea that opportunity can be secured by early childhood investment alone. The metaphor of a race is itself misleading. People do not merely run with capacities formed in childhood; they move through terrain built by law, policy, and power.

The proper conclusion is a two-sided agenda. First, invest heavily and intelligently in early childhood: prenatal care, nurse visiting, high-quality child care and preschool, parent support, and attention to socio-emotional development. Second, rebuild the economic institutions that make effort pay: full employment policy, stronger wage standards, better community colleges and apprenticeships, labor protections, infrastructure jobs, and support for collective voice at work. Add housing and health care security, and the developmental benefits of early programs will be more likely to last.

Heckman is right that birth should not be fate. But neither should the labor market. A child saved by excellent early intervention should not grow up to discover that the only jobs available offer low wages, unstable hours, and no dignity. The promise of social mobility requires both capable people and a fair economy. We should build both.
