"While the vision of participatory democracy remains a noble ideal, it cannot be realized today without first dismantling the economic and political structures—such as crushing debt, corporate personhood, and labor precarity—that have systematically eroded the material foundations for civic engagement."

Tom Hayden’s elegy for the Port Huron Statement is a moving tribute to a document that, by his own admission, is simultaneously “irrelevant and retro” and eerily prescient. He finds its spirit alive in the Dreamers occupying congressional offices, in the pepper-sprayed faces of tuition protesters, and in the General Assemblies of Zuccotti Park. There is a genuine comfort in this lineage, a sense that the moral arc of history bends not merely toward justice but toward participation. Yet reading his essay on this fiftieth anniversary, I am struck less by the persistence of the ’60s spirit than by how radically the material conditions for that spirit have been eviscerated. Hayden sees participatory democracy as a timeless human need; I see it as a plant that requires specific soil, soil that has been systematically poisoned over the past five decades.

The Port Huron Statement was drafted at a moment when the American economy was still riding the wave of postwar prosperity. Union density was near its historic peak, public universities were generously funded, and the manufacturing sector provided a ladder to the middle class for millions of families without college degrees. In that context, the call for participatory democracy was not merely a moral appeal; it was a plausible demand for a share of the abundance. Students who sat in at lunch counters and later at draft boards could do so with the implicit safety net of a society that, for all its brutal exclusions of Black Americans, offered a tangible baseline of economic security for the white middle class from which many of those activists came. The indignation was real, but so was the room to maneuver.

Today, that room has shrunk to a coat closet. The Occupy movement’s great and lasting contribution was to inject the language of the 99 percent into public discourse, but its encampments were cleared not only by police batons but by the crushing realities of student debt, precarious employment, and the evisceration of the social wage. The brave undocumented students Hayden invokes are fighting for the Dream Act not as a step toward a radically democratic future but as a desperate scramble for a foothold in a brutal economy that criminalizes their existence. The tuition protesters are not raging against the university-turned-knowledge-factory in the abstract; they are drowning in a debt load that has surpassed credit card debt in this country. Participatory democracy is a noble ideal, but it rings hollow when the participants are too exhausted, too indebted, and too atomized to gather.

Hayden briefly mentions “progressive tools of referendum, recall, and initiative” and nods to the Wisconsin uprising against Governor Scott Walker’s assault on collective bargaining. What he misses is that Wisconsin was not a victory for participatory democracy but a bitter testament to how money has warped the public sphere. The recall effort, while stirring immense grassroots energy, ultimately failed because an avalanche of corporate and out-of-state cash swamped the incipient democratic will. The same tools of initiative and referendum that Hayden praises have been systematically hijacked by well-funded interests to push anti-tax measures, anti-labor laws, and the very privatization that makes a mockery of public participation. The Citizens United decision, which Hayden does not mention, has perfected a system in which the speech of a few corporations can drown out the assembled voices of millions. This is not a terrain where participatory democracy can flourish; it is a terrain where genuine participation is smothered by the sheer volume of purchased amplification.

Even the Internet, which Hayden anoints as “the greatest expansion of participatory democracy in history,” is proving to be a double-edged sword. In April 2012, we are already beyond the early utopianism of the blogosphere. Social media does enable the rapid assembly of angry bodies, but it also reduces political engagement to a flickering stream of likes, shares, and outrages that evaporate as quickly as they congeal. The independent media that grew out of the ’60s ferment—the underground papers and pirate radio stations—required physical communities of trust and editorial labor. Today’s online ecosystems produce information cascades that can just as easily serve reactionary populism as progressive awakening. Hayden’s optimism about the Internet feels dated even now, a relic of the 2008 Obama campaign’s digital magic rather than a sober assessment of how these technologies actually discipline and segment publics.

My quarrel is not with the beautiful vision at the core of the Port Huron Statement. The longing to shape one’s own life, to be more than a passive subject of remote institutions, is genuinely universal. But Hayden’s essay is too serene, too willing to assemble a parade of inspiring moments without attending to the countervailing forces that have undermined those moments. He mentions that the establishment concedes reforms to prevent more radical destabilization, and then we forget, and the countermovement tries to contain the achievement. This is a description not of a rising tide but of a grueling cycle of protest, co-optation, and retrenchment that has, on balance, left us with a crueler and more unequal society than the one the original statement confronted.

If participatory democracy is to become more than a nostalgic totem, we must confront the political economy that strangles it. That means moving beyond the inside-outside strategy of mobilizing moral energy while hoping sympathetic politicians open the doors. It means fighting squarely for the decommodification of basic goods—housing, education, health care—so that people actually have the time and security to be citizens. It means breaking the stranglehold of corporate money on the political process through a constitutional amendment overturning Citizens United and moving toward the public financing of elections. It means a rebirth of labor organizing not merely to protect wages but to democratize the workplace itself. These are not single-issue causes that can simply be united under the banner of participatory democracy; they are preconditions for that banner to mean anything at all.

Tom Hayden’s generation did great things in pursuit of a more democratic world, and I do not wish to denigrate their courage. But the fiftieth anniversary of the Port Huron Statement should be less an occasion for finding our own reflection in the past than a moment of harsh reckoning. We are not simply the ’60s spirit reborn. We are its abandoned children, left to assemble the scattered tools of democracy on a much bleaker landscape. That work demands more than a timeless slogan; it demands a ruthless analysis of what has gone so wrong and a willingness to build the economic foundations upon which genuine participation can finally rise.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The economic security and public funding that allowed 1960s activists the 'room to maneuver' have been replaced by a climate of extreme debt and precarious employment that exhausts and atomizes potential participants.
  2. 02The tools of direct democracy, such as referendums and recalls, have been hijacked by corporate interests and an avalanche of private cash that effectively smothers the genuine democratic will.
  3. 03The internet and social media, rather than being pure engines of democracy, often reduce political engagement to fleeting, superficial interactions and facilitate the spread of reactionary populism.
  4. 04Participatory democracy is a hollow slogan unless it is preceded by the decommodification of basic goods like education and healthcare to ensure citizens have the time and security to participate.
  5. 05The current political landscape is not a continuation of the 1960s spirit but a much bleaker environment that requires a ruthless analysis of systemic failure rather than nostalgic optimism.
