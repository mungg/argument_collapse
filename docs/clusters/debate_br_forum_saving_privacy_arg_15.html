Diversified Gemini

"True digital privacy cannot be achieved through government-negotiated rights or state-certified tools, but only through the widespread adoption of robust, decentralized encryption that is mathematically immune to state interference."

Reed Hundt is absolutely correct that the unholy alliance between massive data-mining firms and the national security apparatus has eroded the sovereignty of the individual. His call for a "digital bill of rights" and his embrace of cryptographic tools like Tor and Bitcoin are welcome departures from the usual Beltway consensus that views encryption with deep suspicion. However, when it comes to the execution of his vision, Hundt falls into a fatal contradiction. He calls for individuals to empower themselves through mathematics, but then invites the state back into the equation to "police the crossing points" and "certify" the technology.

Hundt's proposed compromise—that citizens should enjoy secure digital spaces but that the government must be able to "cross between the analog and digital sides of the street" via warrants—is a technical and political impossibility. You either have end-to-end encryption, or you have a compromised system. There is no such thing as a "warrant-compatible" cryptographic protocol that is safe from abuse.

Hundt served as FCC Chairman during the Clinton administration, an era defined by the first great "Crypto Wars." Back then, the government attempted to force the adoption of the Clipper Chip—a piece of hardware with a built-in backdoor for law enforcement. The cypherpunks and civil liberties advocates fought that battle and won, establishing that strong, uncompromised encryption is a vital necessity. It is baffling that Hundt now suggests a digital bill of rights where the government helps "standardize" our privacy tools. Has he forgotten the history of his own era in Washington?

He suggests that the government should "invest more in providing access to encrypted, safe technology" and that it could "certify or standardize the capacities of these tools." Has he also forgotten the lessons of the last twelve months? The Snowden revelations did not just expose bulk collection of metadata; they revealed that the NSA actively worked to undermine cryptographic standards. The NSA heavily influenced the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) to approve a random number generator containing a backdoor, deliberately weakening the encryption relied upon by millions.

Trusting the state to standardize and certify the very tools meant to protect us from the state is like trusting a wolf to inspect the fences of a sheep pen. The government's institutional imperative is surveillance. It will never willingly certify an encryption standard that it cannot, given enough resources or a secret court order, break or bypass.

The beauty of the tools Hundt praises—open-source encryption, the blockchain, distributed networks—is that they do not require government certification. They rely on the peer-reviewed laws of mathematics. Mathematics does not require a digital bill of rights to be negotiated in Congress. It does not require a federal judge to interpret it. A properly encrypted message is immune to the whims of the political class, the overreach of an intelligence agency, or the panic of a crisis.

Hundt believes that a digital bill of rights "will have to be negotiated." But with whom are we negotiating? The very intelligence agencies that lied to Congress about the extent of their domestic surveillance? The tech giants who willingly handed over server access under the PRISM program? We cannot negotiate our way out of a surveillance state using the very legal architecture that the state has spent a decade manipulating.

Furthermore, Hundt’s insistence that cyberspace must ultimately bow to analog law enforcement is a failure of imagination. He writes that "cyber-libertarianism as a system for organizing complex societies has its limits," and that the state must be able to "obtain the real identities of victims, witnesses, and suspects." But forcing digital identities to securely map to analog ones destroys the very protections anonymity provides. We cannot build systems that are "secure" but also transparent to law enforcement, because the technical vulnerability required for the police to enter is the exact same vulnerability that a hacker or a hostile foreign government will eventually exploit.

True privacy in the digital age will not come from legislative reform or a newfound respect for the Fourth Amendment by intelligence agencies. It will come from the widespread adoption of robust, decentralized, open-source cryptography that mathematically prohibits surveillance. We must reject the idea that we need the state to manage our digital spaces. We do not need the government's permission to reclaim our privacy.
