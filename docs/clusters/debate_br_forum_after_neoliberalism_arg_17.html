"While the lead essay’s call for a post-neoliberal economics is a welcome step, its reliance on mainstream economic tools is insufficient because it prioritizes technocratic inclusion over the fundamental redistribution of power and democratic control."

The call for “economics after neoliberalism” is welcome, but it is too gentle on economics and too optimistic about the capacity of mainstream tools to repair the damage. Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman want to distinguish modern economics from market fundamentalism. They argue that neoliberalism is a “primitive, simplistic perversion” of economics, not its logical expression. There is truth in this. Economics is not reducible to Milton Friedman, perfect competition, or the claim that markets always know best. But it is not enough to say that neoliberal policymakers misused economics. The deeper problem is that the mainstream discipline helped make neoliberalism plausible.

This happened not only through policy advice but through habits of thought. Mainstream economics begins with individuals, preferences, constraints, incentives, and equilibrium. These tools can be powerful. But they also tend to translate social conflict into technical trade-offs, institutions into transaction costs, and power into deviations from a competitive benchmark. Even when economists study market failure, the “failure” is often defined against an idealized market world that never existed and could never exist. The result is a discipline that can analyze inequality while still making capitalism itself appear natural.

The authors insist that economics is full of indeterminacy: “it depends.” That is both a strength and a weakness. If economics can generate many conclusions depending on assumptions, then economists cannot claim special authority over the broad direction of society. They can clarify mechanisms, estimate effects, and warn about unintended consequences. But the choice between a society organized around shareholder value and one organized around social need is not a technical matter. It is a political and moral matter.

The essay’s most promising moments come when it edges toward this recognition. The authors discuss property rights as constructed rather than given. They ask whether jobs belong to firms, workers, or some combination; whether government should retain ownership claims over publicly supported innovation; whether trade rules should be subordinated to social contracts. These are fundamental questions. But if taken seriously, they lead beyond “inclusive prosperity” toward economic democracy.

The phrase “inclusive prosperity” has a technocratic softness. It suggests that the existing economic order can be made more inclusive through better policy design: wage boards here, tax reform there, better social insurance, smarter financial regulation, a more reasonable trade regime. Many of these proposals are good. But inclusion into what? Prosperity for whom, controlled by whom, and on what terms?

A worker can be “included” in prosperity as a better-paid employee who still has no say over investment decisions, scheduling, automation, plant closures, or the distribution of surplus. A community can be “included” through adjustment assistance while still being dependent on mobile capital that may leave when subsidies expire. A citizen can receive tax credits and public benefits while corporations retain enormous authority over production, employment, technology, and politics. Inclusion is not the same as power.

The lead essay acknowledges power but does not fully reorganize economics around it. Power is not merely one variable among others; it is constitutive of markets. Employers’ power over workers is not an imperfection accidentally introduced into an otherwise neutral labor market. It arises from property relations: one side owns productive assets, the other must sell labor to live. Corporate power is not simply a matter of market concentration measurable through markups, though that matters. It is also legal personhood, control over investment, access to the state, intellectual property, and the capacity to shape desires and knowledge. Financial power is not only systemic risk; it is the ability of creditors to discipline households, firms, cities, and governments.

Mainstream economics can study pieces of this, but it often lacks a vocabulary for domination. A model of monopsony may show why wages are below marginal product. Useful. But the lived reality of work includes surveillance, arbitrary scheduling, sexual harassment, immigration status, racial hierarchy, and fear of retaliation. A model of tax competition may show why corporate rates fall. Useful. But the political story includes lobbying, revolving doors, campaign finance, and the ability of wealth to define what counts as “responsible” policy. A model of trade may show localized losses. Useful. But deindustrialization also destroys solidarities, local tax bases, and democratic confidence.

The authors are right that economics has become more empirical. Yet empiricism does not automatically free a discipline from ideology. The credibility revolution in economics has improved causal inference, but it can also narrow the imagination. Economists study what can be identified. Natural experiments, discontinuities, and administrative data are valuable, but the most important questions are not always the most easily identified. What is the long-run effect of union decline on democracy? What forms of ownership best support human flourishing? How does capitalist dependence on growth interact with planetary limits? What is the cumulative political effect of wealth concentration? These questions require history, sociology, political theory, and institutional imagination, not only clever identification strategies.

The policy proposals discussed in the lead essay are strongest when they move toward countervailing power: unions, wage boards, campaign finance reform, tougher corporate taxation. But they should be understood as steps toward a more democratic economy, not merely corrections to market failures. Wage boards are not just a response to monopsony; they are a way of taking wages partly out of unilateral employer control. Unions are not just devices to solve collective action problems; they are schools of democracy and institutions of working-class power. Corporate tax reform is not just an efficient way to raise revenue; it is a challenge to the idea that capital may opt out of social obligation. Trade safeguards are not just exceptions to free trade; they are assertions that democratic communities have the right to shape their economic lives.

A more ambitious post-neoliberal economics would put ownership and control at the center. It would ask how to expand worker cooperatives, public options, municipal enterprise, social wealth funds, codetermination, sectoral bargaining, public banking, and democratic planning for investment. It would revisit not only redistribution after markets generate outcomes, but predistribution: the rules that determine who has claims over income, assets, knowledge, and decision-making before tax and transfer. It would treat the firm not as a production function but as a political institution. It would treat the state not as an external fixer of market failures but as a creator of markets, a coordinator of investment, and a site of democratic struggle.

This does not require abandoning rigor. Quite the opposite. We need rigorous analysis of institutional alternatives. But rigor should not be confused with marginalism. A society facing climate change, mass inequality, and democratic erosion needs structural thinking. Carbon pricing may be useful, but decarbonization will also require public investment, industrial policy, transit, regulation, and planning. Minimum wages help, but rebuilding labor power requires law and organization. Taxing multinationals is necessary, but democratizing investment requires new ownership forms.

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman have written an important essay because they break with complacency. They show that economics need not be an apology for the powerful. But the question “after neoliberalism” should not be answered only by a better economics. It should be answered by a more democratic political economy—one that asks not just how to make markets work better, but which decisions should be made by markets at all, which by democratic institutions, and which by communities and workers themselves.

Economics can contribute to that project. But only if it gives up the desire to be the master discipline of social life. It must become one language among others in a broader struggle for democracy, equality, and ecological survival.
