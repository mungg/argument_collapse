"In an era of rising fascism and genocide, intellectuals must move beyond individual truth-telling and heroic dissent to become organized, organic participants in insurgent social movements."

Robin D. G. Kelley is right that the old formula—“speak truth to power”—has become too thin for the moment we inhabit. It is not false; it is insufficient. Truth remains necessary, but fascism is not a seminar mistake. Genocide is not an error in citation. The machinery that produces, excuses, funds, and normalizes mass violence is not waiting to be corrected by a better footnote. It is organized. It has donors, think tanks, lobbyists, police departments, university counsels, congressional committees, media ecosystems, and private blacklists. If intellectuals are to meet their responsibility, they must be organized too.

That is the central force of Kelley’s essay. He does not discard Chomsky’s demand that intellectuals expose lies. He historicizes and radicalizes it. The problem with many invocations of Chomsky is that they turn an insurgent argument into a professional ethic: the intellectual as heroic dissenter, armed with facts, standing outside institutions and judging them. Kelley asks us to look instead at relations of power. Who pays? Who disciplines? Who is vulnerable? Who is protected? Who is invited to speak, and who is made into a dossier? Who can risk arrest, and who can be disappeared from the profession after one controversial sentence?

The answer is not that truth no longer matters. In Gaza, truth has mattered enormously. Palestinian journalists, doctors, poets, scholars, and ordinary residents have documented destruction in real time, often while starving, grieving, and awaiting death. Human rights organizations, UN officials, genocide scholars, legal advocates, Israeli dissidents, and Jewish anti-occupation groups have all supplied facts: the scale of civilian death; the targeting or destruction of hospitals, schools, universities, archives, mosques, churches, refugee camps, bakeries, and water systems; the rhetoric of elimination from Israeli officials; the use of starvation as a weapon; the mass displacement of nearly an entire population. South Africa’s case at the International Court of Justice, the International Criminal Court prosecutor’s applications for arrest warrants, and the mounting record assembled by journalists and legal scholars have all made denial more difficult.

But Kelley’s point is that denial has not needed to be plausible in order to be powerful. The truth has been visible, and still the bombs have fallen. The truth has been available, and still U.S. weapons, diplomatic cover, and congressional apologetics have continued. The truth has been spoken on campuses, and administrators have responded not with serious moral inquiry but with police, suspensions, evictions, and public relations language about “safety.” Truth can puncture a lie. It cannot by itself overcome a coalition committed to preserving domination.

Kelley’s turn to Gramsci is therefore not ornamental. It is clarifying. Intellectuals do not hover above conflict; they help organize common sense. Some do so for empire, capital, racial hierarchy, and state violence. Others do so for the dispossessed. The fantasy of the neutral intellectual has always concealed a social function. The national security expert who explains why another war is regrettably necessary, the university president who invokes civil discourse while calling in riot police, the columnist who treats Palestinian life as a “complexity” and Israeli military action as an “agonizing necessity,” the legal scholar who discovers procedural reasons to ignore mass death—all are doing intellectual work. They are not failing to think. They are thinking in the service of power.

This is why Kelley’s recovery of Black antifascist traditions is so valuable. The Black radicals he invokes did not understand fascism as an exotic European pathology, defeated in 1945 and irrelevant to American democracy. They saw fascism as one possible form taken by racial capitalism, imperial war, colonial rule, police terror, and organized white reaction. Langston Hughes’s formulation—that fascism was a new name for terror Black people had long faced—was not a metaphorical flourish. It was an analysis of continuity. Lynching, colonial massacre, strikebreaking, passport revocation, deportation, surveillance, and the criminalization of dissent were not aberrations from democracy; they were the means by which democracy was racially bounded.

That history matters because liberal memory often treats fascism as a sudden emergency that arrives from outside the republic. Kelley insists, instead, that the materials are already here. The border camp, the prison, the police raid, the anti-trans panic, the attack on ethnic studies, the campaign against “DEI,” the suppression of Palestine solidarity, the billionaire capture of public institutions, and the demonization of immigrants are not separate issues waiting to be ranked. They are connected fronts in a struggle over who belongs, who may speak, who may live, and who may be sacrificed.

Some readers will object that calling the present “fascist” is excessive. The term can certainly be used carelessly. Not every right-wing policy is fascism; not every authoritarian gesture is a replay of the 1930s. But the objection often functions as a demand that we postpone naming danger until it has fully consolidated. By then, naming may be safer intellectually but less useful politically. Kelley’s essay does not rest on a crude analogy between Trumpism and Nazism. It identifies recognizable features: paramilitary and state violence against stigmatized groups; the cultivation of enemies within; contempt for constitutional limits; attacks on universities, journalism, and independent knowledge; the fusion of executive power with oligarchic interests; fantasies of national purification; the remaking of education around obedience; and the use of law as a weapon against dissent. Whether one calls this fascism, authoritarian nationalism, or racial oligarchy, the responsibility does not shrink.

The campus is a revealing battlefield because it still carries the language of liberalism while increasingly obeying the logic of capital and coercion. Universities speak of inquiry, courage, and community. Yet when students demanded divestment from companies tied to Israeli military violence, or even demanded disclosure of investments, many institutions treated them as security threats. Administrators who had issued eloquent statements about democracy after January 6 became procedural minimalists when students protested Gaza. They discovered time, place, and manner rules; they rediscovered donor management; they invoked antisemitism in ways that flattened Jewish disagreement and erased Palestinian fear.

Kelley is especially persuasive in showing that repression did not begin after October 7. Palestine has long been an exception to free speech norms in American academic life. Scholars critical of Israel have faced tenure denials, campaigns of harassment, event cancellations, donor pressure, blacklists, and accusations that collapse anti-Zionism into antisemitism. The post-October 7 crackdown intensified this structure rather than inventing it. What changed was scale and brazenness: police in encampments, congressional inquisitions, administrators sacrificing faculty and students to appease politicians, and right-wing organizations openly using antisemitism as a pretext to attack the university itself.

This does not mean antisemitism is imaginary. It is real, old, adaptable, and deadly. The responsibility of intellectuals includes fighting it without equivocation. But precisely because antisemitism is real, it should not be instrumentalized to shield a state from accountability for mass violence. Nor should Jewish safety be defined as unconditional support for Israeli militarism. Jewish students and faculty are not a monolith. Many have been central to Palestine solidarity movements, often drawing on Jewish ethical and historical traditions to oppose occupation, apartheid, and genocide. To erase them is also a form of intellectual dishonesty.

The responsibility Kelley describes is not only to denounce repression but to build counterpower. This is the hardest part, and the part most alien to academic training. Scholars are rewarded for critique, distinction, and individual authorship. Movements require discipline, humility, repetition, coalition, and patience. The intellectual who enters struggle must learn to be useful rather than merely brilliant. That may mean writing expert declarations for students facing punishment, helping unions bargain for academic freedom protections, teaching community classes, supporting legal defense funds, translating complex policy into usable language, preserving archives under threat, or simply showing up when police arrive.

This work also requires a changed understanding of risk. Kelley’s closing example of Tiffany Willoughby-Herard is powerful because it refuses abstraction. “What job do I have if the students don’t have a future?” is not a slogan about martyrdom. It is a reordering of professional ethics. The job of a professor is not merely to deliver content or preserve a career. It is to participate in the conditions under which students can think, live, dissent, and inherit a world not wholly organized around domination. If the institution attacks those conditions, loyalty to the institution becomes disloyalty to education.

Still, the call to risk must be made carefully. Tenured professors should not romanticize sacrifice for graduate students, adjuncts, international students, staff, or untenured colleagues who face greater vulnerability. The first obligation of relatively protected intellectuals is not to exhort the precarious to be brave. It is to use protection materially: sign first, speak first, refuse cooperation with investigations, accompany students to disciplinary hearings, redirect resources, name retaliation, and make it harder for institutions to isolate the most vulnerable. Courage is not equally distributed because protection is not equally distributed. Solidarity means changing the distribution.

Kelley’s essay also implies a broader institutional program. Faculty unions should bargain over academic freedom, surveillance, protest rights, nondiscrimination, and the use of police. Senates should demand investment transparency and limits on donor interference. Departments should refuse ideological litmus tests disguised as civility standards. Professional associations should defend targeted scholars and create funds for contingent faculty punished for speech. Libraries and archives should preserve Palestinian intellectual life and the records of repression. Universities should rebuild ties with destroyed or besieged Palestinian institutions, not suspend them under political pressure.

But we should not imagine the university as the main arena. Kelley’s list of movements—tenant organizing, labor, disability justice, immigrant defense, reproductive freedom, anti-debt campaigns, trans rights, left electoral formations—is not a digression. It is a map of antifascism as social reconstruction. Fascism feeds on abandonment: abandoned towns, abandoned workers, abandoned public goods, abandoned futures. It converts loneliness and precarity into hatred. Intellectual work that does not address the material conditions of despair will remain defensive. The fight against fascism must also be a fight for housing, health care, wages, schools, climate survival, and democratic control over the economy.

The most urgent lesson from Kelley’s Black antifascist genealogy is that the victims of one system of domination often see the whole more clearly than those who benefit from partial inclusion. Black radicals, colonized intellectuals, and anti-imperialist organizers identified links that liberal institutions denied: between domestic racism and foreign war, between colonial violence and European fascism, between capitalist crisis and scapegoating, between repression abroad and repression at home. Their clarity came not from purity but from struggle. They knew that rights are not self-enforcing and that democracies can commit atrocities while speaking the language of freedom.

Today’s intellectuals should learn from that clarity. We should say that Palestinian life has equal value. We should say that starving a civilian population is a crime. We should say that destroying universities is an attack on a people’s future. We should say that deporting students for political speech is authoritarian. We should say that billionaires and trustees should not govern education. We should say that antisemitism, anti-Palestinian racism, anti-Blackness, Islamophobia, anti-immigrant hatred, and transphobia are not competing concerns but mutually reinforcing instruments of reaction.

Yet saying is only the beginning. Kelley’s essay asks for a move from witness to commitment. The intellectual’s responsibility in an age of fascism and genocide is to become accountable to movements capable of stopping them. That accountability will not always flatter us. Movements will not always need our preferred vocabulary. They may ask for logistics, not lectures; for testimony, not theory; for presence, not performance. The work may be slow, local, and unglamorous. But the alternative is to become what the university increasingly trains us to be: eloquent spectators of catastrophe.

Chomsky’s injunction still has force: expose the lies of governments, examine motives, refuse the comforts of patriotic myth. But Kelley is right that we must add another imperative. Choose the side of the living against the machinery of death, and then act as if that choice obligates you. The measure of intellectual life is not whether we were correct in private, or even eloquent in public. It is whether our knowledge helped build the power necessary to defend human beings when institutions chose repression, war, and silence.

"The responsibility of intellectuals in an age of crisis is to move beyond mere truth-telling and professional respectability toward a fidelity that combines material solidarity with movements and a rigorous commitment to moral and historical complexity."

The most important sentence in Robin D. G. Kelley’s essay may be the one he quotes from Tiffany Willoughby-Herard: “What job do I have if the students don’t have a future?” It cuts through the evasions that have defined so much university speech since October 7: the statements about pain that name no perpetrator, the invocations of safety that summon police, the appeals to dialogue that begin after administrators have already chosen donors over students. Kelley’s essay is an argument against evasion. It asks intellectuals to stop imagining that professional respectability will protect either truth or life.

I write from a position of partial agreement and deep unease. Kelley is right that Gaza has exposed the moral hollowness of many institutions. He is right that universities have repressed Palestine advocacy with a ferocity that makes a mockery of their advertised commitments to free inquiry. He is right that fascist and authoritarian movements are not anti-intellectual in the simple sense; they have their own intellectuals, their own theories, their own policy shops, their own curricula of cruelty. He is right, above all, that the responsibility of intellectuals cannot end with correct analysis.

But I want to press on the word “responsibility.” Responsibility is not only responsibility to a movement, though it may include that. It is also responsibility to the dead, to the living, to language, to fear, and to the possibility of shared political life after horror. In an age of massacre and propaganda, intellectuals must resist not one but several temptations: the temptation to silence, the temptation to careerist prudence, the temptation to tribal exoneration, and the temptation to make suffering morally useful only when it belongs to one’s side.

Gaza demands a clear response. The devastation inflicted by Israel, with U.S. support, cannot be justified by the crimes of Hamas on October 7. The murder of civilians, hostage-taking, and sexual violence attributed to that day were atrocities. They did not create a license to destroy a people’s means of existence. No state has a right to starve children, reduce neighborhoods to rubble, annihilate universities, kill journalists and doctors, or render return impossible for a displaced population. The language of self-defense collapses when it becomes a rationale for collective punishment. If intellectuals cannot say this plainly, their vaunted independence is worth little.

At the same time, intellectuals should resist the emotional economy in which one people’s grief must be minimized so another’s can be heard. The American public sphere has overwhelmingly minimized Palestinian grief; that is the central injustice Kelley addresses. But the remedy is not to make Jewish grief unspeakable or to treat Israeli civilian trauma as merely a propaganda device. A politics of liberation should be capacious enough to mourn murdered Israeli families and murdered Palestinian families without pretending the two stand in symmetrical political positions. Asymmetry of power is real. So is the humanity of victims on both sides.

This distinction matters because antisemitism and anti-Palestinian racism are not abstractions. Both are active forces, though not equivalent in their institutional expression. Anti-Palestinian racism has been normalized in U.S. politics to a stunning degree: Palestinians are asked to condemn before they grieve; their dead are doubted, their history treated as inflammatory, their advocates investigated. Antisemitism, meanwhile, remains a conspiratorial grammar ready to explain capitalism, war, media, migration, and modernity through fantasies of Jewish control. It appears on the far right with particular menace, but it can migrate into left spaces when anger at Israel becomes suspicion of Jews as such, or when Zionism is described not as a political ideology and state project but as an almost supernatural evil.

Kelley knows this, and he does not endorse antisemitism. Yet his essay might have said more about the responsibility to fight it in the very moment one fights the weaponization of it. The weaponization is real. Politicians who tolerate white nationalists have discovered Jewish safety as a convenient cudgel against universities, immigrants, and the left. Some organizations have blurred distinctions between criticism of Israel and hatred of Jews so thoroughly that the fight against antisemitism is weakened. But bad-faith accusations do not relieve movements of the obligation to confront real instances. Indeed, they make that obligation more urgent. If we do not draw principled lines, our enemies will draw cynical ones.

A similar point applies to Kelley’s use of Black antifascist history. His genealogy is powerful because it provincializes Europe. It reminds us that fascism was not simply an eruption of irrationality in civilized nations but was connected to colonial violence, racial hierarchy, and capitalist crisis. Black thinkers saw continuities that white liberals ignored. They understood that the techniques of domination used abroad often return home, and that democracy for some can coexist with terror for others.

Yet analogy is both illumination and risk. The Jim Crow South, Nazi Germany, apartheid South Africa, colonial Algeria, Vietnam, Gaza, and Trump’s America are connected histories, not identical ones. Intellectuals have a responsibility to trace connection without flattening difference. Fascism should not become a master word that absorbs every cruelty. Genocide should not become a synonym for every injustice. Apartheid should not become a mere intensifier. These terms carry histories and legal meanings. Their power depends on care.

In the case of Gaza, care strengthens rather than weakens the accusation. The destruction of conditions of life, the mass killing, the displacement, the official rhetoric, and the obstruction of adequate aid have made the charge of genocide not only emotionally intelligible but legally and morally serious. One need not prove that every Israeli soldier or every supporter of the war desires extermination. Genocide is a structure of action and intent, not a census of private hatred. But because the word is so grave, intellectuals should show their work. They should teach the Genocide Convention, explain intent, compare cases responsibly, and distinguish genocide from other war crimes and crimes against humanity even when several may be present at once.

Kelley’s strongest challenge is to the self-image of the intellectual as a person whose main contribution is speech. He is right: speech alone cannot stop bombs, deportations, evictions, or police batons. But I would not say that truth-telling has lost its power. Rather, truth-telling has become more dangerous and more dependent on institutions of transmission. The Palestinian journalist charging a phone from a car battery to upload footage is telling the truth. The physician recording preventable deaths from siege is telling the truth. The Jewish student saying “not in my name” is telling the truth. The Israeli human rights worker documenting settler violence is telling the truth. The archivist preserving the names of the dead is telling the truth. The question is not whether truth has power. The question is whether truth has protection, amplification, and consequences.

That is where organization enters. Kelley is persuasive that intellectuals need unions, associations, legal networks, community alliances, and political homes. The solitary dissident is too easy to isolate. A professor can be smeared; a graduate worker can be threatened; an international student can be deported; an adjunct can simply not be rehired. The repression of Palestine advocacy has exposed the cruelty of academic precarity. Tenure, where it exists, is not a private perk but a public resource. Those who have it should use it for those who do not.

But organization brings its own dangers. Movements can punish dissent, simplify reality, and reward performance over judgment. Anyone who has spent time in activist spaces knows that moral intensity does not abolish hierarchy. It can produce informal hierarchies that are harder to contest. Intellectuals entering movements should not arrive as commanders, but neither should they abandon discernment. Their responsibility includes asking: Is this tactic helping the vulnerable or exposing them? Are we speaking for people who have not authorized us? Are we making room for those most affected to disagree? Are we reproducing the exclusions we denounce?

The campus encampments of 2024 and 2025 deserve to be remembered with more seriousness than either their enemies or some of their romantic defenders allow. Many were remarkable experiments in political education, interfaith solidarity, and moral courage. Students read together, prayed together, negotiated, cooked, built libraries, and demanded that their universities stop profiting from war. They also made mistakes, as all movements do. The appropriate response to student error is not police violence, suspension, or congressional humiliation. It is education. Universities that answered encampments with force confessed that they had forgotten how to teach.

Kelley’s critique of administrators is devastating because administrators often spoke as if they had no choices. Donors demanded, politicians threatened, parents called, lawyers warned, police advised. But leadership consists precisely in not treating pressure as fate. A president or chancellor could have said: we will protect Jewish students from harassment, Muslim and Palestinian students from bigotry, and all students from political retaliation; we will enforce content-neutral rules narrowly; we will not call police on peaceful protest; we will disclose investments; we will debate divestment; we will not allow donors to dictate discipline. Some did parts of this. Too many did the opposite.

Still, the defense of the university should not be left to administrators. Nor should the university be abandoned as merely a neoliberal shell. Kelley calls it contested terrain, and that is exactly right. Classrooms remain places where students encounter histories their governments would rather suppress. Libraries remain repositories against enforced forgetting. Laboratories and public health schools produce knowledge that can serve life rather than profit. Humanities departments, however battered, teach interpretation, memory, and moral imagination. The right attacks universities not because they are revolutionary, but because even compromised spaces of inquiry can obstruct authoritarian common sense.

The task, then, is not to choose between the university and the street. It is to build circuits between them. Scholars can support tenant unions with research on landlords and evictions. Lawyers can defend protesters and immigrants. Historians can document repression. Scientists can expose environmental racism and the military uses of technology. Physicians can testify about famine and trauma. Area studies scholars can challenge imperial ignorance. Philosophers and theologians can clarify ethical claims now buried under euphemism. Students can force institutions to confront where their money goes. None of this requires pretending the university is innocent. It requires refusing to surrender it.

What would responsibility look like in practice? First, name the violence accurately: Hamas’s atrocities, Israel’s devastation of Gaza, U.S. complicity, rising antisemitism, anti-Palestinian racism, and authoritarian repression. Second, defend speech and protest even when slogans offend, while drawing firm lines against threats and discrimination. Third, protect the vulnerable materially: bail funds, legal defense, emergency grants, visa support, tenure protections, union clauses. Fourth, refuse donor governance and demand transparency. Fifth, build relationships with communities beyond campus so that academic freedom is not mistaken for faculty privilege. Sixth, cultivate habits of internal criticism within movements, because liberation without self-critique curdles.

Kelley’s essay ends with embodiment: a professor arrested while protecting students. That image should haunt us, but it should not become the only model of courage. Some responsibilities are visible; others are quiet. The faculty member who votes against a punitive policy in a closed meeting, the staff worker who warns students of disciplinary traps, the librarian who preserves threatened materials, the doctor who carefully records injuries, the Jewish chaplain who stands with Palestinian students while comforting frightened Jewish ones, the Palestinian scholar who continues teaching through grief—these, too, answer the question.

The responsibility of intellectuals in an age of fascism and genocide is to refuse abandonment. Refuse to abandon Palestinians to the language of collateral damage. Refuse to abandon Jews to those who instrumentalize their fear. Refuse to abandon students to police and blacklists. Refuse to abandon truth because propaganda is powerful. Refuse to abandon complexity because urgency is real. Refuse to abandon institutions because they are compromised, or movements because they are imperfect.

Kelley is right that neutrality is not enough. But the opposite of neutrality is not certainty. It is fidelity: to human beings, to evidence, to freedom, to a future in which no people’s safety requires another people’s disappearance. Intellectuals cannot create that future alone. But they can help make it thinkable, defensible, organized, and harder to betray.

"Intellectuals must practice 'truthful solidarity,' which balances a commitment to social justice movements with a rigorous, independent responsibility to evidence and moral consistency."

Robin D. G. Kelley’s essay is a bracing indictment of cowardice in the academy and complicity in Gaza. It also raises a question that deserves more argument than it sometimes receives: if truth-telling is no longer enough, what exactly should replace or supplement it? Kelley’s answer is that intellectuals must choose sides, become organic to movements against fascism and genocide, and build power inside and outside the university. Much of this is right. But there is a danger in moving too quickly from the insufficiency of truth to the subordination of intellectual life to political struggle. The responsibility of intellectuals includes solidarity. It also includes preserving the conditions under which solidarity can be criticized, corrected, and made more truthful.

I begin from agreement. The assault on Palestinian life in Gaza has been morally catastrophic, and the United States has been indispensable to it. It is not possible to defend a rules-based international order while exempting allies from rules against collective punishment, starvation, indiscriminate bombardment, forced displacement, and the destruction of civilian infrastructure. It is not possible to mourn Israeli civilians murdered by Hamas on October 7, as one must, and then treat Palestinian civilians as regrettable background matter. The hierarchy of grief in American public life has been unmistakable. Kelley is right to insist that intellectuals have a responsibility to reject it.

He is also right that universities have often behaved disgracefully. The police crackdowns on encampments, the haste to suspend students, the donor-driven panic, the congressional theater, and the administrative use of “safety” as an all-purpose rationale for repression have exposed how shallow many institutional commitments to academic freedom are. The university that celebrates dissent in brochures can become startlingly punitive when dissent threatens trustees, politicians, or wealthy alumni. Faculty who had imagined themselves protected by norms discovered that norms without organized constituencies are fragile.

Kelley is further right that Palestine has long been treated as an exception. Criticism of Israel has been uniquely vulnerable to campaigns of professional punishment. The claim that anti-Zionism is always antisemitism is intellectually untenable and politically dangerous. It erases Jewish anti-Zionist and non-Zionist traditions, silences Palestinians describing their own dispossession, and turns a necessary struggle against antisemitism into a shield for state violence. Genuine antisemitism must be confronted; it should not be diluted by bad-faith efforts to classify ordinary human rights advocacy as hatred of Jews.

Yet precisely because these stakes are so high, I worry about Kelley’s dismissal—or apparent downgrading—of truth-telling. “Whatever power truth-telling might have had against fascism has been radically diminished,” he writes. As a sociological observation, this is hard to dispute. Lies travel faster than corrections. Propaganda ecosystems manufacture alternative realities. Authoritarian movements do not concede when refuted. But if this becomes more than an observation—if it becomes a theory of intellectual practice—then we risk giving up the very ground on which emancipatory politics depends.

The reason to tell the truth is not only that it persuades adversaries. Often it does not. The reason is also that movements need truth in order to act wisely. Governments lie; so do movements. States manipulate evidence; so can parties, factions, and charismatic leaders. Atrocities committed by one’s enemies are real; atrocities committed by one’s allies may be real too. If intellectuals define responsibility primarily as choosing sides, they may be tempted to treat inconvenient facts as distractions from the struggle. That temptation has a long history on the left as well as the right.

Chomsky’s formulation retains its importance because it is not simply liberal naïveté about reason. It is a discipline against apologetics. In 1967, his target was not only ignorance but expertise in the service of power: the production of rationalizations by people who knew better or should have known better. That problem has not disappeared. Indeed, it is everywhere. But one can also become an expert rationalizer for a movement, a nation, an identity, or a wounded community. Intellectual responsibility requires resisting that role even when the pressure comes from one’s own side.

Kelley invokes Gramsci’s distinction between traditional and organic intellectuals to show that intellectuals are embedded in social relations. This is an indispensable corrective to fantasies of pure independence. But Gramsci should not be read as licensing the collapse of inquiry into loyalty. Organic intellectuals help social groups understand the world, not merely repeat their slogans. They refine strategy, expose contradictions, develop concepts, and sometimes tell movements what they do not wish to hear. A labor intellectual who cannot criticize a union bureaucracy, an anticolonial intellectual who cannot criticize authoritarianism among national liberation leaders, or a feminist intellectual who cannot name abuses within progressive spaces is not serving the struggle. Such a person is serving cohesion at the expense of emancipation.

The Black radical tradition Kelley discusses demonstrates this more complex responsibility. W. E. B. Du Bois, C. L. R. James, Claudia Jones, Ella Baker, and others were not neutral observers, but neither were they stenographers of a line. Their greatness lay partly in their ability to connect immediate injustice to global structures while also revising inherited categories. James could celebrate revolutionary masses and criticize party domination. Baker could work inside movements while warning against messianic leadership. Du Bois changed his mind over a long life, sometimes dramatically. Their intellectual courage included both commitment and judgment.

The present requires the same dual obligation. Intellectuals should defend students punished for Palestine advocacy. They should oppose laws and executive actions that deport or discipline people for protected political speech. They should object when universities use antisemitism as a pretext for suppressing criticism of Israel. But they should also preserve distinctions that some activists blur: between Jewish institutions and the Israeli state; between Zionism as a family of political projects and Jews as a people; between support for Palestinian liberation and endorsement of armed attacks on civilians; between civil disobedience and coercive intimidation; between discomfort and danger; between offensive speech and harassment.

These distinctions are not pedantic. They are the infrastructure of democratic politics. Without them, administrators will exploit ambiguity to repress dissent, and activists will sometimes exploit ambiguity to avoid accountability. The answer to bad-faith accusations is not indifference to all accusations. It is careful judgment. Some Jewish students have cynically invoked fear to silence Palestinians; some have also experienced real hostility. Some protest slogans have been deliberately misrepresented; some have been needlessly cruel or politically obtuse. Some encampments were models of multiracial, interfaith solidarity; some tolerated rhetoric or behavior that organizers should have addressed. A serious intellectual culture must be able to say all these things at once.

Kelley’s use of “genocide” also deserves engagement. I think the term is defensible with respect to Gaza, especially given the scale of destruction, the deprivation of conditions necessary for life, and statements by officials that plausibly indicate genocidal intent. International law does not require gas chambers or an explicit written plan. Genocide can be carried out through killing, bodily and mental harm, and deliberately inflicted conditions of life. Still, intellectuals should be careful in how they use the word. Its force depends on argument, evidence, and legal-moral clarity. If “genocide” becomes simply the name for any atrocious war, it loses precision. If fear of imprecision prevents us from naming Gaza, it becomes cowardice. The task is neither inflation nor avoidance, but rigorous moral description.

The same is true of “fascism.” Kelley is persuasive that Trumpism and its allied movements contain fascistic elements: cultic leadership, racial nationalism, contempt for pluralism, attacks on universities and the press, fantasies of mass deportation, tolerance for political violence, and an alliance between oligarchic power and authoritarian politics. But fascism is not the only term we need. The United States is also marked by constitutional decay, oligarchy, Christian nationalism, settler colonial inheritance, carceral governance, and neoliberal abandonment. Different terms illuminate different mechanisms. To call everything fascism can obscure the ordinary institutions that enabled the crisis: courts, legislatures, philanthropies, police unions, zoning boards, university trustees, media companies, and liberal administrations that normalized emergency powers abroad.

This matters because strategy follows diagnosis. If we imagine fascism as a single regime centered entirely on Trump, then elections become everything. If we imagine fascism as a social tendency rooted in racial capitalism and imperial power, then elections are necessary but insufficient. Kelley rightly favors the second view. But if everything from donor influence to bad university governance to U.S. foreign policy is folded into fascism, we may lose the ability to identify specific remedies. Defending birthright citizenship, protecting trans health care, bargaining union contracts, ending arms transfers, reforming police, preserving research independence, and winning local housing fights require overlapping but distinct coalitions.

Where Kelley is most compelling is in his insistence that intellectuals must organize. Academic freedom is not self-executing. The American Association of University Professors can issue statements; they matter. But without unions, legal defense networks, faculty senates willing to act, student-worker alliances, and public pressure, statements become elegies. Tenure protects too few people. Graduate workers, adjuncts, international students, staff, and untenured faculty bear the greatest risk. Those with security must spend it down in defense of those without it. That is not romantic militancy; it is institutional realism.

Still, I would frame the goal somewhat differently from Kelley. He calls for “a new university,” and I share the aspiration. But the university we need must not be merely an instrument of the left, just as it must not be an instrument of donors, corporations, or the national security state. It should be a democratic institution committed to inquiry, public goods, and equal dignity. That means it must protect radical scholars, conservative scholars, religious students, antiwar activists, Zionists, anti-Zionists, and those who are still uncertain. It must draw lines at threats, harassment, discrimination, and violence. But it must not confuse protection with ideological comfort.

This is not a plea for false balance. Universities need not pretend that climate change is uncertain, that race science is respectable, or that all views on human equality deserve equal standing. But inquiry requires more than correct conclusions. It requires practices: evidence, openness to revision, fair characterization of opponents, and willingness to distinguish between what we know, what we infer, and what we hope. These practices are not bourgeois ornaments. They are defenses against authoritarianism. When the right attacks universities as indoctrination factories, the best answer is not to embrace counter-indoctrination. It is to make universities more democratic, more courageous, and more intellectually serious.

Kelley’s closing story of Professor Tiffany Willoughby-Herard is moving because it embodies a truth no proceduralism can capture: education is a relation of responsibility. A professor who stands between students and police is doing intellectual work in the deepest sense. She is teaching what institutions value and what they betray. But after the arrest, other responsibilities begin: documenting what happened accurately, defending due process, building coalitions, contesting budgets, protecting the most vulnerable students, and making room for disagreement within the movement about tactics and goals. The dramatic moment must be followed by durable practice.

The responsibility of intellectuals in this age, then, is neither detached truth-telling nor uncritical enlistment. It is truthful solidarity. Truthful solidarity means choosing the side of those facing domination while refusing to surrender one’s responsibility to evidence, complexity, and moral consistency. It means opposing genocide in Gaza and antisemitism at home; defending Palestine advocacy and the rights of Jewish students; resisting Trumpist authoritarianism and criticizing liberal complicity; organizing within universities and preserving the university as a place where movements themselves can be questioned.

Power without truth becomes domination. Truth without power becomes lamentation. Kelley is right that lamentation is not enough. But in building power, intellectuals must not abandon the austere, sometimes lonely obligation to say what is true even when it inconveniences the side they have chosen. That obligation is not a retreat from struggle. It is one of the ways struggle remains worthy of the name liberation.

Perhaps the issue is not that truth-telling is impotent, but that we have misunderstood what truth-telling does and for whom. The liberal model, which Chomsky largely inherited even as he radicalized it, imagines truth as a kind of solvent: expose the lie, and the edifice of power will corrode. The fantasy is that the state’s legitimacy depends on its credibility, that there is a public sphere in which reasons can be adjudicated, and that the intellectual’s primary audience is the undecided citizen who can be swayed by facts. What, then, does truth-telling accomplish if it does not persuade the powerful? Kelley offers an answer without quite naming it: truth-telling is an act of movement-building, not persuasion. The document was suppressed, its authors harassed and blacklisted, but it became a touchstone. It told the truth not to the state but to the insurgent class, and in doing so it helped constitute that class as a political subject.

This reframing helps resolve another tension in Kelley’s essay: his simultaneous celebration of the university as “contested terrain” and his catalog of the university’s utter capitulation to donors, the security state, and the Trump administration. The truth produced in such spaces is not addressed to the dean or the provost, though it may be weaponized against them. It is addressed to the people with whom one is in struggle.

This points to the deepest challenge Kelley’s essay poses. The distinction between the truth-teller and the fighter is not always as sharp in practice as it is in polemic.

For this present moment, Kelley is right that the rubric of “telling truth to power” is exhausted if it means addressing power in a language power has already learned to absorb and neutralize. What remains urgent is telling truth to one another—not in the spirit of self-confirming righteousness, but in the spirit of clarity, strategic analysis, and historical memory. Fascism thrives on the destruction of shared reality, but the counter to that destruction is not simply to insist on facts. That is the work Willoughby-Herard was doing on that concrete, and it is the work Kelley’s essay invites us to take up. The essay does not, in the end, abandon truth-telling.
