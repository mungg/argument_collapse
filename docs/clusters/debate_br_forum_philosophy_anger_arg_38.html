"Agnes Callard’s defense of the eternal grudge is unconvincing because it relies on a flawed, atomistic view of human action that ignores how moral repair and apologies fundamentally alter the normative meaning of past wrongs."

Agnes Callard’s "The Reason to Be Angry Forever" offers a startling defense of the eternal grudge. Her argument fundamentally challenges the Strawsonian consensus in contemporary moral philosophy, which holds that while negative reactive attitudes like resentment are essential to holding others responsible, they are also sensitive to moral repair. Callard argues that because the past cannot be changed, the reason for anger remains forever. Thus, to hold a grudge is perfectly rational, and the philosophical attempt to separate the moral value of anger from the irrationality of grudges is a failure. While Callard’s critique of the "purification" of anger is incisive, her Argument for Grudges is ultimately unconvincing because it misconstrues the nature of moral reasons and the ontology of human action.

According to Callard, the reason you are angry on Tuesday about a theft on Monday is the fact that the theft occurred and that it was wrong. When the thief apologizes and pays you back on Wednesday, Callard asserts that "Apologies, restitution, and all the rest do nothing to cancel or alter the fact that I stole, nor the fact that I ought not to have stolen. Those facts were your reasons to be angry." Therefore, the reason persists, and the anger should persist.

This argument relies on a highly localized and atomistic view of human action. It treats the "theft on Monday" as a hermetically sealed event, eternally discrete and unalterable. But human actions, especially in the context of interpersonal relationships, are not discrete physical events like a rock falling; they are highly context-dependent, socially embedded performances whose meanings are subject to retrospective negotiation.

When an offender offers a sincere apology, undergoes remorse, and makes restitution, they are not changing the *physical* past, but they are altering the *normative* past. As philosopher Pamela Hieronymi has argued (whom Callard mentions but whose specific arguments on forgiveness she bypasses), an offense constitutes a lingering threat: it stands as a claim by the offender that the victim can be treated in this degrading way. Resentment is a protest against that ongoing claim.

When the offender sincerely apologizes, they retract the claim. They join the victim in condemning the past act. The apology changes the moral significance of the past event. The event is no longer an active assertion of disrespect; it is a shared point of moral failure that has been disavowed. Therefore, Callard is simply incorrect when she says the reasons for anger have not changed. The reason for anger was not just the physical movement of the stolen goods; it was the expressive, insulting meaning of the theft. The apology extinguishes that expressive meaning. Once the threat is withdrawn, the rational basis for the protest—the resentment—dissolves.

To maintain the anger on Thursday, as Callard suggests is rational, is actually an epistemic failure. It is a refusal to recognize the moral reality of the apology. The eternal grudge does not track the truth; it tracks a phantom. It insists on interpreting the offender’s present self entirely through the lens of a past action that the offender has themselves repudiated.

Callard might respond, using her Argument for Revenge, that holding onto the theft is how we hold the person accountable. "You hold me accountable by holding onto my theft, refusing to forget it, turning its one-off opposition between our interests into a rule to which I am now subject." But this conflates accountability with punishment. Accountability is the demand for a moral response; it is the drawing of the offender into the space of reasons to answer for their conduct. Once they have answered—via apology and restitution—the work of accountability is complete. To continue to enforce the "rule" of opposition is no longer accountability; it is cruelty.

Callard’s reliance on Nietzsche to undergird her dark view of morality is telling. Nietzsche indeed saw morality as rooted in *ressentiment* and bloodlust. But Peter Strawson’s great contribution in "Freedom and Resentment" was to show that our reactive attitudes, while sometimes ugly, are fundamentally expressions of our vulnerability to and care for one another. We resent because we care about how others regard us. When that regard is restored through reconciliation, the resentment naturally and rationally abates.

We do not need to invent a "philosopher's fiction" of pure transition anger to avoid Callard’s trap. We merely need to recognize that human beings are temporal creatures capable of changing their minds and their moral stances. The past is fixed in its physical details, but its moral meaning is always subject to revision through the hard work of repair. Callard’s universe is one of terrifying stasis, where every wrong is an eternal stain and every victim is condemned to endless, rational rage. But in the real world, we possess the remarkable technology of forgiveness, a deeply rational process that allows us to be good, and to see others as good, even after they have been bad.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Human actions are not discrete physical events but socially embedded performances whose moral significance is subject to retrospective negotiation and revision.
  2. 02A sincere apology functions as a retraction of the offender's claim of disrespect, thereby extinguishing the expressive meaning of the wrong that served as the original reason for anger.
  3. 03Maintaining anger after an apology is an epistemic failure that refuses to recognize the moral reality of the offender's present self and their repudiation of the past act.
  4. 04Callard conflates accountability with cruelty, as true accountability is a demand for a moral response that is satisfied once the offender offers apology and restitution.
  5. 05Forgiveness is a rational technology that allows temporal creatures to revise the moral meaning of the past and restore mutual regard, avoiding the 'terrifying stasis' of eternal rage.
