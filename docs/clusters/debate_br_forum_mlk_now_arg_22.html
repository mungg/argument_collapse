"While Brandon Terry successfully recovers the radical political strategist in Martin King Jr., he fails to acknowledge that King’s underlying ethical framework of Christian love and redemptive suffering is insufficient and perhaps even a burden for contemporary social justice struggles."

Brandon Terry has done a valuable service by excavating the genuinely radical Martin Luther King Jr.—the critic of liberal proceduralism, the proponent of coercive mass civil disobedience, and the unsparing diagnostician of America’s intertwined evils of racism, militarism, and poverty. Yet in his eagerness to rescue King from a sepia-toned sainthood, Terry ultimately pulls his punches, producing a King whose radicalism remains tethered to a deeply conservative moral cosmology of Christian love, nonviolence, and redemptive suffering. The lead essay celebrates King’s “politics of character” and defends his exhortations to love one’s enemies, even as it acknowledges the strategic coercion lurking beneath the surface of Birmingham-era protest. This tension should prompt a harder question: is King’s ethical framework still suited to an age of resurgent white nationalism, unaccountable state violence, and a Democratic establishment more interested in managing protest than honoring its demands?

Terry insists that King’s theory of racism, with its synthesis of psychological, sociopolitical, and cognitive dimensions, offers a more precise and politically useful analysis than the flattened categories of implicit bias or white supremacy totality. There is much to admire here. King’s understanding that racism thrives on “irrational fears” and requires an attack on legitimating ideas, not just hearts, is genuinely insightful. Yet Terry’s own account reveals the practical limits of King’s prescriptive politics. King believed that nonviolent direct action, animated by agape love, could “dissolve the stereotype” and “disclose new possibilities for living together.” The historical record, including the examples Terry cites, suggests a much messier reality. The success of the Birmingham campaign owed as much to the threat of urban rebellion—as Malcolm X acidly pointed out—as to the moral theater of peaceful suffering. Terry concedes this coercive dimension but then insists that nonviolence itself, not the implicit threat of its breakdown, held a unique power to transform the public imagination. It is hard to read the events from Ferguson to Baltimore in our own time and come to the same conclusion. When the cameras disappeared in Missouri, it was not because the peaceful Moral Mondays protests of North Carolina had captured the nation’s soul, but because the specter of unrest faded. The lesson many activists drew is precisely what King’s defenders are loath to admit: nonviolent discipline is most effective when it is haunted by the credible possibility of its opposite.

This is not an argument for abandoning nonviolent tactics wholesale, but for refusing to enshrine them as a moral absolute. King’s own radicalization, as Terry skillfully traces, moved from a faith in spiritual conversion to a hard-nosed realism about creating crisis situations that force negotiations. Yet Terry still defends King’s warnings against hatred and his insistence that we must seek our opponent’s “friendship and understanding.” In 2018, such exhortations ring hollow against the backdrop of a political moment in which white supremacists openly march with torches chanting “Jews will not replace us,” and a president routinely traffics in racist incitement. The demand that the oppressed cultivate love for those who would see them eliminated or subordinated is a profound and perhaps indecent burden. While King distinguished between righteous anger and corrosive hatred, the line is not as clear as he hoped. Often what is decried as hatred from onlookers is nothing more than the justified fury of people who have been told for generations that their patience is a virtue.

Terry’s retrieval also falters on the economic front. King’s later embrace of a guaranteed income and full employment, his critique of “superfluous wealth and abject poverty,” and his call for a “radical redistribution of economic and political power” were indeed radical for his time. But they remained within a framework that accepted capitalism’s basic structure, seeking to tame its excesses rather than transform its logic. Today’s movements confront a neoliberalism that has eviscerated the welfare state, financialized the economy, and turned racial justice into a matter of corporate diversity branding. King’s moral calls for a “positive peace” cannot substitute for a political economy that directly attacks capital accumulation and its racialized machinery. Here, the secular left has developed analytic tools and organizing models—from the Fight for $15 to prison abolitionism—that draw little sustenance from King’s Christian socialism.

The deepest problem with Terry’s King is the attempt to salvage a “politics of character” as distinct from respectability. Virtues such as humility, compassion, and magnanimity are framed as essential to human flourishing, not as pleas for white approval. Yet this distinction is slippery when the oppressed are expected to exhibit virtues that the powerful rarely bother to cultivate. Terry rightly criticizes the view that black people must prove themselves fit for citizenship, but he ends up endorsing a seemingly universal ethical code that in practice has always fallen heaviest on those already at the bottom. Black women, for example, have been told for centuries that their dignity depends on a particular kind of sexual propriety and selfless endurance. Magnanimity toward an oppressor may be a personal spiritual achievement, but it is not a political obligation. In the current climate, the most radical act may be to stop performing emotional generosity for those who have shown none.

I don’t argue that King should be discarded. His tactical genius around the staging of moral drama, his critique of cultural stereotypes, and his insistence on the mass, disruptive character of protest remain indispensable. But we should be clear-eyed: the core of his ethical vision belongs to a Christian cosmology that most activists today do not share, and its political utility is a contingent question, not a settled truth. To truly take King seriously as a thinker is to refuse the saintly aura and assess him as we would any strategist—ruthlessly, historically, and without sentiment. If that means acknowledging that King’s thought, for all its power, is insufficient for the struggles ahead, then that is the most honest homage we can pay.
