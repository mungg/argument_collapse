**Privacy Is Not the Only Liberty**

Reed Hundt's essay is the work of a serious public servant, and I share his concern that the architecture of surveillance, once built, can be turned to ends very different from those that justified its construction. But I want to register a dissent from the framing that has become standard among privacy advocates—a framing that Hundt at one point seems to renounce ("framing surveillance as a tradeoff between privacy and security is a dead end") and then immediately re-enacts. The truth is that there is a tradeoff. There has always been one. Pretending that clever technology and clever law can dissolve it does the public a disservice.

Begin with the threat. The September 11 hijackers communicated, traveled, and transferred money across borders. The Boston bombers, less than a year before Hundt wrote, were identified after the fact partly through telecommunications and image data. ISIL, AQAP, and a half-dozen other groups recruit and plan over the same platforms that Americans use to order pizzas. To suggest, as Hundt implies, that the bulk collection programs revealed by Snowden are mostly about pattern-recognition fantasies rather than actual investigative leads is to ignore what Judge Pauley actually wrote in December: that the program in question could plausibly have helped identify the 9/11 plotters in time. Reasonable people can disagree about whether his counterfactual is correct, but the question is empirical, not constitutional. Judge Leon's "almost Orwellian" rhetoric is striking; it is not analysis.

Hundt's proposed remedies, taken seriously, would substantially reduce the government's capacity to detect threats. Storing telephone metadata at private carriers rather than at NSA sounds modest, but in practice it means slower queries, inconsistent retention, and the loss of the ability to look back across providers. Requiring individualized warrants for queries against bulk data, when bulk analysis is precisely how connections between unknown parties are discovered, is an attempt to apply Fourth Amendment doctrine designed for searches of a person's home to the wholly different problem of identifying who, among hundreds of millions, might be coordinating an attack. The old categories do not map cleanly onto the new problem, which is why the FISA Court—a body Hundt treats with skepticism—has had to develop its own jurisprudence.

I am not arguing that the intelligence community should be unsupervised. The Church Committee was right; the abuses of the 1960s and 1970s were real; FISA was a necessary corrective. Today, oversight has thickened, not thinned: there are inspectors general, congressional intelligence committees, the FISC, the Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board, and now a presidential review group. Hundt himself catalogues these institutions and concedes that they have produced serious recommendations, some of which the President has adopted. This is not the picture of an unaccountable surveillance state. It is the picture of a democratic system grappling, sometimes clumsily, with genuinely hard tradeoffs.

What troubles me most about Hundt's essay is its enthusiasm for cryptographic self-help as a substitute for democratic deliberation. Tor and Bitcoin are presented as tools of citizen empowerment, with their use by drug traffickers and money launderers acknowledged only in passing. But the Silk Road was not an incidental misuse of Tor; it was, for a time, one of its most economically significant applications. Bitcoin's principal real-world uses to date have included ransomware payments, narcotics, and the laundering of the proceeds of computer crime. When Hundt proposes that the government should "certify," "teach," and "broaden knowledge" of these tools, he is proposing that the state subsidize the construction of spaces deliberately designed to be opaque to law enforcement, and then trust that "pseudonymous police officers" will be able to penetrate them on a case-by-case basis. This is a heroic assumption. The arithmetic of investigation does not favor it.

There is a deeper point. Privacy is one liberty among several. The liberty to walk down the street without being murdered by a terrorist; the liberty of a kidnapped child not to be hidden in an encrypted forum; the liberty of a fraud victim to see her assailant prosecuted—these are also liberties, and they depend on the state's ability to investigate. A constitutional culture that elevates privacy above all other public goods, that treats every government query as presumptively suspect and every encrypted channel as presumptively sacred, is not the culture that produced the Bill of Rights. The framers worried about general warrants; they did not worry that the state would investigate too effectively.

Hundt asks who rules. The honest answer in a democracy is: we do, through messy institutions, accepting that perfect privacy and perfect security are both unavailable. The essay's elegance lies in its suggestion that we can have both. We cannot.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Bulk data collection and surveillance programs provide empirical investigative value in identifying and preventing terrorist threats that cannot be dismissed as mere 'fantasies.'
  2. 02Proposed reforms, such as storing metadata with private carriers or requiring individualized warrants for bulk queries, would significantly degrade the government's practical capacity to detect coordinated attacks.
  3. 03The existing framework of inspectors general, congressional committees, and oversight boards demonstrates that the surveillance state is subject to democratic accountability rather than being an unchecked power.
  4. 04Promoting cryptographic tools like Tor and Bitcoin as 'citizen empowerment' ignores their primary real-world utility for criminal activities and creates opaque spaces that law enforcement cannot effectively penetrate.
  5. 05The state has a fundamental obligation to protect other essential liberties—such as physical safety and the prosecution of crimes—which depend on the ability to conduct effective investigations.
Diversified MiniMax

"Reed Hundt’s proposal for a digital bill of rights and reliance on privacy-enhancing technologies fails to address the practical necessity of balancing national security with individual liberty."

Hundt's essay contains a revealing admission: "If I were asked to relinquish my privacy and put my right of self-expression under some constraint in return for protecting my family from harm, I would agree." This is not the voice of someone who believes privacy is a fundamental right that trumps all other considerations. It is the voice of someone who, under pressure, accepts the tradeoff he later condemns as a "dead end for democracy." The tension between Hundt's theoretical commitments and his practical instincts runs through the entire essay, and it reveals a fundamental problem with his approach.

The "dead end" framing treats privacy as an absolute value, but privacy has never been absolute in American law or practice. The Fourth Amendment protects against unreasonable searches, not against all searches. The First Amendment permits restrictions on speech in contexts like schools, workplaces, and national security matters. Due process requires balancing. The question is not whether to balance but how to balance, and Hundt's insistence that balancing is a "dead end" is itself a rhetorical dead end that forecloses serious policy discussion.

The surveillance programs revealed by Snowden are not primarily about privacy in the abstract. They are about preventing terrorist attacks, cyberattacks, and nuclear proliferation. These are serious threats, and the intelligence agencies charged with addressing them need tools. Hundt acknowledges that "few would deny the benefits of Internet 2.0 or mobile phones" and that "no one should begrudge the government's official purpose." If the purpose is legitimate, the tools must be adequate. The question is not whether to use big data for national security but how to use it without abuse.

Hundt's proposed solutions are either inadequate or counterproductive. Ending bulk collection of phone metadata, as recommended by the Review Group and partially adopted by the president, may reduce certain risks, but it also removes a tool that the NSA has used to detect terrorist networks. Hundt is confident that the programs are "overbroad" because the Privacy and Civil Liberties Oversight Board said so in a "split decision." But a split decision is not a finding of fact; it is a political conclusion. The other federal judge who ruled on the programs found that they "could have helped prevent the September 11 hijackings." The empirical claims about effectiveness are contested, and Hundt treats them as settled.

On the technological side, Hundt's enthusiasm for Tor and Bitcoin is overblown. Tor was developed with government funding, but it is used primarily by criminals, including drug dealers, child exploiters, and terrorists. Bitcoin's most prominent early use was The Silk Road, a black market for narcotics. These are not privacy tools for ordinary citizens; they are enablers for illegal activity. Hundt acknowledges that "government therefore has good reason to seek access to these networks," but he does not grapple with the implications: if these technologies are primarily used for crime, their widespread adoption would make society less safe.

The real solution is not to abandon surveillance but to improve its governance. This means better judicial oversight, more robust classification regimes, stronger protections for Americans, and clearer rules for international data sharing. It does not mean pretending that privacy can be protected by software tools or that the Constitution alone can balance security and liberty. Hundt's digital bill of rights is an interesting thought experiment, but it will not survive contact with the real world of terrorist networks, cyberattacks, and hostile states. The "creaky eighteenth-century structures" he dismisses have served American democracy for over two centuries. Their durability is not an accident; it reflects a deep wisdom about the nature of power and the limits of rights. That wisdom is worth preserving.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Hundt’s rejection of the privacy-versus-security tradeoff is a rhetorical dead end that ignores the legal reality that rights like the Fourth Amendment have never been absolute.
  2. 02The surveillance tools Hundt criticizes are essential for addressing legitimate and serious threats such as terrorism, cyberattacks, and nuclear proliferation.
  3. 03Hundt’s empirical claims regarding the ineffectiveness of bulk data collection are based on contested political conclusions rather than settled facts.
  4. 04Technologies like Tor and Bitcoin are primarily enablers for criminal activity rather than practical privacy tools for ordinary citizens.
  5. 05The solution to surveillance concerns lies in improving governance and judicial oversight within existing constitutional structures rather than abandoning necessary security tools for unproven software solutions.
