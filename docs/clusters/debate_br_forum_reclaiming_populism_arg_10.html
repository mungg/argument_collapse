The Strike, Not the Stadium: Essential Workers and the Politics COVID Has Made

Adom Getachew's introduction touches on the Chicago Teachers' Union strike, almost in passing, before moving to the larger theoretical questions of populist reclamation and global comparison. I want to invert her emphasis. The CTU story, and the wider story of the 2018–2020 wave of teacher strikes, is not a sidebar to the question of how the left should reposition itself. It is the most important political development on the American left of the last decade, and it points to a politics — neither populist in any meaningful sense nor reducible to standard liberal pluralism — that the COVID-19 moment is poised to amplify. The argument I want to make is simple: the political form the left should be cultivating in 2020 is the strike, broadly understood, and the political subject it should be cultivating is the essential worker. Neither of these maps cleanly onto the populist framework Getachew's forum sets out to recover.

Begin with what the strikes of 2018 and 2019 actually accomplished. The wave began in West Virginia in February 2018, when teachers walked out across all fifty-five counties in a state with no collective bargaining rights and won pay raises and benefits through statewide political pressure. It spread to Oklahoma, Kentucky, Arizona, Colorado — red states with weak union laws, where teachers built mass actions through Facebook groups and informal networks as much as through union infrastructure. It then moved to blue cities: Los Angeles in January 2019, Oakland in February, Chicago and St. Paul in October. In each case, the strikes accomplished material gains — pay, class size limits, hiring of nurses and counselors — but they also accomplished something less tangible. They demonstrated, to participants and observers, that mass collective action could still work in the United States in the twenty-first century. They re-introduced the strike, as both a tactic and an idea, into American political imagination in a way that it has not been since the 1970s.

This is more important than it sounds. For four decades, American political culture has been organized around the assumption that the strike is dead — that the institutional and legal frameworks erected in the wake of the air traffic controllers' defeat in 1981 had succeeded in pricing collective action out of the realm of the possible. The teachers' strikes punctured that assumption. They showed that in particular sectors, under particular conditions, with particular tactical innovations, the strike could still produce concessions. And they did so in a sector — public education — that is itself one of the most politically significant in the country, both because of its size (over three million teachers nationally) and because of its connections to nearly every other constituency: parents, students, taxpayers, community organizations.

The COVID-19 moment has the potential to extend this development dramatically. As Getachew notes, over 48 million workers are doing essential work without basic protections; the unemployment rate has spiked toward 18 percent; the pandemic has revealed that the people who keep the country running are systematically underpaid, unprotected, and disrespected. What it has also revealed is that these workers have leverage. The grocery store cannot function without clerks. The Amazon warehouse cannot function without pickers. The hospital cannot function without nurses, aides, and janitors. The meatpacking plant cannot function without line workers. The delivery economy cannot function without drivers. And in a moment when the cost of an outbreak in any one of these workplaces is enormously high — not just to workers but to managers, owners, and the public — workers have a degree of strategic position that they have not had in decades.

We are already seeing this leverage being exercised. The Instacart shoppers struck on March 30 demanding hazard pay and protective equipment. Amazon warehouse workers in Staten Island walked out the same day. Whole Foods workers staged a "sick-out" on March 31. Workers at General Electric demanded the company convert manufacturing capacity to ventilator production. Sanitation workers struck in Pittsburgh. Nurses at multiple hospitals have engaged in protest actions against inadequate protective equipment. Meatpacking workers at Smithfield, Tyson, and JBS have demanded plant closures. These are not, on the whole, traditional unionized actions; they are emerging from the gaps in the existing labor regime, often through informal networks, social media coordination, and ad-hoc workplace committees. But they are strikes in the substantive sense, and they are establishing a pattern that the left should be working hard to extend.

What is the political form here? It is not populism in any usable sense. The Instacart strike was not articulated in terms of "the people" against "the elite." It was articulated in terms of specific demands — hazard pay, gloves, masks, paid sick leave for workers who become ill — against a specific employer, on the basis of a specific workplace relationship. The Amazon workers in Staten Island were not invoking the moral monopoly of authentic America against corrupt cosmopolitans; they were saying, accurately, that they did not have the protective equipment they needed to do their jobs safely, and that they would withhold their labor until they got it. This is the language of class struggle in its most classical sense, and it is also the language of practical politics that wins concessions.

There is, of course, a question about how to scale this. A strike at a single warehouse can win concessions for the workers in that warehouse; what about the millions of workers in similar conditions who lack any organizational infrastructure? Here is where the institutional question becomes central, and where I want to register a partial disagreement with the more optimistic readings of the current moment. Strikes are easier to call than to win, and easier to win than to consolidate. The teacher strike wave produced real gains, but it has not yet produced the durable institutional power that would translate those gains into long-term political transformation. The CTU is exceptional in this regard precisely because it has invested for over a decade in building exactly that durable institutional power — a militant union, deep community ties, an electoral apparatus that has now elected councillors and county commissioners. Most of the strikes of 2018 and 2019 did not have anything like the CTU's infrastructure behind them, and many of the gains they won have been chipped away in the years since.

The COVID-19 strikes face an even harder version of this problem. They are happening, for the most part, in sectors with weak or nonexistent union representation, in workplaces where workers can be fired easily and quickly, against employers (Amazon, Walmart) with enormous resources and well-developed anti-union expertise. The early Amazon strike was followed, predictably, by the firing of its organizer. The Whole Foods sick-out was followed by surveillance and intimidation. Without serious institutional backing, these actions can light up the news for a day or two and then be extinguished. The political task for the left, then, is not to celebrate the strikes as expressions of "people power" but to build the institutional infrastructure — unions, legal defense funds, mutual aid networks, political vehicles — that can sustain them. This is unglamorous, multi-decade work, and it is the work.

Here is where I want to connect back to Getachew's framing. The "reclaiming populism" project, as articulated in the introduction and the WFP literature it draws from, is meant to support exactly this kind of institutional construction. The Working Families Party is, in many ways, a model of the patient electoral and organizational work that the moment requires. The CTU's transformation from a sclerotic AFT local into one of the most politically powerful unions in the country is another model. The Sanders campaign, whatever its failures, has left behind a generation of organizers with experience, contact lists, and political imagination that did not exist a decade ago. All of this is good and important work, and Getachew is right to celebrate it.

But I want to insist that this work is not best understood as populism, and that calling it populism obscures more than it clarifies. It is, more accurately, a kind of new labor politics — a politics that takes the workplace, the strike, and the union as central political sites while extending the boundaries of each of these beyond their twentieth-century forms. The CTU is not just a union of teachers; it is a union that has redefined teaching as care work, education as a public good, schools as community institutions, and bargaining as a vehicle for community-wide demands ("bargaining for the common good"). The Amazon workers' actions are not just narrow workplace disputes; they are interventions in a logistics infrastructure that has become central to the entire economy. The nurses' protests are not just professional grievances; they are challenges to a healthcare system that has been hollowed out by decades of cost-cutting. In each case, the political form is workplace action, but the political content extends far beyond the workplace.

This is the politics COVID has made possible, and it is what the left should be cultivating. It is not populist; it is something better. It is concrete, located, materially grounded, and capable of producing both immediate wins and durable institutional power. It also happens to be multiracial in a deep rather than rhetorical sense: the essential workforce is disproportionately people of color, disproportionately women, disproportionately immigrants, and any politics rooted in that workforce will necessarily be a politics that takes race, gender, and citizenship seriously not as add-ons but as constitutive features.

I will end with a methodological observation. The debate over populism that has dominated left intellectual life for the last several years has, I think, drawn attention away from the more important question of what political forms actually work — what builds power, what wins concessions, what creates durable institutions. The answer to that question is not to be found in theoretical reflection on the structure of populist appeals; it is to be found in the practical experience of strikes, organizing campaigns, electoral interventions, and movement-building. The Chicago Teachers' Union has more to teach us about the future of left politics than Ernesto Laclau does, and the Instacart shoppers have more to teach us than any number of think pieces on whether populism can be reclaimed. The COVID-19 crisis is going to produce, in the coming months, an enormous wave of new organizing and new struggle. The left's task is to be ready to support it, to learn from it, and to help it consolidate into durable power. Whether we call that populism or not strikes me as the least important question we face.
