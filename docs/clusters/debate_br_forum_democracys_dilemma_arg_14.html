"To resolve democracy’s information crisis, we must look beyond technical security fixes and rebuild the intermediary institutions—such as political parties, media organizations, and civic associations—that filter information and organize public disagreement."

The most useful feature of Farrell and Schneier’s essay is its insistence that democracy’s information crisis is not one problem but a system of interacting problems. Russian trolls, fake FCC comments, census distrust, machine-generated propaganda, partisan norm-breaking, and declining confidence in expertise are not identical phenomena. They become dangerous when they reinforce one another. In that sense the language of complex systems is more than metaphor. Democracy is not a machine that fails because one gear breaks; it is an ecology that can be pushed into healthier or more pathological patterns.

I agree with the authors that defending democracy requires negative feedback loops. But I would locate many of those loops in institutions they discuss only indirectly: political parties, media organizations, civic associations, and professional intermediaries. The contemporary American problem is not simply that false information enters the public sphere. It is that the organizations once responsible for filtering, contextualizing, and bargaining over information have weakened, polarized, or been bypassed. The result is a politics in which claims move rapidly from fringe to mainstream without passing through institutions capable of imposing costs on reckless actors.

Political parties are a central example. It is common to say that parties are too strong because partisanship is so intense. In another sense, however, American parties are too weak. They are powerful as brands and identities but weak as organizations with members, local roots, and gatekeeping authority. The presidential nomination process has shifted power toward media spectacle, donors, activists, and individual candidates. Congressional parties discipline votes but often do not cultivate shared policy expertise or cross-faction bargaining. Outside groups and partisan media can punish compromise more effectively than party leaders can reward responsibility.

This matters for democracy’s dilemma because parties are supposed to organize conflict. Nancy Rosenblum is right that partisanship can be constructive: parties turn diffuse grievances into programs, recruit candidates, and give voters a manageable choice. But when parties become hollow vehicles for negative identity, they cease to perform that organizing function. They become accelerants. Rumors, conspiracy theories, and procedural hardball spread because no authoritative party actors are willing or able to say to their own side: this helps us today but damages the system we need tomorrow.

The Republican Party’s relationship to climate science, which Farrell and Schneier cite, is one example. But the issue is broader than any single party. Democrats, too, face incentives to indulge implausible claims when those claims mobilize supporters. The Alabama deception operation is disturbing not because it was equal in scale to Russian interference or to the much larger right-wing misinformation ecosystem, but because it shows how quickly “they did it first” becomes a license for imitation. Parties that cannot discipline their own consultants and allied groups will not protect democratic legitimacy.

One reform agenda, then, should focus on making parties more responsible without making them less democratic. Stronger disclosure rules for online political activity would help. Campaigns should be held accountable for contractors and vendors who engage in impersonation or coordinated deception. Party committees could adopt enforceable norms against fake local news sites, fabricated grassroots pages, and misleading claims about voting procedures. Debates, ballot access rules, and nomination procedures should reward candidates capable of building durable coalitions rather than merely capturing attention. These are not glamorous reforms, but democracy often depends on unglamorous organizational incentives.

Media institutions are the second missing layer. The “more speech” model failed partly because it imagined information as content rather than as attention. In a world of abundance, the scarce resource is not speech but trust and focus. Professional journalism at its best provides verification, hierarchy, and context. At its worst it amplifies spectacle, both-sides bad faith, and conflict stripped of substance. The 2016 election exposed these weaknesses: hacked emails were treated as an endless source of campaign stories, while the provenance and strategic purpose of the leaks often received less sustained attention than their tactical implications.

A healthier media system would not be one in which journalists suppress inconvenient information because it may have been obtained or promoted by hostile actors. That would be too easy to abuse. Rather, newsrooms need norms for handling hacked, leaked, and strategically planted material. They should ask: Who benefits from the timing? What is the public interest? Are we publishing new knowledge or merely helping an operation dominate the agenda? Similar questions apply to viral misinformation. Debunking can spread the false claim. Ignoring can let it fester. The right choice depends on context, and context requires institutional judgment.

Local journalism deserves special emphasis. National information warfare thrives when citizens lack reliable information about their own communities. As local newspapers shrink, statehouses and city councils receive less scrutiny, and citizens increasingly interpret politics through national partisan frames. A school budget dispute becomes a battle between socialism and freedom; a zoning fight becomes evidence of a national conspiracy. Philanthropic support, nonprofit models, public subsidies insulated from political control, and new cooperative ownership structures should all be on the table. If democracy needs common knowledge, it needs people paid to produce it close to where citizens live.

The third layer is civic association. Much discussion of disinformation imagines isolated individuals encountering false posts on screens. But people interpret information socially. They ask friends, pastors, union leaders, teachers, colleagues, and community organizers what to believe. When these networks are strong and cross-cutting, they can dampen extremes. When they are weak or segregated, misinformation travels through closed loops of identity and resentment.

The census controversy illustrates this clearly. A federal advertising campaign alone will not persuade fearful immigrant communities to participate. Trusted local intermediaries might: churches, immigrant rights groups, health clinics, schools, and neighborhood organizations. But those organizations need resources, time, and genuine independence. If they are treated merely as transmission belts for official reassurance, they will lose credibility. The same principle applies to vaccination, disaster response, election administration, and policing. Trust is relational before it is informational.

Farrell and Schneier’s security frame is valuable for identifying vulnerabilities, but security professionals sometimes prefer technical fixes because technical systems have clear boundaries. Democracy does not. The FCC comment process was attacked through fake submissions, so authentication seems like the solution. But if authentication deters undocumented workers, victims of domestic violence, or employees of regulated companies from commenting, the process becomes less representative. Random sampling and deliberative mini-publics may be better than open comment counts for some purposes. A smaller, verified, demographically representative deliberative process could produce more meaningful public input than millions of form letters, whether real or fake.

This points to a broader lesson: not every democratic institution should maximize openness in the same way. Elections require universal access, secrecy of the ballot, accurate counting, and peaceful transfer of power. Public comments require inclusion, relevance, and protection against fraud. Scientific agencies require independence, peer review, and transparency of methods. The census requires participation and confidentiality. Legislative hearings require adversarial questioning but also sanctions for perjury and fabrication. Each institution has its own democratic function, and each needs its own security model.

What worries me about current debate is the search for a single solution: break up Facebook, regulate political ads, teach media literacy, punish foreign interference, revive civics, restore norms, elect better leaders. All are partial. A complex system needs layered defenses. In cybersecurity, no serious person relies on one barrier. Democracy likewise needs defense in depth: secure voting systems, independent election administration, transparent campaign finance, robust parties, responsible media, trusted civic intermediaries, and courts willing to enforce basic rules.

The foreign-domestic distinction also needs care. Farrell and Schneier rightly argue that domestic actors may be more dangerous than foreign ones. A Russian troll can inflame a racial conflict; domestic politicians can build careers on it. A foreign intelligence agency can claim an election is rigged; a candidate can repeat that claim to millions. A bot can submit a fake comment; an agency chair can pretend the resulting record reflects public opinion. The decisive question is often whether domestic elites launder manipulation into legitimate politics.

Elite responsibility therefore belongs at the center of democratic resilience. Ordinary citizens are often blamed for being gullible, tribal, or irrational. There is truth in the psychological research on motivated reasoning, but citizens take cues from leaders. If elected officials, media personalities, and party-aligned intellectuals refuse to impose costs on lies from their own side, no amount of media literacy will suffice. Conversely, when leaders accept losses, correct supporters, and defend neutral procedures even when inconvenient, they create negative feedback.

This is why institutional reform and political culture cannot be separated. Rules shape incentives, but norms determine whether actors exploit every loophole. Norms without rules become nostalgia; rules without norms become gamesmanship. The United States needs both: legal constraints on deception and corruption, and organizations capable of teaching ambitious politicians that some victories are too expensive for democracy to afford.

Farrell and Schneier close by calling for a democratic counterpart to the political technologists of authoritarian regimes. That is a compelling idea, provided we understand “technology” broadly. The relevant technologies are not only algorithms, audits, and authentication protocols. They are also parties, newspapers, hearings, unions, schools, churches, professional associations, and civic rituals. Democracy’s dilemma is that it must remain open enough to learn and closed enough to prevent sabotage. The solution will not be to eliminate vulnerability. It will be to rebuild the institutions that convert openness into judgment.
