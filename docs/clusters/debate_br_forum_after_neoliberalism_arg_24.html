Diversified Gemini

"The Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) project fails to address the root causes of modern crises because it attempts to fix systemic capitalist exploitation with technocratic tweaks rather than fundamental structural transformation."

Dani Rodrik, Suresh Naidu, and Gabriel Zucman offer a compelling, even comforting, narrative for the embattled economics profession. The story goes something like this: neoliberalism was a mistake, a “simplistic perversion” of economic principles pushed by ideologues and think tanks, but *real* economics—the rigorous, empirical, mathematically sophisticated discipline practiced in top universities—is perfectly equipped to solve the crises of inequality, climate change, and corporate power. If only we apply the right taxes, tweak the wage boards, and regulate the banks, we can achieve “inclusive prosperity.”

This is a seductive vision, particularly for center-left policymakers desperate for a technocratic fix to the political crises of 2019. But it fundamentally misdiagnoses both the nature of the crisis and the complicity of the economics profession in creating it. The authors want to save economics from neoliberalism, but they fail to recognize that neoliberalism is not a perversion of mainstream economics; it is its logical conclusion when applied to a capitalist system driven by the imperative of accumulation.

The Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) project relies on the premise that markets can be tamed if they are properly “embedded” in the right institutions. The authors cite Karl Polanyi to justify this, arguing that the “rules of the game” just need to be rewritten. But they strip Polanyi of his radicalism. Polanyi did not merely argue for better-regulated markets; he warned that the commodification of land, labor, and money was an existential threat to human society. The EfIP economists believe we can keep the underlying structure of capitalist property relations intact while designing clever mechanisms to redistribute the surplus.

Consider their proposals. Arindrajit Dube’s wage boards are a noble idea, but they assume a balance of power between labor and capital that simply no longer exists. Capital has spent the last forty years dismantling the institutional capacity of labor to strike, organize, and demand a share of the surplus. You cannot simply legislate a wage board into existence without a militant labor movement to enforce it, and mainstream economics has spent decades providing the intellectual justification for crushing such militancy in the name of "labor market flexibility."

Similarly, Gabriel Zucman’s proposal to tax multinationals based on where they make their sales is a brilliant piece of technocratic engineering. But why are multinationals able to hoard such vast profits in the first place? Because the global intellectual property regime, the suppression of wages, and the monopolization of production have allowed them to extract unprecedented rents. Taxing these rents after the fact is a band-aid on a gaping wound. It does not alter the fundamental dynamic of a system that enriches the owners of capital at the expense of those who produce the value.

The authors boast that modern economics has become “significantly more applied and empirical since the 1990s.” This is true, but empiricism is not a substitute for a theory of power. The "credibility revolution" in economics has produced thousands of highly specific papers identifying the marginal effects of discrete policy changes, but it has largely abandoned the attempt to understand the systemic laws of motion of the economy. By focusing on "market failures" and "second-best worlds," economists treat the systemic exploitation of labor and the degradation of the environment as unfortunate glitches in an otherwise functional system, rather than the inevitable outcomes of production for profit.

The EfIP network wants to subordinate textbook economic efficiency to values like democratic rule and egalitarian relationships. This is laudable. But they still operate within a framework that treats human beings primarily as utility-maximizing individuals and society as an aggregation of their preferences. They still rely on methodological individualism, even if they have softened it with insights from behavioral economics.

If we are truly going to move "beyond neoliberalism," we cannot rely on the same discipline that provided its intellectual scaffolding. We need a political economy that starts not from the assumption of scarce resources and infinite desires, but from the reality of class conflict, exploitation, and the structural imperatives of capital. We need to stop looking for "policy free lunches" and start talking about the expropriation of the expropriators. The crises we face in 2019—from the resurgence of fascism to the burning of the planet—are too severe to be solved by clever mechanism design. They require a fundamental transformation of our political and economic system, a transformation that mainstream economics, no matter how "inclusive," is structurally incapable of imagining.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Neoliberalism is the logical conclusion of mainstream economic principles applied to capitalism, not a mere perversion of the discipline as the authors claim.
  2. 02The authors' reliance on 'embedding' markets in institutions ignores the reality that capitalist property relations are inherently incompatible with social and environmental stability.
  3. 03Technocratic policy proposals, such as wage boards or tax reforms, are ineffective without addressing the underlying destruction of labor power and the systemic extraction of rents.
  4. 04The discipline's shift toward empirical research has come at the cost of losing a systemic theory of power, leading economists to treat exploitation as a series of 'market failures' rather than a core feature of the system.
  5. 05Mainstream economics remains trapped in a framework of methodological individualism that is structurally incapable of imagining the radical political and economic changes required to solve current global crises.
