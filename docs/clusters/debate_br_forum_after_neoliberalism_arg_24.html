"A post-neoliberal economic agenda must move beyond traditional market failures to address the unique power dynamics of the platform economy and the private governance of digital infrastructures."

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman are right that economics after neoliberalism must take institutions and power seriously. But their agenda still underplays one of the defining developments of our time: the rise of the platform economy and the concentration of control over data. If the neoliberal age was marked by faith in deregulated markets, the emerging post-neoliberal challenge may be markets governed by private digital infrastructures that look less like competitive arenas than privately managed social worlds.

By early 2019, this is already visible. A handful of technology companies mediate communication, advertising, retail, search, app distribution, cloud computing, transportation, and information flows. Their products are often useful and sometimes extraordinary. Consumers enjoy services that would have seemed magical a generation ago. Small businesses can reach customers in new ways. Social networks connect people across distance. But the economic and political power of these firms has grown faster than our institutions for governing them.

Traditional economics has tools for this: network effects, economies of scale, two-sided markets, switching costs, data advantages, intellectual property, and market power. These tools show why digital markets tend toward concentration. The more users a platform has, the more valuable it becomes; the more data it collects, the better it can target, predict, and improve; the more sellers depend on it, the more it can set terms. Competition may be “for the market” rather than “in the market,” but even that can weaken once incumbents acquire rivals, copy features, or control access points.

The lead essay’s critique of market fundamentalism is especially relevant here. For years, antitrust policy has been constrained by a narrow consumer welfare standard, often interpreted through price effects. But many digital services are nominally free. Users pay with data, attention, dependency, and exposure to manipulation. Sellers may pay through fees and platform rules. Workers may pay through algorithmic control. Competitors may pay through exclusion. Democracy may pay through misinformation and concentrated control over public discourse. A price-centered framework misses much of the harm.

Economics after neoliberalism should therefore rethink antitrust and regulation for digital capitalism. Market power is not only the ability to raise prices. It is the ability to set the rules of exchange, rank visibility, extract data, impose contract terms, shape innovation, and influence politics. When a platform owns the marketplace and competes in it, conflicts of interest are inevitable. When app developers, merchants, drivers, or media organizations depend on a gatekeeper, their formal freedom can mask practical subordination.

Labor issues are central. Gig platforms present themselves as neutral matchmakers between independent contractors and customers. But many exercise significant control through algorithms, ratings, pricing, deactivation, and information asymmetry. A driver may be called independent while lacking the entrepreneurial autonomy that term implies. Workers often do not know how pay is calculated, why they receive certain assignments, or how to challenge penalties. The old labor law binary—employee or independent contractor—fits poorly, and platforms exploit the ambiguity.

A post-neoliberal labor agenda should ensure that platform workers have basic protections: minimum earnings standards, transparency, unemployment and injury insurance, anti-discrimination protections, rights to organize, and due process before deactivation. Some may prefer flexibility, and policy should preserve genuine flexibility where possible. But flexibility should not mean absence of power or social insurance. Wage boards, as discussed in the lead essay, could be adapted to platform sectors, setting standards across companies that otherwise compete by shifting risk onto workers.

Data should be treated as a political-economic resource. Much data is generated socially: by users interacting, moving through cities, buying goods, writing reviews, and communicating. Yet its value is captured privately. The analogy to public infrastructure is imperfect but useful. If public roads, schools, research, and legal systems make digital platforms possible, and if users collectively generate the data that gives them value, then the public has a claim. This could justify data portability, interoperability mandates, fiduciary duties, public data trusts, taxes on digital rents, or public options in digital services.

Interoperability is especially important. One reason platforms become powerful is that leaving means losing networks. If users could communicate across social networks or move data easily, competition and user autonomy might improve. Regulation has long required interconnection in sectors such as telecommunications. Digital markets may need similar principles, though privacy and security must be handled carefully.

Privacy should not be framed only as individual consent. Neoliberal thinking loves consent forms: if users click “agree,” the transaction is legitimate. But no individual can realistically understand or negotiate the data ecosystem. Privacy is collective. One person’s data can reveal information about others. Behavioral targeting can shape public life. Surveillance can chill freedom. We need substantive limits on data collection and use, not merely longer disclosures.

The political effects are profound. Platforms increasingly shape what news people see, which political ads are targeted to whom, and how misinformation spreads. This is not simply a matter of bad content moderation decisions. It is a business model built around engagement, attention, and advertising. Outrage and falsehood can be profitable. A democratic society cannot leave the architecture of public discourse entirely to firms whose duties run primarily to shareholders.

At the same time, we should avoid simplistic calls for state control of speech. Government regulation of platforms can threaten civil liberties, especially under illiberal leaders. The challenge is institutional design: transparency, auditability, due process, competition, public-interest obligations, and perhaps separation between platform infrastructure and content curation. Economists can contribute by analyzing incentives, but lawyers, technologists, journalists, and civil society must be involved.

Taxation of digital firms also belongs in the post-neoliberal agenda. Profit shifting is rampant among companies whose valuable assets are intangible and easy to locate in low-tax jurisdictions. Zucman’s sales-based apportionment proposal is relevant, though digital services raise questions about where value is created: where users are, where data is generated, where intellectual property is held, where engineers work, where advertisers pay. The current system allows too much income to escape taxation. Fiscal capacity requires new rules.

Innovation policy must distinguish between rewarding invention and entrenching monopoly. The technology sector often argues that large profits are necessary for innovation. Sometimes temporary market power encourages investment. But dominant firms can also suppress innovation by acquiring nascent competitors, restricting access, or directing talent toward advertising optimization rather than social need. Public funding contributed to many foundational technologies, from the internet to GPS. The public should be more assertive in shaping technological development toward social goals: health, climate, accessibility, education, and worker augmentation.

The lead essay’s discussion of technology and labor rightly notes that the direction of innovation is not exogenous. This is crucial. If firms are rewarded for replacing workers, surveilling consumers, and addicting users to screens, we will get more of that. If public policy rewards technologies that complement workers, reduce carbon emissions, improve care, and expand knowledge, innovation will look different. Markets do not simply discover the future; they are guided toward it.

Digital capitalism also challenges the nation-state. Platforms operate globally, while regulation remains national or regional. Europe has moved more aggressively on privacy and competition than the United States, but enforcement remains difficult. Developing countries face additional concerns: dependence on foreign platforms, extraction of data, weak tax capture, and limited domestic technological capacity. A fair digital order will require international cooperation, but not the kind of trade agreements that lock in corporate privileges under the banner of free data flows.

Ultimately, the platform economy vindicates the lead essay’s central claim: laissez-faire does not produce neutral or competitive markets. It produces whatever institutional order law permits. In digital markets, that order is increasingly characterized by private governance. Terms of service become law-like. Algorithms become managers. Platforms become regulators. Data becomes capital. Attention becomes a commodity.

Economics after neoliberalism must therefore ask not only how to redistribute the gains from technology, but how to democratize technological power. That may require stronger antitrust, sector-specific regulation, worker rights, data governance, public investment, and new ownership models. It will certainly require abandoning the comforting assumption that innovation is always best left alone.

The next economy will be built on digital infrastructures. The question is whether they will be governed as private empires or as institutions accountable to the societies that make them valuable.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The traditional consumer welfare standard in antitrust is insufficient for digital markets where harms manifest through data extraction, algorithmic control, and the erosion of democratic discourse rather than just price increases.
  2. 02Labor policy must be updated to provide platform workers with basic protections and social insurance, countering the power imbalances created by algorithmic management and the misclassification of independent contractors.
  3. 03Data should be treated as a social resource rather than private capital, justifying interventions like interoperability mandates, data portability, and public data trusts.
  4. 04Privacy must be regulated as a collective social good rather than a matter of individual consent, requiring substantive limits on data collection and behavioral targeting.
  5. 05The direction of technological innovation is not inevitable and should be steered by public policy toward social goals like worker augmentation and climate solutions rather than just labor replacement or surveillance.
  6. 06International cooperation and new tax frameworks, such as sales-based apportionment, are necessary to prevent digital firms from using intangible assets to evade fiscal responsibilities.
  7. 07The architecture of public discourse and the platform business models that profit from misinformation must be subjected to public-interest obligations and institutional oversight to protect democratic stability.
