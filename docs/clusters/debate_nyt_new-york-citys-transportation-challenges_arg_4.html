"The next mayor of New York City must prioritize pedestrian safety and a 'Vision Zero' approach to street design to eliminate preventable traffic fatalities."

**Put Pedestrians First, Because Pedestrians Are New Yorkers**

Every year, roughly 150 pedestrians are killed on the streets of New York City and thousands more are injured. That is more than the number of people murdered with firearms in most years. We treat one number as a national emergency and the other as weather. The next mayor needs to stop accepting traffic deaths as the cost of doing business in a big city.

Walking is the dominant mode of transportation in New York. Far more trips happen on foot than by bike, taxi, or private car. Yet our streets are still engineered, signed, and policed as if their primary function were moving motor vehicles through as quickly as possible. Crosswalks that give pedestrians seven seconds to cross six lanes. Turn signals that put left-turning cars into the same phase as people stepping off the curb. NYPD precincts that close fatal-crash investigations in hours, almost always concluding "no criminality suspected."

The good news is that the tools to fix this are well understood and inexpensive. Lower the citywide default speed limit from 30 to 25 mph; physics does the rest, because survival rates in pedestrian-vehicle crashes fall off a cliff above 30. Install leading pedestrian intervals at every signalized intersection — a three-second head start before cars get the green. Daylight intersections by banning parking within 20 feet of the corner, so drivers and pedestrians can see each other. Build neckdowns and raised crosswalks on the corridors where children walk to school. Stockholm, London, and now San Francisco have shown that a "Vision Zero" approach — designing streets on the assumption that humans make mistakes but should not die for them — actually works.

This agenda is not in conflict with bikeshare; it is the same agenda. But it does require a mayor willing to confront the NYPD's culture of indifference to traffic violence and to back a DOT commissioner against the inevitable backlash from drivers who feel inconvenienced by a four-second delay. The measure of the next mayor's transportation record should not be how many bike lanes were striped or how fast the FDR moves. It should be how many New Yorkers came home alive.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Traffic deaths are currently treated as an acceptable cost of city life rather than the public health emergency they truly represent.
  2. 02New York City streets are currently engineered and policed to prioritize motor vehicle speed over the safety of pedestrians, who represent the city's dominant mode of transportation.
  3. 03Proven, inexpensive engineering solutions such as lower speed limits, leading pedestrian intervals, and daylighting intersections can significantly increase survival rates.
  4. 04Achieving safety goals requires the mayor to reform the NYPD's culture of indifference toward traffic violence and resist political pressure from drivers inconvenienced by safety measures.
Diversified GPT

"The next mayor of New York City must prioritize pedestrian safety as the central goal of the transportation system by adopting a citywide safety agenda and redesigning streets to eliminate routine traffic deaths."

Before expanding any transportation program, the next mayor should ask a simple moral question: why are New Yorkers still being killed while crossing the street? A great city cannot accept routine traffic deaths as the cost of doing business. Pedestrians are not a special-interest group. Everyone is a pedestrian at some point, including drivers walking from a parking space and subway riders walking from a station. If the streets are not safe for the old, the young and the disabled, the transportation system has failed.

Bike share is a useful addition to the city, and it should grow carefully. But the urgency belongs to pedestrian safety. New York has made progress in some places through plazas, narrower crossings and protected lanes, yet too many avenues remain designed for speed. Wide turns, long crossing distances, poorly timed lights and aggressive driving make ordinary walks dangerous, especially for seniors. The next mayor should adopt a citywide safety agenda with measurable targets and public accountability.

That agenda should include lower speed limits on residential streets and around schools, more speed cameras if Albany will allow them, and serious enforcement of failure to yield. Police should treat dangerous driving as a public safety problem, not merely an unfortunate accident unless alcohol is involved. Crash investigations must be thorough, and the city should publish data in a form communities can use. Street redesign should focus first on the corridors with the most injuries, not the neighborhoods with the loudest advocates.

The city should also redesign intersections for human behavior. People cross where they need to cross. They misjudge speeds. Children run. Older people walk slowly. Good engineering anticipates those facts instead of blaming victims. Pedestrian islands, curb extensions, leading pedestrian signals, daylighting near corners and shorter crossings are not luxuries. They are basic protections.

For outer-borough commuters, safety and mobility should be linked. Many dangerous streets are also bus corridors. Give buses priority, shorten crossings to bus stops, and enforce lanes so riders are not trapped in traffic. Faster buses and safer walking can be achieved on the same streets.

The next mayor will face pressure to choose a symbol: bike share, cars, subways, ferries. The better choice is a standard: no transportation improvement should come at the expense of human life, and every street project should make walking safer. If New York can move millions each day, it can also stop treating pedestrian deaths as inevitable.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Pedestrian safety is a universal concern because every citizen, regardless of their primary mode of transport, is a pedestrian at some point.
  2. 02The city should implement a safety agenda that includes lower speed limits, increased use of speed cameras, and stricter police enforcement of traffic violations.
  3. 03Street engineering and intersection redesign should proactively account for human behavior and vulnerability rather than blaming victims for accidents.
  4. 04Infrastructure improvements should be prioritized based on injury data and high-risk corridors rather than political advocacy or neighborhood influence.
  5. 05Improving outer-borough commutes can be achieved by linking safety and mobility through dedicated bus lanes and safer access to transit stops.
