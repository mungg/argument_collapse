Diversified Gemini

"While Aziz Rana correctly identifies the settler-colonial and imperial roots of the American concept of freedom, his analysis is incomplete because it fails to address how that concept is fundamentally built upon patriarchal foundations and the subjugation of women's labor."

Aziz Rana’s "A Different Freedom" provides a necessary and powerful deconstruction of the American mythos of liberty. By exposing how the dominant, right-wing concept of freedom is rooted in the violent, zero-sum dynamics of settler colonialism and imperial expansion, Rana maps the architecture of racial capitalism that has long defined the United States. He compellingly argues that to reclaim freedom, the left must build institutions—particularly labor unions—that foster an experience of collective self-rule and interdependent solidarity. However, while Rana’s intersectional analysis deftly weaves together race, class, and empire, it suffers from a glaring omission: it almost entirely ignores the dimension of gender and the patriarchal foundations of American freedom.

Any historical account of how "self-mastery" and "independence" came to define American liberty is incomplete without analyzing how these concepts were intensely gendered. Rana writes that Anglo-European settlers envisioned freedom as "directing the terms of one’s own work and enjoying security from worries regarding financial ruin or dependence." He codes this independent individual as "white," which is true, but he fails to explicitly code him as *male*.

The very concept of the autonomous, self-made man—the yeoman farmer on the frontier or the breadwinner of the mid-20th century—was fundamentally dependent on the subjugation and unpaid labor of women. The economic independence of the male citizen was subsidized not only by the expropriation of Indigenous land and Black labor, as Rana rightly notes, but also by the invisible, uncompensated reproductive and domestic labor of women. The "independence" of the white male settler was a fiction; he was entirely dependent on a patriarchal domestic sphere to sustain him, raise his children, and maintain his property.

When Rana quotes the son of a white steelworker from the 1970s celebrating the "sense of independence and self-mastery his family enjoyed following World War II," he is reproducing a deeply patriarchal framing. That mid-century working-class "liberation," secured through the labor movement and imperial rents, was structurally designed around the family wage—a system that assumed a male breadwinner and a female homemaker. The freedom of the male industrial worker was built on the economic dependence of his wife. By failing to critique this, Rana allows a masculine ideal of freedom to remain the default.

This omission severely limits Rana’s proposed solutions. He argues that the primary battleground for instituting a new, solidaristic freedom is "the American workplace," advocating for the PRO Act and the expansion of unions and strikes. While workplace democracy is undeniably crucial, framing the traditional, formal workplace as the primary site of emancipation marginalizes the vast amount of labor that occurs outside the formal wage economy—labor that is still overwhelmingly performed by women.

If we want to build a truly inclusive and solidaristic freedom, we must redefine what we mean by "the workplace" and what we value as "work." A feminist vision of freedom cannot simply demand that women be integrated into the capitalist wage-labor system on equal terms with men so they too can join a union. It must demand a structural reorganization of how society handles care.

True collective self-rule requires that we socialize the burdens of social reproduction. Universal childcare, paid family and medical leave, and robust support for eldercare are not just "social programs"; they are foundational prerequisites for freedom. Without them, women are forced to constantly negotiate a zero-sum game between economic independence and the demands of care, effectively barring them from the "continuous exercise in participation and collective power" that Rana envisions. A labor movement that focuses solely on the point of production while ignoring the crisis of social reproduction will never achieve universal emancipation.

Furthermore, Rana’s brief mention of the fight for "abortion and reproductive rights in the wake of *Roe*’s overturning" feels tacked onto his broader argument, rather than integrated into his core thesis of what freedom means. Bodily autonomy is not a secondary issue; it is the absolute bedrock of self-mastery and independence. The right-wing project of forced birth is the ultimate expression of the "domination" Rana critiques. It is a direct assertion of state violence to control the bodies and futures of women and pregnant people, reducing them to vessels for state and patriarchal interests.

A genuinely emancipatory left-wing freedom must place bodily autonomy and reproductive justice at its very center. It is impossible to experience "meaningful self-rule in the governance of society" if you do not have self-rule over your own internal organs. The state's claim to ownership over reproductive capacity is conceptually linked to the settler state's claim to ownership over indigenous land—both are acts of violent expropriation to secure the future of the dominant class.

Rana is entirely correct that the left must not abandon the language of freedom to the reactionary right. But to reclaim it effectively, we must broaden our historical lens. We must recognize that the traditional American ideal of liberty was not just white, settler, and capitalist; it was fiercely patriarchal. Rebuilding institutions to foster solidarity means more than just democratizing the factory floor and ending military adventurism. It means dismantling the gendered division of labor, valuing care as the essential infrastructure of society, and fiercely defending bodily autonomy. Only when we strike at the roots of patriarchal dependence can we cultivate a freedom that is truly liberating for all.
