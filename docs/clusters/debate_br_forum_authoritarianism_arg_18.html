"To defeat authoritarianism, Democrats must move beyond the false choice of ideological moderation and instead embrace a robust economic populism that confronts concentrated wealth and corporate power."

The argument over moderation often begins in the wrong place. It asks whether Democrats should be more centrist or more progressive, as if voters were waiting to reward the party for finding the right ideological coordinate. Bonica and Grumbach rightly reject that picture. But the deeper problem is not merely that moderation fails to inspire. It is that the kind of moderation usually recommended to Democrats is a class project. It protects the comfort of donors, consultants, lobbyists, and professional elites while asking working people to accept a politics of small adjustments to an economy that has treated them brutally.

If Democrats want to defeat authoritarianism, they need to understand why authoritarianism has become attractive. It is not only racism, misogyny, Christian nationalism, or disinformation, though all of these matter. It is also the collapse of faith that democratic government can discipline capital and improve ordinary life. For decades, large parts of the country have experienced plant closures, wage stagnation, opioid addiction, hospital consolidation, private equity predation, housing insecurity, debt, and the slow disappearance of local institutions. The Democratic answer has too often been a tax credit, a credential, a pilot program, or a lecture about the future economy. The Republican answer has been scapegoating and domination. One answer is inadequate; the other is dangerous. But danger can beat inadequacy.

That is why anti-corruption is promising, but only if it becomes economic populism with teeth. Corruption should not be defined narrowly as bribery, gifts, and insider trading. Those scandals matter because they are visible signs of a deeper order: government captured by wealth. A billionaire giving luxury vacations to a justice is corrupt. So is a tax code that lets billionaires borrow against appreciating assets while workers pay every paycheck. So is a health care system in which insurers profit by denying care. So is a housing system that lets corporate landlords and speculative capital squeeze tenants. So is a labor law regime that allows employers to violate organizing rights with minimal consequences. If Democrats make anti-corruption only about bad apples, they will miss the orchard.

The lead essay’s critique of popularism is especially important here. Polling can tell a campaign which words voters currently like, but it cannot supply a theory of power. Voters may say they dislike “socialism,” support “cutting waste,” oppose “big government,” and favor “Medicare negotiating drug prices.” These answers are not a coherent program; they are sedimented fragments of decades of political conflict. Leadership means organizing those fragments into a story. The right has done this relentlessly: your life is hard because immigrants, liberals, feminists, bureaucrats, and globalists are taking what is yours. Democrats need an equally clear and more truthful story: your life is hard because concentrated wealth has rigged the rules, and authoritarian politicians are using hate to keep you from noticing who profits.

This story can unite the material and the democratic. Authoritarianism is not only a threat to norms; it is a tool of plunder. When an administration attacks civil servants, intimidates prosecutors, weakens independent agencies, and demands loyalty from law enforcement, it is not simply indulging one man’s ego. It is removing obstacles to impunity. The same movement that rails against “elites” empowers billionaires, crypto speculators, anti-union corporations, polluters, and monopolists. Democracy is the mechanism by which ordinary people can fight back. That should be the message.

Would such a message be “left”? In policy terms, yes. It implies stronger unions, higher taxes on wealth, aggressive antitrust, public investment, universal social benefits, stricter ethics rules, and a government willing to confront corporate power. But politically it need not be a boutique ideology. Many working-class voters who would never call themselves progressive already believe the economy is rigged. They are not waiting for a seminar on democratic socialism. They are waiting for someone who will name the villains they can see: employers who steal wages, hospitals that sue patients, landlords who raise rent, corporations that raise prices while buying back stock, politicians who trade on inside information, and judges who accept billionaire favors.

The difficulty is credibility. Democrats cannot run as tribunes of the working class while appearing to be managed by affluent professionals and financed by the winners of the current economy. The party’s cultural style often communicates distance from the people it claims to represent. Its campaign apparatus is expensive, consultant-heavy, and donor-oriented. Its leaders celebrate unions but still treat working-class candidates as novelties. Its policy language is frequently incomprehensible to anyone without a graduate degree. This is not a problem that can be solved by a better slogan. It requires a different party infrastructure.

That infrastructure should begin with labor. The most effective anti-authoritarian institution in American life is not a think tank, a super PAC, or a viral influencer. It is a union. Unions build cross-racial solidarity, increase political participation, train leaders, and give people a direct experience of collective power. They also create a language of conflict that is neither technocratic nor nihilistic: boss versus worker, solidarity versus exploitation, dignity versus domination. If Democrats are serious about turnout and anti-establishment credibility, they should make labor organizing central to their democracy strategy.

This means more than praising unions at rallies. It means passing labor law reform when power allows, using executive authority to support organizing, putting union members in office, refusing to reward union-busting donors, and treating strikes as major democratic events. It also means learning from the new labor militancy of teachers, nurses, autoworkers, warehouse workers, graduate employees, baristas, and service workers. These campaigns often succeed because they connect workplace issues to community concerns: safe staffing, good schools, reliable services, dignity for caregivers. That is the kind of politics Democrats need.

The authors are right that turnout is central. But people do not turn out merely because a campaign asks. They turn out when they are embedded in networks that make politics meaningful. The decline of unions, local parties, churches, newspapers, and civic associations has left many citizens isolated before the algorithmic rage machine. A presidential ad cannot compensate for the disappearance of year-round organization. Democrats need precinct captains, shop stewards, tenant organizers, immigrant-rights groups, parent networks, and local candidates who remain after the election ends. Mobilization is not a message; it is a social relation.

The moderation debate obscures this because it focuses on the candidate as product. Should the product be more centrist or more progressive? Should the packaging emphasize this issue or that one? But working-class politics is not branding. It is taking sides in conflicts that structure people’s lives. When Democrats refuse to take sides clearly, the right fills the vacuum with cultural enemies. A party that will not confront landlords, insurers, monopolists, and union-busting employers will find itself arguing endlessly about whether it has moved far enough away from activists.

There are risks. Economic populism can be reduced to empty rhetoric. It can ignore race, gender, and immigration. It can romanticize a narrow image of the working class. It can become protectionist nostalgia. Democrats must avoid all of that. A multiracial working-class politics must include care workers, migrants, public employees, gig workers, disabled people, and people criminalized by the state. It must defend civil rights not as side issues but as protections against domination. The boss, the landlord, the abusive police officer, the predatory lender, and the authoritarian president are not identical, but they all exercise power without accountability.

Bonica and Grumbach end by saying Democrats need to fight hard and smart. Fighting smart means recognizing that authoritarianism feeds on despair and status loss, but also that it governs for the powerful. The answer is not a more moderate ideology. It is a more grounded democracy: one that gives people institutions through which they can act, not merely candidates for whom they can vote.

The party should stop asking whether to stand five or fifteen degrees from the center. It should ask whether it is willing to stand with workers against concentrated power. If the answer is no, no amount of message discipline will save it. If the answer is yes, anti-corruption can become more than a frame. It can become the beginning of a democratic reconstruction.
