Diversified Gemini

"The Araki Yasusada hoax serves as a devastating indictment of the contemporary avant-garde's inability to distinguish quality poetry from nonsensical garbage due to its abandonment of objective aesthetic standards."

Marjorie Perloff’s detailed autopsy of the Araki Yasusada affair is fascinating, but she buries the lede. She spends thousands of words parsing the theoretical implications of the "author function" and the sociology of the poetry market, but she misses the most glaring, hilarious, and devastating truth exposed by this hoax: the contemporary avant-garde has completely lost the ability to tell good poetry from absolute garbage.

Perloff lists the "wonderful" clues the hoaxer left behind: the appearance of scuba divers, crematoriums, and capitalist investors in a haiku supposedly written in 1925; the impossible references to Paul Celan and Roland Barthes; the sheer syntactic nonsense of sentences like "It is like the idea of the flowers, opening within the idea of the flowers." She wonders why intelligent editors at *American Poetry Review*, *Conjunctions*, and *Grand Street* didn't spot these anachronisms and absurdities.

The answer is painfully obvious. They didn't spot them because the poetry they normally publish is just as nonsensical, arbitrary, and disconnected from reality.

For decades, the avant-garde establishment has championed deliberate obscurity, dislocation, and the destruction of syntax. They have praised the "New Sentence" and Language poetry, where words are treated as abstract material and meaning is considered a bourgeois relic. If you have spent your entire career pretending to understand and admire the impenetrable word-salad of John Ashbery, or the tedious, disjunctive prose-blocks of Ron Silliman, your critical faculties are going to atrophy. When a manuscript arrives featuring "ecstatic-like" severed heads and galaxies spreading like a "cloud of sperm," you don't evaluate it for coherence, historical accuracy, or basic aesthetic merit. You just look for the requisite markers of "edginess."

Kent Johnson simply fed the machine what it is programmed to eat: fragmented syntax, unearned irony, and a veneer of unassailable trauma. The editors swallowed it whole because they have abandoned any objective standard of judgment. If they admit that Yasusada is a fraud and his poems are a joke, they might have to look at the rest of the magazines they edit and ask themselves how much of it is equally fraudulent. The Yasusada hoax is not just a commentary on Orientalism; it is the ultimate proof that the Emperor of postmodern poetry has no clothes.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Editors failed to notice glaring historical anachronisms and absurdities because the poetry they typically publish is equally arbitrary and disconnected from reality.

  3. 03The hoaxer successfully manipulated the literary establishment by providing the specific markers of 'edginess' and 'trauma' that the system is programmed to reward.
  4. 04The uncritical acceptance of the Yasusada manuscript suggests that much of the postmodern poetry currently being published is fundamentally fraudulent.
