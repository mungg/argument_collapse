"While Acemoglu’s diagnosis of AI’s risks is accurate, his proposed solution of centralized government direction and democratic oversight is impractical due to the diffuse nature of AI, the erosion of state capacity, and the difficulty of defining 'good' versus 'bad' technology."

Acemoglu's diagnosis of AI's trajectory is compelling, but his prescription requires scrutiny. While I share his concern about automation's direction, the three-pronged approach he proposes—government policy, shifting norms, and democratic oversight—rests on assumptions about state capacity that seem increasingly fragile. The climate policy analogy, though intuitive, breaks down under careful comparison.

In energy, governments could point to measurable harms and build consensus around solutions. Carbon emissions leave calculable damage. AI's harms are more diffuse, harder to attribute, and often invisible until damage is done. When does an algorithm discriminate? When does surveillance chill speech? These causal chains are murky in ways carbon emissions are not.

More fundamentally, the climate example succeeded because governments controlled the domain—energy production happened within national boundaries with identifiable actors. AI research sprawls across borders, moves at startup speed, and involves actors from hedge funds to university labs to intelligence agencies. Which government regulates the open-source model trained on distributed data across five continents?

Acemoglu rightly notes that democratic backsliding complicates his solution. But he understates the irony: we're hoping democracies will act collectively to restrain technology while those same democracies are fracturing. In the U.S., the filibuster paralyzes legislation; in Europe, nationalist parties weaponize technophobia; in China, the government actively opposes his preferred direction. Democratic coordination requires functioning democracies.

The essay excels at diagnosing why markets fail here. Externalities, competing paradigms, and norm-driven research all get appropriate attention. But when he proposes government measurement frameworks to distinguish "good" AI from "bad," I wonder which government, operating through which procedures, with what enforcement mechanisms, will accomplish this feat. The OECD AI Principles exist. They haven't redirected research.

What Acemoglu calls for resembles industrial policy, which has a spotty historical record. South Korea's semiconductor push succeeded; Brazil's computer industry initiative flopped. The difference often comes down to whether the state had precise information about what it wanted and whether it could credibly commit to a long-term path. AI's direction remains genuinely uncertain—even Acemoglu acknowledges we don't know which path will generate more human-complementary technologies.

A more defensible strategy emphasizes flexibility and reversibility. Rather than directing research toward predefined "good" applications, policy should focus on ensuring AI systems are explainable, auditable, and contestable. This limits harms without requiring perfect foresight about which applications will prove most valuable.

I worry that Acemoglu's framing, despite its sophistication, risks producing a kind of policy paralysis. If we need government leadership, democratic oversight, and norm shifts simultaneously, and if failure in any dimension dooms the enterprise, the probability of success may be low. An approach requiring less simultaneous coordination—emphasizing institutional safeguards and market mechanisms within clear constraints—might prove more robust.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The analogy to climate policy fails because AI's harms are more diffuse and harder to measure than carbon emissions.
  2. 02AI research is too global and decentralized for individual national governments to effectively regulate or direct through traditional industrial policy.
  3. 03The current state of democratic backsliding and political polarization makes the collective action required for Acemoglu’s framework nearly impossible to achieve.
  4. 04Government measurement frameworks designed to distinguish between human-complementary and labor-displacing AI are likely to fail due to the inherent uncertainty of technological development.
  5. 05A more effective strategy would focus on institutional safeguards like explainability and auditability rather than trying to pre-determine the direction of research.
  6. 06Acemoglu’s requirement for simultaneous success in policy, norms, and oversight creates a high risk of policy paralysis.
