"The shift from open PCs to curated appliances like the iPhone is a positive maturation of technology that reflects legitimate consumer preferences for reliability over the burden of system administration."

The iPhone is not the enemy. It never was. Reading Jonathan Zittrain's essay, one gets the impression that the world is sliding inexorably from open, generative PCs toward locked-down information appliances, and that this shift represents some sort of categorical loss for humanity. I want to push back on this framing. The iPhone is not the death of generativity—it's a different instantiation of it, one that reflects legitimate consumer preferences that the early PC era never addressed.

Consider what users actually wanted from personal computing. The Apple II was a blank slate precisely because no one had yet figured out what people might want to do with computers. By 2008, we know better. Most users do not want to program their phones. They want them to work. Zittrain mourns the loss of the "test bed and distribution point" for new software, but this function has not disappeared—it has migrated. Firefox, Apache, MySQL, and thousands of other open-source projects are developed not on desktops but on servers. The generative energy that once flowed through the desktop now flows through the cloud.

This is not a loss. It's a maturation. Zittrain correctly identifies that the three historical controls on bad code have weakened—the hacker ethos has faded, professional administration has given way to consumer broadband, and malware has become profitable. But his response is to try to shore up the old model rather than adapt to the new one. He proposes virtual machines, community-based code assessment, and data portability requirements. These are interesting ideas, but they share a common flaw: they assume that the average user cares enough about generativity to invest time in maintaining it. They don't.

What users want is reliable computing. The iPhone delivers this by design. When a user downloads an application from the App Store, Apple has vetted it. This is not paternalism for its own sake—it's a response to genuine demand. Zittrain objects that "you don't want your phone to be like a PC," as if Jobs were warning against generativity. But Jobs was simply describing what customers wanted: a device that functions when you need it to. This is a reasonable preference, not a failure of imagination.

The essay's central error is treating openness as an intrinsic good rather than an instrumental one. Openness matters because it enables innovation, competition, and user control. When openness becomes a vector for malware, when the freedom to install any code means the freedom to be compromised, the instrumental value of openness diminishes. Users who are not programmers—and most are not—get no benefit from the ability to tinker with their phones. They get only risk.

Zittrain's proposal for "appliance neutrality" is his most puzzling idea. He suggests that companies like Microsoft should be required to maintain a "level playing field" for third-party software on platforms like the Xbox, invoking the antitrust precedent from the 1990s. But this analogy fails. The Microsoft case was about a platform that had achieved dominance partly by leveraging a monopoly in operating systems to disadvantage competitors in adjacent markets. The Xbox faces competition from Nintendo and Sony. No one is forced to buy an Xbox. No one is locked into Windows the way corporations were locked into Office file formats. The market is working.

What would be lost if every device were an iPhone? Not innovation, certainly. Apple has shipped more innovation in the past five years than the open PC ecosystem managed in a decade. The App Store model has generated more applications than any previous platform. Google Android has matched this with an open-source model that allows tinkering while still providing curated application distribution. These are generative systems—they've just learned to be generative in ways that don't require users to become system administrators.

The essay's closing vision of "netizens" using their PCs to protect the generative Internet is touching but unrealistic. Most users are not netizens. They are consumers who want their devices to work. Serving them well means designing systems that are reliable by default, not systems that require constant vigilance. The iPhone is such a system. That's not a tragedy. It's progress.

The real challenge is not preserving the generative PC at all costs. It's ensuring that innovation continues to happen somewhere in the ecosystem, and that users retain meaningful choices about how their devices function. The market is delivering this. We should let it.
