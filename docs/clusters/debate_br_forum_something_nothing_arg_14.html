Diversified Claude

"While Nir Rosen correctly identifies the failures of military-led counterinsurgency (COIN), his claim that aid has had no impact is overly broad and ignores significant developmental gains achieved by Afghan institutions and civilian programs."

Reading Nir Rosen's essay alongside the daily traffic of NGO reports from Afghanistan produces a peculiar double vision. He sees the war from the perspective of the mentor team and the rifle squad. We see it from the perspective of the community health worker, the road-builder, the school administrator, the irrigation engineer. The two pictures are not contradictory, but they emphasize different things, and I want to push back on one of Rosen's more sweeping claims — that aid in a COIN context has had essentially no impact and that the strategy has "raised greater expectations than it has satisfied."

The second half of that sentence is true. The first half is too sweeping.

Begin with what the development picture actually looks like in 2009. Afghanistan in 2001 had a school enrollment of under a million, virtually all boys. It now has somewhere between six and seven million children in school, including approximately two million girls. Infant and maternal mortality, while still catastrophic by any global standard, have measurably improved. Roughly 80 percent of the population now has some access to basic health services, up from less than 10 percent under the Taliban. Mobile phone penetration, almost zero in 2001, is now estimated at over a third of the adult population. Microfinance institutions have disbursed loans to nearly half a million Afghans. The Ring Road, while incomplete, has dramatically reduced travel times between major cities.

None of this is the work of the McChrystal surge, and most of it is not the result of COIN doctrine. Much of it is the work of the Afghan ministries themselves — the Ministry of Public Health, the Ministry of Rural Rehabilitation and Development — supported by sustained donor commitments under the National Solidarity Programme and similar mechanisms. The NSP in particular, which channels block grants directly to elected community development councils in tens of thousands of villages, has had a real and documented effect on local infrastructure and, more importantly, on local governance practice.

Rosen is right that the aggregate aid figures, set against per-capita comparisons with Bosnia or East Timor or Kosovo, are pathetic. Afghanistan has received roughly an order of magnitude less per capita than those reconstruction efforts. He is right that the aid is wildly badly distributed, with the most insecure provinces receiving the most attention — a "punishment" of stable areas that perversely incentivizes instability. He is right that an enormous share of the nominal aid budget vanishes into contractor overhead, security costs, and the so-called "beltway bandits" of Washington consulting firms. He is right that the COIN-era enthusiasm for aid-as-counterinsurgency tool — buy off the village, build the well, win the population — is naive about how Afghan politics actually work and how rumors travel. The notion that handing out a few thousand dollars in CERP funds turns a village pro-government is precisely the magical thinking he indicts.

But to leap from this to "aid has had no impact" is to mistake the shortcomings of the COIN-instrumentalized version of aid for the failure of the broader development project. There are real things that work. The NSP works, imperfectly and at small scale, because it actually devolves authority to communities. Basic Package of Health Services contracting works, imperfectly, because it pays NGOs for measurable outcomes. Community-based education works, in places, because it brings school to children who cannot walk to a district center. The vaccination campaigns work. The de-mining works. The microhydro projects work.

What does not work is the assumption — at the heart of the McChrystal plan and the broader COIN apparatus — that development is a counterinsurgency input rather than an end in itself. When development is yoked to security objectives, it goes where security objectives say it should go, which is to say, the most violent districts, where it is also least likely to succeed. The PRT model, where Provincial Reconstruction Teams run by military officers undertake "development" projects as an arm of the campaign, has produced years of poorly conceived, poorly maintained showpiece projects that bypass the Afghan state and undermine the very ministries we claim to be strengthening. Rosen could have made this argument harder and more usefully than he does.

There is a deeper irony in Rosen's framing. He criticizes COIN for asking soldiers to be "Wyatt Earp and Mother Teresa." He is right that this is an absurd ask of an eighteen-year-old infantryman. But the answer is not to abandon the Mother Teresa half. It is to recognize that the Mother Teresa half is not the soldier's job at all, and never should have been. Development, health, education, governance reform — these are jobs for Afghan institutions, supported by civilian agencies with appropriate expertise. The hyper-militarization of the entire reconstruction project, in which USAID has become a junior partner to CENTCOM and the State Department's civilian surge has been counted in the low hundreds, is one of the great policy failures of the past eight years. Rosen is so focused on the inadequacy of the military's COIN performance that he never quite arrives at the point that COIN as currently practiced has cannibalized everything else.

The Afghan story since 2001 is not the simple story of failure that Rosen's essay sketches. It is a more complicated story in which an extraordinary outpouring of Afghan effort, especially by Afghan women, civil servants, teachers, and health workers, has produced real if fragile gains; in which the international system has been a tremendously inefficient and often counterproductive partner; in which the war has both made some of these gains possible and consistently threatened to unwind them; and in which the present strategic course threatens to subordinate everything that has been built to a security campaign whose own assumptions are increasingly untenable.

What worries me most about the McChrystal plan, and about the response it has provoked from critics like Rosen, is that both sides are talking about Afghanistan as if it were primarily a military problem with secondary political and developmental dimensions. The Afghans I know — and Afghans are the people whose opinion in the end matters most — generally describe their situation in opposite terms. They want security, yes, urgently. But they want it because security is the precondition for the schools, clinics, courts, jobs, and basic dignities that the war has put perpetually out of reach. A debate that argues only about whether 40,000 more troops can produce security, without engaging the question of what security is for, is a debate that has lost the plot.

Rosen's essay is a useful corrective to military triumphalism. It is not, despite itself, a serious engagement with what comes next.
