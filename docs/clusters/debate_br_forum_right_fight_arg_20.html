Diversified Claude

"Richman’s federalist model for domestic intelligence fails to account for the severe human costs borne by Arab and Muslim Americans and overestimates the ability of local police involvement to provide meaningful accountability."

I want to take Richman's essay seriously on its own terms — as a thoughtful piece of administrative-law and federalism scholarship — and then push back on one of its most important framing choices: the near-invisibility, in the essay, of the people on whom the costs of all this fall. Richman writes movingly about how the federal government distributes homeland security dollars, about whether JTTFs should be expanded, about whether mayors get a fair shake compared to governors. He writes far less about what it is like to be an Arab American or Muslim American in the United States in 2004, and how the policy choices he describes look from inside that experience.

The numbers Richman cites in passing are stark. 3,212 interviews of mostly Middle Eastern men in the months after 9/11, 330 in Detroit alone. The "absconder" initiative. NSEERS registration that has, by various estimates, resulted in over 13,000 men placed in removal proceedings without a single terrorism conviction to show for it. The disappearances into the immigration detention system documented in the Justice Department Inspector General's 2003 report — beatings at MDC Brooklyn, communications blackouts, blanket "hold until cleared" policies. The voluntary-but-not-really interviews conducted by federal agents and, in many cities, by local officers as well. From inside the community on the receiving end of these practices, the question of whether the cop conducting the interview wears a federal or local badge is a second-order concern. The first-order concern is that one's neighbors are being interrogated, deported, or detained on the basis of national origin.

Richman's argument that local police involvement will make the system more accountable rests on a particular theory of representation: cities have mayors, mayors have constituents, immigrant communities are constituents, therefore mayoral pressure will moderate the system. This argument has more force in some places than others. In Dearborn, perhaps. In a small Tennessee town with a recently arrived Somali population, considerably less. In a city like Las Vegas, where Muslim residents are dispersed and not politically organized, very little. The accountability mechanism Richman invokes works only where the affected community is concentrated, organized, and electorally significant. For most American Muslims, in most American places, it is not.

Worse, the very fact that local police are dependent on immigrant cooperation for ordinary crime-fighting — the source of Richman's optimism — creates a structural temptation to instrumentalize that cooperation in dangerous ways. Once a community member is known to the local police, they can be approached, leaned on, threatened with immigration consequences, asked to inform on their mosque. The "balanced portfolio" Richman praises can become the basis for a sophisticated coercion infrastructure that no civilian oversight board will ever see. We are already seeing reports — anecdotal, but accumulating — of FBI and JTTF use of immigration status as leverage in informant recruitment. Local police involvement makes this easier, not harder.

There is also the question of what gets defined as "intelligence." Richman repeatedly treats intelligence-gathering as a technical problem of channels and incentives. But every act of intelligence-gathering involves a prior decision about who is suspicious. The Justice Department decided after 9/11 that young men from a list of mostly Muslim countries were categorically of interest. That decision was not made by local cops; it was made in Washington and transmitted downward. Local participation does not change that targeting decision. It just diffuses the labor of executing it. And it imports the targeting decision into the texture of community life, where every interaction with a beat cop becomes haunted by the possibility that it is also an intelligence interaction.

What Richman calls a "modus vivendi" between local police and federal authorities looks, from the community side, like a coordination of pressures rather than a balancing of them. The Portland City Attorney's refusal to allow Portland police to participate in some of the post-9/11 interviews — which Richman treats somewhat ambivalently as part of the "scattered resistance" — is, in my view, a better model of accountability than the JTTF model he ultimately endorses. It is accountability that bites: it actually changes what happens to people on the ground. Resolutions that "do nothing" — Richman is dismissive of them — at least signal a public political position that creates space for refusal. The patient cultivation of local police voice within federal task forces, by contrast, has produced few visible changes in federal practice. The Justice Department has not noticeably softened any of its core post-9/11 enforcement initiatives in response to police-chief grumbling.

If we are serious about accountability — accountability as a substantive constraint on what the government does to people, rather than as a procedural feeling about how the government talks to itself — we should be more interested in the things Richman dismisses. Sanctuary policies that bar local police from civil immigration enforcement. Statutory restrictions like Oregon's, which prohibits gathering information on political and religious belief absent a criminal predicate. Litigation, including the post-9/11 detainee class actions now winding through federal court. Independent monitors and inspectors general with real subpoena power. Federal civil rights enforcement against state and local agencies that abuse counterterrorism authority. These are the mechanisms that have, historically, moved the needle for vulnerable communities, and they sit largely outside Richman's frame.

I do not disagree with everything in Richman's essay. He is surely right that channeling all homeland security money through governors marginalizes cities, and right that the COPS phaseout creates resentments that play out in the counterterrorism arena. He is right that local police know things federal agents do not. But the deep question for a domestic intelligence policy in a constitutional democracy is not how to optimize information flow up the federalist chain. It is how to limit the harms that flow back down. On that question, Richman's essay is more reassuring than the situation warrants.

  2. 02Local police involvement creates a 'coercion infrastructure' where officers can instrumentalize their community relationships to pressure vulnerable residents into becoming informants.

  5. 05The primary goal of domestic intelligence policy should be limiting the harms inflicted on vulnerable populations rather than optimizing the flow of information between levels of government.
