"The Democratic Party's recent economic shift is driven by a fundamental philosophical move from a neoliberal distributive model to a 'constitutive' theory of freedom that views public goods and state institutions as the essential foundation of individual liberty."

Hacker and Pierson’s lead essay is a formidable piece of political analysis that revises our understanding of the contemporary Democratic Party. They are right to push back against the cultural-reductionist narrative and draw our attention to a genuinely ambitious economic agenda. However, their explanatory framework—a story of elite management of a cross-class, multiracial coalition—remains too grounded in a politics of interest distribution. It misses a more fundamental philosophical shift that gives the new Democratic economic agenda its internal coherence: the move from a distributive to a constitutive theory of freedom. The Democrats’ U-turn is not just a matter of taxing and spending more; it is a quiet but profound break with the neoliberal conception of the autonomous individual, in favor of a view that sees freedom as constituted by the public goods, institutions, and forms of security that a well-ordered state provides. This shift transforms economic policy from a mechanistic transfer of resources into a project of building a shared democratic infrastructure, and it is this shift that holds the fragile coalition together.

Hacker and Pierson describe the Democrats’ agenda as “redistribution through public taxes and transfers” joined with “predistribution” through public investment. This is an accurate accounting, but it still frames the state’s role in market-corrective terms, as a force acting upon a pre-existing market order. The philosophical shift at the heart of the new progressivism is more radical. It denies the very existence of a “market” separate from the state. It argues that a market is always a creature of law, a structured set of relationships that reflects political choices about power. Monopoly power is not a market failure; it is a policy success for the monopolist, enabled by the retreat of public authority. Climate change is not an externality; it is the brutal outcome of a legal and regulatory regime that forces the public to subsidize carbon-intensive capital. The care economy’s crisis is not an accident of shifting family structures; it is the direct result of a political decision to organize society around an unexamined norm of unpaid, female-provided domestic labor.

From this constitutive perspective, the Biden administration’s economic agenda is not a grab bag of spending programs. It is a series of structural interventions in the constitution of American life. Antitrust policy under Lina Khan is not technical regulation; it is an argument that the organization of economic power is a question of democratic self-government. Public investment in childcare and home-based elder care is not just a new benefit; it is a declaration that the state has a foundational role in structuring the activity we call “care” so that it is no longer a source of gender subordination and racial extraction. Industrial policy, with its labor standards and domestic content requirements, is a conscious effort to reconstitute the relationship between capital, labor, and the nation-state in the collective undertaking of the green transition.

This governing philosophy is what allows the U-shaped coalition to cohere. It is not simply that affluent suburbanites and working-class people of color happen to have a few discrete, overlapping policy preferences. It is that this framework of constitutive freedom offers a compelling account of the predicaments they both face, however different their material circumstances. The professional with a masters degree and six figures of student debt feels unfree, trapped in a log treadmill of credentialization and high housing costs. The home health aide making poverty wages feels unfree in a more brutal, immediate sense. The constitutive view tells them that their unfreedom has a common source: a forty-year political project that dismantled public housing, defunded public higher education, left care work to the market’s cruelty, and allowed private equity to cannibalize the productive economy. The solution, in turn, is not a series of individualized tax credits. It is the collective re-building of a public infrastructure—of care, of housing, of education, of clean energy—that constitutes the freedom of all. The child tax credit is not the whole story; it is a down payment on the idea that the state has a constitutive obligation to the next generation, not just a distributive check to write.

Hacker and Pierson are right to see that the Democrats’ coalition strategy organized certain thorny issues “out” of politics. But what they organized “in” was more than a pocketbook. It was the outline of a new social contract. The fragility of the moment is not just that Manchin stopped a bill, but that this philosophy has not yet been fully articulated and fought for in public. It remains a code embedded in policy blueprints, not a political language spoken fluently by the party’s leaders. The great risk is not that the coalition will fracture over taxes. It is that it will atrophy for lack of a public philosophy that can make sense of its shared predicament. The 2021 agenda was a glimpse of a different kind of political economy, one where freedom is not left over after the market has taken its cut, but is built, collectively, in the institutions and relationships that make a decent life possible. The work ahead is not just to pass the policies, but to win the argument that freedom is not free—it is public.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The new Democratic agenda rejects the idea of the market as a pre-existing entity, instead viewing it as a legal and political construct that must be structured to serve democratic ends.
  2. 02Policy initiatives like antitrust enforcement and the 'care economy' are not merely resource transfers but structural interventions designed to reclaim economic power for democratic self-government.
  3. 03The 'constitutive' framework provides the glue for a cross-class coalition by framing the diverse struggles of both affluent professionals and the working class as a shared loss of freedom caused by the dismantling of public infrastructure.
  4. 04The primary threat to the Democratic coalition is not tax policy or internal interest conflicts, but the failure of party leaders to explicitly articulate this new public philosophy as a coherent alternative to neoliberalism.
