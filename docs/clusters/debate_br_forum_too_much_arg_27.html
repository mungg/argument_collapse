"Nancy Hirschmann’s critique of stay-at-home motherhood is fundamentally illiberal because it instrumentalizes women as tools for social engineering rather than respecting their individual autonomy to choose their own path."

Nancy Hirschmann writes from within the feminist tradition, but her essay could easily have been written by a commentator from the cultural right. The argument that mothers who stay home are failing to contribute to the fight for equality, that women who care for their children are enabling patriarchy, that individual choices must be evaluated by their collective consequences—these claims sound remarkably like the attacks on feminism from conservative commentators who have long argued that women should be in the home.

I am not suggesting that Hirschmann is a conservative in disguise. She is clearly a committed feminist with a sophisticated understanding of gender politics. But her framework—a framework that measures women's choices by their contribution to a collective feminist project—is one that has been used by authoritarians throughout history to control women's lives. The question is not whether care has value or whether men should share in it; it is whether we have the right to dictate to women how they should live.

This brings me to the central problem with Hirschmann's essay: it treats mothers as means rather than ends. Care feminism, in her telling, is supposed to produce certain outcomes—civic-minded children, gender equality, social change. If it fails to produce these outcomes, then mothers who embody it are doing something wrong. But this instrumentalizes motherhood in a way that should disturb anyone who believes that women are full human beings entitled to live their lives as they see fit.

A mother who chooses to stay home with her children may be making that choice for many reasons: because she wants to, because her children need her, because her husband earns enough that her income is not essential. Whatever the reason, it is her choice to make, not a choice to be evaluated by feminist theorists. If her children grow up to be entitled and selfish, that is a personal failure, not a political one. And if her children grow up to be good citizens, she deserves credit for that achievement, not the suspicion that she is rationalizing patriarchal norms.

Hirschmann's treatment of the welfare issue is particularly troubling. She notes that if care is a public good, then the public has a right to oversee how mothers perform that work. She acknowledges that this logic "was precisely the logic behind hostility to welfare in the 1990s" and that "those arguments were oversimplified and distorted." But she does not follow this observation to its conclusion. The welfare system's hostility to poor mothers—its surveillance, its mandates, its punishments—was not a distorted version of care feminism; it was the logical result of treating motherhood as a public service subject to public accountability.

If we accept Hirschmann's framework, we are accepting that motherhood is a service provided to the state, and that the state has the right to evaluate whether that service is performed adequately. This is a deeply illiberal conclusion, one that should give pause to anyone who cares about women's autonomy.

I am not arguing that feminism should abandon all interest in how children are raised or how families are structured. Public policy affects families, and public policy should support families. But the goal of public policy should be to give families more choices, not fewer; to enable mothers to live the lives they want, not to dictate how they should live.

Hirschmann concludes that "women's power, not care itself, will enable gender equality." Perhaps. But power for what purpose? If women's power means the power to demand that other women conform to feminist theory, then we have traded one form of oppression for another. If women's power means the power to live as they choose, then we are back where liberalism started, with the simple claim that people—women included—should be free to pursue their own conceptions of the good life.

That claim is not enough, some will say, because individual freedom does not guarantee equality. True. But neither does collective feminist theory, as thirty years of care feminism has demonstrated. The answer is not to sacrifice individual freedom on the altar of collective progress but to insist on both—and to recognize that they are ultimately the same thing.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Evaluating a mother's personal choices based on their contribution to a collective feminist project mirrors the controlling logic used by authoritarian regimes and the cultural right.
  2. 02Treating motherhood as a public service subject to state oversight and accountability justifies the same harmful surveillance and punishment historically used against welfare recipients.
  3. 03The essay wrongly treats mothers as means to an end—such as producing civic-minded children—rather than as full human beings entitled to live for their own purposes.
  4. 04True women's power should be defined as the freedom to pursue one's own conception of a good life, rather than the power to demand that other women conform to a specific feminist theory.
  5. 05While individual freedom does not guarantee equality, sacrificing that freedom for the sake of collective progress merely replaces patriarchal oppression with a different form of ideological coercion.
