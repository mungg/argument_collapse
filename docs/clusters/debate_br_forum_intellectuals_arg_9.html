"The responsibility of intellectuals in the age of fascism is not a matter of individual conscience or freelance truth-telling, but rather a commitment to joining and submitting to the discipline of organized political movements."

Robin D. G. Kelley's essay is the most serious attempt I have read in some time to rewrite the question Chomsky posed in 1967 for a moment in which the postwar liberal consensus has collapsed and a domestic fascist movement actually holds the executive branch. He is right that "speak truth, expose lies" is insufficient when the regime is not embarrassed by lies but powered by them. He is right to recover the Black antifascist tradition—Drake and Cayton, Patterson, Jones, Robeson, Du Bois—from the patriotic amnesia that turned World War II into a story of American virtue. And he is right that Project Esther, Canary Mission, and the ADL's collaboration with state and donor power have made the campus a frontline rather than a refuge.

And yet I want to push the argument harder than Kelley does, because I think the framework of "the responsibility of intellectuals"—even reworked through Gramsci—still concedes too much to the very intellectual self-importance it tries to puncture. The deeper problem with Chomsky's original formulation is not that it underestimated fascism. It is that it kept "intellectuals" as the privileged unit of analysis at all. Kelley's substitution of the organic intellectual for the freelance truth-teller is an improvement, but it preserves the assumption that the most interesting political question of our moment is what people who write and teach for a living should do. The most interesting political question of our moment is what working people, debtors, tenants, undocumented neighbors, and rank-and-file union members are doing—and whether intellectuals are useful to them.

Kelley knows this. The most moving passages in his essay are not about scholars choosing sides but about Tiffany Willoughby-Herard cuffed on the pavement at UC Irvine, asking what job she has if her students have no future. That sentence redirects the question entirely. The responsibility of intellectuals is not, in the end, an intellectual question. It is a question of what political organizations the writer is accountable to, and what they will ask of her when the costs come due. Almost every honorable example Kelley adduces—Drake driving a car through a fascist parade in New Orleans, Patterson trying to file *We Charge Genocide* with the UN, the Columbia students who shut down the Nazi ambassador in 1933—is an example of an intellectual whose action was structured by an organization, a discipline of solidarity, a movement that had a use for her and would discipline her if she failed it. Chomsky, by contrast, wrote *as* an intellectual to *other* intellectuals. He addressed conscience. He did not ask, "to which fighting organization are you accountable?"

This matters because conscience without organization is, in our moment, almost cost-free and almost useless. Anyone with a Substack or a Bluesky account can "speak truth to power" in a way Macdonald could not have imagined; the proliferation of dissent has not slowed the regime. What has slowed it, where it has been slowed, is the union local that refused to handle ICE detainees' equipment, the dockworkers' refusal to load arms for Israel, the encampments that disrupted commencements, the tenant organizations that bird-dogged landlords through eviction court, the autoworkers who struck for the right to strike against war. Each of these required organized people who could absorb risk together. None required intellectuals as a category, though they often used them.

If we accept this, then Kelley's conclusion—plug into movements, unionize, build a "new university"—is exactly right but understated. The "new university" cannot be conjured by professors deciding to act differently. It will be built, if it is built at all, by the same forces that built the old labor movement: graduate workers, contingent faculty, custodians, food service workers, and undergraduates who decide that the university's labor relations are themselves a site of antifascist struggle. The wave of graduate worker unionization since 2017, the strikes at the University of California in 2022, the campus workers who refused to scab on the Palestine encampments—these are the real material basis for whatever resistance the university can offer. Tenured professors who chose courageously last spring did so largely because the contingent and the students had already chosen, and because unions made it possible to choose without immediate annihilation.

Kelley nods at this when he writes that "unionizing all faculty and teaching staff is not just about salaries and teaching loads." But I would put the point more sharply: the labor question is not a precondition for the political question, it *is* the political question on campus. Every administrative decision Kelley catalogs—Harvard quietly suspending its Birzeit partnership, Columbia ousting Katherine Franke, UCLA letting counter-protesters beat encampment students for hours before police arrived—was made possible by the structural defenselessness of academic labor. Adjunctification did not just immiserate the professoriate; it built the disciplinary apparatus that the Trump administration is now wielding. Project Esther did not invent the at-will employment of the contingent academic. It merely exploited it.

This is also why I think Kelley is a little too kind to the figure of the "traditional" administrator-intellectual. He writes that "university administrators, most having risen from the ranks of the faculty, are also intellectuals; as such, they too have a moral responsibility and 'choices.'" Do they? The modern university president is not a scholar who has wandered into management; she is a fundraiser who briefly was a scholar, selected by trustees precisely for her willingness to subordinate the academic mission to the capital structure of the institution. To address her in the language of intellectual conscience is a category error. She is not a wayward Gramscian deliberating which side to take. She is a manager executing the interests of the bondholders, the donors, and now the federal regulators who control her grant pipeline. Asking her to be brave is like asking a CFO to abandon fiduciary duty. The only force that has ever made such managers behave better is countervailing organized power from below. Moral suasion has, to my knowledge, never moved a university board.

Kelley's invocation of the Black radical antifascist tradition deepens this point in a way he doesn't quite spell out. Patterson, Jones, Robeson, Du Bois—the figures he names with such respect—were not freelance consciences. They were Communist Party cadres or aligned with the Party at decisive moments. *We Charge Genocide* was not the work of a brilliant individual; it was the work of the Civil Rights Congress, a disciplined organization with international links. The price they paid—passport revocation, deportation, prison—was extracted from them precisely because they were organized, because their speech was backed by collective action that the state feared. Their example does not generalize to the lone tenured professor "choosing a side." It generalizes to the question of which side has institutions one can actually join.

I have one further amendment to suggest. Kelley argues, following Stanley, that "when we pit 'intellectuals' or the university against the Trump regime, as if 'reason' or the lack thereof is the primary antagonism, we commit a grave error." Yes—and yet the inverse error tempts the left as well. There is a tendency in our discourse to treat "truth" as an exhausted weapon, to say with a shrug that the right makes things up and we cannot win on facts, so we must fight on the terrain of will, story, and bloc-building. This is true as far as it goes. But the antifascist tradition Kelley honors did not abandon facts. *We Charge Genocide* was a 240-page documentary indictment. *Black Metropolis* was a methodologically rigorous sociology. Du Bois's *Black Reconstruction* rewrote a historiography. The reason these works mattered politically is that they were undeniable, and the reason they remain useful weapons is the same. The right can lie freely, but our side must refuse to. Not because lying is bad in the abstract—though it is—but because the only durable solidarity is one that withstands honest accounting.

So I read Kelley's essay as making two arguments that are not quite the same. One says: truth-telling is no longer enough; we must organize. With this I agree completely. The other implies, more loosely, that truth-telling has been so devalued that it has become a kind of liberal indulgence. With this I would be more careful. The conditions that have made truth-telling feel impotent are themselves political conditions—the destruction of local journalism, the algorithmic capture of public discourse, the gutting of public broadcasting, the donor capture of universities—and a serious antifascist program must contest each of them. Rebuilding the institutional conditions under which truth can be heard is not separate from movement work; it is movement work.

What does this mean concretely? It means that academic unions should treat protection of speech and research as central bargaining demands, not afterthoughts. It means alliances between faculty unions and the staff unions that overlap with them—the SEIU locals representing custodians, the CWA chapters at NewsGuild outlets, the writers' unions at academic presses. It means refusing the false choice between "the university is finished, abandon it" and "defend the university as it is." Both are pretexts for paralysis. The actual task is to wrest the university from the trustees, donors, and federal extortionists who currently own it, the way earlier generations wrested public schools from segregationist boards and Catholic hospitals from misogynist orders. This is not a metaphor. It is the only history we have to draw on.

Kelley ends with Willoughby-Herard's question: what job do I have if the students don't have a future? It is the right question, and it is also a labor question. Her job, in the most literal sense, was protected by the strength of her union, by the tenure she had earned, and by the colleagues who would defend her if she were retaliated against. The structural conditions of that defense did not exist for the contingent lecturers arrested alongside her, who lost their positions without recourse. If we are serious about generalizing her example, we have to build the conditions under which more people can act as she did without losing everything. That is not, principally, a task of conscience. It is a task of organization.

The responsibility of intellectuals in the age of fascism and genocide, then, is not first to speak the truth, and not first to choose a side. It is to join, and submit to the discipline of, the organizations through which the people on the losing side of this regime might still win. Everything else—the writing, the teaching, the analysis—follows from that subordination, or it does not amount to much.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Focusing on 'intellectuals' as a privileged unit of analysis is a mistake because the most vital political questions are answered by the actions of working people, tenants, and rank-and-file union members.
  2. 02Conscience without organization is cost-free and ineffective in a post-truth era where dissent is easily ignored by the state.
  3. 03The struggle for the university is fundamentally a labor question, as the structural defenselessness of academic labor through adjunctification is what enables administrative and state repression.
  4. 04University administrators should be viewed as managers executing the interests of capital and bondholders rather than as wayward intellectuals who can be moved by moral suasion.
  5. 05The historical figures of the Black radical antifascist tradition were effective because their intellectual work was backed by disciplined organizations like the Communist Party and the Civil Rights Congress.
  6. 06While truth-telling alone is insufficient, the movement must still prioritize factual rigor and rebuild the institutional conditions that allow truth to be heard as a form of resistance.
