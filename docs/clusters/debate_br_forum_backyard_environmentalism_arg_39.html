"The proposed 'rolling-rule regime' of decentralized, performance-based regulation is a dangerous academic repackaging of corporate capture that threatens to dismantle the proven protections of centralized command-and-control environmental laws."

Archon Fung, Charles Sabel, and Bradley Karkkainen have crafted a highly optimistic, undeniably seductive vision of a new environmental era. Who could possibly oppose a regulatory regime that promises to transcend the bitter adversarial battles of the past three decades while simultaneously deepening democratic participation and improving environmental outcomes? Unfortunately, from the vantage point of those of us who spend our days fighting to enforce the nation’s environmental laws, this “rolling-rule regime” looks dangerously like an academic repackaging of a much older, less noble phenomenon: corporate capture of the regulatory process.

The authors claim that we are moving “beyond backyard environmentalism” toward a system where citizens and polluting industries collaborate to establish acceptable environmental targets. But in celebrating this shift, they fundamentally misdiagnose why the first generation of backyard environmentalism—and the centralized command-and-control system it birthed—was necessary in the first place. The original environmental movement did not turn to centralized national standards because of a naive belief in the “modest omniscience” of regulators. They did so because local and state governments were routinely steamrolled by powerful economic interests.

The history of environmental degradation in the United States is largely a history of local “collaboration.” Before the Environmental Protection Agency was established, and before the Clean Air and Clean Water Acts were passed, environmental regulation was highly decentralized. Localities and states competed in a race to the bottom, offering lax environmental standards to attract industry. When citizens tried to negotiate with local factories, they found themselves completely outmatched by the legal and technical resources of the corporations. It took strict, non-negotiable, federally enforced standards to finally clean up our rivers and our air.

Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen now ask us to abandon this proven framework in favor of “Habitat Conservation Plans” (HCPs) and localized “tributary teams.” Let us look closely at the HCP process under the Endangered Species Act (ESA), which the authors highlight as a “dynamic and supple prototype.” Since Secretary Bruce Babbitt took office, the Department of the Interior has drastically accelerated the approval of HCPs. The authors describe this as an opportunity for landowners and environmentalists to hammer out conservation plans. In reality, the vast majority of these plans are negotiated behind closed doors between developers and chronically underfunded, overworked field agents of the Fish and Wildlife Service.

Far from empowering local citizens, HCPs often systematically exclude them. Developers arrive at these negotiations with armies of hired biologists and lawyers. They produce reams of data supporting their preferred development plans. Environmental groups and local citizens, lacking the financial resources to conduct their own independent scientific studies, are relegated to the sidelines, allowed to submit comments during a brief Administrative Procedure Act review period only after the deal has essentially been cut. The result is the issuance of “incidental take” permits that allow developers to bulldoze critical habitat in exchange for mitigation measures that are often scientifically dubious and poorly monitored.

The authors acknowledge the lack of monitoring and the potential for “backroom deals,” but they view these as mere growing pains, technical glitches to be solved by better databases and the passage of the Miller Bill (HR 960). This ignores the fundamental power imbalance at the heart of these negotiations. No amount of database tracking will change the fact that the regulated entity—the wealthy developer or the multinational timber company—holds all the cards in a decentralized negotiation. The strict, inflexible prohibitions of Section 9 of the ESA—the very rigidity the authors lament—are the only leverage citizens have to ensure that species are protected. By trading away that rigid baseline for “flexible” HCPs, we are not deepening democracy; we are disarming it.

Furthermore, the authors’ reliance on the Toxics Release Inventory (TRI) as a model of “performance-based regulation” is equally flawed. TRI is indeed a valuable tool, but it is not a substitute for substantive regulation. TRI requires companies to report their emissions; it does not require them to reduce them. The reductions in toxic emissions we have seen over the past decade are not solely the result of companies being “shamed” by public data, as the authors suggest. They are largely the result of companies anticipating future command-and-control regulations and acting preemptively. To pretend that information disclosure alone can replace binding emission limits is to indulge in a fantasy.

The “rolling-rule regime” requires continuous participation and vigilance from local citizens. But activism is exhausting. Citizens have jobs, families, and lives. They cannot be expected to constantly monitor the scientific intricacies of their local watershed or the toxic use reduction plans of every neighboring factory. The central premise of the administrative state is that we elect representatives who appoint experts to protect the public interest on our behalf. When we decentralize that responsibility, asking “deep users” to negotiate their own environmental protections, we ensure that communities with wealth, education, and resources will secure clean environments, while poorer, marginalized communities will be left to fend for themselves against corporate polluters.

We must not let our frustration with the inefficiencies of the current system blind us to the reasons it was built. Command-and-control regulation is clumsy, rigid, and often frustrating. But it works. It has pulled the bald eagle back from the brink of extinction, it has made Lake Erie fishable again, and it has removed lead from our air. Before we discard this architecture in favor of a pragmatist utopia of continuous local deliberation, we should remember that in negotiations between unequal parties, “flexibility” is usually just another word for surrender.
