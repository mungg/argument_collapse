"Regulators should focus on the systemic risks of industrial meat concentration and corporate power rather than blocking the Smithfield acquisition based on the buyer's Chinese nationality."

The most important risks in the Smithfield transaction are being discussed under the wrong heading. The debate has quickly become “China buying American food,” but the deeper issue is the power of very large meat companies, whatever their nationality.

Smithfield is already a giant. It has long been criticized by farmers, environmentalists, animal welfare advocates and labor groups. Concentrated meat production brings real public costs: manure lagoons, water pollution, dangerous working conditions, pressure on independent hog farmers, and a race toward ever-larger industrial operations. Those problems did not begin with Shuanghui, and they will not be solved by keeping Smithfield under American ownership.

In fact, some of the China-specific fears are overstated. The Chinese buyer is not purchasing Smithfield in order to poison American consumers. It is buying Smithfield because China has a shortage of trusted meat and a growing middle class that wants safer pork. The incentive is to preserve the value of American standards, not destroy them. If Smithfield’s products become suspect, the whole purpose of the acquisition is undermined.

Nor is the food supply likely to be “shipped away” in any simple sense. Pork markets are global, and exports already matter. If the deal increases exports to China, that could raise questions about prices and supply, but those are market questions, not proof of a national security threat. American farmers may even benefit from greater demand.

Still, regulators should not be passive. The Justice Department should examine whether the acquisition reinforces concentration in pork processing. USDA and EPA should ensure that ownership changes do not weaken enforcement at plants and farms. Labor protections should be monitored, especially in slaughterhouses where workers often have little bargaining power. These are the agencies and standards that matter most.

Blocking the deal merely because the buyer is Chinese would satisfy a political impulse but miss the real problem. Approving it without examining the structure of industrial meat would be equally irresponsible. The question should not be whether Chinese capital is uniquely dangerous. It should be whether any corporation, foreign or domestic, should wield so much influence over what Americans eat, how rural communities survive, and what costs are imposed on land, water and workers.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The negative environmental and social impacts of Smithfield's operations are inherent to large-scale industrial meat production and are not unique to Chinese ownership.
  2. 02The Chinese buyer has a strong market incentive to maintain American food safety standards to satisfy its domestic demand for trusted meat products.
  3. 03Concerns about the food supply being depleted are overstated because pork markets are global and increased exports may actually benefit American farmers.
  4. 04Regulatory scrutiny should prioritize antitrust concerns, environmental enforcement, and labor protections rather than national security fears tied to foreign capital.
  5. 05Blocking the deal based on political impulses ignores the broader issue of whether any massive corporation should exert such significant influence over the food system and rural communities.
