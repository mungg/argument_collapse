"While the Democratic Party has successfully embraced a more ambitious economic agenda, its reliance on a 'tax only the rich' fiscal strategy is an unsustainable foundation for a durable social democracy."

Hacker and Pierson’s essay rightly identifies a major development in Democratic politics: the party has become more economically ambitious while relying more heavily on affluent, college-educated voters. But the authors understate the fiscal and political tension at the heart of this arrangement. Democrats have promised a larger welfare state while also promising that almost no one except corporations and the very rich will pay for it. That may work for a legislative cycle or two. It is not a foundation for durable social democracy.

Biden’s pledge not to raise taxes on households earning under $400,000 was politically shrewd. It reassured upper-middle-class suburban voters that the Democratic economic agenda would not come at their expense. It also reflected a real fact about American inequality: enormous revenue can be raised from the top of the distribution and from corporations, especially after decades of tax cuts and avoidance. There is no reason billionaires should pay low effective rates. There is no reason multinational firms should shift profits so easily. There is no reason capital income should be privileged over labor income. Democrats are right to begin there.

But they should not pretend they can end there. The American welfare state is underbuilt not only because billionaires are undertaxed, but because the United States has never developed the broad fiscal contract that supports the most generous welfare states. European social democracies do not fund universal health care, child care, paid leave, elder care, and income supports solely through taxes on the top 1 or 2 percent. They rely on broad-based taxes, including payroll taxes and consumption taxes, alongside progressive income taxes. Their citizens pay more, and they also get more.

American Democrats often want the “get more” part without admitting the “pay more” part. That evasion has costs. It reinforces the idea that government benefits are gifts financed by someone else. It prevents the creation of contributory social insurance programs that people understand as earned rights. It leaves new programs vulnerable to deficit politics. It also narrows the scale of what can be built.

Hacker and Pierson recognize this problem in passing when they note that the $400,000 pledge makes contributory programs harder to design. The point deserves more emphasis. Social Security and Medicare are politically durable partly because they are financed broadly. Workers contribute; beneficiaries claim. The link is imperfect and redistributive, but it creates ownership. By contrast, programs financed through general revenue and targeted taxes can be easier to portray as welfare, giveaways, or partisan spending. If Democrats want paid leave, child care, long-term care, and child benefits to become permanent pillars of American life, they need a fiscal politics that creates broad buy-in.

That does not mean starting with middle-class tax hikes in a campaign ad. Politics requires sequencing. But there is a difference between sequencing and denial. Democrats should tell voters that a decent society costs money, that the rich should pay much more, and that over time broad benefits require broad contributions. The promise should not be “you will never pay.” It should be “what you receive will be worth more than what you pay, and the system will be fair.”

This is especially important because the category “under $400,000” contains very different households. A family earning $55,000 in rural Ohio, a renter household earning $110,000 in Queens, and a two-professional household earning $350,000 in Montgomery County are not similarly situated. The last group is not plutocratic, especially in a high-cost area with child care, student loans, and housing expenses. But it is affluent by national standards. A politics that treats such households as off-limits for any tax increase may be necessary to win some suburbs, but it also limits honesty about class.

The upper-middle class benefits enormously from public goods. Its members depend on educated workforces, research funding, courts, infrastructure, public health, safe food and drugs, stable financial systems, and government-backed mortgage markets. Many also benefit from tax expenditures: retirement savings preferences, employer health exclusions, mortgage interest deductions, college savings vehicles, and favorable treatment of capital gains inside retirement accounts. To say that households below $400,000 should never pay more is not obviously progressive. It may simply protect the comfort zone of the new Democratic coalition.

There is also a problem of program design. Because Democrats fear middle-class taxes, they often rely on complicated financing and delivery mechanisms: tax credits, subsidies, phaseouts, public-private partnerships, and incentives. These can be useful, but they make programs harder to understand and defend. A universal child allowance funded by broad progressive taxation is easier to explain than a temporary expanded credit subject to income thresholds, refundability rules, and congressional renewal fights. A public paid leave system funded by payroll contributions is easier to institutionalize than a program forever dependent on reconciliation math.

The politics of “tax only the rich” also makes Democrats vulnerable when inflation and deficits dominate public concern. Republicans are hypocritical on deficits; they routinely cut taxes for the wealthy and then rediscover fiscal restraint under Democratic presidents. Hacker and Pierson are right about that. But hypocrisy does not eliminate the underlying issue. Voters know that resources are finite, especially when prices are rising. If Democrats respond to every spending proposal by saying billionaires will pay for it, the claim eventually sounds too easy. Public trust requires some acknowledgment of tradeoffs.

At the same time, affluent suburban resistance to taxes may be less fixed than politicians assume. Many upper-middle-class voters already support school bonds, property taxes, and local levies when they believe the benefits are concrete and well managed. They pay high housing premiums to access good public schools and safe communities. The obstacle is not simply selfishness; it is distrust that federal taxes will return value. A successful Democratic fiscal politics would therefore emphasize competence, universality, and visible benefits. People are more willing to pay when they believe everyone contributes, everyone benefits, and the system works.

The Affordable Care Act shows both sides of the problem. Its subsidies and Medicaid expansion have helped millions, and the law has become more popular over time. But its complexity made it easy to attack and hard to love. Many beneficiaries did not know what they were receiving or why premiums were changing. Medicare, by contrast, is simple in its political identity: a public program for older Americans. If Democrats want a durable care state, they should prefer programs that people can name, use, and defend.

Hacker and Pierson emphasize that Democrats’ broad spending proposals could disproportionately help racial minorities without foregrounding race in ways that trigger backlash. That strategy is defensible. But fiscal universalism must be real, not just rhetorical. If programs are broad enough to include the middle and upper-middle classes, then financing should eventually be broad enough to create shared ownership while remaining progressive. Otherwise Democrats will build a welfare state rhetorically for everyone but fiscally imagined as paid for by a tiny villain class. That may win applause, but it will not produce institutional stability.

None of this means Democrats should retreat to Clinton-era austerity. Quite the opposite. The party’s break with deficit obsession was overdue. The United States needs more public investment, stronger social insurance, better care infrastructure, climate action, and a more equal distribution of income and wealth. But precisely because those goals are important, Democrats need a more mature fiscal story. A politics built on painless abundance will falter when interest rates rise, inflation bites, or congressional majorities narrow.

The new blue divide is therefore not only cultural or geographic. It is fiscal. The party’s affluent voters are willing to support redistribution as long as it does not ask too much of them. The party’s working-class voters need programs large and durable enough to change their lives. The party’s leaders try to square the circle by taxing only corporations and the very rich. That square may hold temporarily because American inequality is so extreme. But a serious welfare state cannot be permanently built on the premise that 98 percent of households are fiscal bystanders.

Democrats should begin with the rich because that is where the money and justice are most obvious. They should enforce the tax code, tax capital more effectively, close loopholes, and reverse regressive cuts. But they should also prepare voters for a broader bargain: higher contributions for better health care, paid leave, child care, elder care, housing, transit, and climate security. The bargain must be progressive, but it must be a bargain.

Hacker and Pierson are right that the Democratic Party’s economic ambition is real. The next test is whether it can move from ambition to durable social contract. That will require not only managing affluent suburban anxieties, but challenging them with a proposition more demanding and more honest than “someone else will pay.” A better society is worth paying for. Democrats should have the confidence to say so.
