Diversified Gemini

"Nancy J. Hirschmann’s critique of care feminism is fundamentally flawed because it relies on the experiences of a privileged, elite demographic while ignoring the economic realities and historical contexts of women of color and the working class."

In reading Nancy J. Hirschmann’s critique of "Mothers Who Care Too Much," one is struck by the narrow, overwhelmingly privileged demographic that serves as the foundation for her theory. The essay is ostensibly about the state of feminist care theory and the gendered division of labor, but it reads as a hyper-specific grievance about elite, white, upper-middle-class women and their choices. In doing so, Hirschmann perpetuates a glaring blind spot in mainstream feminism: the total marginalization of women of color and working-class women.

Hirschmann opens with anecdotes about the parents of a teen driving a BMW to Paris after a vehicular manslaughter, a sorority student flying to California for a rush event, and a student heading to a high-profile investment firm. She then transitions to her students at an elite university who plan to "opt out" and become stay-at-home mothers. This is the universe Hirschmann is analyzing.

But the "opt-out revolution" has always been a media-generated myth applicable only to a tiny fraction of women at the very top of the socioeconomic ladder. For the vast majority of women in America, particularly Black and Latina women, the debate over whether to be a full-time stay-at-home mother or a career woman is nonexistent. It is a debate born of luxury. Working-class women do not "opt out" because they cannot afford to; their wages are essential for their families' basic survival.

Historically, Black women in the United States have always worked outside the home at higher rates than white women. During the eras when the white middle-class ideal of the stay-at-home mother was at its zenith, Black women were working as domestic laborers, raising white children while struggling to find time to care for their own. The feminist fight for the "right to work" often rang hollow for women of color who had been denied the right to *not* work for centuries.

When Hirschmann worries that women’s celebration of care reinforces women's roles as caregivers and damages their "economic and social power," she is speaking exclusively to women who have the option to rely on a male breadwinner. She fails to interrogate how this dynamic shifts in communities where systemic racism has actively dismantled male earning power, making the dual-income household a necessity or leaving women as the sole providers.

Furthermore, Hirschmann's critique of mothers who fail to raise "civic-minded" children ignores the very different realities of civic life for marginalized communities. When she criticizes parents who "protect" their children from the consequences of their actions, she fails to recognize that for Black mothers, "protecting" their children often means trying to keep them alive in the face of a hostile, racist criminal justice system. The intense, protective care that Black mothers exhibit is not the "selfish" indulgence of the BMW-driving Carla Wagner's parents; it is a necessary survival strategy.

Hirschmann argues that women must gain economic clout to force men to share the care work. But what about women who work full-time minimum-wage jobs and still have zero leverage because they are easily exploitable by both their employers and their partners? By framing the solution as an individual negotiation based on earning power, Hirschmann abandons women who lack that power.

Feminist theory must move beyond the anxieties of the Ivy League. If we want to formulate a truly liberating theory of care, we must center the experiences of women who have historically performed the most caregiving labor for the least reward. We need policies that support all working families—like a living wage, affordable housing, and universal child care—rather than agonizing over whether elite women are properly utilizing their degrees.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'opt-out' debate is a luxury unavailable to the vast majority of women, particularly Black and Latina women, whose wages are essential for family survival.
  2. 02Hirschmann’s focus on the 'right to work' ignores the history of women of color who have been denied the right to not work and have historically performed care labor for others at the expense of their own families.
  3. 03The essay’s criticism of protective mothering fails to distinguish between the selfish indulgence of the wealthy and the necessary survival strategies used by marginalized mothers to protect children from a racist criminal justice system.
  4. 04Framing gender equality as an individual negotiation based on economic leverage abandons low-wage workers who lack bargaining power in both the workplace and the home.
  5. 05A truly liberating theory of care must prioritize systemic policy solutions like universal childcare and living wages rather than the personal career choices of elite women.
Diversified MiniMax

"The lead essay’s critique of care feminism and its call for women to "care less" is flawed because it ignores how class disparities fundamentally shape the choices and constraints available to women across the economic hierarchy."

I want to offer a class-conscious response to Hirschmann's essay, because I think she underestimates how differently the issues she raises affect women at different points in the economic hierarchy.

Hirschmann's examples—Carla Wagner, Susan, Anthony—are drawn from elite contexts. Carla Wagner's parents were apparently wealthy enough to send her to Paris for the summer. Susan and Anthony attended a university where political theory was taught, and Anthony had secured a job at a "high-profile investment firm." These are not the struggles of working-class families; they are the concerns of the professional and managerial class.

For these women, the choice between career and family is genuinely a choice. They have enough education and credentials to re-enter the workforce if they leave it. They have resources that allow them to cushion the costs of caregiving. And they have husbands or partners who, if not equal, at least share some of the burdens of domestic life.

For women at the bottom of the economic ladder, the choice is not really a choice at all. They cannot afford to stay home with their children; they need wages to survive. But they also cannot afford quality childcare, which means their children are often raised in suboptimal conditions. The welfare system, as reformed in the 1990s, pushed these women into low-wage jobs that pay little and offer no flexibility for caregiving emergencies. The result is that they do poorly at both caregiving and employment, not because they care too much or too little but because the system offers them no good options.

This class dynamic is visible in Hirschmann's account of her students. When they read about the vulnerability of married stay-at-home mothers, they are skeptical; they believe good mothers should be supported by their husbands. When they read about single mothers on public assistance, they are condemnatory; they believe these women should work. This double standard—the expectation that middle-class women can afford to stay home while working-class women should be forced to work—has been present in American feminism from its beginnings, and it is deeply troubling.

Hirschmann correctly identifies this inconsistency but does not fully explain it. She notes that her students "draw on the idea of care to shore up class divisions and selfish interest." But why? Partly, I suspect, because they have been taught to think of feminism as a matter of individual choice and personal empowerment, not collective responsibility. When feminism is privatized in this way, it becomes easy to judge other women's choices while defending one's own.

What would a class-conscious care feminism look like? It would start from the recognition that the problems of caregiving cannot be solved at the individual level. Middle-class women can hire nannies and buy their way out of some childcare burdens; working-class women cannot. The solution must be collective: public childcare, parental leave, flexible work arrangements, wages sufficient to live on.

It would also recognize that the feminist movement has sometimes been more concerned with the advancement of professional women than with the liberation of all women. The "opt-out" phenomenon is a problem mainly for women who have the option to opt in. For women at the bottom, the issue is not whether to work but how to survive in a labor market that pays them poorly and offers them no security.

Hirschmann's argument that women need to care less is particularly problematic from a class perspective. For middle-class women, caring less might mean accepting career setbacks or demanding more from husbands. For working-class women, caring less might mean leaving children in unsafe situations or abandoning them altogether. The advice is class-blind, and its obliviousness to class difference weakens it.

I do not want to dismiss Hirschmann's essay entirely. Her critique of care feminism's failure to change male behavior is valid, and her analysis of the tax system's biases is important. But any serious feminism must take class seriously, and must recognize that the choices facing middle-class women are very different from those facing working-class women. A feminism that speaks only to the former will not be a feminism at all.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The lead essay relies on examples from elite, professional-class contexts that do not reflect the economic realities of working-class families.
  2. 02For women at the bottom of the economic ladder, staying home is not a viable choice, yet the labor market and welfare system provide no support for their caregiving needs.
  3. 03The double standard held by students—supporting stay-at-home middle-class mothers while condemning poor mothers on assistance—stems from a privatized feminism focused on individual choice rather than collective responsibility.
  4. 04The lead essay's advice for women to "care less" is class-blind, as it fails to recognize that for working-class women, reducing care could result in genuine danger or abandonment for their children.
  5. 05A truly effective feminism must move beyond the concerns of professional women to advocate for collective solutions like public childcare and livable wages that benefit all women.
