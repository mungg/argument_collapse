"Journalists should respond to the Trump administration's hostility by shifting their focus away from White House access and presidential rhetoric toward rigorous, decentralized reporting on the actual actions and consequences of government agencies."

The threat to move reporters out of the White House should be treated less as a catastrophe than as an opportunity. The American press has spent too much time confusing proximity to power with knowledge of power. If the Trump administration wants to make the briefing room less central, journalists should take the hint and build a model of coverage that does not depend on being called on by a press secretary.

Access is useful, but it is also seductive. It rewards reporters for maintaining relationships with officials whose job is to spin them. It turns daily briefings into rituals of performance. It can make a president’s mood, insults and stagecraft seem more important than the government’s actions. Mr. Trump is unusually skilled at exploiting that weakness. He knows that a taunt can consume a news cycle. He knows that a tweet can pull cameras away from a policy story. He knows that the media, especially television, finds conflict irresistible.

The answer is to reduce his ability to set the agenda. Cover what the government does more than what the president says. Build teams around agencies: Justice, Homeland Security, Health and Human Services, Energy, the Environmental Protection Agency, Treasury, the Pentagon. Track rule changes, enforcement patterns, contracts, waivers, personnel decisions and inspector general reports. Follow lawsuits. Follow money. Follow conflicts between Mr. Trump’s businesses and his public duties. Follow the lives of people affected by deportations, health care changes, trade policy and policing. The White House podium is not the only place where democracy happens.

News organizations should also cooperate more. They can compete on scoops, but they should share basic defenses against manipulation. If the administration bans an outlet, others can circulate its questions. If officials provide background briefings designed to launder anonymous claims, editors can set stricter rules for using them. If public records are withheld, organizations can jointly sue. Smaller outlets, which cannot afford endless legal fights, need support from larger institutions and press-freedom groups.

Television in particular must change. During the campaign, Mr. Trump received enormous free airtime because he was entertaining. That cannot continue in government. Do not carry every rally live. Do not show an empty podium for half an hour while substantive stories go uncovered. Do not treat every tweet as breaking news. Summarize, verify, contextualize, and then move on. A president’s words matter, but they should not be allowed to displace the consequences of his administration’s decisions.

This approach would also serve readers and viewers better. Most Americans do not need more speculation about palace intrigue. They need to know whether their health insurance will change, whether their relatives may be deported, whether their water is safe, whether their taxes will rise or fall, whether their sons and daughters may be sent to war. Those questions cannot be answered by waiting for a shouted exchange in the briefing room.

Mr. Trump’s hostility is real. But the press should not let him define the battlefield. If journalism becomes a daily duel between the president and celebrity correspondents, he will often win. If it becomes patient, distributed, document-based reporting on the machinery of government, the public will.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The press must prioritize investigating the machinery of government and the impact of policy over the performative rituals of the White House briefing room.
  2. 02News organizations should engage in greater cooperation to defend against administrative manipulation and legal challenges.
  3. 03Television media must stop providing excessive airtime to the president's rallies and tweets, treating them as distractions rather than substantive news.
  4. 04Journalism should focus on the practical concerns of citizens, such as healthcare and taxes, rather than speculating on internal palace intrigue.
  5. 05Reducing the president's ability to set the media agenda prevents him from using conflict and taunts to overshadow policy stories.
