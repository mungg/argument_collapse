Diversified MiniMax

"While Daniel Richman correctly identifies counterterrorism as a field that benefits from a federalist approach, his logic should be viewed as a context-dependent framework rather than a universal template for all areas of national security or governance."

Daniel Richman makes a strong case for why federalism should be central to domestic counterterrorism policy, but his argument ultimately proves too much. If his reasoning is correct—that the distinct interests of federal, state, and local governments should be engaged to ensure accountability—then those same principles should apply to other areas of national security where local interests are at stake. The implications are worth exploring.

Consider domestic counterintelligence against foreign intelligence services. Foreign governments spy on American targets using assets embedded in American communities. The FBI has primary responsibility for countering this threat, but local police may encounter suspicious activities in their patrols. Should local departments have a formal voice in how the FBI approaches counterintelligence in their communities? If the federalism logic applies to counterterrorism, it should apply here as well.

Or consider cybersecurity. Critical infrastructure—power grids, financial systems, transportation networks—often operates at the local level, even when owned by national or international corporations. Cyber threats to this infrastructure require coordination among federal, state, and local agencies, as well as private entities. Should local governments have formal authority to set priorities for cybersecurity investment? Should their concerns about civil liberties implications of monitoring be incorporated into federal policy?

Richman might respond that these cases are different—that counterterrorism presents unique circumstances (the presence of immigrant communities, the role of local police in maintaining community relations) that justify special treatment. But this response raises the question of why those circumstances matter. They matter because local knowledge and relationships are genuinely valuable for certain types of intelligence gathering. They also matter because the costs of counterterrorism policies fall disproportionately on local communities. Both considerations apply in other contexts.

The broader point is that federalism is not a universal principle but a context-dependent judgment about where authority should be located. Richman makes this judgment well for counterterrorism. The specific circumstances he identifies—local police relationships with immigrant communities, the political economy of federal-local funding, the community policing revolution—do make a federalist approach appropriate here. But the essay's framing sometimes suggests that federalism is inherently desirable, which is not the argument he actually makes.

The practical implication is that counterterrorism policy should be designed with attention to the specific institutional and political dynamics that Richman identifies, rather than imported from other contexts where those dynamics may not apply. A federalism that works in counterterrorism may not work in economic regulation or environmental protection or any number of other areas where federal-state-local relations require different balances. Richman provides a useful framework for thinking through these questions; he does not provide a universal template.
