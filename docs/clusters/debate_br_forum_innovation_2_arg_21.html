"Dan Breznitz’s framework for innovation-led growth is fundamentally flawed because it fails to integrate ecological boundaries and sustainability into its economic stages."

Dan Breznitz’s essay is an important intervention in the sterile debate about local economic growth, but it suffers from a glaring omission: nowhere does it grapple with the ecological boundaries within which all innovation must now operate. The “inclusive local prosperity” he champions is implicitly measured in jobs, wages, and equality, as if the Earth’s capacity to absorb material throughput is infinite. But we live in a time of climate emergency, biodiversity collapse, and resource depletion. Any framework for innovation-led growth that does not center sustainability risks becoming part of the problem. To his credit, Breznitz nods toward decarbonisation as a “fundamental system innovation,” but he never integrates this imperative into his stage theory. That silence is deafening.

Take the bicycle example, which Breznitz uses to illustrate the promise of stage-two and stage-three innovation. Yes, Giant’s carbon-fiber frames are lighter and stronger, and they helped popularize bicycles. But carbon fiber production is energy-intensive and notoriously difficult to recycle; the frames often end up in landfills after their useful life. A more ecologically conscious innovation strategy would have asked not only how to make bikes better and more appealing, but how to make them durable, repairable, and composed of benign materials. The Italian shoe district of Brenta may sustain local craft jobs, but the fashion industry overall is an environmental disaster, built on petroleum-derived materials, toxic dyes, and a culture of disposability. Breznitz’s applause for “exquisitely crafted furniture” and luxury goods sits uneasily with the reality that these industries serve a global consumer class whose consumption patterns are the root driver of ecological overshoot.

The same critique applies to the stage-four assembly innovation he praises in Shenzhen. The ability to “ramp up production to millions of units or abort it at a moment’s notice” is ecologically catastrophic. It implies a model of rapid product obsolescence, mountains of electronic waste, and a continuous churn of materials, all powered by coal-heavy electricity grids. Celebrating this as a form of innovation that “created jobs for millions” without acknowledging its environmental footprint is a form of industrial greenwashing. If we are serious about inclusive prosperity for the long term—for the next generation, not just the next quarter—then the measure of innovation must include resource productivity, circularity, and carbon intensity.

Breznitz’s own policy prescriptions point toward an answer but do not deliver it. He calls for public-private partnerships, shared facilities, and collaborative networks. These are exactly the institutional forms that could support ecologically oriented innovation. Imagine a region specializing not in stage-two shoe design but in textile recycling technologies; not in stage-three incremental electronics refinement but in modular, repairable hardware design. The “tech teens” concept could be powerfully redirected toward enterprises developing plant-based materials, energy storage, or water purification. But this requires a deliberate green foresight that guides public investment and shapes market demand through procurement standards, carbon pricing, and regulations that make waste more expensive. Breznitz mentions none of this, as if environmental constraints are merely an afterthought.

One might argue that his essay is about innovation and local prosperity, not about environmental policy, and that the two issues can be addressed separately. But that separation is exactly what got us into this mess. The original Silicon Valley model is not just inequitable; it is ecologically blind, with its ideology of “move fast and break things” that views planetary limits as friction to be overcome. An essay that rightly critiques the Silicon Valley model for its narrowness should not then propose an alternative that simply reallocates innovation activities across stages without questioning their material basis. The true lesson from Cobalt, Ontario, is not just about economic boom and bust; it is about the way extractive logics—whether of silver, venture capital, or consumer goods—leave desolation behind. Today’s global extraction is driving a planetary Cobalt moment, and no amount of local stage specialization will matter if we cross tipping points that make communities unlivable.

A genuinely comprehensive response to Breznitz would integrate ecological constraints into each of the four stages, proposing that stage-one novelty prioritize green technologies, that stage-two design emphasize durability and circularity, that stage-three incremental innovation target efficiency not just in cost but in energy and materials, and that stage-four production aim for zero-waste closed loops. This does not mean abandoning the goal of inclusive prosperity; it means redefining prosperity itself to include a livable planet. Breznitz’s essay is a valuable step away from Silicon Valley orthodoxy, but it needs a green sequel urgently.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The focus on inclusive prosperity through jobs and wages ignores the environmental costs of material throughput and resource depletion.
  2. 02The specific examples of successful innovation cited, such as carbon-fiber bicycles and luxury fashion, rely on energy-intensive production and non-recyclable materials.
  3. 03The celebration of rapid production and assembly in Shenzhen overlooks the ecological catastrophe of electronic waste and product obsolescence.
  4. 04A viable innovation model must redefine prosperity to include resource productivity, circularity, and carbon intensity across all four stages of production.
  5. 05Public-private partnerships and 'tech teens' should be redirected toward green foresight and solving environmental challenges rather than just economic ones.
