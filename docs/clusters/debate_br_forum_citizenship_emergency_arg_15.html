"The events of September 11 demonstrate that centralized, top-down military structures are structurally ineffective and unconstitutional, necessitating a return to a distributed, egalitarian model of national defense rooted in civilian consent."

Elaine Scarry’s “Citizenship in Emergency” performs an essential act of democratic witness. By juxtaposing the inert machinery of our national security state with the swift, deliberative, and ultimately sacrificial action of the passengers on United Flight 93, she illuminates a truth that official Washington has been loath to face: our country’s defense has been hollowed out by a decades-long drift away from the citizenry and toward an unaccountable executive. Scarry’s argument that the events of September 11 expose the hollowness of the “speed” alibi—the perennial excuse that democratic procedures are too slow for modern warfare—is devastating and, I believe, irrefutable. Her essay is not merely a critique; it is a call to reclaim our constitutional inheritance, and it could not have come at a more urgent hour.

The core of Scarry’s case rests on a meticulous reconstruction of two timelines. On the one hand, American Airlines Flight 77, the plane that struck the Pentagon, gave the military ample warning by the very standards the Pentagon itself has promulgated for decades. One hour and twenty-one minutes of escalating certainty—radio silence, transponder loss, a passenger’s phone call to the Justice Department, radar sightings—could not produce an effective defense. On the other hand, the passengers of United Flight 93, working with far less time and with no access to AWACS or F-16s, managed to deliberate, vote, and act to prevent their plane from becoming a weapon. The contrast is not incidental. It illustrates something profound about the relationship between knowledge, consent, and effective action. The passengers could act because the threat was immediate to their senses, because they could verify information through multiple independent sources, and because they alone possessed the moral authority to decide whether to sacrifice their own lives. The F-16 pilots, by contrast, operated within a chain of command that structurally could not grant them that authority, and they were forced to chase ghosts in an information fog that centralized systems inevitably create.

Scarry’s argument that the military was “unable” to defend the Pentagon is not an indictment of individual pilots or soldiers; it is a structural critique. The very attributes that are touted as the strengths of centralized command—hierarchical decision-making, secrecy, reliance on sophisticated but fragile technical systems—proved to be weaknesses when confronted with an unconventional threat. Meanwhile, the distributed, egalitarian model of the passengers, messy and improvised though it was, succeeded. This inversion of official dogma should shake the foundations of our strategic thinking. For fifty years, we have been told that nuclear weapons and supersonic delivery systems necessitate a hair-trigger presidency, that Congress is too slow, that the people cannot be trusted with the levers of survival. September 11 demonstrated that the reverse is true: the people, acting in concert and informed by open communication, are capable of swifter and more legitimate defense than the state’s remote guardians.

What makes Scarry’s argument so politically potent is the connection she draws between the failure of centralized defense and the ongoing erosion of constitutional government. The doctrine of presidential first use of nuclear weapons is not merely a military posture; it is a direct negation of the republican principle that decisions of war and mass death must be shared by the many, not concentrated in one person. Scarry is right to insist that nuclear weapons are “monarchic weapons,” incompatible with democratic self-governance. The fact that the president can, theoretically within minutes, order the incineration of millions without any prior deliberation with Congress or the citizenry is an ongoing constitutional emergency. The Bush administration’s current bellicosity toward Iraq, which seems to be proceeding with only the most perfunctory gestures toward Congress, is a direct extension of this logic. When a president has genocidal power at his sole command, what need does he have to seek authorization for lesser wars?

Some will charge Scarry with romanticizing the passengers of Flight 93 or with naively suggesting that a militia of cell-phone-wielding citizens could replace the United States military. This misreads her. She is not proposing that we disband the armed forces and hand every citizen a rifle. What she insists upon—and what the Constitution insists upon—is that the military be held “within a civil frame.” This means, at minimum, that wars cannot be initiated without the genuine, informed consent of the people’s representatives after full public debate. It means that weapons whose very existence annihilates the possibility of democratic deliberation—above all, nuclear weapons—must be renounced. And it means that homeland defense should not be designed in ways that systematically exclude and disempower the citizenry, whether by commandeering their cell phones or by arresting foreign residents in secret.

Her call for a return to a draft is a bracing one. A draft forces every family to confront the human cost of military action, ensuring that the decision for war is tested against the intimate knowledge that one’s own children may be sent to kill and die. The all-volunteer force, for all its professionalism, has facilitated the compartmentalization of sacrifice and the public’s disengagement from its own defense. Reinstating conscription would not instantly democratize foreign policy, but it would make it far harder for presidents to launch wars of choice without a broad national consensus.

The most uncomfortable implication of Scarry’s essay is that our entire nuclear posture is not only unconstitutional but also useless as a defense against the kinds of threats we actually face. The billions poured into missile defense and the maintenance of an arsenal that can destroy the world multiple times over did nothing to stop nineteen men with box cutters. Worse, our nuclear stance fuels proliferation, as other nations reasonably conclude that the only way to deter American intervention is to acquire their own doomsday weapons. If we are serious about preventing nuclear terrorism and rolling back the arsenals of rogue states, we must lead by example. The day the United States renounces first use and begins verifiable disarmament is the day we regain the moral and political authority to demand the same of others.

“Citizenship in Emergency” forces us to ask: Who is defending whom, and on whose authority? The passengers on Flight 93 answered that question with their lives. They reclaimed their citizenship in the most extreme circumstances and, in doing so, reminded us that the ultimate responsibility for the republic’s safety rests not in the Pentagon or the White House, but in the consent and courage of the governed. We ignore their lesson at our peril.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The failure to protect the Pentagon despite ample warning time proves that the 'argument from speed' used to justify executive overreach is a fallacy.
  2. 02The success of the passengers on Flight 93 illustrates that effective defense requires immediate sensory knowledge and the moral authority of informed consent, which centralized systems lack.
  3. 03Nuclear weapons are 'monarchic weapons' that are fundamentally incompatible with democratic self-governance because they bypass all structures of collective deliberation.
  4. 04The military must be brought back within a 'civil frame' by requiring genuine congressional authorization for war and renouncing weapons that preclude democratic oversight.
  5. 05Reinstating a military draft would serve as a democratic check on power by forcing the citizenry to personally weigh the human costs of war before it is initiated.
  6. 06The current national security posture is both unconstitutional and strategically useless against modern unconventional threats, while simultaneously fueling global nuclear proliferation.
