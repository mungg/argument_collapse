"Commercial genetic testing companies should not broadly share customer data for research because genetic information is inherently identifiable and its distribution poses significant risks to privacy and autonomy."

The premise of “anonymous genetic testing” is dangerously misleading. Genetic information is, by its nature, identifying. It is unique to a person, persistent over a lifetime and informative about parents, siblings, children and cousins. Removing a name and address does not make a genome anonymous. Researchers have already shown that supposedly anonymous genetic data can sometimes be linked back to individuals using genealogical and demographic clues. As more people put family trees, surnames and medical details online, that risk will grow.

That is why commercial companies should not be broadly sharing genetic data for research simply because customers once agreed to a long set of online terms. The F.D.A.’s decision to permit certain carrier tests is about whether a test can be marketed without prior review; it is not a blessing on the creation of private genetic data markets. A person may buy a 23andMe kit to learn about ancestry or reproductive risk, not to become part of a database licensed to pharmaceutical companies.

The harm is not hypothetical. Genetic information can affect insurability, family relationships and social stigma. GINA protects against some forms of discrimination by employers and health insurers, but it leaves major gaps. A positive carrier result may be clinically modest, yet it can still label families or ethnic groups. And once data are copied, sold, merged or transferred during an acquisition or bankruptcy, the original customer has little practical control.

Research is important, but “breakthrough” should not be a magic word that overrides consent. The history of biomedical research includes too many examples of communities and patients being used first and informed later. If scientists need genetic databases, they should build them under public rules: independent review, narrow consent, clear limits on secondary use, penalties for misuse and governance that includes participants. Public biobanks and university studies are not perfect, but at least they operate within recognizable ethical frameworks.

Commercial genetic companies have a conflict of interest. Their value lies in accumulating and exploiting data. That does not make them evil, but it does make them the wrong institutions to define privacy standards for everyone else. Until law catches up with the identifiability of genomes, sharing should be limited, specific and genuinely voluntary. Scientific progress is not served by teaching people that research requires surrendering control over the most intimate data they possess.
