Diversified Gemini

"While Johnson’s framework of racial capitalism is a vital corrective to Eurocentric history, it must be expanded beyond its American-centric focus to encompass a truly global perspective that addresses both historical and contemporary exploitation across the Global South."

Walter Johnson’s “To Remake the World” offers a crucial corrective to Eurocentric histories of capitalism, insisting that we view the global economy from the standpoint of the "Global South" and the dispossessed. By tracking the evolution of slavery into colonialism and neo-imperialism, Johnson provides a robust framework for understanding contemporary global inequality as the direct descendant of the Atlantic slave trade.

However, in framing his argument so heavily around the American experience—specifically the axis of Mississippi and Manchester—Johnson slightly truncates the truly global scope of racial capitalism. While Du Bois’s *The World and Africa* is cited, the essay’s primary gravitational pull remains the antebellum United States. To truly mount a critique from the standpoint of the Global South, we must de-center the United States as the sole paradigm of racial capitalism.

The Caribbean and Latin America, for instance, received the vast majority of enslaved Africans and developed plantation economies that pre-dated the U.S. cotton boom by centuries. The extraction of silver from Potosí, using forced indigenous labor, capitalized Europe long before the mills of Manchester were built. The British East India Company’s pillaging of Bengal and the Dutch colonization of Indonesia were simultaneous and essential components of this global system.

If we are to take Johnson’s project seriously, we must apply the lens of racial capitalism to the contemporary neo-colonial relations that govern the Global South. Structural adjustment programs, sovereign debt crises imposed by the IMF, and the extraction of rare earth minerals from the Congo for Western technology are not just "capitalism"—they are the ongoing operations of racial capitalism. The "uncanny timeliness" of capitalist exploitation that Du Bois described is alive and well in the supply chains of the 21st century. Justice, therefore, cannot be confined to national borders or American debates over reparations; it demands a total restructuring of the global economic order, abolishing the debt of the Global South and ending the perpetual extraction of its wealth.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The essay’s heavy emphasis on the United States risks truncating the scope of racial capitalism by overlooking the earlier and more extensive plantation and extraction economies of the Caribbean and Latin America.
  2. 02A comprehensive critique of racial capitalism must include the historical roles of European colonial ventures in Asia, such as the British East India Company and Dutch colonization, which were essential to the global system.
  3. 03The lens of racial capitalism should be applied to modern neo-colonial mechanisms like IMF structural adjustment programs and mineral extraction in Africa to understand them as continuations of the Atlantic slave trade's logic.
  4. 04True justice and reparations cannot be limited to American national debates but must involve a total restructuring of the global economic order, including the abolition of Global South debt.
