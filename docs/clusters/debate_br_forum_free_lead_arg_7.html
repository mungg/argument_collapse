"While Buddy Roemer’s frustration with the influence of money in politics is justified, his claim that campaign finance reform is the 'master problem' is an oversimplification that ignores deeper structural and social causes of political paralysis."

Buddy Roemer’s anger is understandable. His diagnosis is too simple.

There is no doubt that campaign fundraising has become an exhausting, degrading part of congressional life. There is no doubt that the tax code is distorted by organized interests, that financial firms exercise disproportionate influence, and that ordinary citizens look at Washington with justified disgust. But Roemer moves from these truths to a much larger claim: that money in politics is the master problem, the obstacle without which we could solve debt, unemployment, energy, poverty, inequality, and financial instability. That claim is emotionally satisfying. It is also doubtful.

The American political system is not paralyzed only because donors are powerful. It is paralyzed because Americans disagree deeply about what government should do. They disagree about taxes, spending, health care, unions, abortion, guns, immigration, climate change, and the role of the courts. They sort themselves geographically and culturally. They consume different media. They punish compromise in primaries. They elect representatives who reflect those divisions. Money worsens these conflicts, but it does not create them.

Consider the debt debate now consuming Washington. It is tempting to say Congress cannot reach a reasonable long-term budget deal because special interests block one. There is truth in that. But the central difficulty is not obscure. Many voters want low taxes, generous benefits, a strong military, and no painful adjustments. Politicians did not invent that contradiction; they exploit it because voters reward them for doing so. Campaign money may protect particular tax breaks or spending programs, but the broad fiscal impasse comes from mass preferences that do not add up.

The same is true of financial reform. Roemer is right that Wall Street influence was immense before the crisis and remains immense after it. But the causes of the crisis cannot be reduced to campaign contributions. Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac had political defenders because homeownership was popular. Cheap credit was popular. Rising home prices were popular. Local realtors, builders, mortgage brokers, borrowers, investors, rating agencies, regulators, and politicians of both parties participated in a national delusion that housing prices would not fall broadly. Deregulation played a role, but so did bad monetary incentives, global savings flows, flawed risk models, and old-fashioned greed. A campaign system funded entirely by small donations would not necessarily have made Congress wise enough to foresee the collapse.

Roemer’s treatment of the research-and-experimentation tax credit also shows the danger of too tidy a story. Perhaps Congress repeatedly extends temporary tax provisions because members want to solicit contributions. But temporary tax policy also persists because budget rules make permanent tax cuts look more expensive, because legislators disagree about offsets, because Congress routinely postpones hard choices, and because temporary provisions give lawmakers a way to revisit policy in light of changing conditions. That does not excuse the mess. It does suggest that not every bad institutional habit is a fundraising conspiracy.

The larger weakness in Roemer’s argument is his romantic view of independence. He implies that a member who refuses PAC money becomes “free to think.” Sometimes that is true. Jim Leach was an admirable public servant. But independence from PACs is not independence from ideology, ambition, party pressure, interest-group ratings, media incentives, constituency demands, or personal error. A politician funded by small donors may be beholden to small donors. A politician funded by online activists may become more extreme, not less. The problem of dependence cannot be abolished; it can only be shifted.

That matters because Roemer’s preferred solution—broadening the donor base through small contributions and vouchers—may not produce the deliberative politics he imagines. Small donors are not a random sample of the public. They tend to be more politically intense than non-donors. They often reward confrontation. We already see how Internet fundraising can elevate candidates who nationalize every race and turn every compromise into betrayal. A Congress dependent on millions of $25 contributors might be cleaner in one sense and more demagogic in another.

Nor should we be casual about public vouchers, even if they are funded through voluntary tax checkoffs. Roemer says no citizen should be forced to support political speech he or she rejects. That is a good principle. But if the system uses tax mechanisms, administrative rules, eligibility thresholds, and government enforcement, disputes about who qualifies will be unavoidable. Fringe candidates, unserious candidates, and fraudulent operations will test the boundaries. Established parties will attempt to write rules that favor themselves. Reformers often underestimate how quickly campaign-finance systems become their own arenas of manipulation.

None of this means we should accept the status quo. There are sensible reforms. Disclosure should be faster, clearer, and more complete. Coordination rules should be enforced seriously so that “independent” expenditure committees do not become shadow campaigns. The tax code should be simplified, with fewer temporary provisions and narrower industry favors. Congressional earmarks and regulatory interventions should be more transparent. Members should have to disclose fundraising events in real time. Revolving-door restrictions should be strengthened, especially for senior staff who write legislation and then sell access to it.

But we should resist the promise that campaign-finance reform will restore a lost golden age of statesmanship. The Congress Roemer entered in 1981 was not pure. The older system had powerful committee barons, opaque party machines, corporate influence, union influence, and cozy relationships that the public often could not see. Politics has always involved organized interests because government makes decisions worth influencing. When government allocates trillions of dollars, regulates whole industries, and writes rules affecting every corner of economic life, people will organize to shape those decisions. That is not simply corruption. It is pluralism, often ugly but sometimes informative.

The challenge is to distinguish access from domination, advocacy from purchase, and representation from rent-seeking. Roemer is right that we have allowed domination, purchase, and rent-seeking to grow too common. But the cure is not to imagine a Congress somehow liberated from pressure. A legislature in a republic is supposed to be pressured—by voters, groups, parties, newspapers, local interests, moral movements, businesses, unions, churches, and civic associations. The aim is balance and accountability, not purity.

Roemer’s most valuable line is “We don’t govern, we grovel.” It captures something real about the humiliation of permanent fundraising. Yet a Congress that stopped groveling to donors tomorrow would still face a divided country, hard tradeoffs, constitutional constraints, and limited knowledge. Money is a serious problem. It is not the only problem, and it is not always the deepest one. If reformers promise too much, they risk discrediting reforms that are genuinely needed.

We should clean up campaign finance because self-government requires trust. But we should also tell citizens the harder truth: no financing system can spare us from disagreement, sacrifice, or responsibility.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Political gridlock is primarily driven by deep ideological divisions and contradictory demands among the American electorate rather than just the influence of donors.
  2. 02Major crises, such as the 2008 financial collapse, resulted from widespread public delusions and complex economic factors that small-donor funding alone would not have prevented.
  3. 03The persistence of temporary tax provisions is often a result of budget rules and legislative procrastination rather than a coordinated fundraising conspiracy.
  4. 04Independence from PAC money does not equate to independence from ideology, party pressure, or the demands of intense, potentially polarizing small donors.
  5. 05Proposed solutions like vouchers and small-donor systems can create new problems, such as incentivizing demagoguery and creating new arenas for administrative manipulation.
  6. 06The goal of reform should be to achieve balance and accountability within a pluralistic system rather than pursuing an impossible ideal of a legislature purified of all outside pressure.
