Diversified DeepSeek

"While a legal defense of the right to protest is necessary, the current crisis of campus repression can only be solved by a cultural transformation that restores the individual's capacity to endure political hostility and psychological discomfort."

Alex Gourevitch begins his essay with an image of heavily armed police raiding unarmed, nonviolent protesters, and he ends with a call for a culture of toleration in which even the most offensive speech is allowed to bloom on campus. Between those poles lies an argument of considerable moral force, but also one that is finally too narrow to meet the full dimensions of our crisis. What his essay describes is not merely the death of a right; it is a symptom of a much deeper breakdown in the capacity of people to share a common world. To blame the crackdowns on a confluence of neoliberal funding models, administrative cowardice, and a pernicious ideology of safety is accurate as far as it goes. But it does not go far enough in explaining why the discourse of vulnerability has become so powerful, why the tolerance for discomfort has atrophied so dramatically, or why the very idea of “hostile” speech has come to seem like an existential threat rather than an inevitable part of democratic life. Those are cultural questions, and they demand a cultural answer.

Let us start with the feeling of unsafety. Gourevitch is careful to say that he does not question the reality of the feelings, only their use as a justification for repression. Yet the feelings themselves are a puzzle. Why do twenty-year-olds, living on one of the wealthiest and most physically secure campuses in human history, experience a “flight or fight” reaction upon hearing distant chanting? Why does the sight of a keffiyeh or a protest sign generate a sense of existential dread? Part of the answer, as Gourevitch notes, lies in the broader society’s obsession with safety and the expansion of administrative surveillance. But that is not the whole story. The more profound driver is a loss of meaning, a hollowing out of the symbolic resources that once allowed people to integrate political conflict into a narrative of purpose.

The university used to promise something grand: the cultivation of character, the pursuit of truth, the formation of citizens. In that context, encountering an opposing view—even an offensive one—was part of the process. It was an invitation to argument, to growth, to the refinement of one’s own beliefs. But when the university is reduced to a credentialing service, a stepping stone to a precarious middle-class life, and a site of therapeutic consumerism, as Gourevitch himself laments, that context collapses. Students arrive already burdened by an acute sense of precarity, bombarded by messages that the world is dangerous, and stripped of any shared moral language for processing disagreement. In such a vacuum, all conflict becomes raw threat. The protest chant is not a political claim to be debated; it is an assault on the fragile self.

This is not a condition that can be corrected by a simple reaffirmation of the right to protest. It requires a recovery of the very idea that a human being can be strong enough to bear hostility—that being offended, insulted, even deeply shaken, does not destroy one’s capacity to function. Gourevitch hints at this when he invokes the old liberal notion that suppressing speech makes one’s own arguments look weak. But the real point is not strategic; it is existential. A person who has been taught that their psychic comfort is the baseline requirement for a functional life cannot do democracy. Democracy demands that we accept the risk of being wrong, the pain of being challenged, and the permanent presence of people who wish us ill. That is not a flaw in democracy; it is its fundamental discipline.

The campus protests over Gaza exposed how far we have fallen from that discipline. On all sides, the demand was not to be heard but to be shielded. The protesters themselves often framed their actions in the language of trauma and safety, insisting that the presence of pro-Israel students was a trigger, that certain readings were harmful, that they needed safe spaces from ideas they found violent. That is the same logic that later led Jewish students to demand the removal of the encampments. Both groups, in their own way, were asking the institution to provide a psychic holding environment—a demand the institution, addicted to customer satisfaction, could not refuse. Gourevitch sees the hypocrisy clearly, but he does not quite see that the solution requires a simultaneous reconstruction of the self, not just a change in policy.

What would that reconstruction look like? It would begin with a pedagogical reorientation: from the earliest grades through graduate school, students must be taught the arts of encounter. That means not just reading John Stuart Mill on free speech, but practicing the difficult work of listening to views one finds abhorrent without collapsing. It means cultivating forms of resilience that are not rooted in callousness but in a robust sense of one’s own value, a value that is not annihilated by another person’s hatred. It means re-embedding the intellectual life in a framework of virtue: courage, patience, charity, and even the willingness to suffer. These are words that sound antique, even embarrassing, in the contemporary university, and that is exactly the problem.

Gourevitch’s vision of a campus where everyone is free to be hostile is, in some ways, a necessary corrective. But absent a companion vision of the kind of person who can live in such a space without being destroyed or turning to destruction, it is a recipe for chaos. The right to protest is not a license for unlimited aggression; it is a social practice that presupposes participants who are capable of self-possession and who recognize one another, however grudgingly, as fellow citizens. We have lost that recognition. Restoring it will take far more than a principled defense of the commons; it will take a cultural transformation that is only just beginning. Until then, the cycle of fragility and repression will continue, no matter how many eloquent essays we write.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The extreme sense of vulnerability felt by students today stems from a loss of shared moral meaning and the reduction of the university to a site of therapeutic consumerism.
  2. 02A functional democracy requires citizens who possess the existential strength to be offended or challenged without viewing such encounters as an assault on their safety.
  3. 03Both protesters and counter-protesters have increasingly relied on the same flawed logic of 'safety' and 'trauma' to demand that institutions shield them from opposing views.
  4. 04Universities must move beyond policy changes to a pedagogical reorientation that actively cultivates virtues like courage, resilience, and the difficult art of encountering abhorrent views.
  5. 05A right to be hostile on campus is unsustainable unless it is accompanied by a reconstruction of the self that enables individuals to maintain self-possession in the face of aggression.
