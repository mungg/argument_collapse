Diversified MiniMax

"A marriage should only be ended in the event of a severe breach of trust that fundamentally undermines the covenant, specifically repeated infidelity or physical violence."

Marriage is a covenant that obligates spouses to weather storms, not to abandon ship at the first sign of trouble. My position is that the only legitimate breaking point is a severe breach of trust that fundamentally undermines the contract’s core: physical safety and absolute fidelity. Infidelity, when it is a repeated pattern rather than a single lapse, and physical violence are the two circumstances that cross the covenant’s red line.

The cultural narrative that “love conquers all” can be romantic, but it glosses over the reality that love must be grounded in respect. When a spouse commits to monogamy, that promise is not merely a浪漫理想 but a concrete expectation. Violation of that promise—especially if the offender shows no remorse or willingness to engage in repair—signals that the partnership has lost its foundational trust. Similarly, physical abuse is an unequivocal threat to safety and dignity, and any tolerance of it in the name of marriage ignores the moral duty to protect the vulnerable.

In the realm of law, many jurisdictions have codified this threshold: irreconcilable differences or cruelty are grounds for divorce, but they are not synonyms for ordinary conflict. The lesson is that spouses should exhaust avenues of reconciliation—counseling, mediated talks, and mutual agreements—before concluding that the marriage is beyond salvage. Only after these steps and after a clear, severe breach should a couple consider dissolution.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Repeated infidelity destroys the foundational trust and concrete expectation of monogamy necessary for a partnership to function.
  2. 02Physical abuse constitutes an unequivocal threat to safety and dignity that violates the moral duty to protect one's spouse.
  3. 03Spouses have an obligation to exhaust all avenues of reconciliation, such as counseling and mediation, before concluding a marriage is beyond salvage.
  4. 04Ordinary marital conflict should be distinguished from the legal and moral thresholds of cruelty or irreconcilable differences that justify dissolution.
Diversified Gemini

"Except in cases of abuse or serial infidelity, couples should prioritize resilience and lifelong commitment over personal dissatisfaction to preserve the stability of the marriage and family unit."

The backlash to Alain de Botton's highly sensible op-ed reveals a deeply ingrained, destructive utopianism in modern dating. We have been conditioned by consumer culture to believe that if a product—or a partner—does not perfectly satisfy our needs at all times, we should simply throw it away and upgrade. This consumerist approach to marriage is exactly why our divorce rates remain tragically high.

When is it time to give up? Far later than modern society tells us. With the exception of physical abuse, severe emotional abuse, or unrepentant serial infidelity, most marriages can and should be saved. The feeling of "falling out of love," the realization of deep lifestyle incompatibilities, or periods of profound boredom are not breaking points; they are simply the middle chapters of a lifelong commitment.

A marriage is not supposed to be a constant source of personal entertainment and emotional validation. It is a foundational institution designed to provide stability for families, raise children in a secure environment, and build a shared life through inevitable adversity. When we treat personal dissatisfaction as a legitimate reason to blow up a family unit, we prioritize fleeting emotional states over generational stability.

The definitive breaking point should be practically unreachable for most couples. We must return to the understanding that vows are meant to bind us precisely when our feelings fail us. Accepting that you have married the "wrong" person is, ironically, the very first step toward building a real, mature, and enduring marriage with them. We do not need more breaking points; we need more resilience.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Modern consumer culture has destructively conditioned people to treat partners as disposable products that should be discarded when they no longer provide perfect satisfaction.
  2. 02Common marital struggles like boredom, incompatibility, or fading romantic feelings are normal phases of a long-term commitment rather than legitimate reasons for divorce.
  3. 03The primary purpose of marriage is to provide a stable foundation for families and children rather than serving as a constant source of personal entertainment or emotional validation.
  4. 04Accepting the inherent flaws of a partner and the reality of a 'wrong' match is a necessary step toward building a mature and enduring relationship.
  5. 05Marriage vows are specifically designed to maintain a union during periods when emotional feelings and personal desires fail.
