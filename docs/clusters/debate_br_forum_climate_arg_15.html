"While defeating fossil capital is necessary, a successful climate strategy must move beyond political antagonism to embrace a pragmatic, pluralistic approach that utilizes markets, technology, and diverse coalitions to achieve rapid decarbonization."

Olúfẹ́mi Táíwò’s essay has the virtue of moral clarity, but it risks strategic overconcentration. “Dethrone fossil capital” is a stirring phrase. It identifies a real source of obstruction. Yet as a general theory of climate politics, it may mislead as much as it illuminates. The climate crisis is not simply a struggle between the public and fossil capital; it is a coordination problem involving technology, infrastructure, consumption, geopolitics, finance, and institutional competence. If the left frames the transition primarily as the defeat of an enemy, it may underplay the plural tools and coalitions needed to reduce emissions quickly.

There is no question that fossil fuel companies have behaved badly. They have funded doubt, lobbied against regulation, encouraged delay, and used the language of energy security to protect their assets. They deserve regulation, litigation, and in some cases managed wind-down. But fossil fuels are embedded in modern life not only because companies are powerful. They remain embedded because they are energy dense, transportable, dispatchable, and built into every major system: electricity, heating, transport, agriculture, shipping, aviation, chemicals, steel, cement, and military logistics. Billions of people depend on them indirectly even when they dislike the companies that produce them. That dependence cannot be broken by political antagonism alone.

The most successful climate policy will be technologically pragmatic, institutionally plural, and politically durable. It will include public investment and planning, yes. But it will also require private capital, market incentives, carbon pricing, permitting reform, innovation, international trade, and consumer adoption. Táíwò criticizes the Biden administration’s climate policy as an attempt to “bribe private capital.” That description is not wholly unfair, but it is too dismissive. The Inflation Reduction Act’s tax credits and industrial incentives have already helped catalyze investment in batteries, electric vehicles, solar manufacturing, hydrogen, carbon capture, and grid-related technologies. One may wish it had built a larger public sector, but the climate does not care whether emissions fall through public ownership or private investment. Speed matters.

Of course, not all private investment is socially beneficial. Markets require rules. But a world in which governments try to directly own or plan the entire energy transition is not obviously more feasible than one in which governments set standards, prices, subsidies, and prohibitions that shift private behavior. The scale of capital required is enormous. Public balance sheets are powerful but not infinite, and state capacity varies widely. In countries with weak or corrupt public institutions, state-led investment can become patronage, delay, or white elephants. The goal should not be to replace private coordination wholesale but to discipline it toward public ends.

Carbon pricing is a case in point. Many on the left distrust it because it appears technocratic, regressive, or insufficiently transformative. Bad carbon pricing can be all of those things. But pricing emissions remains one of the most efficient ways to encourage substitution across millions of decisions no planner can individually command. A carbon price with dividends or targeted rebates can protect lower-income households. Border adjustments can prevent leakage if designed fairly. Sectoral standards and public investment should accompany pricing, not replace it entirely. The climate movement’s allergy to market instruments has sometimes been self-defeating.

Similarly, permitting reform cannot be left to conservatives and corporate lobbyists. If we need massive transmission lines, geothermal projects, dense housing, rail, mines for transition minerals, and renewable generation, then legal systems designed primarily to slow construction are a problem. Environmental review has protected communities from real harms, but it has also been weaponized to block clean infrastructure and housing. A serious decarbonization politics must distinguish between democratic consent and procedural veto. If every project takes a decade, fossil incumbents win by default.

Táíwò is right that adding renewables is not the same as phasing out fossil fuels. But the relationship between addition and subtraction is complex. Cheap clean substitutes make fossil phaseout politically easier. Electric vehicles weaken oil demand. Heat pumps weaken gas utilities. Batteries and transmission weaken the claim that coal and gas are necessary for reliability. Alternative proteins may one day reduce land pressure and agricultural emissions. Industrial innovation can reduce dependence on coal-based steel and clinker-heavy cement. Technological optimism is not a substitute for politics, but political will is easier to sustain when clean options are affordable and effective.

This is why the essay’s skepticism toward “going electric” should be carefully bounded. Electrification is not the whole climate agenda, but it is central to any plausible one. A great deal of final energy use can be electrified, and clean electricity is the backbone of decarbonization. The task is not to mock electrification as technocratic fantasy but to make it universal, reliable, affordable, and clean. That requires grid modernization, storage, demand response, nuclear power where socially acceptable, geothermal, hydropower where appropriate, and perhaps carbon capture for hard-to-abate sectors. A rigid ideological preference for only some technologies will slow us down.

Nuclear energy illustrates the problem. Many progressive climate advocates remain opposed to it, often for understandable reasons: cost overruns, waste, safety, and association with militarism. Yet existing nuclear plants provide large quantities of firm low-carbon electricity. Premature closures have often increased fossil generation. New nuclear may be expensive, but advanced designs could play a role, especially in grids with high renewable penetration. A climate politics organized around defeating fossil capital should be open to any low-carbon tool that meets safety, cost, and democratic standards.

The same is true of carbon capture. It has been abused by fossil companies as a license to continue extraction. It should not justify new coal or indefinite oil and gas expansion. But some carbon management is likely needed for cement, industrial processes, and perhaps atmospheric removal. The correct response is governance, not blanket rejection. If fossil firms use carbon capture to delay, regulate them. But do not confuse the misuse of a technology with the technology’s possible role.

There is also a political risk in Táíwò’s language of total defeat. Durable climate policy requires majorities across regions and classes, including many people who work in or depend on fossil-linked industries. If the movement sounds as if it seeks to vanquish not only executives and shareholders but entire communities, backlash will be severe. The fossil industry has expertly exploited this fear. The answer is not softer policy but broader coalition: workers, utilities, automakers, farmers, builders, engineers, local governments, investors, and consumers all need a place in the transition.

This does not mean compromise with climate denial. It means refusing a politics in which virtue is measured by antagonistic posture. Some incumbents can be converted; others must be regulated into decline; still others will disappear. Utilities can become clean electricity providers. Automakers can produce EVs. Oil majors may not successfully transform themselves, but parts of their workforce and technical capacity may be useful in geothermal, offshore wind, hydrogen, or decommissioning. A strategy that treats all fossil-linked actors as a monolith may miss opportunities to split coalitions.

State capacity remains essential. Here I agree with Táíwò. Governments must set clean electricity standards, fund research, build transmission, support public transit, regulate methane, stop new unabated coal, protect vulnerable households, and finance adaptation. They must also coordinate internationally. But state action should be judged by outcomes, not ownership form. Public power can be excellent; it can also be slow, politicized, and resistant to innovation. Private utilities can be abusive; they can also be regulated into decarbonization. The question should always be: what institutional arrangement reduces emissions fastest while protecting justice and reliability?

Climate politics needs less romance—whether romance of the state, the market, or the movement—and more practical institutional design. The world must cut emissions rapidly while expanding energy access, maintaining reliability, and adapting to already locked-in warming. This is an engineering challenge, an economic challenge, and a political challenge. Fossil capital is an obstacle, but not the only one.

Táíwò’s essay usefully reminds us that power matters. Technocrats sometimes talk as if optimal policy will implement itself. It will not. But radicals can make the opposite mistake: talking as if naming the enemy solves the implementation problem. It does not. The energy transition will be won through a messy mix of confrontation and collaboration, rules and incentives, public investment and private execution, local consent and national planning, innovation and restraint.

Dethrone fossil capital where it blocks the future. But do not build a climate strategy around dethronement alone. The point is not to win a historical argument about capitalism and the state. The point is to decarbonize the world fast enough to preserve the possibility of decent politics. For that, we will need every tool that works.
