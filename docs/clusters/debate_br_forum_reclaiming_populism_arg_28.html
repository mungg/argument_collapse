"While reclaiming populism is a noble goal, the left should prioritize the slow work of building durable, base-level institutions like unions and tenant associations over the electoral and rhetorical focus that populist politics often entails."

It is a strange moment to reclaim populism. Adom Getachew’s lead essay, written in the shadow of the COVID-19 pandemic and the collapse of Bernie Sanders’s presidential campaign, issues a call to rethink and rehabilitate a political tradition that has, in recent years, been almost universally maligned. The forum she introduces is animated by a noble aspiration: to build a “left-led, multiracial working class alignment” capable of transforming American politics. As a project, this is both urgent and compelling. Yet as I read the essays and reflect on the strategic conundrums they raise, I find myself worrying that the very language of populism—even a reclaimed, progressive populism—may lead the left into a familiar dead end. Not because “the people” is an inherently dangerous signifier, but because the electoral and discursive focus that contemporary left populism often entails can obscure the slow, unglamorous work of building durable counter-power outside the state. If we want a multiracial working-class majority, we might need less talk about the people and more talk about the union card, the tenant association, the neighborhood assembly.

Getachew’s opening is powerful, in part because it grounds the discussion in a concrete victory: the Chicago Teachers Union strike of 2019. That strike, and the broader movement politics it represents, is a reminder that left populism at its best is not merely rhetorical. It is woven into institutions that can mobilize actual bodies, withhold labor, and extract concessions from capital and the state. The CTU, together with United Working Families and the Working Families Party, has attempted something genuinely new: a fusion of militant rank-and-file unionism with independent political action that challenges a moribund Democratic Party machine. This is the kind of “movement politics and electoral politics” combination that many of us hoped would scale upward, perhaps through a Sanders presidency. The failure of that effort, as Getachew notes, is now part of a global pattern, following the ouster of Evo Morales in Bolivia and the defeat of Jeremy Corbyn in the United Kingdom.

What are we to make of these defeats? The lead essay is careful not to draw a straight line from them to an indictment of populism itself. But the defeats demand a harder reckoning than Getachew’s brief summary allows. In each case, the left popular project was not simply destroyed by elite machinations or media bias, though those were present. Rather, each revealed structural weaknesses that are endemic to the populist form of politics when it is not sufficiently anchored in mass, self-organized institutions. Bolivia’s MAS, for all its genuine achievements in reducing poverty and empowering Indigenous communities, had become increasingly dependent on the person of Evo Morales and the state apparatus he controlled. When the military and the right wing moved against him, the movement’s capacity to resist was not as deep as its electoral victories suggested. In the UK, Corbyn’s Labour Party was hollowed out by internal sabotage, but also by the fact that the massive membership surge of 2015-2017 was never fully converted into a decentralized, movement-like structure capable of withstanding the Brexit cross-pressures and the relentless assault from the right-wing press. And in the United States, the Sanders campaign built a formidable small-donor machine and energized millions, but it repeatedly struggled to translate rally size into the kind of deep organizing that could win over suburban moderates or overcome the party’s institutional resistance. In all three cases, left populism was strong at the top—charismatic leaders, big rallies, national media attention—and relatively thin at the base.

This is not an argument against electoral politics. It is an argument against a certain kind of electoral obsession that treats the presidential race as the primary site of struggle and treats mass mobilization as an auxiliary to that race. The Chicago model, by contrast, suggests a different sequencing: build power in workplaces and communities, win concrete gains, and then use that power to elect your own to local and state office. That is a slow, iterative process. It does not lend itself to the dramatic language of “the people versus the oligarchy” that much left populist rhetoric favors. And it requires a different emotional register—one that embraces partial victories, boring committee meetings, and the kind of patient institution-building that can survive the loss of any single election.

Getachew wisely insists that the crisis of the pandemic “will not create the social transformation we seek, but it will set the terms for it.” I would go further: the terms it sets are deeply unfavorable to the kind of mass mobilizing that electoral populism demands. When millions are unemployed, isolated at home, and fearful of infection, the traditional tools of left organizing—packed union halls, mass demonstrations, door-to-door canvassing—become perilous. This does not render us powerless. Across the country, tenants’ unions are forming to demand rent freezes. Mutual aid networks are sprouting up to deliver groceries and medicine. Wildcat strikes are breaking out among essential workers at Amazon, Instacart, and meatpacking plants. These forms of resistance are not framed as a struggle of “the people.” They are specific, granular, and often defensive. They are also the seeds of a counter-power that could, in time, undergird a new political alignment.

The risk of a reclaimed populism, as I see it, is that it will continue to frame the problem primarily as one of representation—of speaking for the silenced majority, of breaking the grip of the “establishment.” But the crisis of American democracy is not just a crisis of representation; it is a crisis of organization. The working class, in all its multiracial complexity, has been systematically deorganized over the past half-century. Union density is at historic lows. Community institutions, from churches to block clubs, have atrophied. The social infrastructure of the left—newspapers, cultural organizations, educational institutions—is a shadow of what it once was. No amount of populist rhetoric can substitute for that missing tissue. In fact, the rhetoric can become an alibi: if we can just get our message right, if we can just rally the people, then the institutional deficits won’t matter. But they do matter. The populist interpellation of a unified “people” can paper over the fragmenting realities of precarious work, residential segregation, and the chasms of race and immigration status that the right so effectively exploits.

This is not to deny that there are moments when a broad, majoritarian discourse is necessary. The New Deal realignment, often cited as a model, certainly deployed the language of the “common man” and pitted the masses against the “economic royalists.” But that rhetoric was effective precisely because it was backed by mass organizations—the CIO unions, the unemployed councils, the agrarian movements—that had already built a dense infrastructure of solidarity and struggle. The New Deal did not create the labor movement; the labor movement created the political conditions for the New Deal. Today’s left populists sometimes imagine they can reverse that equation, using electoral campaigns to bootstrap a movement that does not yet exist. The Sanders campaign, for all its innovations, fell into this trap. It promised a “political revolution” but struggled to show what that revolution would look like beyond turning out votes for one man.

Where does this leave the WFP’s project of a left-led, multiracial working-class alignment? I remain deeply sympathetic to it. But I believe the path forward must decenter the language of populism and recenter the practice of organizing. The phrase “multiracial working class” is already more precise and more useful than “the people,” because it names an actual social formation—one that must be built, not simply invoked. Building it means confronting the deep racial fractures that have historically prevented poor and working people from acting in concert. This is not just a matter of having the right anti-racist line in your platform. It requires the painfully concrete work of bridging Black, Latino, Asian, and white communities that often live in separate neighborhoods, send their children to separate schools, and hold legitimate mutual suspicions. The CTU strike was a model precisely because it fought for demands—smaller class sizes, more nurses and social workers—that directly benefited the overwhelmingly Black and brown student population, while also improving working conditions for a largely white teaching force. It built a material solidarity, not just a symbolic one.

Similarly, any serious effort to realign American politics must grapple with the international dimensions that Getachew highlights. The pandemic has laid bare the brutal logic of global racial capitalism: Black and brown people dying disproportionately, essential workers exposed without protection, and the Global South outbid for ventilators and protective gear. A populism that remains bounded by the nation-state will inevitably reproduce the violent exclusions that define modern sovereignty. The “people” of the United States, even a progressive version, is always already constituted through borders, citizenship regimes, and imperial projects. To their credit, the forum’s authors seem aware of this tension. But I worry that the grammar of populism, with its necessary invocation of a unified demos, systematically obscures the transnational character of actually existing working classes—from the Filipina nurses staffing American hospitals to the undocumented farmworkers harvesting our food. A politics that takes its cue from these transnational realities would speak less about the American people and more about the global working class, and it would build organizational forms capable of acting across borders, like the cross-border solidarity networks that have emerged in the garment and logistics sectors.

None of this is to discard the insights of the lead essay. Getachew is right that populism is an enduring feature of democratic politics and that the left cannot afford to cede it to the right. But if we are to reclaim populism, we must do so in a way that refuses its temptations. We must reject the cult of the leader, the reduction of politics to electoral spectacle, and the false unity that erases rather than confronts the contradictions among the oppressed. A reclaimed left populism would be a modest, almost pedestrian thing: not the cry of the indignant crowd but the steady hum of organized workers, tenants, and neighbors deciding together what they need and how to get it. It would be populist only in the sense that the populism of the original People’s Party was rooted in farmer cooperatives, educational encampments, and local chapters—a dense network of self-governing institutions that outlasted the electoral campaigns. The tragedy of that first populist moment was that the movement was crushed and its fragments absorbed into a segregationist Democratic Party. The challenge of our moment is to build something that cannot be so easily co-opted or crushed. That means starting not with the candidate nor the slogan, but with the meeting in the church basement, the strike on the shop floor, the rent strike in the apartment complex. If a new populism is to be born, it will be born there, in the slow, patient work of organization, not in the heat of a campaign rally. And it may not need the name “populism” at all.
