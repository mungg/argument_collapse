"While Gilens correctly identifies that American policy is systematically biased toward the affluent, achieving political equality requires more than just electoral competition; it requires rebuilding countervailing institutions like labor unions to offset the structural advantages of organized economic power."

Martin Gilens’s findings are disturbing not because they tell us something wholly unexpected, but because they give empirical shape to what many Americans have come to suspect: public policy is not simply “out of touch” in some vague sense. It is systematically more attentive to those with money. That matters even when the affluent are public-spirited, even when their preferences coincide with those of everyone else, and even when they are right on the merits. Democracy cannot rest on the hope that the rich will often be benevolent.

The most important contribution of Gilens’s essay is to separate agreement from influence. Many defenders of the status quo point out that the United States has programs—Social Security, Medicare, public education, the mortgage interest deduction, veterans’ benefits—that enjoy broad cross-class support. True enough. But that tells us little about what happens when interests diverge. The test of political equality is not whether poor people sometimes get policies they like because rich people like them too. The test is whether they have independent weight when they disagree. Gilens’s answer is: usually not.

That answer helps explain several otherwise puzzling features of American politics. Why has the minimum wage been allowed to erode for long periods despite strong public support for raising it? Why has trade policy moved steadily toward liberalization even though working-class voters have often been skeptical? Why did financial deregulation proceed for decades with little effective popular scrutiny, only to be followed by a crisis whose costs were borne broadly while rescue efforts appeared to favor Wall Street? Why is “shared sacrifice” invoked so frequently when budget cuts are discussed, but so rarely when tax preferences for the affluent are on the table?

Gilens is right that campaign money is a plausible mechanism. In modern campaigns, politicians do not merely need votes; they need donors early, constantly, and in large quantities. A voter can punish you in November. A donor can determine whether you are viable in March. Before ordinary citizens have even begun paying attention, candidates have already passed through a money primary. Those who survive are rarely crude puppets of the wealthy. The process is subtler: viable candidates learn which ideas are “serious,” which reforms are “unrealistic,” and which constituencies must be reassured before any campaign can begin.

Still, I think Gilens may understate the role of organized economic power. He notes that not all interest groups represent the affluent and that some groups, such as the AARP or labor unions, often align with lower- and middle-income Americans. That is true, but it does not follow that interest-group politics balances out. Business interests possess advantages that are not reducible to campaign contributions: permanent Washington offices, technical expertise, revolving-door relationships, control over investment decisions, and the capacity to frame regulation as a threat to jobs. A union or citizens’ group must mobilize episodically. A regulated industry is always mobilized.

This asymmetry is especially important because so much policymaking is invisible. Survey questions usually concern salient policy choices: whether to raise taxes, increase spending, approve a trade agreement, regulate an industry. But the actual work of government often occurs in the details: rule-writing, enforcement budgets, definitions, exemptions, deadlines, administrative waivers, and committee negotiations. Affluent citizens and business interests do not need to win every public argument if they can shape the agenda before most people know an argument is occurring.

The decline of labor is therefore central. For much of the twentieth century, unions did more than bargain over wages. They translated diffuse economic discontent into political demands, supplied volunteers, educated voters, and gave politicians a reason to listen to working-class people between elections. Their weakening has not simply reduced one Democratic constituency. It has removed one of the few institutions capable of matching concentrated wealth with organized numbers. If we want representational equality, rebuilding countervailing institutions matters as much as regulating campaign spending.

Gilens’s discussion of competition is valuable but incomplete. Competitive elections can force politicians toward popular positions. Yet competition in a money-soaked system can also deepen dependence on donors. A vulnerable incumbent may spend more time fundraising, not less. A challenger in a competitive district may avoid offending affluent contributors, consultants, and media gatekeepers. Competition helps only if the electorate is actually activated and if candidates can run viable campaigns without first satisfying a narrow donor class.

That is why reforms should be judged by whether they change who participates, who pays, and who organizes. Public financing, especially systems that amplify small donations, can reduce dependence on large contributors. Disclosure rules can at least make influence visible. Nonpartisan redistricting may help where gerrymandering protects incumbents, though it is no cure for the Senate, the filibuster, or unequal turnout. Automatic voter registration, same-day registration, and nonpartisan get-out-the-vote efforts would make the electorate more representative. Labor-law reform and protection for worker organizing would strengthen a form of political participation rooted outside the donor class.

The point is not to make government a mechanical servant of opinion polls. Gilens rightly acknowledges that majorities can be wrong and that minority rights matter. But there is a difference between principled independence and class-skewed deafness. When politicians defy public opinion to protect civil rights, they may redeem democracy. When they defy it to protect carried interest, weaken workplace protections, or preserve tax advantages for those already thriving, they corrode it.

American democracy has always contained exclusions and inequalities. The hopeful fact is that these have been contested, and sometimes reduced, by organized movements: abolitionists, suffragists, labor organizers, civil-rights activists. Gilens’s research should be read in that tradition. It is not a counsel of despair. It is a map of where power has gone—and a reminder that political equality will not be restored by wishing elected officials would listen. It will require building institutions that make them listen.

"True political equality for low-income Americans requires the rebuilding of durable, mass-membership organizations rather than a simple increase in partisan competition or electoral reforms."

Martin Gilens’s essay should unsettle anyone who believes elections alone guarantee political equality. Yet one of his proposed sources of hope—more partisan competition—deserves a closer look. Competition can make politicians attentive, but it can also make them shallow, poll-driven, and dependent on money. The missing variable in Gilens’s account is not simply competition. It is organization.

The people who lose in Gilens’s story are not merely poorer individuals. They are people who lack durable institutions capable of turning preferences into power. Affluent Americans do not have to march in the streets or attend precinct meetings to be heard. They are embedded in networks of donors, professionals, trade associations, foundations, lobbyists, lawyers, and policy shops. Their preferences travel easily through the political system. Low-income Americans, by contrast, are often asked to express themselves only as isolated voters, and often only after candidates, agendas, and legislative options have already been shaped.

That is why the decline of mass-membership organizations is so damaging. Unions, churches, urban political machines, civil-rights organizations, veterans’ groups, and local party clubs once performed many functions we now expect campaigns to perform in a few frantic months. They recruited leaders, developed loyalties, educated members, negotiated with officeholders, and punished betrayal. Some were corrupt or exclusionary; none should be romanticized. But they gave ordinary citizens a way to act collectively. Without them, politics becomes a contest among professionals speaking through television advertisements financed by people with large checkbooks.

Gilens’s findings about Democrats are especially important here. Many liberals comfort themselves with the belief that the Democratic Party represents the working class. In some domains, particularly Social Security, Medicare, Medicaid, the minimum wage, and labor regulation, that remains partly true. But the national party has also grown comfortable with Wall Street money, free-trade assumptions, deregulation, and deficit politics that often narrow the range of economic debate. This is not simply a matter of bad faith. It is a matter of coalition structure. A party that relies on affluent social liberals, professional-class donors, and business-friendly policy experts will hear some voices more clearly than others, even while speaking in the name of “working families.”

Republicans have a parallel problem. They often mobilize middle- and lower-income voters around religion, guns, patriotism, and hostility to liberal elites, while pursuing tax and regulatory agendas most intensely desired by business and high-income constituencies. The Tea Party movement has shown genuine grassroots anger at bailouts, debt, and Washington insiderism. But its energy has often been channeled into policies—upper-end tax cuts, attacks on public-sector unions, resistance to financial regulation—that do little to increase the independent power of ordinary citizens.

For this reason, I am skeptical that nonpartisan redistricting and generic competitiveness will solve much. Competitive districts can improve accountability, and grotesquely gerrymandered seats are hard to defend. But a competitive campaign in an expensive media market may intensify the candidate’s dependence on donors. It may also encourage cautious centrism, which sounds democratic until one asks whose concerns define the “center.” If the choices placed before voters have already been filtered by donor acceptability, then a close election can legitimate a narrow agenda rather than broaden it.

Nor is gridlock a reliable democratic filter. Gilens argues that gridlock blocks unpopular policies more effectively than popular ones. Sometimes. But it can also preserve unpopular arrangements by making change impossible. If most Americans want higher taxes on millionaires, stronger financial regulation, or action on jobs, gridlock may frustrate them. The Senate filibuster, holds, committee chokepoints, and the malapportionment that gives small states disproportionate influence do not simply restrain reckless majorities. They create abundant opportunities for organized minorities to veto reform.

The better lesson to draw from Gilens’s evidence is that parties must become more programmatic and more socially rooted. By “programmatic,” I mean parties that present clear governing commitments rather than consultant-tested moods. By “socially rooted,” I mean parties connected to organizations that represent people outside election season. A party that depends on unions, tenant groups, small-donor networks, neighborhood associations, and churches will behave differently from one that depends primarily on bundlers and television buys. It will still compromise; democracy requires compromise. But it will compromise under different pressures.

This also means reformers should not focus only on campaign finance, though campaign finance matters. Public matching funds for small donations would help candidates build campaigns around many modest contributors rather than a few large ones. Disclosure is essential, especially after Citizens United opened new routes for corporate and union spending through outside groups. But the deeper reform is to make political participation less episodic. Same-day registration, early voting, weekend voting, and serious enforcement of voting rights can expand the electorate. Labor-law reform can make it less risky for workers to organize. Public support for civic associations, debates, and local journalism can strengthen the connective tissue of democracy.

We should also rethink the role of opinion itself. Gilens treats preferences as things citizens possess and government either follows or ignores. But preferences are shaped by conflict, leadership, and available alternatives. If no major party seriously argues for industrial policy, a financial transactions tax, sectoral bargaining, or large-scale public employment, then many citizens may never form stable views about those possibilities. Representation is not only responsiveness to existing opinion; it is the creation of meaningful choices.

This is why social movements matter. The civil-rights movement did not merely reveal majority preferences; it transformed them. The labor movement did not wait for benevolent elites to discover working-class opinion; it forced new issues onto the agenda. Occupy Wall Street, whatever its limitations, has already changed political language by making inequality and the “1 percent” central to national debate. Whether that language becomes policy depends on whether protest can become organization.

Gilens ends with guarded hope, and he is right to do so. American democracy has repeatedly been widened by people who were told that the system was too fixed to change. But the route to equal influence does not run only through closer elections. It runs through stronger collective institutions for those who currently enter politics alone. The affluent are powerful not just because they are rich, but because their wealth is organized. The democratic answer must be to organize everyone else.
