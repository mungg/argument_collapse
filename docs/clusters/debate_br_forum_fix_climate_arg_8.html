"While experimentalist governance and sectoral learning are valuable, they must be embedded within a binding global legal framework to ensure adequate ambition, accountability, and equitable burden-sharing."

David Victor and Charles Sabel make a compelling case against climate diplomacy as ritual. They are right that international climate negotiations have often produced the appearance of progress while emissions have continued to rise. They are also right that decarbonization is not accomplished by treaty language alone. Wind farms, electric vehicles, heat pumps, zero-carbon industrial processes, methane controls, and clean grids must be built in actual places by actual institutions. Learning matters. Sectoral policy matters. Experimentation matters.

But their essay goes too far in demoting global agreement. The conclusion that binding international commitments should be the “outcome” rather than the starting point of climate cooperation is attractive because it seems pragmatic. Yet it risks repeating, in a more sophisticated vocabulary, the weakness of the Paris Agreement itself: the hope that decentralized action will add up to adequate ambition without a sufficiently strong mechanism for distributing burdens, measuring performance, and compelling laggards.

The climate problem is not only a problem of uncertainty. It is also a problem of scarcity. The atmosphere has a limited remaining capacity to absorb greenhouse gases without pushing warming to dangerous levels. Every ton emitted by one actor reduces the space available to others. This is why climate change is different from many other environmental problems and why the global frame cannot be postponed. We do not need perfect certainty about every technology to know that the world must reduce emissions rapidly and reach net zero around mid-century to have a reasonable chance of meeting the Paris temperature goals. Nor do we need to know every national pathway to know that current pledges are insufficient.

Victor and Sabel are understandably frustrated by universal negotiations in which the least ambitious parties water down the result. But global climate diplomacy did not fail only because it started too globally. It failed because governments were unwilling to make commitments commensurate with the scale of the problem, and because the institutional design did not create enough consequences for failure. That is an argument for stronger international rules, not for treating them as a distant endpoint.

Their use of the Montreal Protocol illustrates both the power and the limits of their argument. Montreal did indeed rely on sectoral learning, expert panels, adjustment, and financial assistance. But it was also a legally binding treaty with phaseout schedules, trade restrictions, reporting obligations, and a funding mechanism. Its experimentalism was embedded in law. Countries did not merely volunteer to search for substitutes; they accepted obligations to reduce and eventually eliminate ozone-depleting substances. The process was adaptive, but the direction was mandatory.

That combination is precisely what climate policy needs. The lesson of Montreal is not that global commitments are premature. It is that binding commitments should be designed to learn. They should be flexible in method but firm in destination. They should allow adjustment when genuine technical barriers arise, but they should not allow governments or industries to relabel inaction as experimentation.

The danger in the Victor-Sabel approach is that it may give too much comfort to incrementalism. Climate change is governed by physical thresholds and cumulative emissions. A regime that learns beautifully but slowly may still fail. There is a moral and scientific difference between discovering in 2040 how to decarbonize a sector and deploying that knowledge fast enough to avoid decades of additional emissions. Experimental governance must operate under a clock.

This is especially important for long-lived infrastructure. Coal plants, gas pipelines, liquefied natural gas terminals, highways, inefficient buildings, and industrial facilities built today can lock in emissions for decades. If governments wait for sectoral consensus before imposing hard constraints, they will permit investments that make later action more expensive and politically difficult. In this context, a global commitment to end unabated coal power, halt routine flaring, eliminate fossil fuel subsidies, and stop financing high-carbon infrastructure abroad would not be a technocratic fantasy. It would be a necessary guardrail.

The same is true of finance. Capital markets are global. Fossil fuel companies raise money across borders. Banks and asset managers finance emissions far from their home jurisdictions. If climate governance remains primarily sectoral and experimental, financial actors will exploit gaps between regimes. A serious climate strategy requires common disclosure rules, stress testing, fiduciary standards, and eventually restrictions on financing new high-carbon assets. These rules cannot be built only from local experiments. They require international coordination among central banks, financial regulators, development banks, and finance ministries.

Victor and Sabel are also too quick to describe Paris as hamstrung by its country-based structure. It is true that emissions reductions happen in sectors. But countries remain the primary units of law, taxation, public investment, welfare policy, and democratic accountability. Steel decarbonization requires industrial firms, but it also requires electricity policy, trade policy, labor policy, research funding, permitting, procurement, and perhaps carbon border adjustments. These are national and regional governmental choices. The sectoral lens is indispensable, but it cannot replace the sovereign one.

Indeed, one reason the Paris Agreement matters is that it creates a recurring political moment in which national governments must state their plans and face comparison. Its nationally determined contributions are too weak, but the architecture is not useless. It invites domestic actors—courts, legislatures, cities, investors, activists, firms—to ask whether national policy aligns with declared goals. In some countries, climate litigation has already begun to use governmental commitments as standards against which policy can be judged. In others, elections increasingly turn on climate plans. Paris does not by itself decarbonize the world, but it supplies a common language of accountability.

The task, then, is not to move “beyond Paris” so much as to harden Paris. The agreement’s pledge-and-review model should evolve toward clearer benchmarks: near-term emissions cuts, sectoral milestones, adaptation finance, methane reductions, coal phaseout dates, and standards for net-zero claims. The global stocktake should not be a diplomatic recital but a confrontation with arithmetic. Countries should be expected to explain how their policies match their fair share of the global effort. Wealthy countries should be pressed to deliver finance, not merely announce aspirations. Major emitters should face reputational, trade, and financial consequences when they expand fossil fuel production while professing climate ambition.

Some will object that this is politically impossible. Perhaps. But the same can be said of rapid sectoral decarbonization. The point of international law is not only to codify what is already easy; it is to alter expectations and empower domestic coalitions. Human rights treaties do not end repression, but they provide tools for dissidents. Trade agreements do not eliminate conflict, but they shape domestic policy. Climate agreements can similarly shift the political terrain, even when compliance is imperfect.

Victor and Sabel’s emphasis on “penalty defaults” is useful here. But those penalties need not arise only from sectoral clubs or market exclusion. They can also be built into global commitments. Trade measures against high-carbon goods, restrictions on international public finance for fossil fuels, procurement standards for low-carbon materials, and common rules against methane leakage can all function as penalties for laggards. The more legitimate and transparent the international framework, the less likely such measures are to be dismissed as arbitrary protectionism.

A stronger global regime would also protect against an inequitable patchwork. Sectoral clubs formed by rich countries and leading firms could accelerate innovation, but they could also set standards that poorer producers cannot meet, thereby excluding them from markets. If a green steel club defines acceptable production methods without financing technology transfer, it may become a climate-friendly version of industrial dominance. If shipping standards raise costs without support for vulnerable economies, they may shift burdens onto those least responsible for warming. Global rules are needed not only to discipline laggards but to discipline leaders.

That is why the finance question is central. Montreal’s Multilateral Fund was not just a pragmatic inducement; it was a justice mechanism. Climate change requires something vastly larger: grants, concessional finance, technology cooperation, and support for adaptation and loss prevention. Without credible finance, developing countries will understandably view calls for rapid decarbonization as an attempt to constrain their development after rich countries used up most of the atmospheric space. Experimentalism cannot solve that distributional conflict by itself.

Nor can it answer the question of ambition. A sectoral process may reveal what is feasible. It does not determine what is fair. Should the United States cut faster than India? Should Europe finance coal retirement in South Africa? Should China peak emissions earlier in exchange for technology access? Should oil-producing states receive transition support? These are political questions about responsibility, capacity, and development. They require negotiation at the global level.

To be clear, the old model of climate diplomacy is not worth defending. A treaty that pretends all nations can be assigned precise targets from above and then comply through sheer legal formality is a fantasy. The Kyoto approach was too narrow, too rigid, and too dependent on a division between developed and developing countries that could not endure. Paris corrected some of those flaws by broadening participation and allowing national flexibility. Victor and Sabel rightly push further, toward practical problem solving.

But the pendulum should not swing too far. Climate change demands both experimentation and obligation. It requires institutions that can learn, but also institutions that can say no: no to new unabated coal, no to indefinite methane leakage, no to fraudulent offsets, no to endless fossil fuel expansion, no to wealthy countries evading finance commitments. Sectoral governance can discover pathways. Global law must set the pace and allocate responsibility.

The world does not need another grand bargain detached from reality. But neither can it afford a thousand experiments without a binding framework strong enough to make them converge. The right lesson from Montreal is not that global agreement should wait until innovation has succeeded. It is that global agreement should be designed to force innovation, fund transition, and adjust intelligently as knowledge improves. Paris is too weak, but it is the beginning of that project, not a mere backdrop to it.
