"Universities must reconcile the right to protest with civil rights obligations by replacing subjective, donor-driven 'safety' standards with objective, conduct-based criteria for discriminatory harassment."

The most urgent question raised by Alex Gourevitch’s essay is not whether the First Amendment protects offensive protest. In many public university contexts, it does. Nor is it whether private universities should honor similar commitments. They should. The more difficult question is how to reconcile protest rights with civil rights obligations without turning either into a weapon against the other.

Gourevitch’s answer is to exclude protest, or nearly exclude it, from the hostile environment framework. He argues that protest is a distinctive form of political expression: public, hostile, accusatory, and often uncomfortable. If subjective feelings of fear or hurt are enough to trigger antidiscrimination intervention, protest becomes impossible. This is a powerful warning, especially in light of federal pressure on universities and the use of antisemitism allegations to suppress pro-Palestinian advocacy.

But the relationship between hostile environment law and protest cannot be resolved quite so cleanly. A hostile environment is not established merely because someone feels unsafe. On that Gourevitch is plainly right. But neither is it limited to imminent threats or individualized harassment. Civil rights law has long recognized that discriminatory exclusion can arise from a pattern of conduct that is severe or pervasive enough to deny equal access. The conduct may include words, symbols, and repeated public acts. Context matters.

That means two errors must be avoided.

The first error, now widespread, is to treat political protest against Israel as presumptively discriminatory against Jews or Israelis. That move is legally and morally disastrous. It chills debate about a foreign state, U.S. policy, war, occupation, human rights, and university investments. It also ignores the diversity of Jewish opinion. Many Jews oppose Israel’s war in Gaza; some oppose Zionism; many support protest demands; many others find those views painful or wrong. Universities cannot define Jewish identity through the politics of the most institutionally powerful Jewish organizations.

The second error is to imagine that protest can never create or contribute to a discriminatory environment. Suppose a campus protest movement repeatedly follows individual Jewish students, calls them “baby killers,” blocks them from entering buildings unless they denounce Israel, vandalizes Jewish spaces, and circulates lists of “Zionists” to be shunned. The fact that the movement frames its conduct as antiwar protest would not immunize it. Similarly, pro-Israel students who target Palestinian classmates as terrorists, celebrate mass death in Gaza, or harass Muslim student groups cannot hide behind political expression.

The distinction must turn on conduct, targeting, repetition, and access—not on the emotional valence of the political message.

This distinction is what many universities failed to maintain. Under pressure from donors, lawsuits, Congress, and now the Trump administration, they have often substituted institutional risk management for legal judgment. Rather than asking whether particular students were denied equal access through severe, pervasive, objectively offensive conduct, they asked whether influential constituencies perceived the campus as unsafe. That is not civil rights enforcement. It is reputational triage.

The examples Gourevitch quotes from the Harvard Jewish Alumni Alliance report illustrate the problem. A student cannot focus because people in keffiyehs are nearby writing an op-ed. Another uses a side entrance to avoid a protest. Another wakes up afraid on hearing chants. These are real experiences of distress. They are not, without more, evidence of unlawful harassment. If they became sufficient to discipline protesters, then almost any passionate demonstration by or against any identity-linked cause could be suppressed.

The asymmetry is striking. Muslim and Palestinian students have also reported high levels of fear, self-censorship, and alienation. Many have watched universities swiftly condemn antisemitism while speaking evasively about anti-Palestinian racism and Islamophobia. Some have been doxxed or threatened for advocacy. If subjective fear is the measure, their claims should have generated comparable institutional intervention. They generally have not. This selectivity reveals the danger of abandoning objective standards: power fills the vacuum.

Yet Gourevitch’s proposed threshold—likely and imminent threat, or direct and individualized harassment and intimidation—may be too narrow for the educational civil rights context. A student need not wait until danger is imminent to seek protection from discriminatory harassment. A hostile environment can be cumulative. A university should be able to respond before a pattern escalates into violence. The key is that the response must be evidence-based, targeted, and compatible with free expression.

What would that look like in practice?

First, universities should explicitly protect political speech about Israel, Palestine, Zionism, Hamas, war, occupation, genocide, apartheid, terrorism, and U.S. foreign policy, including speech that is harsh, one-sided, or offensive. This protection should include slogans unless they are used in context to threaten or target individuals.

Second, universities should define discriminatory harassment narrowly: conduct directed at persons or groups based on protected status that is severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive enough to deny equal access to educational programs or activities. Feelings matter as evidence of impact, but they cannot substitute for an objective inquiry.

Third, universities should separate protest rule violations from discrimination violations. An overnight encampment without a permit may violate a time, place, and manner rule. That does not make it antisemitic, Islamophobic, or racist. Conversely, a student can engage in discriminatory harassment even while complying with sound limits and lawn-use policies. Conflating these categories invites abuse.

Fourth, sanctions should be individualized. If a protest includes both protected speech and unprotected conduct, punish the unprotected conduct. Do not dissolve the whole protest unless there is no less restrictive way to address a serious, ongoing problem. This principle is especially important because large demonstrations inevitably include varied participants and messages.

Fifth, universities should create independent review processes insulated from trustees, donors, political officials, and public relations staff. The credibility of civil rights enforcement depends on independence. When discipline appears to follow a congressional hearing or a donor revolt, trust collapses.

Sixth, the federal government should enforce Title VI through regular, transparent procedures, not through threats that resemble ideological extortion. The Trump administration’s suspension of funds to Columbia and threats to other universities are ominous not because antisemitism is unworthy of federal concern, but because civil rights law is being used as a lever to reshape universities politically. A government may require compliance with law. It may not demand submission to a partisan “counterrevolution blueprint.”

Finally, universities should stop promising emotional safety. They cannot deliver it. Political life includes grief, anger, offense, and fear. What universities can promise is equal access, physical safety, fair procedures, and protection from targeted harassment. That more modest promise is also more defensible.

Gourevitch is right that democracy requires discomfort. But civil rights require more than telling students to develop thicker skins. The real task is to protect both: the ability of protesters to confront power and the ability of students from vulnerable groups to participate in campus life without being targeted or excluded. That task is difficult because speech can wound, identity and politics overlap, and institutions are tempted by shortcuts.

The shortcuts have failed. Police raids have not made campuses safer. Expulsions have not clarified principles. Congressional theater has not protected Jews. Donor panic has not protected Palestinians. Broad speech codes have not produced equality. They have produced fear, resentment, and opportunism.

A better settlement is possible: strong protest rights, narrow harassment rules, equal concern for all affected groups, due process, and resistance to state coercion. That settlement will disappoint maximalists on every side. It will not satisfy those who want anti-Zionism banned as antisemitism. It will not satisfy those who want all protest conduct immunized by moral urgency. But it offers the best chance of preserving both academic freedom and civil rights in a moment when each is being invoked to destroy the other.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A hostile environment should not be defined by subjective feelings of distress, but rather by a pattern of severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive conduct that denies equal access to education.
  2. 02Political speech regarding Israel and Palestine must be explicitly protected and distinguished from discriminatory harassment, regardless of how offensive or one-sided it may be.
  3. 03Universities should separate the adjudication of protest rule violations, such as encampment permits, from the adjudication of civil rights violations like antisemitism or Islamophobia.
  4. 04Disciplinary actions must be individualized to address specific unprotected conduct rather than used as collective punishment to dissolve entire protest movements.
  5. 05To maintain institutional integrity, universities must insulate their disciplinary and civil rights review processes from the influence of trustees, donors, and partisan government pressure.
  6. 06Universities should stop promising students emotional safety from political discomfort and instead focus on guaranteeing physical safety, equal access, and fair procedures.
