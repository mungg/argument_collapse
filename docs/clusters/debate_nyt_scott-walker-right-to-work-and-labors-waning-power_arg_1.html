"Right-to-work laws are not worthwhile because they are designed to reduce collective bargaining power at a time when labor power is already historically weak and economic inequality is high."

“Right to work” is an appealing slogan for a narrow and misleading policy. No one should be forced to join a union, and under federal law workers already cannot be compelled to become union members. What these laws prohibit is a contract requiring represented workers to pay a fee for the bargaining and grievance services the union must provide to them. That creates a classic free-rider problem: employees may receive negotiated wages, benefits and representation without helping finance them. Over time, that weakens unions not by proving they are unwanted, but by making collective bargaining harder to sustain.

The economic case for right-to-work laws is also overstated. Supporters point to faster job growth in many Southern and Western right-to-work states, but those states often differ in many other ways: lower housing costs, warmer climates, faster population growth, different industrial mixes and lower taxes. It is difficult to isolate right-to-work laws as the cause. Meanwhile, the wage evidence is more troubling. Unionized workers generally earn more than comparable nonunion workers, and unions have historically raised standards beyond their own membership by setting norms for pay, benefits, safety and due process. Weakening bargaining power is therefore likely to reduce wages or benefits, especially for workers without college degrees.

That matters because the United States is already experiencing unusually weak labor power. Private-sector union membership has fallen to below 7 percent. Productivity and corporate profits have risen far more robustly than typical wages. Many workers face unpredictable schedules, insecure pensions and rising health costs. In that environment, further reducing workers’ ability to bargain collectively is not a recipe for broadly shared prosperity.

This does not mean unions deserve immunity from criticism. Some have protected poor performance, resisted necessary change or become too close to political machines. Public-sector bargaining raises different questions than private-sector bargaining, especially when elected officials negotiate with unions that support campaigns. But the remedy for union flaws should be greater transparency, internal democracy and labor-law modernization, not starving unions of resources while still requiring them to represent everyone in a bargaining unit.

Right-to-work laws may modestly please some employers and some workers who dislike unions. But as economic policy, they are a blunt instrument aimed less at individual liberty than at reducing collective power. At a time when bargaining power is already tilted heavily toward employers, they are not worthwhile.

Vanilla Claude

"Right-to-work laws are not worthwhile because they lack a strong economic basis and serve primarily to weaken labor unions at a time of record income inequality."

Right-to-work laws are not really about workers' rights; they are about altering the balance of power between organized labor and capital, and on that score they are doing exactly what their architects intend. Whether that is "worthwhile" depends on what kind of economy and polity we want.

The economic case made by Governor Walker and his allies is surprisingly thin. The states that have seen the strongest manufacturing rebounds since 2009 — including Michigan before it flipped in 2012, Ohio, and Indiana — were union states. Cross-state studies by economists at the Economic Policy Institute and others consistently find that, after controlling for cost of living, education, and industry mix, right-to-work states have wages roughly 3 percent lower than comparable non-RTW states, with smaller shares of workers receiving employer-sponsored health insurance and pensions. The Congressional Research Service's own surveys of the literature conclude that evidence of net job creation is weak; firms appear to relocate within the country, not expand overall employment.

What right-to-work clearly does accomplish is to starve unions of resources. Because the National Labor Relations Act already requires a union to represent every worker in a bargaining unit, RTW laws engineer a textbook free-rider problem: workers can enjoy union-negotiated wages and grievance protection without paying for them. Predictably, dues income falls, organizing budgets shrink, and political spending — overwhelmingly Democratic — declines. Walker's 2011 Act 10, which gutted public-sector bargaining, cut AFSCME's Wisconsin membership in half within two years. This week's law extends that logic to the private sector.

One can favor that outcome on ideological grounds — believing individual workers should never be compelled to support an organization — but it should be argued honestly. The libertarian principle has real force; the growth-and-jobs claim does not.

My own view is that right-to-work laws are not worthwhile. At a moment when labor's share of national income is at a postwar low, median wages have stagnated for a generation, and inequality is the defining political question, deliberately weakening the one institution that historically gave non-college workers bargaining leverage seems exactly backwards.

"Right-to-work laws are detrimental because they are designed to weaken unions and lower wages rather than deliver on their promises of economic growth."

The promise of right-to-work laws is a classic bait and switch: proponents pledge economic dynamism, but the evidence from the 24 states that previously adopted such measures tells a sobering story of stagnant wages, weakened worker voice, and an illusory jobs miracle. These laws are not about freedom; they are a deliberate strategy to starve unions of resources and, in doing so, tilt the economic playing field further against working people.

At the heart of the issue is the free-rider problem. Federal law requires unions to represent all workers in a bargaining unit, whether they join or not. Right-to-work laws make it illegal to require non-members to pay even a fair-share fee for the cost of that representation. The inevitable result is a slow bleed of union finances, as rational individuals opt out of dues while still enjoying the wages, benefits, and grievance procedures that collective bargaining secures. This is not a hypothetical concern. A 2011 study by Gordon Lafer and Sylvia Allegretto found that right-to-work laws are associated with a significant decline in unionization rates, a drop that directly saps labor’s ability to negotiate from a position of strength.

And when unions weaken, the damage radiates outward. Wages fall—not just for union members, but for all workers in the state. Research from the Economic Policy Institute shows that workers in right-to-work states earn, on average, 3.2 percent less than comparable workers in non-right-to-work states, even after controlling for factors like industry, education, and cost of living. The wage penalty is especially acute for middle-income families. Meanwhile, the supposed job-growth bonus is largely a mirage. Companies might relocate to right-to-work states, but they are often lured by the prospect of a cheaper, more compliant workforce, not by a more productive one. The resulting jobs tend to be lower-paying, with fewer benefits, and the broader economic “lift” fails to materialize; on most measures of economic health, from per capita income to poverty rates, right-to-work states lag behind.

Wisconsin’s move is particularly instructive. Governor Walker had already eviscerated public-sector collective bargaining with Act 10, claiming it would spur a hiring boom. Instead, the state’s job growth has trailed the national recovery. Extending the same logic to the private sector only deepens the false choice between a paycheck and a voice on the job. A healthy democracy and a vibrant middle class depend on institutions that allow ordinary people to counterbalance concentrated corporate power. Right-to-work laws dismantle those institutions, replacing the prospect of shared prosperity with a race to the bottom.

"Right-to-work laws are a distracting and potentially harmful response to the decline of labor power, which is actually caused by an outdated labor relations system that no longer fits the modern economy."

Right-to-work laws are neither the economic cure promised by their supporters nor the sole cause of labor’s decline feared by their opponents. They are a blunt answer to a deeper problem: the American system of labor relations no longer fits the economy it is supposed to govern.

The current model depends on workplace-by-workplace organizing, exclusive representation and adversarial bargaining. That system made more sense when large firms operated stable plants with long-term employees. It works poorly in an economy of subcontracting, franchises, temp agencies, global supply chains and high turnover. Private-sector union density has been falling for decades in states with and without right-to-work. Wisconsin’s law may accelerate that trend, but it did not create it.

Still, right-to-work is not harmless. If unions are legally required to represent all workers in a bargaining unit, then allowing some workers to pay nothing undermines the institution. It is like allowing residents to opt out of paying condo fees while still using the elevator and expecting the roof to be repaired. The free-rider problem is real, and it weakens unions’ ability to enforce contracts. A contract that exists only on paper is not much protection.

At the same time, labor cannot simply defend the old arrangement and expect public sympathy to return. Many workers have never belonged to a union. Some see unions as bureaucratic, partisan or protective of insiders. Others fear that traditional bargaining can make firms less flexible. Those perceptions are not always fair, but they are politically important. A labor movement that depends on compulsory fees without visible member engagement is vulnerable.

The better path would be to modernize labor law. We should experiment with forms of worker representation that do not depend entirely on single-workplace majority elections: sectoral wage boards, works councils, portable benefits, easier voluntary recognition, and bargaining structures that include contractors and franchised workers. We should also enforce wage-and-hour laws more aggressively, because many low-wage workers are never going to be protected by conventional union contracts under the current system.

So are right-to-work laws worthwhile? As policy, they are mostly a distraction and often a weapon. They may give some individual workers more choice, but they do so by destabilizing a bargaining framework that already rests on fragile foundations. The real choice is not between compulsory unionism and right-to-work. It is between updating worker power for a changed economy or watching it continue to erode while both sides fight old battles.

"Right-to-work laws are a trivial distraction from the necessary task of modernizing labor laws to fit a 21st-century economy where traditional union structures are increasingly irrelevant."

The question "Are right-to-work laws worthwhile?" assumes we should be debating right-to-work at all. We shouldn't. It's a sideshow. The real story is that American labor law, written for the industrial economy of the 1930s, no longer fits the economy we have, and both parties are squabbling over the rusted-out machinery instead of building something new.

Consider the landscape. Private-sector union density is 6.6 percent and falling. The fastest-growing parts of the workforce — home health aides, fast food workers, Uber drivers, freelance designers — are almost entirely outside the Wagner Act framework. Many of them aren't even classified as "employees." Whether or not Wisconsin allows agency fees in unionized shops does precisely nothing for the 94 percent of private-sector workers who have no union to pay or not pay.

Meanwhile, the genuinely important developments in worker power are happening outside traditional collective bargaining. The Fight for $15 has moved minimum wages in Seattle, San Francisco, and Los Angeles. Worker centers like ROC United and the National Domestic Workers Alliance are organizing the un-organizable. Cities are passing fair-scheduling ordinances. The NLRB is wrestling with whether McDonald's is a "joint employer" with its franchisees — a far more consequential question than any state-level right-to-work fight.

What would a serious labor policy look like? Probably some version of what Germany or the Nordic countries have: sector-wide bargaining that sets wage floors across an entire industry, so that Walmart can't undercut a unionized grocer by paying poverty wages. It would also include portable benefits that follow workers between gigs and jobs, since the single-employer career is largely dead. And it would update the definition of "employee" to cover the millions of misclassified workers in the on-demand economy.

Against that backdrop, right-to-work is a trivial fight. It will modestly weaken the unions that still exist in manufacturing and construction in Wisconsin. Wages for those workers will drift down a couple of percentage points. A few firms may relocate. None of it will alter the trajectory of American inequality or the underlying decline of worker power, both of which are driven by forces — technology, trade, the rise of shareholder capitalism — that have nothing to do with Section 14(b) of the Taft-Hartley Act.

So: not worthwhile, but not catastrophic either. The tragedy isn't that Wisconsin passed right-to-work. It's that this is the labor debate we're having in 2015.

Right-to-work laws are not about freedom; they are about power. The conservative governors and legislatures pushing these laws — Walker in Wisconsin, Snyder in Michigan, Daniels and Pence in Indiana — understand exactly what they are doing. By starving unions of the dues that sustain them, they weaken the single most effective institution working people have for bargaining over wages, benefits, and working conditions. That is the whole point.

The "freedom" framing is a clever piece of rhetoric, but it falls apart on inspection. No worker in America is forced to join a union. Under the Supreme Court's 1988 Beck decision, workers in unionized shops who object to political spending can already get a refund of those portions of their dues. What right-to-work laws actually prohibit is "fair share" or "agency" fees — modest payments to cover the cost of contract negotiation and grievance representation that unions are legally obligated to provide to all workers in the bargaining unit, members or not. In other words, these laws engineer a free-rider problem and then point to the resulting weakness of unions as proof they were never wanted.

Studies from the Economic Policy Institute and the Center for Economic and Policy Research find that workers in right-to-work states earn roughly 3 percent less, all else equal, and are less likely to have employer-provided health insurance and pensions. But the deeper issue is what the decline of unions has meant for American inequality. The share of private-sector workers in unions has fallen from roughly a third in the 1950s to under 7 percent today, and that collapse tracks closely with the stagnation of median wages and the soaring share of income going to the top 1 percent. Economists like Bruce Western and Jake Rosenfeld have argued persuasively that de-unionization explains a substantial fraction of the rise in wage inequality since 1973.

Right-to-work laws accelerate this trend. They may sound reasonable to voters who haven't thought hard about labor economics, but their function is to lock in a low-wage, low-bargaining-power model of growth. Wisconsin will likely get a few more warehouses and a lot more workers wondering why their paychecks aren't growing. Worthwhile? Only if you think labor's waning power is something to celebrate.
