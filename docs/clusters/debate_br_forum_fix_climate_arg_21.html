"While experimentalist governance offers a superior institutional framework for climate action compared to traditional diplomacy, its success depends on first building the necessary political will and addressing global equity concerns."

Victor and Sabel have written an essay that deserves credit for intellectual honesty. Rather than calling for more ambitious global targets or more binding commitments—the standard prescription for failed climate diplomacy—they ask what actually works and why. Their answer, experimentalist governance, is grounded in comparative institutional analysis and tested against historical evidence. This is the right approach, and the essay deserves serious engagement from anyone concerned with climate policy.

Yet I want to raise a concern that sits beneath the essay's framework: the assumption that the primary obstacle to climate action is institutional design rather than political will. Victor and Sabel document decades of failure in global climate diplomacy and correctly identify governance pathologies as central to that failure. But governance pathologies are themselves products of political dynamics—the power of fossil fuel interests, the short-termism of electoral politics, the divergence between global costs and local benefits. Fixing governance without fixing these underlying politics leaves the fundamental problem unaddressed.

Consider the Montreal Protocol, which the essay rightly celebrates. What made Montreal possible was not clever institutional design but a specific political configuration: American leadership under a Republican administration that was willing and able to impose costs on noncompliant actors, an industry structure in which major players saw profit in alternatives, and a manageable set of chemicals whose sources and substitutes were well understood. These conditions do not currently exist for climate change. The United States is polarized on climate policy; the fossil fuel industry remains politically powerful; and the technologies for deep decarbonization are often not yet competitive. Institutional innovation cannot substitute for the political work of creating conditions analogous to those that enabled Montreal.

This suggests that experimentalist governance, as Victor and Sabel describe it, is better understood as a complement to political mobilization than a substitute for it. The framework provides tools for organizing sectoral efforts, creating learning mechanisms, and applying pressure to laggards. But it does not generate the political will necessary to use these tools aggressively. That will must come from movements, elections, and sustained advocacy.

The essay is weakest on the question of how experimentalist governance would be constructed in the current political environment. Victor and Sabel note that "we must build institutions for learning under uncertainty as rapidly as possible" and that "global agreements will not be the engine for change." But they do not specify who will build these institutions, under what political conditions, or with what resources. This is not a fatal flaw—the essay's primary contribution is analytical rather than prescriptive—but it leaves a gap between diagnosis and program.

I also want to note what the essay gets exactly right: the critique of the top-down/bottom-up dichotomy that has paralyzed climate diplomacy. Paris was adopted in reaction to Kyoto's failure, which was understood as a failure of top-down binding commitments. Paris's voluntarism was thus presented as the opposite of Kyoto's approach. But the opposite of a failed approach is not necessarily a successful one; it may simply be a different failure mode. Victor and Sabel's observation that "the opposite of a failure does not make a success" applies not just to Paris but to much of climate discourse, which has oscillated between binding commitments and voluntary approaches without finding a third way.

The Dewey quotation is well chosen. Dewey's pragmatism emphasized that democratic governance must integrate expert knowledge with citizen participation. The Montreal Protocol's sectoral committees approximated this integration by bringing together industry, academia, and regulators. Climate governance needs more of this kind of working collaboration and less of the formal diplomacy that has dominated to date.

My final concern is about equity. Victor and Sabel note that the Multilateral Fund helped developing countries participate in Montreal by covering compliance costs. They do not ask whether this arrangement was sufficient or equitable. In practice, Montreal's framework allowed wealthy countries to maintain their technological advantages while requiring developing countries to adopt standards set by others. The technology transfer was real, but it reinforced rather than challenged existing patterns of dependency.

Climate change raises these equity concerns more acutely. Deep decarbonization in developing countries cannot be achieved through technology transfer alone; it requires comprehensive transformation of energy systems, industrial processes, and economic structures. The investment needs are staggering. Unless wealthy nations commit to financing this transformation at scales comparable to the problem, experimentalist governance in the Global South will remain incomplete.

Victor and Sabel might respond that experimentalism's emphasis on local adaptation and capacity-building addresses these concerns. Perhaps. But local adaptation without adequate resources is adaptation to poverty rather than adaptation to climate. A truly equitable experimentalist framework would combine local flexibility with global solidarity—recognizing that decarbonization is a shared responsibility requiring burden-sharing commensurate with capacity.

Despite these concerns, I endorse the essay's core argument. Global climate diplomacy has failed because it was designed around assumptions—universal binding commitments, formal consensus, top-down implementation—that do not match the nature of the problem. Experimentalist governance offers an alternative: thin consensus on goals, devolved problem-solving, peer review, and penalty defaults that make obstruction costly. This framework has worked in domains like ozone protection and water quality; it might work for climate change.

But "might" is the operative word. The essay provides a vision of what successful climate governance could look like. Building the political conditions for that vision is the work that remains. It is the harder work, and the essay's silence on how to accomplish it is its most significant limitation.

  2. 02The success of the Montreal Protocol was driven by specific political conditions—including American leadership and industry cooperation—that do not currently exist for the broader climate crisis.
  3. 03Experimentalist governance should be viewed as a complement to, rather than a substitute for, political mobilization and grassroots advocacy.
  4. 04The proposed framework lacks a clear strategy for how these new institutions will be funded and constructed within the current polarized political environment.
