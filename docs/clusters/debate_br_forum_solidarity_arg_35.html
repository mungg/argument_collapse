Diversified Gemini

"While social endurance and productive conflict are necessary for solidarity, they can only be transformative and sustainable if they are mediated by robust democratic institutions and formal decision-making procedures."

In Mie Inouye’s compelling essay “Solidarity Now,” she correctly identifies that the Left’s current reliance on “deference politics” is a dead end. Deference, as a crude application of standpoint theory, flattens individuals into avatars of their demographic categories and treats organizing as a mere redistribution of speaking time within a room. Inouye’s alternative—a commitment to “social endurance” and productive conflict—is a vast improvement. Yet, her analysis overlooks the structural prerequisites for productive conflict. Conflict is only transformative when it is mediated by democratic institutions.

Inouye draws heavily on Nathan DuFord’s *Solidarity in Conflict*, arguing that internal conflict prevents organizations from reproducing exclusion and domination. She also notes, correctly, that neoliberalism prefers a false consensus where everyone leaves each other alone. Thus, engaging in conflict is an anti-neoliberal act. But Inouye struggles to differentiate between "constructive" and "destructive" conflict, ultimately concluding that we must simply "keep showing up even when doing so is unpleasant."

This is a failure of institutional imagination. The reason conflict in left-wing spaces so often devolves into toxic, atomizing feuds is not simply that activists lack "endurance." It is because these spaces often lack robust democratic structures to resolve disputes and bind the minority to the majority's decision.

Consider a labor union. When a union debates whether to accept a contract or go on strike, the conflict can be incredibly intense. But the conflict is constructive because there is a clear, democratic mechanism for resolving it: a vote. Once the vote is taken, the debate ends, and the members are structurally bound to act in solidarity with the decision, regardless of their personal feelings. The institution absorbs the conflict and translates it into collective action.

Conversely, many contemporary activist spaces operate on models of "consensus" or informal, horizontal leadership. In these spaces, conflict cannot be definitively resolved because there is no legitimate democratic authority. Disagreements fester, personalities clash, and the organization is paralyzed. In such a structural vacuum, "social endurance" simply means subjecting oneself to endless, unresolvable agonizing.

If we want people to endure conflict and stay in the room, we have to promise them that the conflict will eventually result in a decision. We must rehabilitate the concept of majority rule and formal democratic procedure within our movements. Epistemic humility, which Inouye rightly praises, is structurally enforced by democracy: you must accept that you might be wrong, because you might lose the vote. Solidarity is not just the emotional capacity to endure annoying comrades; it is the democratic discipline to fight passionately for your position, lose the vote, and then organize alongside those who defeated you.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The failure of contemporary activist spaces to resolve conflict stems from a lack of institutional structure rather than a lack of personal endurance among participants.
  2. 02Informal or consensus-based models often lead to paralyzed organizations because they lack the legitimate democratic authority required to definitively settle disagreements.
  3. 03Labor unions demonstrate that conflict becomes constructive when democratic mechanisms, such as majority votes, translate internal debate into binding collective action.
  4. 04Solidarity should be understood as a form of democratic discipline where individuals remain committed to the group's decisions even after losing a debate.
  5. 05Formal democratic procedures structurally enforce epistemic humility by requiring participants to accept the possibility of being outvoted.
