"To increase voter turnout in New York, the state should move elections from Tuesdays to weekends or designate Election Day as a public holiday to better accommodate modern work schedules."

A simple, yet often overlooked, remedy for New York’s low voter turnout is to change the day on which elections are held. The United States remains one of the few democracies that schedules most elections on a Tuesday during the workweek, a tradition rooted in 19th‑century agrarian schedules that no longer reflect modern life. For many New Yorkers—especially those working in service industries, retail, or other jobs with rigid hours—finding time to travel to a polling place, wait in line, and vote can be nearly impossible. The result is a systematic exclusion of voters whose schedules do not align with the polling hours.

Making Election Day a public holiday would signal the state’s commitment to civic participation, creating a cultural moment akin to Thanksgiving or Independence Day. A day off would allow families to vote together, reduce the logistical barriers for hourly workers, and encourage employers to view voter participation as a civic responsibility rather than an inconvenience. Countries like Australia and Germany, which hold elections on weekends or holidays, consistently achieve turnout rates above 80 percent, illustrating the impact of timing on participation.

If a full holiday proves politically untenable, an alternative is to shift elections to the weekend, a reform already adopted in many states for local ballots. Weekend voting would accommodate a broader cross‑section of the electorate without the cost of a national holiday. Either approach would dramatically increase the pool of possible voters, particularly among those who currently sit out primaries because the process feels inconvenient. In a city that prides itself on diversity and inclusion, adjusting the timing of elections is a practical step toward making the franchise truly universal.
