Diversified Claude

"The F.D.A.’s approval of genetically engineered salmon is a mistake because a regulatory agency focused on toxicology is unequipped to address the profound ethical, environmental, and systemic consequences of industrializing animal genetics."

Let's set aside, for a moment, whether AquAdvantage salmon is safe to eat. I'll stipulate that it probably is. The deeper question the F.D.A. has refused to engage is whether we should be industrializing animal genetics this way at all — and whether a regulatory agency staffed with toxicologists is the right body to answer that.

What the F.D.A. has actually approved is a business model. AquaBounty's pitch is a salmon that reaches market weight in roughly half the time, raised in dense tank facilities, marketed to consumers without any indication of what they are buying. This is not a story about feeding the hungry; farmed salmon is a premium product sold mostly in wealthy countries. It is a story about extracting more protein per dollar of capital, faster, with a patentable animal at the center of it. The salmon itself is collateral — engineered, in essence, to outgrow its own body.

We already know what factory aquaculture does. Concentrated fish operations generate concentrated waste, encourage pathogen evolution, and require enormous inputs of feed often derived from wild forage fish — anchovies and sardines hoovered out of South American waters to be ground into pellets. A fish that grows faster eats faster. Whatever efficiency gains accrue on the spreadsheet are paid for somewhere in an ecosystem that doesn't send invoices.

And then there is the precedent. AquAdvantage is the first transgenic animal approved for the American dinner plate; it will not be the last. Fast-growing pigs, hornless cattle, disease-resistant chickens, and double-muscled trout are all in various stages of development. Each will arrive at the F.D.A. citing AquAdvantage as proof that the pathway works. By the time the public realizes the cumulative shape of this transformation, the relevant decisions will already have been made, one quiet approval at a time.

I don't think the answer is to ban genetic engineering of animals. I think the answer is that decisions of this magnitude — about what kind of food system we want, what we owe to the animals in it, and how much novelty we want introduced before public deliberation catches up — shouldn't be made by a drug-approval office applying a 1938 statute. This isn't fearmongering. It's asking for the conversation we never had.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The AquAdvantage salmon is designed to maximize corporate profit through a patentable business model rather than to address global food security.
  2. 02Accelerated growth in farmed fish exacerbates the environmental damage caused by factory aquaculture, including concentrated waste and the depletion of wild forage fish for feed.
  3. 03Approving this salmon sets a dangerous precedent that allows for the incremental, large-scale transformation of the food system without adequate public deliberation.
  4. 04The current regulatory framework, based on outdated statutes, fails to account for the animal welfare and ecological costs inherent in genetic engineering.
