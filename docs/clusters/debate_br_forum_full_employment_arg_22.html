The Climate Connection Pollin Almost Makes

The most interesting and underdeveloped argument in Robert Pollin's essay is the claim that full employment and decarbonization can be joined as a single political project. He says this early — "creating a full-employment economy can be joined effectively with another fundamental policy aim: ending our dependence on fossil fuels and creating an economy powered by clean energy" — and returns to it when proposing his $330 billion reallocation. But the strategic logic of the alliance gets less attention than it deserves. I want to spell it out, because I think it is the most promising path forward for both causes.

Climate change is the defining long-term threat of our era. The science is unambiguous: without rapid, aggressive emissions reductions, we face temperature increases that will displace tens or hundreds of millions of people, flood coastal cities, accelerate species extinction, and disrupt agricultural systems on which billions depend. The minimum stabilization target Pollin cites — 80 percent reduction below 2000 levels by 2050 — requires, in essence, the wholesale rebuilding of the energy system in less than forty years. Every power plant, every building's heating system, every car, every industrial process must be reimagined. This is the largest physical undertaking in the history of the human species.

Meanwhile, the American economy is unable to put fifteen million unemployed people to work, with another fifteen million working less than they want or having given up entirely. The Federal Reserve, terrified of inflation that does not exist, sits on its hands. Congress, terrified of deficits that are entirely affordable, gestures at austerity. Tens of millions of able workers are wasted.

The connection is so obvious it borders on offensive that it has not been the central organizing principle of the past four years of policy debate. We have unprecedented unmet labor supply. We have unprecedented unmet labor demand — every building in America needs to be retrofitted, every utility grid modernized, every coastline assessed for resilience, every farm helped through climate adaptation. Connect the two, and you have not just a stimulus program but a generation-long industrial transformation.

This is the framing Pollin reaches for, and it is exactly right. But I want to push the political argument further than he does.

The biggest obstacle to climate policy in the United States is the political coalition that includes (a) fossil-fuel companies and their immediate dependents, (b) workers in carbon-intensive sectors who fear job loss, and (c) consumers worried about higher energy prices. The cap-and-trade fight of 2009–10, which collapsed in the Senate, demonstrated that this coalition can defeat even modest climate action when economic anxiety is high.

The climate movement has tried to respond to this in two ways. One is the "win-win" framing: climate action will create jobs, save money, etc. The other is the moral framing: we have an obligation to future generations, to the developing world, to non-human nature. Both are necessary. Neither, by itself, is sufficient.

What Pollin's framing offers is a structural response: not just "climate creates some jobs" but "the climate transition is the largest jobs program available, and it should be designed and presented as such, with strong protections for displaced workers." A serious "Green New Deal" — to use a phrase that has begun to circulate — would offer:

— Guaranteed transition jobs for fossil-fuel workers, with full wage and pension replacement during retraining. No coal miner should bear the personal cost of a transition that benefits the species. — Massive public investment in retrofits, renewables, grid modernization, and public transit, deployed through public works programs reminiscent of (and updated from) the WPA and CCC. — Federal funding for state and local climate adaptation work — wetland restoration, flood control, urban heat island mitigation — that creates substantial unskilled and semi-skilled employment in every region. — Industrial policy that ensures the manufacturing of solar panels, wind turbines, batteries, and electric vehicles happens domestically, with good union jobs, rather than being outsourced to lower-wage producers.

This approach has political advantages over both pure climate framing and pure jobs framing. It speaks to the economic anxiety that has otherwise been mobilized against climate policy. It gives organized labor a stake in the transition, instead of treating unions as the enemy of progress (as too many environmentalists have done). It offers a coherent story about what the federal government is for — not means-tested benefits or bailouts, but the active building of a livable future.

Pollin's calculations support the economic side of this. His estimate that clean-energy investment creates 17 jobs per million dollars compared with 5 for fossil-fuel investment is the kind of fact that should be in every politician's pocket. His point about domestic content — clean energy uses more domestic labor than fossil fuels do — is exactly the argument that should be made to communities anxious about offshoring.

What is missing, in Pollin and in the broader debate, is the institutional architecture. Who builds the wind farms? Public utilities, private contractors, or worker cooperatives? Who certifies the retrofits? Who trains the workers? Who decides which regions get priority? The success or failure of any such program will depend on these institutional details, not just on top-line spending numbers.

There is also the question of scale. Pollin proposes $330 billion in annual reallocation, with $165 billion of that for clean energy. This is a useful start, but is far below what serious climate analysts believe is needed. The International Energy Agency and other bodies have estimated global clean-energy investment needs in the trillions per year. The American share alone is likely several hundred billion above what Pollin proposes. The fiscal capacity exists. The political will, so far, does not.

This brings us back to the question Pollin ends on: do we have the will? My answer is that we will only develop the will if we frame the question correctly. Climate change is not a problem about polar bears. Unemployment is not a problem about statistical aggregates. Both are problems about what kind of country, and what kind of species, we want to be. The climate-jobs nexus offers a story that connects the daily anxieties of ordinary people to the largest civilizational stakes imaginable. We should tell it everywhere, constantly, and back it with policy proposals that match the scale of what we say.

Pollin opens this door. Let us push through it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The massive labor supply of the unemployed should be directly connected to the unprecedented labor demand required to rebuild the nation's energy system and infrastructure.
  2. 02A structural 'Green New Deal' approach can neutralize political opposition to climate policy by providing guaranteed transitions for fossil-fuel workers and creating high-quality domestic manufacturing jobs.
  3. 03Clean-energy investments are superior to fossil-fuel investments for job creation because they are more labor-intensive and rely more heavily on domestic content.
  4. 04To be successful, this movement must move beyond top-line spending figures to develop the specific institutional architecture and public works programs necessary to deploy labor and capital.
  5. 05The scale of investment required for a true climate transition likely exceeds the figures proposed by Pollin and will require a significant expansion of fiscal ambition.
