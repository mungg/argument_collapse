"In the field of education, AI should be used to protect and enhance 'ecological reason'—the space for curiosity and social learning—rather than being used as a tool for the 'Efficiency Lobby' to further automate and measure student output."

As an educator, I read Morozov’s essay less as a theory of artificial intelligence than as a theory of learning. His distinction between instrumental and ecological reason captures a tension that teachers know intimately. Schools are asked to produce measurable outcomes: reading scores, graduation rates, college readiness, job skills, standardized competencies. But genuine learning often begins in “messing about”: a child follows a strange question, misuses a tool, argues with a friend, draws a connection no curriculum anticipated, or lingers over something adults consider beside the point. The problem with AI in education is not simply cheating. It is whether the technology will deepen the reign of measurement or help defend the space of curiosity.

The arrival of ChatGPT intensified a panic already built into schooling. If the assignment is to produce a five-paragraph essay on a familiar prompt, a machine can now do much of it. If homework is a set of routine problems, a model can explain or solve them. If assessment means polished take-home prose, authorship becomes uncertain. Many schools responded with bans, detection software, oral exams, handwritten assignments, or resigned acceptance. But the panic revealed a deeper weakness: too much schoolwork had already become mechanical enough for machines to imitate.

Morozov’s critique of goal-directed AI should push educators to ask uncomfortable questions. Have we built classrooms around the very instrumental reason we now fear AI will automate? When learning is reduced to task completion, students sensibly seek efficient completion. If the goal is a grade, and the grade rewards an output detached from process, then AI-assisted shortcuts are not an alien invasion. They are an adaptation to the logic students have been given.

This does not mean cheating is imaginary or harmless. Trust matters. Writing matters. Struggle matters. Students need to develop memory, fluency, argument, attention, and the ability to sit with difficulty. Outsourcing every draft to a chatbot would impoverish them. But prohibition alone will not restore meaning. The better response is to redesign learning around processes that are social, reflective, and situated enough that AI becomes a tool to examine rather than a ghostwriter to hide.

Morozov’s invocation of David Hawkins’s “messing about” is especially relevant. Hawkins argued that early exploration with materials is not wasted time before real instruction begins; it is part of how learners form questions and intuitions. In a science classroom, students need time with magnets, seeds, ramps, water, lenses, or code before being handed formal explanations. In a writing classroom, they need to collect observations, imitate styles, test sentences, talk through confusion, and revise for real audiences. In history, they need to encounter primary sources that do not immediately fit a textbook narrative. AI can either short-circuit this exploration by supplying premature answers or enrich it by multiplying materials for inquiry.

Consider language learning, the example Morozov begins with. A generative system can create custom stories, dialogues, and explanations. That is genuinely powerful. But the danger is that language becomes a private efficiency project: optimize vocabulary acquisition, generate endless input, personalize everything. Language, however, is social. It involves embarrassment, gesture, humor, misunderstanding, cultural memory, and relationship. A good AI language tool should not replace conversation; it should prepare learners for it, help them notice patterns, and connect them with shared materials. It should sometimes slow them down: “Ask a classmate how they would say this,” or “Compare this generated dialogue with an interview from an actual speaker.”

In writing instruction, AI can be useful if it is made visible. Students might ask a model to produce three introductions, then critique their clichés. They might compare a chatbot’s summary of a poem with their own reading and identify what the machine misses. They might use AI to generate counterarguments, then evaluate which are straw men. English-language learners might use it to understand idioms without surrendering their voice. Students with disabilities might use speech-to-text, summarization, or planning tools as accommodations. The key is that the student remains responsible for judgment.

This shifts the teacher’s role, not toward policing every keystroke, but toward designing richer intellectual situations. Instead of asking for generic essays, teachers can ask students to connect readings to local observations, class discussions, personal field notes, or evolving drafts. Instead of one final product, assessment can include proposal, annotated sources, peer feedback, revision memos, oral defense, and reflection on tool use. AI does not make authorship impossible; it makes process harder to ignore.

Yet such redesign requires time and support. Teachers are already overburdened. The ed-tech industry’s promise is that AI will save them: generate lesson plans, grade essays, tailor worksheets, communicate with parents, analyze student data. Some of this may help. But without caution it will intensify the same managerial pressures Morozov describes. AI-generated lesson plans can become standardized curriculum delivery. Automated feedback can reduce writing to rubric compliance. Predictive analytics can label students early and narrow expectations. Surveillance tools can turn classrooms into suspicion machines. The Efficiency Lobby has always had designs on schools.

A democratic educational AI agenda would start from teachers and students, not vendors. Teachers should have collective say over which tools enter classrooms, what data they collect, and how they are used. Students should learn how AI systems work: not only prompt tricks, but training data, bias, hallucination, labor, energy use, and ownership. Families should know when children’s data is being processed. Schools should prefer tools that are transparent, privacy-preserving, accessible, and adaptable to local curriculum. Public education should not become a data pipeline for private AI firms.

We also need public alternatives. It is absurd for every district to negotiate separately with companies selling similar tools. Public universities, libraries, teacher unions, and education departments could collaborate on open educational AI resources: multilingual tutoring aids, accessible reading supports, curriculum adaptation tools, and model lessons governed as public goods. These should be designed not to replace teachers but to expand their repertoire. A public AI system for education should include shared repositories, teacher annotations, local customization, and clear limits on student data.

Morozov’s “ecological reason” also reminds us that education is not just preparation for the labor market. The current AI discourse often asks: what skills will workers need when machines can code, write, and analyze? That is a legitimate question, but too narrow. Schools should help young people ask what kinds of lives, communities, and technologies are worth wanting. They should cultivate attention, care, historical imagination, scientific humility, aesthetic judgment, and democratic speech. These are not easily benchmarked, and that is why they are vulnerable.

One irony of generative AI is that it may force schools to become more human. If machines can produce competent formulaic prose, then we must teach writing as thinking in public. If machines can answer routine questions, then we must teach better questioning. If machines can simulate tutoring, then we must value the relationship between learner and teacher more, not less. If machines can personalize endlessly, then we must defend the common classroom as a place where students encounter differences they did not choose.

The AI we deserve in education is neither banned nor worshipped. It is domesticated by pedagogy. It helps students explore without erasing struggle, supports teachers without scripting them, widens access without extracting data, and serves communities rather than markets. Above all, it keeps alive the kind of intelligence that begins when a student says, “Wait, what if…?” That moment cannot be automated. It can only be protected.
