Vanilla MiniMax

"While Johnson’s critique of 'dehumanization' and his framework of 'racial capitalism' offer a necessary intervention in the historiography of slavery, his argument requires further methodological and practical elaboration to effectively translate historical scholarship into a contemporary politics of justice."

Johnson's essay offers a provocative and necessary intervention in the historiography of slavery and the political economy of racial capitalism. His central argument—that the concept of "dehumanization" obscures more than it reveals, and that the history of capitalism cannot be understood apart from the history of slavery—resonates deeply with the trajectory of recent scholarship. Yet his argument demands careful engagement on several fronts: first, regarding the epistemological status of "dehumanization" and what alternatives might serve better; second, concerning the relationship between his critique of liberal rights discourse and the practical politics of justice; and third, in considering what the history of slavery can and cannot bear as a foundation for contemporary moral and political commitment.

Johnson's critique of "dehumanization" is compelling in its insistence that enslaved people were, of course, human—and that slaveholders' violence operated precisely upon that humanity. The argument draws strength from the observation that slavery depended upon enslaved people's capacities: their labor, their reproductive potential, their intelligence, their susceptibility to manipulation and terror. One might push this point further, however, by considering what was distinctive about the violence of slavery if not that it targeted persons whose humanity was undeniable. The term "dehumanization" may be misleading in the ways Johnson describes, but the phenomenon it gestures toward—the reduction of persons to instruments, the destruction of social bonds, the violation of bodies understood as persons—remains significant. Perhaps what is needed is not the abandonment of such language but its reconstruction. Johnson's alternative—that we attend to the "human register" of slaveholders' power—suggests a way forward, but it requires elaboration. How, exactly, should we read the evidence of enslaved people's humanity in the archives? What methodology follows from Johnson's epistemological premises?

The engagement with Philip Morgan is particularly illuminating. Morgan's framing—that enslaved people "strove to preserve their humanity" and that extracting acknowledgment of this humanity constituted a form of resistance—illustrates the tensions Johnson identifies. Yet one might ask whether Morgan's critics, including Johnson, have fully reckoned with the methodological challenges that drive such framing. The archive of American slavery is overwhelmingly the archive of slaveholders: ledgers, letters, diaries, legal records, the material culture of domination. To reconstruct enslaved people's experiences, historians must work against the grain of these sources, reading absences, inferring meanings, constructing counter-narratives from fragments. In this context, the emphasis on "agency" and "humanity" functions not merely as a liberal moralism but as an epistemological necessity—a insistence that enslaved people were present, acting, meaning-making subjects, not merely the objects of white power. Johnson is right that this emphasis can collapse into anachronism, but it is not clear that the remedy lies in abandoning such categories altogether.

The critique of liberal rights discourse is where Johnson's argument becomes most politically charged, and here I find myself most sympathetic but also most uncertain. His invocation of Marx's critique of rights—the observation that formal political equality coexisted with material inequality—has obvious relevance to the history of slavery and its aftermath. The Reconstruction amendments promised citizenship and civil rights but left the plantation economy largely intact; the welfare state of the mid-twentieth century was racially structured in ways that perpetuated hierarchy; contemporary calls for "colorblind" jurisprudence reproduce the ideological conditions of racial subordination. Johnson's argument that a justice rooted in the history of slavery must attend to these continuities—to what Du Bois called the "spiritual and cultural" wounds that cannot be healed by formal rights—is persuasive.

Yet the relationship between historical scholarship and political activism is more complicated than Johnson allows. The Movement for Black Lives proposal, which Johnson approvingly cites, calls for reparations and centers the history of slavery and colonialism. But the practical politics of reparations—how to calculate damages, who should pay, who should receive, over what timescale—requires operationalizing historical knowledge in ways that may strain the conceptual framework Johnson provides. Similarly, the critique of liberal human rights discourse, however warranted, must grapple with the fact that such discourse has proven useful, if insufficient, in advancing claims on behalf of the vulnerable. International human rights norms have shaped interventions, constrained abuses, and provided language for movements that might otherwise lack access to political power. Johnson quotes Moyn's critique of human rights as "liberal neo-imperialism," but Moyn's argument is more nuanced than Johnson suggests; Moyn's point is that human rights emerged late and as a limited politics, not that it has been entirely useless.

The concept of racial capitalism, which Johnson develops through Du Bois and Robinson, is perhaps the most significant contribution of the essay. The insistence that capitalism and racism are co-constituted—that racial hierarchy was not an incidental feature of capitalist development but central to it—has profound implications for both historical understanding and contemporary politics. Johnson's citation of Eric Williams is apt: Williams demonstrated that British industrialization was funded by the profits of the slave trade, that the "clot of capital" accumulated in Liverpool and Bristol financed the factories of Manchester. More recent scholarship, including the work of Johnson himself and of historians like Edward Baptist, has extended this analysis, showing how the labor of enslaved people in the American South financed the development of northern banking, insurance, and manufacturing. The result is a reconceptualization of the history of capitalism that places slavery not at the margins but at the center.

Johnson's sixfold enumeration of the virtues of a justice rooted in racial capitalism is suggestive but would benefit from further development. The first point—that such an account proceeds from the standpoint of Africa and the Global South rather than Europe—is methodologically important but raises questions about the relationship between standpoint and analysis. Can a justice conceived from the standpoint of the oppressed speak to the oppressor? Must it? The second point, emphasizing the extraction and distribution of resources between regions and classes, aligns with the emerging "new history of capitalism" but risks reproducing the economism that Johnson critiques in orthodox Marxism. The third point, on restorative justice and reparations, is politically important but requires engagement with the legal and philosophical literature on reparations that Johnson does not directly address. The fourth point, on gender and sexuality, is essential but underdeveloped; the reproductive violation of enslaved women was a core feature of slavery that cannot be assimilated to a simple narrative of extraction. The fifth point, on the relationship between race and class, is perhaps the most historically rich but also the most contested. The "wages of whiteness" thesis has been criticized by scholars who argue that it overstates the significance of racial ideology in shaping working-class consciousness and underestimates the material bases of solidarity across racial lines. The sixth point, connecting slavery to environmental destruction, is intriguing but would require extensive elaboration to be persuasive.

In the end, Johnson returns to Du Bois, to the idea that there are moments when the world might be remade. This is a powerful conclusion, but it raises the question of whether historical scholarship can help identify such moments or merely mourn their passing. Johnson is a historian, and his essay is primarily a contribution to historiography; yet the political stakes are never far from the surface. The question is whether the history he proposes can be translated into a politics adequate to the present. The Movement for Black Lives, which Johnson cites, represents one attempt at such translation; the persistence of racial inequality in wealth, health, incarceration, and political representation represents the challenge. Johnson's argument suggests that any adequate response must be historically deep, structurally systemic, and attentive to the ways that racial hierarchy continues to organize the distribution of resources and recognition. This is a tall order, but his essay provides a framework for thinking about what such a response might look like.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The term 'dehumanization' remains a significant descriptor for the reduction of persons to instruments, suggesting that the concept should be reconstructed rather than entirely abandoned.
  2. 02The focus on 'agency' and 'humanity' in slave historiography serves as an epistemological necessity for reconstructing the lives of the enslaved from archives dominated by slaveholders.
  3. 03A critique of liberal rights discourse must reckon with the practical utility such frameworks have provided for vulnerable populations and the complexities of operationalizing historical knowledge into specific policies like reparations.
  4. 04The 'racial capitalism' framework correctly places slavery at the center of capitalist development but must address scholarly debates regarding the 'wages of whiteness' and the material bases of cross-racial solidarity.
  5. 05Johnson’s proposed virtues of a justice rooted in slavery require deeper engagement with legal, philosophical, and environmental literature to move beyond suggestive points toward a persuasive systemic analysis.
