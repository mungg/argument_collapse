"The failures of modern parenting and gender inequality should be addressed by refining and politicizing the ethic of care rather than abandoning it or urging women to care less."

Nancy Hirschmann’s essay is bracing, but it risks throwing away the most radical insight of care feminism in order to discipline a particularly visible group of privileged mothers. The parents who defend plagiarism, manipulate professors, or shield children from consequences deserve criticism. But they are not examples of “mothers who care too much.” They are examples of bad care. To call their conduct care is like calling bribery “generosity” because money changes hands.

Care theory has never meant indulgence, clannishness, or the conversion of children into narcissistic extensions of the parent. At its best, the tradition associated with Carol Gilligan, Sara Ruddick, Joan Tronto, Nel Noddings, Eva Kittay, and others has emphasized attentiveness, responsibility, competence, responsiveness, and relational judgment. Those qualities are not present when a mother lies so her daughter can rush a sorority. They are not present when parents treat a dead girl as an unfortunate obstacle to a summer in Paris. They are not present when a mother insists that her son is not a cheater after being shown that he cheated. These are failures of moral perception, not excesses of maternal virtue.

Hirschmann is right that “care” can be appropriated by class privilege. But every moral language can be appropriated. Rights talk can defend property hoarding. Freedom can mean deregulation for the powerful. Equality can be flattened into sameness and used to ignore pregnancy, caregiving, and disability. We do not abandon rights, freedom, or equality because they are misused. We refine them, contest them, and reconnect them to the realities they were meant to illuminate. The same should be true of care.

The danger in Hirschmann’s argument is that the remedy for sentimentalizing motherhood becomes resuscitating an old suspicion of dependency itself. She does not intend this; indeed, she has contributed to feminist work that treats dependency as politically central. But when she says women need to “care a little less,” the formulation can easily be heard as: detach, compete, earn, toughen up. That is a familiar American answer to almost every social problem, and it is not obviously feminist. The workplace to which women are urged to return is often organized around the fantasy of the unencumbered worker: always available, mobile, uninterrupted, supported by someone else’s invisible labor. If feminism simply pushes women into that model, the victory will be thin.

Hirschmann argues that women’s paid employment will force men to do more at home. Sometimes it does. But decades of evidence about the “second shift” suggest that paid employment alone does not produce equality. Women can earn wages and still organize the childcare, buy the birthday presents, remember the in-laws, schedule the doctor, supervise homework, and absorb everyone’s moods. Some men respond to women’s employment not by doing half the care work but by lowering standards, outsourcing what they can, or letting women live in exhaustion. Economic power is necessary; it is not sufficient.

Nor should we underestimate what is lost when care is treated primarily as the trap from which women must escape. Many women, including feminists, want to spend substantial time with young children not because they are dupes of patriarchy but because children are interesting, vulnerable, funny, and beloved. Hirschmann acknowledges the private goods parents receive from children, but she treats those goods almost as an offset against public claims for support. Yet the fact that parents love their children does not make raising them a private hobby. Teachers often love teaching; doctors may find meaning in healing; political activists may derive identity and friendship from organizing. We do not therefore conclude that their work has no public value.

Children are not consumer goods. They become neighbors, workers, voters, friends, patients, artists, prisoners, soldiers, and caregivers. The quality of their early lives matters for all of us. It is therefore strange to suggest that public support for caregiving must lead to intrusive public judgment of mothers. Public support can be universal and enabling rather than punitive: paid parental leave for both parents; child allowances; health care not tied to employment; high-quality childcare; shorter standard work hours; pension credits for caregiving; support for parents of disabled children; good public schools. Such policies need not ask whether an individual mother has produced an adequately civic child. They recognize that dependency is a normal feature of human life.

The real problem is not that care feminism overvalued mothering. It is that American society absorbed the praise and rejected the redistribution. We learned to say that motherhood is priceless while paying childcare workers poorly, denying many workers paid leave, structuring school and work schedules incompatibly, and leaving families to improvise. The language of “choice” then covers over institutional failure. Some affluent women can “choose” to stay home because husbands earn enough; poor women are compelled to work under welfare rules even when childcare is unstable; many middle-class women are trapped between unaffordable care and inflexible jobs. That is not a culture with too much care. It is a culture with too much privatized desperation.

Hirschmann’s students who expect to become stay-at-home mothers while condemning women on welfare reveal a political failure, but not necessarily a failure of care theory. In fact, a robust ethic of care would challenge their lack of empathy. It would ask them to consider the networks of support that make their own imagined choices possible. It would ask why dependence on a husband is romanticized while dependence on the state is stigmatized. It would expose the classed and racialized distinctions between “devotion” and “dependency.” If students are reading care feminism as a validation of private privilege, they need better care feminism, not less of it.

The solution is to politicize care more fully. Joan Tronto has argued that care is not merely a private disposition but a democratic practice. Who receives care? Who gives it? Who is excused from it? Whose needs count? Who pays? Who decides? These questions lead not to maternal glorification but to institutional critique. They also lead to men. A serious care politics would not ask women to bargain one by one with husbands in the privacy of marriage, though such bargaining matters. It would create nontransferable paid leave for fathers, protect part-time work with benefits, reduce penalties for caregiving, and make male care visible and expected. It would also value the paid caregivers—disproportionately women, often women of color and immigrants—whose labor enables professional women’s “choices.”

Hirschmann is right to reject a feminism that says any choice a woman makes is feminist because a woman made it. But she sometimes writes as if the most urgent feminist task is to keep educated women attached to careers. Leadership matters, but feminism cannot be built around rescuing elite women from domesticity while leaving low-wage mothers to patch together care in poverty. The deeper task is to transform the institutions that make care either a private luxury or a private penalty.

Care is not the enemy of women’s power. Care without power is dangerous, yes. But power without care is what produced many of the public disasters Hirschmann invokes: predatory finance, callous employers, punitive welfare policy, and families that train children to win without responsibility. We do not need mothers to care less. We need fathers, employers, citizens, and states to care more—and to care better.

"Feminism should focus on eliminating coercion and expanding meaningful options for women rather than morally condemning stay-at-home motherhood as a failure of political duty."

Nancy Hirschmann’s essay is most persuasive when she criticizes the romantic inflation of motherhood. It is least persuasive when she flirts with the idea that women who stay home are failing a political duty. A free society should be wary of both state-enforced domesticity and feminist-enforced careerism. The proper target is coercion, not the content of women’s choices.

The anecdotes with which Hirschmann begins are appalling, but they do not establish much about full-time motherhood. Parents have been excusing, spoiling, and overprotecting children for as long as there have been parents. Working parents do it too. Fathers do it. Wealth does it. A mother’s presence in the home may give her more time to intervene, but the moral failure is entitlement, not domesticity. To move from “some mothers lie for their children” to “stay-at-home mothering undermines citizenship” is too quick.

The stronger part of Hirschmann’s case concerns dependency. A woman who leaves paid work may become financially vulnerable, especially in divorce. Her bargaining power in marriage may decline. Her retirement security may depend on a husband. These are serious risks, and young women should hear about them before they make irreversible decisions. The fantasy of lifelong marital teamwork can be dangerous. Divorce, disability, unemployment, death, and simple male selfishness are not rare enough to ignore.

But acknowledging risk is different from declaring the choice politically suspect. Adults routinely make choices that create dependence or reduce market power: becoming an artist, joining the clergy, caring for an elderly parent, starting a small business, following a spouse to a new city, taking years out for graduate school, serving in the military, or accepting low-paid nonprofit work. Some of these choices may be imprudent. Some may be admirable. We do not usually say that by making them people have betrayed equality.

Hirschmann invokes Ann Cudd’s argument that women who choose conventional feminine roles support patriarchal oppression. There is truth in the idea that individual choices can reinforce social patterns. But if taken too far, this logic becomes suffocating. A woman who takes her husband’s name, wears makeup, has children, stays home, works part-time, chooses nursing over engineering, or enjoys cooking can always be accused of strengthening patriarchy. Feminism then becomes another tribunal judging women’s lives.

The language of “false choice” is especially dangerous. Yes, choices are shaped by institutions and norms. All choices are. Men’s choices are shaped by ideals of masculinity; workers’ choices by markets; students’ choices by debt; consumers’ choices by advertising; citizens’ choices by political systems. If social influence invalidates choice, autonomy disappears altogether. The question should be whether people have meaningful alternatives and accurate information, not whether their decisions conform to a theorist’s view of liberation.

A liberal feminism should defend a wide range of family arrangements while working to make them genuinely voluntary. That means eliminating legal and economic penalties that push women into dependence, but also avoiding policies that push mothers into employment against their judgment. The same feminism should support a woman who wants to return to work six weeks after birth and a woman who wants to spend several years at home. It should support fathers who want to be primary caregivers. It should support childless adults. Equality means expanding options, not replacing one script with another.

Hirschmann raises an important concern about public compensation for care: if caregiving is funded as a public service, the public may demand oversight. This is not a theoretical worry. Welfare policy has long policed poor mothers’ sexuality, work, family structure, and spending. Once the state pays, it often supervises. Middle-class advocates of caregiver wages may imagine respectful recognition; poor women have more reason to expect suspicion. A universal child benefit might avoid some stigma, but direct payment for “good mothering” would be difficult to administer without moral intrusion.

The better approach is to support families indirectly and neutrally. Children should have health care, education, and safety regardless of parental employment. Tax and benefit systems should not strongly favor either single-earner or dual-earner households. Divorce law should recognize sacrifices made for family care through equitable distribution and support. Parents should be free to purchase childcare, provide care themselves, share it with relatives, or have fathers take the lead. The state’s role should be to protect children from abuse and neglect, enforce contracts and support obligations, prevent discrimination, and ensure basic opportunity—not to decide which family model is feminist.

Hirschmann’s argument about tax policy deserves attention. If the tax code penalizes second earners, reform it. If Social Security treats caregiving inconsistently, reform it. If employer health insurance traps women in marriages, create portable insurance. If workplaces punish mothers and caregiving fathers, enforce anti-discrimination law and encourage flexible arrangements. These are liberal reforms because they remove distortions. They do not require moral condemnation of women who, after the distortions are reduced, still choose home-centered lives.

There is also something troubling in the suggestion that educated women owe society continuous labor-market participation because public resources were invested in their education. Education is not vocational conscription. We educate citizens for many reasons: judgment, culture, parenting, volunteering, friendship, self-development, democratic participation. A woman with a college degree who raises children, serves on a school board, cares for aging parents, writes part-time, or organizes locally has not necessarily wasted her education. Indeed, if mothering shapes citizens, educated parenting may be socially valuable even when unpaid.

The challenge is to avoid sentimentalism. Stay-at-home motherhood is not automatically noble. It can be narrow, status-obsessed, and infantilizing. But paid employment is not automatically emancipatory. It can be alienating, greedy, and destructive. Some of the investment bankers who helped create the recent financial crisis presumably had mothers who worked; some had mothers at home. Their moral failures cannot be traced simply to maternal labor-force participation.

What children need is not mothers who work or mothers who stay home. They need adults who love them enough to set limits, tell the truth, respect other people’s claims, and model responsibility. A mother who refuses to excuse plagiarism is doing that whether she is a lawyer or a homemaker. A father who leaves work early to attend a teacher conference is doing that whether his wife earns wages or not. A family that teaches service, humility, and accountability can take more than one economic form.

Feminism should criticize the ideology that women are naturally destined for unpaid care. It should also criticize the ideology that market achievement is the highest form of citizenship. Hirschmann is right that care without power can trap women. But power should include the power to choose domestic life without being told that one has damaged the cause. The feminist future should not be a world in which nobody stays home. It should be a world in which staying home is neither required by gender nor punished by law, neither romanticized as sacred nor dismissed as servile.

Women do not need another doctrine telling them how to live. They need honest information, fair rules, portable benefits, legal equality, and partners who do not exploit them. After that, some will choose careers, some will choose full-time caregiving, many will move between the two, and some will reject family life altogether. A feminism confident in freedom should be able to live with that diversity.

"The solution to gender inequality lies in structural policy reforms rather than Nancy Hirschmann’s call for individual women to care less and bargain harder within their marriages."

**The Tax Code, the Workplace, and the Limits of Exhortation**

Nancy Hirschmann's essay is most persuasive when it gets concrete. She is right about Edward McCaffery's analysis of how the U.S. tax code penalizes second earners, particularly when their incomes approach their spouses'. She is right that maternity leave alone has not redistributed care work. She is right that men face professional stigma for using family-friendly policies, with the result that the policies redistribute leave but not labor. Where I think the essay falters is in its turn, at the close, toward exhortation: women should care less, women should refuse, women should demand. Exhortation is a thin instrument against structural problems, and the structural problems Hirschmann names are amenable to policy in ways her conclusion underplays.

Let me lay out a different set of conclusions from the same diagnosis.

First, the tax code. The marriage penalty on dual earners is not a force of nature; it is a 1948 policy decision (the joint return) updated incoherently across decades. The simplest fix is mandatory individual filing, as is the norm in most other OECD countries. Each adult files on her own income; the household is no longer the tax unit. The "single breadwinner subsidy" disappears, the second-earner penalty disappears, and the implicit incentive for one spouse to stay home dissolves. Sweden made this transition in 1971 and saw a meaningful and durable increase in married women's labor force participation. The U.S. could do the same. Hirschmann mentions McCaffery but does not actually call for this reform; her closing pages turn to individual women's bargaining. The tax code is a far blunter and more effective instrument.

Second, parental leave. The current U.S. regime—unpaid FMLA leave for twelve weeks, available to a minority of workers—is among the worst in the industrialized world. But the more important question, granted that we are pushing for paid leave, is the design. Hirschmann notes that "parental leave" (gender-neutral) has been gamed by men in academia to bank extra time for tenure. The lesson is not that gender-neutral leave is a mistake; it is that leave should be designed to actually be taken for caregiving, with policy guardrails. The Swedish "daddy month," now several months, makes a portion of paid leave non-transferable to the other parent. If the father doesn't take it, the family loses it. The result has been a dramatic increase in fathers' uptake of leave. Quebec adopted a similar policy in 2006 and saw male leave-taking jump from less than 30 percent to over 75 percent in a few years. This is the kind of policy that actually changes behavior. Exhorting wives to refuse household labor does not.

Third, childcare. The U.S. is a low-quality, high-cost outlier among rich democracies. The cost of full-time childcare in major metros now exceeds in-state public university tuition. This single fact pushes second earners out of the labor market more powerfully than any tax code or norm. France, with its écoles maternelles, and the Scandinavian states, with their universal childcare entitlements, have demonstrated that this is solvable. Universal, high-quality, publicly funded childcare from age one onward would do more for gender equality than any reform Hirschmann names. It would also do more for the welfare of children, especially low-income children, than the current patchwork.

Fourth, the workplace. Family-friendly policies fail when they are stigmatized. The stigmatization arises in part because they are perceived as accommodations for a minority, granted at the discretion of management. Make them universal and mandatory, and the stigma dissipates. France's 35-hour work week, Germany's robust works councils with say over working hours, Sweden's general right to part-time work for parents of young children—these structures shift the baseline from which everyone operates. They do not eliminate the motherhood penalty Blair-Loy documents, but they significantly reduce it.

Fifth—and this is where Hirschmann is most clearly right—none of the above is sufficient without a cultural change in men's expectations of their own lives. But cultural change is itself partly produced by policy. When fathers take leave, other fathers expect to take leave. When men leave the office at 5:30 because of paternity policies that require it, this becomes normal. The Scandinavian comparison is again instructive: not only have the policies changed, but male attitudes have followed, with younger men in particular now expressing strong preferences for active fatherhood that would have been unusual a generation ago. Norms and policies co-evolve.

Hirschmann would, I think, accept all of this. But the essay's prescriptive emphasis lies elsewhere—on individual women's bargaining position within marriages, on women's refusal to do excess care work, on the moral hazards of celebrating mothering. I find this emphasis frustrating because it both overestimates what individual bargaining can accomplish and underestimates what policy can. Individual bargaining within a household is structurally constrained: the partner with more outside options will tend to win, and the structures determining outside options are set by tax codes, childcare access, parental leave regimes, and labor markets, not by personal grit. A woman in Sweden has more bargaining power than a woman in Mississippi not because she is a tougher negotiator but because she lives inside a different policy environment.

There is also a political economy point. The reforms I have described are unpopular among the same constituency that resists every other progressive reform—business interests opposed to mandated leave, social conservatives opposed to public childcare, deficit hawks opposed to anything that costs money. Building a movement for these reforms requires alliances across class lines, including with the very stay-at-home mothers Hirschmann is critical of. Many of them, when their children are older or when they face divorce, become the strongest advocates for the policies that would have made their earlier choices less costly. Pitching the argument as a critique of their choices alienates a natural constituency.

I would put the priorities this way. End joint filing. Mandate non-transferable paid parental leave for both parents. Establish universal public childcare. Strengthen workplace protections against the motherhood penalty. And, in parallel, push the cultural conversation about active fatherhood that is already underway. This program does not require women to "care less." It changes the conditions under which caring happens, so that the costs of caring fall more equally on the people who benefit from being cared for—including, crucially, on men.

"The problems associated with intensive mothering should be addressed through institutional redesign and public policy rather than by simply judging individual maternal choices or demanding women care less."

Nancy Hirschmann has written a necessary provocation, but the debate it invites will go wrong if it becomes a referendum on whether stay-at-home mothers are good or bad for feminism. The real issue is institutional design. How can a society organize care so that children and dependent adults are well supported, women are not subordinated, men are not excused, poor mothers are not policed, and families do not become engines of civic irresponsibility?

The answer begins by rejecting three simplifications. First, care is not automatically virtuous. Families can teach selfishness, racism, dishonesty, and entitlement. Hirschmann’s examples make that plain. Second, paid work is not automatically liberating. It can provide income and power, but it can also exploit, exhaust, and punish caregivers. Third, choice is neither meaningless nor sacred. Women’s choices are shaped by social structures, but they are still choices made by agents trying to live decent lives under constraints.

A practical feminism should therefore ask not “Should mothers stay home?” but “What arrangements reduce coercion and distribute care fairly?” Several principles follow.

The first is individual economic security. No adult should have to depend entirely on a spouse for health care, retirement, or basic income protection. Employer-based health insurance and spousal benefits make family decisions more coercive than they appear. A woman considering leaving work to care for children should not also be risking medical insecurity or old-age poverty. Health coverage should be portable and not dependent on marriage. Social Security should better recognize caregiving years without locking women into dependency. Divorce law should account for lost earning capacity when one spouse performs unpaid family labor. These reforms do not tell women what to choose; they make choices less dangerous.

The second principle is father-specific responsibility. Gender-neutral policies often become women’s policies in practice. If parental leave can be taken by either parent, mothers usually take it. If flexible work is available but stigmatized, women use it and pay the penalty. To change norms, some benefits must be designed for fathers specifically: nontransferable paid leave, public campaigns normalizing paternal care, and workplace expectations that men will use caregiving time. The point is not merely to help mothers stay employed, but to alter children’s and employers’ assumptions about men.

The third principle is universal support rather than maternal surveillance. Hirschmann is right that paying mothers because they produce citizens can invite judgment about whether they are doing the job properly. American welfare policy shows how easily support becomes discipline, especially for poor women. But the alternative is not privatization. Universal child benefits, public preschool, paid leave, health care, and high-quality childcare support care without requiring caseworkers to evaluate maternal virtue. Programs should attach to children, caregivers, and workers as citizens, not to moralized categories of deserving mothers.

The fourth principle is quality public institutions. One reason affluent parents overmanage children is that the stakes of institutional sorting are so high. When schools are unequal and opportunity scarce, parental love becomes competitive strategy. Better public schools, safer neighborhoods, accessible libraries, after-school programs, and affordable colleges would reduce the incentive to treat childhood as an arms race. Civic formation cannot be left to private families, especially when families have unequal resources and narrow loyalties.

The fifth principle is workplace redesign. The American workplace still often presumes an ideal worker with no substantial caregiving obligations. Professional jobs reward long hours and constant availability; low-wage jobs impose instability and offer little leave. Both models are hostile to care. Reform should include paid sick days, predictable scheduling, limits on excessive hours, part-time work with proportional benefits, and anti-discrimination protections for caregivers of both sexes. Employers should not be allowed to praise families in advertisements while punishing workers who attend to them.

The sixth principle is respect for plural family forms. Some families will choose a full-time caregiver. Some will have two full-time earners. Some will rely on grandparents, paid caregivers, shared custody, or fathers at home. Some adults will not have children. Feminism need not bless every arrangement as equally egalitarian, but public policy should avoid forcing one model. The goal is not uniformity; it is fairness and non-domination.

Where does this leave care feminism? It needs revision, not abandonment. Care theorists were right that dependency is central to human life and that traditional political theory often ignored it. **The Problem with Pathologizing Choice**

There is a move in Nancy Hirschmann's essay that deserves explicit attention because I think it characterizes a recurring tendency in contemporary feminist thought. The essay then proceeds to recommend a course of action contrary to their wants. The move is sophisticated, but it is also philosophically dangerous, and I want to push back on it.

Begin with the obvious. Of course preferences are shaped by social conditions. This is true of all preferences, everywhere, always. The question is what follows from this fact. If "shaped by social conditions" is sufficient to delegitimize a preference, then no preference is ever legitimate and the language of choice and autonomy is meaningless. The relevant standard, on most accounts of autonomy in the contemporary philosophical literature, involves something like: the agent reflectively endorses the preference; the agent has access to alternatives; the agent has information relevant to the choice; the agent is not under duress. Many stay-at-home mothers can meet these criteria. Some cannot, and we should worry about them. But the wholesale move from "preferences are socially constructed" to "preferences are inauthentic" is a category error.

Hirschmann's essay flirts with this error repeatedly. The students' wish to be mothers is not engaged as a substantive position—as something that might have reasons behind it, including reasons Hirschmann should take seriously—but as a symptom of a deeper structural pathology. The argument never has to grapple with the possibility that intensive mothering might, for some women, be a considered and defensible life plan. It is presupposed to be a mistake; the only question is how to explain why so many women keep making it.

This presupposition is interesting because it inverts the original feminist insistence on listening to women's experience. Carol Gilligan's project, which Hirschmann acknowledges as foundational to care feminism, was rooted in the methodological commitment to take seriously what women actually said about their moral lives, rather than assuming their voices were defective versions of male reasoning. The current generation of women, including Hirschmann's students, is saying something. Are we listening? Hirschmann's answer is, in effect, no—because what they are saying is unhelpful to the political project she favors. But this is precisely the move Kohlberg made when he dismissed women's moral reasoning as developmentally inferior. We should be cautious before repeating it on different grounds.

There is a deeper philosophical point here. Feminist theory has long been internally divided on the question of how seriously to take women's stated preferences when those preferences seem to conflict with feminist goals. Catharine MacKinnon's work, particularly on pornography, took the strong position: women's stated preferences for traditional roles, sexualized self-presentation, and so on are products of patriarchal conditioning so thorough that the preferences carry no normative weight. Martha Nussbaum's capability approach took a more moderate position: we should worry about "adaptive preferences" when women have internalized restrictions that limit their flourishing, but we should also be cautious about overriding stated preferences in the name of women's "real" interests. Drucilla Cornell and others have argued that any feminism that systematically discounts women's voices is internally contradictory.

I find myself in the Nussbaum camp on this. Adaptive preferences are real and politically serious. The Indian widow who reports satisfaction with a diet that has destroyed her health, in Nussbaum's famous example, is not exercising autonomy in a way we should respect at face value. But the leap from "some preferences are adaptive" to "any preference that conflicts with feminist prescriptions is adaptive" is enormous and unwarranted. We need a real account of when preferences should be questioned and when they should be respected. Hirschmann does not give us one. Her essay treats the preferences of her students as pre-emptively suspect, in a way that I think most of us would find troubling if applied to other domains.

Consider an analogy. Suppose a labor theorist concludes that workers in a particular industry should unionize for their own good, but those workers consistently refuse. The labor theorist could conclude that the workers' refusal reflects false consciousness, manipulated by employer propaganda, and that their preferences should therefore be discounted in the political analysis. This conclusion might be right. It might also be that the workers have considered reasons—about the particular history of their union, the particular conditions of their workplace, their particular life plans—that the theorist has not adequately engaged. The intellectually responsible move is to engage the workers' reasons, not to declare them unavailable to themselves.

Hirschmann's students may be wrong about their futures. They may be naïve about marriage. They may underestimate the costs they will pay. They may, in middle age, deeply regret choices made at twenty. All of this is possible. It would not justify treating their current articulated preferences as politically void. They are adults with reasons, and the feminist project's relationship to them should be one of argument, not preemptive dismissal.

I want to add one further point. The history of progressive movements is littered with examples of vanguards who knew better than the people they sought to liberate. The results have generally been bad—bad politically, in that the vanguards alienated their constituencies; bad ethically, in that they treated other adults as objects of correction rather than partners in shared deliberation. Feminism has had its own vanguardist moments and they have been among its least successful. The instinct to look at the next generation of women and conclude that they are insufficiently feminist, that their choices reveal their lack of understanding, that the movement must therefore intensify its prescriptions—this instinct should be examined carefully.

A more productive stance, I think, would be the following. Make the case for sharing care work openly and persuasively, in argument rather than in admonition. Identify the policy reforms that reduce the costs of equal sharing—tax reform, parental leave, childcare. Trust women, including young women, to reason about their own lives once the conditions of reasoning are improved. And resist the temptation to conclude that, when women decide differently from how feminist theorists would prefer, the women must be wrong rather than the theorists. Sometimes the women are wrong. Sometimes the theorists are. The work of distinguishing the two cases is the actual work of feminist thought, and it cannot be done by formula.

For these women, the choice between career and family is genuinely a choice. They have enough education and credentials to re-enter the workforce if they leave it. They have resources that allow them to cushion the costs of caregiving. And they have husbands or partners who, if not equal, at least share some of the burdens of domestic life.

For women at the bottom of the economic ladder, the choice is not really a choice at all. They cannot afford to stay home with their children; they need wages to survive. But they also cannot afford quality childcare, which means their children are often raised in suboptimal conditions. The welfare system, as reformed in the 1990s, pushed these women into low-wage jobs that pay little and offer no flexibility for caregiving emergencies. The result is that they do poorly at both caregiving and employment, not because they care too much or too little but because the system offers them no good options.

This class dynamic is visible in Hirschmann's account of her students. When they read about the vulnerability of married stay-at-home mothers, they are skeptical; they believe good mothers should be supported by their husbands. When they read about single mothers on public assistance, they are condemnatory; they believe these women should work. This double standard—the expectation that middle-class women can afford to stay home while working-class women should be forced to work—has been present in American feminism from its beginnings, and it is deeply troubling.

Hirschmann correctly identifies this inconsistency but does not fully explain it. She notes that her students "draw on the idea of care to shore up class divisions and selfish interest." But why? Partly, I suspect, because they have been taught to think of feminism as a matter of individual choice and personal empowerment, not collective responsibility. When feminism is privatized in this way, it becomes easy to judge other women's choices while defending one's own.

What would a class-conscious care feminism look like? It would start from the recognition that the problems of caregiving cannot be solved at the individual level. Middle-class women can hire nannies and buy their way out of some childcare burdens; working-class women cannot. The solution must be collective: public childcare, parental leave, flexible work arrangements, wages sufficient to live on.

It would also recognize that the feminist movement has sometimes been more concerned with the advancement of professional women than with the liberation of all women. The "opt-out" phenomenon is a problem mainly for women who have the option to opt in. For women at the bottom, the issue is not whether to work but how to survive in a labor market that pays them poorly and offers them no security.

Hirschmann's argument that women need to care less is particularly problematic from a class perspective. For middle-class women, caring less might mean accepting career setbacks or demanding more from husbands. For working-class women, caring less might mean leaving children in unsafe situations or abandoning them altogether. The advice is class-blind, and its obliviousness to class difference weakens it.

I do not want to dismiss Hirschmann's essay entirely. Her critique of care feminism's failure to change male behavior is valid, and her analysis of the tax system's biases is important. But any serious feminism must take class seriously, and must recognize that the choices facing middle-class women are very different from those facing working-class women. A feminism that speaks only to the former will not be a feminism at all.

Diversified MiniMax

"The critique of 'care feminism' fails because it wrongly blames the theory for social stagnation and incorrectly suggests that women must prioritize wage labor over personal caregiving choices to achieve equality."

Nancy Hirschmann has written a thoughtful essay, but I fear it misunderstands both the nature of care and the meaning of freedom. In her eagerness to critique what she calls "care feminism," she repeatedly conflates the legitimate theoretical insight that care work has social value with a policy prescription that women must work for wages to achieve equality. This conflation is not just an analytical error; it reinforces precisely the logic that has made many women feel that feminism is hostile to their lives and choices.

Let us be clear about what care feminism originally claimed. When Carol Gilligan suggested that an "ethic of care" represents a distinctive moral orientation—one that values responsiveness to particular others rather than application of abstract principles—she was not arguing that women should stay home or that men should become more domestic. She was arguing that women's experiences, including experiences of caregiving, offer valid moral insight that has been systematically dismissed or devalued. The claim was epistemological, not prescriptive.

Hirschmann acknowledges this but then treats the failure of men to change as a failure of care feminism itself. But this logic would prove too much. Liberal feminism claimed that equal treatment would lead to equal outcomes; it did not. Radical feminism claimed that consciousness-raising would lead to collective action; it did not. Do we conclude from these failures that the theories were wrong, or simply that social change is hard? Hirschmann seems to apply a standard to care feminism that she would not apply to other theoretical traditions.

More troubling is her suggestion that women who choose full-time motherhood are, in effect, letting down other women. This is a collectivist logic that sits uncomfortably within a liberal feminist framework that has long championed individual choice. When Ann Cudd argues that women who choose traditional roles "support a system of patriarchal oppression," she is treating women as instruments of feminist progress rather than as agents making decisions in their own interests. This is not liberation; it is a new form of obligation dressed in feminist clothing.

The stories Hirschmann tells about Susan, Anthony, and Carla are meant to show the failures of doting mothers. But these are anecdotes, not evidence. Some full-time mothers raise children who cheat and drive drunk; some working mothers raise children who do the same. The existence of individual failures does not indict a lifestyle choice any more than the existence of corrupt lawyers indicts the legal profession. We should judge practices by their potential, not by their worst manifestations.

I also find Hirschmann's treatment of public goods puzzling. She argues that if care is a public good, then the public has a right to oversee how mothers perform that work. But the logic here is strange. Almost everything that benefits society could be framed as a public good—art, science, education—and yet we do not typically think that the state should supervise artists to make sure they produce the "right kind" of art. The value of care does not entail state oversight of parenting any more than the value of education entails state control of what teachers believe.

Finally, Hirschmann's conclusion—that women need to care a little less—is chilling. What does it mean to care less? For a mother, it might mean being less attentive to a child's needs, less present for important moments, less emotionally available. Is this really what Hirschmann wants? Or does she mean something else—that women should care less about whether their choices conform to feminist theory? If the latter, I agree; women should make the choices that are right for their families and their lives, without worrying about whether those choices serve as proper feminist models. But that is a very different conclusion from the one Hirschmann seems to draw.

Care feminism, at its best, has reminded us that the work of sustaining families and communities is morally valuable and should not be dismissed. The solution to inequalities in how that work is performed is not to abandon care but to insist that men take it seriously. That requires political and cultural change, yes. But it does not require women to care less about those they love.
