"While the policy proposals of the Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) network are valuable, the project's technocratic framing mistakenly prioritizes expert-led policy design over the organized worker power necessary to achieve and sustain economic reform."

A Note from Outside the Seminar Room

I have spent the last fifteen years organizing low-wage workers, and I want to offer a worker's-eye view of the EfIP manifesto. The first thing to say is that it is welcome. The proposals on wage boards, on unions, on multinational taxation, on bank regulation, would all materially improve the lives of the people I work with. Arindrajit Dube's wage board proposal in particular is the kind of structural innovation that could transform low-wage labor markets in this country, and it deserves the attention it is getting.

The second thing to say is that I am wary of the framing.

The essay is structured as an internal address from economists to economists, with the broader public as a sort of audience. The implicit drama is whether the discipline can rehabilitate itself, whether mainstream economics can be persuaded to take up the cause of inclusive prosperity. This is a real question and I am rooting for the authors. But it is not the question I face day-to-day. The question I face is whether workers will get organized, whether they will build power, whether they will use that power to extract concessions from employers and the state. Economists are a useful resource in this work but they are not the protagonists. The protagonists are workers themselves.

This matters because the EfIP framing risks a kind of substitution. If "we" (economists) come up with the right proposals, then surely "they" (workers, voters, the public) will adopt them. The arrows run from expert to citizen, from journal article to policy. But policies adopted because they are technically good and not because anyone fought for them do not stay adopted. The minimum wage is a partial exception because it polls so well; but the Fight for $15 did not arise because economists had developed new monopsony models. It arose because workers in fast food walked off the job in 2012 and kept walking off until the political class noticed. The new economic research followed; it did not lead.

I worry, too, about the assumed audience for the proposals. Wage boards, formulary apportionment, contingent debt contracts—these are sophisticated policy instruments that require sustained political support to enact and to defend. Where does that political support come from? In the framing of the essay, it appears almost as a residual: we will produce good ideas and somehow the political system will deliver them. But the political system does not work this way. Ideas that benefit broad publics over concentrated interests require organized constituencies to push them through. Those constituencies have been hollowed out. Union density in the United States is around 10 percent and falling. Civic associations of the kind that used to convert popular sentiment into political pressure are weaker than they were a generation ago. The infrastructure for translating ideas into law has decayed.

The EfIP project does include Naidu's contribution on unions, which is exactly the right kind of thinking. But it sits in a portfolio dominated by technocratic instruments that presuppose a political environment we do not actually have. Wage boards work in Australia in part because Australian unions are stronger than American unions and because Australian political culture takes tripartite negotiation seriously. Transplant the institution to the United States and it will be captured by employer associations within a decade unless workers have independent power to defend it. The institution is not the proposal; the constituency that sustains the institution is.

I make this point not to dismiss the proposals but to situate them. Good policy is necessary but not sufficient. The harder work—building organizations, training organizers, winning fights, sustaining political coalitions over decades—is not work that economists can do, and not work that gets done by writing better white papers. The reason the right has won so much over the last forty years is not that it had better ideas but that it had better infrastructure: think tanks, donor networks, legal organizations, media outlets, training programs, and yes, organized constituencies of small business owners and evangelicals and gun owners. Inclusive prosperity will require infrastructure of comparable scale on the other side.

The good news is that some of this is being built. Worker centers, fight-for-fifteen, the Sunrise Movement, the resurgence of the DSA, teacher strikes from West Virginia to Los Angeles—the political ground is shifting in ways the EfIP framing does not quite capture. The economists associated with this project will, I hope, find ways to ally with these movements rather than simply offering them policy menus. The most useful economists I have worked with are the ones who show up at the union hall, who let the campaign shape the research question rather than the other way around, who treat workers as colleagues in producing knowledge rather than as consumers of expertise.

Inclusive prosperity is a political project, not a technocratic one. The technocrats are welcome. They should not, however, mistake themselves for the lead actors.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The essay incorrectly positions economists as the protagonists of economic change, whereas the true drivers of reform are workers who build power to extract concessions.
  2. 02Technical policy solutions lack longevity and effectiveness if they are not preceded and defended by organized political constituencies.
  3. 03The EfIP framework risks a 'substitution' error, assuming that good ideas naturally lead to adoption while ignoring that recent successes like the 'Fight for $15' were led by labor activism rather than academic research.
  4. 04Sophisticated policy instruments like wage boards are vulnerable to corporate capture in the U.S. unless there is a robust labor infrastructure to counter employer influence.
  5. 05Inclusive prosperity is fundamentally a political project that requires economists to act as allies to social movements rather than detached providers of policy menus.
