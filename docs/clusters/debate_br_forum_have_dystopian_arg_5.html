"Daron Acemoglu’s proposal to redirect AI toward human-complementing ends is an aspirational but unrealistic vision that underestimates the entrenched geopolitical, economic, and structural forces driving the current path of automation and surveillance."

With characteristic sweep, Daron Acemoglu provides a powerful historical narrative and a compelling diagnosis. Yet the very confidence with which he asserts that the future of AI is still open and can be redirected reveals a blind spot about the political economy of technology. The obstacles he acknowledges at the end — global competition, the weakening of democracy, the acceleration of automation during the pandemic — are not secondary complications. They are the main event, and they render his prescriptions mostly aspirational. Acemoglu has given us a utopian demand dressed in pragmatic clothing.

First, consider the global dimension. Acemoglu imagines a democratic awakening in the United States that produces a consensus for redirecting AI, followed by international coordination. This is wishful thinking of the highest order. The AI race is not a policy seminar; it is an ongoing geopolitical and economic contest. China is investing hundreds of billions of dollars in AI with the explicit aim of dominating strategic sectors, and its authoritarian model actively embraces facial recognition, social credit, and automated censorship as tools of state power. No amount of U.S. norm-shifting will alter that reality. Even if, by some miracle, American researchers and firms turned en masse toward human-complementing AI, Chinese firms and state-backed labs would eagerly supply the world with cheaper, automation-focused alternatives and surveillance packages. In global markets, the drive to cut labor costs is relentless, and companies will adopt the most efficient tools available regardless of their origin. Globalization strips national redirection efforts of their force.

Second, the historical parallel with energy technology is instructive but ultimately misleading. Acemoglu cites progress in clean energy as an example of successful redirection. Yet climate change is accelerating precisely because international coordination has failed: global emissions kept rising long after the first treaties, and even the Paris Agreement relies on voluntary pledges with no binding enforcement. The clean-energy sector has grown, but it has not remotely displaced fossil fuels at the pace required. If this is the model for AI, we should expect a long period of coexistence between “good” AI and the dominant surveillance-automation complex, with the latter causing irreversible damage to labor markets and democratic institutions before any redirection takes hold at scale.

Third, the historical account of the postwar golden age is deployed as if its conditions can be reactivated by policy tweaks. That era was not simply the result of enlightened government funding and strong unions. It was the product of a unique geopolitical configuration — the Cold War, which motivated massive public investment in science and technology for national security reasons, and the postwar settlement that incorporated labor into a productivity bargain because capital feared the alternative of socialist revolution. Neither condition holds today. The Soviet Union no longer exists to concentrate minds in Washington, and the global left is not a credible threat to the capitalist order. In the absence of those existential pressures, there is little reason to expect the kind of deep restructuring Acemoglu calls for.

Even the proposed policy levers are weaker than he admits. Removing distortionary tax incentives that favor capital over labor is sensible, but doing so would require overcoming fierce lobbying from the very tech giants that dominate the economy. Shifting government procurement away from surveillance technologies would mean the Pentagon and intelligence agencies voluntarily relinquishing tools they view as essential to national security at a time of great-power competition. Norm change among AI researchers? The field’s incentive structure is set by publication venue prestige, venture-capital funding, and corporate salaries. A few heroic researchers may resist, but as long as automation research is the path to career success and immense wealth, the aggregate direction will remain unchanged.

Acemoglu’s underlying optimism rests on the idea that AI’s future is genuinely “open” and that we face a genuine fork in the road. But the fork may already be behind us. The technological infrastructure of the digital economy — the datasets, the cloud platforms, the sensor networks, the user interfaces — has been built around surveillance and automation logics for two decades. Reversing that would require not just a shift in funding priorities but an active dismantling of existing socio-technical systems. No modern society has ever voluntarily dismantled a profitable technological regime on the scale needed here. The history of asbestos, leaded gasoline, and tobacco suggests that even when harms are unambiguous, path-breaking change takes decades of bitter struggle and often comes only after enormous damage has been done.

None of this means we should abandon hope entirely. But a more honest assessment would acknowledge that the most likely AI future is, in fact, the dystopian one Acemoglu warns against — not because it is inevitable in some metaphysical sense, but because the social forces arrayed against redirection are overwhelming relative to the countervailing power we can currently muster. The real challenge is not to design a blueprint for redirection; it is to build the political movements, transnational alliances, and institutional counterweights that might someday make such redirection possible. That starts by telling the truth: we are not at a fork but on a downward slope, and the first task is to stop the slide before we can even think about climbing back up. Acemoglu’s call to action is welcome, but his map underestimates how far we have already descended.

  3. 03The postwar 'golden age' of shared prosperity was the result of unique Cold War pressures and existential threats to capital that no longer exist to motivate deep structural reform.
  4. 04Proposed policy levers, such as tax reform and norm-shifting among researchers, are insufficient to overcome the massive lobbying power and financial incentives of the dominant tech giants.

"Daron Acemoglu’s proposal to redirect AI through domestic policy and international coordination is dangerously naive because it ignores the reality of great-power competition and the strategic necessity of winning the global AI race."

Daron Acemoglu’s essay opens with a reassuring thesis: the AI future is not preordained, and we can redirect it toward shared prosperity and democratic freedom. It closes, however, with a candid acknowledgment that the international dimension “deepens the challenge” and that any redirection “needs to be founded on at least a modicum of international coordination.” That qualification, tucked into the final section, actually overwhelms the entire preceding argument. In the real world of great-power competition, fragmented governance, and national security imperatives, the global AI race is not a collective action problem to be solved by well-meaning coordination. It is a strategic contest, and treating it as anything else is dangerously naive.

Acemoglu’s three-pronged approach of government policy, norm shifting, and democratic oversight assumes that the primary obstacle to good AI is domestic political dysfunction. But even if the United States achieved a miraculous democratic revival, reformed its tax code, and poured billions into human-complementing AI research, the rest of the world would not follow suit. China’s AI development is driven by a state-capitalist model that explicitly ties AI to military modernization, internal security, and economic competitiveness. The Chinese government has made AI supremacy a national priority, and its firms operate with access to vast datasets sourced from an environment where privacy rights are minimal. European democracies may sympathize with Acemoglu’s vision and have begun regulatory efforts like the AI Act, but Europe’s AI investment is dwarfed by both the United States and China, and its regulatory power is often more effective at constraining its own firms than at shaping global norms. In such an environment, unilateral redirection is strategic disarmament.

The automation dimension is particularly susceptible to global competitive pressure. If U.S. policy successfully steers domestic AI toward human-complementing tasks that preserve good jobs, but Chinese AI continues to produce cheap, scalable automation technologies, U.S. firms in tradable sectors will face a brutal choice: adopt the automation or lose market share. No amount of domestic tax reform can insulate the entire economy from global cost competition. The same logic applies to surveillance technologies. Even if democratic governments restrict their own use of facial recognition, Chinese firms like Hikvision and Dahua will continue to export advanced surveillance systems to authoritarian and democratic-leaning governments alike, often with financing from China’s Belt and Road Initiative. The result is a global infrastructure of surveillance that undermines freedom everywhere, regardless of what Western firms do.

Acemoglu’s reply might be that this is exactly why international coordination is needed. But what does coordination mean in a world where the major players have fundamentally incompatible political systems and strategic interests? The historical analogy of climate change, which he uses to illustrate successful redirection, is far from reassuring. After three decades of international negotiations, global greenhouse gas emissions are still rising, and the Paris Agreement’s voluntary pledges remain largely aspirational. If we cannot coordinate on a threat as existential as climate breakdown, how likely is coordination on the far more ambiguous terrain of AI’s social effects, where definitions of “beneficial” technology diverge along ideological lines?

A more realistic path acknowledges that AI is a dual-use technology with deep implications for national security and economic power. Rather than trying to redirect the entire field toward a universal notion of social benefit, democratic nations should adopt a bifurcated strategy. Domestically, they should impose strong democratic conditions on AI deployment — bans on government use of real-time biometric surveillance, strong privacy protections, algorithmic accountability — while simultaneously investing in defensive AI capabilities to counter authoritarian uses. Internationally, they should treat AI as akin to nuclear technology, where a regime of nonproliferation, export controls, and strategic arms limitation is the appropriate framework. This would mean restricting the export of the most capable AI hardware and software to authoritarian regimes, building secure, Western-centric AI supply chains, and using trade agreements and alliances to set binding rules on surveillance technology.

Acemoglu’s call for “democratic oversight” is exactly right, but it must be embedded in a national-security framework that recognizes the Hobbesian reality of global competition. The alternative is to pretend that norms and goodwill can bridge the gap between liberal democracies and authoritarian states, and in that gap, the dystopia Acemoglu fears will be realized — not because we failed to choose the better path, but because our adversaries chose a different one while we were busy building consensus. The future of AI will be shaped not by deliberative forums but by the balance of power. The task for democrats is to win that contest, not to escape it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Unilateral domestic redirection of AI toward human-complementing tasks constitutes strategic disarmament in the face of adversaries like China who prioritize military and economic supremacy.
  2. 02Global market pressures ensure that if the U.S. avoids automation to protect jobs, domestic firms will simply lose market share to foreign competitors using cheaper, scalable AI automation.
  3. 03The export of surveillance technologies by authoritarian states creates a global infrastructure of control that cannot be stopped by Western norm-shifting or domestic regulations.
  4. 04International coordination is an unrealistic solution given that major global powers have fundamentally incompatible political systems and a poor track record of cooperation on other existential threats like climate change.
  5. 05Democratic nations should abandon the hope of universal consensus and instead adopt a bifurcated strategy that combines domestic democratic constraints with aggressive national security measures, such as export controls and secure supply chains.
