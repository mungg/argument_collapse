"The lead essay’s sweeping condemnation of pre-Trump counterterrorism policies is a dangerous distortion that ignores the reality of jihadist threats and the necessity of robust, legally-sanctioned security tools."

The lead essay’s central claim—that pre-Trump counterterrorism policies constitute a bipartisan assault on Muslim Americans—deserves a serious response, not least because it mistakes entirely the moral calculus at play. The United States faces a real, ideologically motivated threat from jihadist groups that have killed thousands of Americans and tens of thousands of Muslims worldwide. While any decent person deplores bigotry against peaceful Muslims, the essay’s sweeping condemnation of surveillance, watchlisting, and prosecution obscures the necessity of robust counterterrorism tools and ignores the considerable efforts successive administrations have made to calibrate those tools with civil liberties. To treat the Obama administration’s program as merely a polite version of Trump’s crass bigotry is a dangerous distortion.

Let us begin with what the lead essay does not mention, or mentions only in passing: the thousands of terror incidents, both successful and foiled, since 9/11. The Boston Marathon bombing, the San Bernardino attack, the Pulse nightclub massacre, the bombings in New York and New Jersey, the vehicular attacks in Nice and Berlin—these are not abstractions. Each required planning, communication, and often financial support. The material support statute, which the authors caricature as a tool for criminalizing association and speech, is in fact what allows prosecutors to go after those who send money to al-Qaeda, those who buy plane tickets for aspiring foreign fighters, and those who translate extremist literature as part of a recruitment pipeline. Without it, we would be forced to wait until a bomb goes off before acting. The authors’ complaint that the statute is “overbroad” ignores the crucial distinction that courts have consistently drawn: it does not punish mere association or religious conservatism; it punishes the provision of specific material aid to designated foreign terrorist organizations. The Supreme Court upheld the law in *Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project* precisely because the government demonstrated that even ostensibly benign support like legal advice can free up a group’s resources for violence. That reasoning remains sound.

The essay’s treatment of surveillance is similarly one-sided. Yes, Edward Snowden revealed that the NSA’s bulk collection program swept up metadata from millions of Americans, including many with no connection to terrorism. But the program was not a rogue operation targeting Muslims; it was a bipartisan creation, renewed under both Bush and Obama, and its defenders argued—with some merit—that it closed intelligence gaps that might have prevented 9/11. Crucially, the program was reformed in 2015 through the USA FREEDOM Act, which ended bulk collection and increased transparency. The lead essay writes as if nothing has changed since 2001. In reality, the debate over surveillance has been robust, and the pendulum has swung toward privacy protections even while preserving necessary capabilities.

More troubling is the insinuation that the War on Terror has been an anti-Muslim project from the start. This claim collapses two distinct things: the necessary targeting of jihadist networks that happen to operate in Muslim-majority regions, and animus toward Islam as a faith. The United States has bombed six of the seven countries in Trump’s ban not because they are Muslim, but because they harbor terrorist groups that threaten American interests and citizens. Syria is a cauldron of ISIS and al-Qaeda factions; Somalia is home to al-Shabaab; Yemen hosts al-Qaeda in the Arabian Peninsula. The Obama administration’s drone campaign, while morally fraught and at times legally dubious, was directed at specific militant leaders, not at civilian populations. That civilians died is a tragedy, but it is not evidence of Islamophobia; it is the grim reality of asymmetric warfare against an enemy that hides among the innocent.

What about domestic counterterrorism? The essay points to entrapment-style stings as proof of a biased system. There is a kernel of truth here: some FBI operations have been ham-fisted and have targeted individuals who would never have considered violence without the government’s prodding. But these cases represent a small minority of terrorism prosecutions. The vast majority involve defendants who actively sought to join terrorist groups, purchased bomb-making materials, or conspired to carry out attacks. To paint the entire national security apparatus as a machine for manufacturing crimes is to slander the thousands of law enforcement officers who have prevented attacks—including the 2015 Garland, Texas shooting and the 2009 plot to bomb the New York subway. The alternative to preemptive surveillance and prosecution is reactive policing, which means more dead bodies.

The airport protests, which the lead essay holds up as a model of civic awakening, missed a crucial point. The first travel ban was a ham-handed, poorly drafted order, but the core idea of tighter vetting from states with dysfunctional security services is not irrational. The Obama administration itself identified these seven countries as “countries of concern” under the Visa Waiver Program Improvement Act of 2015, imposing additional travel restrictions. The difference was one of degree and process, not of kind. If we accept that some level of screening is legitimate—and virtually every nation exercises border control—then the debate should be about the precise mechanisms, not about the supposed bigotry of wanting to know who is entering the country from war zones.

The litmus test that the essay derides—“if you’re a Muslim and you love America and freedom and you hate terror, stay here and help us win”—is in fact a perfectly reasonable expectation of all citizens, regardless of faith. Immigrants have long been asked to pledge allegiance to the Constitution and to renounce foreign allegiances. That Clinton’s version singled out Muslims reflects the sad reality that a tiny minority of those who claim to act in Islam’s name have waged war against the United States. It is not bigoted to ask that all Americans, Muslim or not, reject terrorism and support the rule of law. The Khans were celebrated not because they performed a script of “good Muslim” submission, but because they modeled the highest form of civic virtue: sacrificing for a country that, despite its flaws, offers more freedom and opportunity than any society in human history. That is not orientalism; it is patriotism.

None of this is to deny that American Muslims have suffered unjust suspicion. Post-9/11 backlash was real and ugly. The No Fly List is opaque and in dire need of reform, and the government’s reliance on secret evidence in some proceedings should trouble anyone who cares about due process. But the reforms we need are targeted, not revolutionary. We need a clear, adversarial process for challenging No Fly List inclusion. We need sunset provisions and independent oversight for surveillance authorities. We need to ensure that CVE programs are strictly limited to violent extremism and do not become tools for thought-policing. These are the kinds of changes that can be achieved legislatively without abandoning the fundamental obligation of the state to protect its people.

The greatest danger of the lead essay’s frame is that it equates the fight against jihadist terrorism with the War on Drugs—a deeply flawed analogy. Mass incarceration of drug offenders was a domestic policy, driven largely by the criminalization of nonviolent conduct that was widespread across all racial groups. The War on Terror, by contrast, is a response to a transnational ideology that explicitly seeks to kill Americans and destroy free societies. To treat the monitoring of potential terrorists as analogous to the racist stop-and-frisk of Black youth is to misunderstand both the nature of the threat and the purpose of the policing. A young Black man stopped for “fitting the description” is a victim of pure racial profiling; a known associate of an ISIS recruiter placed under surveillance is the subject of legitimate intelligence-gathering.

Trump’s rhetoric has been disgraceful, and his executive order was a sloppy, cruel mess. But the way to oppose his excesses is not to pretend that the pre-Trump settlement was equally illegitimate. It is to insist on a strong but lawful counterterrorism framework, one that recognizes the threat for what it is, protects civil liberties through rigorous oversight, and refuses to tar an entire religion with the sins of a fanatical fringe. That middle path requires a clarity that the lead essay, for all its moral fervor, does not offer.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The material support statute is a vital tool for preventing terrorism before it occurs rather than a mechanism for criminalizing mere association or speech.
  2. 02Surveillance programs are bipartisan, subject to ongoing reform and oversight, and are designed to close intelligence gaps rather than target a specific religious group.
  3. 03U.S. military actions in Muslim-majority countries are responses to specific militant threats and the realities of asymmetric warfare, not expressions of Islamophobia.
  4. 04Preemptive domestic policing and stings are necessary to prevent mass-casualty attacks, and cases of government overreach represent only a small minority of prosecutions.
  5. 05The expectation that citizens reject terrorism and support the rule of law is a reasonable standard of patriotism rather than a discriminatory litmus test for Muslims.
  6. 06The analogy between the War on Terror and the War on Drugs is flawed because counterterrorism targets a violent transnational ideology rather than nonviolent domestic conduct.
