"Professional cheerleaders should be treated as legitimate employees entitled to fair wages, workplace protections, and collective bargaining rights rather than being exploited as hobbyists."

The problem is not that professional cheerleaders lose their discipline after college. It is that the professional leagues often stop treating that discipline as work. A performer who must audition, rehearse, maintain a prescribed appearance, appear at games and charity events, follow detailed conduct rules and risk injury is plainly an employee, not a hobbyist grateful for “exposure.”

The first step is legal and simple: pay cheerleaders for every required hour. That means auditions after selection, practices, games, calendar shoots, sponsor appearances, travel time where required, and overtime where law requires it. The recent lawsuits by Raiderettes, Buffalo Jills and others have made visible a business model that depends on glamour to conceal wage theft. In multibillion-dollar leagues, minimum wage compliance is not a financial burden; it is a test of seriousness.

Second, teams should professionalize the job in the same way schools are beginning to professionalize the sport. Competitive cheerleading demands strength, timing and coaching. Pro squads should have written contracts, workers’ compensation coverage, medical protocols, safe training standards and independent channels for harassment complaints. Rules about weight, dating players or how to behave in public should be limited to legitimate workplace concerns, not humiliation or control.

Finally, cheerleaders need collective power. Whether through unions, guilds or state-by-state legal action, they should bargain over pay, image rights, safety and scheduling. Individual cheerleaders are easily replaced; organized workers are not.

Keeping a competitive edge should not mean accepting second-class status. It should mean bringing the athleticism of cheerleading into the workplace with the protections every other professional performer expects: pay, safety, dignity and a real voice in the terms of the job.

"Professional cheerleaders can maintain their competitive edge and athletic status by collectively demanding the same legal protections, fair wages, and structural reverence afforded to other professional athletes."

The cheers that echo through high school gymnasiums and packed college arenas carry a message that professional sports leagues have stubbornly ignored: cheerleading is an elite athletic discipline, not a sideshow. New York just joined 34 other states in declaring competitive high school cheerleading a sport, a recognition that reflects the tumbling, stunting, and rigorous training that rival gymnastics and dance. Yet the moment a cheerleader signs with an NFL or NBA franchise, that hard-won athletic identity evaporates. The women who fly, flip, and land with precision on the sidelines are rebranded as “entertainers” and handed a pay structure that violates minimum wage laws—sometimes doling out a flat fee per game that, once broken into hours of practice, appearances, and community events, works out to less than $3 an hour. The recent lawsuits from Raiderettes, Buffalo Jills, and Cincinnati Ben-Gals have peeled back the pom-pom curtain, revealing a system where cheerleaders are stripped of their competitive edge not by a lack of talent but by a refusal to see them as athletes.

Keeping that edge begins with the cheerleaders themselves rejecting the am-bassadorial label and reclaiming their athletic authority. In the high school and college ranks, they train year-round, compete for national championships, and suffer concussions and fractures just like their male peers on the field. That mindset—the discipline of perfecting a routine under scoreboard pressure—doesn’t vanish when they swap a varsity uniform for a pro one. What vanishes is the structural reverence that protects athletes: minimum salary floors, health benefits, and a voice in working conditions. To carry their competitive fire into the professional world, cheerleaders must collectively insist on those very protections. The federal court decision last month classifying Raiderettes as employees, not independent contractors, is a critical wedge. It means these women are legally entitled to minimum wage and overtime, and it opens the door to demanding the same union representation that players’ associations provide. A collective bargaining agreement wouldn’t just fix the money; it could mandate safe practice facilities, limit the number of unpaid appearances, and establish injury protocols—the scaffolding that lets an athlete focus on performance instead of survival.

Critics argue that pro cheerleading is a part-time gig, a stepping stone, and therefore unworthy of a salary akin to professional sports. But that logic dissolves when you watch a squad execute a stunt sequence that requires the trust and athleticism of a competitive partner stunt. The only difference is that nobody pays a high school point guard pennies to suit up for a varsity game. If we are serious about closing the respect gap, fans and team owners must treat the transition from the competition mat to the sideline not as a demotion but as a logical next step for gifted athletes. Cheerleaders can keep their competitive edge by building on the legal victories already in motion, transforming a humiliating workplace into an arena where their prowess—not just their appearance—takes center stage.

"Professional cheerleaders can maintain their competitive edge and professional status by being legally classified as employees, paid fair wages, and protected from discriminatory workplace practices."

The contrast the question draws is real and worth taking seriously. A high school senior who tumbles competitively for ESPN cameras under recognized safety rules can sign with an NFL squad and suddenly find herself earning $5 an hour, paying out of pocket for hair and nails, submitting to "jiggle tests," and being fined for weight gain. The Raiderettes, Buffalo Jills, and Bengals Ben-Gals lawsuits filed over the past year have laid this out in unflattering detail. The problem isn't that cheerleading stops being athletic at age 22; it's that pro franchises have classified these athletes as something between hobbyists and hostesses.

The fix is mostly unglamorous employment law, not cultural reinvention. Teams worth over a billion dollars cannot credibly argue that the women performing in their stadiums are independent contractors or seasonal amusement workers exempt from wage-and-hour rules. Courts will likely sort this out — the Raiderettes case looks strong under California labor law — but legislatures could move faster by clarifying that game-day performers are employees entitled to minimum wage, overtime, and reimbursement for required expenses. Unionization, modeled on the way dancers organized on Broadway through AGMA, is another avenue, though the short seasons and large applicant pools make it harder.

Beyond pay, the appearance-policing deserves scrutiny. Personal grooming policies that require thousands of dollars in mandatory salon visits, or weigh-ins enforced by fines, would not survive in almost any other workplace. Teams should be made to defend them under the same standards applied to flight attendants decades ago.

Cheerleaders keep their competitive edge the same way other skilled workers do: by being paid like professionals, organized like professionals, and treated as athletes rather than as decorative extensions of the franchise's marketing budget.

"Professional cheerleaders should be legally recognized and protected as employees entitled to fair wages and labor standards rather than being treated as hobbyists or ambassadors."

Professional cheerleaders are workers, and the law should treat them like workers. That is the simple insight behind the lawsuits filed in recent months by former members of the Oakland Raiders' Raiderettes, the Cincinnati Ben-Gals, and the Buffalo Jills. The complaints describe pay structures that, when divided by the actual hours of practice, rehearsal, charity appearances, and game-day work, fall well below the federal minimum wage. They describe fines for gaining weight, for forgetting pompoms, for failing to bring the right shade of lipstick. They describe mandatory "jiggle tests." If any other category of employee in a billion-dollar industry were treated this way, the case would already be over.

The "competitive edge" framing in the question is a distraction. These women are not asking to be respected as athletes in some abstract cultural sense; they are asking to be paid for the hours they work and not to be charged for the privilege of being employed. The NFL likes to describe cheerleading as a part-time hobby or a community ambassadorship, but the contracts at issue require hundreds of hours of unpaid rehearsal and dozens of unpaid appearances. That is employment, and the Fair Labor Standards Act applies whether the employer calls you an athlete, an entertainer, or an "ambassador."

The structural problem is that cheerleading sits at the intersection of two industries — sports and entertainment — that have both perfected the art of extracting unpaid labor from people who feel lucky to be there. Hollywood has its unpaid interns; sports leagues have minor leaguers and cheerleaders. Both rely on the same logic: there are more aspirants than slots, so the labor market clears at zero. The answer is the one labor law has always given to that logic: a floor below which contracts cannot go.

What cheerleaders need is not a pep talk about keeping their edge. They need the wage-and-hour suits to succeed, they need state labor commissioners to investigate, and ideally they need a union. The NFL Players Association cannot organize them, but nothing prevents an independent association of professional cheerleaders from forming, especially now that several squads are litigating in parallel. The women on the sidelines are doing the hard work. The least we can do is recognize that it is, in fact, work.

A man who plays professional sports is celebrated for physical excellence and paid handsomely for the risks of the job. A woman who performs beside him may be told that the privilege of proximity is payment enough.

That is a deeply gendered bargain. It depends on the idea that young women should be grateful for visibility, even when the visibility is packaged and sold by someone else. It also depends on silence. Many cheerleaders are replaceable in the eyes of management because there is always another talented applicant who hopes the job will open doors.

Keeping a competitive edge, then, requires more than better choreography. It requires refusing the old script. Cheerleaders should have written contracts, wage protections and anti-harassment procedures, but they also need a voice in defining the work. Who decides what uniforms communicate? Who writes the rules about body size? Who assigns public appearances? Who protects a cheerleader when a sponsor, fan or executive crosses a line?

Those decisions should not be made solely by team executives or squad directors whose job is to preserve the brand. Cheerleaders need representation in the room: elected captains with real authority, alumni advisory boards, access to outside counsel and, where possible, collective bargaining. Employment law can establish the floor; self-governance can change the culture.

It is encouraging that states are beginning to recognize cheerleading as a sport at the school level. But recognition in adolescence means little if adulthood brings a return to ornamental status. The test of respect is not whether a cheerleader is praised for being fit, pretty or spirited. The test is whether she can say no, report mistreatment, negotiate pay and still keep her job.

Professional cheerleaders do not need rescue from cheerleading. They need protection from an industry that has too often mistaken discipline for docility.

  2. 02The industry relies on a gendered bargain that expects women to accept visibility and prestige in lieu of actual wages and workplace protections.
  3. 03The replaceability of cheerleaders and the pressure for docility allow management to maintain a power imbalance that silences complaints of mistreatment.
  4. 04True professional respect requires structural changes such as written contracts, anti-harassment procedures, and collective bargaining rights.
  5. 05Cheerleaders need a formal voice in self-governance and decision-making to challenge the culture that treats them as brand assets rather than athletes.

The answer is not to pretend that every professional cheerleading job can become a lucrative athletic career. Many sideline squads are part-time entertainment and promotion jobs in a market with far more applicants than openings. That reality will not disappear because states recognize high school cheerleading as a sport.

But market reality is not a license to break labor law. A restaurant cannot pay servers less than the law allows because many people want restaurant jobs. A fashion company cannot avoid wages by saying models receive “exposure.” Sports teams should be held to the same standard. If they require rehearsals, game appearances, calendars, promotional events and strict conduct rules, then they should pay for the time they control.

A more honest system would begin before auditions. Applicants should receive a realistic estimate of required hours, expected expenses and likely pay. If the job pays a seasonal stipend, the team should translate that into an hourly figure. If dancers must buy certain products, maintain particular styling or pay for travel, that should be disclosed. No one should have to win the spot before learning that the prize costs money.

Cheerleaders also need to be strategic. The prestige of an N.F.L. or N.B.A. logo can cloud judgment. A young woman with elite dance or cheer training may have other options: coaching, choreography, college programs, fitness instruction, commercial dance, teaching, or independent performance groups. Teams have bargaining power because applicants treat the uniform as priceless. The more cheerleaders compare opportunities and share information, the less power secrecy will have.

Some franchises may decide that paying legal wages and providing protections makes a squad less attractive financially. If so, that is not a tragedy. A business that cannot exist without underpaid labor should change or disappear. Fans attend games for the game; if cheerleaders are retained, it should be because the team values them enough to compensate them openly.

The competitive edge cheerleaders need in the working world is not just a sharper toe touch or cleaner routine. It is economic literacy: read the contract, count the hours, ask what the opportunity costs, and be willing to walk away from a famous brand that offers applause instead of pay.
