The Climate Analogy Is Worse Than Acemoglu Thinks

Daron Acemoglu's essay reaches, near its end, for a comparison: just as we have begun to redirect technological progress toward low-carbon energy through a combination of policy, norms, and democratic oversight, so can we redirect AI toward labor-augmenting and rights-respecting applications. The analogy is meant to provide reassurance—we have done this before, we can do it again—and a template for action.

I want to challenge this comparison, not to abandon his project but to clarify what it actually requires. The climate analogy is, in important ways, misleading. AI governance differs from climate governance in features that make Acemoglu's prescription harder to implement than he acknowledges. Understanding these differences should sharpen the agenda rather than soften it.

First, climate has a measurement victory that AI lacks. The success of climate policy, such as it is, rests on a clear, physically grounded metric: tons of CO2-equivalent emitted into the atmosphere. This metric is not always easy to measure at the source, but it is conceptually clean, scientifically validated, and aggregable across jurisdictions and sectors. Disagreements exist about how to count, but the underlying quantity is real. Decades of work by climate scientists produced this measurement infrastructure before the political battles began, and that infrastructure is what allows carbon taxes, cap-and-trade systems, and emissions disclosures to function at all.

AI has nothing remotely comparable. Acemoglu proposes the labor share of value added as a candidate metric, but the labor share is endogenous to dozens of variables—wages, hours, capital stocks, organizational form, accounting practices, sectoral composition—that have nothing to do with the technology itself. Two firms deploying identical AI systems can show wildly different labor-share effects depending on their existing workforce composition, their reimbursement rates, their tax planning, and their accounting choices. Any policy that subsidizes "labor-augmenting" AI based on this metric will be a subsidy for accounting cleverness, not for technology direction.

What would be required to give AI policy a measurement foundation comparable to climate? Something like a basic-research agenda within the social sciences, on the order of decades, dedicated to developing valid measurements of AI's labor-market and political effects. This is not a luxury; it is a precondition for the kind of redirection Acemoglu wants. We are nowhere near it.

Second, climate has a stationary target; AI does not. The carbon problem has been understood, in its essentials, for at least forty years. The basic science of greenhouse forcing has not changed materially since the 1980s. This stability has allowed institutions to develop, scenarios to be modeled, and policies to be iterated against a fixed target.

AI is a moving target. The state of the art changes every six months. The applications that exist today were unimaginable five years ago, and those that will exist in 2026 are unimaginable now. Building a regulatory framework against this kind of moving target is extraordinarily hard, because any specific rule risks being either over-broad (capturing technologies that should be encouraged) or instantly obsolete (failing to capture the actual harm). This is a structural problem that no amount of climate-style framework-building can solve. AI governance has to be principle-based and outcome-focused, not rule-based and technology-specific, and this changes what "policy" can look like.

Third, climate has a global cooperation problem that is genuine but bounded; AI's is unbounded. The climate problem is shared—every country emits, every country suffers, and no country can solve it alone. This creates real coordination challenges (free riding, monitoring, enforcement), but it also creates a shared interest that can, with effort, be operationalized in treaties.

AI is not like this. AI is dual-use, with massive military and economic implications that make it the focus of strategic competition rather than cooperation. The U.S. and China are not collaborating on AI governance; they are racing. The leading developers within each country are competing for talent and markets, not coordinating on standards. Climate, even with all its difficulties, has a Paris Agreement and an IPCC. AI has neither and is unlikely to get either in the next decade. The international coordination Acemoglu calls for is not just hard; it is structurally less likely than the climate analogy suggests.

Fourth, the political economy of redirection differs in critical ways. Climate redirection involved displacing incumbents (oil and gas) in favor of new entrants (renewables, EV makers). The incumbents had political power, but they also had geographically concentrated political weakness: their plants and jobs were in specific places, while the costs of their pollution were diffuse. This made it possible, over time, to build coalitions for restraint, especially as renewables became cheaper and produced their own constituencies.

The political economy of AI is different. The incumbents (Google, Meta, Amazon, Microsoft) are not geographically concentrated and do not have visible local liabilities. Their products are loved by consumers; their political contributions are diffuse and bipartisan; their workforce is mobile and well-paid. The constituency for restraint is harder to build because the harms are diffuse and probabilistic, while the benefits are immediate and tangible. This is the inverse of the climate situation, where harms are increasingly visible (wildfires, hurricanes) and benefits of the status quo are taken for granted.

Fifth, climate has had decades of slow institutional accretion that AI has not. The EPA was founded in 1970; the IPCC in 1988; the UNFCCC in 1992; the Paris Agreement in 2015. The institutional infrastructure for climate policy is now massive and is the product of half a century of effort. AI policy, by comparison, is starting from near zero. The EU AI Act is in draft; the U.S. has no comprehensive AI legislation; the major international bodies (OECD AI Principles, GPAI) are voluntary and toothless. Building the institutional infrastructure for AI governance is itself a project of decades, and we do not have decades.

What follows from all this? Three things.

First, Acemoglu's optimism about redirection is too optimistic about its feasibility. The tools that worked for climate—measurement, treaties, market mechanisms—will work less well for AI, for the structural reasons above. This does not mean we should give up; it means we should pursue different tools.

Second, the practical agenda has to be more localized and more functional than the climate analogy suggests. Rather than trying to redirect "AI" as a category, we should regulate specific high-stakes applications in specific sectors: hiring algorithms in employment, credit-scoring in lending, recommendation systems on social media, facial recognition in policing, autonomous systems in safety-critical settings. Each of these has tractable governance questions that can be addressed with existing legal frameworks (anti-discrimination law, consumer protection, securities regulation, election law). The accumulation of these sector-specific interventions will, over time, shape the broader trajectory of the field.

Third, the most realistic levers are competition policy and procurement. The U.S. government is a massive purchaser of AI through the DoD, the intelligence community, Medicare, and federal contractors. If federal procurement required vendors to meet certain standards—on transparency, on bias auditing, on labor practices—the market for AI would shift quickly. Antitrust enforcement against the dominant platforms would create space for alternative business models that do not depend on surveillance and behavioral manipulation. These are blunt tools, but they have the advantage of being usable within existing legal frameworks and political coalitions.

Acemoglu's diagnosis is right; his analogy is misleading. The path he wants requires recognizing how unlike climate AI really is, and adapting accordingly. Otherwise we will spend years building measurement frameworks and international coordination mechanisms that will not arrive in time to matter.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01AI lacks a conceptually clean and physically grounded metric equivalent to CO2 emissions, making labor-share measurements vulnerable to accounting manipulation.
  2. 02The rapid, six-month evolution of AI technology makes it a moving target that renders traditional, rule-based regulatory frameworks obsolete almost immediately.
  3. 03Unlike the shared global interest in climate stability, AI is a dual-use technology driven by zero-sum strategic and military competition between nations.
  4. 04The political economy of AI differs from the energy sector because tech giants have diffuse political power and provide immediate consumer benefits that mask their long-term social harms.
  5. 05Effective AI governance should abandon broad category redirection in favor of regulating specific high-stakes applications within existing legal sectors like hiring, lending, and policing.
  6. 06The most practical levers for shaping AI's trajectory are localized tools like federal procurement standards and aggressive antitrust enforcement rather than international treaties.
