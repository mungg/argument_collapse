Diversified Gemini

"The pro-worker technological trajectory of the postwar era was driven by organized labor power rather than technocratic policy or researcher ethics, meaning the only way to redirect AI today is through the revitalization of unions and collective bargaining."

In his comprehensive analysis of artificial intelligence and the labor market, Daron Acemoglu identifies a crucial dynamic: technology is not destiny, and its current anti-worker trajectory is a choice. However, his roadmap for correcting this trajectory—relying on government funding metrics, tax tweaks, and an appeal to the "norms" of AI researchers—is a technocratic fantasy that entirely ignores the raw mechanics of political and economic power. The postwar shared prosperity Acemoglu rightly admires was not achieved because scientists had a strong moral compass or because the government properly measured Total Factor Productivity. It was achieved because organized labor held a gun to the head of capital. If we want AI to benefit the working class, we do not need nicer engineers; we need stronger unions.

Acemoglu acknowledges that labor market institutions played a role in the postwar boom, but he treats them as a parallel factor, secondary to the "direction" of technological research. This is backwards. The direction of technology is dictated by the balance of class power. In the 1950s, when a third of the private sector was unionized, corporations were forced to invest in technologies that augmented their workforce rather than replacing it, because replacing workers without the union's consent would lead to crippling strikes. The United Auto Workers, under Walter Reuther, famously negotiated the "Treaty of Detroit," which tied wage increases to productivity gains. It was this institutionalized worker power that forced technology to be deployed in a way that created "good jobs."

Today, the devastation wrought by automation in manufacturing and clerical work is not the result of AI naturally trending toward replacement. It is the result of a forty-year, bipartisan assault on the labor movement. Since the Reagan administration, the systematic dismantling of union power has removed the only meaningful friction preventing corporations from deploying technology solely for labor arbitrage. When Amazon implements algorithmic management systems that track warehouse workers' every movement and fire them by automated email, this is not a failure of "norms" among AI researchers. It is a failure of labor law that allows workers to be treated as disposable meat-cogs in a machine.

Appealing to the conscience of the AI community, as Acemoglu does, is a dead end. We cannot expect researchers whose livelihoods depend on the massive budgets of Alphabet, Meta, and Amazon to suddenly pivot to "worker-friendly" AI out of a sense of civic duty. The recent firings of ethical AI researchers at Google demonstrate exactly what happens when internal norms conflict with the corporate mandate for algorithmic dominance. Capital will always fund the technology that maximizes its return.

To redirect AI, we must alter the power dynamics of the workplace. This means passing aggressive pro-labor legislation, such as the Protecting the Right to Organize (PRO) Act currently stalled in the Senate. It means instituting sectoral bargaining, allowing workers to negotiate technology deployment standards across entire industries rather than fighting defensive battles shop by shop. In Germany, the system of co-determination requires companies to consult with works councils before introducing new technologies that affect working conditions. As a result, German manufacturing has adopted advanced robotics at a higher rate than the United States, yet has maintained a robust, high-wage industrial workforce. The workers have a seat at the table when the technology is implemented.

Acemoglu is right that democracy and shared prosperity are intertwined. But democracy does not just mean voting for a president every four years; it means having democratic agency in the place where you spend most of your waking hours. If the workers who are directly impacted by AI have no legal right or organized power to shape its implementation, no amount of benevolent government research funding will save them. Power concedes nothing without a demand. The future of AI will be decided on the picket line, not in the faculty lounge.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The direction of technological development is a direct reflection of the balance of class power rather than an independent choice made by researchers or government funders.
  2. 02Postwar shared prosperity was forced by high union density and agreements like the 'Treaty of Detroit' which mandated that technology augment rather than replace workers.
  3. 03The current trend toward labor-replacing automation is the result of a decades-long political assault on the labor movement that removed the friction preventing corporate labor arbitrage.
  4. 04Appealing to the moral norms or conscience of AI researchers is futile because corporate mandates for profit and dominance will always override ethical considerations.
  5. 05Meaningful redirection of AI requires structural labor reforms, such as the PRO Act and sectoral bargaining, to give workers a legal seat at the table during technology implementation.
