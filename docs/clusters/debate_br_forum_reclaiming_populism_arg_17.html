"The progressive left should reject the project of reclaiming populism because its inherent anti-institutional and anti-pluralist logic is fundamentally incompatible with a stable, multiracial liberal democracy."

In “Reclaiming Populism,” Adom Getachew poses a question that has haunted the progressive left since the unexpected rise of Donald Trump and the initial surge of Bernie Sanders: Can the language and structure of populism be harnessed for a multiracial, democratic, and egalitarian future? Getachew and the contributors to the symposium she introduces suggest that populism is not inherently a pathology of the right, but a democratic axiom. If every politician must appeal to “the people,” the argument goes, then the left must construct its own version of “the people”—one grounded in working-class solidarity and multiracial inclusion—to combat the right’s exclusionary, ethno-nationalist version.

While the desire to match the right’s political energy is understandable, particularly in the dark early days of a global pandemic that is exposing the brutal inequalities of American society, the project of “reclaiming” populism is a dangerous illusion. It fundamentally misdiagnoses what populism is and why it has historically been so destructive to liberal democracy. Populism is not simply the act of appealing to the public, nor is it merely a synonym for mass politics or popular mobilization. It is a specific political logic that divides society into two homogeneous and antagonistic groups: the “pure people” and the “corrupt elite.” By its very nature, populism claims that a single leader or movement possesses the exclusive moral representation of the “true people.” Consequently, anyone who disagrees with the populist is not merely a political opponent, but an enemy of the people themselves.

To argue, as Michael Denning does, that “every politician is a populist” stretches the definition of the term to the point of analytical uselessness. If making an electoral appeal to specific voting districts makes one a populist, then the term loses its ability to describe the distinct phenomenon we see in leaders like Donald Trump, Viktor Orbán, or Jair Bolsonaro. These leaders do not merely appeal to voters; they systematically attack the institutions of democratic pluralism—the independent press, the judiciary, the civil service, and the electoral system itself—precisely because these institutions are designed to check the unmediated will of the "people" as defined by the populist leader.

The progressive left’s temptation to adopt a left-wing populism—often looking to figures like Bernie Sanders or Jeremy Corbyn, or movements like Syriza and Podemos—rests on the belief that the "people vs. elite" binary can be filled with progressive economic content rather than reactionary racial resentment. In this vision, the "elite" are the billionaire class, Wall Street, and corporate monopolies, while the "people" are the multiracial working class. The goal is to build a majoritarian coalition that can sweep away the neoliberal establishment and enact transformative policies like Medicare for All and a Green New Deal.

However, the recent collapse of Sanders’s campaign in the United States and Corbyn’s devastating defeat in the United Kingdom should serve as a stark warning about the limits of this strategy. The populist framing inherently relies on an anti-institutionalism that alienates broad swathes of the electorate who, while perhaps dissatisfied with the status quo, still value democratic stability and institutional norms. When left-populists attack the “establishment” with the same vitriol as right-populists, they often end up undermining trust in the very state capacities required to enact a progressive agenda. You cannot build a robust, universal welfare state while simultaneously convincing the public that the government and all its institutions are irredeemably corrupt.

Moreover, populism’s reliance on defining a “pure people” inevitably struggles with the reality of democratic pluralism. A truly multiracial, democratic society is inherently diverse, not just in identity but in values, interests, and preferences. There is no single, unified “will of the people” waiting to be unleashed against the elite. There are only competing interests that must be negotiated, compromised, and balanced through democratic institutions. When left-populists encounter resistance from voters who do not share their revolutionary zeal—such as older African American voters in the South who decisively backed Joe Biden over Sanders—the populist logic struggles to compute. If the movement represents the "pure people," how can a vast segment of marginalized citizens reject it? The temptation is often to dismiss these voters as brainwashed by the "corporate media" or the "establishment," a discourse that drips with paternalism and betrays the pluralistic reality of the democratic electorate.

The COVID-19 pandemic, which Getachew rightly highlights as a crucible of existing inequalities, demonstrates precisely why the left should abandon populism in favor of competent, institutionalized social democracy. The crisis has laid bare the catastrophic consequences of right-wing anti-institutional populism. The Trump administration’s hollowing out of state capacity, its contempt for scientific expertise, and its prioritization of political loyalty over bureaucratic competence have led to a profoundly botched response. The solution to this is not a left-wing anti-institutionalism that simply swaps out the targets of populist ire. The solution is the patient, unglamorous work of rebuilding state capacity, restoring faith in democratic institutions, and fostering a civic culture based on pluralism, the rule of law, and evidence-based policymaking.

Progressives point to local movements, like the Chicago Teachers Union strikes, as evidence of a successful left populism. But these are better understood as examples of traditional labor organizing and civic mobilization. They are interest groups fighting for their constituents within a pluralistic framework, not movements claiming exclusive moral ownership of the polity. Strengthening unions, protecting voting rights, and building broad coalitions are essential tasks for the left. But these tasks are hindered, not helped, by wrapping them in the divisive and anti-pluralist rhetoric of populism.

As we look toward the 2020 election and the colossal task of pandemic recovery, the left must choose between the intoxicating but self-defeating purity of populism and the difficult, compromising, but ultimately durable work of democratic pluralism. We do not need a left-wing Trump; we need a renewed commitment to the messy, pluralistic reality of liberal democracy, bolstered by a strong welfare state. Reclaiming populism is a dead end. The true task is reclaiming democracy from the populists.
