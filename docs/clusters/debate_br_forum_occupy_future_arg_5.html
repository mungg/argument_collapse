"To achieve lasting institutional change and align American practices with its principles, the Occupy movement must transition from outsider protest to strategic engagement with formal political and legal institutions."

The lead essay asks what can be done to bring American institutions into better alignment with American principles. My answer begins with a warning: no movement can repair democracy while standing aloof from the institutions through which democratic decisions are made. Occupy Wall Street has changed the national conversation, but unless that change enters elections, legislation, administrative rulemaking, and party politics, it will be remembered as a dramatic season rather than a turning point.

This is not an argument for taming Occupy or turning every encampment into a precinct operation. Movements need independence. They need the ability to criticize allies, embarrass the complacent, and speak in moral terms broader than the next bill. But the history invoked by the lead essay—abolition, suffrage, civil rights—does not support the fantasy that outsider protest alone wins. Each of those struggles required translation into law, and law required politicians, votes, appointments, enforcement, and courts. The civil rights movement had sit-ins and marches; it also had the Civil Rights Act and Voting Rights Act. Without both, moral witness would not have become institutional change.

The immediate danger is that Occupy will mistake attention for power. Media coverage, viral images, and sympathetic polls matter, but they do not by themselves alter tax rates, foreclosure procedures, labor law, or bank capital requirements. Meanwhile, organized interests know how to wait. Banks can lobby quietly through the winter. Wealthy donors can shape the 2012 campaign. State legislatures can pass voter ID laws and weaken unions. A movement that refuses to engage the machinery of politics may preserve its purity while losing the country.

What would engagement look like? First, voter registration and protection should be central. If the movement believes that everyone has a right to be heard, it should fight laws that make voting harder for students, the poor, the elderly, and minorities. The right to vote is not a symbolic issue; it is the floor of democratic equality. Occupy groups could partner with civil rights organizations, unions, campus groups, and churches to make 2012 a referendum not only on candidates but on democratic access itself.

Second, Occupy should identify winnable campaigns at local, state, and national levels. At the local level: prevent foreclosures, defend public services, create participatory budgeting experiments, support living-wage ordinances, and move public money from banks that abuse communities to institutions with better practices. At the state level: protect collective bargaining, invest in public colleges, resist regressive tax structures, and regulate payday lending. At the national level: push for a serious jobs program, extension of unemployment benefits, mortgage relief, stronger implementation of Dodd-Frank, and higher taxes on very high incomes.

Third, the movement needs allies inside institutions. Some activists distrust unions, nonprofits, and progressive elected officials as compromised. Sometimes they are. But compromise is not the same as uselessness. Unions bring membership, resources, and experience. Community organizations bring local knowledge. Policy experts can turn outrage into enforceable rules. Sympathetic officials can open hearings, introduce bills, and resist repression. A movement that cannot distinguish co-optation from coalition will isolate itself.

Fourth, Occupy must develop a language that reaches beyond those already convinced. “The 99 percent” is a brilliant start because it avoids the narrowness of older left vocabularies. But the movement’s practices sometimes speak a smaller language: insider hand signals, long meetings, suspicion of ordinary political disagreement, and a style that can appear culturally exclusive even when formally open. If the goal is majoritarian reform, then people who attend church, run small businesses, serve in the military, work two jobs, or live far from big cities must see themselves in the movement. They may be angry at Wall Street without wanting to abolish capitalism. They may support higher taxes on millionaires while worrying about deficits. They must be persuaded, not scolded.

Fifth, demands are necessary, though not because pundits demand them. Demands help people know what joining means. They create standards by which politicians can be judged. They allow victories, and victories build confidence. A short national agenda could be broad enough to preserve unity: jobs now; foreclosure relief; debt relief for students; fair taxation; public financing and disclosure in elections; and real accountability for financial fraud. Not every occupier would find this sufficient, but movements need front doors.

The lead essay’s emphasis on principles can help here. The principle is not envy; it is equal citizenship. The problem is not that some people are rich; it is that wealth has become a form of political power that undermines opportunity and voice. That claim can unite liberals, many moderates, some libertarians, and disaffected conservatives who oppose crony capitalism. But unity will not happen automatically. It requires disciplined communication and strategic patience.

There is a romance in refusing politics as usual. After the bank bailouts, the Senate filibusters, the debt-ceiling debacle, and the endless fundraising rituals of Washington, that romance is understandable. But the alternative to politics as usual is not no politics. It is better politics. Better politics must be organized.

Occupy has given America a new moral vocabulary. It has made inequality speakable and plutocracy visible. Now it must decide whether it wants influence commensurate with its insight. To occupy the future is not only to hold space; it is to contest power where power is exercised. Parks matter because they gather citizens. Polling places, council chambers, regulatory hearings, union halls, and legislatures matter because they govern them.

"While the Occupy movement has successfully reframed the national conversation around inequality, it must now transition from dramatizing moral contradictions to building durable political institutions and specific policy campaigns to achieve lasting democratic reform."

The lead statement is right to begin with principles rather than policy. Occupy Wall Street has been most powerful when it has refused to let the country discuss the financial crisis as if it were a weather event: unfortunate, impersonal, nobody’s fault, and now behind us. The occupations have insisted that the crisis revealed a broken moral and political order. Millions lost homes and jobs; banks were rescued; executives kept bonuses; prosecutions were rare; and Washington quickly returned to arguing about deficits, as if the urgent problem were the moral failings of the unemployed rather than the institutional failures of finance and government.

The phrase “the 99 percent” matters because it translates statistics into citizenship. We already had data on inequality. We knew, from work by economists and from the Congressional Budget Office’s recent report, that income gains over recent decades have been extraordinarily concentrated at the top. We knew that productivity rose while wages stagnated for many workers. We knew that the share of income going to the top 1 percent had climbed toward levels not seen since before the New Deal. But data by themselves do not create democratic energy. “We are the 99 percent” turns inequality from an expert finding into a public identity.

Still, the lead essay’s historical analogy should be handled carefully. Slavery, women’s suffrage, and civil rights were not simply moments when America remembered its ideals. They were conflicts in which those excluded from full membership forced the country to change. They also required organization, sacrifice, and political strategy. Occupy has been magnificent at dramatizing a contradiction: a republic formally devoted to equal citizenship is increasingly governed by wealth. But dramatization is only the first democratic act. The next is institution-building.

The central disjuncture today is not merely that some people have more money than others. Inequality becomes politically poisonous when money is able to purchase voice, access, and impunity. It is one thing for a society to reward innovation, risk, or hard work. It is another for financial elites to socialize losses, privatize gains, and then use political influence to prevent meaningful reform. The foreclosure crisis, student debt, municipal austerity, and unemployment are not separate grievances. They are connected by a system in which ordinary people are told to bear risk alone while large institutions are treated as indispensable public wards.

A democratic response must therefore have several parts. First, financial reform must be made real, not merely enacted. The Dodd-Frank Act was a beginning, but the battle over implementation is where much of the future will be decided. Capital requirements, derivatives regulation, consumer protection, and limits on abusive lending are not technical footnotes; they are the architecture of democratic accountability. Second, the country needs a serious jobs program. It is perverse to tolerate mass unemployment while roads, schools, transit systems, and energy infrastructure decay. Public investment would not only put people to work; it would assert that citizens are not disposable.

Third, democracy itself needs repair. Campaign finance is not the only source of distortion, but after Citizens United it is impossible to deny that constitutional doctrine has moved in the wrong direction. Public financing, disclosure, shareholder approval for corporate political spending, and stronger lobbying rules are not procedural niceties. They are ways of restoring the equal right to be heard. Fourth, debt relief must be on the table. Student debt and underwater mortgages are shaping a generation’s life chances. A society that rescues banks but refuses to renegotiate impossible household burdens has announced whose futures count.

Occupy should resist the false choice between purity and pragmatism. Some critics demand a list of demands as if politics were a customer-service transaction. Others inside the movement fear that demands will narrow its imaginative force. Both sides have a point. A movement that becomes only a legislative agenda can be absorbed and forgotten. A movement that never translates outrage into institutional change may inspire but not govern. The task is to keep the moral horizon wide while building campaigns specific enough to win.

Nor should Occupy imagine that horizontal decision-making alone can redeem democracy. General assemblies are experiments in participation, and they are valuable for that reason. But they can also be exhausting, opaque to newcomers, and vulnerable to informal hierarchies. Democracy requires more than the absence of formal leaders; it requires accountability, inclusion, memory, and capacity. The movement’s commitment to prefiguring a better politics should be joined to an honest assessment of what works.

The lead essay asks whether we are entering another reform moment. The answer depends less on the purity of American principles than on the pressure citizens can bring to bear. The New Deal was not inevitable. Civil rights legislation was not inevitable. Both required movements capable of changing what politicians considered possible. Occupy has already changed the conversation. Inequality, plutocracy, and the moral economy of capitalism are now on the public agenda in a way they were not six months ago.

But conversations can fade. Winter will test the encampments; police actions already have. The deeper question is whether the occupations become networks—linking students, unions, foreclosed homeowners, indebted graduates, public workers, immigrants, and the unemployed. If they do, Occupy may help make American ideals less hollow. If not, it will remain an important protest against an intolerable arrangement, but not yet a democratic transformation. The future is open, but it will not occupy itself.
