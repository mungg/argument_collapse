"The societal crisis caused by AI is not a result of excessive automation or market failure, but rather the direct consequence of the monopolistic business model of surveillance capitalism."

Daron Acemoglu rightly sounds the alarm on the societal dangers of artificial intelligence, but his synthesis of labor economics and democratic decline fundamentally misidentifies the root cause of the crisis. He posits that AI is undermining society primarily through "excessive automation" that destroys good jobs, with the erosion of privacy and democracy acting as a parallel, secondary threat. In reality, the labor market disruptions are merely a byproduct of the true underlying disease: the monopolistic business model of surveillance capitalism. If we want to save democracy from AI, we must stop tinkering with R&D tax credits and focus entirely on breaking the unchecked power of Big Tech and outlawing the commercial extraction of human behavioral data.

Acemoglu wonders why technological progress since the 1980s has focused so heavily on automation rather than human augmentation. The answer is not a mysterious shift in "researcher norms" or a simple market failure of unpriced externalities. The answer is data. The breakthrough in AI during the late 1990s and 2000s—the shift toward statistical prediction—was entirely dependent on the massive, unstructured datasets harvested by a few emergent tech monopolies. Companies like Google and Facebook realized that every human interaction online could be tracked, commodified, and fed into machine learning algorithms to predict and manipulate future behavior.

This business model—surveillance capitalism, as articulated by Shoshana Zuboff—dictates the direction of AI research. AI is not primarily designed to automate factory floors; it is primarily designed to maximize user engagement, target advertising, and alter human behavior at scale. The algorithms that radicalize users on YouTube or manipulate voters on Facebook (as seen in the Cambridge Analytica scandal Acemoglu cites) are not side-effects of rogue AI. They are the core product working exactly as intended.

Acemoglu suggests we need a "measurement framework" to determine which AI applications are beneficial, combined with a shift in social norms among researchers. This is a hopelessly weak prescription for a structural monopoly problem. You cannot rely on the "norms" of a system whose fundamental economic incentive is the strip-mining of human privacy. The fact that a handful of companies—Google, Facebook, Amazon, Microsoft—account for the vast majority of global AI investment means they control the infrastructure of the future. They buy up potential competitors, monopolize the top academic talent, and control the cloud computing platforms required to run advanced AI models.

To redirect AI, we must deploy the blunt instrument of antitrust law. Just as the Progressive Era reforms broke up the railroad and oil trusts that threatened early 20th-century democracy, we must break up the data monopolies. The forced separation of platform utilities (like Amazon's marketplace or Google's search engine) from their proprietary products and advertising networks would instantly decentralize AI development.

Furthermore, we must fundamentally alter the legal status of data. Acemoglu worries about the government's ability to use AI for surveillance, but governments often simply purchase these capabilities from private data brokers. We need federal privacy legislation that goes beyond Europe’s GDPR. We must shift from a framework of "consent" (where users blindly click "agree" to labyrinthine terms of service) to a framework of strict data minimization, making it illegal to collect, store, or sell behavioral data that is not strictly necessary for the immediate function of a service.

If we cut off the raw fuel of surveillance capitalism—unlimited, unregulated behavioral data—we break the economic engine driving the most socially destructive applications of AI. Only by legally dismantling the surveillance business model and breaking up the tech monopolies can we clear the way for a decentralized, competitive market where AI might actually be directed toward solving human problems rather than manipulating human minds.
