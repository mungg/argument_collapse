Bad literary fiction can be every bit as formulaic as a bad thriller; it merely hides the formula behind better furniture and more weather.

This does not mean all pleasures are equal, or that taste is meaningless. Some books are deeper, sharper, more original, more durable. But the way to develop taste is not through shame. It is through reading widely and noticing what lasts after the plot is gone. The pleasure that begins with “what happens next?” can lead to “how did the writer do that?” and eventually to “what does this reveal about us?”

So my recommendation is simple: take the book you actually want to read, not the one you want strangers to see you holding. If it embarrasses you, remove the dust jacket. Better yet, remove the embarrassment.

Summer is not a test. It is an opening. The question is not whether the reading is easy, but whether the reader feels free.

  2. 02Engaging with a serious book during summer serves as a necessary rebellion against the fragmented and shallow attention spans fostered by modern digital media.
  3. 03The patience required by complex literature provides a unique mental depth and intoxication that television and lighter media cannot replicate.
  4. 04The idleness of summer provides the perfect conditions to overcome the intimidation or lack of time that usually prevents readers from starting significant works.
