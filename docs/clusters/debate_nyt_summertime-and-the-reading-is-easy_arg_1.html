I have never liked the idea of “guilty” reading. The guilt is usually class guilt in disguise: we are supposed to be embarrassed by books with plots, jokes, murders, ghosts, sex, dragons, or happy endings. Yet these are among the oldest engines of storytelling. The ancient Greeks did not sit around feeling sheepish about monsters and family curses.

A summer reading list should make room for appetite. If someone wants to spend a hot weekend with Agatha Christie, Stephen King, Raymond Chandler, Terry Pratchett, Nora Roberts, George R. R. Martin or Sue Grafton, that person is participating in a real literary tradition: suspense, comedy, romance, fantasy, detection. These genres require craft. Bad literary fiction can be every bit as formulaic as a bad thriller; it merely hides the formula behind better furniture and more weather.

This does not mean all pleasures are equal, or that taste is meaningless. Some books are deeper, sharper, more original, more durable. But the way to develop taste is not through shame. It is through reading widely and noticing what lasts after the plot is gone. The pleasure that begins with “what happens next?” can lead to “how did the writer do that?” and eventually to “what does this reveal about us?”

So my recommendation is simple: take the book you actually want to read, not the one you want strangers to see you holding. If it embarrasses you, remove the dust jacket. Better yet, remove the embarrassment.

Summer reading is not a confession booth. I would rather we drop the pretense. Read what holds you. If that is Aeschylus on the porch, wonderful. If it is a Danielle Steel novel inherited from your aunt's beach house, equally wonderful. The page does not know who is looking at it, and neither, in July, should you.

"The concept of the "summer read" is an artificial marketing construct created by the publishing industry rather than a genuine shift in reader psychology or taste."

The entire debate over "summer reads" and "guilty pleasures" is nothing more than a triumph of publishing industry marketing. There is no biological or environmental reason why a warm July breeze should suddenly make us crave badly written thrillers and formulaic romances.

The "beach read" is an artificial category, constructed by publishers to drive sales during what used to be a sluggish quarter. By branding mass-market commercial fiction as essential summer accessories—right alongside sunscreen and flip-flops—the industry successfully created a seasonal mandate to buy disposable books. We don’t experience a change in our literary tastes; we are simply responding to seasonal end-caps in bookstores and targeted summer marketing campaigns. Let's stop pretending that "summer reading" is a distinct psychological phenomenon and recognize it for the highly effective retail strategy it is.
