"While Sypnowich is correct that egalitarians must move beyond 'opportunity' toward 'flourishing,' a truly radical egalitarianism must specifically center the realities of human care and dependency to avoid the pitfalls of paternalism and the 'ideal worker' myth."

Christine Sypnowich’s case against equal opportunity is most compelling when read from the perspective of care. The language of opportunity imagines an independent chooser standing before a menu of options. The chooser has resources, rights, and access; then she decides. But much of human life does not look like that. We begin as dependent infants. Many of us become disabled, ill, frail, or cognitively impaired. All of us rely on networks of care, paid and unpaid, visible and invisible. Many people—disproportionately women, and disproportionately poor and racialized women—spend large parts of their lives meeting the needs of others. An egalitarianism centered on opportunity will miss these facts unless it is radically transformed.

Sypnowich’s turn to flourishing helps. Flourishing directs attention to how people actually live, not only to what choices are formally available. It asks whether they have health, rest, love, recognition, meaningful activity, beauty, and community. It recognizes that loneliness, anxiety, humiliation, and exhaustion are not peripheral to justice. A society can proclaim equal opportunity while leaving care workers underpaid, parents unsupported, disabled people isolated, and elderly people warehoused. Such a society has not achieved equality.

The care perspective also supports Sypnowich’s critique of responsibility. In luck egalitarian thought, the moral drama often concerns whether an individual’s disadvantage results from choice or circumstance. But care shows that need is not an embarrassing exception to agency; it is a normal feature of human life. A baby has not chosen dependence. A person with dementia has not chosen dependence. A worker injured on the job may not have chosen dependence. But even where choices are involved—having a child, caring for a parent, loving someone who becomes ill—the resulting needs should not be treated as private burdens that fall outside justice. Human beings are not self-sufficient atoms who occasionally suffer unfortunate deviations from independence. Dependence is part of the human condition.

This matters for equal opportunity because opportunity is often organized around the ideal worker: continuously employed, geographically mobile, unencumbered, available for long hours, and supported by someone else’s unpaid labor. When public policy aims to give everyone an equal chance to compete in that world, it may simply invite women and caregivers to conform to a male-coded life pattern. The problem is not only that women have unequal opportunities to become executives, professors, or politicians. It is that caregiving itself is devalued, economically penalized, and treated as an obstacle to real achievement.

An outcome-oriented egalitarianism should therefore ask different questions. Do caregivers have enough income, time, social respect, and support to live well? Do disabled people have not merely access ramps but real inclusion in schools, workplaces, neighborhoods, and cultural life? Do children receive attentive care regardless of their parents’ income? Do elderly people retain agency and connection? Are migrant care workers protected from exploitation? Are paid care jobs treated as skilled and socially necessary rather than as low-wage labor performed by people assumed to have “natural” capacities for nurturance?

These are not merely opportunities. They are arrangements of life. Paid family leave, universal child care, home care services, respite care, disability benefits, accessible transportation, inclusive education, flexible work, living wages for care workers, and health care are all flourishing policies. They change whether people can sleep, love, participate, and avoid despair. They also challenge the false separation between economic and intimate life. The market depends on care, but market societies often refuse to pay for or honor the care that sustains them.

At the same time, a feminist and disability-informed egalitarianism should be cautious about the phrase “equal flourishing.” Some versions of flourishing can smuggle in able-bodied, middle-class, achievement-oriented assumptions. A person with chronic pain, severe disability, depression, or cognitive difference may not flourish according to conventional measures of productivity, independence, sociability, or self-development. Yet her life may possess dignity, attachment, pleasure, and meaning. Conversely, a state eager to promote flourishing may pressure disabled people toward normalized functioning rather than transforming the world to accommodate diverse bodies and minds.

This is why the capability approach, which Sypnowich treats as still too opportunity-focused, remains valuable. The distinction between capability and functioning was developed partly to respect diversity and agency. If a person fasts for religious reasons, her lack of eating has a different meaning from starvation. If a disabled person chooses not to pursue paid employment, the significance depends on whether she had genuine alternatives and social respect. If a parent chooses years of caregiving over career advancement, we need to know whether this was a constrained sacrifice, a valued vocation, or both. Functionings matter, but interpreting them requires attention to context and voice.

Sypnowich is right that “capability” language can become too passive: society offers the option and then looks away. But replacing capability with actual functioning risks another error: treating observed differences as deficits to be corrected. Suppose some women, after feminist reforms, still choose more caregiving than men. Is that unequal flourishing, adaptive preference, or valuable difference? The answer cannot be given abstractly. We would need to examine power within families, workplace norms, cultural expectations, income security, and the testimony of the women themselves. The goal should be not numerical sameness in outcomes but non-subordinating conditions under which caring and non-caring lives can both go well.

Sypnowich’s point that we must sometimes “do in order to be able to do” is especially important for disability. A child who is never included in play may appear not to want friendship; a student denied accommodations may appear not to want education; an adult confined by inaccessible transit may appear not to want public life. Functioning can build capability. Participation teaches desire, confidence, and skill. This supports proactive institutions: inclusive classrooms, supported decision-making, personal assistance, community-based living, and public design that assumes bodily variation from the start. Waiting for people to demand opportunities they have been trained not to imagine is not equality.

The same applies to care. Men will not become equal caregivers merely because parental leave is formally available. They need nontransferable leave, workplace cultures that expect fathers to use it, shorter workweeks, and social narratives that honor male caregiving. Children will not equally access enrichment merely because a city contains museums and music programs; transportation, school integration, cost, parental time, and cultural welcome matter. Care teaches us that opportunities must be embedded in relationships.

Where I part company with Sypnowich is in the suggestion that the egalitarian state should broadly “promote what is genuinely of value.” That phrase requires democratic humility. For many marginalized groups, the history of welfare, medicine, schooling, and family policy includes intrusive judgments by experts about proper motherhood, normal bodies, respectable sexuality, civilized culture, and productive citizenship. Indigenous families, Black mothers, disabled people, queer people, poor women, and migrants have often experienced state concern for their flourishing as surveillance and discipline. An egalitarian politics of flourishing must therefore be anti-paternalist in method even when perfectionist in aspiration.

The key is participation by those whose flourishing is at stake. Care policy designed without care workers, disabled people, parents, children, and elders will reproduce hierarchy. Democratic deliberation must be more than majority rule; it must include organized voice for those usually spoken about. Unions for care workers, disability rights organizations, parent councils, youth participation, and elder advocacy are not add-ons. They are epistemic necessities. People know things about their own needs that philosophers and administrators often miss.

A care-centered egalitarianism would also resist making contribution a condition of worth, even in softened form. Sypnowich writes that contributing to community is part of flourishing, broadly understood. Often it is. But some people cannot contribute in recognizable ways, or can do so only intermittently. Others contribute simply by being loved, by calling forth care, by participating in relationships not captured by productivity. The slogan “from each according to ability, to each according to need” must be read generously: ability varies, need is not shameful, and human value does not depend on output.

Still, Sypnowich’s central challenge stands. Equal opportunity is inadequate because it imagines justice before the fact: distribute chances, then let lives unfold. Care reveals that justice is continuous. Needs arise, capacities change, relationships break and form, bodies age, children grow, and misfortune arrives. A just society must remain involved, not as a supervisor of souls but as a sustaining network that makes dignified dependence possible.

The egalitarian ideal should be equal dignity in flourishing, not identical flourishing. It should ensure that everyone—child, elder, disabled person, caregiver, worker, lonely adult, exhausted parent—has the material support, social recognition, time, and relationships necessary for a life that can be affirmed as their own. Sypnowich helps move us beyond the cold language of opportunity. The next step is to put care at the center, where it belonged all along.

"While equal opportunity is an insufficient ideal for a just society, a framework of equal flourishing must be democratized to ensure it does not become a tool for cultural paternalism or forced assimilation."

Christine Sypnowich’s impatience with opportunity talk is justified. In settler, immigrant, and multicultural democracies, equal opportunity has too often meant an invitation to assimilate into institutions whose standards were set by dominant groups. The gates open, but the road leads in one direction. Women may enter workplaces designed around male life patterns. Racial minorities may enter schools that devalue their histories. Indigenous peoples may be offered opportunities within economies built on dispossession. Immigrants may be told that success means shedding language, religion, and communal obligation. A thin opportunity ideal cannot answer these problems.

Yet Sypnowich’s alternative—equal flourishing—raises a different worry. Who defines flourishing in culturally diverse societies marked by unequal power? If the answer is “democratic debate,” we must ask what democracy looks like when some voices are louder, richer, more credentialed, and more at home in public institutions than others. A state that promotes “valuable pursuits” may correct market domination, but it may also reproduce cultural domination. The history of public efforts to improve people’s lives is not only a history of emancipation; it is also a history of assimilation, moral regulation, and paternalism.

This concern does not refute Sypnowich’s argument. It complicates it. She is right that neutrality can be a myth. Liberal states already promote values: monogamy or its legal alternatives, paid work, formal schooling, national language, private property, punctuality, credentialed expertise, and particular forms of family responsibility. Markets promote values as well: consumption, individual branding, mobility, competition, convenience. When public authorities claim neutrality, they often mask the dominance of majority norms. The question is not whether society shapes people, but whose values shape whom and through what institutions.

Equal opportunity is especially weak where injustice concerns recognition and collective self-determination. Suppose an Indigenous child has the opportunity to attend a well-funded school, compete for scholarships, and enter a profession. If that school erases her people’s history or treats her language as irrelevant, opportunity coexists with cultural injury. Suppose Muslim, Sikh, Jewish, or other religious minorities may compete equally for jobs but face workplace norms that make observance costly. Suppose Black students may access elite universities but must navigate stereotypes, isolation, and curricula that marginalize Black thought. The issue is not merely whether individuals can rise. It is whether institutions can be transformed so that different communities can belong without surrendering themselves.

Sypnowich’s flourishing framework can help here because flourishing is not reducible to income or position. It includes language, memory, ritual, land, family, art, faith, and communal recognition. People flourish not only as choosers but as members of histories and relationships they did not invent. The liberal picture of the individual selecting among options can miss the way some goods are inherited and shared. A language cannot flourish if everyone merely has the individual opportunity to learn it but no institutions sustain it. A religious community cannot flourish if formal freedom of worship exists but work schedules, zoning, prison rules, and school calendars make practice difficult. A people cannot flourish if land and ecological relations central to its identity are destroyed while its members receive equal opportunities in the labor market.

But precisely because flourishing is culturally embedded, the state should be cautious about defining it from above. Sypnowich’s music example is benign at first glance. Of course children should encounter music. But which music? Classical European traditions? Jazz, hip-hop, Indigenous drumming, devotional song, folk forms, electronic production? Who teaches it, in what setting, with what assumptions about excellence? Public arts education can democratize beauty, but it can also rank cultures. Many students have been taught, explicitly or implicitly, that their home culture is noise while elite culture is music.

The answer is not to abandon public cultural education. That would leave culture to market inequality and private inheritance. The answer is cultural democracy. Public institutions should expose students to many traditions, including those of marginalized communities, and should treat students as bearers of culture rather than empty vessels needing refinement. Flourishing cannot mean bringing everyone to the same canon. It must mean creating conditions in which many canons can be encountered, criticized, preserved, and transformed.

This principle applies beyond the arts. Education should not simply equalize access to dominant credentials; it should share authority over curriculum and pedagogy. Health care should not simply provide equal services; it should respect different understandings of body, family, mental distress, and end-of-life care while protecting individuals from coercion within communities. Urban planning should not simply offer equal housing units; it should sustain social networks, religious life, local commerce, and attachment to place. Environmental policy should not simply distribute green amenities; it should recognize that land may have spiritual and collective meaning.

A multicultural egalitarianism must therefore hold together three commitments. First, material equality matters. Cultural recognition without housing, income, health care, and safe work is hollow. Communities cannot flourish when their members are poor, overpoliced, or excluded from wealth. Second, cultural membership matters. Redistribution that requires assimilation is not equality. Third, individual dissent matters. Communities are not harmonious wholes. Women, queer people, converts, secular members, youth, and internal minorities may challenge communal norms. A politics of flourishing must avoid empowering community leaders to define flourishing for everyone within the group.

This third point is crucial. Liberalism’s emphasis on individual choice arose partly to protect people against imposed roles. Sypnowich is right that choice is socially shaped, but socially shaped choices can still be acts of resistance. A young woman leaving a restrictive family, a queer teenager rejecting religious condemnation, an Indigenous artist transforming tradition, a child of immigrants choosing a different vocation from parental expectation—all need rights and opportunities as shields. If egalitarians move too quickly from individual choice to communal flourishing, they may overlook internal domination.

Thus equal opportunity remains indispensable, though insufficient. Anti-discrimination law, freedom of conscience, exit rights, language access, voting rights, fair employment, and education for girls are not neoliberal distractions. They are tools by which people navigate and sometimes contest the communities that formed them. The task is to embed these rights in social conditions that make them real, not to replace them with a collective ideal of flourishing.

Sypnowich’s critique of responsibility also has multicultural dimensions. Dominant societies often interpret minority outcomes as evidence of bad choices: parents who do not value education, cultures that resist integration, neighborhoods that lack work ethic. Such judgments ignore discrimination, segregation, wealth gaps, immigration status, trauma, language barriers, and the social meaning of institutions. They also ignore the possibility that people may define success differently. A family that prioritizes elder care, religious study, or small business over elite mobility is not necessarily failing to flourish. Egalitarians should distinguish between adaptive preferences formed under oppression and legitimate plural values. That distinction can only be made through dialogue, not assumption.

What would a culturally democratic version of Sypnowich’s view require? It would invest in universal material goods: health care, housing, child care, income security, education, and transit. It would support minority languages, community media, cultural centers, and curricula shaped by affected communities. It would protect religious and cultural practice while enforcing individual rights. It would democratize workplaces and schools so marginalized groups have voice, not merely access. It would treat land, environment, and public space as conditions of collective flourishing. It would collect data on unequal outcomes but interpret them with communities rather than over them. It would regulate markets that commodify culture or displace neighborhoods. It would ensure that public institutions can be challenged by those they claim to serve.

This approach accepts Sypnowich’s central insight: we cannot stop at chances. If an immigrant child has an equal chance to become a lawyer but loses her language, sees her parents humiliated, and grows up in a city where her community is priced out, equality has failed. If an Indigenous person has equal access to employment while her nation lacks control over land and education, equality has failed. Outcomes matter, including collective and cultural outcomes.

But the language of equal flourishing must be democratized from the bottom up. It should not authorize the state to promote a settled list of valuable lives. It should require institutions to ask, continuously and publicly, what different people and communities need in order to live without domination, deprivation, or erasure. Flourishing is not a single destination beyond opportunity. It is a contested field of material, cultural, and political conditions.

Equal opportunity is not enough because people do not enter society as abstract competitors. They enter as members of families, cultures, neighborhoods, nations, religions, and histories. Equal flourishing is a promising corrective only if it honors that plurality and protects dissent within it. The egalitarian ideal should be neither assimilation into a fairer race nor state-defined human fulfillment, but a democracy in which many ways of living can endure, change, and meet one another on equal terms.

"The ideal of equality should be understood as the democratization of social conditions to ensure equal flourishing rather than a mere procedural competition for unequal outcomes."

Christine Sypnowich is right to press on the weak spot in our public language of equality. “Opportunity” has become the word by which politicians congratulate themselves for egalitarian concern while leaving most of the structure of inequality intact. It is compatible with staggering differences in wealth, security, leisure, health, voice, and dignity. It offers the poor a fairer audition for positions that remain scarce, exhausting, and hierarchically arranged. And it places moral attention on the individual at precisely the point when we should be looking at institutions.

The most powerful part of Sypnowich’s argument is not the claim that outcomes matter. That should be obvious. If a society proudly equalized applications to universities, jobs, loans, and public offices, but year after year produced one class that lived longer, safer, richer, and more culturally expansive lives and another that died earlier, worked harder, feared eviction, and lacked time for family or civic life, nobody should be satisfied that justice had been done. The deeper point is that the opportunity framework invites us to misdescribe such a society. It asks whether the gates were formally open, whether the starting line was moved closer to fairness, whether the race was clean. But life is not a race, or at least it should not be organized as one. A good society is not one in which everyone has an equal chance to escape insecurity; it is one in which insecurity is not the common fate from which a few escape.

This is why the language of flourishing is attractive. It turns our attention to what inequality does to people. It asks whether they can enjoy health, education, friendship, beauty, meaningful work, political agency, and time free from domination. It also makes visible forms of deprivation that cash measures alone miss: loneliness, social humiliation, cultural exclusion, the narrowing of aspiration that comes from growing up in a place where no one expects much from you. Sypnowich is correct that an egalitarianism worthy of the name must care about these things.

Still, the appeal to equal flourishing needs institutional translation. Otherwise it risks remaining a moral horizon rather than a political program. The question is not merely whether the state should care about outcomes, but which outcomes, by what means, with what authority, and under what democratic constraints. Here the socialist tradition is useful, but only if socialism is understood less as centralized direction of life and more as the democratization of the social conditions under which lives are lived.

Begin with material security. Equal flourishing requires that people not be forced to spend their energies merely avoiding disaster. In the United States, and to lesser degrees in Britain and Canada, class stratification is reproduced through housing, health care, education, labor markets, and inherited wealth. A flourishing egalitarianism would therefore prioritize universal institutions: health care not tied to employment; child care as public infrastructure; schools funded so that a child’s neighborhood does not determine the range of her future; social housing and tenant protections; reliable public transportation; and old-age and disability supports that do not degrade recipients. These are not just opportunities. They are shared conditions of life.

Second, equal flourishing requires time. Opportunity rhetoric often celebrates the heroic individual who works two jobs, studies at night, and climbs upward. But a society in which flourishing depends on heroic exertion is unjust. Leisure, family life, civic participation, and artistic or athletic pursuits are not luxuries to be earned after market success. They are components of a decent life. Shorter working hours, predictable schedules, paid leave, vacation, and stronger bargaining power are therefore egalitarian policies. They alter not only income but the texture of life.

Third, equal flourishing requires democratic power at work. Sypnowich rightly criticizes meritocratic hierarchy, but an exclusive focus on distribution after the fact can miss the authoritarian character of many workplaces. For most adults, work is where they spend much of their waking life. If work is humiliating, insecure, surveilled, or devoid of control, then even decent wages will not secure flourishing. Unions, sectoral bargaining, works councils, codetermination, and cooperative ownership are not merely instruments for raising pay. They are institutions of equal standing. They say that workers are not inputs into other people’s projects but participants in common enterprises.

Fourth, flourishing requires a built and cultural environment that is not abandoned to private profit. Sypnowich’s invocation of music is apt, though it may make some liberals nervous. No one is born with a fully formed menu of worthwhile pursuits. We discover what we love through exposure, encouragement, and institutions. Public libraries, parks, recreation centers, museums, music education, adult education, community theaters, and safe public spaces expand the world people can inhabit. The important point is not that the state should decide that everyone must love Mozart, oil painting, or organized sports. It is that without public provision, the capacity to explore and develop such loves becomes class-bound.

The best version of Sypnowich’s view is therefore not paternalistic perfectionism but democratic perfectionism. Every society promotes some values. The supposedly neutral market promotes acquisitiveness, convenience, status competition, and whatever attracts profitable attention. Advertising shapes desire. Social media platforms shape recognition. Real estate markets shape family life and school access. Employers shape time, identity, and ambition. To refuse public judgment in the name of neutrality is not to leave people free from influence; it is to leave influence to those with money. A democratic society should instead ask openly which institutions support lives of depth, reciprocity, health, and freedom.

But here a caution is necessary. Equal flourishing cannot mean that the state monitors citizens’ happiness or treats every deviation from an approved life as a problem to be corrected. Some people flourish in solitude more than sociability, in religious devotion more than artistic experimentation, in craft more than academic achievement, in caregiving more than career advancement. Some lives will look modest from the outside and be rich from within. Others will include chosen risks, failures, eccentricities, and griefs that no egalitarian program can or should eliminate. If flourishing is to guide politics, it must remain plural, revisable, and democratically contested.

This is where Sypnowich’s critique of responsibility should be paired with an account of agency. She is right that the line between choice and circumstance is often impossible to draw cleanly. Poverty changes time horizons. Trauma shapes judgment. Social class forms taste and confidence. Addiction, debt, illness, and family obligation complicate the idea of freely chosen outcomes. Yet it would be a mistake to conclude that responsibility has no place in egalitarian politics. The point is rather to relocate responsibility. Instead of asking whether a person’s deprivation is sufficiently unchosen to merit help, we should ask what we owe one another as co-members of a society. People remain agents; indeed, they become fuller agents when they are not crushed by avoidable deprivation. Social rights do not deny responsibility. They make responsible life possible.

The political challenge is that “equal opportunity” is popular because it sounds fair without sounding threatening. “Equal flourishing” is more demanding. It implies taxes on wealth and inheritance; restrictions on predatory markets; investment in public goods; empowerment of workers; and democratic planning of some social priorities. It also challenges the cultural comfort of the affluent, who often reconcile themselves to inequality by believing that the system gives everyone a chance. The language of flourishing tells them that a chance is not enough when the predictable result is a society of avoidable domination and deprivation.

Yet egalitarians should not abandon opportunity entirely. Equal opportunities matter because closed doors are real injustices. Anti-discrimination law, fair hiring, educational access, voting rights, and mobility remain crucial. The mistake is to treat them as sufficient. Opportunity should be subordinate to flourishing, not a substitute for it. We should care whether people have chances because chances are among the means by which lives go well. But if the chances do not produce lives of dignity, security, and participation, then the work of equality has not been done.

Sypnowich’s essay is valuable because it asks us to stop mistaking a procedural ideal for a human one. The aim is not a perfectly fair competition for unequal lives. **The Radical Promise of Liberal Egalitarianism**

Christine Sypnowich's case for an outcome-focused, flourishing-based egalitarianism is bracing, but it rests on a caricature of the tradition it sets out to displace. Read generously, liberal egalitarianism is not a thin doctrine of removing barriers and stepping back; it is a demanding theory whose implementation would transform the economic and social structures of contemporary capitalism beyond recognition. Sypnowich's argument lands only if we accept her relatively impoverished portrait of what Rawls, Dworkin, and their successors have actually been arguing about.

Consider Rawls. His two principles do not amount to a program of equal opportunity in the colloquial sense. Fair equality of opportunity is the *second* principle's first clause, and it is itself qualified by the difference principle. The first principle, lexically prior, guarantees a set of basic liberties whose fair value must be maintained — meaning that wealth cannot be permitted to corrupt political equality. Taken seriously, Rawls's framework rules out private financing of elections, requires very substantial limits on inheritance, and probably requires what he later called a "property-owning democracy" or liberal socialism. The difference principle itself demands that inequalities benefit the worst-off; in a world where, as Sypnowich rightly observes, vast disparities of wealth coexist with stagnant mobility, a Rawlsian audit of existing institutions would call for enormous redistribution. The fact that Rawls "permits" inequalities in principle does not mean his theory permits the inequalities we actually have. It almost certainly does not.

The same applies to Dworkin. The hypothetical insurance market that grounds his equality of resources is a thought experiment whose policy implications include something like a universal basic capital endowment, robust public provision against disability and unemployment, and steeply progressive taxation. Dworkin is not a defender of laissez-faire markets; he is a defender of *counterfactual* markets corrected for the unequal starting points and unchosen contingencies that real markets enshrine.

Sypnowich treats these positions as compatible with significant inequality and therefore as wanting. But "significant inequality" relative to a fully-implemented Rawlsian or Dworkinian society would mean differences in resources and opportunities so much smaller than current ones that the political distance between liberal egalitarianism and her preferred socialism is considerably narrower than her essay suggests. The disagreement is real, but it is not the disagreement between a complacent liberalism that washes its hands and a radical egalitarianism that gets serious about outcomes.

A second concern is that Sypnowich underestimates the moral significance of what she calls "neutrality." Liberal egalitarians did not arrive at neutrality through carelessness about flourishing; they arrived at it through hard reflection on what happens when states get into the business of ranking ways of life. The track record is grim. States that have undertaken to promote "flourishing" have promoted particular sectarian, class, and gender visions of it. They have done so with confidence that they were liberating their citizens from "cultures of fatalism, low expectations, self-abnegation, or limited horizons" — phrases that, with all due respect, could have been lifted from any number of paternalist tracts of the past two centuries directed at the poor, at women, at colonized peoples, at religious minorities. Dworkin and Cohen were not denying that we should care about people who suffer because of their own choices. Collapsing these distinctions does not give us more equality; it gives us a less articulated moral vocabulary. We need to be able to say both that a person who has gambled away her savings is owed something by her community *and* that this is not the same kind of claim as that of a person born with a debilitating illness.

We are told that the egalitarian community will be "proactive," using "incentives and supporting institutions," that it will "revisit our policies" when outcomes diverge, that it will involve "compulsory arts and music education, of the right kind." But the hardest questions in political philosophy are: which institutions, on what authority, accountable to whom, and what happens when citizens, exercising their democratic agency, reject the community's verdicts about what flourishing requires? Liberal egalitarianism's commitment to neutrality is not a refusal to think about these questions; it is a considered answer to them. One can disagree with that answer — many do — but it cannot be set aside by gesturing at solidarity and Marx.

G. A. There is a robust socialist argument to be made against contemporary liberal egalitarianism, but it is principally an argument about political economy. By relocating the disagreement to the terrain of opportunity-versus-outcome and neutrality-versus-perfectionism, Sypnowich obscures rather than illuminates what is really at stake.

None of this is to defend the cramped, mobility-talk version of equal opportunity that pervades centrist political rhetoric. Sypnowich is entirely right that Bernstein's and Trudeau's invocations of opportunity are anemic, and that they often serve to license the inequalities they purport to address. But the answer is not to abandon opportunity as a category; it is to take the more demanding versions of equal opportunity seriously, push their institutional implications all the way down, and recognize that doing so requires confronting the structural inequalities of capitalist economies. Liberal egalitarianism, fully realized, is a far more radical doctrine than its critics — and, frankly, many of its defenders — have allowed. We should not leave its radical potential on the table.

To collapse contemporary liberal egalitarianism into its Dworkinian wing is to fight the battles of 1990 rather than 2023.

This matters because Sypnowich's most powerful arguments — that choice is shaped by circumstance all the way down, that "from each according to ability, to each according to need" cannot be reconstructed in luck-egalitarian terms, that solidarity is constitutive of the egalitarian ideal — are widely shared on the liberal left. This is not a knockdown objection — sometimes the truth is politically inconvenient — but it suggests that the rhetorical case for outcome-talk should be made with more caution than Sypnowich allows.

The flourishing horizon is the right one. But getting there will require more of the liberal egalitarian toolkit, and less confidence in the state's capacity to identify the valuable, than her essay suggests.

Christine Sypnowich’s provocative lead essay argues that equality of opportunity, in its many guises, is insufficient for genuine egalitarianism, and that we must instead aim for equal flourishing. Her critique of liberal egalitarianism is sweeping, but it rests on a caricature of that tradition. Properly understood, the most influential liberal egalitarian theories not only accommodate but actually require a deep concern with outcomes; they do not wash their hands of how people live. Sypnowich’s call to abandon opportunity for outright outcomes is both theoretically misguided and politically perilous, for the liberal tradition already contains the resources to address her concerns while respecting individual freedom and pluralism.

Sypnowich’s central charge is that liberal egalitarians, from Rawls to Dworkin to Sen, focus on opportunity and thereby tolerate massive inequalities of outcome. She laments that “liberal egalitarians do not even aim for equality per se” and that the luck egalitarian distinction between choice and circumstance leads to a “sink-or-swim” mentality. But this ignores the full architecture of Rawls’s justice as fairness. Rawls’s first principle guarantees equal basic liberties, but his second principle contains two parts: fair equality of opportunity and the difference principle. The latter explicitly requires that social and economic inequalities be arranged so that they are to the greatest benefit of the least advantaged. This is not a mere opportunity floor; it is an ongoing outcome-sensitive criterion that governs the basic structure of society. Under the difference principle, if the worst-off group is not doing well—if they lack adequate income, wealth, access to meaningful work, and the social bases of self-respect—the society is unjust, regardless of whether they had an “equal start.”

Moreover, Rawls’s vision of a property-owning democracy (or liberal socialism) involves dispersing wealth and capital widely, so that over time, the fair value of political liberties and fair equality of opportunity are preserved. These institutional arrangements are designed to prevent the kind of dynastic inequality and social sclerosis that Sypnowich rightly condemns. The “washing of hands” is nowhere to be found: the basic structure continuously corrects for the cumulative effects of the market and family background. As for Dworkin’s equality of resources, his hypothetical insurance scheme is explicitly outcome-oriented: it asks what level of insurance rational people would buy behind a veil of ignorance against the risk of low talents or disability. The resulting social minimum would be substantial and would guarantee a dignified life. Sypnowich complains that Dworkin allows inequalities to persist, but only those that result from genuine choice, and after compensation for bad brute luck. This is not indifference to outcomes; it is a principled limit on when inequalities are unjust—a limit that any plausible egalitarian theory must draw.

Sypnowich’s objection that the choice-circumstance distinction is impossible to draw cleanly is well-taken. But it does not warrant throwing out the distinction altogether. The progressive luck egalitarians she mentions, such as Cohen and Roemer, were well aware that social conditions shape choices, and they spent decades refining the notion of “advantage” and the conditions under which choices could be considered genuinely free. Cohen’s “On the Currency of Egalitarian Justice” argued that we should compensate for any disadvantage not traceable to a genuine choice, but he also noted that extensive redistribution and a socialist ethos would be required to make choices truly voluntary. In that light, liberal egalitarianism can—and should—support robust social insurance, universal basic services, and a job guarantee, precisely to ensure that the background conditions for responsible choice are met. That is an outcome-oriented agenda if there ever was one.

Even Sypnowich’s claim that opportunity talk neglects community and solidarity is overstated. Rawls’s difference principle expresses a conception of society as a system of fair cooperation for mutual advantage; it embodies the idea of fraternity, as he explicitly stated. The property-owning democracy would reshape not just material distribution but also workplace democracy and the character of social relations. The liberal tradition, far from ignoring the social bases of self-respect, makes them a primary good. To be sure, Sypnowich is calling for something even thicker: an “egalitarian ethos” of generosity and mutual care. That is a noble aspiration, but it cannot be mandated by the state without risking the core liberal value of personal freedom. As Samuel Scheffler has argued, the liberal egalitarian project does not require perfect altruism; it relies on institutions to do the work of justice, leaving individuals free to pursue their own conceptions of the good within those just institutions.

Which brings us to the most significant threat posed by Sypnowich’s proposal: its perfectionism. She wants the state to actively guide people toward valuable pursuits and away from harmful ones, using incentives, education, and institutional design. While her music education example is superficially benign, the principle is deeply troubling. Who gets to define what is valuable? Sypnowich says there are many forms of flourishing, but she also suggests that some ways of life “do not warrant social support.” This opens the door to a state-appointed cultural elite deciding which lifestyles, careers, and relationships are acceptable. The liberal commitment to neutrality, as interpreted by Jonathan Quong and others, exists precisely to stop the state from becoming the final arbiter of the good life. Citizens must be treated as capable of forming and revising their own conceptions of the good. Sypnowich’s dismissal of this concern as “poor sociology” underestimates the danger of majoritarian moralism.

A liberal egalitarian society can support a wide range of pursuits and address non-material shortfalls—loneliness, alienation, mental health—through public goods like community centers, parks, libraries, and a robust mental health care system, all of which can be justified on neutral grounds of providing resources that individuals can use as they see fit. It does not need to stamp some ways of life as inferior to accomplish this. The socialist tradition Sypnowich invokes has a long and tragic history of defining “flourishing” in ways that crushed dissent. Liberal egalitarianism, with its insistence on basic liberties and the separation of justice from comprehensive doctrines, remains the better safeguard against such oppression.

Finally, Sypnowich’s call for “equal flourishing” as an outcome is a mirage. Even in an ideal society, people will have different temperaments, desires, and metabolisms, and no amount of redistribution can ensure that everyone experiences the same level of “flourishing.” As Sen noted, converting resources into well-being varies from person to person. A just society should ensure that everyone has a fair share of resources and opportunities to live a life they have reason to value, but it cannot promise identical outcomes in happiness or fulfillment. That would be to overpromise and invite the very leveling down she rightly rejects.

In sum, Sypnowich’s critique is a useful corrective against anemic versions of equal opportunity that stop at antidiscrimination law. But the robust liberal egalitarian tradition already points beyond that, to a society where inequalities must lift the worst off, where brute luck is compensated, and where the background conditions of genuine choice are secured. We do not need to abandon opportunity for outcomes; we need to realize the full implications of fair equality of opportunity and the difference principle. That would be radical enough.

**Against the Cultivating State**

Christine Sypnowich's argument has the shape of an irresistible philosophical inference: since choices are socially conditioned, choices cannot ground responsibility; since opportunity is realized only in actual flourishing, equality must be measured by flourishing rather than by opportunity; since neutrality is a fiction, the state must take a substantive view of the good. From these moves, she concludes that the egalitarian community must proactively cultivate the flourishing of its members, intervening against limited horizons, fatalism, and unworthy pursuits. The conclusion follows from the premises, more or less, but the premises are doing more work than meets the eye. Each of them sounds harmless in isolation. Together, they license a cultivating state whose powers and ambitions go well beyond what any liberal democracy currently authorizes, and well beyond what the historical record suggests is wise.

I want to defend, against this drift, a deflated but I hope still serious commitment to something like state neutrality — not because I have any illusion that neutrality is fully achievable or that any state is wholly innocent of substantive judgments about the good, but because the demand for neutrality is a *constraint* that disciplines the use of state power in ways the cultivating state will lack.

Begin with the choice/circumstance argument. It is true that the line between chosen and unchosen is blurry. But blurry lines do not authorize the abolition of the categories they separate. The line between adulthood and childhood is also blurry, but no one concludes from this that the distinction should be abandoned. We use blurry distinctions productively all the time: we make our best judgments in clear cases, treat marginal cases with appropriate humility, and accept that errors will sometimes be made in both directions. The choice/circumstance distinction can be used the same way. Most of us have a reasonably good sense, in our own lives and in the lives of those we know, of when someone has been let down by circumstance and when they have made choices that they themselves would acknowledge as their own. The philosophical impossibility of drawing the line with mathematical precision does not undermine the moral and political utility of the distinction.

Sypnowich's argument here, taken at face value, would license a degree of paternalism that goes well beyond what most defenders of the flourishing approach would endorse. If choices are not really choices because they are socially conditioned, then the state is licensed to override them whenever the state's judgment about flourishing diverges from the citizen's. There is no principled limit to this, since by hypothesis the citizen's preferences are themselves products of social conditioning. The only question is which conditioning we prefer: the unaccountable conditioning of markets and culture, or the deliberate conditioning of democratic institutions. Sypnowich plumps for the latter, but she does not really argue for it; she assumes that "democratic" institutions will produce wiser judgments than the alternatives. Why should we believe this? Democratic politics has produced its share of catastrophes, and a democratic majority that has decided to override the supposedly conditioned preferences of a minority is not obviously a more attractive prospect than the market.

The next move concerns neutrality. Sypnowich is right that the state cannot be perfectly neutral. The state's tax code, education system, criminal law, and zoning regulations all encode substantive judgments about valuable activities. Fine. The question is whether, given the impossibility of perfect neutrality, the state should aspire to as much neutrality as practicable, or should embrace its substantive role with vigor. The deflated neutralist position holds that the state should make its substantive judgments narrow, transparent, and revisable, and should refrain from extending them into areas (religious belief, intimate relationships, aesthetic preferences, broad life-plans) where citizens have legitimate claims to authority over their own lives. The cultivating state Sypnowich describes seems to lack this constraint. It will weigh in on the value of various enterprises, promote some pursuits and disincentivize others, override expressed preferences when they reflect fatalism or limited horizons. Where, exactly, does the cultivating state stop?

This is not a hypothetical worry. Liberal democracies routinely face questions about how far to push their substantive judgments about citizens' lives. Should the state discourage certain religious practices it considers oppressive? Should it intervene in family structures it considers unhealthy? Should it require certain dietary practices on health grounds? Should it discourage certain leisure pursuits it considers degrading? Liberal neutrality, even in its limited form, provides a default against such interventions, requiring a high burden of justification before they are undertaken. The cultivating state inverts this default. It begins from the premise that the community has a stake in citizens' flourishing and proceeds to ask only what means are appropriate to advance that stake. The result is a politics in which the burden of justification falls on the citizen who wishes to be left alone, rather than on the state that wishes to intervene.

Sypnowich's response is that the cultivating state will use only legitimate, non-coercive means: incentives, education, supporting institutions. But this is not a real constraint. Almost all state power operates through incentives, education, and supporting institutions; the question is what the state may do with these instruments. A tax system designed to discourage certain pursuits is coercive in any meaningful sense, even if it does not involve police powers. An education system that compels children to take certain forms of cultivation is coercive of those children's parents. An institutional landscape that systematically privileges certain forms of life over others is shaping the choices available to citizens in ways that are not, on Sypnowich's own analysis, easy to consent to. The distinction between coercion and incentive is much less clean than the essay implies.

There is also a question about who, in actual political practice, will operate the cultivating state. Sypnowich gestures at democratic deliberation, but the heavy lifting will be done by educational bureaucracies, cultural institutions, public health authorities, and welfare agencies — bodies staffed by professionals with their own class and cultural backgrounds, their own conceptions of valuable pursuits, their own blind spots. The cultivating state, in practice, will reflect the substantive judgments of the educated middle class. Its compulsory music lessons will be lessons in the music the educated middle class values; its incentives against fatalism and low horizons will be incentives toward the aspirations the educated middle class endorses. We can call this "democratic" if we like, but it is a particular kind of democratic, in which some citizens' conceptions of flourishing become the standard by which others' lives are judged and shaped.

For these reasons, I think the cultivating state should be approached with great caution. The fact that opportunity-talk does not deliver equal flourishing is a problem, but it is not the worst problem in egalitarian politics. The worst problems involve states that have decided they know what their citizens need. The history of those states is mostly grim.

The better path, I think, is to push redistribution and the equalization of resources further than current liberal egalitarianism does, while preserving as much of the neutrality presumption as possible. Give citizens robust material security, broad access to a wide range of cultural and educational opportunities, real political voice, freedom from domination at work and in their relationships. Then let them work out for themselves what flourishing means. They will not always get it right, in the eyes of those who would have preferred different choices. But the alternative — a state confident enough in its own conception of flourishing to override those of its citizens — is worse than the residual inequalities of taste and aspiration it would correct.

Sypnowich diagnoses a real disease. The cure she proposes risks being worse than the disease.

Sypnowich attempts to wave away this concern by noting that we should not be "hamstrung by a prohibition on seeking equality," but the bureaucratic mechanisms required to manage such a subjective metric would be staggering.

People do not need the state to teach them how to live well; they need the state to stop enforcing the property laws that allow billionaires to hoard the wealth that the working class creates.

True solidarity is not about paternalistically guiding one’s neighbors toward higher cultural pursuits; it is about standing shoulder to shoulder to dismantle the structural machinery of exploitation. Sypnowich’s essay is a brilliant critique of liberal philosophy, but her socialist remedy needs less Amartya Sen and more Karl Marx.

The core of Sypnowich’s argument against neutrality is that choices are “never made in a vacuum” and that all societies shape values. Her example is music education: simply making music lessons available is not enough; we must require them, or at least heavily incentivize them, because without initiation people will not know what they are missing. This line of reasoning, while seductive, is a classic perfectionist fallacy. Sypnowich’s argument that liberal societies already promote some values and discourage others—state support for education and the arts, discouragement of smoking—is not a proof that neutrality is impossible, but rather a warning that state action in the realm of values must be carefully constrained by non-perfectionist reasons. Discouraging smoking rests on the negative externality of secondhand smoke and the health burden on public systems, not on a judgment that the life of a smoker is less flourishing. Sypnowich’s framework, by contrast, explicitly invites the state to decide which ways of life “warrant social support” and which do not. The room for abuse is vast.

The history of state paternalism is not a pretty one: from the forced assimilation of Indigenous children to the pathologizing of homosexuality under the guise of “well-being,” the state has a miserable track record when it presumes to steer its citizens toward true happiness.

Liberal neutrality, as defended by thinkers like Jonathan Quong, does not demand that the state be silent on values. It demands that the state justify its actions on grounds that all reasonable citizens can accept, regardless of their comprehensive doctrines. This allows for many of the policies Sypnowich favors—universal healthcare, generous social insurance, public parks—without requiring the state to endorse a particular meaning of life. For instance, we can fund mental health services because we recognize that depression severely impairs people’s ability to pursue their chosen ends, not because we have decided that cheerful people are more flourishing than melancholic introverts. The distinction may seem subtle, but it is the difference between a society that treats citizens as autonomous agents and one that treats them as subjects to be perfected.

If the curriculum is designed to inculcate particular values, it is coercive to the extent that schooling is compulsory. None of this is to deny that inequality inflicts profound suffering and that current liberal democracies fail many of their citizens. But the answer is to strengthen the safety net, enforce genuine equal opportunity, and ensure that everyone has the real capability to live as they see fit—not to task the state with making everyone flourish equally. Sypnowich’s call for “socialism” as the realization of the egalitarian ideal is not a magic wand; it would only shift the power to define and enforce flourishing to an even more centralized authority. True solidarity and community cannot be commanded from above; they must grow from a culture of mutual respect and freedom. That culture is better protected by a state that declines to take sides on the question of the good life than by one that sets itself up as the gardener of souls.

It requires eradicating systemic racism, guaranteeing material wealth, and dismantling patriarchal structures. The capabilities approach does not "wash its hands" at the starting line; it actively structures society so that the threshold for a dignified life is accessible to all.

Sypnowich is right that humans are socially embedded creatures and that our choices are shaped by our environments. She is also correct to criticize luck egalitarians for their harsh, punitive approach to those who make "bad" choices. The capabilities approach agrees: we do not let the imprudent starve, because the capability to be nourished and housed must be permanently guaranteed, regardless of past choices.

But acknowledging social conditioning does not mean we must extinguish the concept of agency altogether. As Nussbaum has argued, practical reason—the ability to form a conception of the good and to engage in critical reflection about the planning of one's life—is an architectonic capability. It is what makes us human. Sypnowich’s proposal to mandate outcomes treats citizens as passive vessels to be filled with state-approved "flourishing," explicitly rejecting their practical reason if it leads them away from the societal consensus.

Equalizing capabilities, rather than functionings, walks the narrow, necessary path between two disastrous extremes. It rejects the cruel indifference of laissez-faire opportunity, demanding robust material justice and structural transformation. But it also rejects the paternalistic overreach of mandated outcomes, preserving the individual’s right to fast, to decline the music lesson, and to author their own life.

"The critique of equality of opportunity as a hollow framework is based on a mischaracterization of liberal egalitarianism, and the proposed alternative of state-directed 'flourishing' risks an oppressive paternalism that undermines individual agency and freedom."

Christine Sypnowich offers a provocative, though ultimately misguided, indictment of equality of opportunity. In her telling, liberal egalitarianism’s commitment to opportunity is a thinly veiled capitulation to laissez-faire capitalism—a hollow framework that abandons the disadvantaged at the starting line of life while comforting itself with the fiction of "personal responsibility." The alternative she posits is an outcome-oriented socialist framework dedicated to "human flourishing." While her diagnosis of contemporary inequality’s severity is accurate, her dismissal of opportunity-centered liberalism relies on a profound mischaracterization of what liberal egalitarians actually argue, and her proposed alternative risks an unacceptable paternalism that undermines the very freedom it purports to enhance.

To begin, Sypnowich greatly underestimates the radical potential of the liberal egalitarian tradition. By flattening John Rawls, Ronald Dworkin, and luck egalitarianism into a single, market-friendly apologia for meritocracy, she ignores the sweeping redistributive demands of their theories. Rawls’s concept of "fair equality of opportunity" does not, as Sypnowich implies, merely outlaw discrimination or ensure formal equality before the law. It requires that those with similar talents and willingness to use them should have the same prospects of success regardless of their initial place in the social system. Achieving this in any real-world society would necessitate the total dismantling of entrenched class privilege, massive investments in universal public education, the elimination of inherited wealth as a determinative factor in life prospects, and the robust provision of social goods.

If we actually achieved fair equality of opportunity as Rawls envisioned it, the "hopeless situations" Sypnowich describes—precarious work, chronic unemployment, dismal living conditions—would be largely eradicated. Liberal egalitarianism does not wash its hands of outcomes; it understands that unconscionable disparities in outcomes are the clearest evidence that opportunities were never truly equal to begin with. Sypnowich attacks a hollowed-out, neoliberal caricature of opportunity, but ignores the transformative demands of the philosophical tradition she is critiquing.

More troubling, however, is Sypnowich’s affirmative vision. She argues that because choices are socially influenced, the state should actively intervene to shape those choices to ensure "equal flourishing." She dismisses liberal neutrality—the idea that the state should not dictate what constitutes a "good life"—as a sociological fiction, suggesting that since the market already influences our desires, the state should step in to purposefully mold them toward truly valuable pursuits. She uses music education as an example: we should not just make lessons available, we should mandate them so that citizens can properly flourish.

This pivot from enabling choices to dictating outcomes crosses a dangerous line into paternalism. The state is a coercive apparatus. To say that it should use its power to "inculcate support for valuable pursuits" and correct "cultures of fatalism" or "low expectations" is to open the door to profound overreach. Who decides what constitutes a "valuable pursuit"? Sypnowich suggests that democratic debate will sort this out, but historically, state-defined visions of the "good life" have been weaponized against minorities, neurodivergent individuals, and marginalized communities whose ways of living do not map onto the majority’s normative ideals of "flourishing."

The liberal commitment to neutrality is not based on a naive belief that individuals form their desires in a vacuum. Rather, it is a defensive mechanism born of historical experience. It recognizes that state power, when married to a singular, substantive vision of human flourishing, inevitably oppresses those who dissent. When Sypnowich writes that the state must make people "better choosers" and that we should not be "squeamish about an outcomes approach that takes a stand on how people should live," she discards the core liberal protection against tyranny.

Furthermore, her critique of the choice-circumstance distinction—the idea that individuals should be held responsible for the outcomes of their free choices, but compensated for disadvantages outside their control—is surprisingly dismissive of human agency. By reducing bad choices to the downstream effects of "unjust social conditions," she flattens individuals into mere products of their environment. While it is true that poverty and inequality drastically restrict one's horizon of choice—a fact liberal egalitarians fully recognize—eliminating the concept of personal responsibility strips individuals of their moral agency. To treat people with equal concern and respect means taking their choices seriously, which inevitably means allowing them to bear the consequences of those choices, provided they were made from a baseline of genuine equality.

Ultimately, Sypnowich asks us to trade the ideal of equal opportunity for a state-directed project of equal flourishing. But a society that ensures robust, genuinely fair equality of opportunity—alongside a social safety net that prevents anyone from falling into destitution, as demanded by the Rawlsian difference principle—already provides the material conditions for flourishing without dictating its form. We do not need a state to tell us how to live well; we need a state that ensures we have the resources, education, and structural fairness to figure it out for ourselves. Liberal egalitarianism remains the best framework for achieving this balance between equality and freedom.

"Sypnowich’s proposal for state-mandated 'equal flourishing' should be rejected because it replaces individual agency and market-driven progress with a coercive, totalizing bureaucracy that is both economically ruinous and threatening to human liberty."

Christine Sypnowich’s essay against equality of opportunity and in favor of state-directed "human flourishing" is a classic example of an age-old intellectual fallacy: the belief that society is a machine that can be engineered by experts to produce a morally perfect result. Drawing heavily from socialist tradition, Sypnowich looks at the complexities of human disparity, throws her hands up, and declares that only a totalizing, outcome-oriented state can fix the problem. In doing so, she casually dismisses the very engines of human progress—free markets, individual choice, and the epistemic humility of liberal neutrality. From a Hayekian and classical liberal perspective, her vision is not only economically ruinous, but deeply threatening to human liberty.

Sypnowich’s fundamental grievance is with the "choice as circumstance" problem. She argues that because individuals are shaped by their social environments, their choices are never truly their own. Therefore, it is "harsh" to hold them responsible for the outcomes of those choices. This deterministic view of humanity is the fatal flaw in her argument. While it is obviously true that we are influenced by our environment, erasing the concept of individual agency destroys the basis for any functional society. If individuals are not responsible for their choices, then they cannot be trusted with freedom.

And indeed, Sypnowich does not trust them with freedom. Because she believes people are constrained by "unaccountable markets" and "cultures of fatalism," she demands that "representative political institutions" take over the job of "guiding by incentives, education, and supporting institutions that offer pursuits of value." She admits this means abandoning neutrality. The state must decide what constitutes a good life—she likes music lessons—and push citizens toward it.

What Sypnowich fails to grasp is the knowledge problem. How does the state know what constitutes flourishing for 330 million diverse individuals? The beauty of the free market, which Sypnowich derides, is that it is an information-processing system. Prices, profits, and losses are signals that tell us what people actually value. The market allows individuals to define their own flourishing and coordinate with others to achieve it. Sypnowich views market outcomes as "deleterious" because people often choose things she, as an academic, does not value. But substituting the decentralized, subjective valuations of millions of people for the centralized, "democratic" decisions of state bureaucrats does not enhance flourishing; it merely enforces the preferences of the powerful onto the powerless.

Furthermore, Sypnowich’s critique of the liberal emphasis on "meritocracy" and "talent plus effort" completely ignores the instrumental value of these concepts. She laments that liberal egalitarians tolerate hierarchies of wealth if they reflect differences in talent and effort. But those hierarchies serve a crucial purpose: they incentivize the production of the very wealth Sypnowich wants to distribute. We reward doctors, engineers, and entrepreneurs not just because it is "fair" to reward their effort, but because we *need* their services. If you sever the link between contribution and outcome—as Sypnowich explicitly advocates, praising Marx's ideal of distribution regardless of contribution—you destroy the incentive structure that sustains modern civilization.

Sypnowich complains about the "grudging attitude" of luck egalitarians and pines for a society characterized by "solidarity and comradeship," where everyone happily shares the fruits of their labor. This relies on an impossibly utopian view of human nature. In small, voluntary communities—families, churches, mutual aid groups—this kind of organic solidarity thrives. But you cannot scale familial solidarity to the level of a modern nation-state through coercion. When the state forces distribution regardless of effort, the result is not warm comradeship; it is resentment, stagnation, and the inevitable rise of an authoritarian bureaucracy needed to enforce the distribution.

Equality of opportunity, in the classical liberal sense—meaning the abolition of legal privileges, the protection of property rights, and free exchange—is the only form of equality compatible with freedom. It allows individuals to use their unique, localized knowledge to pursue their own ends. Sypnowich is correct that this system produces unequal material outcomes. But the historical record is perfectly clear: a free society with unequal outcomes produces a dramatically higher standard of living for its poorest members than any state-managed attempt to mandate "equal flourishing."

Sypnowich asks us not to be "squeamish about an outcomes approach that takes a stand on how people should live." We should be absolutely terrified of it. A state powerful enough to ensure equal flourishing is powerful enough to mandate conformity, suppress dissent, and destroy the spontaneous order that makes true human progress possible. We must reject the socialist temptation and defend the only equality that matters: the equal right of every individual to be free from state coercion.

Sypnowich's essay effectively diagnoses why opportunity-focused egalitarianism falls short. Her critique of luck egalitarianism's sharp distinction between choice and circumstance is compelling: if our preferences, dispositions, and even what we take to be our authentic choices are shaped by social conditions, then the idea that individuals should bear full responsibility for their outcomes becomes difficult to defend. The flourishing perspective she advocates offers a more humane measure of equality—one that asks not whether people had formal chances but whether they actually live well.

Yet her argument requires greater specification in at least two respects. First, the flourishing approach must clarify what genuine equal flourishing actually demands. Sypnowich rightly insists that flourishing comes in many forms and need not be imposed coercively. But she also argues that we should "not be squeamish about an outcomes approach that takes a stand on how people should live." Second, Sypnowich gestures toward cultivating responsibility and encouraging people to become "better choosers," yet if choices are thoroughly shaped by social context, the meaning of responsibility becomes elusive. The essay would benefit from distinguishing among different senses of responsibility—moral, practical, and political—rather than conflating them. We might reasonably hold people to commitments they actually make while still recognizing that those commitments arise from social circumstances we can and should address. This would provide a more defensible foundation for the responsibility-attention that Sypnowich rightly retrieves from the luck egalitarian tradition without endorsing its harshness.

Sypnowich's emphasis on solidarity offers the most promising path forward. Her observation that opportunity-talk sidesteps "the responsibility members of an egalitarian society may have to their fellows" names a genuine gap in liberal egalitarianism. The Rawlsian framework, for all its sophistication, tends to treat justice as a matter of institutions rather than relationships. Yet moving from the recognition that we are social beings to the claim that "the egalitarian ideal requires socialism" requires more argument than the essay provides. One can accept that equality involves community and solidarity without concluding that these require the abolition of private property or the central planning associated with historical socialism. Democratic socialism, market socialism, and robust welfare state approaches all incorporate solidarity while differing on institutional design. The flourishing ideal would benefit from engaging more directly with these institutional questions rather than treating socialism as a conclusion reached once we reject opportunity-talk.

Finally, the flourishing approach raises questions about political strategy. Sypnowich is surely right that a polity committed to genuine equality cannot simply present people with options and wash its hands of what follows. But the history of progressive reform suggests that such commitments often prove vulnerable to capture by expert administrators who define flourishing in technocratic terms disconnected from democratic contestation. A flourishing-based egalitarianism needs to specify not merely that people should flourish but who gets to decide what flourishing means and through what democratic processes those determinations can be revised. Without such mechanisms, the flourishing ideal risks becoming another guise for expert-driven paternalism, however benevolently intended.

In the end, Sypnowich is right that equal opportunity is insufficient. Her essay should prompt further inquiry into what an equality of flourishing would actually require—including difficult questions about pluralism, responsibility, institutional design, and democratic accountability that the flourishing perspective must address if it is to replace rather than merely supplement opportunity-focused egalitarianism.
