"The primary threat to American democracy is not the formal constitutional structure of checks and balances, but rather a collective action problem and the deliberate subversion of informal democratic norms."

The weakness in Miller's analysis is its assumption that constitutional structure drives political outcomes rather than the reverse. She describes how veto points empower elite minorities to block popular reforms, but she does not adequately explain why those veto points persist despite their unpopularity. The answer is that they persist because organized minorities value them more than dispersed majorities value their removal. This is not a constitutional problem; it is a collective action problem. The same logic applies to her proposed reforms: eliminating the Electoral College requires building a coalition that spans regional and partisan lines, which is precisely the kind of coalition-building she advocates but cannot magic into existence through argument. More fundamentally, Miller's focus on formal constitutional structures ignores informal norms that matter far more for democratic stability. The Senate filibuster has been eliminated and reinstated multiple times; its current form is not constitutionally mandated. The unitary executive theory that undergirds Trump's expansive use of executive authority is not in the Constitution; it was constructed by conservative scholars and imposed on a compliant judiciary. What we are witnessing is not the natural working of constitutional checks and balances but their deliberate subversion by actors who understand that formal rules can be reinterpreted or simply ignored. The solution is not to reform the Constitution but to rebuild the norms and institutions—civil society organizations, professional associations, independent media—that sustain democratic governance. Constitutional text without democratic culture is just parchment.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Veto points persist because organized minorities are more motivated to protect them than dispersed majorities are to remove them, making it a problem of political organization rather than constitutional design.
  2. 02Proposed constitutional reforms face the same coalition-building hurdles that currently prevent policy change, meaning they cannot serve as a simple shortcut to progress.
  3. 03The current political crisis stems from the reinterpretation and subversion of rules by bad-faith actors rather than the inherent mechanics of the Constitution.
  4. 04Democratic stability depends more on informal norms and a robust democratic culture—sustained by civil society and independent media—than on the literal text of the Constitution.
  5. 05Focusing on formal structural reform ignores the fact that many obstructive tools, such as the filibuster and the unitary executive theory, are not constitutionally mandated but are products of political choice.
