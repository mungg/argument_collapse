Diversified Gemini

"To achieve lasting reform and combat extreme inequality, the Occupy movement must move beyond symbolic protest and align itself with organized labor to build the institutional power necessary to challenge capital."

As we debate the future of the Occupy movement, it is essential to ground our aspirations in the historical reality of how power actually operates in the United States. The Boston Review editors beautifully articulate our nation's overarching commitment to principles and point out that our history is driven by the tension between those ideals and our practices. They mention the end of slavery, women's suffrage, and the civil rights movement. But there is a glaring omission in this historical summary, one that is critical to understanding the current crisis of inequality: the labor movement.

The "opportunity to get ahead or otherwise lead a good life" was not handed down by benevolent politicians who suddenly realized they were failing to live up to American ideals. The middle class of the 20th century was built with blood, sweat, and picket lines. The eight-hour workday, the weekend, workplace safety standards, and employer-provided healthcare were won through the collective, organized power of working people in unions.

The extreme inequality we face today is not merely a vague moral failing; it is the direct mathematical consequence of the systematic destruction of organized labor over the past four decades. Since the late 1970s, corporate America has waged a relentless war on workers' right to bargain collectively. From the firing of the PATCO strikers to the globalization of supply chains that pitted American workers against deeply exploited labor abroad, to the current legislative assaults on public sector unions in states like Wisconsin and Ohio, the strategy has been clear: break the institutional power of the working class.

Occupy Wall Street has injected a desperately needed burst of energy into the American left. The slogan "We are the 99%" has brilliantly captured the public imagination and shifted the national conversation away from draconian deficit reduction and toward the obscenity of wealth concentration. But moral outrage, no matter how eloquently expressed or highly visible in public parks, does not by itself alter the balance of power.

Capitalism responds to leverage, not to moral suasion. The fatal weakness of the Occupy movement in its current form is its reliance on spontaneous, localized encampments and "horizontal" consensus processes that, while deeply democratic in spirit, lack the institutional muscle to enforce demands. To achieve the "dramatic reform" the editors speak of, Occupy must pivot from being a protest movement to an organizing movement, and it must fuse its energy with the existing, albeit weakened, labor movement.

We cannot re-democratize American politics without re-democratizing the American workplace. The right to be heard when our country makes decisions about its future must extend to the right to be heard when a corporation makes decisions about our livelihoods. This means prioritizing the passage of legislation like the Employee Free Choice Act to make it easier to form unions. It means engaging in strategic, targeted strikes. It means recognizing that an occupation of a public square is only the precursor to the necessary occupation of the shop floor. Without a robust labor movement anchoring it, the Occupy moment will pass, and the 1% will simply wait us out.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The American middle class and its associated rights were not granted by ideals alone but were won through the collective, organized power of the labor movement.
  2. 02Current extreme inequality is the direct result of a decades-long, systematic corporate campaign to dismantle the institutional power of unions.
  3. 03Moral outrage and horizontal consensus processes lack the structural leverage required to force concessions from the capitalist system.
  4. 04Meaningful democratic reform requires extending democratic principles to the workplace by strengthening unions and utilizing strategic labor actions like strikes.
  5. 05Without the institutional muscle of a robust labor movement, the Occupy movement risks being a fleeting moment that the ruling class can simply outwait.
