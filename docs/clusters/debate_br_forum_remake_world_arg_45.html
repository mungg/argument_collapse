"While Walter Johnson’s 'racial capitalism' framework offers a powerful political tool for linking historical slavery to modern systemic inequality, its broad definition of capitalism risks sacrificing analytical and historical precision."

Walter Johnson's essay is a sophisticated intervention in the debate on slavery, capitalism, and justice, and it deserves careful engagement with its central historiographical claims. One of the most important of these claims is about the relationship between slavery and capitalism, and Johnson's argument that they were constitutively intertwined rather than accidentally connected.

Johnson draws on Du Bois and Williams to argue that the history of the Atlantic economy cannot be separated into the history of slavery and the history of capitalism. The plantation and the factory were nodes in a single network, and the profits of the slave trade funded the industrial revolution. This argument has substantial support, especially when we consider the financial and commercial infrastructure of the Atlantic economy.

Yet the question of what we mean by "capitalism" remains. The concept is often used so broadly that it encompasses almost any economy organized through markets, which makes it difficult to distinguish between different forms of economic organization. If capitalism means simply the pursuit of profit through market exchange, then the plantation was capitalist—but so was the household economy of the ancient world, and so is almost every economy that has ever existed.

A more precise definition would emphasize the specific form of labor control that characterized capitalism: the free worker who sells her labor power for a wage and is legally free to refuse. By this definition, the plantation was not capitalist but something else—a system of labor control based on property-in-persons rather than contract.

Johnson recognizes this distinction but argues that it should not be overemphasized. The plantation was commercially oriented, and its operations depended on market relationships; the enslaved were not only workers but capital, invested and leveraged in sophisticated financial instruments. The plantation was thus "capitalist" in important respects, even if it was not organized through wage labor.

This is a reasonable position, but it raises questions about the analytical utility of the concept. If "capitalism" can include both the wage labor of industrial capitalism and the slave labor of the plantation, then the concept becomes so broad that it loses its explanatory power. What is gained by calling both systems "capitalist"?

Perhaps the answer is that "racial capitalism" is not so much an analytical concept as a political one. It is a way of linking the history of slavery to the history of capitalism in order to make claims about the present. If capitalism was constitutively intertwined with slavery, then the racial hierarchy that slavery created is not a residue that can be eliminated by equal opportunity reforms; it is built into the system itself.

This is a powerful political claim, and Johnson makes it well. But it should not be confused with a neutral historical analysis. The question "was slavery capitalist?" is not only a historical question; it is also a political question, and its answer depends on what we want the concept of capitalism to do.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Defining capitalism solely as profit-seeking through market exchange makes the term so expansive that it fails to distinguish between vastly different economic systems across human history.
  2. 02A more precise historical definition of capitalism centers on the specific transition to free wage labor and contract, which fundamentally differs from the property-in-persons model of the plantation.
  3. 03Collapsing the distinction between wage labor and chattel slavery into a single category of 'capitalism' diminishes the concept's explanatory power regarding different modes of labor control.
  4. 04The 'racial capitalism' thesis functions more as a political intervention to argue that racial hierarchy is inherent to the current global system than as a neutral historiographical analysis.
