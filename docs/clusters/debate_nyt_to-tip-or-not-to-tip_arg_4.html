"While a universal ban on tipping may be impractical, restaurants should transition toward a 'service included' model where staff are paid stable salaries and gratuities are treated as optional bonuses rather than essential compensation."

The answer may differ for a sushi temple, a diner and a dive bar. Sushi Yasuda can eliminate tipping because its service is controlled, its clientele is relatively affluent, and its cultural reference point—Japan—fits the cuisine. That does not mean every restaurant should immediately follow. But the experiment points in the right direction, especially for full-service dining.

Tipping persists partly because each restaurant fears moving alone. If one place raises prices by 20 percent and says “service included,” it may look more expensive on a menu than the place next door, even though the final cost is the same. That first mover problem is real. It explains why the custom survives even among people who dislike it. Diners overestimate the posted price and underestimate the add-ons.

For that reason, change should begin in sectors where customers are most likely to understand it: fine dining, tasting-menu restaurants, hotel restaurants, and places with strong reputations. These businesses can explain that service is included, pay staff salaries, and build the cost into reservations or prix fixe menus. If it works there, the norm may spread downward. If it fails, the damage is limited.

Casual restaurants and bars require more care. A bartender serving quick beers to regulars and a server managing a three-hour tasting menu do not have the same job. A universal rule would be clumsy. Some workers also like tipping because busy shifts can produce high earnings. Reformers should not ignore those voices in the name of tidiness.

Still, the present system is too dependent on customer whim. It produces volatile income, invites discrimination, and relies on a subminimum wage structure that is hard to defend. The long-term goal should be to separate basic compensation from gratuity. If customers want to leave something extra for extraordinary service, fine—but that should be a bonus, not the worker’s paycheck.

So other restaurants should follow Sushi Yasuda selectively and experimentally. The best model may not be “tips forbidden” everywhere, but “service included” as the default, with wages high enough that no employee has to depend on a stranger’s arithmetic at the end of the night.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'first mover problem' prevents restaurants from eliminating tipping because customers perceive higher menu prices as more expensive even when the total cost remains the same.
  2. 02Transitioning to a service-included model should begin with fine dining and high-reputation establishments where customers are more likely to accept and understand the change.
  3. 03A universal rule against tipping may be inappropriate for casual bars and restaurants where the nature of the work and the preferences of the staff differ significantly from formal dining.
  4. 04The current tipping system is flawed because it creates volatile income for workers, invites discrimination, and relies on an indefensible subminimum wage structure.
  5. 05The long-term goal of the industry should be to decouple basic worker compensation from the unpredictable whims and arithmetic of customers.
Diversified DeepSeek

"Restaurants should adopt a service-included model that covers base compensation while allowing for small, truly voluntary bonuses to preserve human connection without the psychological burdens of traditional tipping."

Tipping is one of the strangest social rituals we have: a supposedly voluntary payment that is, in practice, compulsory. The Sushi Yasuda model strips away that ambiguity, forcing us to confront what dining out actually costs and what we owe the people who serve us. From a cultural psychology standpoint, tipping serves as a tangible gesture of gratitude, a small act of generosity that can create a moment of human connection between stranger and server. Without tips, the dining experience becomes more transactional, more like buying a product off a shelf. That loss is not trivial; it contributes to a broader atomization of service interactions. Yet the ritual comes with a dark side. The pressure to tip induces anxiety among customers—Am I being cheap? Will they judge me?—and can lead to performative overspending. For servers, the need to earn tips encourages emotional labor—smiling through exhaustion, tolerating unwanted advances—that can be psychologically draining. So is a service-included model emotionally healthier? Possibly, but it also flattens the interaction. When everyone’s compensation is guaranteed, the incentive to provide that extra bit of warmth or care may diminish. We might find ourselves in a world of indifferent, tip-free service like one sometimes encounters in parts of Europe. The question, then, is what kind of dining culture we want to cultivate. Sushi Yasuda’s decision pushes us to redesign a deeply ingrained norm. Perhaps we can imagine a future where the base obligation is covered by a service charge, but customers are still permitted to offer a small, truly voluntary bonus as a “thank you”—not out of guilt, but out of genuine appreciation. That could preserve the best of tipping while mitigating its worst excesses. The debate is not just economic; it’s about how we want to treat one another when we break bread.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Traditional tipping creates a sense of social anxiety for customers and forces servers into exhausting emotional labor to secure their income.
  2. 02Eliminating tips entirely risks making the dining experience feel overly transactional and may reduce the incentive for servers to provide warm, personalized care.
  3. 03A service-included model provides transparency regarding the true cost of dining and ensures fair compensation for staff regardless of customer whims.
  4. 04Allowing for optional, non-compulsory gratuities on top of a service charge can maintain the positive ritual of expressing genuine gratitude between diners and servers.
