"While the lead essay correctly critiques the commodification of 'natural' reproduction, its techno-optimistic framework risks alienating women from their own embodied experiences and ignoring the vital ecological and political importance of a reclaimed, inclusive concept of nature."

The essay “All Reproduction Is Assisted” is an indispensable demolition of a certain kind of smug, Gwyneth Paltrow‑esque naturalism that markets pregnancy as a lifestyle accessory. But in its zeal to expose the ideological work of “the natural,” it veers toward a position that is almost allergic to embodiment itself. It treats the body as mere equipment, a machine that should be optimized or bypassed in the name of freedom, and it dismisses any attachment to embodied experience as a “reactionary hippie‑Rousseauean Return‑to‑Nature.” There is a genuine feminist tradition, however, that takes nature not as a fixed destiny but as a site of meaning, a relationship to cultivate rather than a prison to escape. I want to suggest that reclaiming the natural, critically and self‑consciously, might be a more fruitful starting point for a reproductive politics that values care, connection, and ecological sustainability.

The lead essay’s central example of the suffocating power of the natural is the online world of “natural childbirth” influencers—white women in well‑appointed apartments doing water births and selling hypnosis tutorials. I find this world as cloying and exclusionary as the author does. But the problem with those influencers is not that they embrace natural experiences; it is that they commodify them, racialize them, and use them to build hierarchies of good and bad motherhood. One can be deeply critical of those practices without concluding that everything associated with natural birth—midwifery, extended breastfeeding, skin‑to‑skin contact, minimal medical intervention when safe—is a weapon of the patriarchy. Indeed, much of the modern natural‑birth movement grew out of a left‑wing reaction against the medicalization of childbirth in the mid‑twentieth century, when women were routinely knocked out with twilight sleep and separated from their babies for days. The reclaiming of birth as an active, embodied, even pleasurable process was, in that context, a genuinely liberatory impulse. To equate that impulse with Adrienne Rich’s mystical invocation of “lunar cycles” is to flatten a complex history.

The essay’s reliance on Firestone’s “shit a pumpkin” thought experiment is rhetorically powerful, but it’s also revealingly partial. It reduces the experience of labor to pain and boredom, as if there were nothing else—no waves of oxytocin, no moments of intense focus, no meeting of a person whose face you have imagined for months. I do not romanticize pain; I have given birth twice, and it was the hardest physical work I have ever done. But it was also the most meaningful. To describe that meaning as simply the School of Great Experience brainwashing women into “internalized misogyny” is to refuse to listen to what women say about their own lives. It is, in its own way, as patriarchal as the dismissal it critiques: a male‑voiced ventriloquism that says, Whatever you feel, you have been duped, and I will explain your own desires back to you in a language of falsity.

The essay’s resistance to the body goes hand in hand with a broader blind spot about ecology. The manufactured “all‑natural” supplement bottle is an easy target, but there is a deeper sense in which reproduction is indeed part of a natural world that we ignore at our peril. The rise of endocrine‑disrupting chemicals in plastics and pesticides has been linked to declining sperm counts and rising rates of infertility, miscarriage, and endometriosis. Climate change threatens reproductive futures in the most literal sense—through extreme heat, famine, and displacement. An environmental feminism that connects the health of the planet to the health of pregnant bodies is not a return to Eden; it is a materialist analysis that the techno‑optimistic framework of the lead essay largely sidelines. The question isn’t whether we should use technology—of course we should, and we always have. The question is which technologies, owned by whom, and in what relationship to the wider ecosystems that sustain all life.

Queer and trans reproduction, which the essay rightly brings to the center, actually strengthens the case for a reworked concept of the natural. When a trans man carries a pregnancy, or a lesbian couple uses home insemination with a known donor, they are not merely using technology as a neutral tool. They are remixing biological capacities in ways that reveal the plasticity and strangeness of nature itself. That is not “unnatural”; it is nature’s own baroque creativity, which human‑made technologies extend rather than negate. The problem is not that such arrangements are technological; the problem is that they are stigmatized and under‑resourced. The natural, in other words, is not the enemy; the narrow, heterosexist, ableist version of the natural that has been peddled by the Christian right and the wellness industry is the enemy. We need a thicker, more capacious account of the natural—one that includes miscarriage and disability and menopause and the astonishing variety of ways that human bodies can nurture life.

In the end, I find the essay’s argument that “all reproduction is assisted” both true and somewhat banal. Yes, we use tools; yes, a pregnancy test is a piece of technology; yes, every birth is socially embedded. But so what? Pointing out that a woman who gives birth in a hospital is just as “assisted” as a woman using IVF doesn’t get us very far politically. The difference between assistance that amplifies bodily autonomy and assistance that alienates us from our bodies matters, and it is a difference that can’t be articulated without some reference to what our bodies need and desire. The birth I experienced involved an epidural and a fetal monitor—assistance, certainly—but it also involved a primal, animal focus that I would not trade for the most advanced artificial womb in the world. Telling me that this feeling is ideological doesn’t make it go away; it just tells me that the person doing the telling hasn’t felt it. A feminism that scoffs at the body is a feminism that has amputated its own roots. I want a feminism that helps all of us, in all our gendered and embodied diversity, to inhabit our bodies fully, to get the assistance we need without shame, and to love the natural world that makes our lives possible in the first place. That seems like a project more worth building than a cybernetic flight from the flesh.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The lead essay unfairly dismisses the liberatory history of the natural-birth movement, which originally emerged as a feminist resistance against the excessive medicalization of women's bodies.
  2. 02Reducing the experience of childbirth to pain and 'internalized misogyny' ignores the profound meaning and agency many women find in the physical process of labor.
  3. 03A purely technological focus on reproduction sidelines essential environmental concerns, such as how ecological degradation and endocrine disruptors directly impact reproductive health.
  4. 04Queer and trans reproductive experiences demonstrate that nature is plastic and diverse rather than a rigid heteronormative prison, suggesting we need a more capacious definition of the natural rather than an abandonment of it.
  5. 05The claim that 'all reproduction is assisted' is a reductive observation that fails to distinguish between technologies that enhance bodily autonomy and those that alienate individuals from their physical selves.
