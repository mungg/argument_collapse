**Strengthen What We Already Have: State Victim Compensation**

Lost in nearly every conversation about terrorism compensation is the fact that the United States has been quietly compensating crime victims for decades. Every state runs a Crime Victim Compensation program, most of them funded by a mix of court fees, criminal fines, and federal money from the Victims of Crime Act. These programs paid out roughly half a billion dollars last year to victims of assaults, sexual violence, drunk-driving crashes, and yes, mass shootings. They are unglamorous, bureaucratic, and they work.

The problem is that they are too small and too obscure. Most state programs cap benefits at $25,000 to $50,000 per victim, which is fine for funeral expenses but inadequate for catastrophic long-term injury. Many require victims to exhaust other sources first and to navigate a paperwork-heavy claims process. Awareness is dismal: surveys consistently find that the majority of eligible victims never apply.

The right reform is to build on this existing infrastructure rather than invent new mechanisms after each headline-grabbing attack. Raise the caps. Expand eligibility to include mental health treatment, which mass-shooting survivors often need for years. Fund outreach so that hospitals and police departments routinely inform victims of their rights. And create a federal supplemental program that automatically tops up state benefits whenever a mass-casualty event overwhelms a state fund's capacity.

This approach has several advantages over the bespoke-fund model. It treats the victim of a workplace shooting in Wichita the same as the victim of a bombing in Boston, which is more morally defensible than our current habit of compensating the famous tragedies generously and the obscure ones not at all. It removes the political theater of debating each new compensation scheme during a moment of grief. And it preserves a role for private charity to address the genuinely unusual needs that no statutory program can anticipate.

The 9/11 fund will probably always stand as a unique response to a unique event. But Boston, Aurora, Newtown, Tucson, Virginia Tech — these are tragically not so unusual anymore. We need a system, not a series of improvisations. The bones of that system already exist; what's missing is the political will to give them flesh.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Existing state programs provide a proven, functional bureaucratic infrastructure that currently handles a wide range of violent crimes.
  2. 02Current state programs are insufficient because their benefit caps are too low and public awareness of their existence is poor.
  3. 03The government should increase benefit caps, expand eligibility for mental health services, and create a federal supplemental fund for mass-casualty events.
  4. 04A standardized system ensures moral equity by treating victims of obscure tragedies the same as victims of high-profile terrorist attacks.
  5. 05Formalizing compensation through existing programs removes the need for political theater and reactive legislation following every national tragedy.
  6. 06A permanent statutory system allows private charity to focus on unique, specialized needs rather than basic financial restitution.
