Diversified Gemini

"Aziz Rana’s proposal to reclaim freedom through democratic institution-building is insufficient because it seeks to reform the settler-colonial state rather than pursue true decolonization and Indigenous sovereignty."

In "A Different Freedom," Aziz Rana offers a sweeping diagnosis of American political culture, astutely tracing the contemporary right-wing monopoly on the rhetoric of "freedom" back to its roots in settler colonialism and imperial expansion. By identifying that the American experience of liberty was fundamentally subsidized by Indigenous expropriation and racial capitalism, Rana shatters the mythology of a pristine, classical liberal founding. However, while his historical diagnosis is largely accurate, his proposed solution—rebuilding American democratic institutions to foster a "solidaristic" and "inclusive" domestic freedom—reveals a lingering attachment to the very settler-state apparatus he critiques. From a post-colonial and Indigenous perspective, Rana’s vision remains fundamentally reformist; it seeks to democratize the settler colony rather than decolonize it.

Rana accurately observes that for early American settlers, "to own land was to be free," and that this land had to be violently taken. He recognizes that "Indigenous conquest and Native expropriation... became the basic engine of liberty." This is the foundational truth of the United States: the nation is built on a stolen land base, and its political economy is permanently scarred by this original sin.

Yet, when Rana pivots to his solutions, this foundational truth is suddenly sidelined in favor of a traditional left-liberal agenda: passing the PRO Act, strengthening labor unions, reforming voting rights, and reducing military spending. He suggests that by transforming the American workplace and political system into engines of "collective self-rule," we can replace the exclusionary, zero-sum freedom of the past with an inclusive, democratic freedom for the future. He adds a nod to "redistributive and reparative policies that commit to Indigenous freedom," but this feels like an add-on to a project that is still overwhelmingly centered on the settler working class.

The fatal flaw in Rana’s argument is the assumption that the institutions of the United States—its legal frameworks, its borders, its economy—can simply be retrofitted to serve emancipatory ends without addressing the underlying question of sovereignty and land. You cannot build a truly "solidaristic" freedom on a foundation of unredressed theft. Democratizing a settler-colonial state just means that the settlers are sharing the spoils of conquest more equitably among themselves.

If a factory is built on stolen land, unionizing that factory and giving the workers democratic control over production is certainly better for the workers, but it does absolutely nothing to restore the sovereignty of the Indigenous nation whose land is still occupied. The workers are still participating in, and benefiting from, the ongoing dispossession of Native peoples. An "inclusive" American freedom that brings marginalized groups (immigrants, Black Americans, the white working class) into the fold of collective self-rule without returning land and power to Indigenous nations is merely an expansion of the settler class.

Rana correctly notes that freedom struggles must grapple with their specific histories. But true decolonization is not a metaphor, nor is it synonymous with socialism or social democracy. As scholars Eve Tuck and K. Wayne Yang have argued, decolonization specifically requires the repatriation of Indigenous land and life. It is unsettling. It cannot be achieved through the "institution building" of the very state that perpetrated the colonization.

When Rana advocates for "expanding and equalizing voting rights" and reforming the "state-based, and gerrymandered framework," he is legitimizing the jurisdiction of the United States over Indigenous territories. Indigenous sovereignty is inherently contradictory to the absolute sovereignty claimed by the U.S. federal government. A genuinely emancipatory project would not seek to integrate Indigenous peoples into a perfected American democracy; it would seek to honor nation-to-nation treaties, dismantle the plenary power doctrine, and support the Land Back movement—transferring jurisdiction, resource management, and actual territory back to Native nations.

Furthermore, Rana’s linking of domestic freedom to foreign policy, while crucial, falls short in its treatment of borders. He advocates for "presumptive admission for all" and the decriminalization of entry, viewing immigration reform as a way to build a "richer account of shared class." But from an Indigenous perspective, the U.S.-Mexico border is a colonial imposition that violently bisects ancestral homelands, such as those of the Tohono O'odham Nation. Advocating for open borders within the framework of the U.S. state still presumes that the U.S. has the legitimate authority to manage that land. A decolonial approach to migration would center Indigenous protocols of hospitality and movement, recognizing that the current border crisis is a clash between different modalities of colonial statecraft.

Rana’s desire to reclaim the word "freedom" for the Left is understandable, given its mobilizing power. But we must ask: freedom for whom, and on whose terms? The anticolonial struggles that Rana cites—leaders like Michael Manley and Julius Nyerere—were not fighting to integrate into a reformed global empire; they were fighting for national sovereignty and self-determination. In the context of North America, that same logic must apply to the hundreds of Indigenous nations that predate the United States.

To move beyond the "corrosive path" of settler freedom, we cannot simply rely on the American Left's traditional toolkit of unionization and voting rights reform, no matter how necessary those are for immediate harm reduction. We must possess the radical imagination to envision a future that challenges the territorial integrity and absolute authority of the United States itself. Anything less is merely a kinder, gentler form of occupation. Real freedom requires not just shared decision-making in the workplace, but the return of the land upon which that workplace sits.
