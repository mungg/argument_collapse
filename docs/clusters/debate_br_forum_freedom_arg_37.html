"While Aziz Rana correctly identifies that reclaiming freedom requires building domestic institutions for collective self-rule, this project can only succeed if it explicitly targets and dismantles the global structures of American empire and capitalism that actively suppress such democratic experiments both at home and abroad."

Aziz Rana’s bracing genealogy of American freedom will unsettle anyone who cherishes that word as an unalloyed good. By embedding the U.S. experience in settler colonialism and global empire, Rana shows how the dominant conception of liberty became a vehicle for exclusion, militarism, and atomized individualism. Yet his most urgent insight is not historical; it is strategic. He argues that reclaiming freedom for the left requires more than clever messaging—it demands the slow, unglamorous work of building institutions that make collective self-rule a lived reality. This is a powerful and necessary call. But if we take it seriously, we must push it further, grappling with the global structures that continue to make such institution-building difficult and asking what an anti-imperial freedom project actually demands of us today.

Rana’s central claim is that settler expansion and global primacy have repeatedly undergirded a zero-sum liberty: freedom for insiders rests on domination of outsiders. The corollary is that when the material basis of that arrangement crumbles—as it did before the New Deal and, he suggests, is happening now—the door opens for a solidaristic alternative. I am largely persuaded. Yet there is a tension in his account. He treats empire largely as a domestic enabler of individualistic freedom, but he underplays the way contemporary American empire actively forecloses the very institution-building he champions, not just at home but across the globe. Global capitalism, underwritten by U.S. military power, disciplines nation-states that might otherwise experiment with deeper forms of social democracy. The same forces that hollowed out American unions and offshored manufacturing also devastated labor movements in the Global South and imposed austerity on governments that tried to prioritize public goods over investor rights. Reclaiming freedom at home, then, is inseparable from contesting the architecture of international economic governance.

Consider the realm of labor, which Rana rightly centers. He calls for expanding unionization and workplace democracy through reforms like the PRO Act. But these domestic efforts are perpetually undermined by the threat of capital flight and the downward pressure on labor standards exerted through trade agreements. The United States–Mexico–Canada Agreement, for instance, includes some enforceable labor provisions, but its core logic still empowers corporations to seek the lowest common denominator. Building genuine workplace self-rule in America thus requires not only domestic legal change but a transformation of the global rules that pit workers against one another. Without that, the “experiments in linking freedom and democracy” Rana envisions will remain boutique—a few cooperatives and union shops swimming against a tsunami of precarity.

Likewise, his treatment of immigration is suggestive but incomplete. He correctly identifies that reversing the settler binary of insider/outsider means embracing presumptive admission and decriminalizing entry. But this cannot be just a domestic legislative project. It must confront the reality that the same economic might underpinned by U.S. global dominance creates the displacement that drives migration. Decades of trade liberalization, structural adjustment, and client-state backing have shattered local economies from Central America to West Africa. If Americans are to experience immigration not as a threat but as an opportunity for shared class solidarity, as Rana hopes, then the left must champion not just open borders but a radical reordering of the global economy—debt cancellation, development policies built on local food sovereignty rather than export-oriented agriculture, and an end to the kind of military interventions that destabilize entire regions.

This points to a deeper challenge. Rana notes that today fewer Americans enjoy material benefits from U.S. global dominance. That is true, but a significant minority still does: the shareholder class, the security state’s contractors, the credentialing cartels that manage the global order. Even as inequality widens, the material and ideological grip of empire remains powerful, and it generates intense resistance to any anti-imperial politics. The bipartisan Washington consensus still frames American primacy as essential to global stability and, ironically, to freedom. Breaking that consensus will require not just domestic institution-building but a transnational movement capable of challenging the political power of the military-industrial complex and the financial sector. Rana gestures toward this when he mentions security spending and the Freedom Budget, but the scale of the transformation he intimates is staggering: a wholesale repurposing of the American state away from projection and toward domestic and global redistribution.

My point is not to dismiss his argument as infeasible, but to push it to its logical conclusion. The left freedom project Rana envisions is not just a matter of strengthening unions, reforming the political system, and welcoming immigrants—though all that is essential. It is a project of global reconstruction, one that takes seriously the anti-colonial demand for a New International Economic Order and its contemporary heirs. This means that the left’s “institution-building” cannot be merely national in scope; it must cultivate solidarity across borders, linking struggles for workplace democracy in the United States with movements for land rights and labor sovereignty elsewhere. It requires engaging with the very “worldmaking” that leaders like Nyerere and Manley pursued, learning from the experiments in participatory democracy and social ownership that have emerged from Cochabamba to Rojava, and insisting on a democratic foreign policy.

This is a tall order, and it runs against the grain of a political culture that has for centuries equated internationalism with weakness. But Rana’s own argument suggests that moments of imperial uncertainty open space for such leaps. The present is one such moment. The debacle of the “forever wars,” the rise of a multipolar world, and the growing climate emergency—which is itself a consequence of the imperial mode of production—are fraying the old certainties. The question is whether the left can seize the opening not by backing away from freedom but by reimagining it as a collective and planetary project. Rana has given us a vital framework. The task now is to stretch it beyond the boundaries of the nation-state, because in an era of climate collapse and global supply chains, no one can be free alone. The solidaristic freedom he calls for must ultimately be a freedom that dismantles the empire from within and without, building institutions that serve not just the American working class but all those whose labor and lands have been sacrificed to the idol of a freedom that was never meant to be shared.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The American empire does not just enable individualistic domestic freedom; it actively forecloses social democratic institution-building by using global capitalism and military power to discipline states and labor movements.
  2. 02Domestic labor reforms, such as workplace democracy and unionization, are insufficient unless the global trade rules that empower capital flight and pit workers against one another are fundamentally transformed.
  3. 03A solidaristic approach to immigration must go beyond domestic decriminalization to address the U.S.-led global economic policies and military interventions that cause displacement and migration in the first place.
  4. 04Reclaiming freedom requires a transnational movement for global reconstruction that revives anti-colonial demands for a New International Economic Order and links domestic struggles with movements for sovereignty in the Global South.
  5. 05The left must seize the current moment of imperial uncertainty to redefine freedom as a planetary project that rejects national boundaries in favor of a democratic foreign policy and global redistribution.
