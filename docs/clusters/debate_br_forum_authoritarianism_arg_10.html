"The empirical case against political moderation is far from settled, and the proposed shift toward a confrontational mobilization strategy relies on selective data and questionable causal assumptions."

I am a professor of American politics at a large public university, and I have watched with growing dismay as the discipline has been drawn into partisan advocacy masquerading as social science. The essay by Bonica and Grumbach is a perfect specimen of this trend. It deploys sophisticated methods and a tone of hard-nosed realism to argue that “moderation doesn’t work,” urging Democrats to adopt a confrontational, anti-corruption mobilization strategy. Whatever one thinks of the political advice, the empirical case is far from settled, and the piece takes liberties with the scholarly record that a non-academic audience might miss. In the interest of intellectual honesty, a few corrections are in order.

First, the claim that the “moderation advantage has largely disappeared” in the Trump era is overstated. The authors cite their own work and a handful of recent studies, but the literature is more mixed than they let on. For example, a 2022 study by political scientists Thomas Nelson and James Gimpel found that ideological moderation still provides a significant electoral boost in House races, particularly for Republicans—a finding that complicates the narrative that moderation is useless for Democrats. Other work by Nicholas Carnes and Noam Lupu (2020) shows that voters continue to punish extreme candidates when they are perceived as such, but the perception of extremism is heavily shaped by party cues, not just candidate positioning. The authors’ own analysis, using “very closely contested Democratic primaries,” may suffer from a selection problem: the races that are close enough to be essentially random are precisely those where the electorate is already evenly divided, meaning that candidate ideology might not tip the scales. That doesn’t prove ideology never matters; it shows it doesn’t matter in those specific, exceptional cases. The real-world test is more ecological: do districts with more moderate incumbents retain those seats at higher rates than districts with progressive ones, controlling for district partisanship? Many analyses say yes, even in recent cycles.

Second, the authors lean heavily on the “turnout gap” explanation for Democratic losses, but they underplay the possibility that the causal arrow runs the other way. That is, perhaps Democrats turned out less in 2024 because the party’s brand had become so associated with cultural positions they found off-putting. The essay acknowledges that “nonvoters likely leaned slightly toward Trump over Harris,” which is a polite way of saying that many working-class voters are no longer Democrats and don’t vote because they’ve been ideologically or culturally alienated. If that’s true, then a mobilization strategy that leans further into the positions that alienated them—even if it comes wrapped in anti-corruption language—could be counterproductive. The essay’s own evidence that progressive candidates slightly boost turnout is, as they admit, an aggregate effect; it may be driven entirely by safe blue districts, not competitive ones. And even in blue districts, a leftward lurch could trigger a backlash among more conservative primary voters of color, as we saw in the 2021 New York City mayoral primary.

Third, the proposed anti-corruption frame is, from a political science perspective, a classic valence issue. It’s popular because everyone is for it, but it rarely decides elections because it lacks specific partisan ownership. Both sides can claim to be anti-corruption. Trump did, effectively. Moreover, valence issues tend to be crowded out by position issues when the latter are highly salient—like crime, immigration, or inflation. The 2024 election was dominated by inflation and border security, two issues on which Democrats were deeply unpopular. An anti-corruption message would have been drowned out. To believe that it could fundamentally shift the electorate’s behavior is to ignore the fundamentals of issue salience.

Fourth, the essay’s reading of the comparative politics literature is selective. The authors cite the finding that European center-left parties do not benefit from adopting anti-immigration positions. That is true in many cases, but the broader picture is nuanced. In Denmark, the Social Democrats regained power by pairing tough immigration stances with a robust welfare chauvinism. In Sweden, the center-left long held off the far right by maintaining a universal welfare state and a relatively open immigration policy, but eventually lost ground as integration challenges mounted. The lesson is not that policy positions don’t matter; it’s that they interact with existing political cleavages and institutional contexts. The U.S. is not Europe. The racial and identity-based polarization in the U.S. makes simplistic analogies hazardous. The authors’ use of these analogies feels more like cherry-picking than careful inference.

Finally, a word on the essay’s tone and purpose. It is presented as a research-based argument, but it is essentially an advocacy piece that marshals evidence in support of a predetermined conclusion. The authors dismiss methods they dislike (Split Ticket’s WAR) as “proprietary” and “biased” while presenting their own transparent analyses, but their own study design choices—how to measure ideology, which elections to examine, how to handle the Trump era—are themselves contestable and likely influenced by their priors. That is not to say they are wrong, only that readers should be cautious. The “scholarly consensus” they invoke is not a monolith; plenty of political scientists still believe that candidate positioning matters, especially on cultural issues. The problem is that the party’s activist wing is unwilling to accept the necessary trade-offs, and scholars who point this out are shouted down or accused of being “popularists,” as if that were an epithet.

In the end, the essay is a sophisticated brief for a position that many progressives already hold. It offers emotional catharsis: stop compromising, fight harder, and we’ll win. But wishing does not make it so. The risk is that Democratic elites, desperate for a new playbook, will embrace this one and discover that it alienates the very voters they need to win back. If that happens, the authoritarian threat the authors fear will only grow stronger.

Diversified DeepSeek

"While the lead essay correctly identifies the limitations of traditional moderation, it overcorrects by dismissing the persistent, marginal electoral benefits of ideological positioning and the necessity of strategic coalition maintenance."

Among the many arresting claims in “How Not to Defeat Authoritarianism,” the most consequential is the assertion that in the Trump era, a candidate’s ideological moderation has no consistent, measurable effect on vote share. If true, this finding would indeed upend decades of conventional wisdom and necessitate a wholesale reorientation of Democratic strategy. But a closer examination of the evidence reveals that this conclusion is much more fragile than the authors let on, and that in some contexts—particularly the very swing districts that decide House majorities—a moderate advantage likely persists.

The authors base their conclusion on several converging analyses. They cite their own research designs that exploit close primary elections as a natural experiment, finding that the “coin-flip” winner—whether moderate or progressive—performs roughly equally in the general election. They also critique the Split Ticket WAR metric and point to the broader scholarly consensus that moderation’s effect has diminished. However, the very research they cite doesn’t uniformly support the claim of zero effect. As they concede, “A small moderation bonus may exist in some contexts,” and other work has found that candidate ideology can still matter under certain conditions. The difference between “no consistent, measurable effect” and “a small, context-dependent effect” is not a semantic quibble; it is the difference between a null result and a lever that can be pulled in tight races. In an era of closely divided chambers, a candidate who captures even an extra percentage point by credibly signaling cultural moderation can be the difference between a majority and a minority.

One reason the authors’ findings may understate moderation’s value is their operationalization of ideology. Their composite measure includes campaign finance data, voting records, and other metrics that capture economic ideology well but are less sensitive to the cultural issues that now dominate voter perceptions. A Democrat who votes for labor rights but supports “defunding the police” or transgender girls in sports may register as moderate on the authors’ scale (because their roll-call voting is constrained by party leadership and their donor base is mainstream) while being perceived by voters as an extreme cultural warrior. The real-world “moderation” that voters reward is not a DW-NOMINATE score but a demonstrated willingness to break with the party on high-salience social issues. The authors’ own paradigmatic case, Joe Manchin, was not just a generic moderate; he was a conservative on abortion, guns, and coal, and voters knew it. When that kind of substantive, cross-cutting moderation can be reliably communicated, it still matters.

Moreover, the 2024 election provides at least some counterexamples to the “no effect” thesis. In some swing districts, candidates who had established clear, local brands distinct from the national party managed to hold on while the presidency was lost. For example, in Maine’s 2nd district, Jared Golden eventually collapsed, but that collapse was as much about the overall national environment and his own missteps as about the failure of moderation. In other districts, moderate incumbents who had survived the 2022 midterms against a red wave—such as those in the “Frontliner” group—fought their own local battles and often ran ahead of the presidential ticket, even if they ultimately lost. A systematic look at frontliners in 2024 might reveal that the most successful ones in difficult environments were those who aggressively and persistently broke with the party on culturally toxic issues. The authors’ aggregate approach may miss these micro-level dynamics.

The essay’s dismissal of popularism also paints with too broad a brush. There is a defensible version of popularism that does not reduce voters to fixed consumers but rather says: in a democratic system, you must listen to what voters tell you they want, and you must trust that they know their own experiences. The authors cite Achen and Bartels to argue that voters don’t have freestanding policy preferences, but that’s only half the story. Voters do have strong, stable reactions to certain symbols and positions that they find offensive or threatening. The job of a smart party is to recognize which of its favored ideas are so toxic to so many people that they prevent the party from acquiring the power to implement the rest of its agenda. That is not pandering; it’s coalition maintenance.

The anti-corruption frame the authors propose is, in fact, one of the most popular ideas in American politics—in the top tier of issues that overwhelmingly unite people. Thus, the authors are themselves engaging in a form of popularism: they are recommending that Democrats talk about the popular thing (corruption) and, by implication, stop foregrounding the unpopular things (like, say, taxpayer-funded gender transition surgeries for inmates, which polls terribly). They just don’t want to call it that. And that’s fine, but it underscores that the debate is less about whether to listen to voters and more about which voters to listen to and how to interpret what they say.

The deeper challenge for the anti-corruption approach is that it is easily neutralized if the other party can simply lie about it. Trump has already shown an unmatched ability to project an anti-corruption image even while engaged in staggering corruption himself, by attacking the “deep state” and “fake news.” Democrats cannot win a pure anti-corruption messaging war against a propaganda machine that can generate alternative facts. What they can do is deliver good government: actually prosecute corporate crime, actually close the revolving door, actually reduce the influence of money in campaigns. But those actions require winning majorities first. This is the chicken-and-egg problem the authors don’t resolve: to earn anti-establishment credibility, you must govern in an anti-establishment way; to govern, you must win elections; to win elections, you need anti-establishment credibility. The way out of this loop is not a new frame but the slow, unsexy work of building trust through local governance and candidate recruitment over many cycles.

In short, the essay is a valuable corrective to naive moderation triumphalism, but it overcorrects. The truth is messier: ideology still matters at the margins, especially on culture, and the smartest Democratic candidates will continue to find ways to signal their independence from the party’s most unpopular activists. The call to experiment is welcome, but it should include experimenting with different forms of moderation—cultural, procedural, relational—rather than jettisoning the entire concept. The stakes are too high to bet everything on a single new master frame.
