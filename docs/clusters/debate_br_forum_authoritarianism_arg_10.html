"The failure of political moderation is driven by a modern attention economy that rewards conflict and salience over centrist positioning, requiring Democrats to adopt a more aggressive, narrative-driven communication strategy."

Bonica and Grumbach are right that the old electoral rules have weakened, but the explanation requires more attention to the attention economy. Moderation fails not only because voters are polarized or because candidate ideology matters less. It fails because contemporary politics is mediated through platforms and partisan information systems that reward conflict, identity, novelty, and outrage. A carefully moderated message often does not travel. If it does not travel, it cannot persuade.

The popularist model imagines a campaign discovering popular positions and communicating them to voters. But communication is not a neutral pipe. Messages move through television, social media, influencers, group chats, podcasts, talk radio, local networks, and algorithmic feeds. Each medium selects for certain qualities. A white paper on lowering prescription drug costs may be popular in a poll, but it may not survive the journey through TikTok, Fox News, YouTube, Facebook, and a fragmented local news desert. A false claim about immigrant crime or “woke” schools may travel faster because it provokes fear and anger. Strategy must begin with this asymmetry.

Donald Trump understands attention intuitively. He creates spectacles that force opponents, journalists, and supporters to orient around him. He changes positions without paying the usual price because the emotional relationship precedes the policy content. He floods the zone, making fact-checking reactive. He offers simple villains and memorable phrases. The democratic response cannot be to imitate his lies or cruelty, but it must recognize that politics is now a battle over salience as much as preference.

Moderation is often anti-salient. It is designed to minimize attack surfaces, reassure elites, and avoid controversy. That may make sense in a low-information environment where voters encounter a candidate mainly through paid ads and local news. In a high-noise environment, it can make a candidate disappear. Voters may never hear the moderate message, or they may hear it only after it has been reframed by opponents. “I support a balanced border policy” becomes “Democrats want open borders” because the latter is louder, repeated more often, and emotionally charged.

Bonica and Grumbach’s call to reframe the battlefield is therefore crucial. A party cannot simply choose popular issues; it must choose issues that can organize attention. Corruption has that potential because it produces stories: the justice with billionaire gifts, the senator trading stocks, the corporation price-gouging families, the lobbyist writing loopholes, the president protecting cronies. These stories have villains, victims, and stakes. They can be told in thirty seconds without becoming trivial. They connect outrage to reform.

But Democrats must learn to communicate in ways that fit the media environment without surrendering to it. The party’s language is often too abstract: “strengthening democracy,” “lowering costs,” “protecting institutions,” “building an economy from the middle out.” These phrases may poll adequately, but they lack narrative force. Who did what to whom? Who got rich? Who got hurt? What rule will stop it? What side are you on? The right answers those questions constantly, often dishonestly. Democrats answer them episodically and timidly.

The attention economy also punishes inauthenticity. Voters are exposed not only to a candidate’s ads but to old clips, activist statements, staff tweets, donor events, awkward interviews, and hostile edits. A repositioning that might once have gone unnoticed now becomes content. This is why late-stage moderation often fails. If a politician once endorsed a position in a primary and later retreats, the retreat is not a quiet adjustment; it is a viral contrast clip. The more consultant-driven the shift, the more it confirms the charge that politicians say anything.

Authenticity, however, is not merely personal style. It is consistency between message, messenger, and medium. A billionaire-funded ad about fighting corruption lacks authenticity. A union nurse saying the hospital corporation bought politicians has it. A senator reading a poll-tested line about working families may not travel. A laid-off worker confronting a CEO might. Democrats should invest less in perfect slogans and more in networks of credible messengers who can tell concrete stories.

This means embracing a distributed communications strategy. The national campaign cannot be the sole voice. Labor leaders, local officials, teachers, veterans, small-business owners, doctors, faith leaders, students, creators, and community organizers should be equipped with facts, narratives, and platforms. Republicans have built a vast ecosystem that translates national themes into local and subcultural idioms. Democrats still rely too heavily on mainstream media validation and paid advertising. That is malpractice in 2026.

The decline of local news worsens the problem. In many communities, corruption is visible but not investigated; policy successes occur but are not reported; local candidates are defined by national stereotypes. Rebuilding democratic media infrastructure should be part of anti-authoritarian strategy. This does not mean party propaganda disguised as news. It means supporting nonprofit local journalism, ethnic media, labor media, civic information projects, and legal protections for journalists. Authoritarianism advances when citizens cannot see what power is doing near them.

The essay’s critique of popularism should also be applied to polling in the attention economy. Polls measure responses to prompts in artificial conditions. They do not measure whether an issue can command attention, survive hostile reframing, or build identity over time. A popular but boring issue may matter less than a slightly less popular issue that creates a durable coalition. Conversely, a controversial issue may be unavoidable if opponents make it salient. “Just don’t talk about it” is not a strategy when the other side spends millions talking about it for you.

Democrats need a theory of agenda-setting. What do they want voters to think the election is about? In 2024, too often the answer seemed to be a rotating mix of democracy, abortion, costs, competence, Trump’s danger, and bipartisan reassurance. All were important, but the overall story blurred. Trump’s story, however false, was simpler: the country has been betrayed, and I will punish the betrayers. Democrats need not match the darkness, but they need comparable clarity: the powerful rigged the rules, Trump protects them while distracting you with hate, and we will give power back to the people.

That story must be repeated obsessively. Democrats often abandon messages before voters have heard them. They confuse elite boredom with public saturation. The right understands repetition. Anti-corruption politics will work only if every scandal and policy fight is tied back to the same frame: they are using public power for themselves; we will make public power serve you.

There is also an affective gap. Liberal communication often assumes that facts plus decency will move people. But people seek belonging, status, humor, anger, hope, and a sense of agency. Democratic politics needs more than moral alarm. It needs cultural production: memes, songs, jokes, rituals, symbols, local events, and shared victories. This may sound unserious, but movements are sustained by emotion. Authoritarians understand that. Democrats should too.

None of this means abandoning truth or becoming demagogues. On the contrary, democratic communication must be more truthful because it asks citizens to govern themselves. But truth must be made vivid. A chart showing inequality is less powerful than a story about a worker denied care while an insurance executive cashes out. A lecture on judicial ethics is less powerful than a simple account of a billionaire flying a justice on private trips before cases affecting his interests. Facts need narrative.

Bonica and Grumbach are right: positioning is not enough. But purpose must be communicable, and communicability is now shaped by a brutal attention economy. The party that wants to defend democracy must learn to set the agenda, create memorable conflict, elevate trusted messengers, and build media channels that do not depend on the goodwill of platforms or legacy institutions.

Moderation fails when it is inaudible. Democracy will fail if its defenders cannot be heard.
