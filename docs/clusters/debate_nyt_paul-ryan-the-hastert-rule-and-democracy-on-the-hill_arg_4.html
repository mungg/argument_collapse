Diversified Gemini

"The Hastert Rule is a flexible political guideline rather than a binding law, and Paul Ryan will likely ignore it when necessary to prevent national crises just as his predecessors did."

The current hand-wringing over Paul Ryan’s supposed embrace of the Hastert Rule ignores a fundamental reality of congressional politics: the rule is not, and never has been, an absolute law. It is a political guideline, a stated preference for how a Speaker wishes to operate. Ryan’s reassurances to the Freedom Caucus are a necessary rhetorical maneuver to secure the gavel, but they do not mean he will willingly allow the nation to plunge into a catastrophic default or a prolonged government shutdown.

History provides a clear guide here. The rule's namesake, former Speaker Dennis Hastert, violated his own maxim on several occasions when the stakes were high enough. John Boehner regularly paid lip service to the rule, only to cast it aside when fiscal deadlines loomed, famously doing so to avert the "fiscal cliff," to pass Hurricane Sandy relief, and to end the 2013 government shutdown. A Speaker’s primary job is to protect their party's majority. Allowing a global financial crisis by defaulting on the debt ceiling because of a rigid procedural pledge would be political suicide for the GOP, and Paul Ryan is far too savvy to let that happen.

We must separate the performative aspects of the Speaker's race from the actual mechanics of governing. The Freedom Caucus holds enough votes to block Ryan’s ascension, so Ryan must offer them concessions. Promising to abide by the Hastert Rule as a general operating principle costs him nothing today. It gives the conservative base a symbolic victory and allows the party to project a facade of unity after weeks of embarrassing public chaos.

When November arrives and the Treasury runs out of borrowing authority, Ryan will do exactly what his predecessors did. He will try his hardest to find a formulation that appeases his right flank, but when that inevitably fails, he will put a bill on the floor that passes with overwhelming Democratic support and a minority of pragmatic Republicans. He will then justify breaking the rule by pointing to the extraordinary nature of the crisis.

Therefore, debating whether the House would be more functional if the rule were officially "ignored" is somewhat beside the point. In practice, on the issues that truly threaten the stability of the country, the rule is already ignored. It exists primarily as an instrument of intra-party political management, not a suicide pact.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Historical precedent shows that past Speakers, including Hastert and Boehner, frequently violated the rule when faced with high-stakes fiscal deadlines.
  2. 02Ryan's public commitment to the rule is a performative rhetorical maneuver necessary to secure the votes of the Freedom Caucus and project party unity.
  3. 03A Speaker's primary responsibility is to protect the party's majority, which would be jeopardized by the political fallout of a national default or government shutdown.
  4. 04The rule functions as a tool for intra-party management rather than a rigid procedural requirement that dictates the actual mechanics of governing during a crisis.
Diversified Claude

"The Hastert Rule is an anti-majoritarian practice that fosters legislative dysfunction, yet Paul Ryan will likely be forced to maintain it as a nominal default while selectively breaking it to avoid national catastrophe."

Let's be candid about the political reality Paul Ryan faces. He did not seek the speakership. He was drafted into it because no one else could assemble 218 Republican votes, and the Freedom Caucus extracted concessions as the price of letting him have the job. Among those concessions, reportedly, is a pledge to honor the Hastert Rule. Whether he actually intends to honor it in every case — or whether this is the kind of promise speakers make and then quietly break when reality intrudes — is the more interesting question.

I suspect the latter. Ryan is not a fool, and he watched what happened to John Boehner. Boehner adhered to the Hastert Rule most of the time and broke it perhaps a dozen times over five years — each time on a matter where the alternative was genuinely unacceptable: defaulting on the debt, denying Sandy victims aid, killing the Violence Against Women Act. Each violation enraged the right flank. The cumulative effect was Boehner's ouster. If Ryan governs the same way, he will meet the same fate.

So the real question is not whether the Hastert Rule should be ignored in some abstract sense, but whether Ryan can find a sustainable model the previous speaker could not. One possibility is structural: change House rules so that motions to vacate the chair cannot be filed by a single member, insulating the speaker from constant blackmail. Another is procedural: route must-pass legislation through bipartisan negotiations early enough that Freedom Caucus objections look like grandstanding rather than principled opposition. A third is rhetorical: make a public case, again and again, that governing requires occasional cooperation with Democrats and that voters reward results.

On the merits of the rule itself, I think it is plainly anti-majoritarian and contributes to dysfunction. A House in which 218 members cannot pass a bill that 218 members support is not operating as the Constitution envisions. The rule made some sense in the Gingrich-Hastert era when the parties were more ideologically heterogeneous and the majority could usually find its own 218. It makes much less sense now, with a Freedom Caucus willing to torpedo its own leadership over symbolic fights.

But abstract merits aside, the rule will likely persist as a default that gets broken when it must. Ryan will pay lip service to it, govern as Boehner did, and hope that his stronger ties to the conservative movement give him a longer leash. Whether that strategy works is the most important political question in Washington right now — more important, in my view, than the 2016 presidential race.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Hastert Rule is fundamentally undemocratic because it prevents the House from passing legislation supported by a true majority of its members.
  2. 02Adherence to the rule is currently driven by political blackmail from the Freedom Caucus rather than constitutional or procedural necessity.
  3. 03Strict adherence to the rule is unsustainable because it forces unacceptable outcomes like debt defaults and the denial of emergency aid.
  4. 04To survive the speakership while ignoring the rule, Ryan must implement structural changes to House rules that insulate the Speaker from being easily ousted by a small faction.
  5. 05The rule's historical utility has vanished in an era where the majority party is too ideologically fractured to reach a consensus on its own.
