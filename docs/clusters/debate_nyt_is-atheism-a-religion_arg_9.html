"Atheism is not a religion in a theological or literal sense, but it must be treated as one within legal and constitutional frameworks to protect freedom of conscience."

Whether atheism is a religion depends on why we are asking.

For theology, the answer is usually no. Atheism rejects the central claim of theistic religion. It has no object of worship and no revelation. For ordinary conversation, too, it is misleading to call atheism a religion in the same sense that Methodism, Islam or Judaism are religions.

For law and public policy, however, atheism must often be treated like religion. A public school may not favor belief over unbelief any more than it may favor Baptists over Catholics. A prisoner who is an atheist should not be told that only the devout have consciences worthy of accommodation. A city council that opens meetings with prayer cannot solve the establishment problem by saying unbelief is “not a religion” and therefore need not be respected. In a constitutional democracy, freedom of religion includes freedom from religion.

This double answer is not hypocrisy. We routinely adjust definitions to context. Buddhism complicates the claim that religion requires a creator God. Judaism complicates the idea that religion is only private belief. Quakers complicate the idea that religion requires elaborate ritual. Atheism complicates the idea that conscience must be theistic to be serious.

Still, we should resist the polemical use of the word “religion.” Some believers call atheism a religion to suggest that atheists are just as faith-based as everyone else, as if disbelief in Zeus, Thor or Yahweh required a leap equivalent to belief. That is bad reasoning. Not accepting a claim is not the same as affirming an elaborate alternative creed. If someone says there is a dragon in the garage, my doubt is not a rival dragonology.

At the same time, some atheists call religion uniquely irrational while ignoring the unproved commitments in their own lives: trust in reason, confidence in moral equality, the conviction that truth is better than comforting falsehood. Those commitments can be defended, but they are not laboratory results.

The most accurate answer is layered. Bare atheism is not a religion. Organized humanism may function as one. Constitutionally, unbelief deserves the same protection as belief. Culturally, atheists and believers alike should stop using “religion” as either a badge of honor or a term of abuse.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Theologically, atheism lacks the central tenets of religion such as an object of worship, revelation, or an elaborate creed.
  2. 02In law and public policy, atheism must be categorized with religion to ensure that the state does not favor belief over unbelief.
  3. 03Defining atheism as a religion is often a polemical tactic used to falsely equate the lack of belief in a deity with a faith-based leap.
  4. 04While bare atheism is not a religion, organized humanism can function as one, and both atheists and believers rely on unprovable moral and philosophical commitments.
