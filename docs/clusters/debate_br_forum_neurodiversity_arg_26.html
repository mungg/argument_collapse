Diversified Gemini

"To achieve true liberation, the neurodiversity movement must move beyond Western-centric political and medical frameworks and embrace a decolonial approach that dismantles global psychiatric imperialism and capitalist metrics of productivity."

In "The Future of Neurodiversity," Robert Chapman provides a sweeping, systemic critique of how the neurodiversity movement has been co-opted by the capitalist and "neuronormative" status quo. Chapman powerfully names the limits of "Liberal Neurodiversity"—its focus on white, middle-class success stories, its complicity in "extractive abandonment," and its failure to liberate multiply marginalized people. Yet, as Chapman looks toward "Neurodivergent Power" and draws inspiration from the Black Panther Party, a crucial element is missing from this revolutionary equation: a truly decolonial understanding of what it means to be "neurologically different" in a globalized world.

If we are to move beyond the limitations of Liberal Neurodiversity, we cannot simply replace a liberal, rights-based framework with a Western, Marxist-Leninist framework of the "revolutionary party." Both paradigms are rooted in Euro-American political idioms that fundamentally misunderstand the relationship between mind, community, and the legacy of global colonialism. A true future for neurodiversity must decenter the Global North and recognize that "neurodivergence" itself is a concept born out of Western psychiatry and Western economic metrics of productivity.

Chapman points out that the ADA and similar rights-based laws are not effectively enforced in the Global South, and they highlight how imperialist violence—such as the ongoing tragedy in Gaza—is a mass-disabling event. This is a vital connection. However, Chapman’s solution involves building "neurodiversity studies departments in universities" and reconstructing "the project of the party." This approach threatens to impose yet another Northern intellectual and political structure onto communities whose ways of addressing human difference have already been violently disrupted by colonialism.

Before the imposition of Western psychiatric models, many Indigenous and non-Western societies did not categorize variations in attention, social communication, or even sensory experiences through the lens of pathology—nor did they necessarily need a "neurodiversity movement" to demand rights, because their social structures were not built around the rigid, individualized productivity demanded by industrial capitalism. Community care, collective responsibility, and diverse social roles were integrated into the fabric of daily life. The imposition of colonial administration, followed by globalized capitalism, introduced the concept of the "productive individual" and, consequently, the "disordered individual" who fails to meet that metric.

Therefore, true Neurodivergent Power cannot just be about unionizing workplaces or fighting for free housing within the imperial core, as Chapman outlines. It must involve the active dismantling of global psychiatric imperialism. Organizations like the World Health Organization often export Western diagnostic categories (like ADHD or Autism) to the Global South, which frequently paves the way for the pharmaceutical industry to open new markets. This process erases localized, culturally specific understandings of mental distress and cognitive difference, replacing them with a biomedical model that demands pharmacological intervention.

A decolonial neurodiversity movement must listen to and elevate the voices of those in the Global South who are resisting this psychiatric imperialism. It means recognizing that the very terms "neurotypical" and "neurodivergent" rely on a baseline of "normality" that is inextricably linked to white, Western, capitalist modes of functioning.

Furthermore, Chapman’s reliance on the Black Panther Party as a historical parallel is illuminating but limited. While the Panthers’ anti-imperialist solidarity was groundbreaking, relying on the model of a centralized "party" to achieve liberation has historically failed to accommodate those whose forms of neurodivergence make participation in rigid, high-stakes, hierarchical political organizations impossible. The history of revolutionary parties is often a history of intense demands on executive function, emotional regulation, and conformity to a vanguardist line—environments that can be profoundly hostile to neurodivergent individuals.

Instead of a revolutionary party, we should look to models of localized, autonomous community care that flourish in the margins of the Global South and in marginalized communities in the North. We should look to mutual aid networks, healing justice frameworks, and Indigenous practices of restorative justice. These frameworks do not seek to seize state power to enforce a new, supposedly better standard of normality; rather, they seek to build resilient communities capable of holding complexity, distress, and difference without resorting to incarceration or institutionalization.

Chapman briefly mentions the intersection of neurodivergence and race, but a decolonial approach must go deeper. We must understand how the label of "defiant" or "disordered" is weaponized to justify the policing and murder of Black and Brown people globally. When a Black person experiencing a mental health crisis is killed by the police, it is not merely a failure of "reasonable adjustments"; it is the functioning of a colonial architecture designed to eliminate those who cannot be easily subdued or exploited.

Liberation for neurodivergent people will not come from rebranding our diagnoses as a new, radical political identity to be leveraged against the state. It will come from dismantling the structures that necessitate these diagnoses in the first place. It requires ending the debt mechanisms that keep the Global South impoverished, ending the resource extraction that destroys environments and bodies, and returning to models of community where human worth is not tied to economic output or cognitive conformity.

Robert Chapman has masterfully diagnosed the dead end of Liberal Neurodiversity. But to find the way forward, we must look beyond the universities and the revolutionary parties of the West. We must look to the long, ongoing histories of anti-colonial resistance and community care that have survived outside the gaze of the psychiatric establishment. Only by intertwining neurodivergent liberation with the broader struggle for decolonization can we hope to build a world where the vast, beautiful spectrum of human minds can truly flourish.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The concepts of 'neurotypical' and 'neurodivergent' are themselves products of Western psychiatric models and economic standards that pathologize those who do not fit the mold of the productive capitalist individual.
  2. 02Relying on centralized revolutionary parties or academic departments risks imposing Northern intellectual structures that are inaccessible to many neurodivergent people and disruptive to non-Western social fabrics.
  3. 03True neurodivergent power requires resisting the global export of Western diagnostic categories that erase localized, culturally specific understandings of cognitive difference to create new pharmaceutical markets.
  4. 04Liberation should be modeled on localized, autonomous community care and Indigenous practices rather than the pursuit of state power or the rebranding of medical diagnoses into political identities.
  5. 05The movement must recognize that the policing and pathologization of 'disordered' behavior are tools of a colonial architecture used to subdue and eliminate marginalized bodies globally.
