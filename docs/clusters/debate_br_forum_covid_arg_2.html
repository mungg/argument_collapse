"The postmortem of the COVID-19 pandemic must look beyond the effectiveness of non-pharmaceutical interventions to address how America's weak social safety net and lack of state capacity forced a false choice between blunt lockdowns and mass infection."

The lead essay by Stephen Macedo and Frances Lee is right to demand a reckoning, but its frame is too narrow. It asks whether lockdowns, school closures, mask mandates, contact tracing, and other non-pharmaceutical interventions were justified by evidence, and whether experts suppressed dissent. Those are important questions. But if the goal is to understand “how we fared,” we also have to ask a different one: why was the richest country in the world so incapable of protecting people without either abandoning them to infection or shutting down their lives?

The pandemic was not only a test of public health judgment. It was a test of social democracy, and the United States failed that test long before March 2020. We entered COVID-19 with millions uninsured or underinsured, no universal paid sick leave, fragile hospitals, understaffed nursing homes, overcrowded prisons, weak workplace safety enforcement, large racial health disparities, precarious low-wage employment, and schools funded through unequal local tax bases. Then we argued, bitterly, over whether to “lock down” or “reopen,” as if those were the only two choices.

They were not. The alternative to crude restrictions was not simply “let low-risk people live normally.” It was a massive program of social protection: paid leave, wage replacement, eviction prevention, safer workplaces, nursing home staffing, rapid testing, ventilation, high-quality masks for high-risk settings, health care access, and community-based support for isolation. We did some of this, unevenly and temporarily. We did not build it into a durable pandemic response.

Macedo and Lee’s most powerful point is that the burdens of pandemic policy were distributed unjustly. They are right. The laptop class could continue working; the service class could not. Public schools stayed closed in many places while better-off families purchased alternatives. Children in poor neighborhoods lost more learning. Minority-owned businesses were hit hard. Essential workers were praised as heroes while being paid as expendable. But this class critique should not stop at condemning restrictions. It should also condemn the labor market and welfare state that made every policy choice so brutal.

Take “essential workers.” The term sounded noble, but it often meant “people whose exposure we are willing to tolerate.” Grocery workers, home health aides, bus drivers, meatpacking workers, janitors, warehouse employees, delivery drivers, and nursing assistants kept the country functioning. Many lacked paid sick leave. Many lived in crowded housing. Many could not work remotely. Many had higher baseline health risks because of poverty, environmental exposure, and unequal access to care. For them, a policy of rapid reopening without strong workplace protections was not liberation. It was coercion by paycheck.

This is why some critiques of NPIs ring hollow. A restaurant server does not become free because the state allows indoor dining if she must choose between infection risk and rent. A meatpacking worker does not become free because politicians denounce lockdowns while leaving ventilation, spacing, line speed, protective equipment, and sick pay to employer discretion. Freedom during a pandemic requires material capacity to protect oneself. Without that, “personal choice” is often just risk shifted downward.

The Great Barrington Declaration identified real harms from broad restrictions and correctly emphasized COVID-19’s age gradient. But its promise of “focused protection” was underdeveloped. How exactly would the United States protect older and medically vulnerable people while encouraging widespread infection among others? Many vulnerable people are workers. Many live in multigenerational households. Many rely on caregivers who are low-wage workers moving between facilities and homes. Nursing homes were not sealed fortresses; they were porous institutions dependent on staffing, testing, infection control, and community transmission. “Protect the vulnerable” is a moral imperative, not a policy by itself.

Nor should we romanticize pre-pandemic planning documents. It is true that many plans warned against extended closures and coercive quarantines. They also assumed a level of state capacity and public health infrastructure the United States did not possess. If you cannot test quickly, trace competently, provide paid isolation, supply protective equipment, or protect nursing homes, then every available option becomes worse. The failure was not merely that officials panicked in March 2020. It was that decades of austerity, privatization, fragmented health care, and neglect left panic as one of the few tools on the shelf.

The school closure disaster illustrates this broader failure. Macedo and Lee are right that children suffered enormously and unequally. But the reason reopening became so hard was not only irrational fear or partisan tribalism. Many school buildings had poor ventilation before COVID-19. Many classrooms were overcrowded. Nurses were scarce. Teachers and staff included older and medically vulnerable people. Communities with high transmission often had families suffering job loss, illness, and bereavement. Parents did not all experience reopening as an obvious good; some feared bringing infection home to grandparents or immunocompromised relatives.

None of this justifies keeping schools remote as long as some districts did. By fall 2020, and certainly by 2021, the burden of proof should have shifted strongly toward in-person schooling, especially for younger children and students with disabilities. But a serious reckoning must ask why we did not use the emergency to make schools safer: ventilation upgrades, outdoor classrooms where possible, smaller class sizes, rapid tests, school nurses, paid leave for staff, and targeted accommodations. Instead, debates hardened into caricatures: teachers as lazy hysterics, parents as reckless, unions as villains, reopeners as murderers. Children lost while adults fought.

Macedo and Lee also criticize public health communication, and here again they are largely right. Officials should not have shaded the truth about masks to preserve supplies. They should not have overstated certainty about cloth masks. They should not have allowed the lab-leak hypothesis to become taboo. They should not have treated the public as a population to be managed rather than citizens to be persuaded. But the communication failure had another dimension: officials repeatedly issued guidance that assumed people had resources they did not have.

“Isolate for ten days” is not simple if you share a bedroom. “Work from home” is not advice for a bus driver. “Get tested” is not easy if tests are scarce, hours are limited, and transportation is unreliable. “Stay home when sick” is a fantasy without paid sick leave. “Avoid crowded housing” is not guidance; it is sociology. Public health that ignores material conditions becomes moral scolding.

The censorship question should also be understood in class terms. It was dangerous for platforms and government officials to suppress legitimate debate about NPIs, school closures, natural immunity, and possible viral origins. But the most consequential misinformation was not only viral nonsense online. It was also the reassuring fiction that America could reopen safely without empowering workers. It was the claim that vaccination alone would end the need for investments in indoor air. It was the idea that nursing home deaths were unavoidable rather than tied to staffing, infection control, ownership structures, and low-wage care work. It was the bipartisan desire to return to normal without changing the normal that made the pandemic so unequal.

What did work? Vaccines worked, especially against severe disease and death, and their development was a historic achievement. But vaccine policy also revealed inequality. Access, trust, time off, transportation, language, prior medical mistreatment, and partisan media environments all shaped uptake. Mandates became a substitute for persuasion and access in some places, while anti-mandate politics became a substitute for responsibility in others. We needed trusted community messengers, mobile clinics, paid time off, and honest communication about benefits and limits. Instead, vaccination too often became another front in the culture war.

A better pandemic review would not merely compare red and blue states on stringency indexes. It would examine meatpacking plants, prisons, nursing homes, public schools, hospitals, shelters, warehouses, and multigenerational households. It would ask how many people had paid leave. It would ask whether OSHA standards were adequate and enforced. It would ask why so little of the enormous federal spending went to ventilation, public health staffing, and health care capacity. It would ask who profited: large firms that consolidated market share, asset owners buoyed by monetary policy, contractors paid through emergency programs, fraudsters exploiting rushed relief. And it would ask why children and low-wage workers were asked to absorb costs that better-off adults could evade.

The next pandemic strategy should begin with a principle: do not make the least powerful people pay the highest price for everyone else’s safety. That principle cuts against prolonged school closures. It also cuts against laissez-faire reopening. It demands universal paid sick and family leave, wage replacement during isolation, free testing and treatment, eviction protections, emergency child care for essential workers, enforceable workplace safety standards, high-quality masks in health care and elder care, ventilation investments, and special protection for congregate settings. It demands that school closure be a last resort paired with meal delivery, devices, tutoring, disability services, and rapid reopening benchmarks. It demands that public health agencies communicate uncertainty honestly and that emergency powers be democratically supervised.

It also demands a better language than “lockdown.” The word compresses wildly different policies into one emotional category. Closing a playground, banning outdoor worship, shutting an elementary school for a year, requiring paid isolation after a positive test, limiting visitors to a nursing home during a surge, and requiring ventilation in a workplace are not the same. Some were foolish. Some were tragic but defensible. Some were necessary because we failed to build gentler alternatives. The question is not whether the state should ever act to reduce transmission. The question is whether its actions are effective, equitable, transparent, and accompanied by support.

Macedo and Lee are right that liberal democracy requires accountability. But accountability should not become a story in which experts and blue-state elites imposed costs on everyone else while skeptics simply defended common sense. Many experts erred; many skeptics minimized mass death. Many Democratic jurisdictions kept schools closed too long; many Republican leaders undermined vaccination and basic precautions. Many journalists moralized uncertainty; many alternative media figures monetized paranoia. The failures were distributed, though not equally.

The pandemic exposed a society with too little trust because it had too little solidarity. People did not trust institutions that spoke dishonestly, but they also did not trust a government that could not guarantee health care, income, housing, or safe work. They did not trust employers to protect them. They did not trust schools to be safe. They did not trust agencies that changed guidance without admitting why. Trust cannot be rebuilt by better messaging alone. It requires institutions that materially protect people.

So yes, we need a postmortem. But it should be a social postmortem, not only a scientific one. The lesson of COVID-19 is not that public health did too much or too little in the abstract. It is that America tried to manage a collective threat with weak collective institutions. When the next pandemic comes, the humane alternative to blunt restrictions will have to be built in advance. Otherwise we will repeat the same false choice: shut down society or sacrifice the vulnerable and the poor. A decent country should refuse both.
