"While full employment is a vital policy goal, achieving it requires a fundamental restructuring of trade and industrial policy to prevent global capital mobility from undermining domestic labor power and production."

Robert Pollin is correct that full employment must return as a central objective of American policy. But his essay understates the extent to which trade policy and the global organization of production have weakened the possibility of decent work in the United States. He argues that globalization itself is not the problem; the problem is the neoliberal framework that has governed globalization. That distinction is useful, but it should not become a way of minimizing the damage done by persistent trade deficits, offshoring, and the entry of hundreds of millions of low-wage workers into a system designed to maximize capital mobility while leaving labor comparatively trapped.

Pollin notes that the United States reached near-full employment in the late 1990s despite immigration and a sizable trade deficit. This is true, and it is an important warning against simplistic scapegoating. Immigrants did not cause the jobs crisis. Nor does every imported good represent a lost American job. But the late 1990s are not a sufficient answer to the trade problem. That expansion was powered by a stock-market boom, strong consumption, and rising household wealth that proved temporary. It also coincided with the early stages of China’s emergence as a manufacturing powerhouse, before the full effects of permanent normal trade relations and China’s accession to the World Trade Organization were felt. Even by 2011, communities across the Midwest and South have experienced plant closures not as an abstraction but as a civic collapse.

The issue is not nostalgia for a sealed national economy. It is bargaining power. Pollin himself emphasizes that employers can threaten to move production or source from abroad if workers demand higher wages or union representation. This “threat effect” matters even when outsourcing does not occur. If so, then a full-employment strategy must do more than invest in domestic education and clean energy; it must reduce the credibility and profitability of the threat.

A country cannot maintain a broad middle class if it treats the location of production as irrelevant. Manufacturing is not the whole economy, and it employs a smaller share of workers than it once did. But manufacturing has distinctive importance: it supports supplier networks, engineering capacity, research and development, exports, and regional economies. It offers non-college workers a path to decent wages when unions or tight labor markets are present. When factories disappear, the loss is not limited to assembly-line jobs. Tool-and-die shops, machine maintenance, trucking, local restaurants, tax bases, Little League sponsors, and community institutions all suffer. Service jobs may eventually appear, but often at lower wages and with less security.

Pollin’s clean-energy proposal could help rebuild domestic industry, but only if paired with domestic-content rules, procurement policy, and active manufacturing strategy. Otherwise we may subsidize demand for wind turbines, solar panels, batteries, and rail equipment that are substantially produced abroad. Retrofitting buildings is necessarily local; manufacturing components is not. If public dollars create clean-energy markets, those dollars should support domestic supply chains wherever consistent with law and practicality. We should not repeat the pattern in which American consumers buy the final product while production knowledge migrates elsewhere.

Trade policy must also confront currency manipulation and labor standards. It is not protectionist to object when trading partners suppress consumption, manage exchange rates to sustain export surpluses, or deny basic labor rights. Open trade can be mutually beneficial when it occurs among countries that allow wages to rise with productivity and do not base competitiveness on repression or mercantilism. But a system that asks American workers to compete directly with workers who cannot organize freely and are paid a fraction of American wages, while capital crosses borders at will, is not a level playing field. It is an institutional choice favoring mobile capital.

This does not mean blaming foreign workers. Chinese, Mexican, Vietnamese, and other workers are not the enemy. They too need higher wages, rights, and domestic development. A progressive trade policy should encourage wage growth abroad, not a race to the bottom. That means enforceable labor standards in trade agreements, not side letters. It means environmental standards that prevent dirty production from gaining an artificial advantage. It means allowing countries enough policy space to develop while preventing multinational firms from using jurisdictional arbitrage to evade responsibility everywhere.

Pollin is skeptical of export-led strategies and currency skirmishes. He is right that every country cannot export its way to full employment simultaneously. But that does not mean trade balances are irrelevant. A persistent trade deficit subtracts from demand. In boom times, private borrowing can offset it; in busts, the weakness becomes more visible. If the United States consumes more than it produces year after year, financing the gap through debt and asset sales, the pattern has consequences. The answer is not crude import suppression but a strategy to rebuild productive capacity and align exchange rates more closely with fundamentals.

Full employment also requires labor-law reform. Trade pressure is devastating partly because American workers have so little institutional power. Employers can threaten relocation during organizing drives; penalties for violating labor law are weak; subcontracting fragments responsibility. A stronger right to organize would not eliminate global competition, but it would ensure that productivity gains are shared and that workers have a voice in adjustment. Pollin invokes Sweden, where unions participated in wage restraint and labor-market policy. The United States cannot borrow that model while leaving unions too weak to bargain.

There is a risk, however, in making trade the master explanation. Many service jobs cannot be offshored. Health care, education, construction, hospitality, public safety, and much personal service work remain local. The financial crisis itself was homegrown, rooted in deregulated credit, housing speculation, and excessive leverage. A serious employment program must address demand, finance, public investment, and household debt. Trade reform is necessary but not sufficient.

Still, the politics of full employment will fail if it appears indifferent to communities that have borne the costs of globalization. Telling displaced manufacturing workers to become health technicians or solar installers may be necessary in some cases, but it is not enough. People want not only income but continuity, skill, pride, and a place for their town in the national economy. A green industrial policy could provide that, but only if it is intentionally industrial, not merely environmental.

Pollin’s moral claim is strong: full employment is indispensable to a decent society. To make it real, we need a new social contract for globalization. Public investment should create domestic production as well as domestic consumption. Trade agreements should protect workers and the environment, not just investors. Exchange-rate policy should not be left to diplomatic drift. Labor law should give workers enough power to bargain in a global economy. And communities damaged by trade should receive serious redevelopment, not token retraining.

The choice is not between autarky and neoliberal globalization. It is between a global economy governed for capital mobility and one governed for broadly shared development. Full employment in the United States will be far easier to achieve if work is not constantly undermined by a world system that treats labor costs as the first thing to cut and communities as disposable.

  3. 03Persistent trade deficits and currency manipulation by trading partners subtract from domestic demand and erode the nation's productive capacity.
