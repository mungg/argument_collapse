"Illinois should replace Iowa and New Hampshire as the first-in-the-nation primary state because it serves as a true demographic and economic microcosm of the entire United States."

If the goal of the early primary calendar is to test how candidates appeal to the American electorate, we should start the process in a state that actually looks like America. Neither Iowa nor New Hampshire fits that bill. The solution is not to create a complex national primary or a regional system, but simply to pass the first-in-the-nation baton to a true microcosm of the country: Illinois.

Demographically and economically, Illinois is the most representative state in the union. It features one of the world’s major global cities in Chicago, which boasts deeply rooted Black and Latino populations. It has vast, sprawling suburbs that reflect the moderate, middle-class anxieties of the broader American electorate. And its downstate region is heavily rural, agricultural, and culturally conservative.

When a candidate campaigns in Iowa or New Hampshire, they are speaking to a narrow slice of the electorate. But to win a primary in Illinois, a candidate must prove they can build a complex coalition. They have to articulate a vision for urban transit and criminal justice reform in the city, address public education and taxation in the collar counties, and speak to agricultural and manufacturing concerns downstate.

Furthermore, Illinois possesses multiple media markets—ranging from the expensive Chicago market to cheaper, localized markets in Peoria, Springfield, and Carbondale. This means that while a candidate with deep pockets can compete on the airwaves in Chicago, a grassroots underdog can still practice retail politics and build momentum in the central and southern parts of the state.

The current system forces candidates to spend a year catering to the whims of unrepresentative electorates, artificially shaping the policy platforms of our major parties. Moving the first primary to Illinois would ensure that the issues defining the start of the presidential race are the exact same issues that define the lives of the majority of Americans.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Illinois's diverse mix of a global city, sprawling suburbs, and rural regions accurately reflects the demographic and cultural composition of the American electorate.
  2. 02Winning a primary in Illinois requires candidates to build broad, complex coalitions across urban, suburban, and rural interests rather than catering to a narrow slice of voters.
  3. 03The state's varied media markets allow both well-funded candidates and grassroots underdogs to compete through a combination of mass media and retail politics.
  4. 04Starting the primary process in Illinois ensures that the policy issues prioritized early in the campaign align with the concerns of the majority of Americans.
