"While the lead essay correctly deconstructs the moral superiority of 'natural' reproduction, its call for a 'positive right to reproduce' overlooks the complex ethical conflicts and potential for exploitation inherent in technologically assisted reproduction."

The lead essay offers a necessary rebuke to the moralism that surrounds assisted reproduction. Women who freeze eggs, single people who pursue IVF, lesbians who use sperm banks, and trans women who preserve fertility are too often treated as though their desires require public adjudication. The essay is right to insist that “natural” reproduction is neither morally superior nor actually unassisted. Yet I worry that the essay’s concluding gesture toward a “positive right to reproduce” moves too quickly over difficult ethical terrain. A just society should support family formation far more generously than ours does. But not every reproductive desire can or should be translated into a right to technological fulfillment.

The language of rights is powerful because it identifies something that should not depend on charity, luck, or market access. The right not to be forced into pregnancy is one such right. The right not to be sterilized against one’s will is another. The right to marry, to parent one’s existing children without discrimination, and to receive medical care without humiliation are rights in this strong sense. But a right to reproduce through assisted technology is more complicated because it may require the labor, tissue, risk, or participation of others. It may involve egg donors, sperm donors, gestational carriers, embryos, physicians, insurers, taxpayers, and future children. To say that someone has a right to reproduce does not yet tell us what others may be required to provide.

This distinction matters most in cases involving surrogacy and gamete markets, which the lead essay touches only lightly. If all reproduction is assisted, we still need to ask which forms of assistance are compatible with equality. Commercial surrogacy may allow people without functional uteruses to have genetically related children. That can be profoundly meaningful. But it can also turn economically vulnerable women into reproductive workers for wealthier intended parents. Even when surrogates consent, the background conditions of consent matter. A woman choosing surrogacy because it is the best available way to pay debts or support her children is exercising agency, but agency under constraint is not the same as justice.

The same is true, though often less visibly, of egg donation. Egg retrieval is physically invasive. It involves hormones, monitoring, sedation, and risk. Young women may be recruited through advertisements emphasizing altruism, beauty, intelligence, or high compensation for desirable traits. The intended parents’ longing is real, but so is the donor’s bodily exposure. A reproductive justice framework must protect both. It cannot treat one person’s desire for a child as automatically overriding another person’s interest in not having her reproductive capacities commodified.

The lead essay might respond that the answer is not prohibition but regulation, public funding, and respect. Often that is right. But some conflicts cannot be dissolved by better access. For example, if public insurance covers IVF, should it also cover unlimited cycles? Should it cover selection for sex? Should it cover genetic selection for nonmedical traits if such selection becomes available? Should it cover posthumous reproduction using stored gametes? Should age matter? Health status? Existing children? These questions are uncomfortable because they sound like rationing, and rationing has often been used to discriminate against disabled people, queer people, single women, and poor women. But refusing to ask them does not avoid rationing. It leaves rationing to price.

A public system would need principles. One principle should be nondiscrimination: no exclusion based on sexual orientation, gender identity, marital status, disability, or moral disapproval. Another should be bodily autonomy: no person should be pressured to provide gametes, gestate, or undergo medical treatment for another’s reproductive project. A third should be child welfare, carefully defined so it does not become a cover for prejudice. Children born through assisted reproduction deserve legal security, access to medical history where possible, and protection from being treated as consumer products. A fourth should be proportionality: public resources should support reasonable opportunities to have children, not every possible genetic preference.

This last point is where I part ways with some techno-optimist feminism. The desire for a genetically related child is human and should not be mocked. But it is not the only form of kinship, and it should not become the measure of reproductive justice. The lead essay rightly criticizes people who ask queer couples, “Why don’t you just adopt?” Adoption is not easy, and it should not be presented as a moral obligation imposed on infertile or queer people. Yet the opposite error is to treat adoption and foster care as irrelevant to reproductive politics. There are children who need safe, permanent homes. There are birth families who need support so they are not separated by poverty. There are foster systems that require reform. A society that spends lavishly helping affluent people produce genetically related children while failing existing children has not achieved justice.

This is not an argument against IVF coverage. It is an argument for balance. We should fund fertility treatment, but we should also fund childcare, foster care reform, family preservation, disability services, and adoption supports that do not coerce poor women into relinquishment. We should recognize the pain of infertility without allowing that pain to eclipse the needs of children already here. We should make room for biological longing without enthroning biology.

The lead essay’s best insight—that natural reproduction is assisted by invisible structures—can guide a more cautious rights language. Instead of a broad right to reproduce, perhaps we should speak of reproductive equality and reproductive support. Equality means the state may not privilege heterosexual married reproduction as the norm. Support means society should help people form and sustain families through health care, leave, childcare, and fair access to fertility services. But support need not mean that every reproductive technology, at every age, for every purpose, must be guaranteed.

There is also an ecological and social dimension that feminists sometimes hesitate to discuss because population politics has an ugly history. Coercive population control has targeted poor women, women of color, disabled people, and people in the Global South. Any talk of limiting reproduction must be alert to that history. Still, in a world of climate change, inequality, and strained public goods, reproductive politics cannot be only about satisfying adult desire. The decision to have children is personal, but children enter a shared world. A just reproductive politics must include obligations to that world.

The lead essay asks why anyone wants children and concludes that reproductive desire is fundamentally irrational. I agree, but irrational desires can be politically organized in better and worse ways. Love is irrational too, yet we regulate marriage, custody, domestic violence, and child support. Desire does not become sovereign because it is deep. The longing for a child deserves tenderness, not automatic triumph.

What I most admire in the essay is its attention to ambivalence. S’s eggs are liberating and burdensome. B’s embryos are hope and debt. N and K’s IVF is both queer family-making and an encounter with medical erasure. cárdenas’s sperm banking is resistance and exhaustion. That ambivalence should carry into policy. We can reject naturalist shaming without embracing technological entitlement. We can expand access without denying limits. We can honor reproductive desire without making genetic parenthood the highest good.

A better reproductive strategy would begin with humility. No one should be forced into or out of parenthood. No family should be demeaned because it required assistance. No technology should be adopted simply because it promises control over contingency. No person’s body should become another’s means of self-realization without robust protection. No child should be welcomed only if optimized.

“All reproduction is assisted” is a clarifying slogan. But justice depends on the terms of assistance. Assistance can be solidarity, or it can be purchase. It can be care, or it can be extraction. Feminism’s task is not merely to multiply reproductive options. It is to ask which options build a world in which parents, nonparents, children, donors, surrogates, and caregivers can all live with dignity.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A right to assisted reproduction is distinct from negative rights like bodily autonomy because it often requires the labor, tissue, and bodily risk of third parties, such as egg donors and surrogates.
  2. 02The framework of reproductive justice must account for the background economic constraints that can turn assisted reproduction into a form of extraction from vulnerable women.
  3. 03A public system for reproductive assistance requires clear limiting principles to manage resources and prevent the commodification of children through genetic optimization.
  4. 04Feminist reproductive politics should balance the desire for genetically related children with support for existing children and alternative forms of kinship like adoption and foster care.
  5. 05Reproductive desire, while deeply personal and human, should not be treated as an absolute right that automatically overrides broader social, ecological, and ethical obligations.
