"A truly democratic AI politics must move beyond the Western-centric "realist vs. refusenik" debate to prioritize global self-determination and the decolonization of technological infrastructures."

Morozov’s most important contribution is to provincialize Silicon Valley. By returning to Cold War institutions and then to Latin American experiments in democratic computing, he reminds readers that AI’s dominant history is not the only possible history. This is especially valuable because today’s AI debate is still overwhelmingly narrated from the vantage point of the United States, with China as rival and Europe as regulator. The rest of the world appears as market, data source, labor pool, risk zone, or beneficiary. A democratic AI politics must begin by rejecting that geography.

The lead essay’s discussion of Chile’s Project Cybersyn, the Bariloche Foundation, and Oscar Varsavsky points toward a richer tradition: technological imagination under conditions of dependency. Latin American thinkers saw that computers were not neutral tools floating above world order. They arrived embedded in development theory, military pressure, corporate power, and Northern assumptions about modernization. Their response was not simply to demand access. It was to ask what development was for and who had the authority to define it.

That question is even sharper in the age of generative AI. The dominant models are trained disproportionately on data from powerful languages, wealthy societies, and highly digitized populations. English sits near the center. Cultures with extensive web presence become more legible to machines; oral traditions, low-resource languages, local archives, and communities with limited connectivity are marginalized or distorted. A model may appear universal because it can answer in many languages, but its fluency often masks asymmetry. It knows some worlds from the inside and others through scraps, stereotypes, missionary documents, tourism pages, government reports, or translated fragments.

This is not only representational injustice. It has material and political consequences. If AI systems become mediators of education, administration, health information, translation, and economic opportunity, then communities poorly represented in data will receive worse tools. Their languages may be “supported” in a superficial sense while idiom, history, humor, and legal or medical precision fail. Their knowledge may be extracted to improve systems they do not control. Their workers may perform low-paid data labeling and content moderation to make products safer for affluent users. Their governments may be sold AI systems built elsewhere, optimized for assumptions that do not fit local institutions.

Morozov’s critique of the Efficiency Lobby should therefore be extended to the global division of AI labor. The AI stack is planetary. Minerals are mined in one place, chips fabricated in another, models trained in data centers elsewhere, datasets scraped from everywhere, moderation outsourced to workers often exposed to traumatic material, and profits concentrated in a handful of firms. “Democratizing AI” cannot mean giving more people access to chatbots at the end of this chain while leaving the chain itself intact.

The Latin American examples also warn against technological dependency disguised as modernization. Many governments today are told they need national AI strategies to compete. The policy language is familiar: innovation ecosystems, talent pipelines, public-private partnerships, productivity, digital transformation. Some of this is necessary. Countries do need technical capacity. But the danger is that AI becomes another imported development package, with cloud contracts replacing older forms of dependency. A ministry adopts foreign AI services. Public data flows to private vendors. Local researchers become integrators rather than agenda setters. Public money subsidizes external platforms. The country is “included” by becoming a customer.

A decolonial democratic AI agenda would prioritize sovereignty, but not in a narrow nationalist sense. Data sovereignty, language sovereignty, compute sovereignty, and epistemic sovereignty all matter. Communities should have power over how their knowledge is digitized and used. Nations and regions should have public or cooperative compute capacity sufficient for critical needs. Universities should be funded to build models and datasets rooted in local languages and priorities. Indigenous peoples should be able to refuse extraction, set protocols for sacred or sensitive knowledge, and develop tools on their own terms. Openness is valuable, but “open data” can become a polite name for taking.

This complicates the usual enthusiasm for open-source AI. Open models can empower local adaptation, especially where proprietary services are too expensive or culturally misaligned. They can support low-resource language work and reduce dependence on U.S. platforms. But openness without resources leaves poorer communities doing unpaid localization for tools whose architectures and standards are set elsewhere. Worse, open release of scraped cultural materials may violate collective rights. A commons is not the same as a free-for-all. Some knowledge should be shared; some should be governed; some should remain outside machine training.

Morozov’s “ecological reason” is useful here because it insists that intelligence is situated. The knowledge of a farmer adapting to rainfall changes, a midwife reading embodied signs, a street vendor navigating informal regulation, a migrant translating between bureaucracies, or an elder preserving a language cannot be reduced to generic problem solving. AI tools designed for such contexts must be built with the people who inhabit them. Not “human-centered design” as consultation after the fact, but co-governance from the beginning.

What might this look like? Regional public AI centers in Africa, Latin America, South Asia, the Caribbean, and the Pacific could focus on locally defined priorities: climate adaptation, public health, legal access, agriculture, disaster response, language preservation, and education. They should be funded not as charity but as part of a more just global technology order. International institutions could support shared compute and open scientific tools without imposing vendor lock-in. Debt-constrained countries should not have to choose between basic services and digital sovereignty.

Language should be central. The loss or marginalization of languages is not merely a communication problem; it is a loss of ways of perceiving and relating. AI can help produce dictionaries, speech tools, educational materials, and translation systems for endangered and under-resourced languages. But these projects must be governed by speakers, not by extractive researchers or companies seeking “coverage.” Some communities may want broad digitization; others may set boundaries. Respecting those choices is part of democratic AI.

The same applies to public-sector deployment. A welfare chatbot in a multilingual country can expand access, or it can become a brittle gatekeeper. A predictive policing system can import racialized assumptions from elsewhere and intensify state violence. Agricultural AI can help smallholders, or it can make them dependent on proprietary platforms tied to seeds, insurance, and credit. Health AI can support rural clinics, or it can drain scarce budgets into unreliable tools. The difference lies in governance, capacity, and accountability.

Morozov is right that the problem must be posed politically before it is posed technologically. For much of the world, the political problem is not only capitalism in general or bureaucracy in general. It is unequal incorporation into a world system where technological futures are designed elsewhere. The AI we deserve cannot be one universal model made safe by Northern regulators and distributed globally. It must be many infrastructures, many languages, many governance regimes, and many rights to refuse.

The Latin American experiments failed under immense pressure, including authoritarian violence and global economic constraints. We should not romanticize them. But their unfinished lesson remains powerful: computers can help societies deliberate about alternative futures only if societies first claim the authority to have alternative futures. For AI, that means moving beyond inclusion toward self-determination. The stonefield must not be privately owned, but neither should it be mapped only by empires.
