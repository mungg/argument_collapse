Vanilla MiniMax

"While the recovery of the 'democracy-of-opportunity' tradition is a necessary intellectual project, a progressive constitutional politics carries the risk of entrenching judicial power and cannot succeed without first building the underlying political power and multiracial solidarity required to enact reform."

The lead essay's call to "make progressive politics constitutional again" offers a compelling historical recovery and a necessary challenge to liberal timidity about constitutional argument. Yet the proposal raises significant questions about strategy, institutional design, and the risks of entrenching a vision that may prove as constraining as it is liberating.

The authors are right that the postwar liberal consensus on constitutional interpretation was a strategic error. Treating constitutional meaning as the exclusive province of courts while ceding economic policy to technocrats handed conservatives a profound advantage. The democracy-of-opportunity tradition recovered here—stretching from Jacksonians through Reconstruction to the New Deal—reminds us that generations of reformers understood the Constitution as a charter for democratic self-governance against oligarchy. This is genuine intellectual history, not mythmaking.

But the essay understates the risks of constitutionalizing economic policy debates. The authors acknowledge that "constitutional confrontations over rival visions of our political and economic future are inevitable." Yet they seem more confident than they should be that a progressive constitutional politics can remain a "shield" rather than becoming a "sword." In practice, arguments about what the Constitution requires are arguments about what courts may strike down. If progressives succeed in establishing that the Constitution compels particular economic arrangements, they create precedents that future courts—perhaps less friendly than the present moment's courts would be to progressive goals—could wield against different reforms. The Lochner era offers a cautionary tale: conservative courts once read the Constitution to protect liberty of contract and strike down labor protections. Progressive constitutionalism risks creating a mirror image, in which judicial review becomes a mechanism for locking in whatever economic arrangements the current interpretive consensus favors.

The more lasting contribution of the democracy-of-opportunity tradition may be not a set of specific constitutional doctrines but a broader understanding of what constitutional democracy requires. The authors rightly note that constitutional arguments move between courts and public debate, shaping possibilities in both spheres. But this cuts both ways. If progressives succeed in making anti-oligarchy arguments central to constitutional discourse, they may also make those arguments vulnerable to being captured, diluted, or turned to unexpected purposes. The Tea Party's invocations of constitutional originalism suggest that populist constitutional argument can be weaponized in unexpected directions.

The essay's treatment of racial justice is particularly thoughtful, recovering the connection between economic redistribution and the struggle for equality that mainstream liberal discourse has often obscured. The observation that the Medicaid expansion's destruction fell disproportionately on the former Confederacy, and on Black Americans, is an important reminder that constitutional doctrine operates in a political economy shaped by centuries of racial hierarchy. Yet here too the analysis could go further. The history of Reconstruction demonstrates not only that economic transformation was necessary for racial justice, but that racial exclusion was deliberately used to prevent economic reform. White elites weaponized racial fear to fragment populist coalitions. This history suggests that even a revived democracy-of-opportunity tradition will face the same challenge: building multiracial solidarity against concentrated economic power requires more than constitutional argument.

The proposal for progressive constitutional politics faces a practical obstacle the essay acknowledges but does not fully reckon with: the current configuration of political power. The Senate's structure, the filibuster, and the composition of the Supreme Court all create veto points that constitutional argument alone cannot overcome. The authors are right that "there is no future for the liberal idea of a sharp separation between constitutional arguments in court and political arguments outside the courts." But there is also no future for constitutional politics without the political power to act on constitutional claims. The PRO Act, antitrust reform, and campaign finance innovation all require not merely compelling constitutional arguments but the votes to enact them. Constitutional argument is most powerful when it reinforces and legitimizes political mobilization, not when it substitutes for it.

Finally, the essay's confidence that the democracy-of-opportunity tradition can be recovered and deployed may underestimate how thoroughly that tradition was displaced. The postwar settlement that replaced it—combining economic growth with limited social insurance and a color-coded welfare state—was not simply imposed by elites. It reflected the limits of progressive coalition-building and the durability of racial exclusion in American politics. Recovering the tradition requires not only intellectual work but the reconstruction of political capacity. The authors are correct that this intellectual work is necessary. Whether it is sufficient is another question.
