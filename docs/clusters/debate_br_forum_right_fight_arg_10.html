"While institutional structure and federalism provide useful friction in counterterrorism, they are insufficient substitutes for a comprehensive legal framework of judicial and legislative oversight to protect civil liberties."

Daniel Richman concludes that many of the most important decisions in domestic counterterrorism are not amenable to effective judicial or legislative control and that accountability must therefore come primarily from institutional structure. I agree that structure matters. But I worry about the resignation embedded in that conclusion. If courts and legislatures are poorly positioned to review domestic intelligence, the answer should be to improve their capacity, not to rely chiefly on intergovernmental bargaining among police agencies.

The Constitution is not self-enforcing, and it is certainly not perfectly adapted to preventive intelligence. Courts often see cases too late, if at all. Standing doctrines, secrecy, informants, classified evidence, immigration proceedings, and prosecutorial discretion all limit judicial review. Legislatures may lack information and political courage. But these limitations do not make law secondary. On the contrary, they make clear legal rules more important. In a domain where much conduct remains invisible, public law supplies the baseline by which officials are trained, audited, and judged.

Richman is right that repealing a controversial Patriot Act provision would not eliminate all routes to the same information. Library records, for example, might be obtainable through grand-jury subpoenas. But that observation cuts in more than one direction. It shows not that statutory fights are futile, but that oversight must address the full range of government powers, not merely the newest or most notorious ones. If grand-jury subpoenas can be used without meaningful showing of need, then grand-jury practice deserves scrutiny. If administrative subpoenas proliferate, they deserve standards. If national-security letters are used broadly, they deserve reporting requirements and opportunities for challenge. The fact that government has multiple tools is an argument for comprehensive legal architecture.

Structural federalism can moderate zeal, but it cannot define rights. Local officials may resist immigration enforcement for pragmatic reasons today and embrace political surveillance tomorrow. Federal agencies may be more professional in one city and less so in another. Rights should not depend on the temperament of a police chief or the electoral coalition of a mayor. A Muslim student in Boise, a peace activist in Portland, and an immigrant shopkeeper in Detroit should be protected by law, not merely by local bargaining power.

The Supreme Court’s decisions this year in Hamdi and Rasul are instructive. They did not solve the legal problems of the war on terror. But they rejected the most sweeping claim of unchecked executive power. Hamdi held that a citizen detained as an enemy combatant must have a meaningful opportunity to contest the factual basis for detention before a neutral decision maker. Rasul held that federal courts have jurisdiction to hear habeas petitions from Guantánamo detainees. These decisions concern detention, not ordinary domestic intelligence. Still, they remind us that courts have a role even when national security is invoked. The executive may deserve flexibility, but not a blank check.

Domestic intelligence needs a similar legal insistence on reason-giving and review. Not every tip can be litigated. Not every surveillance decision can be disclosed. But rules can require factual predicates for investigations involving First Amendment activity. Rules can limit retention of information about lawful religious and political conduct. Rules can require minimization when information about innocent people is collected. Rules can mandate audits by inspectors general. Rules can protect whistleblowers who report abuse. Rules can provide redress when individuals are wrongly placed on watchlists or subjected to repeated government action based on erroneous information.

One of the central lessons of earlier intelligence abuses is that vague missions produce mission creep. COINTELPRO did not begin with a public statute authorizing harassment of dissenters. It grew from a culture in which “subversion” was elastic, secrecy was normal, and oversight was weak. Local police intelligence units similarly collected information because they could, because federal agencies encouraged them, and because political dissent was conflated with danger. The legal response after the Church Committee—guidelines, oversight committees, and restrictions on domestic intelligence—was imperfect, but it reflected an important principle: security agencies need rules before scandals occur.

Richman suggests that current local police incentives differ from those of the late 1960s. Police now need community trust to fight crime, so they will resist federal actions that alienate neighborhoods. Sometimes yes. But local police also face incentives to acquire federal funds, equipment, status, and access to information. A department invited into national-security work may gain prestige. Officers may prefer exciting intelligence assignments to ordinary patrol. Chiefs may fear being blamed if they decline cooperation and an attack occurs. Local political accountability may therefore push toward over-participation as well as restraint. After an attack, no mayor wants to explain that the city refused a federal request in order to preserve civil liberties.

A legal framework would help local officials resist excessive demands. It is easier for a police chief to say, “We are forbidden by law to collect information based solely on religious activity,” than to say, “We think this federal request is unwise.” Law provides cover for restraint. It also creates uniformity. Federalism can allow experimentation, but basic rights should not vary by zip code.

What should the legal framework include? First, Congress should clarify the permissible scope of domestic intelligence investigations. Investigations based substantially on speech, association, religion, or ethnicity should require articulable facts suggesting a threat of violence or support for violent activity. Ideology alone should not suffice.

Second, federal-local task forces should be governed by public memoranda of understanding. Operational details may be classified, but the legal rules applicable to deputized local officers should be known. Local governments should not be allowed to evade their own restrictions by assigning officers to federal units, and federal agencies should not use local participation to avoid federal accountability.

Third, data systems require statutory controls. The ambition to “connect the dots” encourages database expansion. But databases collect errors, rumors, and outdated information. Individuals should have mechanisms, consistent with security needs, to challenge watchlist mistakes that affect travel, employment, or repeated law-enforcement encounters. Agencies should be required to document sources, reliability, dissemination, and retention periods.

Fourth, immigration enforcement must be separated from counterterrorism except where there is a specific security nexus. Civil immigration law is so broad and technical that it can be used as a pretext against disfavored communities. If national security requires detention or investigation, the government should say so and meet appropriate standards, not rely on paperwork violations while implying terrorist suspicion.

Fifth, oversight bodies need access to information. Congressional committees, inspectors general, and where appropriate local civilian review entities must be able to examine compliance. Secrecy from the public is sometimes necessary; secrecy from all oversight is not. The executive branch cannot be the sole judge of its own restraint.

None of this denies Richman’s insight that local police relationships matter. They do. A legally constrained system still needs local knowledge. But law and federalism should reinforce each other. Local police can identify when federal tactics will damage trust; legal rules can forbid tactics that should not depend on local protest. Communities can raise concerns through city institutions; courts and legislatures can give those concerns durable form.

The danger of the post-September 11 period is not only that government will be malicious. It is that officials acting in good faith, under pressure to prevent catastrophe, will expand surveillance in ways that seem reasonable one step at a time. Each interview, each database entry, each task-force assignment, each immigration arrest may be defensible in isolation. Together they can alter the relationship between citizen and state. Structural checks may slow that process, but only law can name it and limit it.

The “right fight,” then, is not between those who trust local police and those who trust federal agencies. We should not trust power unconditionally at any level. The right fight is to build a domestic intelligence system under law: capable of preventing violence, disciplined in its methods, transparent in its rules, and answerable when it errs. Federalism may provide useful friction. Rights require more than friction; they require guarantees.
