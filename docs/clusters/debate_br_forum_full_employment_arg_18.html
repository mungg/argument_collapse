Full Employment Requires Worker Power, Not Just Spending

Robert Pollin has performed a vital service by insisting that full employment must return to the top of the American policy agenda. His proposal to shift public investment from military and fossil fuels to education and clean energy is a welcome departure from the stale orthodoxies of deficit hysteria. But Pollin’s vision has a glaring hole: it assumes that creating millions of jobs, even good ones, will automatically translate into decent wages, fair treatment, and lasting prosperity for working people. History shows otherwise. Without a massive resurgence of worker organization and collective bargaining power, a high-employment economy will either prove fleeting—snuffed out by Federal Reserve inflation hawks and financial markets—or will simply offer a proliferation of low-paying, insecure jobs that fail to lift families out of poverty. The path to full employment runs through the picket line and the union hall, not just the appropriations committee.

Pollin wisely resurrects the Swedish model as a touchstone, but he draws the wrong lesson. The key to Sweden’s success in maintaining both low unemployment and manageable inflation for three decades was not just the clever design of active labor market policies or the government’s willingness to run deficits. It was the overwhelming organizational strength of the Swedish working class, which peaked at over 80 percent union density, and a highly centralized system of wage bargaining that allowed unions to coordinate their demands in the interests of the whole class. The wage restraint that kept inflation in check was defensible only because it was exercised by a labor movement that had already won the battle for workplace democracy, a generous welfare state, and a political party that governed in its name. American unions, by contrast, represent less than 12 percent of workers overall and a mere 7 percent of the private sector. Asking them to restrain wage demands to fight inflation would be both politically impossible and an invitation to further wage decline. The working class cannot give up what it doesn’t have.

Pollin notes that average wages for non-supervisory workers have fallen 7 percent since 1972 while productivity doubled. That divergence is not a natural outcome of globalization; it is a direct result of the crushing of the American labor movement, the rise of corporate-dominated trade policy, and a financial system that rewards short-term layoffs and outsourcing. In the late 1990s, when the labor market finally tightened, workers did see real wage gains and falling poverty. But those gains were fragile and largely erased in the subsequent recession and jobless recovery. The reason is that the temporary tightness of the labor market was not backed by permanent institutional changes that could sustain worker power in the face of the next downturn. The 1990s experience actually reinforces a central concern of Kalecki’s: that business will tolerate full employment only when it is accompanied by a boom that boosts profits, and will move swiftly to restore discipline once conditions change.

What, then, would a genuine full-employment program that empowers workers look like? First, it must include a massive overhaul of labor law to make it possible for workers to form unions without employer intimidation. The Employee Free Choice Act, which would allow majority sign-up instead of contested elections, is the minimum necessary step. Without it, any new jobs in green energy or education are likely to be non-union and low-wage, as the experience of the fast-growing retail and health-care sectors demonstrates. Government spending should also be conditioned on project labor agreements and prevailing-wage standards that ensure public dollars create middle-class careers, not poverty-wage positions.

Second, we need a direct attack on the power of finance. Pollin gestures toward redirecting credit from speculation to productive investment, but he doesn’t go far enough. The Wall Street banks that caused the crisis remain too big to fail and are still siphoning resources out of the real economy through predatory lending, excessive fees, and shareholder demands for short-term profits. A financial transactions tax, strict capital controls, and the break-up of the mega-banks would give public authorities and unions more leverage over the allocation of credit and dampen the destructive boom-bust cycle that always punishes workers.

Third, we should embrace a public employment guarantee—a federally funded, locally administered jobs program that offers a living wage to all who want work. Such a program would not only directly absorb the unemployed but would also set an effective floor for wages and benefits in the private sector. If employers have to compete with a public option that offers good pay and decent conditions, they will have no choice but to raise their standards. This is a far more powerful tool for raising wages across the board than hoping that a tight labor market alone will do the job.

Pollin is correct that the fight for full employment is fundamentally a fight against neoliberalism. But neoliberalism was never just a set of ideas; it was a class project that used the state to redistribute wealth and power upward. Undoing it will require rebuilding the collective power of workers to demand not only jobs, but good jobs, a say in how the economy is run, and a guarantee that no one is left behind. The question of political will that Pollin poses at the end is really a question of labor’s will to organize. Without a fighting, democratic labor movement, his full-employment dream will be just another set of policy papers gathering dust.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Swedish model succeeded because of overwhelming union density and centralized bargaining, not just clever policy design, making it a poor comparison for the currently weakened American labor movement.
  2. 02The decoupling of wages from productivity since the 1970s is a direct result of the institutional crushing of labor power rather than inevitable economic forces like globalization.
  3. 03Tight labor markets alone provide only fragile, temporary gains for workers that are easily erased by business interests unless backed by permanent institutional changes to labor law.
  4. 04A genuine full-employment program must include a federal public employment guarantee to set a mandatory floor for wages and benefits across the private sector.
  5. 05Ending neoliberalism requires a direct attack on the power of the financial sector through measures like a financial transactions tax and the breaking up of mega-banks to prioritize the real economy over speculation.
