The Practical Logic of Moving On

Agnes Callard has offered an elegant argument to show that, from the standpoint of pure moral reasoning, anger can be a permanent, rational stance. I do not wish to quarrel with the internal coherence of her case. But I do wish to insist that pure moral reasoning is not the only kind of reasoning that matters when deciding how to live. Practical rationality, which takes into account the costs and benefits of our emotional policies, gives us powerful reasons to let go of anger even when some abstract moral reason persists. The eternal grudge may be logically defensible, but it is practically self-defeating.

Callard’s Argument for Grudges says that apology and restitution do not alter the past, so the reason for anger remains. But notice that human beings have finite emotional resources and limited time. To remain angry forever is to commit a portion of one’s mental life to the original wrong, indefinitely. Callard herself describes the “uncanny intimacy” of anger: the sufferer perpetually monitors the wrongdoer, thinks about them, complains about them. This is a costly enterprise. Even if it is fitting in some metaphysical sense, it is a poor investment of one’s finite attention. The opportunity costs of a lifelong grudge are immense: hours spent ruminating could have been spent on relationships, creative work, or simply enjoying life. The practical question is not “Do I have a reason to be angry?” but “Do I have a good enough reason to remain angry given everything else I care about?” The answer, in most cases, is no.

This is not a call to suppress anger or pretend it does not exist. It is an acknowledgment that we can choose, over time, to redirect our emotional energies. Cognitive behavioral therapy, for instance, teaches that anger can be managed by questioning the thoughts that sustain it. Even if we grant Callard’s premise that “I ought not to have been stolen from” is an eternal truth, we are not forced to dwell on it. The mind can learn to recognize that truth without feeding the hostile passions. This is not a philosophical fiction; it is a skill many people develop. They accept that a wrong occurred, seek what repair is available, and then turn their attention forward. Callard calls this “letting go,” and places it outside the bounds of rationality as she defines it. But rationality is larger than deductive coherence; it also encompasses the wise allocation of one’s internal resources.

The Argument for Revenge fares even worse under practical scrutiny. Callard claims that revenge is how we hold people accountable, a form of “teaching a lesson.” But as a pedagogical method, revenge is notably ineffective. It invites escalation, strain, and often fails to produce the intended acknowledgment. Even if it is formally “rational” by her lights, the strategy is practically ruinous. We hold people accountable more effectively through social pressure, legal mechanisms, and the withdrawal of trust—methods that do not require us to embrace the hostile logic of the offender. These forward-looking accountability practices can coexist with a decision to no longer actively stoke one’s anger. One can say, “I will not let this happen again, and I will take steps to ensure that, but I refuse to let the wrongdoer occupy my mental landscape any longer.” That is not a denial of the wrong; it is an assertion of autonomy.

Callard might reply that such pragmatic considerations are all well and good, but they do not address the underlying moral truth. She is correct that, on her terms, the moral fact of the wrong remains. But this simply reveals that there are two distinct registers of evaluation: the timeless moral ledger and the lived economy of well-being. We can grant that, sub specie aeternitatis, the theft is eternally wrong, while still concluding that, as finite creatures with a stake in our own happiness, the best response is to stop caring about that eternal wrong. This is not an evasion; it is a recognition that morality, as a guide to action, must take account of the kind of beings we are.

Callard’s story of her grandmother shows the dark side of treating the eternal moral ledger as the sole arbiter of conduct. Her grandmother’s anger, however historically warranted, led her to prohibit a friendship that could have brought a small measure of cross-generational healing. That anger was, in practical terms, a mistake. It didn’t punish the Nazis; it punished a child and isolated her granddaughter. The moral fact of the Holocaust remained, but the grandmother’s expression of anger failed doubly: it did no good and it caused further harm. A practical rationality that weighs consequences would have urged a different course—not amnesia, not approval, but a conscious limiting of anger’s scope. That is a wisdom that philosophy must acknowledge, even if it cannot be reduced to a neat deduction from eternal truths.

In short, Callard has shown that anger has a certain dark rationality. But she has not shown that this rationality should govern our lives. There are many things that are “rational” in a narrow sense that we nonetheless refrain from doing because they make us unhappy or harm those around us. The eternal grudge is one of them. We can and should choose to let go, not because the wrong is undone, but because we have better things to do with our limited time. That, too, is a form of rational agency—one that Callard’s analysis, for all its force, cannot touch.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Remaining angry indefinitely is a poor investment of finite emotional resources and attention that could be better spent on relationships, creativity, and well-being.
  2. 02Rationality encompasses more than just deductive coherence; it includes the wise and strategic allocation of one's internal resources toward a flourishing life.
  3. 03Revenge is a practically ruinous method of accountability that is less effective than forward-looking social and legal mechanisms which do not require adopting the offender's hostile logic.
  4. 04Choosing to move on is an assertion of personal autonomy that allows an individual to acknowledge a moral truth without being perpetually colonized by a wrongdoer's actions.
  5. 05A purely moralistic adherence to anger can cause further harm to innocent parties and prevent healing, as demonstrated by the negative impact of the author's grandmother's rigid resentment.
