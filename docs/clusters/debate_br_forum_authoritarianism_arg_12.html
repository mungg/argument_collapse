"Democrats must reject the strategic sacrifice of vulnerable minorities' rights in the name of moderation, as true anti-authoritarianism requires a commitment to indivisible freedom and solidarity rather than polling-driven retreat."

The moderation debate becomes most dangerous when it turns vulnerable people into liabilities. Bonica and Grumbach are right to reject the idea that Democrats can defeat authoritarianism by trimming their commitments until conservative pundits deem them respectable. But the point needs to be made more sharply: a democracy movement that bargains away the rights and dignity of trans people, immigrants, Muslims, protesters, or any other targeted minority is not defeating authoritarianism. It is learning to speak its language.

After 2024, many commentators argued that Democrats had become too identified with transgender rights, immigration leniency, racial justice activism, or campus progressivism. Some of these critiques were made in good faith. Campaigns do need to think about public opinion, and activists are not always wise strategists. But the speed with which certain pundits proposed sacrificing trans people in particular was chilling. The implied message was clear: if a small minority is unpopular enough, defending them becomes an electoral indulgence.

That logic misunderstands the authoritarian threat. Authoritarian movements consolidate power by isolating vulnerable groups and forcing opponents to choose between solidarity and safety. The target changes: migrants, queer people, racial minorities, religious minorities, teachers, journalists, judges, civil servants, “enemies within.” The purpose is not merely policy victory. It is domination through fear. If Democrats respond by distancing themselves from each newly targeted group, they ratify the method. They teach authoritarians that the coalition can be divided by sufficient cruelty.

This does not mean every policy question is simple. Democratic politics involves competing interests, institutional constraints, and public disagreement. The details of youth gender medicine, school sports, asylum processing, public safety, and protest regulation are complex. People of good will can disagree about some of them. But complexity should not be confused with cowardice. There is a bright line between careful policy and scapegoating. Too many calls for moderation cross that line while pretending to be strategic realism.

Bonica and Grumbach’s critique of popularism helps explain why. If politics is reduced to polling, then the rights of unpopular minorities are always precarious. A poll can tell us whether voters support a policy description today; it cannot tell us what obligations equal citizenship imposes. Nor can it tell us how public opinion might change if leaders made a principled case. Many rights now considered fundamental were once unpopular or controversial. Leadership matters most when the public is uncertain, misinformed, or afraid.

The authors are also right that strategic retreat often fails even on its own terms. When Democrats avoid defending trans people, Republicans do not stop attacking them as extremists. They escalate. Silence is read not as moderation but as weakness. Worse, it leaves targeted people undefended while allowing the opposition’s frame to dominate. If voters hear only lurid stories from right-wing media and evasions from Democrats, fear will grow. Persuasion requires speech.

What would principled persuasion look like? It would begin with shared values rather than jargon. On trans rights, Democrats should say: every child deserves safety and dignity; families should be able to make medical decisions with qualified doctors, not politicians staging campaigns; schools should solve practical problems with fairness and privacy; no one should be harassed, excluded, or used as a political prop. This language does not require denying that some situations are difficult. It refuses to make a tiny minority the symbol of national decline.

On immigration, Democrats should say: borders should be orderly, asylum claims should be processed quickly and fairly, employers should not be allowed to exploit undocumented labor, families should not be terrorized for political theater, and people who have built lives here deserve a path to legal status. This is not “open borders.” It is humane governance. The party’s mistake has often been to oscillate between defensive technocracy and enforcement mimicry, leaving the moral center undefended.

On racial justice and policing, Democrats should say: people have a right to be safe from crime and from state violence; communities need prevention, accountability, treatment, housing, and competent emergency response; police should not be above the law; neither fear of crime nor fear of police abuse should be dismissed. That is a stronger position than either abolitionist sloganeering or law-and-order triangulation because it speaks to real experiences rather than symbolic camps.

The lead essay’s anti-corruption frame can incorporate this rights-based politics. Authoritarian attacks on minorities are often corrupt in a broad sense: they use state power for factional gain, distract from elite impunity, and create classes of people whose rights depend on political convenience. When politicians target trans kids while taking money from billionaires, or stage border spectacles while employers profit from undocumented labor, the connection should be made explicit. Scapegoating is how corrupt elites govern.

Still, anti-corruption alone is not enough. A politics organized only around “the people versus the corrupt elite” can become majoritarian in ugly ways if it lacks a strong commitment to rights. The people can be misled into demanding repression. The majority can be mobilized against a minority. That is why democracy must be understood not only as popular rule but as equal citizenship under law. The anti-authoritarian coalition needs both populist energy and liberal safeguards.

The authors’ call for authenticity is also relevant. Voters can sense when politicians are embarrassed by their own coalition. A candidate who privately supports trans rights but publicly changes the subject whenever the issue arises will not reassure skeptics; she will demoralize supporters and appear evasive to everyone. Authenticity does not require making every issue central. It does require not sounding as though human beings are regrettable complications in a campaign plan.

Some will object that this approach ignores electoral reality. But the electoral reality is not that abandoning vulnerable groups guarantees victory. Harris did not run a campaign centered on radical social positions. Many Democrats in competitive races were cautious to the point of invisibility on controversial rights questions. Republican attacks came anyway. The fantasy that Democrats can make themselves unattackable by retreating has been tested repeatedly and failed repeatedly.

The better strategy is to choose conflicts carefully but fight them clearly. Democrats need not adopt every activist formulation. They should avoid academic language that alienates more than it explains. They should be willing to admit tradeoffs and correct policy mistakes. But when authoritarians target a minority, the democratic answer must be solidarity in a language broad enough to persuade.

This is not only moral; it is strategic. A coalition that knows its members will be defended is more likely to turn out, volunteer, donate, and trust leadership. A coalition that sees leaders panic whenever Republicans choose a new target will fracture. Young voters especially are unlikely to be inspired by a democracy message that excludes their friends or treats equality as a negotiable accessory.

Democrats should indeed stop worshiping at the altar of caution. But rejecting caution should not mean embracing performative radicalism. It should mean recovering the courage to say simple democratic truths: the law must protect everyone; politicians should not bully the vulnerable; families, doctors, teachers, and communities need practical solutions, not state-sponsored panic; and the powerful use scapegoats to hide their corruption.

Authoritarianism is not defeated by proving that one can be cruel more politely. It is defeated by building a majority that understands freedom as indivisible. If Democrats cannot defend that principle when it is hard, voters may reasonably doubt that they will defend democracy when it is harder.

  3. 03Principled persuasion using shared values—such as dignity, safety, and fairness—is more effective than either academic jargon or defensive mimicry of right-wing positions.
