"Agnes Callard’s essay correctly identifies that the 'dark' aspects of anger, such as grudges and revenge, are not irrational corruptions of morality but are logically consistent responses to the permanent fact of wrongdoing."

Agnes Callard has written an essay that many readers will want to resist not because it is obviously false, but because it is indecently well aimed. It strikes a nerve. We like to imagine that anger, at least our anger, is different from the crude rage we condemn in others. Our anger is moral clarity. Their anger is resentment. Our anger is justice. Their anger is vengeance. Our anger speaks truth. Their anger thirsts for blood. Callard’s achievement is to make this distinction look less like a moral discovery than a coping strategy.

Her central claim is not that we ought to indulge anger without restraint. It is more disturbing than that. She argues that the features of anger we most fear—its refusal to let go, its desire for retaliation—are not external corruptions of an otherwise pure moral emotion. They follow from anger’s own logic. If I wrong you, the fact of my wrongdoing never ceases to be true. If that fact gave you reason to be angry yesterday, it gives you reason to be angry tomorrow. And if my wrong consisted in making your bad my good, then revenge consists in holding me to that principle: now my bad becomes your good. This is not pretty. But ugliness is not the same as irrationality.

I find this argument largely convincing, especially as a corrective to the contemporary habit of moral hygiene. Philosophers and public commentators alike often want a protest emotion scrubbed clean of hatred. We are told to be outraged but not vindictive, forceful but not hostile, uncompromising but not punitive. There are good reasons for such advice. Societies cannot survive if every injury becomes a feud. But Callard is right that these formulas often describe not anger as human beings experience it, but anger as institutions wish it to appear at a press conference.

The desire to purify anger is visible everywhere in public life. We praise “speaking truth to power” while recoiling when the speaker seems to enjoy humiliating the powerful. We endorse “accountability” while insisting it not become “cancel culture,” though the line between the two is often just whether we sympathize with the target. We admire victims who testify with composure and distrust those whose voices shake with fury. We tell the wronged that their anger is valid, then immediately instruct them in the forms it must not take. The message is: be angry enough to prove the wrong mattered, but not so angry that you disturb the comfort of those who are ready to move on.

Callard exposes the dishonesty in this posture. If anger is a response to being wronged, then it is not surprising that it resists reconciliation. Reconciliation can repair the future, but it cannot rewrite the past. A sincere apology matters. Restitution matters. Changed conduct matters. Yet none of these makes it false that the wrongdoer did what he did. The victim who says “I forgive you, but I will never forget” is acknowledging precisely this. And often the second clause has more emotional authority than the first.

Critics will object that this makes anger endless. Yes. That is the problem. Callard’s point is not that endless anger is desirable, but that its endlessness has a rational basis. Some facts are permanently reason-giving. If my child dies because of your negligence, I do not stop having reason to grieve because years have passed, or because you apologized, or because safety protocols improved. I may have reason to live, love, work, and laugh again. But the grief is not refuted. Anger at grave wrongdoing may be similar. Other reasons may counsel restraint, but the anger itself is not exposed as a mistake merely because repair has begun.

This is why calls to “move on” are so often experienced as secondary violations. They imply that the wronged person’s continued anger is evidence of moral failure rather than evidence that the wrong remains part of reality. The powerful are especially fond of temporal laundering: yes, slavery happened, but that was long ago; yes, there was discrimination, but laws have changed; yes, abuse occurred, but the offender apologized; yes, workers were exploited, but the company has new policies. Time is made to perform the work that justice has not done. Callard’s argument for grudges refuses that trick.

Her argument for revenge is more dangerous, but also more illuminating. She says revenge is a way of making the wrongdoer live under his own principle. The thief treated my loss as his gain; I answer by treating his loss as mine. This is why revenge feels like communication. It is not only harm. It is a message: now you understand. Now the world you made has arrived at your door.

We should admit how deeply this structure informs even respectable punishment. A prison sentence tells the offender: because you violated another’s freedom, yours will be taken. A fine says: because you imposed costs, costs will be imposed on you. Public disgrace says: because you disregarded the moral standing of others, your own standing will be lowered. Liberal legal theory tries to distinguish punishment from revenge through procedure, proportionality, public authority, and forward-looking social purpose. These distinctions are important. But they do not eliminate the retaliatory core. The state often does in a cooled and bureaucratic form what the victim might do hotly and personally.

Recognizing this is not an argument for abolishing punishment tomorrow, nor for celebrating cruelty. It is an argument for moral honesty. We should stop pretending that accountability is free of aggression. Even the demand that someone “take responsibility” can contain a wish to see him brought low. The pleasure people take in a public apology that is insufficiently abject reveals as much. We may need accountability, but we should not imagine that needing it makes us innocent.

Callard’s invocation of Nietzsche, Girard, and Foucault is apt because all three saw that moral orders are built by channeling violence, not by transcending it. Nietzsche saw guilt and conscience as internalized cruelty. Girard saw vengeance and anti-violence as twins. Foucault saw humane punishment as a reorganization, not an abolition, of coercion. One need not accept their histories in full to grasp the shared insight: morality often works by authorizing what it condemns. It forbids harm, then harms the harmer. It denounces domination, then dominates the dominator. It condemns exclusion, then excludes the excluder.

The most uncomfortable part of Callard’s essay is her claim that oppression morally damages the oppressed. Many will call this victim-blaming. It need not be. She is not saying that victims are responsible for what oppression makes of them, nor that oppressors and oppressed are equally compromised. She is saying something more tragic: suffering injustice does not confer moral immunity. To be wronged is to be drawn into a relation structured by the wrong. Hatred, suspicion, bitterness, and the desire for revenge may be understandable, justified, even necessary; they are still spiritually costly.

This seems plainly true. The myth of ennobling suffering is one of our crueler consolations. Sometimes suffering deepens compassion. Sometimes it makes people hard, narrow, fearful, and punitive. Often it does both. A survivor may become exquisitely sensitive to injustice and also unable to see the innocence of someone associated with the perpetrators. Callard’s story of her grandmother and the German girl captures that double truth. The grandmother’s anger was not baseless. It was also not just to the child. There is no clean verdict because the world that produced the encounter was unclean.

The current pandemic, unfolding as we write and read, offers a smaller but revealing example. Anger is everywhere: at officials, at hoarders, at the careless, at the rich escaping to second homes, at employers who call workers essential but treat them as disposable. Much of this anger is warranted. Yet already one can see how quickly warranted anger seeks bodies on which to land. It wants culprits, symbols, humiliations. It wants not only masks and tests and wages, but someone to suffer for the fear we have suffered. If Callard’s essay feels harsh, it is because it describes a mechanism active in us right now.

What should we do, then? Callard does not recommend surrender to rage. She says we must decide how much anger to permit, knowing that some anger may be necessary and that all anger carries corruption. This is a more useful framework than the fantasy of pure indignation. We need anger as we need fire: for warmth, warning, and sometimes defense. But fire burns by burning. To use it is not to make it harmless.

The practical lesson is humility. When angry, we should distrust ourselves without dismissing ourselves. We should listen to what anger discloses about wrong, but doubt what it recommends about remedy. We should build institutions that preserve the truth of injury without leaving victims alone with the burden of vengeance. We should punish, when we must, with shame rather than pride. We should honor victims without pretending victimhood sanctifies. Above all, we should stop demanding moral innocence from human beings forced to live after injury.

Callard’s final sentence—“We can’t be good in a bad world”—may be too sweeping. But if “good” means morally untouched, then she is right. The hope available to us is not purity. It is disciplined impurity: to respond to evil without denying that the response implicates us; to fight injustice without imagining that the fight leaves no stain; to remember the dead without conscripting every living stranger into the army of our revenge. That is not innocence. It may be the only honesty we can manage.

  5. 05Acknowledging the 'dark side' of anger reveals that victims of injustice are often forced into a tragic choice between the moral corruption of vengeful anger and the moral compromise of acquiescence.
