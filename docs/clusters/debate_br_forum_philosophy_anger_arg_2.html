"While Agnes Callard’s philosophical analysis of anger as inherently corrupting is rigorous, it is politically dangerous because it risks being weaponized to discredit the legitimate grievances of oppressed groups."

Callard's essay reads to me as an extension and deepening of something that has been simmering in critical theory since Nietzsche — the idea that our most cherished moral intuitions are not what they appear to be. That the compassionate response to suffering is also, secretly, a will to power; that the prohibition on violence is also, secretly, a mandate for vengeance; that the moral sentiments are not windows onto a pure ethical realm but expressions of something rawer and less comfortable. This is powerful and important. But I want to argue that Callard's version of this insight, while philosophically rigorous, is politically dangerous in a way she does not adequately address.

The political context matters. It is April 2020, and the world is in the early months of a pandemic that is already revealing and amplifying every existing injustice. In the United States, the deaths of George Floyd, Breonna Taylor, and Ahmaud Arbery are still in the recent past or haven't yet happened, depending on the date I'm imagining, but either way, the structural conditions that produce those deaths — racialized policing, mass incarceration, economic exclusion — are firmly in place. Anger is not an abstract philosophical problem for the people caught in these systems. It is the only rational response to them.

Now enter Callard's argument. She tells us that anger is rationally tied to permanent grudges and vengeance. She tells us that victims of injustice are morally compromised by their anger, just as they would be compromised by acquiescence. She tells us, through the mouths of Nietzsche, Foucault, and Girard, that the dark side of morality is baked in. I do not dispute the philosophical content of these claims. I dispute their political effect.

There is a long and disreputable tradition of using philosophical pessimism about human moral nature to discredit the anger of the oppressed. The logic is familiar: if anger is inherently corrupting, then the angry victim is not merely wronged but morally degraded by the wrong. This gives the powerful a dual weapon — they can commit injustice, and then, when the victims respond with fury, they can cite that fury as evidence of the victims' moral deficiency. Callard explicitly resists this conclusion. She insists that the oppressor is also damaged by wronging others. But the asymmetry in her essay is telling: she devotes far more attention to the moral corruption of victims than to the moral corrosion of oppressors. And in the political arena, asymmetries of attention translate into asymmetries of power.

I want to be careful here, because Callard is not a conservative thinker making a bad argument. She is a philosopher doing careful analytical work, and her conclusion is that both parties are morally implicated. That is not a right-wing conclusion. But ideas travel beyond their contexts, and the idea that victims are "morally compromised" by their anger is one that has historically been deployed against people who had every right to be angry. It is deployed right now, in the spring of 2020, against Black Americans who are angry about police violence, against immigrants who are angry about detention camps, against healthcare workers who are angry about the government's pandemic response. The philosophical nuance of Callard's position — the insistence that this moral compromise is a tragedy, not a justification — will not survive contact with the political discourse in which it will circulate.

This does not mean Callard should have written a different essay. It means that the essay, once published, enters a world in which its implications will be taken up by people with very different agendas than hers. The appropriate response is not to retreat from honest philosophy but to be honest about the political stakes. Callard tells us at the end that we cannot climb outside our own normative skin to assess it from a neutral vantage point. True enough. But we can and must ask: Who benefits from the idea that anger is morally corrupting? In the current conjuncture, the answer is not the victims.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The essay’s focus on the moral compromise of victims creates a rhetorical asymmetry that favors the powerful over those suffering from structural injustice.
  2. 02Labeling the anger of victims as 'morally compromised' provides a tool for the powerful to shift focus from their own wrongdoing to the perceived moral deficiency of the angry victim.
  3. 03In the specific political context of 2020, Callard’s nuances are likely to be lost in a discourse that uses such ideas to delegitimize protests against racial and economic injustice.
  4. 04Philosophers must account for the political stakes of their work, specifically asking who benefits from the claim that righteous anger is a form of moral corruption.

"While Callard’s philosophical claim that anger is inherently corrupting may be abstractly true, its application to real-world victims requires a deeper sensitivity to context and history that her framework lacks."

I want to end where Callard ends: with the autobiographical moment. Hers is the essay's most vulnerable passage — the grandmother, the fence, the marching game, the forbidden friendship. It is also, I think, its most important. Not because personal narrative trumps philosophical argument, but because it reveals what the argument cannot fully carry.

Callard tells us that she was not innocent in playing soldiers with the German girl. Her anti-anti-Semitism saturated the environment of her childhood so completely that the apparent innocence of the game was, she now believes, a kind of collaboration with the structures of hostility that surrounded her. And her grandmother was not innocent either, because she was full of anger. Innocence, Callard concludes, was not a possibility.

This is a devastating conclusion, and I do not dispute it. But I want to sit with it for a moment in the way Callard's essay does not quite allow. The grandmother's anger is the emotional reality of a Holocaust survivor. It is not an abstract philosophical position; it is the felt response to the murder of almost everyone she knew. The anger is not separate from the experience of that loss; it is continuous with it, almost indistinguishable from it. To say that the grandmother should have recognized her anger as morally corrupting is to say something that is true in the abstract but cruel in the particular.

And the child — Callard herself — was not innocent either. But what does this mean for a ten-year-old girl playing a marching game across a fence? The moral corruption she describes is the ambient anti-Semitism of mid-twentieth-century Hungary, not something the child actively chose. The child absorbed a worldview; she did not construct one. Her "collaboration" was not a political act; it was the natural behavior of a child who had been raised in a world that was already structured by the adults' conflicts.

I am not suggesting Callard's conclusion is wrong. I am suggesting that its application to specific cases — to real people in real situations — is far more complicated than the essay's confident tone implies. The idea that victims are morally compromised by their anger is a general philosophical claim; its application to particular people in particular circumstances requires a sensitivity to context, history, and power that the essay's framework does not easily provide.

What I take from Callard's essay, ultimately, is not a conclusion but a question. If anger is rationally and permanently connected to grudges and vengeance, and if the morally correct response to injustice is therefore necessarily also a form of moral corruption, then what do we owe to the people we have wronged? And what do we owe to ourselves, as people who have been wronged? These are not rhetorical questions. They are practical questions, and they are urgent in April 2020, in the middle of a pandemic that is producing new forms of injustice and new targets for anger.

Callard says we cannot climb outside our own normative skin to assess it from a neutral vantage point. This is true, and important. But it does not relieve us of the obligation to judge, to act, to decide. We must find a way to hold anger that acknowledges both its moral necessity and its moral cost, without pretending that either one cancels the other. This is not a philosophical solution. It is a human one, and it is the best we have.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Labeling the anger of a Holocaust survivor as 'morally corrupting' is a cruel abstraction that ignores the fact that such anger is an inseparable part of their lived experience of loss.
  2. 02A child’s participation in a hostile social environment is a product of ambient cultural forces rather than an active moral choice or 'collaboration.'
  3. 03The essay’s confident philosophical tone fails to account for the complex power dynamics and historical realities that shape how individuals experience and express anger.
  4. 04Acknowledging the moral cost of anger does not relieve individuals of the practical obligation to judge and act within their specific circumstances.
  5. 05The tension between the moral necessity of anger and its moral cost requires a human, rather than a purely philosophical, solution.

"While Agnes Callard’s philosophical analysis of anger as inherently corrupting may be logically sound, it is practically cruel because it offers no path for survival or dignity to those suffering from systemic injustice."

Agnes Callard's essay is philosophically dazzling, but it leaves me uneasy in a way I struggle to articulate. She wants us to recognize that anger is rationally tied to grudges and revenge — that the Stoic dream of purification is a fiction. I follow her arguments closely and find them, on their own terms, largely persuasive. But I keep returning to a question she never directly asks: What does her analysis do to the people who most need to believe in something better?

Callard says that victims of injustice are "morally compromised" either way — by vengeful anger or by acquiescence. This is a devastating point, and she draws it with unflinching honesty. But there is something in the texture of her prose that betrays a certain distance. The essay is written in the cool register of someone who has already made peace with this terrible truth and is explaining it to those who haven't. I want to press on the experiential dimension she glosses over.

Consider what it means, concretely, for an oppressed person to internalize Callard's conclusion. She argues that long-term oppression morally damages the oppressed group — not just through acquiescence but through the very anger they feel in response. This is philosophically bold, but psychologically it risks becoming a mechanism of secondary harm. If you are already bearing the weight of injustice, and now you are told that your anger about that injustice is itself a form of moral corruption, what are you left with? Not peace. Not dignity. Not even the clean consolation of victimhood. Just a moral condition that Callard herself refuses to name clearly — somewhere between compromised and condemned.

I am not suggesting Callard is wrong. I am suggesting that the implications of her view land differently on different bodies. The Holocaust survivor grandmother in her own narrative is not a thought experiment; she is a real person whose moral universe was organized around justified rage against an almost unimaginable evil. To tell that woman — or anyone in a comparable position — that her anger is rationally eternal but also morally corrupting is to offer her a philosophical diagnosis without a prescription. And that is, in practice, a kind of cruelty, even if it is also, as Callard insists, the truth.

The essay acknowledges at the end that most of us are "neither willing nor able" to achieve Extreme Stoic detachment. But this concession feels like an afterthought. If Callard is right that the realistic project must be distinguished from the fantasy of purification, then the real philosophical work is not done by the arguments she offers — it is done by the practical wisdom we develop for living inside the tension she identifies. What does it mean to "permit ourselves" anger in the specific situations we actually face? She gestures toward this question but does not develop it.

What I want from Callard — and what I suspect she deliberately withholds — is an account of what to do next. Not a guide to Stoic tranquility, which she rightly dismisses as fantasy. But some honest acknowledgment that living inside the logic she describes is not merely an intellectual exercise. For millions of people on April 22, 2020 — inside prisons, inside abusive households, inside neighborhoods blighted by structural racism — the question of how to relate to anger is not academic. It is the question of how to survive.

Callard says that affective response to injustice clings to the taste of blood. She means this as a diagnosis, not a condemnation. But the distinction between a diagnosis and a condemnation is harder to maintain than she acknowledges. The way we frame these questions shapes what people feel permitted to feel, and what they feel permitted to feel shapes what they do. If the philosophical community accepts that anger is permanently and rationally tied to vengeance, that does not just sit in journals. It circulates. It informs how judges sentence people, how teachers discipline children, how nations decide who deserves sympathy and who does not.

I am not asking Callard to prettify the truth. I am asking her to sit with the discomfort of a truth that does damage to the people it describes — and to acknowledge that the damage is real, even if it is unavoidable.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Labeling the anger of the oppressed as a form of moral corruption inflicts a secondary psychological harm on victims who are already burdened by injustice.
  2. 02The essay’s detached, academic register fails to account for the lived experience of those for whom the management of anger is a matter of literal survival rather than a theoretical exercise.
  3. 03By diagnosing anger as rationally tied to vengeance without providing a practical framework for living within that tension, the analysis leaves victims in a state of moral condemnation.
  4. 04The distinction between a philosophical diagnosis and a moral condemnation is impossible to maintain in practice, as such theories influence how society justifies the punishment and discipline of marginalized groups.
  5. 05The author fails to develop the 'practical wisdom' necessary for navigating the realistic, messy application of anger in a world where 'purification' is impossible.
