"To move from abstract ideals to actual economic justice, the Occupy movement must abandon its institutional ambivalence and partner with a rebuilt, militant labor movement capable of countering corporate power."

The call to “realize our country’s principles” is stirring, but it risks becoming an empty liturgical exercise if it fails to account for the sustained, organized power necessary to turn ideals into policy. For all its moral clarity, Occupy Wall Street has been ambivalent, if not hostile, to the most effective vehicle for economic justice the twentieth century produced: the labor movement. If the disjuncture between our institutions and the ideal of shared prosperity is to be overcome, it will not be through General Assemblies alone. It will require the rebuilding of a militant, broad-based union movement that can counter corporate power in the workplace and at the ballot box.

The Boston Review’s framing implicitly acknowledges that institutions are the problem, but its language about a “tension between our principles and our practices” is too abstract. The practice that has hollowed out the middle class is straightforward: the systematic assault on collective bargaining. Since the 1970s, a corporate-led offensive has shredded private-sector union density to less than 7 percent today. The decline of unions parallels, almost exactly, the stagnation of median wages and the explosion of income inequality. This is not a coincidence. Organized labor is the only institution capable of forcing a more equitable distribution of productivity gains. It turns “meaningful participation in society” from a philosophical aspiration into a weekly reality at the negotiating table and a daily reality in the workplace.

Occupy has performed an immense service by naming the plutocratic capture of politics. But it has been surprisingly reluctant to embrace its natural partners. In many cities, unions provided crucial logistical support—food, meeting spaces, legal aid—yet the movement’s internal culture often dismissed unions as just another hierarchical, compromised institution. This is a form of political purity that the working class cannot afford. The struggle for the eight-hour day, for weekends, for pensions, was not won by spontaneous encampments. It was won by disciplined, dues-paying organizations capable of sustaining strikes, lobbying legislation, and weathering economic crackdowns over decades. The anti-union legislation now sweeping statehouses from Wisconsin to Ohio is a clear signal that capital understands exactly where its true enemy lies. The epic battle at the Wisconsin capitol earlier this year showed the power of a worker-led movement that combines occupation, electoral challenge, and union muscle. It was a model that Occupy would be wise to study.

The path forward must connect the moral fervor of the occupations to the institutional weight of the labor movement. Imagine a cross-movement alliance where general assemblies of the unemployed, students burdened by debt, and low-wage service workers join forces with teachers’, nurses’, and construction unions. Their demands could be concrete and transformative: a massive public works program to rebuild infrastructure, real financial regulation with teeth, a financial transactions tax to fund education, and above all, labor law reform that makes organizing a union as straightforward as signing a card. Such an alliance would be a modern incarnation of the social democratic compact that built the mid-century middle class. It would, in the Boston Review’s terms, bring our institutions—the labor market, the corporation, the government—back into alignment with the principle that everyone who works hard should share in the fruits of their labor. Occupy lit the match. Now it needs a sturdy furnace.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The decline of collective bargaining and union density is the direct cause of wage stagnation and the explosion of income inequality.
  2. 02Organized labor is the only institution with the structural capacity to force an equitable distribution of wealth and turn participation into a workplace reality.
  3. 03The historical victories of the working class, such as the eight-hour day, were achieved through disciplined, long-term organizations rather than spontaneous encampments.
  4. 04A successful movement requires combining the moral fervor of occupations with the institutional weight of unions to demand concrete policy changes like labor law reform and public works programs.
