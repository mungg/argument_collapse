Diversified Gemini

"The Occupy movement's primary success lies in shifting the national political discourse from austerity to economic inequality, but it must now transition from public protest to technical policy reform to achieve lasting change."

The Boston Review’s introduction to "Occupy the Future" asks whether we are entering a new moment of historical reform. Based on the events of the last few months, the answer is a cautious yes, but not for the utopian reasons some might hope. The true success of Occupy Wall Street is not that it will overthrow capitalism or instantly rewrite the regulatory code. Its triumph is far more pragmatic and essential: it has violently and effectively shifted the Overton window of American political discourse.

Consider where the national conversation was just six months ago, in the spring and summer of 2011. Washington was entirely consumed by the specter of the national debt. The narrative, driven by the Tea Party's electoral victories and acquiesced to by centrist Democrats, was one of severe austerity. We were subjected to the agonizing theater of the debt ceiling crisis, leading to the creation of the bipartisan "Supercommittee," whose sole mandate was to find trillions of dollars in budget cuts, largely aimed at social safety nets like Medicare and Social Security. The suffering of millions of unemployed Americans in the wake of the Great Recession was treated as a secondary concern, if a concern at all.

Then came Occupy. By physically taking space in Zuccotti Park and cities nationwide, and by relentlessly hammering the brilliant "1% vs. 99%" meme, the movement forced the media, the public, and ultimately politicians to change the subject. Almost overnight, the prevailing question shifted from "How do we cut the deficit?" to "Why is all the wealth going to the top?"

This is how the groundwork for the "dramatic reform" mentioned by the editors is laid. You cannot solve a problem until society collectively agrees on what the problem is. Occupy has successfully diagnosed the problem and forced it to the center of the table.

The critical question now is what happens next. As winter sets in and mayors increasingly coordinate to clear the encampments, the physical phase of Occupy is likely drawing to a close. But this should not be viewed as a defeat. Protest movements are the spark, but policy generation is the engine of change.

To bring our institutions into better alignment with our principles, the energy of Occupy must now be channeled into the less glamorous, highly technical work of policy reform. We need think tanks, sympathetic economists, and progressive politicians to take the raw demand for fairness and translate it into actionable legislation. This means aggressively supporting figures like Elizabeth Warren, who is currently building the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau and running for Senate, as she represents the institutional embodiment of the Occupy critique.

It means drafting concrete proposals for progressive tax reform, closing corporate loopholes, and strengthening financial oversight. The movement has brilliantly won the war of ideas in the public square. To occupy the future, it must now win the war of details in the halls of power. The tension between our principles and practices is ripe for resolution, but only if we transition from holding signs to writing laws.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Occupy Wall Street effectively moved the 'Overton window' by replacing the debt-ceiling austerity narrative with a focus on the wealth gap between the 1% and the 99%.
  2. 02The movement has successfully diagnosed the national problem, which is a necessary precursor to any dramatic institutional reform.
  3. 03The physical phase of the movement is ending, which should be viewed as a transition rather than a defeat.
  4. 04To realize its goals, the movement's energy must be channeled into the technical work of drafting legislation and supporting progressive political figures who can institutionalize the Occupy critique.
  5. 05Long-term success requires winning the 'war of details' in the halls of power by translating raw demands for fairness into concrete policy proposals.
