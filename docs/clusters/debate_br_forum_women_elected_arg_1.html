"To achieve true gender parity in U.S. politics, the focus must shift from individual women's ambition to structural reforms within political parties and the electoral system."

Jennifer Piscopo is right to insist that the centennial of the Nineteenth Amendment should not be treated as a victory lap. The vote was necessary, but it was never sufficient. A democracy in which women are a majority of voters but only a quarter of Congress has not solved the problem of political citizenship; it has merely moved the problem from the polling place to the candidate pipeline, the party meeting, the donor call, and the ballot.

The most important move in Piscopo’s essay is to shift the conversation from women’s “ambition” to the structure of opportunity. American politics has become comfortable with a strangely therapeutic account of women’s underrepresentation: women need to be encouraged, trained, persuaded, inspired. There is truth in this. Organizations such as EMILY’s List, Emerge, Higher Heights, She Should Run, and many others have plainly mattered. The record number of women candidates in 2018 did not appear by magic. It was built by networks of recruitment, money, and mutual support.

But the ambition story is incomplete and, at its worst, misleading. It asks why women do not step forward often enough without asking why men are so often invited, forgiven, funded, and presumed qualified. If women who win tend to have stronger credentials than men who win, then the system is not merely failing to “encourage” women. It is overvaluing ordinary men and undervaluing exceptional women. That is not a confidence gap. It is a power gap.

Piscopo’s comparative examples are especially useful because they puncture American exceptionalism. The United States often treats its constitutional arrangements as if they are the natural form of democracy rather than one historically contingent design among many. Other democracies have asked a different question: not simply whether the state refrains from excluding women, but whether democratic institutions are organized so that women actually share power. That question has produced quotas, parity laws, party rules, public financing incentives, and legal penalties for political violence against women. Not all of these measures can be imported wholesale into the United States, but the underlying principle can be: exclusion that persists after formal barriers fall is still exclusion.

The strongest near-term path in the United States is through political parties. A federal gender quota for congressional candidates would collide with our candidate-centered system, single-member districts, constitutional doctrine, and the decentralized nature of ballot access. It would also provoke a political backlash that might swallow the reform before it could be tested. But parties already regulate themselves in countless ways. They decide who receives early endorsements, donor introductions, staff support, debate access, voter files, committee assignments, and leadership opportunities. They cannot pretend to be neutral spectators.

The Democratic Party, in particular, has no excuse for weak action. It already embraces gender balance in convention delegations and internal committees. It celebrates descriptive representation rhetorically. It relies heavily on women voters, especially Black women. Yet in competitive primaries, local party actors still frequently ask women—particularly women of color—to “wait their turn.” That phrase is one of the most revealing in politics. It sounds polite, but it protects incumbency, hierarchy, and informal male entitlement. Parties could adopt rules requiring gender-balanced shortlists for open seats where party leaders make endorsements. They could require state and county committees to report the gender and racial composition of recruited candidates, endorsed candidates, and recipients of party funds. They could commit that in races without an incumbent, party resources will not flow to a candidate slate that reproduces old patterns of exclusion.

Republicans should not be let off the hook either. Women’s underrepresentation is not only a Democratic problem, even if Democratic women have recently driven most of the gains. In fact, the gender gap between the parties has itself become a representational problem. A two-party democracy in which one party elects many more women than the other does not produce parity; it produces polarization around gender equality. Republican women such as Elise Stefanik have noticed the recruitment gap in their own party and tried to address it. Conservative women voters deserve more than being represented mostly by men. If the Republican Party does not cultivate women candidates, it narrows both its talent pool and its democratic legitimacy.

Still, party rules alone will not be enough. The United States should also expand public financing. Money is not the only barrier to office, but it is a barrier that magnifies every other one. Candidates without wealthy networks spend more time dialing for dollars and less time meeting voters. Women, working-class candidates, and candidates of color are less likely to be plugged into donor circles that treat political giving as routine. Small-donor matching systems, democracy vouchers, and grants for qualifying candidates could make running for office less dependent on elite fundraising. Such reforms would not be “women-only,” but they would disproportionately help candidates who lack access to old boys’ networks.

We should also take seriously Piscopo’s point about harassment and political violence. The abuse directed at women in politics—especially women of color—is not simply incivility. It is a mechanism of exclusion. Online threats, sexualized attacks, racist harassment, and disproportionate scrutiny of appearance and family life all raise the cost of candidacy. A system that tells women to run while leaving them to absorb organized intimidation is not neutral. Legal remedies must be carefully designed to protect speech, but parties, platforms, law enforcement agencies, and news organizations can do far more than they do now. Threats should be treated as threats. Coordinated harassment should not be dismissed as the weather of public life.

The hard question is whether the United States can embrace a positive conception of political rights. Piscopo argues that we should. I agree, but I would frame the claim in institutional rather than purely philosophical terms. Americans may resist the language of group rights, quotas, or state-guaranteed candidacy. Yet they already accept that democracy requires active maintenance. We have districting rules, campaign finance rules, ballot access rules, voting rights protections, anti-discrimination laws, and public institutions designed to make political participation possible. The question is not whether government shapes representation. It already does. The question is whose representation the rules facilitate.

This is why “merit” arguments against positive action often ring hollow. No existing system simply selects the best candidates. It selects candidates through a maze of incumbency advantages, fundraising networks, media narratives, party favors, family flexibility, racial stereotypes, gender stereotypes, and sheer luck. Men who benefit from these arrangements are rarely asked to prove that their presence is the result of pure merit. Women are asked to justify reforms that would only partially correct distortions already embedded in the system.

But supporters of parity should be careful about one thing: women’s representation should not be defended primarily on the promise that women will govern better, cleaner, or more compassionately. Women are not civic disinfectant. They can be hawks or doves, reformers or machine politicians, egalitarians or reactionaries. They can disappoint. They can disagree profoundly with one another. The reason women should hold power is not that they are morally superior. It is that they are equal citizens. Any policy benefits that follow are important, but they are not the foundation of the claim.

That foundation is democratic legitimacy. A legislature that persistently overrepresents men is not merely demographically imperfect. It reflects a long history of deciding that men are the normal political actors and women are the exceptions. The point of positive action is not to install token women into an unchanged system, nor to romanticize women’s leadership, but to make political equality real.

The Nineteenth Amendment removed a formal exclusion. It did not dismantle the habits, institutions, and assumptions built around that exclusion. One hundred years later, the United States should stop acting surprised that a political system designed by and for men still produces male-dominated institutions. If we want different results, we need different rules.

"To avoid repeating the historical exclusions of the suffrage movement, any modern agenda for women's political representation in the United States must be explicitly intersectional and address the structural barriers of both patriarchy and white supremacy."

The central strength of Jennifer Piscopo’s essay is also the place where American feminists must be most careful. She argues that women’s suffrage should be understood not only as the right to vote, but as the right to elect and be elected. She draws on Latin American parity movements to show how women transformed a formal legal right into a claim for actual governing power. That is a vital intervention. But in the United States, any movement for women’s representation that does not begin with race will reproduce the exclusions that have haunted women’s politics from the start.

The Nineteenth Amendment is often narrated as “women” winning the vote in 1920. Piscopo rightly complicates this by noting that many Black, Native, Latina, and Asian American women remained excluded by law, violence, citizenship restrictions, language barriers, and Jim Crow. But the problem is deeper than delayed inclusion. The mainstream suffrage movement itself often made strategic bargains with white supremacy. White suffragists argued that enfranchising white women would preserve white political power. Some treated Black men’s voting rights as an insult to educated white women. The centennial cannot be disentangled from this history.

That history matters for today’s representational agenda. “Women” are not underrepresented in the same way. White women have had access to certain forms of social power, donor networks, media sympathy, and party legitimacy that Black women, Latinas, Native women, Asian American women, Muslim women, immigrant women, disabled women, and working-class women have not. If the reform goal is simply more women in office, the beneficiaries may be those already closest to power.

This is not a theoretical concern. American politics has often converted diversity demands into opportunities for the least disruptive representatives of excluded groups. A corporate board adds a white woman and declares gender progress. A party recruits women candidates but looks first to lawyers, prosecutors, wealthy professionals, and relatives of male politicians. A donor network celebrates “electability” while filtering out candidates who lack elite credentials. Even the rhetoric of “qualified women” can become a trap if the standard of qualification is built around institutions that excluded many women in the first place.

Piscopo’s critique of the ambition narrative is therefore essential. Women of color are not absent from politics because they lack ambition. Black women have been among the most consistent organizers, voters, and movement builders in American democracy. Latina and Native women have led fights over labor rights, education, land, immigration, and sovereignty. Asian American women have organized across language and citizenship barriers. The issue is not whether these women are politically engaged. The issue is whether parties and donors recognize their leadership as electoral leadership.

This distinction is especially clear in 2020. The Democratic Party depends heavily on Black women voters. Black women helped produce the 2017 Senate victory in Alabama. They were central to the 2018 midterms. They are organizers, strategists, church leaders, union activists, and community anchors. Yet Black women remain dramatically underrepresented in statewide and executive offices. Stacey Abrams’s 2018 gubernatorial campaign in Georgia showed both the promise of a multiracial pro-democracy coalition and the ferocity of structural barriers, including voter suppression. Ayanna Pressley’s victory in Massachusetts, Ilhan Omar’s in Minnesota, Rashida Tlaib’s in Michigan, Deb Haaland’s in New Mexico, Sharice Davids’s in Kansas, Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez’s in New York, and Lauren Underwood’s in Illinois expanded the public imagination of who can serve in Congress. But their victories were not gifts from party establishments. Many had to defeat the assumptions of those establishments.

A serious right-to-be-elected agenda in the United States must therefore be intersectional not as a slogan, but as institutional design. First, parties should collect and publish data about recruitment, endorsements, staffing, vendor contracts, and funding by gender and race together. It is not enough to say a party supported women if most of that support went to white women. It is not enough to say it supported candidates of color if women of color were told to wait. Transparency would not solve exclusion, but it would make denial harder.

Second, public financing should be central. Wealth tests are racial and gender filters. Candidates from communities with less accumulated wealth face a donor gap before they make their first call. Small-donor matching systems and democracy vouchers can help candidates who have broad community support but lack access to max-out donors. Filing fees and ballot access costs should be lowered across the board, not only for women, because poverty is not evenly distributed by gender alone. Candidate childcare expenses should be eligible campaign expenditures, as the Federal Election Commission has allowed in some cases, and that principle should be made clear and universal.

Third, harassment must be understood through both misogyny and racism. Women in politics face sexualized abuse, but women of color face abuse that is also racist, xenophobic, Islamophobic, and sometimes tied to threats against their communities. The attacks on Ilhan Omar and Rashida Tlaib, for example, are not generic sexism. They question national belonging. They cast Muslim women as enemies. Likewise, Black women candidates are often framed as angry, ungrateful, or illegitimate. If parties want women of color to run, they must build rapid response systems, legal support, digital security training, and public norms that treat threats as anti-democratic conduct rather than background noise.

Fourth, reforms should reach local offices. The pipeline metaphor can be misleading because it implies a smooth route upward, but local office does matter. School boards, city councils, county commissions, sheriffs’ offices, prosecutors’ offices, and state legislatures shape daily life and create future candidates. These arenas often receive little media scrutiny while being governed by informal networks. Women of color are frequently encouraged to organize for candidates but not recruited as candidates. Party committees, unions, civic groups, and advocacy organizations should invest in local leadership long before a congressional seat opens.

Fifth, representation must include agenda-setting power. Descriptive representation without power can become display. Women of color should not be asked merely to diversify the photograph while leadership, committee chairs, consultants, and donors remain unchanged. Congresswoman Pressley’s statement that “the people closest to the pain should be closest to the power” is more than a campaign line; it is an institutional test. Who writes the platform? Who chairs the committee? Who controls the budget? Who is considered a messenger on “universal” issues such as the economy, health care, and national security, rather than confined to identity topics?

Piscopo is right to be wary of instrumental arguments that women should be included because women will make politics kinder or cleaner. That burden falls even more heavily on women of color, who are often expected to save democracy while being denied authority within it. Black women, in particular, are praised as the “backbone” of the Democratic Party in ways that can sound admiring but function exploitatively: backbone, not head; labor, not leadership. The claim should not be that women of color will redeem American politics. The claim is that American politics has no democratic legitimacy while it marginalizes them.

What about quotas? I am not opposed to them in principle. In many countries, they have worked. In the United States, however, a gender quota that does not account for race could deepen inequality. A race-conscious quota would face severe constitutional and political obstacles, especially in a judiciary increasingly skeptical of affirmative action. But voluntary party rules could be bolder. Parties could require diverse candidate slates for open seats. They could set targets for women of color in appointments, leadership, and endorsed races. They could refuse to spend in open primaries unless their process included real outreach beyond established networks. They could treat the absence of women of color from candidate pools as evidence of institutional failure, not demographic accident.

The question “What does it take to get women elected?” must be followed by “Which women, elected by whom, to do what, with what power?” Without those questions, parity risks becoming a ceiling for some and a cover story for others. The goal should not be a Congress where half the members are women but the class composition, racial hierarchy, militarism, and donor dependence of American politics remain untouched. Nor should the goal be symbolic firsts that leave communities unrepresented once the celebration ends.

The suffrage centennial arrives amid a pandemic that has exposed the racialized and gendered structure of American life. Women are disproportionately essential workers, caregivers, and job losers. Black, Latino, and Native communities are suffering devastating health and economic harms. Decisions about paid leave, unemployment insurance, schools, evictions, prisons, policing, and health care are decisions about whose lives count. Representation is not ornamental in this context. It is about who is in the room when survival is negotiated.

Piscopo’s essay gives us the right starting point: voting is not enough. But in the United States, the right to be elected must be built as a right against both patriarchy and white supremacy. Anything less would commemorate the Nineteenth Amendment by repeating its failures.

"Achieving gender parity in U.S. politics requires moving beyond individual empowerment to an affirmative duty for institutions to dismantle structural barriers through comprehensive electoral and party reforms."

Jennifer Piscopo is right that the centennial of the Nineteenth Amendment should not be treated as a victory lap. A political system in which women can vote but remain dramatically underrepresented in office has not fulfilled the democratic promise of suffrage. Her most important move is to reject the familiar American story that women’s underrepresentation is mainly a problem of confidence, ambition, or training. The question is not why individual women fail to “lean in” hard enough. It is why parties, donors, media, electoral rules, and voters continue to make women clear higher hurdles than men.

Still, importing the language of the “right to be elected” into U.S. politics requires more than adding gender-conscious candidate policies to the existing system. The American problem is not only that women are underrepresented; it is that representation itself is filtered through institutions unusually hostile to broad inclusion. Single-member districts, winner-take-all elections, privately financed campaigns, weak parties, strong incumbency advantages, and state control over voting rules all narrow the range of people who can plausibly run and win. Gender parity efforts will be most durable if they are tied to a larger democratizing agenda: voting rights, public financing, fair districting, party accountability, and electoral systems that make representation less zero-sum.

Quotas have worked elsewhere in part because parties control candidate lists. In the United States, by contrast, primaries are candidate-centered and decentralized. Party leaders cannot simply alternate women and men on a national list, as in many proportional representation systems. That does not make positive action impossible, but it does change the target. The most promising reforms are likely to be internal party rules and incentives: gender-balanced recruitment committees, transparent endorsement procedures, parity targets for open seats, public reporting on candidate support, and guaranteed party resources for women—especially women of color—who enter competitive primaries. Parties already shape the field long before voters see a ballot. They should be judged not by how warmly they praise women candidates after they win, but by whether they help create fair contests before anyone has declared.

Money is another place where the state can act without pretending that ambition is the main barrier. Running for office in the United States often requires access to wealthy networks, flexible employment, family resources, and tolerance for financial risk. These are not evenly distributed by gender or race. Small-donor matching systems, democracy vouchers, spending limits where constitutional, and public grants for qualifying candidates would not be women-only reforms, but they would reduce the premium placed on elite networks that have historically favored men. Campaign rules could also recognize caregiving as a legitimate campaign expense. A mother of young children should not have to treat childcare as a private obstacle unrelated to public service.

Piscopo is also right to emphasize harassment and sexist treatment as political barriers, not merely unpleasant background noise. Women in public life face threats, sexualized abuse, and scrutiny of appearance and family life that men rarely experience in the same way. This is especially acute for Black women, Muslim women, immigrant women, and other women whose candidacies provoke racialized and gendered backlash. Legal remedies must be crafted carefully to protect speech and press freedom, but parties, legislatures, and platforms can do far more: enforce codes of conduct, provide security resources, sanction members who traffic in misogynistic attacks, and treat online threats as threats to democratic participation rather than as the cost of doing politics.

At the same time, the category “women” cannot do all the work. Descriptive representation matters because social hierarchies matter, but American hierarchies are never only gendered. The Nineteenth Amendment did not enfranchise all women in practice; Black women in the South still faced terror, poll taxes, literacy tests, and white primaries, while Native women and many Asian immigrant women were excluded by citizenship law. A parity politics that celebrates women’s presence without confronting racial exclusion risks reproducing the old suffrage movement’s failures. If positive action is defended as a remedy for patriarchy, it must also be defended as a remedy for white supremacy. Otherwise the beneficiaries will be disproportionately women already closest to power.

The partisan dimension is unavoidable too. The surge of women candidates in 2018 was real, but it was heavily Democratic. Republican women remain strikingly scarce in Congress, and the reasons include both party recruitment patterns and ideological resistance to gender-conscious remedies. A national conversation about women’s representation therefore cannot rely only on feminist organizations aligned with one party. If half the political system treats parity as a partisan slogan, gains will be uneven and fragile. Voluntary party rules may be easier to imagine among Democrats, but democratic legitimacy requires pressure on both parties to recruit, fund, and support women candidates.

Finally, we should ask whether winner-take-all districts themselves are part of the problem. Countries with proportional representation often elect more women, not only because of quotas but because multi-member districts allow parties and voters to imagine representation as a team rather than a single heroic individual. American reformers should take seriously proposals for ranked-choice voting and multi-member districts. These would not guarantee gender parity, but they could weaken incumbency, reduce spoiler fears, and make it easier for voters to elect delegations that reflect the public’s diversity.

The right to be elected should become part of the American democratic vocabulary. But it should not be understood narrowly as a right of individual candidacy, nor even only as a demand for more women in the same offices under the same rules. It should mean that political institutions have an affirmative duty to remove structural barriers to equal power. Women do not need more exhortations to be brave. They need parties that stop telling them to wait, campaign systems that do not punish caregiving and poverty, election rules that broaden representation, and a democracy that recognizes exclusion from office as a public injury.

"While Piscopo’s comparative analysis of international gender quotas is rhetorically powerful, it underestimates the significant institutional, constitutional, and cultural obstacles that make such reforms difficult to implement within the United States' unique political system."

Piscopo's essay performs an important act of comparative imagination, asking U.S. feminists to look outward—particularly to Latin America—for models of how to translate formal suffrage into substantive representation. The reframing of citizenship as "the right to elect and be elected" is rhetorically powerful, and the catalog of quota mechanisms is a useful corrective to American provincialism. But the essay underplays features of the U.S. constitutional order that make the prescription harder than it appears, and it too quickly dismisses the candidate-recruitment model whose limits it diagnoses.

Start with the institutional problem. Piscopo acknowledges in passing that U.S. parties do not control ballot access, then proceeds as though this were a quirk rather than the foundation of how American elections work. Latin American and European quotas operate through party lists—the law tells a party which candidates it must nominate, and voters then ratify the list. The U.S. uses single-member districts filled by primaries in which the voters, not the party, choose nominees. A statutory requirement that, say, 40 percent of nominees be women would either have to bind primary voters (almost certainly unconstitutional under the First Amendment associational reasoning of *Tashjian* and *California Democratic Party v. Jones*) or apply to general-election ballot composition in ways that cut across the geography of districts. The reforms Piscopo offers as translatable—lower filing fees, party board quotas, short-list requirements—are real, but they are precisely the small-bore measures unlikely to move the 24-percent needle she wants moved.

Second, the essay's treatment of constitutional culture is too brisk. The negative/positive rights distinction Piscopo draws is real, but the United States is not merely missing a positive-rights tradition—it has an active jurisprudence of skepticism toward race- and sex-conscious government action. *Adarand*, *Croson*, and the line of equal protection cases applying strict and intermediate scrutiny to classifications by race and sex would almost certainly capture any quota statute. Rubio-Marín's worry, which Piscopo notes but waves past, is the central one: in a constitutional order where affirmative action for African Americans hangs by a thread, it is implausible that legislatures or courts would carve out a more aggressive regime for women. Zackin's state-constitutional optimism is appealing, but the positive rights she catalogs (education, labor protections) don't classify individuals by ascriptive characteristics in the way quotas do.

Third, the dichotomy between "lean in" and "lean out" is overdrawn. The candidate-training and donor-network model has actually produced results: 2018 saw a substantial increase in women's representation, and the pipeline organizations Piscopo somewhat condescends to have built the infrastructure that made it possible. The performance premium she cites cuts two ways—yes, it indicates discrimination, but it also suggests that mobilization and recruitment work. Twenty-five percent of the Senate is dismal compared to Rwanda, but it is roughly double what it was fifteen years ago, achieved without quotas.

None of this is to defend American complacency. Piscopo is right that the framing of "women's right to be elected" as a structural political right, rather than an individual ambition problem, would deepen the conversation. She is right that other democracies have outpaced us. But the path from that diagnosis to actionable U.S. reform runs through constitutional and institutional terrain her essay treats as scenery rather than obstacle. A serious comparative agenda has to engage those obstacles directly.

The Fifteenth and Nineteenth Amendments did not "merely" remove obstacles to voting. The standard argument for descriptive representation — that legislatures should "mirror" the citizenry along socially salient lines — has at least three well-known problems that the essay does not engage. First, it has no principled stopping rule. Once we accept that women, as a socially constructed group, deserve proportional presence, we must explain why the same logic does not apply to every other socially constructed group: religious minorities, gay and lesbian citizens, disabled citizens, rural citizens, the working class, the elderly, the young, immigrants. Piscopo's nod toward "politically salient" characteristics is a real attempt at a stopping rule, but it is one that simply transfers the contested judgment to a different level. Who decides which groups are politically salient? In practice, the groups that get represented are the groups that organize most effectively. Quotas thus tend to entrench the political map of one moment as the political map of the future.

Second, descriptive representation treats group membership as politically meaningful in a way that classical liberalism explicitly rejects. The whole point of representative democracy, on a Madisonian view, is to refine and enlarge the public view by passing it through representatives chosen for judgment, not for demographic resemblance. A representative is supposed to represent constituents on the basis of interests articulated through deliberation, not on the basis of shared bodily characteristics. The slippage from "women are underrepresented" to "women cannot be represented except by women" is real, and it has consequences. It implies that a male representative cannot adequately represent his women constituents, which is empirically dubious and politically corrosive.

Third, and most importantly, the empirical claims about what descriptive representation accomplishes are weaker than Piscopo's essay suggests. Yes, there is research linking women in office to certain family-friendly policies. There is also research showing that the gender of legislators is dwarfed in explanatory power by their party affiliation. Republican women in the U.S. Congress vote like Republican men. Democratic women vote like Democratic men. If you want different policy outcomes for women, the most efficient path is to elect more Democrats, not to ensure that the Republicans you elect happen to be female. Piscopo gestures toward this complication when she warns against purely instrumental arguments, but she does not let it disturb the broader case.

I want to address one specific argument because it appears repeatedly in the quota literature: the claim that women candidates are systematically higher-quality than their male counterparts, citing Sarah Fulton and the Center for American Women in Politics. Piscopo treats this finding as evidence of discrimination. It can be read just as plausibly as evidence of selection: women who survive a more rigorous self-screening process are higher-quality because the screening is more rigorous, not because the electoral system is biased against them. Distinguishing these interpretations is hard, and the quota literature has generally not done so. If the quality premium reflects selection, then quotas — by lowering the implicit bar for entry — would lower the average quality of women officeholders, which is not the result advocates want.

There is also a normative concern that quota advocates rarely confront. A 30 or 50 percent reservation for women is, mechanically, a 70 or 50 percent ceiling for men. In a candidate field where women already have a quality advantage, the quota is asking voters to accept lower-quality male candidates over higher-quality female candidates in any race where the male is the quota-favored category. This is not a hypothetical. It happened in France after parity, where parties shuffled women into unwinnable seats and men into safe ones, while quality variation across both groups remained considerable. Quotas do not in fact guarantee that legislatures fill with the best-qualified members of each sex. They guarantee that they fill with the members of each sex whom the relevant nominating authority chooses to advance. That authority — usually a party committee — gains enormous power under a quota regime, and the historical record on party committees with enormous power is not encouraging.

Finally, Piscopo's enthusiasm for Latin American precedent should be tempered by the political context of those reforms. Argentina, Mexico, Costa Rica, and most of the other quota adopters had legislatures that were corrupt, party systems that were elite-captured, and political cultures in which constitutional change was a top-down affair conducted by political insiders. In those contexts, quotas were a relatively easy reform because the insiders deciding the question were not facing accountability mechanisms strong enough to constrain them. The United States, for all its dysfunctions, has a more decentralized, more contested, and more litigious political environment. Importing quotas here would mean fighting the reform through fifty state legislatures, the federal courts, and a deeply skeptical electorate, with no obvious constituency strong enough to win those fights.

None of this is to deny that women are underrepresented in U.S. political office. They are. Nor is it to deny that the underrepresentation matters. It does. But the path to addressing it does not run through quotas. It runs through the slower and less satisfying work of recruiting, training, and funding women candidates within the existing system — the work organizations like EMILY's List and She Should Run are already doing, and doing with measurable results. The 2018 cycle saw the largest single-cycle gain in women's congressional representation in American history. That is not the limit of what voluntary mobilization can accomplish. It is closer to the beginning.

Piscopo's framework asks us to remake American constitutional culture so that quotas become possible. The simpler path is to keep doing the work that has, over the past three decades, doubled women's congressional presence, and to trust that the same trajectory, accelerated by social change, will continue. That is not a counsel of complacency. It is a recognition that the American answer to the question Piscopo asks is unlikely to be the Argentine one, and need not be.

Race Is Not a Footnote

Jennifer Piscopo's essay arrives at a moment when American politics is being rearranged, in real time, by a mass mobilization against police violence that has put racial justice at the center of national debate in a way it has not been for decades. It is striking, then, that her account of women's representation treats race as a complication to be acknowledged near the end rather than as a feature central to the whole story. Piscopo's narrative arc goes like this: in the nineteenth century, Latin American constitutions defined citizenship in terms of "the right to elect and be elected"; twentieth-century suffragists in those countries used this language to demand both vote and office; late-twentieth-century activists revived the framework to push for gender quotas, with great success. The American story is a parallel one in which the same conceptual move was, for historical reasons, not made. Piscopo's prescription is to make it now.

The trouble is that the parallel is partial. Latin American gender-quota campaigns succeeded in part by treating "women" as a unified political category whose claims could be advanced without reference to the racial, class, and indigenous hierarchies that also structure those societies. Piscopo cites Ruth Rubio-Marín making essentially this observation, and acknowledges that the rhetorical separation of gender from race "worked elsewhere" but is harder in the United States, where "white supremacy and patriarchy are intertwined." She then moves on. I want to stay with this point, because I think it is the crux of the American question.

When American women first organized for suffrage in the nineteenth century, the movement was already racially fractured. The white-led wing, exemplified by Stanton and Anthony, made strategic decisions to exclude Black women and Black suffrage demands, sometimes explicitly. When the Nineteenth Amendment passed in 1920, southern Black women remained effectively disenfranchised through poll taxes, literacy tests, and white terror for another four and a half decades. Native women had to wait until 1924 for citizenship and longer for the franchise in many states. Asian American women's access to citizenship was constrained by immigration and naturalization law until the 1940s and beyond. The 1920 centennial is, accurately understood, the centennial of white women's enfranchisement; for many other American women, the more meaningful date is 1965, or even later.

This history matters for the quota debate because it determines which "women" American advocates are talking about when they talk about women's representation. The 2018 wave of Democratic women elected to Congress was a genuine moment of change, and it was disproportionately a moment of change for women of color. Ayanna Pressley, Ilhan Omar, Rashida Tlaib, Sharice Davids, Deb Haaland — these are the names that mattered. The "wave" was not a wave of generic women; it was a wave of specifically situated women whose campaigns were rooted in racial-justice and immigration-justice constituencies. The structural exclusions they were overcoming were not only gendered but racialized. The local party leaders Piscopo mentions, who told women aspirants to wait their turn, were not just sexist. They were operating within party machines whose racial assumptions about who can win are deeply entrenched.

This suggests that any positive-action framework for the United States must be intersectional from the start, not as an afterthought. A gender quota that does not specify racial composition would, in the U.S. context, likely produce a Congress whose women members were disproportionately white, because white women have greater access to the donor networks, party connections, and educational credentials that current candidate-development infrastructure relies on. We have evidence for this. State legislatures with the highest percentages of women are not, on average, the state legislatures with the highest percentages of women of color. The two dimensions of representation are partially independent, and a reform aimed at one without attention to the other can entrench inequality on the other.

There is a deeper point here too. The reason positive-action frameworks have struggled in the United States is not just that the constitutional tradition is built on negative rights. It is that the most visible American experiment with positive action — race-conscious affirmative action — has been under sustained legal and political attack for forty years, has been narrowed by the Supreme Court in case after case, and is now hanging by a thread. American jurisprudence has moved steadily toward a "colorblind" reading of the Fourteenth Amendment that treats any race-conscious remedy as constitutionally suspect. To then introduce a sex-conscious remedy — a gender quota — into this legal environment is to invite a doctrinal collision. Opponents will argue, with some legal force, that if race-conscious remedies require strict scrutiny and narrow tailoring, sex-conscious remedies require at least intermediate scrutiny, and that a sweeping quota law fails both. The Latin American countries Piscopo discusses do not face this jurisprudential challenge in the same form, because their constitutional traditions accept "compensatory inequalities" as a legitimate framework. American constitutional law does not, except in narrow remedial contexts.

This means that any serious American positive-action campaign for women's representation must either (a) limit its ambitions to measures that survive existing constitutional doctrine — voluntary party rules, public-financing incentives, recruitment programs — or (b) commit to the much larger project of changing American constitutional doctrine to accept positive group rights. The latter would require revisiting the trajectory of affirmative-action jurisprudence and, in effect, winning the political and legal fight for race-conscious remedies as well. You cannot, in the American context, secure sex-conscious positive action while race-conscious positive action is being dismantled. The two stand or fall together.

This is, in some ways, a forbidding prospect. But it is also clarifying. It tells us that the path to gender quotas in the United States runs through, not around, the politics of racial justice. And it tells us that white-led feminist organizations whose strategic instinct has been to keep the gender question separate from the race question — to make the quota debate easier by not entangling it with affirmative action — are misreading the political terrain.

The encouraging news is that the women-of-color-led feminist organizing of the past decade has already begun to chart this integrated path. Higher Heights, Latino Victory Project, EMILY's List's increasing attention to women of color candidates, the Black Women's Roundtable's representation work — these organizations are doing the kind of intersectional candidate development that an honest American positive-action framework requires. They have built the infrastructure that produced the 2018 wave and that will produce future waves. If Piscopo's reframing of representation as a political right is to take root in the United States, it will take root through their work, not through an imported Latin American template.

I want to close by underlining what I take from Piscopo's essay, even as I push back. She is right that the American obsession with women's individual ambition is inadequate. She is right that representation is properly understood as a political right rather than a personal achievement. She is right that the centennial moment invites a deeper conversation than the one we are having. I just want that conversation to begin from the recognition that American women have never been a single political category, that the suffrage history she narrates has been racially uneven from the start, and that the representation we should be fighting for is one that takes that unevenness seriously. Otherwise, we will end up with a more demographically diverse version of the same exclusion.

The U.S. Constitution’s Equal Protection Clause is interpreted through a lens of individual, not group, rights. Does this mean American women are doomed to inch forward at a glacial pace, entirely dependent on organizations like EMILY’s List to fight the systemic headwinds? Not necessarily. If we cannot legally mandate gender parity through positive rights, we must instead dismantle the underlying electoral mechanics that passively enforce male dominance. The U.S. relies on a first-past-the-post voting system. In a single-member district, political parties and donors are heavily incentivized to coalesce around the candidate they perceive as the most "electable" or "safe." Despite data showing that women actually possess a "performance premium," the lingering biases of party gatekeepers—often the local boys' clubs Piscopo mentions—mean the "safe" choice is traditionally a white man. Women, and especially women of color, are routinely told to "wait their turn" because gatekeepers fear splitting the vote or backing a candidate who deviates from the historical norm.

Transitioning to multimember districts combined with Ranked-Choice Voting (RCV) would fundamentally alter this calculus, achieving the effects of a quota system without triggering its constitutional tripwires. In a multimember district where three or five representatives are elected simultaneously, political parties are heavily incentivized to field a diverse slate of candidates to appeal to the broadest possible coalition of voters. A party running a slate of five identical men would be politically punished by the electorate. Furthermore, RCV eliminates the "spoiler" effect. Women can run without being accused of dividing the base, and voters can rank their true preferences without fear of wasting their vote. Early data from jurisdictions that have adopted RCV, such as Maine and various municipal governments across the country, show it consistently results in more women and people of color running and winning.

Beyond how we cast ballots, we must also reform how we fund campaigns. Piscopo is entirely correct that the burden of raising money acts as a discriminatory barrier. The U.S. candidate-centered system requires individuals to build their own fundraising infrastructure from scratch. Implementing robust public campaign financing—such as democracy vouchers or small-dollar matching funds—would dilute the power of wealthy, predominantly male donor networks. Crucially, these reforms align perfectly with the American "negative rights" framework: rather than mandating an outcome, they remove the structural and financial barriers that impede women's right to candidacy.

Piscopo provides a vital service by reframing women’s political underrepresentation as a violation of democratic justice rather than a mere failure of female ambition. She proves that a democracy cannot claim to be fully representative when half its population is systemically locked out of the halls of power. By implementing ranked-choice voting, multimember districts, and public financing, the United States can create an electoral environment where parity is not an artificial mandate, but the natural outcome of a truly representative system.

In many countries that adopted gender quotas in the 1990s and 2000s, the primary beneficiaries were white, elite, urban women. Indigenous and Afro-descendant women remained severely underrepresented. The structural barriers of race and class often proved stronger than the corrective of a gender quota. Without specific, intersectional safeguards, policies designed simply to boost "women" tend to default to those women who already possess social and economic capital—namely, white women of means.

In the U.S. context, the assumption that gender parity will universally benefit all women ignores the deep political divides among American women themselves. Piscopo cites the argument that "women know women’s policy interests better than men," leaning into the idea of substantive representation. But what are "women's policy interests"? In recent national elections, a majority of white women voted for political platforms that explicitly sought to dismantle reproductive rights, shred the social safety net, and implement harsh immigration policies—policies that disproportionately harm Black, Latina, and Indigenous women. If a U.S. gender parity initiative simply replaces conservative white men with conservative white women, the sheer demographic presence of "women" will do little to advance substantive justice for the most marginalized.

Furthermore, Piscopo’s diagnosis of the structural barriers holding women back focuses primarily on party boys' clubs, the performance premium, and the absence of positive rights. Yet, for women of color, the barriers begin long before candidate recruitment. They are rooted in systemic voter suppression. In the wake of the 2013 *Shelby County v. Holder* Supreme Court decision, which gutted the Voting Rights Act, states across the South and Midwest have closed polling places, purged voter rolls, and instituted strict voter ID laws. When Black and brown communities are systematically disenfranchised, the pipeline of local leadership—the very pool from which state and federal candidates are drawn—is choked off.

We cannot discuss "descriptive representation" for women as a whole without recognizing that women of color are fighting a two-front war against both sexism and racism within the electoral system. Black women, for instance, are the most reliable voting bloc for the Democratic Party, yet they are often marginalized when it comes to national institutional support and campaign finance. The success of leaders like Stacey Abrams, who built a massive grassroots movement in Georgia, was achieved not by leaning on the abstract concept of gender parity, but by directly confronting structural racism and voter suppression.

If we are to embrace the "positive action" Piscopo advocates, it must be deeply intersectional. Legal scholar Ruth Rubio-Marín, whom Piscopo quotes, is absolutely correct: in the U.S., any discussion of positive action for women requires a simultaneous discussion of positive action for Black Americans and other marginalized groups. Piscopo views this intertwined history as a hurdle that makes positive rights "unlikely" to succeed. But rather than retreating from this complexity, advocates for democratic reform must lean into it.

Instead of a broad call for gender parity, which risks elevating white women while leaving women of color behind, the focus should be on multidimensional structural reforms. We need a new Voting Rights Act to ensure communities of color can actually vote for the candidates who represent them. We need public financing of elections to break the monopoly of wealthy, predominantly white donor networks. And if political parties are to adopt internal mandates—as Piscopo suggests they should—these mandates cannot merely require that 50 percent of candidates be women; they must ensure that candidate slates reflect the racial and economic diversity of the electorate.

Piscopo is right that the U.S. needs to move beyond the individualistic, "lean in" narrative that places the burden entirely on women’s ambition. But as we look to structural solutions, we must ensure we are not simply building a more gender-equitable patriarchy for white elites. True representation requires dismantling all the interlocking systems of oppression that keep the halls of power looking nothing like the people they govern.

"While gender quotas are a compelling tool for increasing representation, any proposal for the United States must explicitly address intersectionality to avoid repeating the historical exclusions of the suffrage movement that prioritized white women over women of color."

Piscopo's essay makes a compelling case for gender quotas, but it perpetuates a significant blind spot that limits its analysis: the tendency to discuss "women" as a monolithic category while largely ignoring how gender intersects with race, class, and other dimensions of identity. This omission matters because the quota models that have succeeded globally often reflect the specific political and demographic contexts of the countries implementing them, and transplanting those models without attention to American racial politics risks reproducing the exclusions of the suffrage movement itself.

The historical parallel is instructive. Piscopo discusses how Black women organized to run for office immediately after the Nineteenth Amendment, but she does not adequately explore how the suffrage movement's racial politics limited its reach. Susan B. Anthony and Elizabeth Cady Stanton courted Southern suffragists by excluding Black women from their vision of female citizenship. The resulting amendment enfranchised white women while leaving Black women, men, and communities vulnerable to Jim Crow restrictions for another half-century. A quota system that focuses exclusively on gender without attention to race would repeat this error.

Today, women of color—particularly Black women—play a disproportionate role in electoral politics, serving as the backbone of Democratic Party mobilization while remaining underrepresented among elected officials. A pure gender quota might increase the number of white Republican women in office without meaningfully advancing the representation of women of color. The data support this concern: studies of Latin American quota systems show they often benefit elite and light-skinned women more than poor and indigenous women. Argentina's 1991 quota law increased women's representation substantially, but research by Michelle Everett and others documents how party machines often nominated women from politically connected families while excluding grassroots organizers.

Piscopo herself acknowledges that "patriarchy created a sexual division of labor that relegated women to domestic and reproductive labor," but she does not fully explore how that division intersects with racial capitalism. In the United States, the gendered division of labor has always been racialized: Black women's labor was extracted for domestic work and caregiving without compensation or political incorporation, while white women's domestic labor was protected as bourgeois respectability. Any serious theory of political rights must grapple with this intertwined history rather than treating gender and race as separate problems with separate solutions.

Ruth Rubio-Marín's insight—that gender parity arguments work elsewhere by separating women's exclusion from racial exclusion, but that American racism and patriarchy are intertwined—deserves fuller development. In practice, what would "simultaneous discussion" of positive action for women and for Black Americans look like? Would it mean that any gender quota must include explicit provisions for racial diversity among women? Would it require extending quotas to Black men alongside Black women? Would it mean rejecting quotas entirely in favor of broader structural reforms targeting both racial and gender inequality simultaneously?

I do not have definitive answers, but the questions themselves demonstrate that Piscopo's framework requires substantial revision for American application. The international evidence she cites comes predominantly from countries without America's particular history of slavery, segregation, and ongoing racial hierarchy. Rwanda's remarkable progress in women's representation followed genocide and deliberate national reconstruction; its lessons may not translate directly to the United States. Mexico's parity laws emerged from specific post-revolutionary political contexts; Tunisia's from Arab Spring reforms. Each case requires attention to local context, and the American context includes racial dynamics that cannot be bracketed.

This does not mean quotas are impossible or undesirable in the United States. It means that any serious proposal must address race explicitly. Perhaps this means advocating for Black women-focused candidate recruitment programs alongside gender-neutral race-conscious quotas. Perhaps it means prioritizing proportional representation systems that tend to elect more women of color than single-member districts. Perhaps it means focusing on the structural conditions—childcare, criminal justice, economic security—that keep women, especially women of color, from entering politics. The goal should be substantive representation for all women, not just numerical parity among the already-powerful.

"The United States must shift from a negative to a positive conception of political rights by adopting structural reforms like gender quotas to achieve true legislative representation for women."

Jennifer Piscopo's essay arrives at exactly the right moment. The centennial of the Nineteenth Amendment should prompt not just celebration but honest reckoning: the United States ranks alongside Afghanistan in women's legislative representation, a fact that should embarrass any citizen who believes their country leads the world in democratic values. Piscopo's central insight—that the United States has conceptualized political rights as negative rather than positive, focusing on what government cannot restrict rather than what it must actively guarantee—offers a framework for understanding why the nation has lagged so dramatically behind countries in Latin America and Europe.

The evidence from gender quota adoption worldwide is compelling. Of the fifty countries achieving thirty percent or more women in their lower or single legislative chamber by 2019, twenty-nine employed gender quota laws. This is not coincidence but causation. The voluntary, individualistic approach championed by organizations like EMILY's List and She Should Run has merit but cannot substitute for structural change. As Piscopo correctly notes, these organizations respond to "the individual ethos of U.S. elections, helping aspirant women pull themselves up by their electoral bootstraps." But when boots are chained to the ground, bootstrapping accomplishes nothing.

The philosophical arguments for gender parity are more powerful than opponents acknowledge. Critics invoke meritocracy as though politics operated as a pure merit-based competition, but no such competition exists. Incumbency advantages, name recognition, family connections, and party establishment backing all distort candidate emergence. The performance premium documented by Sarah Fulton—where women who win demonstrate higher quality across eleven metrics than male winners—reveals not meritocracy but selection bias. Women must be exceptional to run; men can be ordinary.

Piscopo's observation that the "right to be elected" language in Ibero-American constitutions ultimately served as "a trumpet for equality" rather than a door-slam should inspire American advocates. Constitutional language is not fixed in meaning. The Fourteenth Amendment's guarantee of "equal protection" once excluded women and African Americans; it now protects both. Courts in Costa Rica, Spain, and Indonesia have upheld quota laws by recognizing that substantive equality sometimes requires unequal treatment of previously favored groups.

The argument that gender quotas face unique obstacles in the United States due to their association with racial affirmative action deserves attention, but Ruth Rubio-Marín's analysis points toward a solution rather than surrender. By focusing specifically on patriarchy rather than conflating gender and race, parity advocates can build coalitions with constituencies historically skeptical of positive action on racial grounds. The Democratic Party's own 50-50 gender balance requirements for convention delegates and committee positions demonstrate that American political institutions can adopt such measures voluntarily.

What practical steps might jumpstart this process? First, national legislation requiring parity for federal campaigns—perhaps by conditioning public financing on gender balance among major party candidates. Second, state-level reforms mandating that major parties provide gender-balanced slates for statewide offices. Third, judicial appointments specifically calibrated to ensure benches that understand substantive equality. Fourth, constitutional amendments in willing states establishing positive rights to political participation.

The resistance will be fierce. Opponents will invoke constitutional originalism, individual freedom, and the specter of government overreach. But the global evidence demonstrates these fears are overblown. Countries that adopted parity did not become totalitarian; they became more representative. Courts consistently upheld these measures when framed around substantive rather than formal equality. The women of Rwanda, who now occupy more than sixty percent of legislative seats, have not undermined their democracy but strengthened it.

Piscopo concludes that "the impossible only becomes possible if someone tries." This is correct, but we should note what happens when others try: they succeed. The question is not whether gender parity can work—evidence proves it can—but whether American institutions have the will to adopt it before the next centennial.
