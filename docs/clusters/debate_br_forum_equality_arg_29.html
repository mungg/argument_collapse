"While Sypnowich correctly identifies the failures of liberal 'equality of opportunity,' her proposed alternative of 'human flourishing' is a paternalistic, culturalized metric that distracts from the essential socialist task of dismantling structural capitalist exploitation."

Christine Sypnowich’s essay brings an urgent and refreshing clarity to the debate over economic and social justice. For too long, the prevailing discourse has been trapped in the anesthetizing language of "equality of opportunity"—a framework that, even in its most robust liberal forms, fundamentally legitimizes vast disparities in wealth, power, and quality of life. As a Marxist, I share Sypnowich’s conviction that equality of opportunity is a woefully insufficient metric for justice, and her call for an outcome-oriented socialism is necessary. However, while she rightly diagnoses the failure of liberal egalitarianism, her proposed solution—a focus on "human flourishing"—drifts into a cultural utopianism that misses the structural core of capitalist exploitation. In attempting to broaden the egalitarian horizon, Sypnowich loses sight of the material base.

Sypnowich correctly observes that the liberal fixation on "opportunity" and "personal responsibility" serves an ideological function. It provides a moral justification for inequality. By framing the economy as a meritocratic race, liberal egalitarianism ensures that those who "lose" can be blamed for their own condition. As Sypnowich points out, this framework "washes its hands" of the actual suffering people experience, rationalizing it as the inevitable outcome of free choices. She is also exactly right to challenge the liberal conceit of neutrality. The capitalist state is never neutral; it actively enforces the property relations and market dynamics that dictate how people can live.

But where Sypnowich stumbles is in her affirmative metric. She adopts Amartya Sen’s framing of "flourishing," arguing that an egalitarian society must ensure that citizens live "equally flourishing lives," expanding this to include cultural pursuits like music and arts, as well as overcoming "alienation and loneliness." While these are beautiful ideals, anchoring a political program to the subjective and infinitely expansive concept of "flourishing" misdirects our political energies.

The problem with capitalism is not merely that it fails to provide the opportunity for music lessons or that it makes our environments, in William Morris’s words, "plainer, grimmer and barer." The fundamental problem with capitalism is the structural domination and exploitation inherent in private ownership of the means of production. By shifting the focus to "flourishing," Sypnowich psychologizes and culturalizes a problem that is fundamentally material and structural.

When Marx wrote of "from each according to his ability, to each according to his needs," he was not proposing a state apparatus that evaluates and ensures subjective flourishing. He was describing a society that had abolished the class contradictions of capitalism, where the collective wealth generated by social labor is democratically managed to meet human needs, rather than expropriated as profit. Sypnowich’s vision of a society that intervenes to "make people better choosers" or to initiate them into "valuable pursuits" sounds less like socialism and more like a radicalized social democracy with a paternalistic cultural agenda.

Focusing on "flourishing" as an outcome metric also introduces unresolvable administrative and political paradoxes. How does a society measure equal flourishing? If one person finds profound fulfillment in reading philosophy and another is perpetually dissatisfied despite having access to every cultural and material resource, must the state continuously intervene to elevate the latter’s "flourishing" to match the former’s? Sypnowich attempts to wave away this concern by noting that we should not be "hamstrung by a prohibition on seeking equality," but the bureaucratic mechanisms required to manage such a subjective metric would be staggering.

Instead of an abstract commitment to equal flourishing, the socialist project should remain firmly rooted in the abolition of class hierarchy and the decommodification of life’s necessities. The goal is not to guarantee that everyone plays the piano or feels equally fulfilled, but to ensure that housing, healthcare, food, education, and elder care are provided as guaranteed public goods rather than market commodities. When we focus on the democratic control of the economy and the universal provision of material needs, the "flourishing" takes care of itself. People do not need the state to teach them how to live well; they need the state to stop enforcing the property laws that allow billionaires to hoard the wealth that the working class creates.

Sypnowich is entirely correct that liberal egalitarianism’s "opportunity" is a dead end. But the alternative is not to replace the measurement of opportunity with a measurement of flourishing. The alternative is to seize the mechanisms of wealth creation and democratize the economy. True solidarity is not about paternalistically guiding one’s neighbors toward higher cultural pursuits; it is about standing shoulder to shoulder to dismantle the structural machinery of exploitation. Sypnowich’s essay is a brilliant critique of liberal philosophy, but her socialist remedy needs less Amartya Sen and more Karl Marx.
