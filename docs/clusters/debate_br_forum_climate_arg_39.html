Diversified Gemini

"The responding essay argues that the defeat of fossil capital cannot be separated from or prioritized over the struggles against racial capitalism and colonialism, as these systems of oppression are the very structures that sustain the fossil fuel industry."

Olúfẹ́mi O. Táíwò’s “Climate, State, and Utopia” is a brilliant articulation of the strategic necessity of state power in the fight against climate collapse. His diagnosis of the left’s historical squeamishness regarding the state is spot-on, and his call to "first dethrone fossil capital; then transform the world" provides a clear, action-oriented mandate. However, Táíwò’s "two-step" framework rests on a dangerous premise: the idea that the defeat of fossil capital can be analytically and practically separated from the struggles against racial capitalism, gendered oppression, and colonialism. In arguing that we must not confuse the "moral parity" of social struggles with their "temporal priority," Táíwò unintentionally sidelines the very forces and realities that are essential to achieving his first step. There is no step two, because fossil capital cannot be dethroned without dismantling the structures of white supremacy and environmental racism that sustain it.

Táíwò uses the analogy of slavery to justify his sequencing, arguing that while slavery was always worth opposing, it took specific historical conditions (the Civil War) to make abolition winnable. He suggests that, similarly, we might have to wait to win racial justice until after we have defeated fossil capital. But this analogy misconstrues the nature of the climate crisis and the mechanics of fossil fuel extraction. Fossil capital is not a colorblind economic machine that simply happens to emit carbon; it is fundamentally reliant on the existence of racial sacrifice zones.

From the cancer alleys of Louisiana to the Niger Delta, and from the indigenous lands of North America to the smog-choked peripheries of the Global South, the extraction, refinement, and burning of fossil fuels are subsidized by the devaluation of Black, Brown, and Indigenous lives. The political power of fossil capital—which Táíwò rightly identifies as the primary obstacle to our survival—is maintained precisely by exploiting these racial hierarchies. If a society did not tolerate the poisoning of minority and low-income communities, the fossil fuel industry in its current form would be economically unviable. Therefore, any political strategy aiming to "dethrone" fossil capital must fundamentally be an anti-racist strategy.

By treating intersectional justice as a second-order priority—a "step two" to be achieved after we secure the state and the energy transition—Táíwò risks validating a technocratic, "colorblind" approach to decarbonization. We have already seen the consequences of such approaches. When climate policy is divorced from racial and economic justice, it produces carbon pricing schemes that disproportionately burden the working-class poor, or it leads to the siting of industrial-scale solar arrays and lithium mines on sacred indigenous lands. These "solutions" do not dethrone the extractive logic of the system; they merely update its energy source while maintaining the racialized geography of sacrifice.

Furthermore, Táíwò's sequencing ignores the actual political composition of the climate movement. The most vibrant, resilient, and effective resistance to fossil capital today is not coming from abstract, single-issue environmentalists, but from frontline communities for whom the climate crisis is inextricably linked to housing justice, police abolition, and indigenous sovereignty. The Sunrise Movement, which Táíwò mentions, gained its power precisely by linking the demand for decarbonization with the demand for a Green New Deal—a framework that insists on a federal jobs guarantee, housing rights, and massive investments in communities of color historically marginalized by redlining and pollution.

To tell these organizers that their specific struggles for racial and economic justice must wait until after fossil capital is defeated is not only patronizing; it is politically disastrous. You cannot build the mass democratic coalition required to seize state power—Táíwò’s essential first step—if you ask the most affected constituencies to put their survival on the back burner in the name of a generalized, temporally sequenced "planetary" victory. The energy of the Black Lives Matter uprisings and the indigenous land defense movements are the very engine of the climate justice movement. To separate them is to cut the engine from the car.

Táíwò is absolutely correct that the material conditions for justice (a habitable planet) must be secured, and that the state is the necessary vehicle for this massive logistical undertaking. But the state itself is an institution deeply structured by racial capitalism. If we capture state power merely to execute an energy transition, without simultaneously transforming the state's relationship to policing, border control, and property rights, we will build a green fortress state. A state that maintains its militarized borders to repel climate refugees while powering its surveillance drones with solar energy has not achieved liberation; it has achieved eco-apartheid.

Thea Riofrancos, whom Táíwò briefly quotes, notes that "state policies and movement tactics should work in tandem." We must take this further: the transformation of the state's racial and colonial logics must work in tandem with the energy transition. They are the same struggle. The fight to close a coal plant in Chicago is a fight for Black respiratory health; the fight against a pipeline in Minnesota is a fight for Indigenous treaty rights.

We cannot afford the luxury of stagism. The twentieth-century Marxist "two revolutions" theory Táíwò references failed precisely because the promised "second step" of true emancipation never arrived once the state consolidated power. If we build a green state that does not uproot environmental racism and colonial extraction from day one, we will find that the structures of domination are too deeply entrenched to ever be dismantled. We must fight for an intersectional climate justice that demands everything, all at once, because our lives depend on it.
