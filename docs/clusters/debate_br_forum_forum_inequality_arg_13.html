# The Missing Word Is Power

There is much to admire in David Grusky's essay—its impatience with the "deregulative compact," its insistence that pre-tax inequality has institutional roots, its refusal to treat market outcomes as natural facts. But for all its institutional vocabulary, the essay keeps reaching for an oddly depoliticized analytic frame. Grusky calls the central problem "rent" and the solution "market repair." Both phrases obscure what is really at stake. The word he keeps avoiding is power.

Consider Grusky's two illustrations. The college wage premium, he says, persists because of "bottlenecks" in educational supply. CEO pay balloons because boards are "captured." Both descriptions are accurate as far as they go, but they treat the underlying mechanism as a kind of market malfunction—as if these were technical glitches that a sufficiently clever reform could iron out. They aren't. They are the predictable outputs of a forty-year political project in which capital, having decisively defeated labor, restructured American institutions to serve its interests. To call this "market failure" is to misname it. It is class victory.

Take the more telling example Grusky barely mentions: unions. In 1955, roughly 35 percent of American private-sector workers belonged to a union. Today the figure is under 7 percent. There is now a substantial body of work—Western and Rosenfeld's recent paper estimates that the decline in union density accounts for perhaps a third of the growth in wage inequality among men since the 1970s. Unions compressed wages not through "market repair" but through organized worker power: strikes, contracts, political mobilization. Their destruction was not the unwinding of a bottleneck; it was a sustained campaign by employers, conservative politicians, and the courts to break the institutions that gave workers leverage. The Reagan administration's firing of the air traffic controllers in 1981 was not a market correction. It was a class signal.

Grusky's framework cannot quite see this because his vocabulary insists that the alternative to "competitive markets" is "rent." But the alternative most relevant to wage compression in the postwar era was not perfect competition; it was countervailing power. Galbraith understood this in 1952. Big firms have always exercised market power; the question is whether other organized actors—unions, regulators, antitrust enforcers, civil society—exercise enough countervailing power to constrain them. Since the late 1970s, almost every form of countervailing power has been systematically dismantled: antitrust enforcement narrowed by Bork and the Chicago School, labor law neutered by NLRB packing and right-to-work expansions, financial regulation gutted by repeated waves of deregulation. None of this resembles the spontaneous emergence of "bottlenecks." It is a coordinated reconfiguration of power.

Why does this analytic difference matter? Because it changes the prescription. If the problem is "market failure," the solution is technocratic: expand college slots, redesign board procedures, fine-tune competition policy. If the problem is the collapse of countervailing power, the solution is political: rebuild unions, restore antitrust, reinvigorate the regulatory state, and—yes—use the tax system as both revenue-raiser and political weapon. The former approach treats inequality as a puzzle for policy analysts. The latter recognizes it as a fight.

Grusky's anti-tax rhetoric is especially puzzling in this light. He fears that taxation can be "discredited as socialist equality mongering," whereas his preferred institutional reforms supposedly cannot. Has he watched the politics of any institutional reform proposed in the last twenty years? Card check, the public option, the Employee Free Choice Act, Dodd-Frank's Volcker Rule—each was savaged as un-American, socialist, job-killing. The notion that proposals to break up CEO power over boards or open the gates of Stanford would face less ideological resistance than a higher top marginal rate is wishful thinking. Powerful actors do not relinquish their advantages because reformers have appealed to "competition." They relinquish them when they are politically defeated.

There is a still deeper problem. Grusky writes as if the goal is "inequality in which we can believe"—a chastened meritocracy where bottlenecks are gone and competition is genuine. But the harshest forms of inequality the United States produces are not really about competition at all; they are about the conditions of life for the bottom half: stagnant wages, eroding benefits, precarious work, deteriorating health outcomes, mass incarceration. Even a perfectly competitive labor market, in a society where workers are atomized and employers consolidated, can produce brutal outcomes. The market is not a neutral arena to be perfected. It is a structure of power to be politically contested.

OWS, at its best, understood this. The slogan was not "fix the rules." It was "we are the 99 percent." That is a statement about power, not about competition. Grusky has translated it into a more comfortable academic language—and lost something essential in the translation.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The rise in inequality is better explained by the systematic destruction of unions and countervailing power than by the emergence of 'bottlenecks' or 'rent.'
  2. 02Labeling inequality as a 'market failure' incorrectly frames a political struggle as a technocratic puzzle, leading to insufficient policy prescriptions.
  3. 03The author's belief that institutional reforms are less politically vulnerable than tax increases is naive, as any challenge to entrenched interests is met with fierce ideological opposition.
  4. 04A perfectly competitive market can still produce brutal outcomes for workers if they remain atomized and lack the collective power to contest consolidated capital.
  5. 05The core message of movements like Occupy Wall Street is a demand for a redistribution of power between the 99 percent and the 1 percent, not a request for more efficient market competition.
