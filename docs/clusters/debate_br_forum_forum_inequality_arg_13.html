# The College Bottleneck Is Not What Grusky Thinks It Is

David Grusky's essay deserves a careful response on its own terms, and I want to focus on the case that does most of the analytic work for him: the claim that the college wage premium reflects "bottlenecks" in educational supply, and that "market repair" through expanded access would substantially compress wages. This argument is appealing, intuitive, and—I think—mostly wrong. Examining why it is wrong is useful, because it reveals broader problems with the "rent everywhere" framing.

First, the basic facts. The college wage premium did indeed expand sharply, particularly during the 1980s, and the gap between college-educated workers and the rest of the labor force is a major component of rising inequality. But the explanation Grusky offers—that supply has been artificially constrained by elite universities rationing their slots and by poor families being unable to send their children to college—does not fit the data well.

Consider that college enrollment has actually risen substantially over the past three decades. The share of young adults attending some form of post-secondary education has grown from roughly 50 percent in the mid-1970s to roughly 70 percent today. The share with a bachelor's degree has risen from about 25 percent to over 30 percent. If the bottleneck story were doing the heavy lifting, we would expect the substantial expansion in college-educated supply to have compressed the premium. It hasn't. The premium has continued to grow, or at best plateaued at historically elevated levels. That is the central puzzle—and the bottleneck framing cannot resolve it.

The mainstream economic explanation, due primarily to Goldin and Katz, fits the data far better. The demand for skilled labor has been rising rapidly, driven by skill-biased technical change and globalization. From roughly 1915 to 1980, the supply of educated workers in the United States grew fast enough to keep ahead of this demand, and wage inequality fell or stayed flat. Starting in the 1970s, the rate of educational attainment growth slowed, while demand continued to accelerate. The result was an expanding premium—not because of newly imposed bottlenecks, but because demand outran supply. This is, in their famous phrase, "the race between education and technology."

The distinction matters. Grusky's bottleneck framing implies that if we just opened the gates at Stanford and Berkeley—or expanded financial aid sufficiently—the premium would shrink, executive demand would suddenly be sated, and inequality would fall. The Goldin-Katz framing implies that we need a much more ambitious expansion of educational attainment, beginning in early childhood, sustained across K-12, and dramatically increasing college completion—not selectivity—rates. Elite universities are largely a red herring; the binding constraint is not how many people graduate from Harvard, but how many graduate from any four-year institution and with what skills. Grusky's image of Harvard as a Toyota dealership rationing hybrids is rhetorically effective but analytically misleading. The supply problem is at community colleges and non-selective state universities, where completion rates are catastrophically low, not at the Ivy League.

This matters for the larger argument because Grusky's first case study is meant to show that "market repair" can produce both more equality and higher national income. But the actual policy implication of the Goldin-Katz analysis is more sobering: closing the gap requires decades of sustained investment in human capital from infancy through young adulthood, against substantial headwinds. It is not a matter of removing artificial scarcity. There is no quick win lurking behind a rationing apparatus.

A similar caution applies to the CEO pay example. Yes, board capture is real, and yes, executive compensation has features of rent-extraction. But the rise of executive pay also tracks the rise of firm size, the spread of stock-based compensation, and the explosion of finance—global, structural forces that aren't going to be undone by tweaking proxy rules. Bebchuk and Fried's "managerial power" critique is important but partial. Even if every board were perfectly independent, top executive pay in trillion-dollar global firms would still be enormous.

What follows? Not, I think, Grusky's confident conclusion that institutional reform dominates redistribution. The empirics suggest something humbler: the forces driving inequality are deep, structural, and partially exogenous to American policy choices. They can be ameliorated by both serious educational investment and serious redistribution, and we should pursue both without pretending that one renders the other obsolete. Grusky frames the choice as "market repair versus band-aid taxation." The honest framing is that we need a sustained, multifront response, and that for the next decade or two, the tax-and-transfer system will inevitably do most of the heavy lifting, simply because educational and institutional change moves slowly. That is not a counsel of despair. It is a counsel of seriousness.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'bottleneck' theory of educational supply is contradicted by data showing that the college wage premium remained high despite a significant increase in college enrollment over the last thirty years.
  2. 02Rising inequality is better explained by 'skill-biased technical change' and globalization outstripping the supply of skilled labor rather than artificial rationing by elite institutions.
  3. 03The primary constraint on educational attainment is low completion rates at non-selective institutions rather than the admissions policies of elite universities.
  4. 04Executive compensation is driven by global structural forces like firm size and the growth of finance, which are unlikely to be reversed by simple institutional tweaks to board governance.
  5. 05Because institutional and educational reforms take decades to yield results, a robust tax-and-transfer system remains the most effective tool for addressing inequality in the near term.
