"A true African development miracle must be measured by improvements in the daily lives and capabilities of women and children rather than just macroeconomic indicators like GDP and commodity prices."

Edward Miguel’s essay asks whether Africa may be entering a period of economic and political transformation. It is a vital question, but the answer depends on whose lives we use to measure the turn. GDP growth, roads, elections, commodity prices, and foreign investment matter. Yet a development miracle that does not change the daily prospects of women and children will not be much of a miracle.

Consider the indicators Miguel mentions: school enrollment, HIV prevalence, rural poverty, violence, displacement, and employment. In each domain, gender is central. Girls’ schooling shapes fertility, child health, future earnings, and political voice. HIV/AIDS has fallen heavily on women, both biologically and through caregiving burdens. Civil conflict exposes women to displacement, sexual violence, widowhood, and loss of land. Agricultural productivity often depends on women farmers who lack secure land rights, credit, extension services, and control over income. If Africa’s recovery is real, it must be visible here.

The good news is that some changes already are visible. Across much of Africa, more children are in school than a decade ago, and in many places the gender gap in primary schooling has narrowed. Public-health campaigns, vaccination, malaria prevention, and HIV treatment are saving lives. Mobile phones help traders, including women in informal markets, obtain price information and maintain networks. Micro and small enterprises are expanding in towns like Busia. These are not side issues. They are the texture of development.

But growth can also bypass women. A commodity boom in oil or minerals often creates male-dominated employment and enriches central governments while doing little for maternal health or girls’ education. Large infrastructure projects may compensate male landholders while ignoring women’s use rights. Commercial agriculture can employ women in difficult conditions with low wages and insecure contracts. Even when household income rises, women may not control it. National income statistics do not reveal these distributions.

Kenya’s post-election violence is a reminder that political breakdown has gendered consequences. When families are driven from homes, women often become responsible for feeding children in camps, caring for the injured, and holding households together after livelihoods are destroyed. Sexual violence frequently rises in situations of displacement and impunity. Girls may leave school because fees become unaffordable or because families fear for their safety. A few weeks of political violence can undo years of quiet progress in health and education.

This does not mean women are only victims. African women have been central to democratization, peacebuilding, farming, trade, and community survival. Market women, church groups, nurses, teachers, lawyers, and mothers’ associations have often sustained civic life when formal politics became predatory. In Liberia, women’s peace activism helped push a brutal war toward settlement. Across the continent, women’s organizations have fought for legal reforms on inheritance, domestic violence, and representation. Any account of African resilience that focuses only on male politicians and armed groups is incomplete.

Miguel’s skepticism about aid should therefore be tempered by attention to social investments. Aid may not explain GDP growth, but it can help fund the services that determine whether growth becomes human development. Maternal health clinics, girls’ secondary schools, clean water, antiretroviral treatment, and malaria prevention may not immediately produce export booms, but they alter life chances. A woman who survives childbirth, avoids HIV infection, learns to read, and can decide when to have children contributes to development in ways that macroeconomic tables capture poorly.

The foreign-aid debate also needs a gender lens. Some aid has been wasteful or captured by elites. But community health programs, support for girls’ education, and funding for women’s legal rights can be highly consequential even when national growth effects are hard to estimate. The standard of success should not be only whether aid raises GDP next year. It should be whether it expands capabilities, reduces preventable death, and increases people’s control over their lives.

Agriculture is another area where gender matters. Women produce a large share of food crops in many African countries, yet extension services often target men. Credit may require land title that women do not possess. Improved seeds may be distributed through male-headed cooperatives. Irrigation schemes may ignore women’s plots. If climate change brings more drought, these inequalities will become more dangerous. Adaptation policy must reach the farmers who actually grow food, not just those recognized by land registries.

Land rights are especially important. Widows and daughters may lose access to land through customary practices or legal ambiguity. In areas of conflict, displaced women may return to find property claimed by others. Without secure rights, women have less incentive and ability to invest in soil improvement, trees, irrigation, or housing. Land reform is often politically explosive, as Kenya shows, but ignoring women’s claims perpetuates both poverty and injustice.

Democracy can help, but only if women are represented beyond token numbers. Multiparty elections do not automatically make maternal mortality a priority. Women need access to parties, local councils, courts, media, and budgets. Quotas, where well designed, can change agendas, but representation must be substantive. A parliament with more women may be more likely to debate family law, health services, water access, and violence against women. These issues are not “soft.” They are state-building issues because they shape whether citizens experience the state as protector or predator.

China’s growing role also deserves scrutiny from this perspective. Chinese-built roads and telecommunications can benefit women traders and farmers. But Chinese-run mines, construction sites, and resource deals may reproduce male-dominated employment and weak labor protections. Labor standards should include not only wages and safety, but also protections against harassment, discrimination, and abuse. Infrastructure contracts should consider market access, resettlement effects, and local employment for women.

If Africa’s current growth is to become a genuine transformation, governments should adopt a simple test: does each major policy expand the capabilities of the poorest women and children? A road should reduce the time to reach clinics and markets. A school policy should keep girls enrolled through adolescence. A health budget should reduce maternal deaths. An agricultural program should reach women farmers. A peace agreement should protect displaced families and prosecute sexual violence. A climate plan should reduce the burden of water and fuel collection.

Miguel ends with concrete proposals: reduce Western farm subsidies, insure fragile countries against conflict, end wars in Darfur and Congo, and prepare for climate change. I would add: make gender equality a measure of whether those policies work. Africa’s turn will not be proven by skyscrapers, oil revenues, or higher averages alone. It will be proven when a girl born in Busia, Bunia, Bamako, or Botswana can expect to survive childhood, attend a good school, inherit or own property, avoid coerced marriage and violence, earn an income, vote without fear, and raise children in peace.

That would be a development miracle worthy of the name.
