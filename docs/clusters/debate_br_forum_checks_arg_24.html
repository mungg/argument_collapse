Diversified Gemini

"To overcome the structural gridlock and elite rule inherent in the American constitutional system, the United States must transition from a presidential system to a parliamentary democracy with proportional representation."

In "The Dead End of Checks and Balances," Lisa L. Miller provides a forceful and largely accurate diagnosis of the American political disease. She rightly points out that the U.S. Constitution’s labyrinth of veto points—bicameralism, the presidential veto, federalism, and judicial review—has created a system structurally biased toward gridlock and elite minority rule. As she notes, this dysfunction has fueled public cynicism and paved the way for the authoritarian populism of the Trump administration.

However, while Miller’s critique of American institutions is incisive, her proposed remedies reveal a fatal blind spot. By advocating for a loosely defined "mass majoritarianism" and minor structural tweaks without addressing the foundational architecture of the American system, Miller falls into a trap long identified by comparative political scientists. The problem is not just that we have too many checks and balances; the problem is that we combine these veto points with a presidential system. If we wish to rescue American democracy, we must look beyond our borders and recognize that the solution lies in transitioning toward a parliamentary democracy with proportional representation.

The late, eminent political scientist Juan Linz famously argued in "The Perils of Presidentialism" that presidential systems are inherently unstable because they create dual democratic legitimacies. Both the president and the legislature are directly elected, and both can claim to speak for "the people." When they disagree—especially when they are controlled by different parties—there is no democratic principle to resolve the impasse. The system simply grinds to a halt.

Miller rightly laments this gridlock, noting how often popular legislation is killed. But her solution is to simply urge the mobilization of "mass majorities" to overcome these hurdles, perhaps by eliminating the filibuster or reducing the power of the Senate. She envisions a scenario where one party decisively controls the presidency and both chambers of Congress, similar to the New Deal era.

But relying on occasional, massive unified waves to pass necessary legislation is a recipe for wild pendulum swings and policy instability. Furthermore, in our deeply polarized, closely divided electorate of 2025, the idea of a permanent, dominant majoritarian coalition is a mirage. If Democrats manage to abolish the filibuster and pass sweeping voting rights or healthcare reform by a one-vote margin, what happens when Republicans inevitably win back bare majorities two or four years later? Without veto points, a presidential system devolves into a winner-take-all bloodsport, where every election is an existential crisis and the law is entirely rewritten every few years.

To achieve the responsive, democratic governance Miller desires without risking majoritarian tyranny or regime collapse, we must embrace the mechanisms of parliamentarism. In a parliamentary system, the executive is drawn from and relies upon the confidence of the legislature. There is no divided government. If the prime minister loses the support of the parliamentary majority, the government falls, and new elections are held. This fuses legislative and executive power, eliminating the institutional friction Miller decries and allowing the government to actually implement the mandate it was elected to fulfill.

Coupled with parliamentarism, we must implement proportional representation (PR). Miller points out that "elites" exploit our system to block popular will. But the deepest elite entrenchment occurs because our first-past-the-post, single-member-district system mathematically guarantees a rigid two-party duopoly. Millions of voters are trapped in uncompetitive districts where their votes do not matter. The Democratic and Republican parties have become massive, incoherent coalitions, held together only by negative polarization—the fear of the other side.

Under PR, a party receives seats in the legislature roughly proportional to its share of the popular vote. This would shatter the two-party duopoly. The Trumpist right, the traditional chamber-of-commerce conservatives, the establishment liberals, and the democratic socialists would all form their own distinct parties.

Miller correctly notes that the checks and balances narrative forces "consensus building" in a way that often just allows minorities to block change. Under a PR parliamentary system, consensus building happens openly, post-election, through the formation of coalition governments. Parties must negotiate concrete policy platforms to achieve a ruling majority. This creates true democratic accountability. If a coalition government fails to deliver on its negotiated agenda, the voters can easily shift their support to other parties in the next election, without the fear of "wasting" their vote.

Miller calls for a constitutional amendment that gives "regular people a constitutional role, such as citizens’ assemblies." While innovative, this is a cumbersome patch on a broken machine. A PR system inherently gives regular people a voice by ensuring that every vote matters and every significant political faction is represented in the legislature. It would immediately solve the problem of gerrymandering (since districts would be multi-member or national) and would eliminate the geographic bias of the Electoral College and the Senate.

Admittedly, transitioning to a proportional parliamentary system requires a monumental constitutional rewrite, perhaps a new constitutional convention. But if we are in a moment, as Miller suggests, that requires "a long-overdue reckoning with the system’s deep vices," we should not waste our energy on half-measures. Trying to graft majoritarian efficiency onto a Madisonian presidential structure is like trying to turn a horse-drawn carriage into a race car by simply whipping the horses harder.

Miller is right: the dead end of checks and balances has brought us to the brink of democratic collapse under the weight of right-wing populism and institutional decay. But the path forward is not a romanticized return to FDR-style unified government within our current flawed framework. It is to learn from the thriving democracies of Northern Europe and elsewhere, and build a modern, parliamentary republic where the government is empowered to act, and the people are truly and proportionally represented.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The inherent instability of presidentialism stems from 'dual democratic legitimacies' where both the executive and legislature claim a mandate, leading to irresolvable impasses.
  2. 02Relying on occasional unified majorities to overcome veto points is insufficient because it leads to extreme policy instability and a winner-take-all political culture.
  3. 03A parliamentary system eliminates institutional friction by fusing legislative and executive power, ensuring the government can actually implement its mandate.
  4. 04Proportional representation is necessary to break the two-party duopoly and ensure that all political factions are represented according to their actual share of the popular vote.
  5. 05Consensus-building is more effective and accountable when it occurs through transparent post-election coalition negotiations rather than through the current system's opaque veto points.
  6. 06Structural reforms like proportional representation are more effective than 'half-measures' like citizens' assemblies because they fundamentally solve issues like gerrymandering and geographic bias.
