"While technology is a magnifier of existing human capacity, it can also serve as a decisive catalyst for building and strengthening that capacity when it is intentionally designed and embedded within broader development programs."

Reading Kentaro Toyama’s essay, I found myself nodding in agreement with nearly every specific observation yet resisting the overarching pessimism. Yes, technology is a magnifier; yes, human intent and capacity are the linchpins; yes, the myth of scale has led to spectacular waste. But to conclude that technology therefore cannot play a decisive role in ending poverty mistakes the current state of affairs for a permanent limitation. The more productive question is: Under what conditions can technology help build the very capacity it currently requires?

Toyama’s point is well taken that giving a laptop to a child without a trained teacher, adequate curriculum, and a supportive school environment is a recipe for disappointment. The One Laptop Per Child project has indeed been a poster child for techno-utopian overreach. Yet Toyama’s own Digital Green success story reveals a different path. There, technology did not replace skilled agricultural extension workers; it extended their reach. A single excellent trainer, recorded on video, could influence thousands of farmers. The magnitude of amplification—ten times more cost-effective than traditional extension—is not trivial. It represents a genuine breakthrough that would have been impossible without the technology.

The key insight is that technology can be both a magnifier of present capacity and a catalyst for building future capacity. When a health worker in rural Uganda uses a mobile phone to look up treatment protocols or to report disease outbreaks, the phone is not merely making the health worker more efficient; it is creating a feedback loop that strengthens the entire health system. Over time, that system generates institutional knowledge, trains more workers, and raises community trust in formal medicine. Toyama’s framework treats capacity as a prerequisite; in reality, it can be an outcome of sustained technology use.

Of course, this requires intentional design. A telecenter that offers only typing lessons in a foreign language is likely to fail, as the Retawadi example shows. But replace the typing tutor with localized agricultural price information, mobile banking access, or a platform for reporting broken water pumps, and suddenly the value proposition changes. The problem is not that “locals rarely saw much value in the Internet” as Toyama claims; it is that the Internet’s value was never translated into their terms. This is a design failure, not an inherent limitation of the technology.

Toyama’s argument about differential motivation—that poor people use technology for entertainment rather than self-improvement—also deserves tougher scrutiny. The same can be said of every human being on the planet. The difference is that the educated and wealthy have already acquired the skills and networks to make entertainment a leisure choice rather than a trap. Giving the poor a richer set of digital tools, combined with mentorship and relevant content, can shift their aspirations. The success of community radio and locally produced video content, which Toyama acknowledges, shows that when people see their own lives reflected in technology, motivation follows.

The magnifier metaphor is a helpful warning, but it risks becoming an excuse for inaction. If we wait until every poor village has the institutional capacity of a Danish municipality, we will wait forever. The more promising path is to embed technology within broader development programs, measure outcomes rigorously, and iterate. Technology is not a silver bullet, but it is an indispensable arrow in the quiver. Toyama’s essay does a great service by puncturing hubris; it should not be read as a funeral oration for ICT4D.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The significant cost-effectiveness and reach of successful projects like Digital Green demonstrate that technology can provide breakthroughs in development that are impossible through traditional methods alone.
  2. 02Technology can create feedback loops that generate institutional knowledge and strengthen systems over time, making capacity an outcome of technology use rather than just a prerequisite.
  3. 03The failure of many technology initiatives is a result of poor design and a lack of localized value rather than an inherent limitation of the technology itself.
  4. 04The tendency of users to prioritize entertainment over self-improvement can be overcome by providing relevant, localized content and mentorship that shifts user aspirations.
  5. 05Waiting for perfect institutional capacity before introducing technology is impractical; instead, technology should be used iteratively to help develop those very institutions.
