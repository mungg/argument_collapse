"The ultimate summer guilty pleasure is the nostalgic indulgence of rereading Young Adult novels from one's youth."

For me, the ultimate summer guilty pleasure isn't a trashy romance or a fast-paced thriller; it is the indulgence of nostalgia. Summer is the only time of year I allow myself to regress and reread the Young Adult novels that shaped my childhood and adolescence.

There is a unique comfort in returning to a world where you already know the outcome. When the real world is chaotic and unpredictable, cracking open an old, dog-eared copy of a *Harry Potter* book or diving back into the *Hunger Games* trilogy provides a deep, anchoring sense of security. Critics might sneer at adults reading YA fiction, calling it a refusal to engage with mature themes. But that criticism entirely misses the point of comfort reading.

During the summer, we seek to recapture the boundless, unstructured joy we felt during our childhood summer vacations. Rereading the books we loved back then is the quickest route to that emotional state. It is a guilty pleasure not because the books are poorly written—many are modern classics—but because it feels delightfully self-indulgent to ignore the stack of acclaimed new releases in favor of spending a weekend with old, fictional friends.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Returning to familiar stories provides a unique sense of security and comfort in an unpredictable world.
  2. 02Rereading childhood favorites allows adults to recapture the unstructured joy and emotional state of past summer vacations.
  3. 03The guilt associated with this reading habit stems from the self-indulgence of choosing familiar stories over acclaimed new releases.
