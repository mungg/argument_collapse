Diversified Claude

"The most vital contribution of Robin Kelley’s essay is its call to revive the tradition of deep, collective political study as the necessary foundation for a radical movement that can outlast temporary protests."

Reading Robin Kelley's "Black Study, Black Struggle" against the noise of the current campus debates is a strange experience. Most of what is said about the protests in the mainstream press—on both the right and the center-left—is variations on the theme that today's students are coddled, illiberal, overly sensitive, opposed to free speech. Kelley's critique is recognizably from the left, and recognizably immanent: he is criticizing the movement on its own terms, from a position of solidarity, not heckling it from the sidelines. This is a different mode of engagement, and a more useful one.

What I want to lift up is the essay's recovery of an older tradition of political education that has, I think, been largely lost. Kelley describes the political study group of his youth as "our lifeblood." He names the texts: James, Fanon, Rodney, Smith, Davis, Marx, Engels, Lenin, Williams, Firestone, Nkrumah, Carmichael, Luxemburg, Gramsci, Cabral. This list is striking not just for its breadth but for what it implies about how political consciousness gets formed. It is not formed by tweeting. It is not formed by attending a lecture. It is formed by sitting in a room with other people, week after week, working through difficult texts together, arguing about them, applying them to the political situation at hand.

This practice has thinned out considerably. There are still study groups in some movement spaces, but they are not the central pedagogical institution they once were. What has replaced them, I think, is a combination of social media discourse and one-off training sessions—neither of which produces the kind of deep, sustained political formation that the older study groups produced. Social media discourse rewards quick reactions and tribal positioning. Training sessions deliver compressed content in a transactional format. Neither builds the slow, painful, communal practice of working through hard ideas together over time.

Kelley is right that this is a crisis of political education, and he is right that the consequences are visible in the contemporary movement. When students demand "cultural competency training" as a response to structural racism, they are reaching for the closest available pedagogical format, because the format of the radical study group is largely unfamiliar to them. The training has the shape of education without the substance of it. It produces credentialed but not transformed participants.

The rebuilding of political study—as a practice, an institution, a culture—seems to me the most urgent task Kelley implicitly identifies. The protests will come and go. The institutional reforms they win will be partial and often cosmetic. What lasts beyond a movement moment is the political formation of the people who participated in it. If the formation is shallow—if participants come out of the moment with a handful of slogans and no deep engagement with the political-economic structures they were protesting—then the movement leaves little behind. If the formation is deep—if participants come out having read Fanon and Du Bois and Robin Kelley and figured out together what those texts mean for their own situation—then the movement leaves behind a generation of political workers.

The UCLA freedom school Kelley describes at the essay's end is a beginning. So are the various Black Lives Matter reading groups that have sprung up in cities across the country. So are the radical bookstores that have hung on through the lean years and are now finding new audiences. The infrastructure exists, in pieces. The question is whether it can be scaled, networked, sustained.

This is not glamorous work. It does not produce headlines or hashtags. It is slower than a protest and less photogenic. But it is, I think, the precondition for any radical politics that will outlast its moment. The students who emerged from the Mississippi Freedom Schools went on to shape American politics for decades. The students who emerge from contemporary campus protests will shape the next several decades. What they read together, and with whom, will matter as much as which buildings they occupied.

Kelley's essay is, among other things, a plea for the older practice to be revived. I take that plea seriously. It is the part of his essay that has stayed with me longest, more than the critiques of trauma-talk or multiculturalism. Build the study groups. Read the books. Argue about them. Apply them to the situation at hand. This is the foundation on which a movement that is more than an outburst can be built.
