"The moral case for amnesty is more robust when grounded in a religious framework of universal human rights and family dignity rather than the secular liberal focus on the passage of time."

The Catholic bishops of the United States have called for immigration reform, including legalization of long-resident undocumented immigrants, since well before Joseph Carens' essay. They have done so on grounds that overlap substantially with Carens' but that draw from a different and, I think, deeper tradition. Carens' argument is essentially secular and liberal. The religious case for amnesty proceeds from premises that liberal philosophy can borrow but does not generate on its own.

Catholic social teaching holds that the human person has a right to migrate when conditions in his home country do not allow him to support himself and his family with dignity. This right is rooted in the universal destination of created goods — the principle that the goods of the earth are given for the use of all human beings, and that political and economic arrangements that prevent some people from accessing them are arrangements that have to be justified, not assumed. This is the framework within which the Catholic Church has spoken about migration for over a century, going back to Rerum Novarum and continuing through the documents of the Second Vatican Council, Paul VI's Populorum Progressio, John Paul II's Centesimus Annus, and a series of more recent statements by Benedict XVI.

What this framework offers, that Carens' essay does not, is a positive account of why migration is morally legitimate in the first place. Carens argues that long-resident migrants have a right to stay because of their accumulated ties to the receiving society. The right is generated by the residence. But this leaves the question of how the residence began in a kind of moral limbo: Carens accepts, provisionally, that the state had the right to refuse entry, and the moral defense of the migration depends on the subsequent passage of time. The Catholic framework offers a different starting point. The migration was, in many cases, morally legitimate from the start — not because the state had to admit the migrant, but because the migrant had a real human right to seek the means of subsistence and dignity for himself and his family, a right that exists prior to and independent of state authorization.

This matters because it changes the moral burden. On Carens' view, the long-resident migrant has earned a claim through time; the recent migrant has not yet earned it; the border-crosser intercepted at the border has earned nothing. On the Catholic view, the migrant has a claim from the start, which the state may regulate but cannot extinguish. The state has its own rights — to regulate immigration in service of the common good — but these rights are not absolute, and they have to be weighed against the migrant's prior right to migrate.

I am not asking Carens to become a Catholic, and I recognize that his essay is addressed to a different audience. But I want to suggest that his argument is stronger when it is rooted in a thicker moral framework than the one he explicitly invokes. The membership argument, by itself, accepts too much of the restrictionist premise. It concedes that the migrant began in a state of moral wrong, which has been gradually transformed by time into a state of moral right. The Catholic view is that the migrant began in a state of moral right that the receiving state may have been justified in qualifying but never in extinguishing. This is a stronger position, and it is, I think, closer to the truth.

A related point: the Catholic framework emphasizes the family as the basic unit of moral life. This is not just a rhetorical commitment; it is constitutive of how the Church thinks about migration. The family is the place where the right to subsistence and dignity is exercised. The migrant who crosses the border to send money home is not exercising an individual preference; he is fulfilling a family obligation. The migrant who is later joined by a spouse and children is not chaining migration; he is reuniting a family that was sundered by economic necessity. The state's enforcement actions that separate families — through deportation, through long detention, through prevention of family reunification — are not just administratively harmful but constitute violations of a fundamental moral structure.

Carens' essay nods to the family in his treatment of marriages between irregular migrants and citizens, but he does not develop the broader point. The family is the place where most of the human ties Carens cites — the ties that constitute membership — are formed and sustained. Membership is mediated through family. Removal disrupts family. The harm of removal is centrally a familial harm. This is not just a point of emphasis; it is, I think, the core of the case for amnesty, and it is undertheorized in Carens' essay.

I want to acknowledge a tension in the religious case for amnesty. Catholic social teaching also holds that the state has a duty to regulate immigration for the common good, and that potential migrants do not have an unqualified right to entry into any particular country. This is not the same as a blanket endorsement of open borders. The bishops, in their formal statements, have been careful to balance the right to migrate against the duty of states to govern immigration. The position is not "open borders" but "humane reform" — which, in practical terms, looks rather like what Carens proposes, but rooted in a different moral framework.

What this religious framework offers to the broader debate, I think, is a sense of moral seriousness that the liberal-philosophical debate sometimes lacks. The Catholic Church has been talking about migration in terms of human dignity, family integrity, and the universal destination of goods for a century. Liberal philosophy is, in some respects, just catching up. Carens' essay is a fine piece of liberal philosophy, and I do not want to disparage it. But I want to insist that the moral case for amnesty is older and deeper than its current liberal formulation, and that the deeper case is in some respects stronger than the liberal version that has come to dominate the secular debate.

The Catholic bishops have been talking about Miguel Sanchez and his family for decades. They have done so in language that emphasizes his humanity, his dignity, his role as father and husband, his right to seek the means of life. This is the moral language we need. Carens' essay is welcome and important, but it is a translation of a deeper case into the vocabulary of secular liberal philosophy. Those of us who can speak both vocabularies should not let the translation become the original.

  3. 03The family, rather than the individual, is the basic unit of moral life, making the disruption of family ties through deportation a violation of a fundamental moral structure.
