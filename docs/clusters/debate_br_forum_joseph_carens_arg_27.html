"While supporting a broad amnesty for long-term residents, liberal democracies should prioritize granting legal status to irregular migrants who arrived as children because their social identity is formed entirely within the host society and they bear no moral responsibility for their legal status."

The most compelling figures in Joseph Carens’s essay are not the adult migrants who crossed borders knowingly, but the children who inherit the consequences. A child brought to the United States at age three, six, or twelve has not violated the law in any meaningful moral sense. Yet under current law, that child may grow up reciting the Pledge of Allegiance, attending American schools, speaking English, and planning for college, only to discover that adulthood brings exclusion. This is a failure not only of immigration policy but of moral imagination.

Carens argues that being raised in a society makes one a member of it. That proposition should be uncontroversial. Childhood is when language, habits, loyalties, friendships, and expectations are formed. A teenager who has spent ten years in Los Angeles, Phoenix, Houston, Chicago, or Raleigh is not a visitor in any meaningful sense. To deport her to a country she barely remembers is not to send her “home.” It is exile.

The pending DREAM Act reflects this insight. Its basic idea—that young people brought here as children should be able to earn legal status through college or military service—has attracted support across ideological lines because it appeals to a widely shared intuition: children should not be punished for their parents’ decisions. But even the DREAM Act is, in some respects, too narrow. Why must belonging be proven through military service or higher education? What about the young person who works to support siblings, attends community college part-time, cares for a sick parent, or enters a trade? If the moral basis is social formation, then the evidence is years of residence and schooling, not heroic achievement.

Carens’s broader amnesty proposal includes adults as well, and I am sympathetic to it. But politically and morally, childhood arrivals deserve first priority. Their case exposes the absurdity of treating legal status as the sole marker of membership. A child cannot choose lawful entry. A child cannot navigate visa categories. A child cannot decide whether a parent should overstay. Yet the law often waits until that child becomes useful—ready for work, college, or military service—and then denies the legal identity needed to proceed.

The consequences are severe. Unauthorized students may be excellent classmates but unable to obtain lawful employment. In many states, they face out-of-state tuition rates or barriers to financial aid, even after years in local schools. They may be unable to drive legally, intern, travel, or accept scholarships. Teachers encourage them to dream, while the law quietly blocks the path. The waste is staggering. Public schools invest in these students for years; then federal policy prevents society from benefiting fully from their talents.

Opponents say that legalizing childhood arrivals will encourage parents to bring more children unlawfully. This objection should be taken seriously, but it cannot be decisive. We do not generally impose severe lifelong penalties on children to deter adults. Nor is it clear that a parent deciding whether to migrate is calculating the details of a future legalization bill. Parents move because of work, safety, family, and hope. If deterrence is needed, it should be directed at future unlawful hiring and dangerous smuggling, not at children who are already here.

The family dimension matters too. A policy that legalizes only young people while deporting their parents creates a cruel split. Many DREAM Act students live in mixed-status families with U.S.-citizen siblings and unauthorized parents. Their stability depends on family unity. Carens is right that marriage and parenthood create claims. At minimum, parents of legalized childhood arrivals and U.S.-citizen children should have access to provisional status if they have no serious criminal record. We should not celebrate a student’s American identity while threatening to remove the mother who raised her.

Still, there is a strategic argument for beginning with youth. In a polarized Congress, comprehensive reform has repeatedly failed. The 2006 and 2007 debates collapsed under the weight of distrust. A focused bill for childhood arrivals could establish a principle of earned inclusion and reduce suffering quickly. It would not solve the status of eleven million people, but it would prevent another generation from being trapped.

Carens’s five-to-seven-year threshold may work for adults, but for children the clock should be different. A child who completes several years of schooling here has already undergone social formation. Legal status should be available before the transition to adulthood, not afterward. Ideally, unauthorized children should be able to obtain lawful status while still minors, perhaps through a parent or guardian application, so that college and work decisions are not made in fear.

The United States has long embraced birthright citizenship, unlike many European countries. That commitment reflects a generous and practical idea: children born and raised here belong here. But our treatment of children brought here at a young age contradicts the same principle. The difference between arriving at six months and being born here is legally enormous, but morally less so. In both cases, the child’s identity is formed in American society.

Carens’s essay asks liberal democracies to align law with lived membership. Nowhere is that alignment more urgent than with young people. Deporting an adult after many years may be harsh. Deporting someone raised here is a denial of reality. We should pass a generous DREAM Act immediately and then build toward broader legalization for families and long-settled adults.

A country reveals its character by how it treats children who stand at the edge of belonging through no fault of their own. These young people are already part of our classrooms, neighborhoods, churches, and workplaces. The law should stop telling them that the only home they know is not theirs.
