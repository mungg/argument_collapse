"The Democratic Party's failure in 2024 was not a failure of moderation itself, but rather a failure to execute a disciplined moderation strategy that prioritizes brand management on cultural issues while embracing bold economic populism."

Bonica and Grumbach have written a valuable takedown of the moderation myth, but in their eagerness to jettison the median voter, they throw out the baby with the bathwater. The real lesson of 2024 isn’t that moderation doesn’t work; it’s that Democrats have been doing moderation badly—and on the wrong issues.

Let’s start where we agree. The strategic environment has changed. Nationalization, polarization, and the party’s structural disadvantages are real. Candidate ideology, on its own, is a weaker predictor of electoral success than it once was. And a slavish adherence to poll-tested positions, decoupled from any larger vision, produces the kind of hollow, inauthentic campaign that Kamala Harris ran in 2024: a bundle of checked boxes that satisfied neither the base nor swing voters.

But from this, the authors conclude that moderation as a strategy is largely useless, that Democrats should stop trying to calibrate their positions to win over persuadable voters, and that the answer is a full-on mobilization strategy centered on an anti-corruption frame. This is a leap too far, and a dangerous one. The evidence they muster against moderation is less conclusive than they suggest, and their proposed alternative ignores the critical insight at the heart of popularism: some positions are political poison, and Democrats must stop drinking it, even if they have good moral reasons to hold those positions.

Take the authors’ core empirical claim: that in the Trump era, a candidate’s ideological moderation has no consistent, measurable effect on vote share. Note the word “consistent.” They acknowledge that some studies find small effects in some contexts. In a political landscape where control of the House and Senate comes down to a handful of razor-thin races, a small effect can be decisive. Moreover, their analysis relies heavily on a measure of ideology that combines voting records, campaign finance data, and issue positions—but ideological moderation in the sense that matters most to voters isn’t about DW-NOMINATE scores. It’s about brand perception. When voters tell pollsters that Democrats are “too extreme” on immigration, crime, or gender identity, they are not critiquing statistical models; they are making a judgment about the party’s cultural sensibilities. The authors’ preferred measure may be too bloodless to capture what actually drives swing voters away.

There’s a deeper methodological problem: the “moderates” the authors studied were often not perceived as moderate by the public on the issues that matter most. Joe Manchin, their exemplar of a man whose personal brand mattered more than his ideology, was indeed a unique case—but his brand was precisely that he was a conservative Democrat on cultural issues. He opposed abortion in many cases, supported gun rights, and backed coal. The Democratic moderates who lost between 2016 and 2024 may have had centrist roll-call voting records, but many were still tagged with the national party’s “defund the police” and “open borders” image. That’s the heart of the problem: the Democratic brand is dragged leftward not by the median member’s voting record but by the activist class, the media, and the party’s loudest progressive voices. A candidate can vote like a Blue Dog all day, but if voters in rural Pennsylvania see a clip of AOC calling to abolish ICE and associate it with the Democratic Party, that candidate is in trouble. Moderation, to be effective, requires aggressive brand management, not just individual repositioning.

This is where popularism, properly understood, offers a more plausible path than the authors’ anti-corruption gambit. Popularism is not simply “pandering.” It is the recognition that in a two-party system, your coalition necessarily includes voters who disagree with you on many things, and you must decide which policy commitments are essential and which are luxuries that cost you elections. The authors dismiss this as akin to Cleon’s demagoguery, but the better historical analogy is to Franklin Roosevelt, who famously did not campaign on racial justice in 1932 because he knew it would fracture the New Deal coalition. He focused relentlessly on economic security, the one issue that could unite northern workers and southern whites. That wasn’t pandering; it was coalition management. The authors’ anti-corruption frame is itself a form of popularism: it selects an issue (corruption) that polls well, and it implicitly tells Democrats to “shut up” about less popular topics like transgender inclusion in youth sports or abolishing ICE, so as to focus on the winning message. They just won’t admit it.

And that’s the central weakness of their proposal. Voters don’t experience issues in a vacuum. A Democrat could spend all day hammering corporate lobbyists and insider trading, but if the same candidate is perceived as soft on crime or committed to allowing biological males in women’s sports, that anti-corruption message will land with a thud. The authors cite David Broockman’s finding that many voters hold a mix of extreme positions rather than a consistent centrist ideology. All the more reason to avoid embracing any issue position that a significant majority of the electorate finds extreme, even if it pleases a progressive base. The turnout-mobilization argument also has a glass jaw: yes, progressive candidates may slightly increase turnout among the base, but they risk alienating the suburban moderates who have become the marginal voters in key swing states. Turnout gains among the left may be offset by Republican voters who are even more motivated to turn out against a “socialist” Democratic candidate. The net effect is ambiguous, and the authors’ own data on the 2024 turnout gap—which was driven in part by young and Black voters staying home—doesn’t prove that running to the left would fix it. It may simply reflect a demoralized party that couldn’t sell its economic record, or a candidate who never excited anyone.

What would a smarter moderation strategy look like? It would start by distinguishing between economic and cultural issues. On economics, Democrats should indeed be bold: the public supports higher taxes on the wealthy, expanding Medicare, and cracking down on corporate price-gouging. These are popular positions that have been underutilized because of donor sensitivity. But on cultural and social issues, the party must be ruthlessly disciplined in avoiding positions that alienate the median voter without winning commensurate support. That doesn’t mean abandoning transgender Americans or immigrants, but it does mean choosing arguments and policies that don’t sound like academic jargon. It means being able to say, “I believe in transgender rights, but I also believe that women’s sports should be fair and based on biological sex,” or “We need a secure border and a path to citizenship.” These are not extreme positions; they are where most Americans are. Polling consistently shows that large majorities support abortion rights with some restrictions, background checks for gun purchases, and a pathway to citizenship for Dreamers—while also supporting strong border enforcement and opposing transgender athletes in women’s sports. A party that aligns itself with these popular instincts can neutralize the right’s most potent attack lines and reclaim the common-sense mantle.

The authors’ anti-corruption frame is not wrong, but it is insufficient. Americans do hate corruption, but they also hate chaos, disorder, and a party that seems more interested in pronouns than in prices. The Democrats’ fatal mistake in 2024 wasn’t that Harris was too moderate—it was that too many voters had absorbed years of viral clips and GOP attacks painting her as a radical. By the time she pivoted, it was too late. The lesson is not to abandon moderation but to embed it in a long-term project of brand repair, one that begins with loudly embracing the popular and quietly leaving the unpopular on the sidelines. That, not vague talk of “moral clarity,” is how you win elections in a democracy.
