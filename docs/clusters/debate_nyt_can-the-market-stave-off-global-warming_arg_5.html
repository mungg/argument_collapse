"The effectiveness of climate policy depends less on the specific mechanism of cap and trade and more on providing a stable, predictable regulatory framework that allows the utility industry to plan long-term capital investments."

I've spent a career in the utility industry, and I want to offer a practitioner's view on what this week's announcement actually means. The EPA's proposed rule under Section 111(d) is a big deal, but not for the reasons most commentators are emphasizing. It is significant because it accepts, finally, that the power sector is going to be restructured around carbon constraints, and it gives us a workable timeline to do so.

Cap and trade, in this context, isn't a magic solution. It's a planning tool. When you run a utility, what you need above all is predictability—knowing what the rules will be over the 30- and 40-year lifespans of major capital assets. A well-designed cap-and-trade program provides that predictability through a declining cap and a price signal. You can model it, plan around it, and make investment decisions accordingly. The alternative—command-and-control regulation that prescribes specific technologies—locks in today's solutions and stifles the search for cheaper ones.

The complaints about the EU ETS are largely overblown. Yes, the price has been low, but that's partly because the cap was poorly calibrated to a recession that crushed demand. The system worked: emissions fell within the cap. The fact that they fell below the cap means the cap was too loose, not that trading failed. Adjustments are underway. RGGI in the Northeast has used a price floor to avoid the same problem and has generated significant revenue for efficiency programs while emissions have declined.

What worries me about the political conversation is the assumption that we must choose between cap and trade and a carbon tax, or between markets and regulation. In practice, utilities will face all of these: state renewable portfolio standards, federal performance standards under the new EPA rule, possibly state-level carbon pricing, plus existing requirements for SOx, NOx, mercury, and now CO2. We will navigate all of it, but the more coherent and stable the framework, the cheaper the transition will be for our customers.

The current 30 percent reduction target from 2005 levels by 2030 is meetable. Coal is already in retreat, gas is cheap, wind costs have fallen dramatically, and solar is following. Many utilities are already on trajectories that would substantially meet the targets through ongoing fleet transitions. The question is whether we do this efficiently or expensively.

My honest assessment: cap and trade isn't the best chance to stem climate change. The best chance is sustained, predictable policy of any reasonable design, applied long enough to allow capital stock turnover. Cap and trade can be part of that, and the EPA's approach is sensible. But what matters most is staying the course, election after election, for the decades it will take.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Cap and trade serves as a valuable planning tool by providing a clear price signal and a declining cap that utilities can model over the long lifespans of their assets.
  2. 02Market-based approaches like cap and trade are superior to command-and-control regulations because they encourage the search for cheaper solutions rather than locking in specific technologies.
  3. 03Past failures in carbon markets, such as the EU ETS, are often the result of poorly calibrated caps rather than a fundamental flaw in the trading system itself.
  4. 04The current carbon reduction targets are achievable because market trends, such as the decline of coal and the falling costs of renewables, are already driving a transition in the power sector.
  5. 05The primary challenge for stemming climate change is maintaining a consistent policy course across multiple decades to facilitate the necessary turnover of industrial capital stock.
