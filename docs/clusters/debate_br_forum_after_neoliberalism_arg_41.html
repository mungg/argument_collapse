"While the authors correctly argue that mainstream economics provides the tools to dismantle neoliberalism, they underestimate how the profession's internal sociology and the lack of external political infrastructure hinder the implementation of these ideas."

The diagnosis offered by Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman is largely correct, and their prescription deserves serious engagement. Economics has been wrongly tarred with the brush of neoliberalism, and mainstream economic thinking contains ample resources for building a more equitable society. However, the authors underestimate the degree to which the sociology of the profession continues to distort both research priorities and policy advice. Reshaping economics requires not merely producing better ideas, but changing the incentive structures that determine which ideas get amplified and who gets to speak with authority.

The authors are right that the competitive equilibrium model dominates only in introductory courses, and that thoughtful economists quickly move beyond it. Yet the dominance of the model in introductory courses is not incidental—it shapes how generations of students come to understand the discipline and what they expect from economists. When undergraduates leave introductory courses believing that markets always work, they carry that assumption into their later careers as journalists, policymakers, business leaders, and citizens. The first-best reasoning the authors critique does not remain confined to academic seminars; it circulates through the professional networks and informal mentoring that connect elite economics departments to central banks, finance ministries, and the financial press.

The EfIP project represents a welcome corrective, but its participants remain embedded in institutional structures that privilege certain kinds of contributions over others. Tenured academic economists at mainstream departments face strong incentives to produce technical work in established subfields, to publish in top journals that reward methodological sophistication, and to accumulate citations through incremental contributions to existing debates. Public-facing policy work—however important—rarely counts toward tenure and promotion in the same way that theoretical and empirical contributions to academic literature do. The authors note that some members of the project have engaged in writing for nonacademic audiences, but they do not address the structural barriers that make such engagement difficult for most academics.

Furthermore, the relationship between economic research and policy formation is more complex than the authors suggest. Policy ideas do not simply flow from academic research into government; they are filtered through think tanks, advocacy organizations, and professional networks that have their own agendas and constraints. The authors decry the monopoly that conservative foundations and think tanks have exercised over economic policy discourse, but they do not fully explain how their alternative framework will gain traction in a landscape dominated by well-funded adversaries. The Economics for Inclusive Prosperity network is a good start, but it lacks the institutional infrastructure—the permanent staff, the sustained funding, the media relationships—that would allow it to compete effectively with established players.

The authors' specific policy proposals are generally sound. The case for wage boards, for stronger social insurance, for taxation of multinationals based on sales rather than profits, and for rebalancing power in labor and financial markets—all of this reflects both good theory and empirical evidence. But the transition from good proposals to actual policy requires more than intellectual credibility; it requires political organization, coalition-building, and strategic communication. Economists can contribute technical expertise to these efforts, but they cannot substitute for the harder work of politics.

What is missing from this framework is a serious account of how economic ideas gain political purchase. The authors write that "it is up to economists to convince our audience about the merits of these claims," but they do not specify which audience they have in mind or how that conviction is to be achieved. The audiences that matter for policy—legislators, regulators, business leaders, journalists—operate within information environments shaped by interests and ideologies that cannot simply be overridden by better arguments. Persuasion matters, but so does power, and the authors have relatively little to say about how to build the kind of countervailing power needed to sustain progressive economic policy over time.

Despite these limitations, the essay makes an important contribution by showing that the false identification of economics with neoliberalism can be challenged on intellectual grounds. The authors are right to insist that market fundamentalism represents a perversion of economic science, not its culmination. They are right that contemporary economics contains sophisticated tools for analyzing inequality, market failures, and institutional diversity. And they are right that these tools can be deployed in service of a more inclusive vision of prosperity. What remains to be done is to build the political infrastructure that would allow those tools to be used.
