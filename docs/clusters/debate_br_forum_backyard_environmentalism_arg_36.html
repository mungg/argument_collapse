"While the proposed 'rolling-rule' regulatory regime correctly identifies the failures of command-and-control systems, its emphasis on continuous, fluid revision of rules undermines the property rights and regulatory certainty essential for private enterprise."

For years, private landowners, farmers, and developers have been caught in the crosshairs of a federal regulatory apparatus that treats them not as citizens, but as suspects. The Endangered Species Act (ESA), in particular its draconian Section 9, has long operated as a massive, uncompensated federal zoning law, effectively stripping property owners of their right to use their land the moment a listed species is found on it. Against this backdrop, Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen’s description of Habitat Conservation Plans (HCPs) as a step toward a more cooperative, localized regulatory regime is a welcome acknowledgment that the old command-and-control system is broken. However, as an advocate for property rights, I must inject a note of caution regarding their vision of a "rolling-rule regime."

The authors praise the HCP process because it allows landowners and conservationists to negotiate plans that allow for economic development while setting aside habitat. But they go further, advocating for a system of "adaptive management" where rules and requirements are continually revised based on ongoing monitoring and new scientific information. They envision a regulatory environment that "forces continuous improvements in both regulatory rules and environmental performance."

For a landowner considering a multi-million dollar, multi-year development project, the phrase "continuous improvements in regulatory rules" is terrifying. It means the goalposts are constantly moving.

The primary reason landowners have been willing to come to the table to negotiate HCPs in Southern California and elsewhere over the last few years is the Clinton administration's "No Surprises" policy. Under this policy, once an HCP is approved and the landowner is in compliance, the federal government guarantees that it will not come back years later and demand more land, more money, or more restrictions, even if circumstances change.

Fung and his co-authors seem profoundly uncomfortable with this kind of finality. They argue that because "even the best science gets better," we can never make "timeless determinations of fixed rules." They advocate for mechanisms that force "laggards" to adapt to new benchmarks. But what they call "laggardness," the business world calls "relying on a contract."

If a landowner dedicates fifty percent of their property to a conservation easement as part of a negotiated HCP, they need the certainty to build on the other fifty percent. If, five years later, a "tributary team" or a Fish and Wildlife Service database determines that the initial biological targets are not being met, who pays the cost of the "adaptive management"? Under the authors’ rolling-rule regime, it seems the burden falls right back on the private citizen to continually surrender more property rights to satisfy shifting scientific opinions.

Environmental protection is a societal good, and it should be borne by society as a whole. If the public, through its regulatory agencies, discovers that an HCP is insufficient, the government should use public funds to purchase additional habitat, not retroactively change the rules on private landowners who negotiated in good faith.

The authors correctly note that the command-and-control system was inflexibly hostile to economic reality. But a system of constant, fluid revision is no better. Private enterprise requires stable property rights and regulatory certainty. We can support localized negotiation and data pooling, but only if they result in agreements that are respected as binding contracts, not as temporary truces in a never-ending regulatory war.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'adaptive management' model creates a 'moving goalposts' scenario that makes long-term economic development projects prohibitively risky for landowners.
  2. 02The success of current Habitat Conservation Plans (HCPs) depends on 'No Surprises' policies that provide landowners with legal finality and protection from future government demands.
  3. 03What the authors characterize as 'laggardness' is actually a necessary reliance on binding contracts that must be respected by the state.
  4. 04The financial and land-use burdens of adjusting to new scientific findings should be borne by the public through government funding rather than by retroactively stripping rights from private citizens who negotiated in good faith.
  5. 05Localized negotiation and data pooling are only beneficial if they result in stable, binding agreements rather than temporary truces in a perpetual regulatory war.
