"The crisis of modern democracy stems not from internal procedural failures of national legislatures, but from a structural mismatch between the global scale of modern problems and the limited jurisdiction of the nation-state."

Ira Katznelson’s diagnosis of the structural ailments of modern democracy is eloquent, but his framing suffers from a fatal limitation: he is looking through the wrong end of the telescope. Katznelson remains stubbornly fixated on the *national* legislature as the ultimate horizon of democratic governance. By framing the crisis as a failure of domestic parliamentary procedure and an erosion of national consensus, he ignores the fundamental reality of the 21st century. The primary reason national legislatures seem paralyzed and irrelevant is that the most consequential problems of our time have fundamentally transcended the nation-state.

Katznelson briefly mentions the anxiety that "national borders no longer define a zone of security" against global threats and transnational institutions. But he treats this as a mere psychological worry rather than a structural reality. We are living in an era defined by hyper-globalization. Capital moves across borders at the speed of light, multinational corporations dwarf the GDPs of entire nations, supply chains are inextricably entangled, and carbon emissions in one hemisphere determine the sea level in another.

In this context, expecting the United States Congress—or any national parliament—to effectively regulate global capital or solve climate change is like expecting a city council to manage a global pandemic. The mismatch in scale between the problems and the jurisdiction of the national legislature is the true source of our democratic anxiety.

Katznelson notes the frustration of citizens and the temptation to turn to populism or executive action. But this frustration is entirely rational. When citizens elect a representative to protect their industrial jobs, and those jobs vanish because of a global supply chain logic beyond the reach of domestic law, the citizen realizes that the legislature is impotent. When voters demand action on climate change, but national commitments are constantly undermined by the lack of a binding global enforcement mechanism, cynicism is the logical response.

The national legislature cannot manage the "physics of consent" regarding wealth inequality when the wealthy can simply park their assets in offshore tax havens. It cannot maintain the "principle of non-convertibility" when transnational corporations can leverage their mobility to force national governments into a race to the bottom on taxes and labor standards.

Katznelson’s nostalgia for the New Deal era is telling. The New Deal was possible because it occurred during a unique period when national economies were relatively closed, and capital controls were stringent. The national government possessed the actual sovereignty required to restructure the economy. That sovereignty has since been steadily eroded, not by a lack of civic virtue, but by the architecture of the global economy—an architecture, it must be said, that was often built by those very same legislatures.

To address the "anxieties of democracy," we must stop pretending that fixing the filibuster or reforming campaign finance will magically empower Congress to master global forces. We need to begin the hard work of building and democratizing transnational institutions. Currently, global governance exists, but it is deeply undemocratic, managed by technocrats at the WTO, the IMF, and central banks.

If we value the "dignity of legislation," we must seek to elevate the legislative function to the international level. We need robust, binding international agreements on taxation, labor rights, and environmental standards, overseen by institutions that have actual democratic accountability. Until we align the scale of our democratic institutions with the scale of our global economy, national legislatures will remain nothing more than localized theater, endlessly debating problems they have no real power to solve.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01National legislatures are inherently incapable of regulating hyper-globalized forces like mobile capital, multinational corporations, and climate change.
  2. 02Citizen cynicism and the rise of populism are rational responses to the actual impotence of national representatives who lack the sovereignty to control global economic outcomes.
  3. 03The success of the New Deal was dependent on a closed national economy that no longer exists, making it a flawed model for contemporary democratic restoration.
  4. 04The 'principle of non-convertibility' between wealth and power cannot be maintained domestically when the wealthy can utilize offshore tax havens and transnational mobility.
  5. 05Democratic renewal requires shifting the legislative function to the international level by building and democratizing transnational institutions that can match the scale of the global economy.
