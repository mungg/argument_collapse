"The threat AI poses to privacy and liberty is a structural feature of its data-extractive business model rather than a research byproduct, requiring aggressive prohibitions and institutionalized regulation rather than mere shifts in norms or technical fixes."

Daron Acemoglu has performed a vital service by connecting the automation of work with the rise of digital surveillance, but his essay understates the severity of the second threat. The assault on privacy and liberty from AI is not a side effect of poorly directed research; it is the core logic of a new form of social control that is already operational. Redirecting AI toward human-complementing tasks while merely tinkering at the margins of surveillance will not suffice. We need an outright prohibition on the most dangerous applications and a rethinking of the entire data-extractive business model. The threat to democracy runs deeper than polarization and disinformation: it is about the construction of a panopticon in which dissent becomes rationally impossible.

Acemoglu notes the existence of spyware like Pegasus and the Chinese surveillance state, but he treats these as exceptional cases that a redirected AI could avoid. In reality, the trajectory is clear and accelerating. The same pattern-recognition systems that power voice assistants and personalized ads are being repurposed for employee monitoring, predictive policing, and automated social-credit scoring. The technical distinction between a benign “human-augmenting” application and a coercive one is often nonexistent. A facial-recognition system that helps a teacher track student engagement can, with minimal modification, be used to identify protesters. The datasets collected for healthcare AI — biometric and behavioral — become irresistible to insurers, employers, and security agencies. You cannot simultaneously build a vast infrastructure of data collection and behavioral prediction while hoping that its worst uses will be constrained by norms and public pressure. The pressure toward mission creep is structural.

Acemoglu’s reliance on “differential privacy, adversarial neural cryptography, secure multi-party computation, and homomorphic encryption” as technical fixes is deeply misleading. These technologies are, at best, research-stage curiosities that impose massive computational overhead and are unusable in most real-world settings. Even if they mature, they cannot solve the fundamental problem that the most valuable economic use of AI is to know more about individuals in order to influence their behavior. The market rewards surveillance precision, not privacy preservation. No amount of nudging researcher norms can overcome that brute fact.

The Cambridge Analytica episode, far from being a wake-up call that prompted a change of direction, demonstrated how quickly the system normalizes exploitation. The company’s practices were an open secret in the data-broker industry long before the scandal broke. The same infrastructure of personality profiling and microtargeting continues today, more sophisticated and more deeply embedded in every online interaction. Facebook’s entire business model depends on it, and Google’s advertising machine is the most effective surveillance engine in history. These companies will not voluntarily dismantle their profit centers because a handful of researchers feel uneasy.

What, then, is to be done? First, we must abandon the illusion that we can guide AI toward benevolent uses while leaving the surveillance apparatus intact. We need bright-line prohibitions. Facial recognition in public spaces should be banned, as a growing number of cities have begun to demand. The sale of spyware to foreign governments must be treated as a human rights violation subject to sanctions. The collection and brokering of behavioral data without explicit, ongoing, and informed consent should be illegal, with personal-data ownership rights enforceable through a private right of action. Acemoglu mentions tax code distortions favoring automation; similar distortions favor surveillance through the legal treatment of personal data as a free raw material rather than a protected asset.

Second, we need to move toward public-interest digital infrastructure. Much as we have public libraries, public broadcasting, and public roads, we should have public option platforms for search, social media, and cloud storage that operate without surveillance-based advertising. These would not replace private innovation but would set a baseline that forces commercial platforms to compete on privacy and user control. Without such an alternative, the public has no escape from the panopticon.

Third, the oversight of AI cannot be left to the self-regulation of tech firms or the gentle prodding of funding agencies. We need a dedicated, independent regulatory agency with the power to audit algorithms, mandate transparency, and impose severe penalties for violations — analogous to the Food and Drug Administration for medical products. Acemoglu’s democratic oversight remains too abstract; it must be institutionalized with real teeth.

Acemoglu is correct that the weakening of democracy makes reform harder, but his essay inadvertently contributes to a dangerous fatalism by stringing the problems together without prioritizing them. The battle for liberty is prior to, and a condition for, the battle for good jobs. A population that is comprehensively monitored, profiled, and manipulated cannot form the collective will necessary to demand economic justice. Before we can redirect AI, we must erect a sturdy legal and institutional barrier around the private sphere. The lesson of history is not that norms and democratic input eventually triumph; it is that once surveillance infrastructure is built, it is rarely torn down. We are building it now. Stopping that construction, brick by brick, is the most urgent task we face.

  3. 03The battle to protect the private sphere from a digital panopticon is a necessary prerequisite for achieving economic justice and meaningful democratic oversight.
  4. 04Effective reform requires bright-line legal bans on dangerous applications like public facial recognition and the creation of public-interest digital infrastructure to provide alternatives to surveillance-based platforms.
  5. 05Democratic oversight must be institutionalized through a dedicated regulatory agency with the power to audit algorithms and impose penalties, similar to the Food and Drug Administration.
