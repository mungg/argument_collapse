"To prevent AI from creating a dystopian future of surveillance and inequality, we must move beyond technical design principles and empower workers with the collective bargaining rights and institutional authority necessary to shape how technology is implemented in the workplace."

Daron Acemoglu asks who controls AI. His answer is largely correct: a small group of technology companies, elite researchers, and government funders shape the field’s direction. But one group is almost entirely absent from the control room: workers. Unless that changes, calls to redirect AI toward human flourishing will remain aspirations rather than power.

The problem is not simply that AI automates tasks. Workers have lived with technological change for centuries. The problem is that workers usually encounter technology as something done to them. A new scheduling system appears. A scanner starts tracking speed. A camera is installed. A route algorithm changes the pace of delivery. A chatbot takes over customer interaction. A warehouse worker, nurse, teacher, driver, or clerk is told that the system is objective, efficient, and nonnegotiable. If the worker objects, the answer is familiar: adapt or leave.

This is the missing institutional link in much discussion of AI ethics. We hear about fairness, transparency, privacy, and accountability. These are important. But in the workplace, accountability requires collective power. An individual employee cannot audit a machine learning model, challenge a multinational vendor, or bargain over data rights. Even a highly skilled worker may not know what information is being collected or how it is used. A low-wage worker who depends on the job is unlikely to refuse. Without unions, works councils, or legally enforceable consultation rights, “human-centered AI” becomes a slogan managers use while intensifying work.

Acemoglu’s essay is strongest when it situates AI within the decline of the postwar compact. That compact was never complete or just; it excluded many women, Black workers, immigrants, domestic workers, and farmworkers. But where it worked, it rested on bargaining power. Unions did not merely raise wages. They gave workers a say over technological change, job classifications, training, seniority, safety, and grievance procedures. They forced employers to share productivity gains. They also created a political constituency for public investment and social insurance.

The collapse of private-sector union density changed the meaning of technology adoption. In a unionized workplace, a new machine or software system can become the subject of negotiation: How will it affect staffing? Will displaced workers be retrained? Will productivity gains lead to higher wages or shorter hours? How will safety be protected? In a fissured, nonunion workplace, the same technology becomes a unilateral management weapon. It may reduce headcount, increase surveillance, shift risk to subcontractors, or turn employees into “independent contractors” managed by app.

This is why AI policy must be labor policy. It is not enough to fund research into tools that complement workers. Complementarity is not a technical property alone; it is an institutional arrangement. A hospital AI system may complement nurses if it provides better information, reduces paperwork, and supports clinical judgment. It may undermine nurses if it deskills their work, imposes rigid protocols, or justifies understaffing. An educational AI tool may help teachers personalize instruction. It may also become a pretext for larger classes, scripted lessons, or replacing experienced educators with lower-paid monitors. Whether AI complements or substitutes often depends on who has authority when the system is implemented.

Worker voice should therefore be built into the entire life cycle of AI. Before adoption, employers should disclose what systems they plan to use, what data will be collected, what decisions will be automated or influenced, and what labor impacts are expected. During design and procurement, workers should participate in evaluating vendors and identifying risks. After deployment, workers should have rights to explanation, appeal, and correction when algorithmic systems affect discipline, pay, scheduling, promotion, or termination. Independent audits should include worker testimony, not only statistical performance metrics.

The European tradition of works councils offers one model, though not a complete one. In some countries, employees have consultation or co-determination rights over workplace changes. The United States lacks comparable structures for most workers. American labor law is built around collective bargaining after union recognition, but the path to recognition is obstructed by employer opposition, legal delays, and weak penalties. If AI is going to reshape work quickly, workers need rights that do not depend entirely on winning a traditional union election under hostile conditions.

One possibility is sectoral bargaining. AI adoption often occurs across an industry: logistics, retail, health care, education, finance, hospitality. Sector-wide standards could prevent a race to the bottom in which firms compete by using technology to squeeze labor. Wage boards or industry councils could set minimum compensation, training obligations, staffing standards, and data protections. This would also help responsible employers. A firm that wants to use AI to improve service rather than degrade jobs should not be undercut by competitors that use the same technologies for surveillance and speedup.

Public money provides another lever. Government funds AI research, purchases AI systems, subsidizes business investment, and sets tax rules. Every public dollar should come with labor conditions. If a company receives an AI research grant, tax credit, or procurement contract, it should commit to job quality, nondisplacement planning, worker consultation, and limits on surveillance. If a school district buys AI tutoring software, teachers and parents should be involved. If a city buys policing technology, residents and civil-rights groups should have a vote. If a hospital adopts predictive tools, nurses, doctors, technicians, and patients should be part of governance.

Acemoglu is right that the tax code currently encourages replacing workers with software and equipment. But changing tax incentives will not be enough. Firms automate not only because capital is cheap, but because labor is weak. A company facing strong unions, high wage floors, and enforceable job protections may use technology to raise productivity. A company facing atomized workers may use technology to cut labor costs, monitor effort, and avoid responsibility. The same AI revolution will look different under different labor regimes.

The rise of platform work makes this especially urgent. Companies such as Uber, Lyft, DoorDash, Instacart, and Amazon’s delivery network show how algorithmic management can control workers while denying them employee rights. The app sets prices, allocates work, monitors performance, nudges behavior, and can deactivate workers. Yet the company often claims that workers are independent entrepreneurs. This is a legal fiction enabled by software. AI and data analytics make control more granular while making the employment relationship appear less direct.

A democratic AI agenda must close that loophole. Workers managed by algorithms should be presumed employees unless the company can prove genuine independence. Even independent contractors should have rights to collective action, data access, and protection from arbitrary deactivation. Antitrust law should not be used to prevent gig workers from bargaining collectively. Algorithmic management is management, and management must be accountable.

There is also a racial and gender dimension to workplace AI. Many of the workers most exposed to surveillance and displacement are women, immigrants, and people of color: home care workers, retail clerks, warehouse employees, call-center staff, delivery drivers, teachers’ aides, hospital support staff. Algorithmic systems may reproduce discrimination in hiring, scheduling, discipline, and evaluation. But even when bias is reduced, the underlying power imbalance remains. A perfectly “fair” productivity tracker can still be oppressive if it pushes all workers beyond humane limits.

Researchers and engineers have responsibilities, as Acemoglu argues. They should refuse to build systems that enable repression, manipulation, or exploitative labor practices. But worker power cannot depend on the conscience of professionals. Engineers are themselves workers, often under pressure from executives and investors. Some are well paid, but they still lack final authority over business models. Tech worker organizing is promising precisely because it connects ethical refusal to collective action. When employees at large firms protest military contracts, harassment, or climate irresponsibility, they reveal that even inside Silicon Valley the direction of technology is contested.

The goal should not be to freeze the labor market. Some tasks should disappear. Dangerous and degrading work should be automated when possible. But workers affected by these changes deserve more than severance and inspirational speeches about reskilling. They deserve advance notice, retraining on paid time, wage insurance, placement rights, and a share of productivity gains. If AI makes a firm more profitable, workers should benefit through wages, shorter hours, safer conditions, or stronger benefits.

Acemoglu’s warning is timely. AI could produce a society of extraordinary technical capability and miserable work: monitored, insecure, unequal, and politically volatile. Avoiding that future requires more than better design principles. It requires democratizing the workplace. The people who will live with AI every day must have enforceable rights to shape it. Otherwise, “AI for humans” will mean AI for consumers, managers, and shareholders—not for the workers whose labor still holds society together.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The primary problem with AI in the workplace is not automation itself, but the lack of worker agency, as technology is currently used as a unilateral management tool to intensify work and increase surveillance.
  2. 02Complementarity between AI and humans is an institutional arrangement rather than a technical property, meaning the same tool can either empower or deskill workers depending on who holds the power during its implementation.
  3. 03The decline of the postwar social compact and private-sector unions has allowed technology to become a weapon for cutting labor costs rather than a shared engine for productivity and wage growth.
  4. 04Worker voice must be legally integrated into the entire lifecycle of AI, including mandatory disclosure before adoption, participation in procurement, and the right to appeal algorithmic decisions.
  5. 05Public policy should link government AI funding, tax credits, and procurement contracts to strict labor conditions, such as job quality commitments and worker consultation requirements.
  6. 06Labor laws must be updated to close loopholes created by algorithmic management, ensuring that platform workers are recognized as employees with full collective bargaining and data access rights.
  7. 07A democratic AI agenda must address the fact that workplace surveillance and displacement disproportionately affect women, immigrants, and people of color, reinforcing existing power imbalances.
