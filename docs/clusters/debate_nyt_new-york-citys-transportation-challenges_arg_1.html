"The next mayor should prioritize a comprehensive transportation strategy that integrates pedestrian and cyclist safety while addressing geographic inequities through improved bus service and congestion management."

The next mayor should reject the false choice between bikes and pedestrians. Citi Bike is a modest, useful addition to the transportation network, especially for short trips below 59th Street and in Brooklyn, but it cannot be allowed to become the symbol of transportation policy. The larger test is whether streets are designed for people moving at human speed. That means protected bike lanes where they fit, shorter crossing distances, leading pedestrian intervals, stricter speed enforcement and calmer arterials such as Queens Boulevard, Atlantic Avenue and the Grand Concourse. A city where cyclists are separated from fast traffic is also usually a city where pedestrians are safer.

The bigger inequity is geographic. Manhattan already has the densest transit web; many outer-borough residents face two-fare, two-hour commutes. The city should push the MTA and Albany for more Select Bus Service, real bus lanes that are enforced, all-door boarding, signal priority and better crosstown routes that do not force every trip through Manhattan. These improvements are cheaper and faster than new subways, though projects like the Second Avenue subway and East Side Access still matter.

For drivers and the bridge-and-tunnel crowd, pretending that roads can be widened out of congestion is fantasy. Pricing scarce road space—through congestion pricing or more rational bridge tolls—should be back on the table, with revenue dedicated to transit. Freight delivery hours, curb management and parking policy also deserve attention. The goal should not be to favor one mode, but to move more New Yorkers safely and reliably.

  2. 02To address geographic inequities, the city must expand and optimize bus services like Select Bus Service to reduce long commute times for outer-borough residents.
  3. 03The administration should pursue congestion pricing and rationalized bridge tolls to manage road space and generate revenue for transit improvements.
  4. 04Transportation policy should focus on moving people safely and reliably across all modes rather than favoring one specific type of transit.

"The next mayor of New York City should integrate cycling and pedestrian safety into a single infrastructure agenda while prioritizing transit improvements and congestion pricing to address outer-borough commute times."

The framing of this debate—bikeshare versus pedestrian safety—presents a false choice. Citi Bike, launched just months ago, has already logged millions of trips with no fatalities, and the protected bike lanes that accompany its expansion are themselves pedestrian safety infrastructure. Wider medians, shorter crossing distances, and slower turning speeds protect everyone on foot. The next mayor should treat cycling investment and Vision Zero-style street redesign as a single agenda, not competing priorities.

The harder problem is the outer-borough commute. A worker in eastern Queens or southeast Brooklyn can spend ninety minutes each way on a bus-to-subway slog, while a commuter from Westchester rides express rail into Grand Central in forty. Three concrete moves deserve priority. First, accelerate Select Bus Service with genuine physical bus lanes, signal priority, and camera enforcement—Fordham Road and First/Second Avenue have shown this works cheaply and quickly. Second, push the MTA and the next governor to allow CityTicket-style fares on Metro-North and LIRR stops within city limits, unlocking underused commuter rail capacity for places like Co-op City, Rosedale, and eastern Queens. Third, finish the Second Avenue Subway's first phase on schedule and plan Phase 2 seriously.

For the bridge-and-tunnel crowd, the politically painful but honest answer is congestion pricing, in some form resembling the Ravitch or Sam Schwartz plans. It would shorten car commutes, fund transit for those without alternatives, and rationalize a tolling system that currently punishes free-bridge users in the outer boroughs. The next mayor should spend political capital making that case in Albany.

The next mayor will inherit a transportation system that, while remarkable in many respects, has accumulated years of deferred attention to pressing needs beyond Manhattan's core. The question of bikeshare versus pedestrian safety presents a false choice. The Citi Bike program, launched in late May, has shown early promise, but its expansion must go hand in hand with infrastructure that protects all users of the street—cyclists, pedestrians, and drivers alike. The beauty of a robust bikeshare network is its potential to reduce short-hop transit reliance, easing crowding on buses and subways while giving New Yorkers a healthier, more flexible commuting option. But this expansion only makes sense if paired with protected lanes and redesigned intersections that bring down the pedestrian injury rates that have made our streets too dangerous for seniors and children alike.

The harder challenge lies in the outer boroughs, where transit options remain thin and commute times remain stubbornly long. The MTA's capital needs are enormous, and the city must advocate forcefully for state investment in subway reliability and expansion. But the city itself has tools it has underused: better bus service through dedicated lanes, expanded ferry options along the waterfront in Queens and Brooklyn, and zoning incentives that would encourage development around existing transit nodes. The "bridge-and-tunnel" framing suggests this is merely a suburb problem, but hundreds of thousands of New Yorkers living in the Bronx, eastern Queens, and southern Brooklyn face commutes that would be unthinkable to most Manhattan residents.

The next mayor should resist the temptation to treat transportation as a zero-sum game between modes. The goal must be a unified, multimodal system that serves working-class New Yorkers wherever they live, and that makes the city's streets places for people first.

The debate over whether the next mayor should prioritize the expansion of the new bikeshare program or focus on pedestrian safety rests on a false choice. Making streets safer for pedestrians and improving bicycle infrastructure go hand in hand. The “complete streets” approach championed during the Bloomberg administration must be expanded, not rolled back. Dedicated bike lanes and pedestrian plazas naturally calm traffic, reducing the speeding that is the primary threat to pedestrians, while organizing the historically chaotic flow of cars, cyclists, and foot traffic. Citi Bike is a triumph, but to truly serve the city, it must expand alongside physical infrastructure that protects all vulnerable road users.

For the millions of New Yorkers living beyond the current reach of bikeshare and outside Manhattan’s dense subway grid, commute times are the defining transportation crisis. Because large-scale subway expansions are prohibitively expensive and agonizingly slow to build, the next mayor must look to the surface. Expanding Select Bus Service into a true, comprehensive Bus Rapid Transit network is the most cost-effective, immediate way to connect outer-borough transit deserts. By implementing physically separated bus lanes, off-board fare collection, and transit signal priority, the city can drastically reduce commute times in Queens, Brooklyn, the Bronx, and Staten Island without waiting decades for new rail lines.

Finally, we cannot improve commute times for the bridge-and-tunnel crowd—or adequately fund the MTA—without rethinking how we price our road space. The next administration should seriously pursue toll reform, such as the widely discussed "fair pricing" plans. By tolling the currently free East River bridges while lowering tolls on outer-borough crossings that lack viable transit alternatives, the city could dramatically reduce gridlock in the central business district. This would simultaneously speed up travel for those who must drive and generate essential, dedicated revenue to modernize our aging transit infrastructure.

The next mayor must recognize that bikeshare expansion and pedestrian safety are not opposing goals but complementary halves of a safer, more efficient street network. The Citi Bike launch in May 2013 demonstrated enormous latent demand, with over 40,000 annual members in just months and few serious crashes. That success shouldn’t obscure the pedestrian safety crisis: in 2012, 148 pedestrians were killed in traffic, a number largely unchanged for years. Expanding protected bike lanes and calming traffic with curb extensions and slower speed limits directly protects walkers while making cycling feel safer, especially for the less confident riders a larger bikeshare system would attract.

Improving commutes for residents beyond Manhattan and the “bridge-and-tunnel crowd” demands investments that shorten the door-to-door trip, not just subway ride times. The mayor should aggressively scale up Select Bus Service across outer-borough corridors, where dedicated lanes and pre-board payment have cut travel times by up to 20 percent on routes like Fordham Road. More ambitiously, the city should partner with the MTA to restore and expand commuter rail capacity on the Long Island Rail Road and Metro-North, and implement CityTicket pricing all day, not just weekends, to make those trains a viable alternative for neighborhoods far from the subway. Ferries are not a panacea, but a thoughtfully planned East River route expansion, with full MetroCard integration, could relieve pressure on chronically crowded lines like the 7 train. These steps would signal that the new administration takes seriously a city where more than half of workers commute from outside Manhattan.

"The next mayor must prioritize a comprehensive street design policy that treats bike shares, pedestrian safety, and public transit as integrated solutions for managing limited urban space and reducing car dependency."

The next mayor should reject the premise that bike share and pedestrian safety are competing priorities. They are both part of the same unfinished project: making streets function as public space rather than as speedways for private cars. Citi Bike, only a few months old, has already shown that thousands of short trips can be shifted away from taxis, subways and cars. That is a public benefit, not a lifestyle amenity. But the program will only succeed if riders and walkers are protected by a coherent street design policy.

New York’s street safety problem is not caused by bicycles. It is caused mainly by speed, chaotic intersections, wide avenues and weak enforcement of dangerous driving. The next mayor should continue the Bloomberg administration’s best transportation innovations — protected bike lanes, pedestrian plazas, Select Bus Service and data-driven redesigns of dangerous corridors — while making them less ad hoc and more citywide. The Department of Transportation should publish a list of the most dangerous intersections and corridors and redesign them on a schedule: shorter crossings, leading pedestrian intervals, protected bike lanes where feasible, narrower travel lanes, more refuge islands and stricter rules for turning vehicles.

The larger transportation challenge, however, is that too many New Yorkers outside Manhattan have commutes that are slow, indirect and unreliable. Subway extensions are desirable but expensive and slow to build. The faster answer is a real bus program. Select Bus Service should be expanded well beyond the current handful of corridors, with off-board fare payment, camera-enforced bus lanes, signal priority and better shelters. The bus system is the subway of many outer-borough neighborhoods, yet it is treated as second-class transportation.

For commuters coming from outside the city, the honest answer is road pricing. The current toll structure perversely charges some crossings and leaves others free, pushing traffic onto already congested streets. A rational system would lower some outer-borough bridge tolls while charging the East River bridges, dedicating the revenue to transit improvements. Albany has blocked congestion pricing before, but the mayor should spend political capital on it because no package of signal tweaks will solve gridlock.

New York cannot build its way out of congestion with more road space. It can only manage scarce street space more intelligently. That means more bike share, safer walking, faster buses and a pricing system that tells drivers the truth about the cost of their trips.

"The next mayor of New York City should move away from anti-car rhetoric and instead adopt a practical, comprehensive traffic management strategy that balances the needs of drivers, transit users, and pedestrians."

New York’s next mayor should begin by acknowledging an unpopular fact: cars and trucks are not going away. Millions of people and enormous quantities of goods enter, leave and move around the city every day. Some of those trips can shift to transit or bicycles, but many cannot. A transportation policy that treats every driver as a villain will not make the streets safer or the commutes shorter; it will only deepen resentment and produce worse politics.

That does not mean giving drivers everything they want. In fact, the current system is bad for drivers too. The “free” East River bridges are not free in any meaningful sense; their price is paid in gridlock, wasted fuel, delayed buses and angry neighborhoods. Trucks circle blocks searching for loading space. Taxis crawl through Midtown. Parents double-park because there is no rational curb management. Bridge-and-tunnel commuters sit in traffic and then are blamed for congestion that the city has failed to manage.

The next mayor should pursue a practical bargain. First, rationalize tolls and dedicate the money visibly to transportation. The city and state should revive some form of congestion pricing or bridge toll reform, but pair it with improvements that drivers can see: better-maintained roads, clearer signage, coordinated traffic signals and safer intersections. If people are asked to pay, they deserve a system that works.

Second, manage the curb. Delivery zones, taxi stands, bus stops, bike lanes and parking cannot all be improvised block by block. Commercial deliveries should be encouraged at off-peak hours where possible, and illegal parking that blocks buses or crosswalks should be enforced consistently, including against city employees and placarded vehicles. A bus lane blocked by a parked car is not a bus lane.

Third, expand commuter options without pretending everyone will ride a bicycle. Express buses, park-and-ride lots near transit hubs outside the city core, ferry service where it is cost-effective, and better Metro-North, Long Island Rail Road and New Jersey Transit connections all matter. The “bridge-and-tunnel crowd” includes workers, caregivers, contractors and visitors, not just suburbanites seeking cheap parking.

Finally, slow traffic where people live and walk. Pedestrian safety is not anti-car; it is pro-civilization. A city where drivers know the rules, pedestrians can cross safely, buses move reliably and bikes have predictable space will function better for everyone. The next mayor should be less interested in culture war and more interested in traffic management.

  2. 02The city must implement rational curb management to reduce gridlock caused by delivery trucks, taxis, and illegal parking.
  3. 03Commuter options should be expanded through improved express buses, ferry services, and better regional rail connections rather than relying solely on bicycle infrastructure.
  4. 04Pedestrian safety measures should be prioritized as a functional necessity of traffic management rather than as a punitive measure against drivers.
  5. 05Consistent enforcement of parking and traffic rules must apply to everyone, including city employees and those with official placards, to ensure bus lanes and crosswalks remain clear.

Framing the question as “bikeshare or pedestrian safety” betrays an impoverished imagination about what a great city street can be. The next mayor need not choose one over the other; the task is to knit them together into an integrated mobility system that serves everyone—from the senior woman crossing Queens Boulevard to the NJ Transit rider making the last mile to her office. That vision demands investments on three fronts: safe streets, a fully built-out bikeshare network, and dramatically faster buses for the periphery.

Safe streets are the foundation. The next mayor should immediately direct the DOT to implement a “Complete Streets” template on every major arterial, not just a few showpiece plazas. When a street like Atlantic Avenue gets a road diet, cyclists gain separation from traffic, crossing distances shrink for pedestrians, and the predictable result is fewer deaths. The goal should be a unified network stretching from Coney Island to Co-op City, with docking stations located at every major transit node—subway stops, LIRR stations, ferry terminals. That turns Citi Bike into a feeder system for regional rail, bridging the so-called last mile that plagues outer-borough and suburban commuters. None of this works without tackling the slowness of surface transit. The mayor must push Select Bus Service far more aggressively, converting painted lanes into physically separated rights-of-way on at least a dozen high-demand corridors. When buses fly, the 90-minute cross-borough commutes that exhaust working families can shrink to something humane. Pair that with a network of safe bike and walk paths to reach the buses, and you’ve built a comprehensive alternative to car dependency.

The next mayor can—and must—do all three. A city of eight million cannot afford the false choices of the past. The streets belong to no single mode; they belong to a New York that moves together.

New York's transportation crisis cannot be solved by choosing favorites among competing modes. The next mayor must embrace a comprehensive approach that addresses the interrelated challenges of capacity, safety, and equity across all five boroughs.

Consider the options on the table: Bikeshare programs reduce short-distance car trips and subway crowding while offering healthy, affordable transportation. Pedestrian safety improvements make streets livable and encourage walking. Better outer-borough service addresses the needs of residents who live far from Manhattan's transit richness. Second, continued expansion of the ferry network—a pilot program is already showing promise and deserves scaling. Third, thoughtful bikeshare growth, prioritized in neighborhoods with good cycling topography and connecting to transit stations. Fourth, a Vision Zero-style commitment to reducing traffic deaths through engineering, enforcement, and education.

The funding exists if the will is there. Congestion pricing discussions have stalled, but the next mayor should revive them. Federal grants for infrastructure are available. And redirecting some police overtime budgets toward traffic enforcement would cost little while saving lives.

The "bridge-and-tunnel" question deserves special attention. Regional coordination with New Jersey and Connecticut on transit is essential—New York cannot solve this alone. But the city can improve its own infrastructure for arriving commuters, from better signage to expanded ferry service to reduced-fare zones that make driving unnecessary.

The mayor who treats transportation as a zero-sum game will fail. The mayor who sees connections—who understands that safe streets encourage walking, that bikes connect to trains, that outer-borough residents drive too often because alternatives are lacking—that mayor can build a transportation system worthy of a global city.

New York should reject the premise that bike sharing and pedestrian safety are competing causes. A street safe enough for an eight-year-old to cross is usually safe enough for a cyclist to use, and a street filled with cyclists is usually less dominated by fast private cars. The next mayor should build a real network of these routes on Woodhaven Boulevard, Utica Avenue, Webster Avenue, Hylan Boulevard and other outer-borough corridors.

Subway extensions are wonderful but slow and expensive. Street redesign is fast. If the next mayor wants safer streets and shorter commutes, the answer is not bike share versus pedestrians versus drivers. It is to stop treating the curb lane, the bus lane and the crosswalk as afterthoughts, and to manage them as the essential public infrastructure they are.
