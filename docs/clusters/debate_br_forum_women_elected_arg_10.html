"The United States should move beyond its limited negative-rights view of equality by utilizing existing party structures and state-level federalism to implement affirmative measures that ensure women's political representation."

Jennifer Piscopo’s sweeping historical argument arrives at a moment of extraordinary potential. On the centennial of the Nineteenth Amendment, we are simultaneously celebrating a woman of color’s ascent to a major-party vice-presidential ticket and staring at a Congress that remains three-quarters male. Piscopo is right: the United States has clung to a cramped, negative-rights vision of political equality, and it is long past time to embrace the positive action that has transformed legislatures across the globe. But making that case to American audiences requires not simply importing the Latin American model wholesale; it demands we recognize that the architecture of U.S. federalism and party structures already offers on-ramps for exactly the kind of affirmative measures she champions.

The most obvious entry point is the Democratic Party’s own internal rules. For decades, the party has mandated gender balance on its national committees and convention delegations, even as it studiously avoids the word “quota.” This semantic dance is instructive. It reveals a deep unease with group rights language that, as Piscopo notes, collides with the country’s tortured racial history. Yet the practical outcome is already halfway there: the party has normalized the idea that women’s presence in leadership must be deliberately constructed, not merely hoped for. The next step—voluntary party quotas for candidate slates—is smaller than it appears. State party committees could, without any new legislation, adopt rules requiring that a certain percentage of endorsed or recruited candidates be women. In a candidate-centered system, recruitment and money matter enormously, and local party leaders still serve as gatekeepers. Changing their incentives, as Sweden’s Social Democrats and South Africa’s ANC have done, could reshape the pipeline without triggering the constitutional litigation that a government-imposed quota would inevitably invite.

But federalism offers even more fertile ground. Emily Zackin has shown that state constitutions are thick with positive rights—guarantees to education, workers’ compensation, and environmental protection—that would startle anyone raised on the U.S. Constitution’s negative liberties. A handful of states could become laboratories for gender parity in representation. For instance, states could reduce or waive ballot filing fees for women candidates, a targeted intervention that directly eases the financial burden that research shows falls more heavily on women. California’s top-two primary system, which often leaves two Democrats competing in the general election, creates a space in which party endorsements carry less weight and candidate quality can shine—exactly the conditions under which a “performance premium” for women candidates can translate into real seat gains. Other states might follow the lead of cities that have experimented with public campaign financing, structuring those funds to offer matching bonuses to candidates from underrepresented groups. None of these mechanisms would need to mention gender quotas by name, but they would operate in the same spirit Piscopo traces from Argentina to Senegal: a recognition that formal equality is hollow without affirmative steps to dismantle structural barriers.

A persistent challenge, which Piscopo acknowledges but does not fully resolve, is the intersection of race and gender. In the United States, any positive action for women that does not simultaneously address the exclusion of Black, Indigenous, and other women of color risks replicating the very hierarchies the Nineteenth Amendment’s legacy already embodies. The 2018 midterm wave was powered in no small part by women of color, yet even now, white women hold the overwhelming majority of seats women occupy. Any quota or candidate-support program designed without race-conscious metrics would almost certainly benefit those already closest to power. The solutions, however, are not out of reach. Ranked-choice voting in multi-member districts—a reform already gaining traction in states like Maine and cities like New York—has been shown to increase the election of women and people of color simultaneously, without fixed set-asides. Coupling voluntary party targets with explicit race-conscious recruitment, as the Democratic Party has done with its delegate selection rules, moves in the right direction. The point is not to treat gender and race as competing claims, but to recognize, as Piscopo’s own account of suffrage history makes clear, that they have always been entwined.

Skeptics will say that any move toward positive action imports a foreign logic into a resistant political culture. But Piscopo’s essay reminds us that the very definition of citizenship as the “right to elect and be elected” was once deployed to exclude; it was feminist activists who repurposed that language into a demand for presence. In the United States, the rhetoric has been different, but the raw materials for a similar transformation are already scattered across our statehouses, party rulebooks, and municipal charters. The centennial need not be just a commemoration of a partial victory. It can be the moment we finally admit that the right to run is not enough, and that democracy’s promise requires building the conditions under which women can not only step forward, but be seated at the table.
