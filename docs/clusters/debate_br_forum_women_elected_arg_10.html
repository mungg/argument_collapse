"The United States should pursue gender parity in elected office through a federalist strategy of incremental, localized reforms across cities, states, and political parties rather than waiting for a national constitutional mandate."

Jennifer Piscopo’s essay invites Americans to learn from countries that have treated women’s underrepresentation as a democratic failure rather than an unfortunate social fact. That lesson is welcome. But the American route to parity will probably be less national, less constitutional, and more experimental than the examples of Mexico, Argentina, France, or Rwanda suggest. If the United States is going to discover a right to be elected, it will likely do so through federalism: city by city, state by state, party by party, office by office.

This may sound modest, but it is how many American democratic reforms have begun. Women voted in several western states before the Nineteenth Amendment. Direct democracy, public education guarantees, labor protections, nonpartisan local elections, campaign finance experiments, and ranked-choice voting all developed unevenly across jurisdictions. The federal Constitution is difficult to amend and often interpreted conservatively. State constitutions and local charters, by contrast, can be laboratories of democratic design. Piscopo cites Emily Zackin’s work on positive rights in state constitutions; that point deserves to be put at the center of the strategy.

The first advantage of a federalist approach is legal flexibility. A national gender quota for Congress would face daunting constitutional challenges and political opposition. But state and local governments have more room to regulate their own institutions, especially appointed boards, commissions, party procedures for public financing, and local electoral systems. Cities can require gender balance on certain public boards. States can design public financing rules that reward parties or candidates for broad recruitment. Local governments can adopt ranked-choice voting or multimember districts. State parties can write gender targets into their bylaws. Each reform may be small, but together they can change norms.

The second advantage is visibility. National representation captures attention, but many political careers begin below Congress. State legislatures remain crucial. Governors, attorneys general, secretaries of state, mayors, county executives, prosecutors, sheriffs, judges, school board members, and city councilors make decisions that affect millions of lives. They also form the pool from which future members of Congress and presidents are drawn. If women are underrepresented in these offices, the national pipeline narrows.

At the same time, the “pipeline” should not be understood as a natural stream that carries talent upward. It is built by institutions. Local party committees decide whom to groom. Civic associations decide whom to invite. Donors decide whom to take seriously. Newspapers decide whose campaign is viable. Incumbents decide whom to mentor. When these actors are mostly men, or when they imagine leadership in masculine terms, the pipeline leaks by design. State and local reforms can force these actors to widen their field of vision.

What might such reforms look like? Start with data. Every state should publish regular reports on the gender and racial composition of candidates for state and local office, nominees, winners, appointed officials, party committee members, and recipients of public campaign funds. Some of this information is scattered and incomplete; much of it is not collected in a way that allows accountability. Measurement is not transformation, but it reveals where the bottlenecks are. Are women running but losing primaries? Are they not running for county offices? Are they concentrated in school boards but absent from prosecutorial races? Are women of color excluded from party endorsements? Different problems require different tools.

Next, states with public financing systems should build equity into them. Rather than lowering filing fees only for women, which may raise legal and fairness concerns, states could provide seed grants or matching funds to candidates who demonstrate grassroots support. They could allow campaign funds to cover childcare, eldercare, and disability-related accessibility costs. They could offer additional debate access or public voter-guide space to candidates who qualify through small donations rather than large checks. These reforms would help women because women are more likely to face caregiving burdens and less likely to have access to wealthy donor networks, but they would do so through need- and democracy-based criteria.

Parties should adopt gender-balanced recruitment rules for open seats. The phrase “open seat” matters. Challenging incumbents is difficult, and many incumbents are men. But when an office opens, there is no democratic reason for party leaders to default to the familiar male successor. State parties could require that any official screening committee interview a gender-balanced and racially diverse pool before issuing endorsements. They could refuse early endorsements in open primaries unless recruitment standards are met. They could commit resources to first-time candidates rather than only to those blessed by local insiders.

Local governments should also reconsider electoral structures. Single-member districts can improve neighborhood representation, but they may also entrench incumbents and limit the number of viable opportunities. At-large elections have their own dangers, especially for racial minorities, because cohesive majorities can sweep all seats. But carefully designed multimember districts with ranked-choice voting can allow more diverse representation without racial vote dilution. Cities are well suited to test these models. If successful, states can adapt them for legislatures.

One underappreciated arena is appointments. Governors, mayors, county executives, and legislative leaders appoint thousands of people to boards and commissions. These positions regulate utilities, transportation, zoning, education, health, policing, ethics, and economic development. They also create credentials for future candidates. Gender balance requirements for appointed bodies are common in some democracies and easier to justify legally than candidate quotas. If executives can appoint their friends and donors, surely they can be required to consider whether public bodies reflect the public.

State constitutions may offer another opening. Many contain equality provisions, free elections clauses, education rights, and language that has no exact federal analogue. State courts have sometimes interpreted these provisions more expansively than federal courts interpret the U.S. Constitution. Advocates could test whether severe gender disparities, combined with evidence of discriminatory party practices or election rules, violate state commitments to equal political participation. Litigation should not be the only strategy, and courts may be reluctant. But state constitutionalism offers more possibilities than a federal judiciary likely to be skeptical of group-based remedies.

Piscopo’s international examples also suggest the importance of norm entrepreneurs: activists, lawyers, legislators, and judges who make a new idea plausible. The United States needs such actors, but they need not begin with Congress. A city council member can introduce a gender-balanced appointments ordinance. A state legislator can require campaign finance reports to include demographic transparency. A secretary of state can publish candidate data. A state party chair can adopt recruitment targets. A governor can pledge parity in appointments. A local newspaper can track whether county parties are recruiting women. A civic organization can rate parties on candidate diversity.

The federal government still matters. Congress could strengthen voting rights, regulate money in politics, protect candidates from threats, support childcare, and make Election Day administration more equitable. But in the current political environment, national parity legislation is unlikely. A federalist strategy does not wait for Washington. It creates facts on the ground.

There are risks. Local reforms can be symbolic, uneven, or captured by elites. Gender balance on boards may benefit professional-class white women while leaving working-class women and women of color outside. Public financing can be underfunded. Party targets can be ignored. Data can be collected and forgotten. For this reason, every gender reform should be paired with racial, class, and transparency analysis. “More women” cannot mean merely more women already connected to power.

Still, the perfect should not become the enemy of the possible. Piscopo’s most important challenge is imaginative. Americans often assume that because our institutions are old, they are fixed; because quotas sound foreign, no positive action is possible; because women can legally run, the remaining disparity is regrettable but not democratic failure. Federalism gives reformers a way around that fatalism. It permits experimentation without requiring immediate national consensus.

The United States does not need to copy another country’s quota law to learn from the global parity movement. It needs to absorb the premise that democracy is judged not only by who may cast a ballot, but by who can plausibly wield power. That premise can be written into state party bylaws, city charters, public financing rules, appointment processes, and local election systems. The right to be elected may enter American politics not as a single constitutional thunderclap, but as a thousand smaller reforms that slowly make exclusion harder to defend.
