Diversified Gemini

"Nancy Hirschmann’s proposal to treat childcare as a state-compensated public good is dangerous because it provides a rationale for totalitarian state control over the private family."

Nancy J. Hirschmann’s "Mothers Who Care Too Much" inadvertently exposes the totalitarian underbelly of certain strains of progressive feminist thought. While ostensibly writing an essay about the division of household labor, Hirschmann casually outlines a rationale for massive state surveillance and control over the private family, a logical progression that should alarm anyone who values civil liberties and a free society.

The most disturbing section of Hirschmann’s essay is her exploration of what it means to conceptualize care as a "public good" deserving of state compensation. She notes that feminists have long argued that mothers should receive independent public assistance, social security credits, and health care because they are raising the "next generation of citizens." But Hirschmann, with chilling clarity, points out the unavoidable corollary: if the state is paying for a service, it has the right to demand results.

"If care is a public good, then shouldn’t there be public oversight?" she asks. She argues that if society is paying women to raise citizens, "the public has a right to make sure that it is getting the service it is paying for."

Let us pause to consider what this actually means. If the state subsidizes motherhood based on the "social value" of the work, the state must define what constitutes a socially valuable outcome. Hirschmann wonders aloud what we should do when children turn out to be "drug dealers, prostitutes, or, worse yet, CEOs who sell out their shareholders." She asks, seemingly in earnest, "Do we send the mothers to jail along with their children?"

While she ultimately recoils from the idea of "housework police," the very fact that her theoretical framework leads directly to such authoritarian questions demonstrates the inherent danger of her premises. When you collectivize the costs of child-rearing, you inevitably collectivize the control over it.

The family, in the classical liberal tradition, serves as a crucial buffer between the individual and the state. It is an independent sphere of private life where individuals can transmit their own values, beliefs, and traditions to their children. This pluralism is messy. As Hirschmann rightly notes, it means some parents will teach their children narrow-minded or selfish values. But the alternative—a state that dictates the "right kind" of care and censures others, ensuring parents are producing state-approved "civic contributors"—is the definition of a totalitarian nightmare.

Hirschmann admits that the goal of care feminists may have been exactly this all along: "to promote the 'right kind' of care, to promote the values we endorse, and to censure other kinds of caring." This reveals a breathtaking arrogance. Who gets to define the "right kind" of values? The prevailing political majority? A panel of feminist theorists?

The hostility toward the private family runs deep in this essay. Hirschmann worries that families are a locus of "selfish and self-indulgent rationalization." She laments that parents receive "goods" from their children (love, joy, intimacy) that cannot be cashed out in terms of social value, and she genuinely flirts with the idea that the state is entitled to "tax mothers for the love they receive." This sounds less like political theory and more like a dystopian science fiction novel where human affection is viewed as an untaxed luxury good that must be regulated by the collective.

Furthermore, she complains that women who homeschool their children are depriving them of the "jostlings of equality" necessary for citizenship. Here again, the assumption is that children belong primarily to the state and the community, and that parents who choose to educate their children outside the state apparatus are somehow derelict in their public duty.

Hirschmann's essay should serve as a stark warning. The attempt to solve the domestic division of labor by turning child-rearing into state-compensated, socially-evaluated labor destroys the autonomy of the family. A free society must accept that parents have the primary right and responsibility to raise their children according to their own dictates, not as contractors performing a service for the state. If the price of feminist "equality" is the state sitting in judgment of how much we love our children and what values we teach them, the price is far too high.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01If the state subsidizes motherhood based on social value, it gains the authoritarian right to oversee, define, and demand specific 'state-approved' outcomes for child-rearing.
  2. 02The family must remain an independent sphere of private life to serve as a crucial buffer between the individual and the state.
  3. 03Collectivizing the costs of child-rearing inevitably leads to the collective control of children, treating them as assets of the state rather than members of a private family.
  4. 04Viewing human affection and parental joy as untaxed luxury goods or 'transferable social values' reduces intimate human relationships to cold, dystopian economic metrics.
  5. 05The price of achieving domestic equality through state-compensated labor is too high if it requires the government to sit in judgment of a parent's values and personal affection.
