Vanilla Gemini

"While Aziz Rana correctly identifies that freedom must be reclaimed through domestic institution-building rather than mere messaging, his insistence on linking this project to a radical anti-imperialist and open-border agenda is a strategic error that risks alienating the working-class coalition needed for success."

Aziz Rana’s compelling essay provides a necessary corrective to the Democratic Party’s assumption that reclaiming the concept of "freedom" is merely a matter of better messaging. By tracing the right-wing, hyper-individualistic, and exclusionary version of American freedom to its roots in settler colonialism and imperial expansion, Rana convincingly demonstrates that our political vocabulary is downstream of our material history. If freedom in America has historically meant the right of insiders to dominate outsiders—subsidized by expropriated land and global hegemony—then it cannot be "taken back" with a clever speech. As Rana rightfully concludes, redefining freedom as an inclusive, solidaristic practice requires rebuilding the actual institutions, particularly in the workplace, where Americans might experience collective self-rule.

However, while Rana’s diagnosis of domestic institutional decay is acute, his prescription for how to rebuild this solidaristic freedom relies on a perilous political calculation. By inextricably linking the domestic revitalization of labor and democracy to a maximalist agenda of anti-imperialism, defense cuts, and the effective decriminalization of the border, Rana risks alienating the very working-class coalition necessary to build the institutions he champions. In treating the left’s domestic and global projects as an indivisible package, he overlooks the pragmatic, often messy sequencing required to forge solidarity in a fractured society.

Rana is at his strongest when analyzing the American workplace. He correctly identifies that decades of neoliberalism and Reaganite cultural dominance have stripped away the sites where collective agency is practiced. The atomized American worker of 2023, subject to the arbitrary whims of corporate hierarchies and algorithmic scheduling, experiences a profound lack of self-rule. When Rana argues that strengthening unions and passing legislation like the PRO Act are fundamental experiments in linking freedom to democracy, the events of this past year prove him right. The resurgent labor militancy we have witnessed—most notably the United Auto Workers’ historic strike against the Big Three automakers this autumn—demonstrates exactly what solidaristic freedom looks like in practice. Workers standing on picket lines, demanding a fair share of the wealth they create, and securing historic wage increases do not view freedom as a zero-sum competition against their neighbors. They experience it as a shared triumph.

But friction emerges when Rana attempts to map this domestic blueprint onto global geopolitics. He asserts that we cannot expect an inclusive version of freedom at home if the United States pursues imperial projects abroad. Consequently, he calls for a radical reorientation of foreign policy: halving the $900 billion defense budget and instituting "presumptive admission for all" at the border.

The historical precedent Rana uses to support this linkage is selectively optimistic. He points to the pre-New Deal labor movement as a moment when the collapse of the settler frontier forced white Americans to embrace shared responsibility and cross-racial solidarity. Yet, the history of American labor is also a history of fierce protectionism. Many of the unionists who fought bloodily for the eight-hour day and workplace democracy simultaneously lobbied for the Chinese Exclusion Act and subsequent restrictive immigration laws, viewing open borders not as an expression of universal solidarity, but as a weapon wielded by capitalists to flood the labor market, depress wages, and break strikes. Solidarity, historically, has often required defined boundaries.

Today, arguing for the decriminalization of entry and presumptive admission for all ignores the real material anxieties of the domestic working class. In late 2023, as municipalities across the country struggle to manage the logistics and fiscal strains of surging migrant populations, framing open immigration as an essential pillar of working-class freedom is a hard sell. It risks recreating the exact zero-sum mentality Rana critiques, where local communities feel their underfunded schools and social services are placed in direct competition with the needs of newcomers. To ask a precarious American public to embrace a borderless vision of universal solidarity before they have secured the domestic institutions that guarantee their own stability is to put the cart before the horse.

A similar dynamic applies to foreign policy. While Rana rightly points out the disastrous consequences of the post-9/11 wars in Iraq and Afghanistan, and the deep moral tensions of unconditional military support abroad—highlighted acutely by the current devastation in Gaza—he assumes that a rapid American retreat from global primacy will naturally yield domestic prosperity and democratic renewal. But the global economy is deeply entangled. A sudden contraction of American security commitments could trigger supply chain disruptions, energy crises, and economic shocks that would disproportionately hurt the American working class. When people feel materially insecure, they do not turn to the left’s vision of inclusive solidarity; they retreat into the exclusionary, fortress-mentality freedom of the right.

Rana is entirely correct that freedom must be an ongoing practice of collective self-rule, and that the workplace is the most vital arena for this struggle. But by insisting that domestic workplace democracy must be ideologically yoked to a radical dismantling of American geopolitical power and border enforcement, he creates an impossibly steep hill for the left to climb.

If we are to organically integrate an emancipatory vision of freedom into American life, we must begin where people actually live and work. We must prioritize the revitalization of the labor movement and the protection of the ballot box. We need to demonstrate that collective action can deliver higher wages, secure pensions, and democratic control over daily life. Only after Americans have rebuilt the muscle memory of local solidarity, and feel confident in their own material security, can we successfully invite them to extend that solidarity to the rest of the globe. Reclaiming freedom is indeed a project of institution building, but those institutions must be built on the solid ground of domestic security, not the shifting sands of global utopianism.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The recent success of labor militancy, such as the UAW strike, proves that the workplace is the most effective and resonant arena for Americans to experience freedom as collective self-rule.
  2. 02Rana’s call for open borders ignores the historical reality that labor solidarity has often relied on defined boundaries to prevent capital from depressing wages and undermining worker power.
  3. 03Advocating for the effective decriminalization of the border during a period of domestic precarity risks triggering the very zero-sum, exclusionary mentality that the left seeks to overcome.
  4. 04A rapid dismantling of American global primacy could cause economic shocks and supply chain disruptions that would disproportionately harm the working class, driving them toward reactionary politics rather than solidaristic freedom.
  5. 05The left must prioritize domestic material security and the revitalization of labor unions as a necessary prerequisite before attempting to expand the vision of solidarity to a global scale.
