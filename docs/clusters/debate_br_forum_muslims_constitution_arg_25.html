"The post-9/11 national security state should be challenged as a universal threat to constitutional liberty and executive overreach rather than being framed primarily as a matter of anti-Muslim discrimination."

It is a bitter irony that the very moment we finally summon the will to debate the national security state, we risk narrowing the conversation to a single axis of injustice. Akbar and Theoharis have written a powerful indictment of the war on terror’s impact on American Muslims, and I share their alarm at how easily bipartisan elites have ratified programs that trample basic rights. But by framing the problem principally as anti-Muslim discrimination, they have inadvertently made it smaller than it is. The machinery of surveillance, secret evidence, preemptive prosecution, and indefinite detention was not built to oppress Muslims alone. It was built to expand executive power over all of us—Muslims, yes, but also journalists, activists, antiwar organizers, environmental protesters, and ordinary citizens whose only crime is being digitally connected. To fixate on the identity of the primary targets today is to miss the structural danger, and to craft solutions that will leave the surveillance state poised to identify its next enemy tomorrow.

Let us recall what the Snowden disclosures actually revealed. The National Security Agency was not, as the lead essay suggests, merely subjecting Muslims to “the brunt force” of a surveillance apparatus that also collected data on everyone else. It was, in fact, vacuuming up the private communications of millions of law-abiding Americans with no tie to any foreign threat. The Section 215 bulk telephone metadata program required phone companies to hand over records of every call—numbers dialed, call duration, time—so that the NSA could search for patterns. The PRISM program compelled tech giants to share the contents of emails, chats, and video calls. These were not targeted operations against a specific community; they were blanket intrusions into the intimate lives of the whole populace. The legal justification for this apparatus—an expansive reading of the USA PATRIOT Act, secret FISA court rulings that reinterpreted key terms, and the executive branch’s assertion of Article II war powers—established a precedent that can be weaponized against any group the government deems a threat. Today it is “radical Islamic terrorism”; tomorrow it could be “domestic extremism” covering Black Lives Matter or right-wing militia groups; the next day it could be “climate activists” threatening critical infrastructure.

The lead essay acknowledges, but does not grapple with, the reality that the courts have largely rubber-stamped these expansive powers. It urges us to “draw the line around a host of other injustices that have been legalized in the past two decades.” But that line-drawing is impossible if we accept the premise that the fundamental problem is Islamophobia. If Congress were to pass a new law tomorrow that added robust anti-discrimination protections for Muslims but left the bulk collection, the material support statute, and the secret FISA court untouched, the infrastructure of mass surveillance would remain—ready to pivot. In fact, the very reforms that followed the Snowden revelations, specifically the USA FREEDOM Act, were a partial fix that ended bulk collection but expanded other surveillance authorities and left the underlying philosophy of preemptive intelligence-gathering fully intact. A truly rights-respecting society would reject the entire paradigm of warrantless mass spying, regardless of whose metadata is being hoovered up.

Consider the material support statute. The lead essay rightly notes that it has been used primarily against Muslims. But its logic—criminalizing association and speech under the banner of preventing a future crime—is a direct assault on the First Amendment that threatens all dissent. The Humanitarian Law Project ruling, which held that even advising a designated terrorist group on nonviolent dispute resolution could be prosecuted, opens the door to criminalizing any coordination with disfavored foreign groups. If a peace activist wants to meet with FARC rebels in Colombia to discuss human rights, or an AIDS worker wants to train a Hezbollah-linked health clinic, she is exposed to prosecution. The law is not narrowly tailored; it is a censor’s charter wrapped in a security blanket. Opposing it only because it hits Muslims means defending it when it does not.

The same universalizing logic must apply to the No Fly List, secret evidence, and the pretrial conditions of terrorism suspects. These are not specialized tools for dealing with an “exceptional” threat; they are deviations from the ordinary criminal justice process that can and do migrate. The classified evidence procedures that were developed for Guantánamo and for federal terrorism trials became the model for how the government handles sensitive information in gang prosecutions and even civil litigation. The “special administrative measures” that subject certain prisoners to near-total isolation and communication bans were designed for accused terrorists; they are now used against non-terrorism defendants whom the Bureau of Prisons deems especially dangerous, including organized crime figures and drug kingpins. Once the exception becomes normal, everyone is vulnerable. The only way to prevent that migration is to challenge the exceptionalism itself, not to argue that the wrong people are being exceptionalized.

I want to be clear: none of this is to diminish the specific suffering of American Muslims, who have been the canary in the coal mine. My argument is that defending the canary while reinforcing the mine shaft is a fool’s errand. The airport protests against the Muslim Ban were a moment of genuine popular courage, and I participated in them proudly. But I also remember, not long before, the loud silence from much of the same liberal coalition when the Obama administration was prosecuting whistleblowers, expanding the drone program, and secretly interpreting the PATRIOT Act to permit domestic surveillance that Congress had not explicitly authorized. Too many good people were willing to give a Democratic president a pass because they trusted him to use the tools wisely. That trust was always misplaced, and Trump is the proof. The powers we grant to a benevolent sovereign will inevitably fall into the hands of a malevolent one.

The way forward is not to demand a more courteous anti-Muslim policy. It is to demand the dismantling of the entire post-9/11 security edifice. We need a full repeal of Section 702 of the FISA Amendments Act, which authorizes warrantless surveillance of foreign targets and sweeps up massive amounts of American communications. We need a sunset of the material support provisions as currently written, and their replacement with a statute that requires actual proof of intent to further specific acts of violence. We need an end to secret FISA court rulings; all significant interpretations of surveillance law must be public and subject to adversarial review. We need an independent civilian review board for the intelligence community with full subpoena power. And we need to treat the right to privacy as a universal right, not a privilege subject to the government’s determination of who is suspicious.

The lead essay’s call to link the war on drugs and the war on terror is a step in the right direction, but it still frames the linkage in terms of shared racial impact. The deeper link is structural: both wars depend on the erosion of Fourth Amendment protections, the expansion of surveillance, the militarization of police, and the assumption that some parts of the population can be indefinitely managed rather than governed with consent. Breaking that link means not just connecting the movements but advancing a positive agenda for privacy, due process, and accountability that protects everyone. When we fight the surveillance state as an issue of universal liberty, we build coalitions that are harder to fracture. The Trump era will be a long assault on constitutional norms. Our defense must be as broad as the threat itself.
