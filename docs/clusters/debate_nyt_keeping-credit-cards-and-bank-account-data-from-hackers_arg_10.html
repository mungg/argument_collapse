"Securing financial data requires a shift from focusing solely on domestic corporate security to a comprehensive strategy involving international deterrence, rapid attribution, and consumer protection."

The JPMorgan and Home Depot breaches share a common feature that often gets lost in the focus on corporate security failures: the attacks originated from overseas actors operating beyond the reach of American law enforcement. No amount of domestic security investment fully addresses a threat from international criminal networks with state sanctuary.

Attributing these attacks matters. The JPMorgan breach has been linked to Russian-linked hacking groups with possible state connections. The Home Depot intrusion bears hallmarks of Eastern European criminal operations. These actors do not fear prosecution from countries where they operate, and international extradition for financial crimes remains extraordinarily difficult.

This reality demands a two-track response. Domestically, we must improve security standards, yes—but we also need credible deterrence. The Obama administration should make clear that cyberattacks on financial infrastructure carry real consequences, including targeted sanctions against individuals and entities involved, diplomatic isolation of sanctuary states, and potentially proportional responses in cyberspace.

Congress should also authorize and fund expanded capabilities for attributing cyberattacks. Speed matters here; identifying attackers quickly enables both legal action and diplomatic pressure before stolen data can be fully monetized.

On the consumer side, we must accept that some fraud is unavoidable given current threat levels. The financial system should guarantee zero liability for consumers victimized by data breaches, eliminating the current uneven protections that sometimes leave consumers holding losses. Banks have insurance and scale to manage this risk; individual consumers do not.

International cooperation remains essential but underemphasized. Treaty negotiations on cyber norms, information sharing agreements with allied intelligence services, and coordinated law enforcement operations all require sustained attention and resources. This is fundamentally a foreign policy challenge wearing domestic clothing.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The U.S. government must establish credible deterrence through sanctions and diplomatic isolation against international criminal networks and the states that provide them sanctuary.
  2. 02Congress needs to fund and authorize expanded capabilities to quickly attribute cyberattacks to their sources to enable timely legal and diplomatic responses.
  3. 03The financial system should implement a universal zero-liability policy for consumers to ensure that banks, rather than individuals, bear the financial burden of unavoidable fraud.
  4. 04Addressing cybercrime must be treated as a foreign policy priority through the negotiation of international cyber norms and enhanced intelligence sharing with allies.
