"To realize the significant societal benefits of the Internet of Things while mitigating its inherent security and privacy risks, we must mandate that manufacturers integrate rigorous security standards and privacy-by-design principles into their products from the start."

The most exciting promise of the Internet of Things is not that my refrigerator will tweet when I am out of milk. It is that ordinary objects can become part of a feedback system that saves time, money and even lives. A thermostat that learns when a house is empty can reduce energy use. A pill bottle that reminds an elderly patient to take medicine can prevent a hospitalization. A car that reports a failing part before it breaks can keep a family off the shoulder of a highway. Networked parking meters can reduce the circling that clogs city streets. These are not gimmicks; they are ways to make the physical world less wasteful and less dumb.

But the baby-monitor example captures the danger: once an object is connected, it becomes a computer, and once it is a computer, it can fail like one. The risks are not only embarrassment or voyeurism. Data from “smart” devices can reveal when a home is empty, when a child is alone, whether a person has a medical condition, and what routines a family follows. That information is valuable to advertisers, insurers, employers, burglars and governments.

The answer is not to stop connecting things. It is to stop treating privacy and security as optional features. Manufacturers should have to meet basic standards before selling networked devices: unique default passwords, encrypted communications, automatic security updates, clear data-retention limits and a simple way to disconnect the product from the network. A baby camera should not ship with the same password as a thousand other baby cameras. A thermostat should not quietly keep years of occupancy data unless the consumer has knowingly agreed.

We should also demand “privacy by design.” Collect the minimum data necessary. Store sensitive data locally when possible. Make the default setting the most protective one, not the most profitable one. And when companies do use data, they should explain it in plain English at the moment it matters, not in a 40-page policy nobody reads.

The Internet of Things can become a public benefit or a surveillance infrastructure. The difference will be decided less by the brilliance of the gadgets than by the rules built into them from the start.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Internet of Things offers transformative potential by creating feedback systems that reduce waste, save money, and improve public safety and health.
  2. 02Connected objects are vulnerable to the same security failures as computers, potentially exposing sensitive personal data to malicious actors, advertisers, and governments.
  3. 03Manufacturers should be required to meet baseline security standards, such as unique passwords, encrypted communications, and automatic updates, before selling networked devices.
  4. 04Companies should adopt 'privacy by design' by collecting minimal data, storing sensitive information locally, and using protective default settings.
  5. 05Data usage policies should be communicated to consumers in clear, accessible language at the point of use rather than through lengthy, unreadable legal documents.

"While the Internet of Things offers significant social benefits through increased measurability and efficiency, companies must integrate privacy and security into the core design of these products rather than treating them as optional features."

The most exciting Internet-connected objects will not be the gimmicks. A refrigerator that tweets is a punch line. A thermostat that learns when a house is empty and reduces peak electricity demand is not. A medication bottle that quietly alerts an adult child when an elderly parent misses doses may keep someone out of a nursing home. A network of parking spaces can reduce circling traffic. A pair of running shoes can help a patient recover from knee surgery without waiting three weeks for a doctor to notice a problem.

The point of the Internet of Things is that the world can become measurable in small, useful ways. Energy use, water leaks, sleep patterns, traffic flow, spoiled food and failing machinery can all be detected earlier and managed better. That is a real social gain.

But the same features that make these devices useful make them dangerous: they are persistent, intimate and easy to forget. A baby camera is not just a camera; it is a camera, a microphone, a network connection, a password database, a software update policy and a company’s privacy practices. The recent FTC action against TRENDnet’s insecure cameras is a useful warning. If a product is sold as a way to protect your home, it cannot be engineered as if security were an optional feature.

The answer is not to stop connecting objects. It is to build them as if they matter. First, collect less data. If a thermostat can learn a schedule inside the device, it should not automatically transmit minute-by-minute household behavior to a server. Second, secure devices by default: unique passwords, encrypted connections, automatic security updates and no exposed administrative panels that ordinary consumers do not understand. Third, give users understandable controls. “Agree” buttons buried in 40-page terms of service are not consent.

Regulators should insist on truthful claims and reasonable security, as the FTC has begun to do. Industry should develop a simple labeling system, something closer to an energy-efficiency sticker than a legal contract, showing what data is collected, where it goes, how long it is kept and whether the product will receive updates.

The Internet of Things should be allowed to grow, but not on the theory that consumers must become network engineers to own a baby monitor. The bargain should be simple: if companies want to put sensors in our homes, cars and bodies, they must make privacy and security part of the product, not an afterthought.

  3. 03Companies should minimize data collection by processing information locally on devices rather than automatically transmitting all user behavior to external servers.
  4. 04Manufacturers must implement security by default, including unique passwords, encrypted connections, and automatic updates that do not require consumer expertise.
  5. 05Transparency should be improved through understandable user controls and a simple labeling system that clearly outlines data practices and update policies.
  6. 06Regulators must hold companies accountable for making truthful claims and maintaining reasonable security standards for connected devices.

"To protect privacy and build trust in the Internet of Things, manufacturers must be held to rigorous engineering and security standards that treat connected objects as networked computers rather than simple appliances."

In the Internet of Things, privacy begins with engineering. A connected camera is not just a camera. It is a small computer running software, often with a Web server, a wireless connection and a password. A connected thermostat, toy or medical monitor has the same basic problem. If that computer is poorly built, the privacy policy is just decoration.

Many consumer devices are designed under brutal cost pressures. They ship with default passwords, outdated software, insecure remote-access features and no realistic plan for updates. Consumers are then blamed for not administering them correctly. That is backwards. No parent should need to know how to configure a firewall in order to keep strangers from watching a nursery.

The minimum standards are not mysterious. Devices should have unique credentials, not “admin/admin” logins shared by thousands of units. Communications should be encrypted. Remote access should not require exposing a control panel to the open Internet. Software updates should be signed, automatic when possible and available for a stated support period. Companies should maintain a way for researchers to report vulnerabilities without being threatened. Products should fail safely: a door lock, insulin pump or smoke detector cannot simply stop working because a server is unavailable.

There is also a business-model problem. A $59 gadget may be sold once, but securing it is an ongoing obligation. If manufacturers cannot afford to patch a connected device, they should not connect it. One useful model would be a safety mark, akin to Underwriters Laboratories, for networked products. Certification would not guarantee perfection, but it would tell consumers and retailers that basic practices had been followed.

Regulators can help by treating unreasonable security as an unfair or deceptive practice, especially when companies make claims about safety. The FTC’s action against insecure Internet cameras is a good start. Retailers can help by refusing to stock devices that lack update mechanisms or ship with known defects. Internet service providers can help by notifying customers when devices on their networks appear compromised.

The most exciting uses — home health care, energy management, safer transportation, industrial maintenance — all depend on trust. Trust will not come from slogans about “smart” products. It will come when manufacturers accept that they are no longer just making appliances. They are making networked computers that happen to wash dishes, monitor babies or count steps. They should be held to that standard.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Security must be built into the engineering of a device because a privacy policy cannot compensate for poorly designed software or hardware.
  2. 02Manufacturers should be required to implement minimum technical standards, such as unique credentials, encrypted communications, and automatic software updates.
  3. 03Companies must accept the ongoing financial obligation of patching devices for their entire lifecycle or refrain from connecting them to the internet entirely.
  4. 04A safety certification or 'mark' should be established to help consumers and retailers identify products that follow basic security practices.
  5. 05Regulators, retailers, and internet service providers should collaborate to enforce security standards by penalizing deceptive safety claims and removing defective products from the market.
