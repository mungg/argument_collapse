"Establishing a time-based, automatic transition to legal status for long-settled irregular migrants is a necessary repair to the rule of law that replaces arbitrary enforcement with justice and clarity."

The phrase “the rule of law” appears constantly in immigration debates, usually as a slogan against amnesty. Joseph Carens’s essay invites us to ask what the rule of law actually requires. Does it require the government to deport every unauthorized migrant it can find, no matter how long that person has lived here? Or does it require laws that are general, prospective, humane, and consistent with the social facts they govern? On that question, Carens’s position may be more faithful to the rule of law than the punitive alternative.

A legal system loses legitimacy when it maintains rules that it cannot or will not enforce and then applies them selectively. That is the situation in which the United States now finds itself. Roughly eleven million people live without authorization. They are not hidden in any serious sense. They work, rent apartments, attend churches, send children to school, drive, pay sales taxes, and often pay payroll taxes. The government lacks the capacity, and probably the democratic will, to remove them all. Instead, enforcement is episodic: a workplace raid here, a traffic stop there, a detention tragedy elsewhere. Such a system breeds fear without producing order.

Carens’s proposal for an automatic transition to legal status after a fixed period would replace arbitrary vulnerability with a rule. That is a rule-of-law improvement. People would know the consequences of residence. Officials would have less discretion to decide whose family is sympathetic enough. Courts and agencies could administer clear criteria. Employers would lose some power to use immigration status as a threat. Communities would know who is legally present.

Critics say that amnesty undermines the law because it forgives violations. But law includes mechanisms of forgiveness, repose, waiver, and adjustment. Bankruptcy discharges debts. Prosecutors decline charges. Statutes of limitation bar stale claims. Zoning boards grant variances. Tax authorities settle liabilities. These practices do not abolish law; they recognize that rigid enforcement can become unjust or counterproductive. Immigration law should be no different.

The analogy to statutes of limitation is especially apt. We do not think the state should be able to prosecute every offense forever. Time weakens evidence, changes expectations, and makes punishment less useful. If that is true for many crimes, it is surely true for civil immigration violations. A person who crossed a border nine years ago and has since worked, paid taxes, married, and raised children is not the same policy problem as a person who crossed yesterday. Treating them identically is not principled. It is crude.

Still, a rule-of-law amnesty must be designed carefully. It should not be a vague political pardon announced under pressure. It should be enacted by Congress, administered transparently, and tied to objective criteria. Applicants should prove residence through a broad range of documents, including school records, medical records, leases, church affidavits, employment records, tax filings, and community attestations. Because unauthorized people often live informally, evidentiary rules must not be so strict that only the well-documented can qualify. Fraud prevention is necessary, but suspicion should not swallow justice.

The program should include background checks and exclude those convicted of serious crimes. But lawmakers must avoid defining “criminal” so broadly that minor offenses become traps. Unauthorized migrants often drive without licenses because licenses are unavailable. They may use false numbers to work because work is necessary to live. These acts should not be treated the same as violence, trafficking, or predatory fraud. The law should distinguish between conduct that harms others and conduct that reflects survival under unlawful status.

Carens’s five-to-seven-year suggestion is reasonable. I would favor seven years for ordinary adult entrants, shorter periods for those brought as children, spouses of citizens or lawful permanent residents, and parents of U.S.-citizen children. Seven years is long enough to show settlement and discourage claims that brief unlawful presence creates rights. But the exact number matters less than the principle: after a defined period, the government’s claim to deport should expire.

What about future migration? Here, rule-of-law concerns support reform on both sides. Amnesty alone would be insufficient. The United States needs legal channels that reflect labor demand, especially for lesser-skilled work. It also needs credible workplace enforcement aimed primarily at employers. If Congress legalizes the current population but leaves future workers with no lawful path and employers with continued demand, it will recreate the same unauthorized population. A law that ignores economic reality invites evasion.

At the same time, enforcement must remain bounded by rights. The rule of law is not maximized by raids that deny due process, detention conditions that endanger lives, or local policing that encourages racial profiling. Immigration enforcement must be subject to constitutional norms, judicial review, and humane detention standards. A country cannot defend legality through lawless methods.

Carens’s argument also exposes a contradiction in much anti-amnesty rhetoric. Opponents insist that unauthorized migrants are “illegal” and therefore outside the community, yet they often oppose giving them any way to become legal. If the problem is illegality, regularization is part of the solution. If the real objection is the migrants’ presence regardless of legal status, then “rule of law” is being used as a mask for exclusion.

The rule of law requires that legal categories correspond, at least roughly, to lived reality. When millions of long-settled residents are officially removable but practically integrated, the law becomes a generator of insecurity rather than a guide for conduct. Carens’s amnesty would not reward contempt for law. It would acknowledge that the government, employers, communities, and migrants have together produced a durable social fact. Law should respond to that fact with clarity.

A liberal democracy should enforce its borders, but it should not keep settled people in permanent legal suspense. After enough time has passed, deportation ceases to be ordinary enforcement and becomes banishment. A time-based path to legal status would bring immigration law closer to justice, administrability, and truth. That is not the abandonment of the rule of law. It is one of its necessary repairs.

"The United States should replace the politically charged concept of 'amnesty' with a practical, rule-bound administrative process that allows long-settled unauthorized migrants to adjust their legal status."

I approach Joseph Carens’s essay from a somewhat unusual position: I favor generous immigration and eventual legalization for many unauthorized migrants, but I worry about the word “amnesty.” Not because forgiveness is wrong, and not because unauthorized migrants are uniquely blameworthy. I worry because “amnesty” suggests a dramatic act of absolution when what we need is a more ordinary administrative category: adjustment of status for people who meet defined conditions.

Words matter in politics. “Amnesty” has become a symbol of lawlessness to opponents and a badge of moral urgency to supporters. Both uses obscure the practical task. The United States already adjusts status, waives inadmissibility, grants cancellation of removal, offers temporary protected status, recognizes asylum, and exercises prosecutorial discretion. Immigration law is full of mechanisms that translate messy human situations into legal categories. A residency-based legalization program would be another such mechanism. Calling it “amnesty” may make it harder to see that.

Carens’s argument is strongest when it is institutional rather than emotional. He says time changes the moral relationship between migrant and state. That insight can be embodied in law through a statute of repose. After a fixed period of peaceful residence, the government would no longer treat the original unlawful entry or overstay as grounds for removal, provided the person registers and meets other criteria. This need not be framed as erasing wrongdoing. It is a decision that, after enough time, regularization serves justice and order better than removal.

A well-designed program should be boring in the best sense: predictable, rule-bound, and administrable. Applicants would submit evidence of residence. The government would run background checks. Those with serious criminal convictions would be excluded or placed in separate proceedings. Approved applicants would receive provisional status, work authorization, and protection from removal. After several years of continued good conduct, they could become permanent residents. Later, under ordinary naturalization rules, they could apply for citizenship.

This structure answers several objections. To those who say legalization rewards lawbreaking, it says: the benefit is not immediate and not unconditional. To those who fear arbitrary bureaucracy, it says: the criteria are objective. To those who worry about a permanent underclass, it says: the path leads to full membership. To those who worry about fraud, it says: evidence and background checks matter. To immigrants, it says: come forward without fear if you qualify.

Carens is right to oppose broad discretion. The current system’s hardship standards often require families to prove exceptional misery. This is degrading and inconsistent. Two similar families can receive different outcomes depending on judge, lawyer, geography, or publicity. The case of an elderly woman like Marguerite Grimmond makes headlines; countless others disappear into files. A liberal legal system should not require newspaper sympathy to prevent absurd deportations.

Yet a purely automatic rule may also be too blunt. The law should include multiple tracks. Childhood arrivals should qualify quickly. Spouses of citizens and lawful permanent residents should have a separate adjustment route. Parents of U.S.-citizen children should receive strong protection. Long-term workers without family ties should qualify through residence and good conduct. Agricultural and other essential workers might have documentary flexibility because informal employment makes proof difficult. Different tracks can reflect different moral claims while still limiting discretion.

The program must also be accessible. If fees are too high, documents too narrow, or deadlines too short, the poorest and most fearful will remain unauthorized. The government should fund community legal assistance and use schools, churches, unions, and local organizations to disseminate accurate information. Fraudulent notarios and unscrupulous lawyers thrive when processes are confusing. Simplicity is an anti-fraud measure.

Opponents will demand enforcement in exchange. Some enforcement is reasonable, but it should be carefully targeted. Employer accountability is more important than workplace raids that terrorize laborers. Recent entrants can be treated differently from long-term residents. Serious criminals should be priorities, but minor traffic offenses should not become deportation triggers. Detention should be minimized for those with families and community ties. Due process should not be sacrificed to speed.

The administrative capacity question is often overlooked. Legalizing millions requires trained adjudicators, interpreters, background-check systems, appeals procedures, and coordination with tax and labor agencies. If Congress passes a program but underfunds the agency, backlogs and errors will undermine legitimacy. The 2007 reform debate sometimes treated implementation as an afterthought. It cannot be. A legalization program is not a speech; it is a national administrative undertaking.

Carens compares immigration violations to traffic offenses. I see the point, though the analogy can irritate people who view immigration as more fundamental. A better analogy may be land-use regularization. Cities often discover that informal settlements, unpermitted apartments, or long-standing technical violations cannot simply be demolished without social harm. The solution is inspection, fines, safety upgrades, and legalization where possible. The goal is not to celebrate the violation, but to bring reality into lawful order.

That is what status adjustment would do. It would convert a population that is already here from unregistered vulnerability into lawful participation. It would improve tax compliance, labor standards, policing, family stability, and civic trust. It would also allow immigration enforcement to focus on future rules rather than chasing people whose lives have long since become American.

Carens’s moral claim is sound: after years of residence, deportation becomes disproportionate. My suggested refinement is rhetorical and institutional. Let us spend less time arguing over “amnesty” and more time designing a fair statute of repose and adjustment. The question is not whether to forgive in some grand national drama. The question is whether law will continue to deny reality or whether it will organize that reality justly.

A mature democracy should choose the latter. Long-settled unauthorized residents should be able to regularize their status through clear, demanding, humane rules. If that is amnesty, then amnesty is simply good administration joined to moral sense.
