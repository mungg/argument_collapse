Diversified Claude

"Joseph Carens’ proposal for time-based amnesty is flawed because it undermines democratic sovereignty and rewards the intentional evasion of immigration laws."

Joseph Carens' essay is morally serious and beautifully written. It is also wrong in ways that matter. By making "the passage of time" do almost all the work, Carens has constructed an argument that systematically rewards the most successful evaders of the law and that, if adopted as policy, would guarantee an endless cycle of mass amnesties.

Begin with the deepest problem. Carens insists that he accepts the state's right to control immigration. He says his argument is a "constraint" on that right, not a "repudiation." But it is hard to see what is left of the right once the constraint is fully applied. A state has the right to set immigration rules; it has the right to enforce them; but if it fails to apprehend a violator within five to seven years, the violator acquires a positive legal claim against the state. This is not a constraint. It is a deadline beyond which the state's policy is automatically overridden. Imagine applying the same logic to tax evasion. If a person successfully hides income from the IRS for five years, do we say that the passage of time has converted the income into honestly held property? Of course not. We say the violator got away with it longer than most, and we treat that as an aggravation, not a mitigation. Why should immigration be different?

Carens' answer, I think, is that immigration violations are like traffic violations: trivial, victimless, not really crimes. This is precisely where his argument breaks down. Immigration law is not victimless. Its victims are the citizens and lawful residents whose democratic decision about who shall enter is nullified each time a migrant successfully crosses or overstays. The citizens of a self-governing polity have a collective right to determine the composition and pace of their community's growth. That right has been recognized in every functioning democracy. To say that "most of those who settle as irregular migrants would have no possibility of obtaining authorized entry" is to admit that the political community has decided not to admit them — and then to assert that this decision must be reversed by the brute fact of the migrant's persistence. This is government by fait accompli.

The "social membership" framework that Carens leans on is more rhetoric than analysis. Of course people who have lived in a place for fifteen years have ties to it. So do people who have lived in a country for fifteen years on a tourist visa, on a fraudulent student visa, in violation of a deportation order. The question is whether ties so acquired generate political claims against the host society. Carens treats this as obvious; it is not. A trespasser who has lived for twenty years on someone else's land has accumulated a great deal of attachment to it. We do not therefore conclude that the landowner's title has lapsed. We require, at minimum, that the trespasser have acted openly and under color of right — what the property lawyers call "adverse possession." Irregular migrants by definition cannot satisfy these conditions. They have lived in concealment, knowing that their status was vulnerable. Their attachment to the host society is real but does not generate legal entitlement against the political community whose laws they have violated.

Carens' examples are carefully chosen and slightly misleading. Marguerite Grimmond is a touching case. She is also a complete outlier — a woman who, through her mother's choices, was effectively raised as British, who had no idea of her own irregular status, and who was 80 years old. Conceding that we should not deport Grimmond establishes essentially nothing about Miguel Sanchez, who is in his thirties, who deliberately entered with a smuggler, who knew his status throughout, and whose entire predicament is the product of his own choices and those of his wife (who chose to marry an undocumented man). Hiu Lui Ng's case is genuinely tragic, but the tragedy is the medical neglect in detention, not the deportation order. Conflating these is an argumentative move, not an analytic one.

The "no line" argument is also overstated. There is no line for unskilled workers without family ties because the United States has decided, through its democratic process, that it will not admit unlimited unskilled labor. This is not a gap in the system; it is the system. The argument that "there is no line, therefore line-jumping is acceptable" is logically equivalent to saying that because a nightclub has decided not to admit further patrons, it is acceptable to climb in the back window.

Finally, the empirical question Carens addresses too quickly. He claims that the prospect of distant legalization has little effect on migration decisions. This is implausible on its face and contrary to what every previous amnesty has demonstrated. The 1986 IRCA amnesty legalized about three million migrants. It was followed by a substantial increase in irregular migration, not a decrease, as networks were reinforced and expectations of future amnesties were set. Spain's serial amnesties have produced the same pattern. Italy's likewise. Carens may believe that migrants are myopic and discount the future steeply. Smugglers and labor recruiters are not myopic. They will sell the prospect of eventual legalization as a feature of the package, and they will be right to do so.

What is the alternative? A policy that combines genuine enforcement (workplace verification, visa tracking, removal of recent arrivals, prosecution of employers) with limited and earned legalization for narrow categories — minor children brought without their consent, perhaps, and spouses of citizens. A policy that increases legal channels for the kinds of workers our economy actually needs, so that the next generation has a line to wait in. A policy that does not promise, as Carens' does, that anyone who manages to hide for five years will be rewarded with citizenship.

Carens has written an honest essay. Its honesty exposes its weakness: he believes, ultimately, that the moral claims of migrants against borders are nearly absolute, and the essay's elaborate machinery of "constraints" is a way of reaching that conclusion without quite admitting it. Those of us who believe that democratic self-government includes the right to determine membership cannot accept the deal.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A fixed deadline for legal status effectively nullifies a state's right to enforce its own immigration policies and creates a 'government by fait accompli.'
  2. 02Immigration violations are not victimless traffic-like offenses but are actions that override the collective right of citizens to determine the composition of their community.
  3. 03Social ties and 'membership' acquired through the deliberate violation of laws do not generate a legitimate legal entitlement against the host society.
  4. 04The 'no line' argument fails to recognize that the lack of an admission path for unskilled workers is a deliberate and legitimate democratic decision rather than a systemic gap.
  5. 05Implementing a predictable path to amnesty would incentivize further irregular migration by creating a cycle of expectations for future legalizations.
  6. 06The cases used to support the amnesty argument are outliers or conflate administrative deportation with separate issues like medical neglect in detention.
  7. 07A more appropriate policy would focus on rigorous enforcement and expanded legal channels rather than rewarding those who successfully hide from authorities for a set period.
Diversified Claude

"The proposal for a general amnesty based on the passage of time should be rejected because it undermines the fundamental right of a political community to exercise democratic self-government by controlling its own membership."

Carens' essay is the latest in a long line of philosophical arguments that begin from individual stories of hardship and conclude that the political community must yield. The pattern is recognizable. Pick a sympathetic case — an elderly woman, a sick man in detention, a family with a small child. Pose the question: how could you deport this person? Generalize from the answer. The generalization, predictably, is that the rules must give way to the cases, and the political community must accept whoever, having entered, has stayed long enough to make removal painful.

This pattern of argument has a name in moral philosophy: it is the argument from heart-rending example. It is also the standard maneuver of the activist who has lost the policy debate and seeks to win it through emotion. I do not say this to disparage Carens, whose essay is more sophisticated than most. I say it to identify what is missing from his argument: any serious engagement with the political community as such — with its right to constitute itself, to set the terms of its membership, and to enforce those terms.

The community whose name Carens invokes — the "liberal democratic state" — is not just a collection of individuals with ties. It is a political body that has the right to determine its own composition. This right is not a quirky preference of nation-states; it is the precondition of democratic self-government. A polity that cannot determine its own membership cannot determine anything else, because anyone else's preferences can always be smuggled in by smuggling in additional members. The right of membership-control is not in tension with liberalism; it is what makes liberal democracy possible.

Carens treats this right as a concession — something he provisionally accepts as a premise. But the premise, taken seriously, undoes the argument. If the political community has the right to determine its membership, then unauthorized presence is not the morally trivial thing Carens makes it out to be. It is a usurpation, however small, of the community's authority over itself. Each act of irregular settlement, however sympathetic, is a unilateral declaration that the migrant's preferences shall trump the political community's collective decision. When we tolerate this on a mass scale, we are not engaging in compassion; we are engaging in a form of constitutional surrender.

The traffic-law analogy that Carens offers is therefore not just wrong but revealing. Traffic laws regulate the behavior of members of the political community within shared space. Immigration laws regulate the threshold of membership itself. To compare them is to assume that membership is not morally interesting — that the political community is just a kind of administrative convenience, like a department of transportation. This assumption is the great unstated premise of Carens' essay and, indeed, of much of contemporary liberal political philosophy. It is wrong, and its wrongness is at the root of the entire debate.

What does it mean for the political community to have the right to determine its membership? It means, among other things, that the community may decide, through democratic deliberation, how many members to admit, from where, with what skills, under what conditions. It may decide that it wishes to admit more or fewer; that it prizes cultural continuity or diversity; that it has economic needs that should be reflected in admissions policy. These are not racist or xenophobic decisions. They are the ordinary exercises of self-government. A political community that has them taken away — by judicial mandate, by external pressure, or by the unilateral action of millions of border-crossers — is a community no longer governing itself.

Carens' argument has the effect, whether intended or not, of removing membership-determination from the democratic process. The political community wishes to admit X people; some larger number Y enters; after five or seven years, Y becomes the new floor, regardless of what the political community wanted. The community's right to choose has been replaced with the migrant's right to outlast. This is a transfer of sovereignty from citizen to migrant, mediated by time.

Consider what happens when this principle is institutionalized, as Carens proposes. Every individual considering migration knows that all he must do is enter, hide for five to seven years, and the political community will be obliged to legalize him. The previous amnesties were politically contingent: IRCA in 1986, Spain's various regularizations, Italy's. The next migrant could not assume they would recur. Carens' proposal eliminates that uncertainty. It tells every potential migrant: if you can get in, time will do the rest. The signaling effect is not just inducement to migrate; it is a constitutional redrafting of the immigration system itself.

Carens anticipates this objection but waves it away with the observation that migrants do not think long-term. I am skeptical. Migrants are not stupid, and the networks that organize migration — family chains, ethnic associations, smugglers, employers — most certainly do think long-term. They will market Carens' rule, accurately, as a path to citizenship. The current eleven million will become twenty million. The next amnesty debate will involve twice as many people.

There is a different way to think about all this. The political community has a right to determine its membership. It also has obligations: to enforce its laws humanely, to provide reasonable legal channels for migration, to avoid cruelty and family separation, to respond with mercy to genuine hardship. These obligations are real and significant. They generate, in my view, a strong case against worksite raids that separate families, against the medical neglect that killed Hiu Lui Ng, against the militarized border policy that has driven migration into the deadliest desert routes. They generate a case for narrow legalization in narrow categories — children brought as minors, perhaps, whose entry was not their own choice. They do not generate a case for general amnesty.

The difference between my view and Carens' is finally a difference about the moral standing of the political community. Carens treats it as a constraint on his argument, to be acknowledged and then circumvented. I treat it as the central fact about democratic politics, without which liberal democracy itself is not possible. From his premise, his conclusions follow. From mine, they do not.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The right to determine membership is the essential precondition for a polity to govern itself and set its own collective priorities.
  2. 02Unauthorized settlement is not a trivial administrative violation but a usurpation of the community's authority, where individual preferences unilaterally override democratic decisions.
  3. 03Comparing immigration law to traffic law is a category error that wrongly reduces the moral significance of political membership to a mere administrative convenience.
  4. 04Institutionalizing amnesty based on time effectively transfers sovereignty from citizens to migrants by replacing democratic choice with a migrant's ability to outlast enforcement.
  5. 05A guaranteed path to legal status after a fixed period creates a permanent incentive for irregular migration, fundamentally redrafting the immigration system into one of 'entry by endurance.'
  6. 06While the state has moral obligations to enforce laws humanely and avoid cruelty in specific cases, these duties do not justify a broad surrender of the community's right to exclude.
