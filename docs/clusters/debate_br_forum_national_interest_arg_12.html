"The proposed strategy of offshore balancing is a dangerous return to the failed policies of complacency that ignored the internal character of regimes and ultimately led to the September 11 attacks."

Stephen Walt’s essay is elegant, learned, and profoundly mistaken. It mistakes the temporary unpopularity of American policy for strategic failure, treats the desire for freedom as an optional adornment of foreign policy, and assumes that dictators can be managed indefinitely through balance-of-power techniques that have already failed. His proposed “offshore balancing” would be a return not to wisdom but to the complacency that helped produce September 11.

The old Middle East policy was not a success. For decades the United States did exactly what Walt now recommends: it balanced, contained, reassured, and dealt with regimes as they were. It protected oil flows, backed friendly autocrats, tolerated Saudi ideological exports, contained Saddam Hussein after 1991, and treated tyranny as regrettable but manageable. The result was not stability in any deep sense. It was a region of political suffocation, radical mosques, conspiracy theories, economic backwardness, and anti-American terrorism. Al Qaeda did not arise because America was too committed to democracy. It arose in a region where democracy was almost entirely absent.

Walt is right that America’s military presence in Saudi Arabia after the Gulf War became a grievance for bin Laden. But bin Laden’s grievances are not a policy manual. He objected not only to U.S. troops but to American influence, Israel’s existence, secular Arab governments, women’s freedom, and the presence of non-Muslims in lands he deems Islamic. If the United States had withdrawn from the Gulf, pressed Israel harder, and spoken more politely, would jihadist ideology have disappeared? Hardly. Radical Islamism is not simply a reaction to American error. It is a totalitarian movement with its own ambitions. It must be defeated intellectually and politically, not merely placated geopolitically.

The Bush administration’s National Security Strategy recognized something Walt’s realism obscures: the character of regimes matters. A world of aggressive dictatorships is more dangerous than a world of democracies. Democratic governments are not perfect, but they are more accountable, more peaceful toward one another, and less likely to produce the combination of humiliation and repression on which extremism feeds. To say that America cannot impose democracy everywhere is true. To conclude that America should not use its power to promote freedom is false.

Iraq is the central test. Walt sees only quagmire. No one can deny that the occupation has been costly and badly managed. The failure to secure order after Baghdad fell, the inadequate troop levels, the prison scandal at Abu Ghraib, and the slow reconstruction have all damaged the cause. But as we write, Iraqis have just gone to the polls in the face of terrorist threats. Millions risked death to vote. That fact deserves more than a footnote. It suggests that the desire for self-government is not a Western fantasy and that the battle in Iraq is not simply America versus Iraqi nationalism. It is also Iraqis versus Baathist remnants and jihadists who fear democratic politics precisely because elections undermine their claim to speak for the people.

If Iraq succeeds, even imperfectly, the strategic consequences could be immense. A constitutional Iraq would put pressure on neighboring autocracies, provide an alternative to both dictatorship and theocracy, and show that Arabs can govern themselves democratically. This is not “empire”; it is liberation from one of the most murderous regimes of the late twentieth century. Saddam Hussein invaded two neighbors, used chemical weapons, filled mass graves, defied the United Nations, subsidized terror, and maintained ambitions that no responsible American president could ignore after September 11.

Walt dismisses preventive war as illegitimate and unworkable. It is certainly dangerous and should never be routine. But the old distinction between imminent and non-imminent threats is less reassuring in an age of terrorism and weapons of mass destruction. Waiting until a threat is visible may mean waiting too long. The fact that stockpiles have not been found in Iraq is a serious blow to the administration’s case, but it does not prove that preventive action is always wrong. Intelligence services across several countries believed Saddam had retained prohibited capabilities. He acted as if he had something to hide, obstructed inspectors for years, and had used such weapons before. After 9/11, the burden of proof shifted. Presidents cannot gamble American cities on the good behavior of tyrants.

Walt’s confidence that rogue states will not transfer weapons to terrorists is too categorical. Deterrence may work against identifiable states, but it is much harder when regimes use proxies, cutouts, scientists, smugglers, or deniable networks. A.Q. Khan’s nuclear bazaar has already shown how porous the old nonproliferation system can be. Saddam paid families of Palestinian suicide bombers. Iran supports Hezbollah. North Korea sells missiles. To assume that leaders will always behave cautiously with terrible weapons is to place enormous faith in regimes that have earned none.

Nor is Walt’s proposed bargain with Iran and North Korea likely to work. Nonaggression pledges and American nuclear reductions might make us feel less hypocritical, but Tehran and Pyongyang seek nuclear weapons for reasons deeper than fear of America. Nuclear arms bring prestige, deterrence, leverage, and domestic legitimacy. North Korea has broken agreements before. Iran concealed major nuclear activities for years while proclaiming peaceful intent. A few hundred American warheads rather than a few thousand will not persuade such regimes to abandon ambitions they regard as central to survival and status.

On Israel, Walt’s argument is especially troubling. He presents American support for Israel as a major cause of anti-American terrorism and suggests cutting aid if Israel refuses terms Washington deems fair. This both overstates American leverage and understates Palestinian responsibility. Israel withdrew from Lebanon and received Hezbollah rockets. It offered far-reaching concessions at Camp David and Taba, and the second intifada followed. Whatever the imperfections of Israeli policy—and settlements have indeed been a grave mistake—Israel faces enemies who have repeatedly targeted civilians and, in some cases, reject its existence. Pressuring Israel while Palestinian institutions remain corrupt, armed factions independent, and incitement widespread will not produce peace. It may produce another failed experiment in wishful diplomacy.

Walt also places too much faith in the approval of allies and international institutions. Legitimacy matters, but it cannot be defined by French, Russian, or Chinese consent. Each had its own interests in Iraq. The United Nations is useful when it acts, but its record in Rwanda, Bosnia, and Iraq’s long defiance should make us wary of treating it as the arbiter of American security. A president’s first duty is not to be liked abroad; it is to protect the American people and advance the cause of a safer world.

The United States should be humble about its mistakes. It should improve public diplomacy, secure nuclear materials, reform intelligence, and share burdens where possible. It should not swagger. But humility is not the same as passivity. Walt’s offshore balancing would leave the political pathologies of the Middle East intact, reassure tyrants that America has lost its nerve, and tell democrats that they must wait for history to liberate them.

American primacy is not worth preserving merely for its own sake. It is worth preserving because, used well, it can expand the realm of freedom. That project is difficult, costly, and imperfect. But the alternative—managing dictatorships until the next explosion—has already been tried. September 11 was the verdict.
