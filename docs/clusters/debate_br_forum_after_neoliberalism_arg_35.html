"While the lead essay successfully demonstrates that modern economics can support inclusive policy, its rhetorical strategy and reliance on technical expertise may fail to persuade the public or overcome the discipline's reputational baggage."

I come to praise Dani Rodrik, Suresh Naidu, and Gabriel Zucman, not to bury them. Their lead essay is, on its own terms, a triumph. It refutes, with rigorous evidence and elegant reasoning, the lazy claim that economics is a monolith of market worship. It demonstrates that the frontier of economic research is deeply concerned with inequality, power asymmetries, and market failures. And it offers a coherent, interconnected set of policy ideas that, if implemented, would indeed push the United States toward a more inclusive and prosperous society. This is public intellectual work of the highest order, and it deserves the wide audience it seeks.

That said, I want to examine the essay from the perspective of a political practitioner rather than an academic economist. The authors rightly sense that the battle is not only intellectual but also rhetorical and political. Their project is explicitly about “convincing our audience” that economics is part of the solution. Yet the essay itself deploys a rhetorical strategy that, however satisfying to insiders, may inadvertently reinforce the very perceptions it seeks to overturn.

Consider the central claim that “neoliberalism—or market fundamentalism, market fetishism, etc.—is not the consistent application of modern economics, but its primitive, simplistic perversion.” For an academic audience, this is a potent demarcation: we are the sophisticated ones, they are the primitives. But for the broader public, it can easily sound like a self-serving excuse. After all, the economists who dominated policy circles for decades—the Friedmans, the Greenspans, the Summerses—were not exactly obscure cranks; they were feted by the profession and commanded immense institutional authority. The public can be forgiven for thinking that if that was a perversion, then the boundary between the real thing and the perversion is porous indeed. The essay’s move might be read less as an honest reckoning and more as an attempt to have it both ways: to claim credit for the good policies while disavowing the bad ones that were championed by equally credentialed economists.

A more politically effective approach might be to openly acknowledge the discipline’s failures and internal contradictions. Instead of insisting that the neoliberal era was a mistake in translation, the authors could admit that economics, as a social institution, did produce and legitimize many of the policies they now oppose. A mea culpa, even a partial one, could build trust. By defensively drawing a bright line around “real” economics, they risk coming across as a guild protecting its turf.

Furthermore, the essay’s proposals, however sound, are framed in the language of economics expertise: free lunches, externalities, incentive compatibility, monopsony. This is the native tongue of the profession, but it is alien to most citizens. The authors recognize the need to reach beyond the academy, yet they make few concessions to non-economist readers. Where is the moral language of fairness, dignity, and community? Where is the narrative that connects these policies to peoples’ lived struggles? The essay is a masterclass in how economists talk to each other, but inclusive prosperity will require economists to learn how to talk to everyone else.

None of this diminishes the intellectual achievement. But if EfIP is to become a genuine force for change, its next phase must involve not just better economics, but better storytelling, genuine listening, and a willingness to share epistemic authority with those outside the guild. The authors have shown that economics can be part of the solution; now they must show that economists can be part of the conversation.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Dismissing neoliberalism as a 'perversion' of economics feels like a self-serving excuse that avoids a necessary reckoning with the profession's role in legitimizing failed policies.
  2. 02The authors should offer a more transparent acknowledgment of the discipline's past failures and internal contradictions to build trust with a skeptical public.
  3. 03The reliance on technical economic jargon creates a barrier for non-experts and lacks the moral and narrative language needed to connect with citizens' lived experiences.
  4. 04To achieve political impact, economists must move beyond academic discourse and learn to share epistemic authority with those outside their professional guild.
