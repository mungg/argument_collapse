"Nussbaum’s cosmopolitanism is an elitist, Western-centric philosophy that prioritizes abstract moral reasoning over the concrete political and economic struggles necessary to achieve genuine global justice."

Martha Nussbaum's essay is a well-meaning piece of elite philosophy that, despite its references to the United Nations and global poverty, finally speaks more to the comfort of Western academics than to the urgent needs of the world's dispossessed. Her cosmopolitanism, drawn from Diogenes and Marcus Aurelius, is a child of empire. It asks us to imagine a universal community of reason while remaining blind to the actual structures of power that shape life on this planet. If we are genuinely serious about global justice, we need a very different kind of internationalism—one that arises from below, from the concrete struggles of the poor and colonized, rather than from the armchairs of Stoic Emperors.

The first and most glaring problem with Nussbaum's argument is its historical and cultural provincialism. She traces the ideal of world citizenship from a Cynic philosopher who lived by begging and flouting taboos, through a Roman ruler who presided over a slave state, and then leaps directly to a Bengali novelist whose work is read through the lens of English translations. But there are many traditions of universalism that she ignores entirely: the pan-African solidarity of C.L.R. James and Leopold Senghor, the anti-colonial internationalism of Frantz Fanon and Che Guevara, the Dalit critique of the caste system in India that has always allied itself with universal human dignity against Brahminical nationalism, the feminist networks that have linked women from Mexico to the Philippines, and the labor movements that built international solidarity across the twentieth century. By locating the authentic cosmopolitan voice in a handful of Greek and Roman texts, Nussbaum implicitly presents a Western intellectual lineage as the gold standard, inviting the suspicion that her world citizenship is just the latest version of the civilizing mission.

This suspicion is reinforced by her treatment of Tagore. *The Home and the World* is a novel written in a colonized country, at a moment when the Swadeshi movement represented a genuine struggle for economic and political self-determination against British imperialism. To reduce that movement to a dangerous "ethnocentric particularism" is to miss the point. The problem with Sandip is not that he is a patriot; it is that he is a demagogue who exploits religious divisions and treats human beings as instruments. A nationalist movement led by someone like Tagore's friend Gandhi—who combined anti-colonial resistance with a profound commitment to universal truth and nonviolence—would have been far more complex. Nussbaum's cosmopolitan landlord Nikhil, meanwhile, is hardly an unambiguous hero. As several Indian critics have noted, his passive universalism depends on the privilege of a wealthy landowner who never has to confront the brutal realities of colonial power. His refusal to join the national struggle can be read as a form of collaboration, not enlightenment. The Bengali intellectual community has debated this novel fiercely, and Nussbaum's reading, while one possible interpretation, erases the political context that gives the text its tragic force.

More troubling still is Nussbaum's near-total silence on the economic dimensions of global injustice. The world of 1994 is one in which the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank impose structural adjustment programs that force poor nations to slash health and education budgets in order to repay debts to northern banks. It is a world in which the newly signed North American Free Trade Agreement has been condemned by labor unions and environmentalists on both sides of the border for putting corporate profits above workers' rights and ecological protection. It is a world in which the United States, even as its intellectuals lecture on cosmopolitanism, remains the largest arms dealer on the planet. In this context, calling for children to learn about "the problems of hunger and pollution in India" is almost obscenely timid. The real question is whether our students will be taught to challenge the global economic order that produces that hunger, or merely to feel a vague liberal sympathy while continuing to benefit from it.

A genuine cosmopolitanism from below would start not with Stoic exercises but with the actual movements that have built solidarity across borders. It would teach the history of the international slave trade and the abolitionist networks that fought it. It would teach the story of the Spanish Civil War, when workers from fifty countries formed the International Brigades. It would teach the boycotts and divestment campaigns that toppled South African apartheid. It would teach recent struggles against the Gulf War, in which ordinary Americans, Iraqis, and Europeans found ways to protest a conflict sold on patriotic lies. In every case, the moral energy came not from a disembodied love of "humanity" but from a concrete recognition that the suffering of distant people was linked to one's own society's actions. This is the kind of knowledge that builds genuine international solidarity, not just a tolerant museum-goer's appreciation of diversity.

To be sure, Nussbaum's commitment to universal human rights and her criticism of narrow nationalism are admirable. But her proposal remains trapped within the limitations of a liberal philosophy that wants to purify morality by detaching it from politics. In the real world, global justice will be won, if at all, by movements that combine love for one's own people—however defined—with a militant internationalism that recognizes that the chains of oppression are linked across continents. Nussbaum's Stoic sage, standing naked before the world, is a striking image. But the people who are actually changing the world are putting on their boots.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Nussbaum’s intellectual lineage is provincially Western, ignoring diverse global traditions of universalism and anti-colonial internationalism.
  2. 02The essay’s reading of Tagore decontextualizes the Swadeshi movement, failing to distinguish between legitimate anti-colonial resistance and dangerous demagoguery.
  3. 03Nussbaum’s framework ignores the structural economic realities of global power, such as debt and trade agreements, which produce the very suffering she seeks to alleviate.
  4. 04A meaningful internationalism must be built 'from below' through historical and contemporary solidarity movements rather than through disembodied Stoic exercises.
  5. 05The proposal mistakenly attempts to purify morality by detaching it from the militant political action required to challenge global oppression.
