"Both liberal and conservative orthodoxies are stifling thought on college campuses by narrowing the range of permissible arguments and prioritizing moral purity over intellectual engagement."

Yes, both liberal and conservative orthodoxies are narrowing the range of permissible argument on campus, though the mechanisms differ. On the left, the danger is often a politics of moral contamination: if a speaker has defended a war, criticized Islam, opposed same-sex marriage or worked for an unpopular financial institution, then the mere invitation is treated as an assault. The recent withdrawals of Condoleezza Rice from Rutgers and Christine Lagarde from Smith show how quickly a commencement invitation can become a test of institutional virtue.

On the right, orthodoxy more often comes through trustees, donors, state legislatures and religious administrations: pressure on faculty who teach evolution, sexuality, race, labor history or Middle Eastern politics; demands that universities avoid “political” scholarship; and campaigns to defund disciplines deemed ideological. These pressures may be less visible than student protest, but they can be more powerful because they touch budgets, hiring and tenure.

Trigger warnings are a subtler case. A professor who says, “This novel contains sexual violence; be prepared,” is not censoring anyone. But when warnings become demands that difficult works be avoided, softened or presented only through preapproved moral frames, they undercut the central work of education. Much of literature, history and philosophy is disturbing because human beings are disturbing. A university cannot promise students that learning will not unsettle them.

The same distinction applies to commencement speakers. Students have every right to protest. But a campus culture that treats hearing from a flawed public figure as complicity in that figure’s sins risks replacing argument with excommunication. An honorary degree is not identical to a classroom lecture, but universities should be wary of making ceremonial approval so morally pure that only the bland can be invited.

The university’s obligation is not neutrality between truth and falsehood, nor indifference to injustice. It is to sustain a community where claims can be examined rather than simply denounced. If conservatives and liberals both insist that certain ideas must not be heard because they are offensive, dangerous or “not who we are,” then thought is indeed being stifled. The cure is not politeness for its own sake, but intellectual courage.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Liberal orthodoxy often employs a politics of moral contamination to treat controversial speakers as an assault on institutional virtue, leading to the cancellation of diverse viewpoints.
  2. 02Conservative orthodoxy exerts pressure through institutional power structures like donors and legislatures to defund or suppress scholarship on sensitive topics like evolution, race, and Middle Eastern politics.
  3. 03The demand for trigger warnings undercuts the educational process when it is used to avoid or soften the inherently disturbing nature of human history and literature.
  4. 04A campus culture that equates hearing from flawed public figures with complicity in their actions replaces critical argument with social excommunication.
  5. 05The university's primary obligation is to sustain a community where claims are rigorously examined rather than simply denounced based on ideological offense.
