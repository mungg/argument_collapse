Diversified Gemini

"The United States cannot achieve gender parity in political representation through quotas or positive rights alone without first replacing the single-member plurality electoral system with proportional representation."

In her essay, Jennifer M. Piscopo makes a persuasive and much-needed argument that the United States relies far too heavily on individual ambition to solve the structural problem of women’s political underrepresentation. She looks abroad, highlighting how over 75 countries have implemented gender quotas or parity laws to achieve what the U.S. has utterly failed to do. However, in calling for "positive rights" and pointing to the success of places like Argentina, France, and Rwanda, Piscopo overlooks the fundamental mechanical engine that actually allows these quotas to work: the electoral system itself.

The primary reason the United States remains tied with Afghanistan for women’s representation is not merely a philosophical adherence to negative rights or an aversion to quotas; it is our steadfast adherence to single-member plurality districts, commonly known as First-Past-The-Post (FPTP). Unless we address the architecture of our voting system, discussions about importing global quota models will remain purely academic.

In comparative political science, the link between electoral systems and women’s representation is perhaps the most robust finding in the field. Countries that lead the world in female legislative representation almost exclusively utilize forms of Proportional Representation (PR). In a PR system, particularly a closed or flexible party-list system, districts are large and elect multiple members. Parties put forward lists of candidates, and seats are allocated proportionally based on the total vote the party receives.

It is within this multi-member, proportional framework that gender quotas thrive. If a district elects ten members, a party can easily mandate that its candidate list alternates between men and women (often called the "zipper" system). Because voters are largely choosing a party rather than an individual, party leadership has the power and the mechanical ability to enforce descriptive representation. When Argentina passed the world’s first modern gender quota in 1991, they didn't just have political will; they had a party-list PR system that made the quota practically implementable.

Contrast this with the U.S. single-member district system. Our House of Representatives is divided into 435 distinct geographical fiefdoms, each electing exactly one person. How does one apply a gender quota to a single seat? You cannot. A district cannot elect half a man and half a woman. Therefore, applying a national quota would require a central authority to dictate to local communities which districts are "allowed" to run women and which are restricted to men. In a highly decentralized, candidate-centered system where primary voters—not party bosses—select the nominees, enforcing such a mandate is mechanically impossible and politically suicidal.

Even if the Democratic or Republican national committees wanted to impose the kind of voluntary party quotas seen in Sweden or South Africa, they lack the institutional levers to do so under our current electoral laws. Ballot access in the U.S. is controlled by the states, and primary elections are binding. A party cannot simply legally bar men from running in a specific primary to meet a national quota.

Therefore, if advocates in the United States want to achieve the kind of representation Piscopo rightfully demands, the battleground must be electoral system reform, not just rhetorical shifts toward "positive rights." We must dismantle the single-member district.

The most viable path forward for the United States is the adoption of multi-member districts combined with Ranked-Choice Voting (RCV), often referred to as Single Transferable Vote (STV). Proposed federal legislation, such as the Fair Representation Act, would combine existing congressional districts into larger, multi-member districts electing three to five representatives each. Under this system, if a party wins 60 percent of the vote in a five-member district, it wins three seats.

This structural shift would instantly neutralize the "boys' club" dynamics that Piscopo rightly criticizes. In single-member districts, local party elites are famously risk-averse; they tend to clear the primary field for the "safest" candidate, which historically means a white man. But in a multi-member district, parties are incentivized to run a diverse slate of candidates to appeal to the broadest possible coalition of voters. They must balance their ticket. If a party runs only white men in a multi-member district, they will hemorrhage votes to a rival party that fields a ticket reflective of the community’s diversity.

Furthermore, RCV eliminates the "spoiler effect" and the narrative that women must "wait their turn." As Piscopo notes, women—especially women of color—are often pressured by local establishment figures to drop out of primaries lest they split the vote. With ranked-choice voting, multiple women can run in the same race without cannibalizing each other's support. Voters can rank their preferred female candidates first and second without fear of accidentally electing an ideological opponent.

Piscopo is absolutely correct that relying on EMILY’s List and the exhausting "lean in" narrative is insufficient to achieve true parity in our lifetimes. But moving from a negative to a positive conception of rights requires the institutional plumbing to support it. The international community didn't just embrace new norms of justice; they utilized proportional electoral systems that made those norms actionable. Until American reformers target the single-member district and advocate fiercely for proportional representation, the glass ceiling of U.S. politics will remain structurally intact, no matter how many women decide to run.
