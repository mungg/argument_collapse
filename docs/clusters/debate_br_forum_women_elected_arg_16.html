"Any proposal to implement gender-based positive action in the United States must be explicitly intersectional and race-conscious to avoid disproportionately benefiting white women and entrenching existing racial hierarchies."

Jennifer Piscopo’s masterful tracing of the “right to be elected” from a tool of exclusion to a demand for parity is indispensable reading. Yet for all its global sweep, the essay treats gender as a largely homogeneous category, leaving the distinctively American entanglement of race and gender under-examined. Any serious proposal to import positive action into the United States must confront the hard truth that gender quotas, without race-conscious design, will disproportionately benefit white women and may even entrench racial hierarchies. The suffragist history Piscopo recounts makes this point implicitly: the Nineteenth Amendment enfranchised women, but it left Black women in the South disenfranchised for another forty-five years, and it did nothing to address the racial terror that kept women of color from the polls and from office. A movement for women’s representation that does not center race from the start risks repeating that betrayal.

The Latin American example, while instructive, is not an uncomplicated model. In Argentina, early compliance with the quota law often meant that parties placed women in lower list positions, and when those women did get elected, they frequently came from elite families with established political dynasties—the wives, daughters, and sisters of male powerbrokers. These women were disproportionately white and wealthy in a country with a large Indigenous and Afro-descendant population. The quota law improved descriptive representation by gender, but it did not disrupt the racial and class composition of the legislature. In the United States, where white women constitute a majority of the female population and already hold the lion’s share of women’s seats, a race-blind quota would almost certainly reproduce this dynamic at scale. White women, on average, have greater access to the money, networks, and party connections that translate candidate slates into electoral victories. A mandate that parties nominate 50 percent women could easily result in a Congress that is half women and 90 percent white, a body that is numerically gender-balanced but whose women members look nothing like the multiracial coalition that has powered recent Democratic gains.

Piscopo herself acknowledges that “positive action for women necessitates simultaneous discussion of positive action for Black Americans,” but she treats this as a political impossibility rather than a starting premise. That is a mistake, because Black women in the United States have long practiced an intersectional politics that refuses to separate gender from race. It was Black women who, as historian Nancy Cohen notes, organized to run for office in the immediate aftermath of the Nineteenth Amendment, even as white suffragists declared victory and went home. It is Black women who have been the most reliable voting bloc for the Democratic Party, and who formed the backbone of the 2018 wave that saw a record number of women of color elected to Congress. Any political reform that purports to advance women’s representation but fails to address the specific barriers facing Black, Indigenous, Latina, and Asian American women is not merely incomplete; it is actively harmful, because it would siphon organizing energy and institutional resources into a framework that leaves the most marginalized women behind.

So what would an intersectional positive action look like? It would borrow from the Voting Rights Act’s insight that formal rights are empty without mechanisms to ensure their exercise. Just as Section 2 of the VRA prohibits electoral structures that dilute minority voting strength, a twenty-first-century representation agenda could promote reforms that make single-member districts more responsive to diverse electorates. Ranked-choice voting in multi-member districts, again, offers a path. Such systems allow communities of color to concentrate their votes behind candidates of choice without being packed or cracked by gerrymandering, and they create incentives for parties to recruit candidates who can appeal to multiple constituencies. The Fair Representation Act, introduced in Congress, would do this for the House, and it has the potential to increase representation for both women and people of color simultaneously—without resorting to hard quotas that trigger constitutional litigation.

Even within the current system, party-based positive action can be intersectional from the start. The Democratic Party could expand its existing gender-balance rules for internal committees to candidate recruitment targets that are explicitly race-conscious. A target of 50 percent women candidates is not enough; the party should set sub-targets for women of color, tracking not just gender but race and ethnicity in its candidate pipeline. Those targets would be voluntary, and they would operate as aspirational norms backed by the party’s significant fundraising and endorsement arms. They would answer the legitimate fear that a rising tide of women’s representation lifts only white boats.

Piscopo closes her essay by invoking the centennial as an opportunity for U.S. feminists to do what their global counterparts have done. But the opportunity is greater than she suggests. It is a chance to build a uniquely American response to underrepresentation, one that reckons with the country’s original sin of racism as inextricable from its patriarchy. A movement that insists on “women’s representation” without naming race will fracture at the first test. A movement that insists, from the start, that no woman is free until all women are free could finally deliver the multiracial democracy the Nineteenth Amendment promised but never fulfilled.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The history of U.S. suffrage demonstrates that gender-only reforms frequently leave women of color behind, as seen with the Nineteenth Amendment's failure to protect Black women's voting rights.
  2. 02International examples like Argentina show that race-blind gender quotas often result in the election of elite, white women from established political dynasties rather than a diverse legislature.
  3. 03Because white women in the U.S. have superior access to the capital and networks necessary for winning elections, a simple 50 percent gender mandate would likely result in a numerically balanced but racially homogenous Congress.
  4. 04Effective reform should utilize mechanisms like ranked-choice voting in multi-member districts to empower communities of color and women simultaneously without the legal risks of hard quotas.
  5. 05Political parties should adopt voluntary, race-conscious recruitment targets that specifically track and support the pipeline for women of color rather than focusing on gender as a homogeneous category.
