"The behavioral economics approach, which emphasizes that small design changes and rigorous randomized evaluations can dramatically improve development outcomes, offers a transformative and evidence-based path for poverty alleviation."

Glennerster and Kremer have written a lucid and important essay that deserves wide attention from policymakers, academics, and anyone concerned with improving the effectiveness of development assistance. Their central insight—that small changes in program design can produce large changes in outcomes—has profound implications for how we think about poverty alleviation.

The traditional critique of aid and development programs has been that they are insufficiently scaled, poorly funded, or undermined by government corruption. The behavioral economics perspective adds a new dimension: even well-funded programs may fail if they don't account for how people actually make decisions. A school that provides free education may still see low attendance if families face hidden costs like uniforms or if they procrastinate on coming up with the lump sum for fees. A health clinic that offers free vaccinations may see poor uptake if the clinic is inconveniently located or if parents keep postponing the visit.

The authors' emphasis on randomized evaluations deserves particular praise. The development field has been plagued by fads and ideologies unsupported by evidence—structural adjustment, community-driven development, "good governance" approaches—each championed by its proponents but rarely subjected to rigorous testing. The RCT approach forces a disciplined humility: if your program doesn't work in a well-designed evaluation, you have to admit it. This culture of evidence-testing is more likely to produce durable progress than endless cycles of innovation without measurement.

The finding that even small fees dramatically reduce adoption of health products deserves to be a central element of policy discussions. For too long, international organizations and developing-country governments maintained user fees for primary health care and education on the assumption that they were necessary for cost recovery or that those who paid would value the services more. The evidence from Kenya and elsewhere shows that this assumption is wrong. Eliminating small fees can dramatically increase utilization at modest cost, and the health and education gains justify the expenditure many times over.

The chlorine dispenser example is particularly instructive. By redesigning the delivery mechanism to reduce packaging costs, make the product more convenient to use, and harness social pressure for adoption, the program achieved adoption rates that were an order of magnitude higher than the previous approach. This kind of innovation—working within behavioral insights to redesign programs—is exactly what the development community needs more of.

One area where I wish the authors had gone further is in discussing how to build institutional capacity for this kind of evidence-based policymaking. The fact that NGOs proved more flexible than governments for conducting evaluations is suggestive but raises questions about scalability. If only NGOs can implement these programs effectively, how do we scale up to national-level policies affecting millions of people? The authors mention that countries have abolished fees "on paper only" to see informal fees creep back—addressing this institutional failure will be crucial for translating research findings into lasting policy change.

The authors are right that we should be cautious about assuming every result generalizes everywhere. But the accumulated weight of hundreds of randomized evaluations is beginning to give us a robust base of knowledge about what works and why. This is a genuine advance, and policymakers ignore it at their—and their constituents'—peril.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Development programs often fail not due to lack of funding, but because they do not account for the psychological and practical ways people actually make decisions.
  2. 02Randomized controlled trials provide a necessary culture of disciplined humility and evidence-testing that prevents the development field from falling into unproven ideological fads.
  3. 03The evidence clearly refutes the traditional assumption that user fees are necessary for cost recovery or service valuation, showing instead that eliminating small fees is a highly cost-effective way to increase utilization.
  4. 04Redesigning delivery mechanisms to prioritize convenience and social pressure, as seen in the chlorine dispenser example, can achieve significantly higher adoption rates than traditional marketing.
  5. 05The primary challenge moving forward is building institutional capacity to ensure that evidence-based insights can be scaled from flexible NGO pilots to effective national-level policies.
