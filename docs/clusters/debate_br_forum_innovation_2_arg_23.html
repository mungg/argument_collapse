Diversified DeepSeek

"While Dan Breznitz correctly identifies diverse stages of innovation beyond the Silicon Valley model, his framework overlooks the fact that inclusive prosperity is fundamentally dependent on long-term, deliberate investment in human capital and technical education."

Dan Breznitz’s essay is a welcome reminder that innovation is not synonymous with app development, and that inclusive prosperity depends on far more than chasing tech start-ups. But in his persuasive taxonomy of innovation stages, one factor lies mostly submerged: the quality and distribution of human capital. The stories of Shimano, Giant, the Riviera del Brenta, and MediaTek are at their core stories of skilled people—of the tacit knowledge, craft expertise, and engineering depth that enabled firms to dominate global supply chains. Without deliberate, long-term investment in education and training systems that produce such skills, none of Breznitz’s four stages can take root. The real oversight in innovation policy, in both Silicon Valley and its would-be mimics, is not a failure to pick the right stage, but a failure to build the human foundations.

Breznitz’s own examples illustrate the point vividly. Giant’s move into carbon fiber frames required a partnership with Taiwan’s Industrial Technology Research Institute, but that institute itself was an investment in advanced scientific and technical education. The Italian shoe district of the Riviera del Brenta succeeded because it had a deep bench of artisans, pattern-makers, and designers who could work with the world’s most demanding fashion houses. Those skills did not appear by market magic; they were cultivated through apprenticeship systems, technical schools, and a cultural respect for manual craft that took generations to build. Even stage-four assembly innovation in Shenzhen relies on an enormous workforce of technicians and engineers, trained in a national system that has made massive, though uneven, investments in technical education. Breznitz mentions education only briefly, as a “public good” among others, but it deserves far more central treatment. Without the right skills supply, any talk of local innovation ecosystems is just an empty shell.

The Silicon Valley model, for all its flaws, generated enormous demand for certain kinds of human capital—software engineering, data science, product design—and the education system, both public and private, struggled to keep up. The resulting inequality was not just about capital flows; it was also about a skills chasm between the credentialed elite and everyone else. Breznitz’s alternative rightly seeks to broaden the types of jobs that innovation can create, but it still implies a major upskilling challenge. Stage-two and stage-three innovation require, as he notes, “high-end R&D engineers” alongside shopfloor workers and craftspeople. But in many Western economies, the pipeline for mid-level technical skills has been drying up for decades. The decline of vocational education, the erosion of union apprenticeship programs, and the stigma against “blue-collar” work have left a gaping hole. A community that tries to become the next Sakai or Taichung without rebuilding that pipeline will simply import skilled workers or fail.

This human capital dimension also complicates the notion of choice in innovation stages. A region cannot simply decide to become a stage-two hub for luxury footwear or a stage-three center for semiconductor components unless it has, or can rapidly develop, the relevant skills. The policy sequencing matters. Breznitz’s advice to “foster particular capacities” glosses over the fact that capacity-building in education takes a decade or more and requires political continuity rare in democratic systems. Moreover, the skills needed for one stage may not be readily transferable to another, leaving a community vulnerable if global demand shifts. A renewed focus on broad, adaptable skills—STEM literacy, problem-solving, collaborative work—might serve as a better foundation than a narrowly targeted stage-specific strategy.

Breznitz’s Alabama example, the SiO2 Materials Science “tech teen,” is a successful public-private partnership, but it succeeded because the firm already existed, with a capable workforce and management. To generalize that model, the federal and state governments must dramatically increase funding for community colleges, technical training, and research universities oriented toward problem-solving, not just novelty. The U.S. has a long way to go; many of its peer nations, from Germany with its dual education system to Singapore with its SkillsFuture initiative, far outstrip it in linking education to industry needs. Breznitz’s essay would be strengthened by acknowledging that innovation policy without skills policy is like a car without an engine.

Finally, the human perspective introduces a moral dimension that the essay sidesteps. Inclusive prosperity is not just about job counts; it is about the dignity and agency that come from skilled work. The craftspeople of Brenta or the engineers of Shimano are not merely labor inputs; they are the bearers of a living knowledge tradition. A truly inclusive innovation policy would prioritize the cultivation of such traditions, treating human development as an end in itself, not just a means to economic output. Breznitz is right that we have more choices than the Silicon Valley evangelists admit, but the most important choice is whether to invest in the people who will actually do the innovating, at whatever stage.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The success of the global innovation hubs cited by Breznitz, such as those in Taiwan and Italy, was built on deep benches of skilled artisans and engineers produced by robust public education and apprenticeship systems.
  2. 02The inequality seen in the Silicon Valley model is driven by a skills chasm that can only be bridged by rebuilding the pipeline for mid-level technical and vocational skills.
  3. 03A community's ability to choose a specific innovation stage is constrained by its existing human capital, as capacity-building in education requires decades of political and financial commitment.
  4. 04Innovation policy must prioritize the dignity and agency of skilled work, treating human development as the primary engine of growth rather than a secondary public good.
  5. 05To generalize successful public-private partnerships, governments must dramatically increase funding for community colleges and technical training to link education directly to industry needs.
