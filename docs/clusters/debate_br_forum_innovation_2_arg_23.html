Diversified DeepSeek

"While Breznitz correctly critiques the Silicon Valley model, his proposed alternatives fail to address the underlying capitalist power structures and ownership models that inherently prioritize capital accumulation over genuine social and ecological justice."

Breznitz’s essay is a welcome departure from the boosterish nonsense that dominates innovation discourse, but it stops half a step short of the necessary radicalism. By focusing on “inclusive local prosperity” through better public-private coordination, he tinkers with the capitalist innovation machine without questioning who controls it and for whom. The real problem is not that we have picked the wrong innovation stage; it is that innovation under capitalism is structured to serve capital, and no amount of stage-two or stage-three incrementalism will alter that fundamental fact unless ownership and governance are democratized.

The essay’s examples are instructive but sanitized. Take Shimano and Giant: they are giant corporations that have achieved global dominance, in part by squeezing suppliers and workers in lower-cost locations. The thousands of local jobs they provide are embedded in global supply chains that rely on exploitation elsewhere—the very stage-four assembly hubs in China that Breznitz celebrates for their efficiency operate on razor-thin margins and often under conditions that violate basic labor rights. To present these as models of “inclusive prosperity” is to take a narrow view that counts only the jobs within the lead firm’s home country, ignoring the externalized human costs. An innovation model that is inclusive for one region while perpetuating precarity and environmental harm elsewhere is not a solution; it’s a geographical displacement of inequality.

Breznitz’s own tale of Cobalt, Ontario, points to this dynamic without fully naming it. The silver rush created a financial center in Toronto but left a ghost town and, we can presume, considerable environmental damage. The pattern repeats with high-tech: Silicon Valley’s wealth rests on a global ecosystem of mineral extraction, assembly-line drudgery, and carbon-intensive server farms. To suggest that we can merely shift the innovation focus to design, prototyping, or second-generation product improvement without addressing the underlying logic of private accumulation is to imagine that capitalism can be made fair through technocratic adjustments.

What’s missing from the essay is an analysis of power. Who decides which region specializes in which innovation stage? In practice, it is a combination of historical luck, corporate strategy, and state power, often backed by military spending. The U.S. leads in stage-one innovation not because of some natural comparative advantage, but because of decades of state-funded R&D, looser antitrust enforcement, and a financial system geared toward speculation. Breznitz’s recommendations for local ecosystems—public goods, collaborative networks, specialized training—sound appealing, but they assume that communities have the agency to chart their own course, ignoring the immense gravitational pull of existing tech giants that can hoover up talent, undercut local firms, and capture regulatory systems. A tech teen in Alabama making medical vials is great, but it remains vulnerable to acquisition by a larger firm that might then offshore production; the story ends the same way as Schwinn.

A genuinely transformative innovation policy would start from the premise that the fruits of innovation belong to the many, not the few. That means expanding worker ownership, supporting cooperatives, using public procurement to favor enterprises with good labor practices, and deliberately breaking up concentrations of power. It would also mean questioning innovation direction itself: should we be innovating more efficient ways to produce luxury footwear and carbon-fiber bikes, or should we be innovating toward ecological sustainability and democratic participation? Breznitz’s framework is neutral on such questions, treating any innovation that sustains local jobs as desirable. But what if that innovation deepens the climate crisis? What if it reinforces racial and gender hierarchies in the workforce? The Silicon Valley model is rightly criticized for its inequality effects, but inequality is not just an outcome to be managed; it is a product of who owns the means of innovation and who sets the priorities.

The essay’s greatest strength is its demolition of the Silicon Valley myth, but its positive vision remains within a liberal, managerial frame. It imagines public-private partnerships that keep capital rooted, but does not challenge capital’s ultimate prerogatives. At a time when global supply chains are fraying and trust in large institutions is collapsing, the opportunity exists to build innovation systems that are not merely locally inclusive but also democratic and ecologically responsible. That would require moving beyond Breznitz’s stage theory to a politics of innovation that asks: innovation for whom, by whom, and to what end? Without that, we are rearranging deck chairs on a ship still steered by the same old captains.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The success stories of companies like Shimano and Giant are built on a narrow definition of inclusivity that ignores the exploitation of workers and environmental harm externalized to other parts of the global supply chain.
  2. 02Technocratic adjustments to innovation stages cannot achieve lasting prosperity without addressing the underlying logic of private accumulation and the tendency for large corporations to absorb or undercut local 'tech teens.'
  3. 03The essay lacks an analysis of power, failing to recognize that the direction of innovation is dictated by corporate strategy and state power rather than local community agency.
  4. 04A truly transformative innovation policy must move beyond managerial public-private partnerships toward democratized ownership, worker cooperatives, and a focus on ecological sustainability rather than just market-driven growth.
  5. 05Innovation should not be treated as a neutral tool for job creation, but must be interrogated based on who owns the means of innovation and what social ends it serves.
