"The capabilities approach, as developed by Amartya Sen and Martha Nussbaum, provides a superior framework for achieving egalitarian goals because it addresses unjust outcomes while preserving individual autonomy and value pluralism better than Sypnowich’s 'equal flourishing' model."

Christine Sypnowich’s lead essay is a compelling plea for egalitarians to care about outcomes, not merely opportunities. I share her dissatisfaction with thin conceptions of equal opportunity that leave deep inequalities untouched, and I share her appreciation for the socialist tradition’s emphasis on genuine well-being. However, her wholesale dismissal of the opportunity framework and her advocacy of “equal flourishing” as the political goal threaten to impose a single conception of the good and to override legitimate individual choices about how to live. There is a better way to finesse the opportunity-outcome tension: the capabilities approach, as developed by Amartya Sen and Martha Nussbaum. Sypnowich’s treatment of this influential paradigm is too cursory and ultimately unjust. Properly understood, the capabilities framework addresses her core concerns—taking unjust outcomes seriously and ensuring people can actually live well—while preserving a sphere for individual agency and value pluralism that her flourishing monism would compromise.

Sypnowich notes that Sen and Nussbaum have “tailored their view to take account of choice and responsibility,” deploying “a capability to function, not functioning per se.” She seems to find this concession to opportunity misguided, indicating that in practice it amounts to a hands-off attitude of the sort she criticizes. This is a misreading. For capabilities theorists, the capability to function is not a mere formal permission; it is the real, substantive freedom to achieve various functionings, which often requires massive social provision and institutional support. When Nussbaum insists on the capability to be well-nourished, she does not simply mean that nobody is legally prevented from eating; she means that society must ensure that everyone actually has access to adequate food. If people go hungry, that is a sign that their capability has been denied, and the state has failed. This is outcome-oriented in the most important sense: the metric of justice is whether people can achieve certain functionings, and persistent failures to achieve them trigger remedial obligations.

Why, then, do capability theorists insist on the distinction between capability and functioning? Because they recognize that in many domains, a just society respects people’s choices to not exercise certain capabilities. For example, a society might ensure that everyone has the capability for sexual satisfaction and reproductive choice, but it would not forcibly impose a sexual life on those who choose celibacy. Nussbaum explicitly addresses the tricky cases where the absence of functioning might indicate a lack of capability, such as when adaptive preferences lead someone to not desire basic goods. She argues that the state should, through education and public culture, cultivate the internal capabilities needed for genuine choice, and should sometimes deliver functioning-level support for children and others who cannot yet choose. But for competent adults, the goal remains capability, not mandatory flourishing. This is not “washing its hands”; it is respecting the deep value of autonomy that Sypnowich herself acknowledges when she says “out and out coercion... should be inadmissible.”

Sypnowich’s own examples can be accommodated within the capability framework without requiring her perfectionist turn. She advocates for mandatory arts education because without it people may never discover the value of music, limiting their subsequent choices. But this is fully compatible with capabilities: the state can ensure the capability for aesthetic experience and creative self-expression by providing rich arts education to all children. The mandatory aspect is justified during childhood, when capabilities are being formed, not as an imposition on adults. Once equipped with that capability, adults can choose whether to attend concerts, play an instrument, or pursue other forms of fulfillment. There is no need for the state to go further and “promote the valuable” in a way that “disincentivizes” supposedly less valuable adult pastimes, as Sypnowich suggests. That next step crosses into paternalism and contradicts her own stated pluralism.

The capabilities approach also has a more realistic and defensible attitude toward inequality of outcome. Sypnowich calls for equal flourishing, but as Sen tirelessly argued, interpersonal differences in converting resources into flourishing—what he called “conversion factors”—mean that perfect equality of flourishing is impossible and undesirable. Some people, due to disability, metabolism, or psychological makeup, will need more resources to achieve the same level of functionings, and even then some variations in happiness and satisfaction will remain. The egalitarian aim should be to secure for each person the capability to achieve a threshold of central functionings (or equal capability to achieve functionings, depending on the version), not to make everyone equally happy or fulfilled. Sypnowich’s goal of equal flourishing is a chimera that invites either leveling down or intrusive, constant adjustment by the state. The capabilities metric offers a clearer, more implementable target for public policy: we can ask whether all citizens have the real freedom to be well nourished, healthy, educated, and politically engaged, and we can measure gaps and design policies to close them.

Sypnowich also worries that opportunity talk neglects community and solidarity. But the capabilities approach, particularly in Nussbaum’s version, includes “affiliation” as a central capability: the capability to live with and toward others, to recognize and show concern for other human beings, and to have the social bases of self-respect and non-humiliation. This directly names the social and relational goods Sypnowich values, and it demands that institutions be structured so that people can actually enjoy them—again, an outcome-sensitive demand. Nussbaum’s approach does not require a full-blown socialist community or a state-imposed ethos to pursue this; it requires legal and institutional guarantees against discrimination, together with the material support that makes dignified social participation possible.

I am sympathetic to Sypnowich’s critique of luck egalitarianism’s harshness and her call for a more generous, solidaristic politics. But the capabilities approach already provides much of what she seeks without jettisoning the liberal commitment to respecting persons as choosers. It is a mistake to lump capability theory together with the empty formalisms of “equal opportunity employer” rhetoric. Capabilities theory is a radical framework that demands extensive redistribution, public provisioning, and a focus on what people are actually able to do and be. It is the most promising synthesis of the concern for outcomes and the respect for freedom. Sypnowich’s version of equal flourishing risks becoming a new orthodoxy that would require the state to herd individuals toward a collective vision of the good. I would rather live in a society that guarantees me the real capability to pursue my own flourishing, however quirky or quiet it may be, than one that hands me a government-issued roadmap for living well.
