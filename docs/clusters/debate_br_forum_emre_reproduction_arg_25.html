"Because all reproduction is assisted, society must move beyond theoretical manifestos to establish a pragmatic feminist regulatory framework that ensures reproductive technologies are governed ethically, equitably, and transparently."

The lead essay succeeds because it refuses the stale opposition between natural reproduction and artificial reproduction. But once that opposition falls, a practical question appears: how should we govern assisted reproduction? It is not enough to say that all reproduction is assisted, or that assistance should be available without stigma. We need rules, institutions, funding structures, and clinical norms that can make reproductive assistance ethical. Otherwise, we leave patients to navigate a fragmented marketplace while calling their navigation choice.

A pragmatic feminist agenda should begin with transparency. Fertility medicine is emotionally charged, expensive, and difficult to evaluate. Patients often arrive at clinics after grief, delay, miscarriage, illness, or social exclusion. They may be willing to endure almost anything for a chance at a child. That vulnerability does not make them irrational; it makes honest counseling essential. Clinics should present age-specific success rates in clear, standardized formats. Egg freezing should be explained as possibility, not insurance in the ordinary sense. Patients should understand that frozen eggs may not survive thawing, fertilize, implant, or result in live birth. IVF patients should receive realistic probabilities by age, diagnosis, and number of embryos, not glossy baby photos.

In 2018, professional societies offer guidance, but the fertility industry still relies heavily on consumer choice and variable state regulation. That is insufficient. When clinics compete for patients, they have incentives to emphasize hope. When patients pay out of pocket, they may interpret higher price as higher quality. When success rates are publicized, clinics may select patients in ways that improve statistics. A fair system needs stronger reporting standards and oversight that protects patients without restricting access based on moral disapproval.

Second, informed consent must include burdens, not just risks. The lead essay’s descriptions of injections, blood draws, ultrasounds, ovarian swelling, weight gain, mood changes, retrieval pain, and waiting are important because public discourse often sanitizes ART. We speak of “freezing eggs” as if placing groceries in a freezer. We speak of “doing IVF” as if it were a single procedure rather than a sequence of bodily intrusions, decisions, expenses, and disappointments. Consent forms listing ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome are not enough. Patients need time, counseling, and opportunities to discuss emotional and logistical demands: daily appointments, work absences, medication timing, storage fees, decisions about unused embryos, and the possibility of failure.

Third, nondiscrimination must be built into coverage. Insurance definitions of infertility based on twelve months of heterosexual intercourse exclude lesbian couples, single people, many trans people, and others whose need for assistance does not arise from failed intercourse. This is not a technicality; it is a moral judgment disguised as medical criteria. Coverage should be based on the services required to achieve pregnancy or preserve fertility, not on whether a patient has attempted conception in the approved heterosexual manner. Fertility preservation should be available before cancer treatment, gender-affirming medical care, and other interventions likely to impair fertility. Employers that provide fertility benefits should not be allowed to structure them around marital or sexual norms.

Fourth, access should move from employment perks to health policy. The lead essay’s story of S shows both the value and the absurdity of corporate benefits. Good for S that her company covered egg freezing. Bad for everyone that such coverage depends on working for a wealthy firm in a competitive industry. Employer-based benefits produce inequality among workers and give companies undue influence over reproductive timing. A national health system, or at minimum stronger public insurance mandates, would distribute fertility care more fairly. But mandates must be designed carefully so they do not raise costs without expanding access for low-income patients. Coverage should include evaluation, medications, IUI, IVF where indicated, fertility preservation, and counseling. It should also include contraception and abortion. Reproductive medicine cannot be severed into politically acceptable and unacceptable parts.

Fifth, we need clinical competence for queer and trans patients. The lead essay’s examples of doctors assuming husbands, ignoring partners, and mishandling lesbian patients are not mere awkwardness. They degrade care. Medical schools and residency programs should train clinicians in sexual orientation, gender identity, and family diversity. Intake forms should be redesigned. Clinics should ask what terms patients use for their bodies and relationships. Partners should be recognized. Trans patients should receive accurate information about fertility effects of hormones and surgeries, including uncertainty where evidence is limited. No patient should have to choose between educating the doctor and receiving care.

Sixth, reproductive technology must be integrated with mental health support without pathologizing patients. Infertility, miscarriage, fertility preservation, transition-related fertility decisions, donor conception, and embryo disposition can be emotionally intense. Counseling should be available and affordable. But mental health screening should not become a gatekeeping tool used to deny treatment to single women, queer people, disabled people, fat people, or those with psychiatric histories. K’s encounter with a doctor who treated depression and anxiety as reasons not to reproduce shows how easily “concern” becomes eugenic judgment. The proper question is not whether a patient’s life is ideal. No parent’s life is. The question is what support would help this family thrive.

Seventh, embryo and gamete practices require clear ethical standards. Patients should decide what happens to unused embryos before crisis moments, and they should be able to revise decisions by consent where appropriate. Storage facilities must be regulated to prevent catastrophic failures; tanks of embryos and eggs are not ordinary property, even if law sometimes struggles to categorize them. Donor anonymity should be reconsidered in light of children’s interests in medical and genetic information, especially now that consumer DNA testing is making true anonymity less realistic. Donors should be protected from exploitation and given accurate information about future identifiability.

Eighth, genetic testing should be offered with humility. Preimplantation genetic testing can reduce miscarriage risk for some patients and help avoid serious genetic disease. It can also foster unrealistic expectations of control and stigmatizing attitudes toward disability. Counseling should distinguish between conditions that are well understood and probabilistic findings of uncertain meaning. It should include disability-informed perspectives. Sex selection for nonmedical reasons should be discouraged or restricted, given the global history of gender bias, even if the American context differs from countries with skewed sex ratios. As testing expands, the line between disease prevention and preference selection will become harder to maintain; we should begin drawing it democratically now.

Ninth, assisted reproduction policy must be linked to support after birth. It is incoherent to help people conceive and then deny paid leave, postpartum care, childcare, lactation support, disability services, or legal parentage. B’s delay because of maternity leave eligibility is a perfect example: her embryo transfer is governed not only by biology but by employment law. Fertility coverage without paid leave is like building a bridge halfway across a river. A complete reproductive policy includes parental leave for all parents, protections for pregnant workers, affordable childcare, and health care for children.

Finally, patients should have a voice in governance. Too often, reproductive policy is made by legislators pursuing abortion politics, physicians protecting professional discretion, insurers controlling costs, or companies branding benefits. Patients—infertile people, queer and trans parents, donor-conceived adults, disabled people, surrogates, egg donors, people who have stopped treatment, people who cannot afford treatment—should be represented in advisory bodies and institutional review. Narrative, as the lead essay argues, is not a decorative supplement to policy. It reveals where systems fail.

A pragmatic approach may sound less inspiring than manifesto. But reproductive life is lived in details: the insurance code, the clinic form, the storage bill, the leave policy, the doctor’s assumption, the consent document, the blood draw scheduled during work hours. Justice will be built or denied there.

“All reproduction is assisted” should not mean that every intervention is equally good or that technology will liberate us automatically. It should mean that we take responsibility for the assistance we already provide and the assistance we withhold. The task now is to make reproductive assistance honest, equitable, safe, noncoercive, and connected to the social supports that families need after conception succeeds. That is not a complete feminist future, but it would be a substantial beginning.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Fertility clinics must provide honest, standardized, and age-specific success rates to protect vulnerable patients from the predatory marketing of the commercial marketplace.
  2. 02Informed consent in reproductive medicine should emphasize the significant physical, emotional, and logistical burdens of treatment rather than just medical risks.
  3. 03Insurance coverage for fertility services must be decoupled from heteronormative definitions of infertility to ensure equal access for LGBTQ+ and single individuals.
  4. 04Reproductive assistance should be treated as a public health priority and a universal right rather than an elite employment perk provided by wealthy corporations.
  5. 05Medical institutions must implement clinical competence training to eliminate the discrimination and erasure experienced by queer and trans patients in reproductive settings.
  6. 06Mental health support should be integrated into fertility care as a supportive resource rather than a gatekeeping tool used to pathologize or exclude marginalized parents.
  7. 07Clear ethical and regulatory standards are required for the management of embryos, gamete donor anonymity, and the use of preimplantation genetic testing.
  8. 08Reproductive policy must be holistically linked to post-conception supports, such as paid parental leave and universal childcare, to make reproductive choice truly meaningful.
  9. 09Patients and marginalized stakeholders must have a direct voice in the governance and oversight of reproductive technologies to ensure policy reflects lived experience.
