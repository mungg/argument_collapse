Diversified Gemini

"The pursuit of 'equal flourishing' through equalized outcomes should be rejected because it necessitates an authoritarian state that overrides individual agency and pluralism."

Christine Sypnowich’s argument that we must abandon "equality of opportunity" in favor of "equality of outcomes" (specifically, "equal flourishing") rests on a foundational misreading of both the nature of a free society and the epistemological limits of government. In her zeal to eradicate inequality, she proposes a framework that is not only unworkable in practice but profoundly authoritarian in its implications. Her essay inadvertently demonstrates exactly why classical liberals, conservatives, and even pragmatic progressives have long coalesced around opportunity rather than outcomes as the bedrock of democratic justice.

The core of Sypnowich’s argument is that because our choices are shaped by our social environment, the concept of "personal responsibility" is largely a fiction. Therefore, she concludes, the state should not merely provide a level playing field, but must actively intervene to shape citizens' choices and ensure they arrive at specific, "valuable" destinations. She admits this means the state must abandon "neutrality" and actively "take a stand on how people should live."

This is a breathtaking concession, and one that should set off alarm bells for anyone who values pluralism. Sypnowich’s framework assumes the existence of an enlightened administrative class capable of objectively determining what constitutes a "flourishing" life, and possessing the moral authority to "incentivize," "educate," and "support" citizens into aligning with this vision. She casually uses mandatory music lessons as a benign example, but the underlying principle—that the state knows what is best for your soul and has a mandate to enforce it—has a very dark historical pedigree.

In a diverse, modern society, there is no consensus on the definition of flourishing. For some, it is material accumulation; for others, religious devotion; for others, familial connection; and for still others, creative solitude. When Sypnowich asserts that the egalitarian community "must be prepared to revise what counts as valuable," she is describing a process whereby a political majority imposes its cultural and moral preferences on everyone else. If the state is empowered to aggressively promote "valuable" ways of living and disincentivize others, marginalized groups—who inevitably possess minority views on what constitutes a good life—will be the first to have their preferred modes of flourishing deemed "unvaluable" by the bureaucratic apparatus.

Furthermore, Sypnowich’s dismissal of the market as merely an "unaccountable" force that "constrains" choices ignores the reality of what markets actually do. The market is not a sentient monster; it is the aggregate mechanism through which millions of individuals peacefully express their preferences and coordinate their labor. Free exchange allows for a multiplicity of "flourishing" paths exactly because it is decentralized. Sypnowich prefers "representative political institutions," but political institutions are inherently majoritarian and coercive. Moving power from the decentralized market to the centralized state does not eliminate constraint; it simply replaces the constraint of resource scarcity with the constraint of bureaucratic dictates.

Sypnowich also fundamentally misrepresents the concept of equality of opportunity. She paints it as a callous, sink-or-swim "luck egalitarianism" that leaves the unfortunate to suffer the consequences of their mistakes. But a robust commitment to equality of opportunity is perfectly compatible with a strong social safety net. One can believe that individuals should be held responsible for their economic choices while simultaneously supporting policies that ensure no one falls below a baseline standard of living. Providing universal access to healthcare, strong public education, and unemployment insurance are all ways to enhance equality of opportunity. We do not need to jettison the concepts of personal responsibility and agency to justify helping those in need.

What we *do* need personal responsibility for is maintaining the dynamism and basic functioning of society. Sypnowich notes with dismay that some people view the mandate "those who do not work do not eat" favorably, contrasting it with Marx's ideal where contribution is severed from distribution. But a society that severs contribution from reward quickly finds itself with a dearth of contribution. The "talent plus effort" model may not perfectly capture the nuance of human existence, but it captures enough of it to function as a necessary incentive structure. Innovation, hard work, and the taking of calculated risks are what generate the very resources Sypnowich wishes to distribute. If outcomes are equalized regardless of effort, the engine of prosperity stalls.

Sypnowich's essay is a useful clarifying document. It reveals the endgame of a certain strain of left-wing thought: the belief that freedom is too messy, that people cannot be trusted to make their own choices, and that the state must be enlisted to mold humanity into a prescribed state of grace. Equality of opportunity may be an imperfect and difficult ideal to realize, but it remains the only egalitarian framework compatible with a free, pluralistic society. Equalizing outcomes does not elevate the disadvantaged; it merely levels the diverse vibrancy of human life down to the gray specifications of the state.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A state-mandated vision of flourishing requires an enlightened administrative class to impose its moral preferences on a diverse citizenry, threatening marginalized groups.
  2. 02Replacing decentralized market choices with centralized political institutions replaces the natural constraint of scarcity with the coercive constraint of bureaucratic dictates.
  3. 03Equality of opportunity is not inherently callous and can be paired with a strong social safety net without abandoning the essential concepts of personal responsibility and agency.
  4. 04Severing the link between individual contribution and reward destroys the incentive structures necessary to generate the resources a society needs to function and prosper.
  5. 05The 'equal flourishing' model relies on a foundational misreading of human nature that views freedom as a problem to be solved by state molding rather than a right to be protected.
