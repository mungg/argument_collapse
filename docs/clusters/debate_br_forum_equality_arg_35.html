"The liberal egalitarian focus on equality of opportunity should be replaced by a socialist orientation toward equal flourishing, as the former fails to address substantive inequality and ignores the social nature of human agency."

Sypnowich's essay arrives at exactly the right moment. The past decade has witnessed a conspicuous retreat from distributive ambition in progressive politics—a pivot toward opportunity rhetoric that has allowed genuine inequality to flourish under the guise of procedural fairness. Her diagnosis is essentially correct: the liberal egalitarian framework, for all its theoretical sophistication, has proven incapable of delivering substantive equality because it systematically excuses outcome disparities. What we need is not a refinement of opportunity-based thinking but a wholesale reorientation toward flourishing as the metric of egalitarian success.

The most compelling section of Sypnowich's argument concerns the instability of the choice-circumstance distinction. Luck egalitarianism—the dominant variant of liberal egalitarianism since the 1980s—depends on our ability to separate what individuals freely choose from the conditions that shape those choices. But this distinction crumbles under scrutiny. Our preferences, dispositions, and even the framework within which we make decisions are themselves products of social circumstance. A person raised in poverty who develops a "short-term" orientation toward consumption is not exercising an autonomous preference distinct from their circumstances; they are responding rationally to the conditions they have inherited. To hold such individuals responsible for choices that are clearly products of their social position is not to respect autonomy but to compound injustice.

Sypnowich is right to invoke Marx here. The socialist tradition has always understood that freedom requires the material conditions for its exercise. Formal liberty—the right to choose—means nothing without the substantive capacity to make meaningful choices. A hunger-stricken person has the formal right to purchase organic produce; this right is worthless. The flourishing approach captures this insight by insisting that we evaluate egalitarianism by what people actually achieve, not merely by what options they nominally possess.

The author's discussion of solidarity deserves particular attention. Liberal egalitarianism treats equality as a matter of institutional design—rules that distribute opportunities fairly—rather than as a feature of social relationships. This produces a curiously atomized vision of justice: individuals competing on a level field, with the state ensuring procedural fairness but remaining indifferent to whether anyone actually thrives. The flourishing vision, by contrast, insists that equality is inherently relational. We are not just in the world as isolated choosers; we are members of a community whose well-being is bound together. The rich can flourish in gated isolation, but genuine equality requires that flourishing be shared—that the community as a whole advances or fails together.

I would push Sypnowich's argument further on one point: the flourishing approach should be explicitly political, not merely about individual well-being. Equality of flourishing cannot be achieved through technocratic redistribution alone; it requires democratic participation as both a means and an end. People flourish not just by consuming goods but by exercising political agency, by participating in collective decision-making, by having genuine voice in the institutions that shape their lives. The flourishing approach thus demands not just a welfare state but a democratic transformation of the economy and polity.

One objection Sypnowich should address more directly: the charge of paternalism. If we judge societies by flourishing outcomes and intervene to improve those outcomes, are we not imposing a vision of the good life on reluctant citizens? The essay gestures at pluralism—flourishing comes in many forms—but critics will ask how we distinguish valuable from debased forms of flourishing without arbitrarily privileging some preferences over others. My response would be that this objection applies with equal force to the opportunity framework. Neutrality about flourishing is itself a controversial stance; it implicitly favors market-conforming preferences over others. The question is not whether we take stands on value but whether our stands are democratically legitimated and respectful of human dignity. The flourishing approach, properly understood, is committed to open deliberation about what constitutes flourishing and to revising that understanding in light of democratic participation.

The essay's conclusion—that the egalitarian ideal requires socialism—is rhetorically strong but perhaps underspecified. What form of socialism? Democratic socialism? Market socialism? Libertarian municipalism? Each has different implications for how we might achieve flourishing equality. Nonetheless, Sypnowich is surely right that the current arrangement—capitalist economies with residual welfare measures—is fundamentally incompatible with the aspiration to equal flourishing. The market is not a neutral mechanism for distributing opportunity; it actively shapes preferences, alienates workers, and generates hierarchical relationships fundamentally at odds with egalitarian solidarity. Replacing it with democratic control over the conditions of production is not a utopian fantasy but the logical implication of taking flourishing seriously.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The distinction between choice and circumstance used by liberal egalitarians is unstable because individual preferences and decisions are themselves products of social conditions.
  2. 02Formal liberty and the right to choose are meaningless without the substantive material conditions required to exercise that freedom effectively.
  3. 03Equality should be viewed as a relational feature of community solidarity rather than a set of procedural rules for atomized individuals competing for resources.
  4. 04A flourishing-based approach must incorporate democratic participation and political agency as essential components of well-being rather than relying solely on technocratic redistribution.
  5. 05The charge of paternalism against the flourishing approach is misplaced because the supposed neutrality of the opportunity framework implicitly favors market-conforming values.
  6. 06Achieving equal flourishing necessitates a socialist transformation of the economy because capitalist markets inherently generate hierarchies and alienate individuals from the conditions of their own development.
