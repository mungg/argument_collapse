"Amnesty for irregular migrants should not be an automatic entitlement based solely on the passage of time, but rather a process that balances the migrants' social membership with their ongoing moral obligations to respect the rule of law."

Carens's argument has a blind spot that is shared by much of the amnesty literature: it focuses almost exclusively on the moral claims of irregular migrants while ignoring their moral obligations. This asymmetry is philosophically troubling and practically consequential.

Consider the parallel Carens himself draws to statutes of limitations for criminal offenses. He argues that just as the state loses the right to prosecute old crimes, it should lose the right to deport long-settled irregular migrants. But statutes of limitations apply to offenses where the perpetrator has typically ceased the harmful activity. A murderer who committed a crime twenty years ago is no longer murdering; the harm has ended. By contrast, an irregular migrant who entered without authorization continues to reside in the country without authorization every single day. The offense, such as it is, is ongoing.

This matters because it suggests that the appropriate response may not be amnesty (which forgives the offense) but something closer to "tolling" (which pauses the enforcement clock during the period of apparent settlement). If the state's interest in controlling borders is legitimate, then the passage of time should make deportation less desirable, not less permissible. A policy that acknowledges the moral claims of long-term residents while still requiring them to take some affirmative step toward regularization—paying fines, demonstrating employment, learning the language—would respect both the migrants' social membership and the state's legitimate interest in the rule of law.

Carens resists discretionary criteria because he fears discriminatory application, but this concern does not support an automatic entitlement. The answer to potential abuse is to structure discretion carefully, not to eliminate it entirely. A system with clear criteria but individual assessment is not the same as arbitrary bureaucratic power.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The analogy to statutes of limitations is flawed because irregular residence is an ongoing offense rather than a past act that has ceased.
  2. 02The passage of time should make deportation less desirable as a policy choice rather than morally or legally impermissible for the state.
  3. 03A just regularization process should require migrants to take affirmative steps, such as paying fines or demonstrating employment, to acknowledge the state's legitimate interest in border control.
  4. 04The risk of discriminatory application should be addressed by carefully structuring discretionary criteria rather than eliminating individual assessment in favor of automatic amnesty.
