"While Aziz Rana correctly identifies that reclaiming freedom for the left requires institution building rather than just better messaging, his proposal may underestimate the difficulty of creating these institutions within a landscape where the structural and psychological legacy of empire has systematically eroded the capacity for collective agency."

Aziz Rana's essay provides a sweeping account of how American empire has shaped exclusionary visions of freedom, linking settler conquest to contemporary right-wing politics with impressive historical depth. His central argument—that the left's challenge is fundamentally one of institution building rather than messaging—will ring true to anyone who has watched progressive movements struggle to make solidaristic ideals feel lived rather than abstract. Yet the essay's policy prescriptions, while sensible, may undersell how profoundly the terrain has shifted. Reclaiming freedom as a left vocabulary requires not only strengthening unions and expanding democratic participation but also confronting the structural conditions that make such strengthening possible in the first place.

The essay's most penetrating insight concerns the way empire naturalized atomized accounts of liberty. When white settlers could simply move west to escape state constraints, freedom literally meant self-sufficiency. When mid-century prosperity derived from global dominance rather than domestic solidarity, individual striving seemed like the only legible path to self-rule. This history matters because it explains why solidaristic alternatives have repeatedly lost ground: they require people to see themselves as fundamentally interdependent, a self-conception empire actively foreclosed. The essay rightly notes that today's opening derives partly from the collapse of that old bargain—fewer Americans now benefit materially from U.S. global primacy, and the wars fought in freedom's name have brought devastation rather than prosperity. But this opening is also a crisis: when the promise of individual advancement through empire no longer delivers, what replaces it?

Rana emphasizes that inclusive freedom narratives have historically gained traction among marginalized communities and during moments when the old settlement bargain collapsed. The civil rights movement, the pre-New Deal period, the social democratic revival of recent years—all represent times when people could imagine collective agency as a genuine possibility. This framing is valuable, but it also highlights a paradox. The essay argues that the primary task is building institutions where collective self-rule can be experienced. But such institutions themselves depend on prior shifts in consciousness and political will. How, then, do you generate the conditions for institution building when the institutions that might generate those conditions have been systematically weakened?

The essay gestures toward this difficulty when noting that today's activists operate within a landscape "dramatically altered by neoliberal ascendance and decades of Reaganite cultural and political dominance." It is telling, however, that the most potent left invocations of freedom it identifies involve immediate, defensive struggles—against police violence, for reproductive rights, against LGBTQ+ targeting—rather than positive visions of transformed society. These movements work precisely because they respond to direct threats, mobilizing solidarity through shared vulnerability. But they also risk reinforcing a transactional politics in which freedom means protection from harm rather than expansion of agency. The question is whether defensive mobilization can evolve into the kind of institution building Rana envisions, or whether the conditions that make the latter possible require transformations that defensive struggles alone cannot produce.

One underexplored dimension concerns the relationship between domestic political possibilities and the global order Rana rightly insists we cannot ignore. The essay emphasizes anti-imperial ethics and immigrant freedom, but its framing remains largely national. What would it mean to actually practice internationalist solidarity in a country whose prosperity has always depended on external extraction? The Freedom Budget's vision of redirecting military spending toward human needs is compelling, but it assumes a political community capable of collective self-limitation. Can such a community exist within an empire that structures its members' identities around dominance? Perhaps the deepest challenge is not institutional but psychological: breaking the psychic hold of empire on American self-understanding so that freedom might finally mean something other than the sword directed outward.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The historical link between American empire and atomized liberty has created a deep-seated self-conception of independence that makes solidaristic alternatives difficult for citizens to envision.
  2. 02There is a strategic paradox in the idea that we must build institutions to foster collective agency when those very institutions require a prior, currently absent, level of collective political will to establish.
  3. 03Current left-wing invocations of freedom are primarily defensive and reactive to immediate threats, which risks limiting the concept of freedom to protection from harm rather than the positive expansion of democratic agency.
  4. 04Truly reclaiming freedom requires breaking the psychological hold of empire on the American identity, as domestic solidarity is fundamentally at odds with a national identity structured around global dominance and extraction.
