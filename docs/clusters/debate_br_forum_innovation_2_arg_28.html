Diversified DeepSeek

"While Breznitz correctly critiques the Silicon Valley model, his proposed four-stage innovation framework is largely inapplicable to developing nations because it ignores the prerequisite of foundational structural development and state capacity."

Dan Breznitz’s forum essay arrives at a moment when policymakers around the world are desperately seeking alternatives to the extractive, inequality-deepening model of high-tech development. His four-stage framework is elegant and his examples vivid. But reading this from the perspective of a developing country—say, a mid-sized African or South Asian nation—the argument feels curiously flat. It assumes a world where communities already possess the foundational infrastructure, institutional density, and industrial base needed to “choose” an innovation stage. For most of the globe, that choice is a luxury. The real Silicon Valley error is not that the model is overhyped; it is that the entire conversation about innovation-led growth ignores the prior problem of structural underdevelopment.

Consider Breznitz’s key examples. Shimano and Giant emerged in Japan and Taiwan, countries that by the 1970s already had strong educational systems, functioning bureaucracies, and significant manufacturing experience. The Riviera del Brenta drew on centuries of artisanal tradition and proximity to Europe’s wealthiest markets. MediaTek’s success was enabled by decades of state investment in semiconductor technology in Taiwan, a process itself heavily shaped by Cold War geopolitics and U.S. aid. Even the Israeli story, however cautionary, presupposed a highly educated population and deep integration with U.S. financial markets. None of these are starting conditions available to a typical low-income country today, where basic electricity is unreliable, credit markets dysfunctional, and the state unable to coordinate a local innovation ecosystem even if it had the will.

The essay’s policy recommendations—shared specialized facilities, collaborative public spaces, institutionalized networks—are sensible for advanced economies or emerging markets with strong state capacity. But they read like a fantasy in a place where the government cannot reliably pay teachers or maintain roads. In many such contexts, the immediate priority is not cultivating stage-two or stage-three innovation capacity in a specific industry; it is building the generic capacities—infrastructure, health, basic education—that make any innovation possible. Breznitz’s framework implicitly presumes these as background conditions, but for billions of people, they are the central challenge. The “fetishism” he critiques is a rich-world problem; the rest of the world still needs the basics.

Furthermore, the globalization that has allowed communities to specialize in particular innovation stages has simultaneously locked many developing nations into stage-four assembly or simple extraction, precisely the roles Breznitz admits are least likely to yield inclusive prosperity. His examples of Chinese stage-four success—Shenzhen’s rapid manufacturing prowess—are presented approvingly, but China’s ascent was built on a unique combination of authoritarian state capacity, massive internal migration, and an undervalued currency that few democracies can replicate. For a Tanzania or a Bolivia, the global structure of production channels them into raw materials and low-value assembly, and the Breznitz menu of innovation stages looks like a set of doors that are welded shut. The advice to “understand and react to changing circumstances” is cold comfort when your only leverage is a desperate offer of cheap labor and tax holidays.

Even the essay’s celebration of “tech teens” and local embeddedness can ring hollow when the global economy is dominated by corporations with supply-chain power that dwarfs national GDPs. The SiO2 Materials Science example from Alabama is heartening but it required a $143 million contract from the world’s richest government. Where does that leave a country without such fiscal firepower? The implicit message is that well-targeted public investment can nurture local champions, but the global playing field is tilted to ensure that most such champions are bought out or crushed.

Does this mean Breznitz’s argument has no value for the developing world? Not entirely. His debunking of the Silicon Valley myth is vital because so many fledgling tech hubs in Nairobi, Lagos, and Dhaka are being sold the same false dream. But the alternative must be more radical than choosing a different column in his table. It requires a global development architecture that allows for infant-industry protection, technology transfer on fair terms, and the policy space to build those elusive foundational capacities. It also means that rich countries must stop preaching innovation stage specialization while blocking the very product standards and intellectual property reforms that would allow poorer nations to climb the ladder. Breznitz’s essay is a fine guide for the already wealthy; for everyone else, the conversation needs to start much earlier in the chain.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The successful examples cited, such as Taiwan and Italy, relied on pre-existing institutional density, educational systems, and industrial bases that most low-income countries currently lack.
  2. 02Policy recommendations like shared production facilities and collaborative networks are unrealistic in contexts where basic infrastructure, such as reliable electricity and roads, is not yet established.
  3. 03The global economic structure often locks developing nations into low-value extraction or assembly, making the choice to move into higher innovation stages a luxury they cannot access.
  4. 04The 'tech teen' model of growth requires significant fiscal firepower and public investment that most developing governments cannot afford to provide.
  5. 05True inclusive prosperity for the developing world requires a radical shift in global development architecture, including technology transfer and intellectual property reform, rather than just a different innovation strategy.
