"While genetic engineering is a safe and potentially valuable tool for addressing crop failure, it must be integrated into a broader food justice framework that addresses the underlying socio-political causes of hunger rather than being treated as a standalone technical solution."

Pamela Ronald begins with a farmer whose bananas are dying. That is the right place to begin. Too much debate about genetic engineering starts in the supermarket aisle, among people for whom food choice is an expression of identity. For many families, food is not identity; it is survival. A technology that might protect a staple crop from disease deserves serious consideration.

But beginning with hunger should also make us careful. Hunger is rarely caused by a lack of technology alone. People go hungry because they are poor, because land is unequally distributed, because women lack power, because roads and storage are inadequate, because war disrupts planting, because prices spike, because governments neglect rural communities. Improved seed can help, sometimes enormously. But when biotechnology is presented as the answer to hunger, it risks narrowing a political problem into a technical one.

Ronald does not wholly make that mistake. She acknowledges the need for public investment and regulation. Still, the essay’s momentum runs toward a familiar story: science has solutions; activists block them; the poor suffer. That story contains truth, but it is incomplete. The poor also suffer when technologies are developed without their participation, when seed systems become dependent on outside suppliers, when local crops are neglected because they are not profitable, and when development agencies prefer visible technical fixes to slower investments in health, education, and land rights.

Consider Golden Rice. Vitamin A deficiency is devastating. If rice enriched with beta-carotene can safely and effectively reduce blindness and death, it should not be vandalized or blocked for ideological reasons. Yet vitamin A deficiency is not simply a rice problem. It is a poverty and diet-diversity problem. Children need access to vegetables, fruits, fats that help absorption, breastfeeding, sanitation, and health care. Supplementation programs have saved lives where they reach people. Home gardens and public health systems matter. Golden Rice may be a useful complement, but it should not become a substitute for the broader work of reducing poverty.

The same is true of disease-resistant crops. A wilt-resistant banana could be a blessing. But a farming system built around one crop is vulnerable even when that crop is improved. Diversity is a form of insurance. Agroforestry, mixed cropping, local seed conservation, farmer field schools, and better market access may do as much for resilience as a single resistance gene. The choice should not be genetic engineering versus traditional knowledge. The best development work often combines scientific breeding with farmers’ own experimentation.

Ronald is persuasive on one point that food justice advocates should accept: rejecting genetic engineering as inherently unsafe is a mistake. It is not progressive to frighten people with weak health claims. The Séralini rat study was widely criticized for good reasons. Major scientific organizations have found approved GE foods safe to eat. If we care about poor communities, we should not base policy on rumors. Poor farmers and consumers are often the first to pay for rich-world misinformation.

But biotechnology advocates should also accept a food justice point: “safe to eat” is not the same as “just.” A crop can pass toxicological review and still be part of an unjust system. Herbicide-tolerant soybeans may be safe as food while contributing to land consolidation, deforestation pressures, or rural displacement in some regions. Bt cotton may reduce insecticide poisoning but fail farmers if seed is too expensive, rainfall is poor, credit is predatory, or markets collapse. Evaluating justice requires looking beyond the gene to the livelihood.

Labeling reflects this broader issue. Ronald sees GMO labels as misleading warnings. Sometimes they are used that way. But consumers may want to know whether a food is connected to patent regimes, herbicide-intensive farming, or companies they distrust. Those concerns are not reducible to health risk. A neutral label would not solve food injustice, but transparency is usually better than secrecy. At the same time, labels in wealthy countries should not become trade barriers that discourage poor countries from using beneficial crops. The design matters.

The more important label, however, is the one we rarely see: who controlled the research, who owns the seed, and who benefits? A publicly developed, locally adapted, royalty-free disease-resistant cassava is ethically different from a proprietary crop sold through restrictive contracts. Both may be “GMO.” The category hides more than it reveals.

A food justice approach would set several conditions for supporting genetically engineered crops. First, the problem should be defined with affected farmers and communities, not only by scientists or donors. Second, the crop should be compared with non-GE alternatives, including agroecological ones. Third, licensing should protect affordability and farmer rights. Fourth, biosafety review should be independent and transparent. Fifth, deployment should be accompanied by extension, monitoring, and attention to gender and local food culture. Sixth, biotechnology should not drain resources from proven public health and rural development programs.

Under those conditions, many GE crops might deserve support. Others would not. The point is not to be pro-GMO or anti-GMO. The point is to be pro-poor, pro-democracy, and pro-ecology.

Ronald’s warning is important: ideological opposition can prevent useful tools from reaching people who need them. But there is an equal and opposite warning: technological enthusiasm can prevent us from seeing the social arrangements that make people vulnerable in the first place. Mama Moses needs healthy bananas. She may also need extension services, fair prices, crop diversity, secure land, and political voice.

If genetic engineering helps provide one piece of that larger justice, let us use it. But let us not mistake one piece for the whole.
