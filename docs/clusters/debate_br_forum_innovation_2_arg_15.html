"While Dan Breznitz correctly identifies the flaws of the Silicon Valley model, his proposed alternative of stage-based innovation policy still places an unrealistic burden on local governments to solve systemic economic problems that require national-level social and fiscal solutions."

Dan Breznitz’s essay is a useful antidote to the “next Silicon Valley” fever that has infected local economic development. Yet the very persuasiveness of his examples raises a different worry: perhaps the problem is not only that cities are choosing the wrong innovation model. Perhaps we ask far too much of local innovation policy in the first place.

Every few years a new development vocabulary sweeps through city halls and regional planning agencies. First it was industrial parks, then science parks, then clusters, then creative cities, then accelerators, then smart cities, then innovation districts. Each language contains some truth. Firms do benefit from proximity. Skills accumulate in places. Public institutions can help coordinate private action. But the recurring pattern should make us cautious. Local leaders are repeatedly told that if they identify the right niche and build the right ecosystem, they can reverse decades of deindustrialization, fiscal strain, racial segregation, and labor market polarization. That is an enormous burden to place on any city.

Breznitz does not promise a magic bullet. On the contrary, he warns against rigid templates. Still, his essay may leave readers with more confidence in place-based innovation strategy than is warranted. Shimano in Sakai, Giant in Taichung, luxury shoe producers in Brenta, furniture makers in Alto Livenza, semiconductor firms in Taiwan, and manufacturers in the Pearl River Delta are impressive. But success stories have a way of concealing the graveyard of failed strategies that pursued similar ambitions. For every region that becomes a specialized hub, many others invest in training centers, research institutes, incubators, and public-private partnerships that never achieve critical mass. The difference between a wise bet and a costly boondoggle is often visible only afterward.

This is not an argument for laissez-faire. It is an argument for humility. Local economies are embedded in national and global systems that cities do not control. Exchange rates, trade rules, federal research spending, immigration policy, antitrust enforcement, health care costs, capital markets, tax law, and macroeconomic demand all shape local possibilities. A mayor can convene manufacturers, support a community college program, and streamline permitting. She cannot by herself reverse shareholder pressure for offshoring, create a national apprenticeship system, discipline monopolistic platforms, or guarantee demand for domestically produced goods. When local innovation strategies fail, it is often because they are trying to solve problems at the wrong scale.

The United States is especially prone to this error because its federal system combines local responsibility with limited local capacity. Cities are expected to compete for investment, subsidize firms, maintain infrastructure, educate workers, manage poverty, and absorb the social costs of economic change. But their fiscal tools are weak and their tax bases are mobile. The result is a destructive competition in which jurisdictions offer incentives to companies that would have expanded somewhere anyway. Amazon’s HQ2 search was only the most theatrical example. The deeper problem is routine: local governments bidding against one another for jobs, often with little evidence that subsidies produce net benefits.

Breznitz’s preferred approach—building collective assets rather than simply courting big firms—is better than smokestack chasing or start-up boosterism. But even collective assets can become monuments to wishful thinking. A specialized training facility is valuable if employers need those skills and workers can get jobs. It is wasteful if built around an imagined future industry that never arrives. A shared prototyping center can help firms innovate if there is already a base of firms capable of using it. It cannot conjure an industry from thin air. The language of ecosystems sometimes obscures this: real ecosystems evolve over time; they are not assembled by consultants on a three-year grant cycle.

There is also a distributive question. Suppose a region successfully identifies a niche in stage two design or stage three component innovation. Suppose it creates a cluster that employs thousands. That is good. But what about the regions that cannot? What about towns whose industrial base is too thin, whose young people have left, whose local institutions are weak, whose infrastructure has decayed, whose schools are underfunded? A national prosperity strategy cannot consist of telling every place to find its unique innovation stage. Some places will not win that competition, no matter how intelligently they plan.

This is where the Cobalt story is both illuminating and misleading. Cobalt boomed and declined; Toronto converted the boom into enduring financial capacity. But not every Cobalt can become a Toronto, and not every region touched by extraction can retain the high-value functions. The modern economy tends to concentrate advanced services, headquarters, research, and finance in a limited number of metropolitan areas. That concentration is not solely the result of local choices. It reflects cumulative causation: skilled workers go where opportunities are; firms go where skilled workers are; investors go where firms are; amenities follow income; and the cycle continues. Policy can alter this pattern at the margins, but it cannot make agglomeration disappear.

A more honest approach would distinguish between three goals that are often blurred: helping people, helping places, and helping firms. Sometimes these goals align. Supporting a local manufacturer may preserve jobs and strengthen a community. But sometimes they diverge. A worker in a declining town may be better served by wage subsidies, health care, child care, relocation assistance, or remote-work infrastructure than by a speculative attempt to build a local innovation cluster. A place may need fiscal stabilization, environmental cleanup, broadband, schools, and hospitals more urgently than an accelerator. A firm may become more competitive by automating in ways that reduce employment. Calling all of this “innovation policy” can prevent clear thinking.

The pandemic has made this distinction urgent. COVID-19 exposed weaknesses in supply chains and the dangers of relying too heavily on distant production for critical goods. It also exposed the fragility of care systems, public health, housing, and low-wage service work. Many of the people who kept society functioning were not employed in innovation sectors at all. They were nurses, aides, grocery clerks, warehouse workers, delivery drivers, teachers, janitors, farmworkers, and public employees. Inclusive prosperity cannot depend primarily on whether their region finds a clever entry point into global production networks. It requires universal social supports and labor standards.

This is the strongest case for national policy. A child allowance, stronger unemployment insurance, universal health coverage, affordable child care, wage floors, labor rights, and investments in public education would do more for many struggling communities than another round of innovation district planning. So would infrastructure investment that improves daily life: safe water, reliable transit, broadband, parks, flood protection, and energy-efficient housing. These policies are not glamorous, but they reduce the stakes of local economic competition. They make it possible for people to live decent lives even if their city does not become a global hub for bicycle components or luxury shoes.

None of this means local economic development is pointless. Regions should absolutely map their capabilities, support existing firms, invest in skills, and build institutions that encourage learning. Breznitz is right that copying Silicon Valley is foolish and that innovation happens in many stages. His framework can help local leaders avoid wasting money on generic start-up theater. It can redirect attention to firms that make, improve, repair, design, and scale things. That is a real contribution.

But we should resist the implication that every locality’s future depends on selecting the right innovation niche. Some regions can and should pursue that path. Others need stabilization more than transformation. Still others may need to help residents connect to opportunity elsewhere, including through remote work or mobility assistance. A humane policy would not treat leaving as failure or staying as virtue in all cases. People should have the real freedom to choose.

The danger of the Silicon Valley myth is that it tells places they can all become winners in a high-tech tournament. The danger of a broader innovation myth is subtler: it tells places they can all become winners if only they choose the right stage of the value chain. But capitalist development produces uneven geography. Public policy can mitigate and redirect it, but not abolish it through local strategy alone.

The better lesson is this: build capabilities where there is a plausible foundation; stop subsidizing fantasies; provide universal supports everywhere; and recognize that the dignity of communities should not depend on their success in global competition. Innovation matters. But it is not a substitute for solidarity.
