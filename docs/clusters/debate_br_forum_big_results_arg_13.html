"While behavioral economics and randomized evaluations offer powerful tools for poverty alleviation, their use in developing nations raises urgent ethical concerns regarding paternalism, power asymmetries, and the lack of accountability in who designs these behavioral 'nudges.'"

**Yes, Small Changes — But Whose Small Changes?**

I want to take Glennerster and Kremer at their word: that small adjustments to defaults, incentives, and convenience can produce dramatic changes in behavior, and that this is one of the most robust findings of the experimental development literature. I think they are right. The evidence is strong. What I want to press them on is a question they do not directly engage: who decides which small changes to make, and on whose authority?

Behavioral economics, in its developed-world applications, has always sat uneasily on a paternalist edge. Thaler and Sunstein tried to defuse the worry with the phrase "libertarian paternalism" — the idea that choice architectures inevitably exist, so we may as well design them to promote welfare, while leaving people free to opt out. Even in the U.S. context, this framing has been contested. Cass Sunstein, defending it, has had to argue carefully that the chooser remains sovereign. But when we move from automatic 401(k) enrollment for middle-class Americans to lentil bribes for Rajasthani mothers, the power asymmetries become much sharper, and the question of who is nudging whom becomes much harder to wave away.

Consider the Rajasthan immunization study Glennerster and Kremer cite as a triumph. Full immunization rates rose from 5 percent to 38 percent when mothers received a kilogram of lentils per visit and a set of plates at the end. The authors present this as a behavioral solution: present-biased mothers procrastinate on immunization; small incentives overcome the procrastination; everyone wins. But notice what is implicit. We — the researchers, the NGO, the development economists — have decided that the right behavior is full immunization. We have decided that the deviation from this behavior is a defect to be corrected. We have designed an incentive to correct it. And we have done so for some of the poorest, least politically powerful people on earth, who were not consulted about the choice architecture being designed for them.

The proximate justification is that immunization saves lives, and the mothers themselves, on reflection, would presumably endorse it. Perhaps. But this justification depends on a particular theory of what their behavior reveals. The behavioral story is that their non-vaccination expresses present bias — a failure to act on preferences they themselves would endorse. An alternative story is that their non-vaccination expresses rational distrust of unreliable public clinics, doubts about side effects, or rival demands on their scarce time. The lentil incentive does not distinguish between these stories. It just changes the behavior. If the behavior was rational on the original information set, the incentive has manipulated rather than empowered.

This may seem like an overly philosophical objection to a program that is, after all, saving children's lives. I am not arguing against immunization. I am arguing for explicitness about what we are doing. The randomista literature has, almost unintentionally, become a powerful technology of behavioral modification aimed at the world's poor. It works. The question is what kind of accountability accompanies its use.

In the U.S. case, automatic 401(k) enrollment passes through Congress, employer benefit committees, and individual opt-out provisions. In the Rajasthan case, the choice architecture is designed by economists at a Cambridge or Boston institution, implemented through an Indian NGO, and presented to mothers who have effectively no voice in its design. We tell ourselves that we are correcting behavioral biases. We could equally well say we are exploiting them: identifying the small interventions that produce large behavioral changes is, after all, the same skill set whether one's goals are benign or otherwise. The current generation of randomistas has, by and large, benevolent intentions. The next generation, working for governments or corporations with less benevolent goals, will inherit the same toolkit.

There is also the question of which behaviors get targeted. Glennerster and Kremer's examples are mostly uncontroversially good: get children vaccinated, get girls in school, get water chlorinated. But the methodology is agnostic about goals. It will work equally well to nudge consumers into buying products that are not in their interest, to nudge voters into electoral choices they would otherwise reject, or to nudge populations into demographic behaviors deemed desirable by elites. The Chinese government has been a careful consumer of behavioral economics. So have political consultancies in many countries. The randomista community has, as far as I can tell, given little thought to how its methods might be appropriated by less benign actors.

I would urge the authors, and their colleagues, to take three steps. First, develop a more explicit ethical framework for the design of choice architectures targeting poor populations. The IRB process, which governs much of this research, is concerned mostly with informed consent during the experimental phase. It says little about the ethics of permanent policy changes that emerge from the research. Second, build in mechanisms of voice. The mothers in Rajasthan should not just be objects of intervention. They should, where possible, be participants in deciding which behaviors are problems and which incentives are acceptable. This is harder than running an RCT, but it is essential to the legitimacy of the project. Third, be more candid about the limits of "welfare" as a justification. When the researcher's theory of welfare diverges from the subject's revealed preferences, that divergence is a moral claim, not just an empirical one, and it deserves defense.

Glennerster and Kremer have produced a body of evidence that genuinely improves our ability to help the global poor. That is a serious achievement. But the very effectiveness of the methods makes it urgent to think carefully about their governance. Small changes produce big results. They also produce big responsibilities. The discipline has not yet caught up with the implications of its own power.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The use of incentives like 'lentil bribes' assumes that non-compliance is a behavioral defect to be corrected rather than a rational choice based on local constraints or distrust.
  2. 02Unlike in developed nations, the poor in developing countries often have no political voice or participation in the design of the choice architectures imposed upon them by external researchers and NGOs.
  3. 03The methodology of behavioral modification is agnostic about goals, meaning the same tools used for health and education could be appropriated by less benign actors to manipulate populations.
  4. 04Development economists must establish an explicit ethical framework and mechanisms for local participation to ensure that behavioral interventions are legitimate and respect the agency of the poor.
  5. 05Justifying interventions based on a researcher's theory of 'welfare' is a moral claim that requires a defense of why it should override the revealed preferences of the subjects.
