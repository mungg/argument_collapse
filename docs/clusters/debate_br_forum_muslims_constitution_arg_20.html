"The responding essay argues that the post-9/11 national security apparatus is a necessary and rational response to empirical threats rather than a product of institutionalized Islamophobia, and dismantling it would leave the United States dangerously vulnerable."

In “Islam on Trial,” Amna A. Akbar and Jeanne Theoharis present a sweeping, passionate indictment of American counterterrorism policy, arguing that the national security apparatus built over the past fifteen years is inherently predicated on anti-Muslim prejudice. In their view, the recent election of Donald Trump and the implementation of his travel ban are not aberrations but rather the logical culmination of a bipartisan consensus that has steadily eroded the rights of Muslim Americans. While their essay rightly points out that the rhetoric of the current administration represents a dangerous escalation, their foundational premise—that the post-9/11 national security state is primarily a vehicle for institutionalized Islamophobia—relies on a fundamental misunderstanding of the nature of the threat the United States faces and the difficult, necessarily imperfect tools required to combat it.

To suggest, as the authors do, that the government’s efforts to distinguish between “acceptable and radical Islam” are part of a nefarious “orientalist script” is to ignore the very real ideological and geopolitical developments of the twenty-first century. Radical Islamic terrorism is not a phantom conjured by a paranoid national security state to justify the surveillance and subjugation of a minority population. It is a highly organized, lethal, and ideologically driven movement that has explicitly declared war on the United States and its allies. From the orchestrated devastation of September 11, 2001, to the horrific attacks in San Bernardino and Orlando, the threat is empirical and undeniable. When the government enacts policies designed to identify and disrupt these networks, it is not engaging in a project of racial or religious subjugation; it is fulfilling its most basic constitutional duty to protect the lives of its citizens.

The authors direct much of their ire at the “material support” statute, which they characterize as a “black box of domestic counterterrorism charges” that criminalizes association and charitable giving. This characterization completely strips the law of its vital context. The material support statute, expanded under the USA PATRIOT Act and upheld by the Supreme Court in *Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project*, is the cornerstone of modern counterterrorism for a very specific reason: modern terrorism relies on vast, sophisticated networks of funding, logistics, and propaganda. By criminalizing the provision of material support—even when that support is ostensibly intended for non-violent activities—the law recognizes the fungible nature of resources. A dollar given to the “charitable” wing of a designated terrorist organization frees up a dollar to be spent on explosives.

If we accept the authors’ premise that prosecuting material support is inherently unjust, we are left with a reactive law enforcement paradigm that can only intervene *after* a plot has been set in motion or, worse, after an attack has occurred. The “preemptive prosecution” paradigm that the authors deride is, in fact, the only rational response to a threat where the cost of a single failure is measured in mass casualties. Are there instances where law enforcement overreaches, or where the use of informants borders on entrapment? Certainly. These cases must be rigorously scrutinized by the courts and the public. But to throw out the entire framework of preventive counterterrorism is to demand that the government fight a twenty-first-century asymmetric war with the tools of a 1990s local police department.

Furthermore, the authors attempt to equate the domestic War on Terror with the War on Drugs and the mass incarceration of African Americans, suggesting that they are all part of an interlocking system of state violence against communities of color. This intersectional framing, while currently fashionable in academic circles, flattens crucial distinctions. The policing of urban neighborhoods for narcotics and the monitoring of international communications for terrorist plots are governed by entirely different legal frameworks, operational directives, and strategic goals. To conflate them is to suggest that federal counterterrorism policy is driven by the same historical and sociological forces that produced structural racism in the American criminal justice system. But the FBI does not monitor radicalization out of an animus toward brown skin; it monitors radicalization because organizations like ISIS have mastered the use of digital propaganda to recruit individuals across the globe to commit acts of horrific violence.

The authors also critique the idea of “Muslim innocence,” arguing that defending only those who are “innocent” leaves in place the idea of the “dangerous” Muslim. This is a perplexing philosophical corner to paint oneself into. The distinction between innocence and guilt, between peaceful citizen and dangerous actor, is the bedrock of any legal system. By attempting to deconstruct the idea that there are, in fact, dangerous individuals who require special law enforcement measures, the authors undermine their own credibility. The public outcry against Trump’s travel ban was powerful precisely because it highlighted the arbitrary and cruel targeting of innocent people—students, doctors, and refugees fleeing the very violence we claim to oppose. If we abandon the concept of innocence in our rush to condemn the security state, we lose the moral high ground that makes such protests effective in the first place.

Moreover, the authors critique the Countering Violent Extremism (CVE) programs as inherently suspicious of Muslims. It is absolutely true that CVE programs must be designed carefully to avoid stigmatizing communities, and early iterations of these programs were often clumsy and alienating. However, the underlying premise of CVE—that communities, mental health professionals, and religious leaders should work together to provide "off-ramps" for individuals showing signs of radicalization before they cross the threshold into criminal activity—is a humane alternative to purely punitive law enforcement. In a world where teenagers can be lured into violent extremism via social media in their own bedrooms, we need robust community intervention strategies. To dismiss all such efforts as state-sponsored Islamophobia is to abandon vulnerable individuals to the predations of sophisticated terrorist recruiters.

The geopolitical realities of the past fifteen years are indeed grim, and the authors are correct that the United States has relied too heavily on military force—from drone strikes to ground invasions—in the Muslim world. The cost in civilian lives has been staggering, and the strategic benefits are often dubious at best. We must hold our government accountable for its foreign policy blunders and the inevitable blowback they produce. But we cannot allow legitimate critiques of American foreign policy to blind us to the necessity of a robust domestic security apparatus.

As we navigate the deeply unsettling waters of the Trump administration, with its penchant for inflammatory rhetoric and crude policy instruments like the travel ban, we must be careful not to conflate the excesses of the current moment with the necessary, carefully constructed security architecture that has largely kept the homeland safe since 2001. Akbar and Theoharis offer a vital warning about the dangers of unchecked executive power, but their prescription—a wholesale dismantling of the legal and investigative tools used to combat terrorism—would leave the United States dangerously exposed. We can, and must, protect the civil rights of Muslim Americans while simultaneously aggressively pursuing those who seek to do us harm. These two imperatives are not mutually exclusive; indeed, the survival of our liberal democracy depends on our ability to do both.
