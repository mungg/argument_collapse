"Societies should reject the fatalistic notion of "living with terrorism" and instead commit to a relentless, multifaceted strategy to reduce and defeat the threat."

The phrase “live with terrorism” is wrong because it lowers the ambitions of the state at precisely the moment citizens need seriousness. Of course no government can guarantee perfect safety. But the first duty of government is to protect the public, and leaders should not sound as if massacres in Paris, Brussels, Orlando and Nice are the new weather.

France is not facing random misfortune. It is facing an organized ideological movement, inspired and sometimes directed by the Islamic State, that has repeatedly found ways to kill civilians. The fact that the weapon in Nice appears to have been a truck does not make the threat less political. It shows adaptability. A society that shrugs and says “we must live with this” risks training itself to accept an unacceptable level of violence.

The alternative is not hysteria or a police state. But it does require more than candlelight vigils and speeches about resilience. France and its allies need to destroy the Islamic State’s territorial base in Syria, Iraq and Libya, because territory gives jihadists prestige, training space, money and propaganda. European governments need far better intelligence sharing. Known radicals should not be able to move easily between jurisdictions while bureaucracies fail to connect files. Prisons, which have become incubators for jihadist recruitment in France, require urgent reform. Online recruitment networks should be disrupted aggressively, with judicial oversight but without naïveté about their consequences.

Immigration and integration must also be discussed honestly. This does not mean blaming Muslims collectively; that would be unjust and strategically foolish. Most victims of jihadist violence worldwide are Muslims, and Muslim citizens are essential partners against extremism. But it is not bigotry to ask why some French-born citizens or residents come to hate France so intensely that they embrace mass murder. Segregated suburbs, unemployment, criminal networks and extremist preaching all deserve attention. So does the failure of the French state to make some of its citizens feel fully included.

Civil liberties matter, but liberty itself depends on order. A public square where families fear fireworks displays is not a free public square. Emergency powers should not be permanent, but neither should constitutional scruples become excuses for paralysis. Democracies have fought fascism, communism and insurgency without abandoning themselves. They can fight jihadism too.

We should refuse the fatalism embedded in “living with terrorism.” Citizens can live through terrorism; they should not be asked to live with it as a settled condition. The proper goal is not perfect safety, but relentless reduction of the threat.

  3. 03European governments must improve intelligence sharing and reform prisons to prevent radicalization and the movement of known extremists.
  4. 04Addressing the root causes of domestic extremism requires honest discussions about immigration, integration, and the socio-economic failures of the state.
  5. 05Protecting civil liberties depends on maintaining public order, meaning democracies must use their power to fight jihadism without succumbing to paralysis.
Diversified DeepSeek

"Society must reject the fatalistic notion of "living with terrorism" and instead commit to a robust, proactive defense that preserves both security and fundamental freedoms."

Prime Minister Manuel Valls was wrong to suggest that France must “live with terrorism.” This phrase is a retreat dressed up as pragmatism, and it signals to our enemies that their campaign of violence is working. The public backlash against his comment was not hysteria; it was the natural reaction of a people who refuse to accept slaughter in the streets as a permanent feature of daily life. We can, and must, continue to fight terrorism without dismantling the open society we cherish.

Critics of a robust response often claim that any security measure automatically erodes liberty. History doesn’t bear this out. The United Kingdom endured decades of IRA bombings without suspending habeas corpus nationwide or becoming a police state. They combined targeted intelligence, community policing, and selective legislation while preserving the fundamental freedoms of the majority. France’s own state of emergency, declared after the November 2015 Paris attacks and extended after Nice, has allowed house raids and administrative controls — but it hasn’t stamped out café culture or free expression. Such temporary measures, subject to judicial oversight, strike a defensible balance. We’re not talking about gulags; we’re talking about giving security services the tools to stop a truck before it plows through a crowd.

The alternative Valls hints at — a permanent low-grade acceptance of mass casualty attacks — is far more corrosive to freedom. When citizens stop going to concerts, markets, or fireworks displays out of fear, the terrorists have already won. That is a more profound loss of liberty than a few extra CCTV cameras or the monitoring of extremist networks. The goal is not to live with terrorism but to live without it, and that demands a steady, unflinching response. Europe’s intelligence failures before the Nice and Brussels attacks reveal not a need to dial back ambition, but to improve cross-border cooperation, invest in human intelligence, and confront the ideological incubators that radicalize young men.

Accepting terrorism as a background noise of modern life is also politically dangerous. It breeds a fatalism that fuels support for far-right movements promising brute force solutions — the very thing civil libertarians fear. The French government should be saying clearly: we will defend our people, not just manage their anxiety. That isn’t warmongering; it’s the baseline duty of a state. Valls should retract his statement and instead lay out a credible path to defeating ISIS and dismantling the domestic networks that enable such attacks. Living *in defiance* of terrorism — with intensified security, not a shrug of resignation — is the only way to ensure that our freedoms survive the age of the suicide bomber.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02History demonstrates that states can implement targeted security measures and intelligence operations to combat terrorism without dismantling the open society or becoming a police state.
  3. 03The fear and self-censorship caused by a resignation to frequent attacks represent a more profound loss of liberty than the implementation of enhanced security protocols.
  4. 04Improving cross-border intelligence cooperation and addressing radicalization are more effective strategies than simply managing public anxiety.
  5. 05A government's failure to aggressively defend its citizens breeds political fatalism and fuels the rise of extremist movements that offer authoritarian solutions.

"Societies must reject the notion of "living with terrorism" and instead demand that governments fulfill their fundamental duty to protect citizens by aggressively eradicating the threat."

Manuel Valls’ assertion that France must "live with terrorism" is a staggering abdication of a government's most fundamental duty. The primary purpose of the state is the protection of its citizens. When a Prime Minister tells his people they must simply accept the occasional mass slaughter of families celebrating a national holiday, he is implicitly admitting that his government has failed, and worse, that it has no intention of doing what is necessary to succeed.

We cannot and must not "live with" terrorism. Accepting the murder of 84 innocent people as a new, unavoidable feature of modern life is a moral surrender to a barbarous death cult. Normalizing this violence creates a baseline where citizens look at horrific carnage and simply shrug it off as the cost of doing business in a democracy.

The idea that securing our nations will inevitably turn them into police states is a false dichotomy peddled by defeatists. The problem is not that total security is impossible; the problem is that Western leaders have been utterly unwilling to take off the gloves to fight the Islamic State and its sympathizers. We are fighting an asymmetric war, yet we are still treating it like a specialized law enforcement issue.

Not "living with terrorism" means making hard choices. It means dramatically tightening border security across the Schengen Zone, which currently allows returning jihadists to move freely across Europe. It means aggressively deporting foreign nationals suspected of radical ties, without getting bogged down in years of human rights litigation. It means monitoring radical mosques and shutting them down, rather than tip-toeing around the sensibilities of community leaders who look the other way while their youth are radicalized. And abroad, it means committing the necessary military force to completely obliterate the territorial caliphate of ISIS in Iraq and Syria, which serves as the inspirational beacon for these lone-wolf attacks.

A civilized society does not learn to live with a cancer; it aggressively roots it out. To accept the slaughter in Nice, Paris, Brussels, and Orlando as our new normal is to accept the eventual collapse of Western civilization. We must demand leaders who will fight to end terrorism, not managers who ask us to peacefully coexist with it.

Diversified GPT

"Democracies must reject the fatalistic notion of "living with terrorism" and instead pursue a forceful, lawful counterterrorism strategy to protect the fundamental freedoms that security enables."

“Live with terrorism” is a dangerous phrase because it can make a failure of government sound like wisdom. Citizens do not create states merely to issue statements of resilience after massacres. They create states to secure borders, enforce laws, protect public spaces and defeat enemies who deliberately murder civilians. A democracy must not become a police state, but neither should it console itself with the idea that terrorism is just another unfortunate feature of modern life.

The freedom at stake is not only the freedom from surveillance or intrusive policing. It is also the freedom to take one’s children to fireworks, to sit in a café, to travel on a train, to attend a concert, to worship in peace. When those ordinary acts become recurring targets, liberty has already been diminished. Security is not the opposite of freedom; it is one of freedom’s preconditions.

That means France and its allies need a more forceful and coherent counterterrorism strategy. The Islamic State’s territory in Syria and Iraq matters, because territory gives jihadists money, training grounds, prestige and propaganda. Its online networks matter, because they inspire or direct people who may need little more than a vehicle or a knife. Prisons matter, because they have too often served as incubators of radicalization. Borders and intelligence sharing matter, because European governments have repeatedly discovered after attacks that known extremists moved too easily or were lost in bureaucratic gaps.

None of this requires abandoning the rule of law. Surveillance should be supervised by courts. Emergency powers should be reviewed by legislatures. Police should be punished when they abuse authority. But democracies can be lawful and tough at the same time. Closing institutions that openly preach violence, deporting noncitizens who support terrorist organizations, monitoring returned fighters, expanding human intelligence and using military force against organized terrorist sanctuaries are not betrayals of liberal society. They are ways of defending it.

There is a moral problem, too, with telling people simply to adjust. The burden of “living with terrorism” falls unevenly. It is easy for officials behind security details to praise stoicism. It is harder for families who have buried children, for Jewish communities guarding schools, for Muslims facing backlash, and for police officers asked to stand between crowds and killers.

The goal should not be to eradicate every risk; that is impossible. But the goal should be to make terrorist activity rarer, harder, more costly and less glamorous. Resilience is necessary after an attack. Before the next one, complacency is not resilience. A free society should refuse both panic and fatalism.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The primary purpose of the state is to provide security and protect citizens from deliberate violence, not merely to offer statements of resilience.
  2. 02Security is a necessary precondition for freedom, as the constant threat of violence in public spaces diminishes the liberty of ordinary life.
  3. 03Defeating terrorism requires a coherent strategy that targets extremist territory, online networks, radicalization in prisons, and intelligence gaps.
  4. 04Tough counterterrorism measures, such as deporting supporters of violence and using military force, are compatible with the rule of law and serve to defend liberal society.
  5. 05The burden of terrorism falls unevenly on vulnerable communities and victims, making it morally problematic for officials to demand stoicism from the public.
  6. 06While total eradication of risk is impossible, the goal of a free society should be to actively minimize the frequency and impact of terrorist activity rather than accepting it as inevitable.
