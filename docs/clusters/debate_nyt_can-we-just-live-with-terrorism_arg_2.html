"Societies should reject the fatalistic notion of "living with terrorism" and instead commit to a relentless, multifaceted strategy to reduce and defeat the threat."

The phrase “live with terrorism” is wrong because it lowers the ambitions of the state at precisely the moment citizens need seriousness. Of course no government can guarantee perfect safety. But the first duty of government is to protect the public, and leaders should not sound as if massacres in Paris, Brussels, Orlando and Nice are the new weather.

France is not facing random misfortune. It is facing an organized ideological movement, inspired and sometimes directed by the Islamic State, that has repeatedly found ways to kill civilians. The fact that the weapon in Nice appears to have been a truck does not make the threat less political. It shows adaptability. A society that shrugs and says “we must live with this” risks training itself to accept an unacceptable level of violence.

The alternative is not hysteria or a police state. But it does require more than candlelight vigils and speeches about resilience. France and its allies need to destroy the Islamic State’s territorial base in Syria, Iraq and Libya, because territory gives jihadists prestige, training space, money and propaganda. European governments need far better intelligence sharing. Known radicals should not be able to move easily between jurisdictions while bureaucracies fail to connect files. Prisons, which have become incubators for jihadist recruitment in France, require urgent reform. Online recruitment networks should be disrupted aggressively, with judicial oversight but without naïveté about their consequences.

Immigration and integration must also be discussed honestly. This does not mean blaming Muslims collectively; that would be unjust and strategically foolish. Most victims of jihadist violence worldwide are Muslims, and Muslim citizens are essential partners against extremism. But it is not bigotry to ask why some French-born citizens or residents come to hate France so intensely that they embrace mass murder. Segregated suburbs, unemployment, criminal networks and extremist preaching all deserve attention. So does the failure of the French state to make some of its citizens feel fully included.

Civil liberties matter, but liberty itself depends on order. A public square where families fear fireworks displays is not a free public square. Emergency powers should not be permanent, but neither should constitutional scruples become excuses for paralysis. Democracies have fought fascism, communism and insurgency without abandoning themselves. They can fight jihadism too.

We should refuse the fatalism embedded in “living with terrorism.” Citizens can live through terrorism; they should not be asked to live with it as a settled condition. The proper goal is not perfect safety, but relentless reduction of the threat.

Moral clarity about the crime is essential. But policy clarity requires a wider lens. If “living with terrorism” means accepting occasional massacres at home while continuing policies that generate chaos abroad and alienation at home, then it is not realism. It is avoidance.

Western airstrikes may be necessary against its military capacity, but bombs cannot by themselves produce legitimate politics in Mosul, Raqqa or Sirte. Nor can European security services solve the problem if prisons and impoverished suburbs continue to serve as recruiting grounds for men who feel no stake in the country around them. France’s republican model promises equal citizenship, but too many French citizens of North African or Muslim background encounter discrimination in employment, policing and housing. Extremists exploit this gap between promise and experience.

If we merely harden borders, expand surveillance and bomb more targets, we may reduce some plots while replenishing the anger on which jihadist movements feed. The goal should be not to “live with” terrorism, but to stop helping produce the conditions in which it thrives.

"Societies should reject the rhetoric of "living with terrorism" because it represents a preemptive resignation that absolves the political class of its responsibility to address fixable policy failures."

The phrase "live with terrorism" should make us deeply uncomfortable, and the discomfort is appropriate. Manuel Valls deserved the criticism he received, not because he was wrong about the difficulty of preventing every attack, but because the language of accommodation is itself a form of surrender.

Words matter in politics. When a head of government tells citizens they must "live with" something, he sets the terms of public expectation. He defines the horizon of the possible. Telling the French people, three days after 84 of their compatriots were mowed down at a national celebration, that this is the new normal — that is not stoicism, it is preemptive resignation. It tells the families of the dead that their losses are part of the price of doing business as a modern European nation.

There is nothing inevitable about the current wave of jihadist violence in Europe. It has identifiable causes: the collapse of Syria and Iraq, the rise of ISIS as a state-sponsor of attacks abroad, the radicalization of marginalized young men in the banlieues, porous external borders, intelligence services that share too little, prisons that function as jihadist seminaries, and an integration model that has failed in measurable ways. Each of these is a policy failure, and each could in principle be addressed. To shrug and say "we must live with it" is to absolve the political class of responsibility for fixing things that are fixable.

Compare the response to other catastrophic risks. We do not tell parents that they must "live with" school shootings, or air travelers that they must "live with" plane crashes. We pour resources into prevention, we investigate causes, we change procedures, and we expect improvement. Terrorism deserves the same seriousness — not as an excuse for emergency powers without end, but as a problem to be solved through patient, intelligent statecraft.

Yes, freedoms must be protected. The choice is not between liberty and security in some simple zero-sum way. But the answer to a security failure is to fix the failure, not to redefine it as a permanent condition of life. Valls essentially announced that the French state has given up trying to keep its citizens safe at public gatherings. That is an extraordinary admission, and the public was right to recoil from it.

"Living with terrorism" sounds like resignation. It sounds like a prime minister who has run out of ideas and is asking a grieving nation to lower its expectations. In the immediate aftermath of Nice—84 dead, including children, lying on the Promenade des Anglais—that is simply not good enough.

The problem with accepting "living with terrorism" as policy is that it mistakes a description of reality for a prescription for action. These questions point toward actionable remedies: better coordination between French domestic intelligence and their European partners, more robust border controls within the Schengen zone, greater attention to the ideological ecosystems that produce lone-wolf attackers, and sustained investment in the kind of community policing that can identify threats before they materialize.

To "live with" terrorism as a governing philosophy means accepting that our security apparatus is essentially reactive—playing defense after the attack has already occurred. That is not a strategy; it is an abdication of responsibility. The families of Nice deserve a government that promises more than endurance.

This does not require abandoning our values or descending into authoritarianism. But it does require refusing to normalize the abnormal, demanding better from our institutions, and maintaining the political will to confront an enemy that operates across borders and ideologies.

Prime Minister Manuel Valls was wrong to suggest that France must “live with terrorism.” This phrase is a retreat dressed up as pragmatism, and it signals to our enemies that their campaign of violence is working. They combined targeted intelligence, community policing, and selective legislation while preserving the fundamental freedoms of the majority. France’s own state of emergency, declared after the November 2015 Paris attacks and extended after Nice, has allowed house raids and administrative controls — but it hasn’t stamped out café culture or free expression. Such temporary measures, subject to judicial oversight, strike a defensible balance. We’re not talking about gulags; we’re talking about giving security services the tools to stop a truck before it plows through a crowd.

The alternative Valls hints at — a permanent low-grade acceptance of mass casualty attacks — is far more corrosive to freedom. When citizens stop going to concerts, markets, or fireworks displays out of fear, the terrorists have already won. That is a more profound loss of liberty than a few extra CCTV cameras or the monitoring of extremist networks. The goal is not to live with terrorism but to live without it, and that demands a steady, unflinching response. Europe’s intelligence failures before the Nice and Brussels attacks reveal not a need to dial back ambition, but to improve cross-border cooperation, invest in human intelligence, and confront the ideological incubators that radicalize young men.

Accepting terrorism as a background noise of modern life is also politically dangerous. It breeds a fatalism that fuels support for far-right movements promising brute force solutions — the very thing civil libertarians fear. The French government should be saying clearly: we will defend our people, not just manage their anxiety. That isn’t warmongering; it’s the baseline duty of a state. Valls should retract his statement and instead lay out a credible path to defeating ISIS and dismantling the domestic networks that enable such attacks. Living *in defiance* of terrorism — with intensified security, not a shrug of resignation — is the only way to ensure that our freedoms survive the age of the suicide bomber.

The idea that we can calmly “live with” terrorism while preserving our freedoms is a dangerous delusion. The end result will not be a stoic, liberty-loving society; it will be the steady construction of a surveillance state, built on fear, justified by the very attacks we were told to accept.

Police raids without warrants, house arrests without trial, expanded digital surveillance — these are becoming routine. If we follow Valls’s logic and normalize terrorism, we normalize its antidotes as well. The security regime will grow layer by layer, crisis by crisis, until we wake up in a country where the liberty to protest, to keep a secret, or to move freely is a memory.

The only way to defend freedom, paradoxically, is to refuse to accept terrorism as a permanent condition. We must treat it as an enemy to be defeated, not a chronic pain to be managed. That means a far more aggressive military and intelligence campaign against ISIS strongholds in Syria and Libya. It means dismantling the funding networks, the safe houses, the online echo chambers. It means, when necessary, deploying troops to protect critical infrastructure so citizens can gather without feeling like targets. These are serious measures, but they can be temporary and targeted. They do not require indefinite suspension of civil law or the mass internment of communities.

A clear-eyed look at recent history shows that half-measures and talk of “living with it” only prolong the agony. After Madrid, London, Paris, Brussels, and now Nice, we have neither crushed the jihadist networks nor maintained full civil liberties. We are stuck in a gray zone of fear, where each new attack is met with a fresh round of security theater and political recriminations. This is the worst of both worlds. The humane answer is not to learn to live with terrorism, but to wage a decisive, finite campaign to render it incapable of mass operations, and then — and only then — to reclaim the full spectrum of our freedoms. Accepting a body count as the price of an open society is not wisdom; it is a political failure that will eventually cost us the very openness we cherish.

“Live with terrorism” is a dangerous phrase because it can make a failure of government sound like wisdom. Citizens do not create states merely to issue statements of resilience after massacres. They create states to secure borders, enforce laws, protect public spaces and defeat enemies who deliberately murder civilians. A democracy must not become a police state, but neither should it console itself with the idea that terrorism is just another unfortunate feature of modern life.

The freedom at stake is not only the freedom from surveillance or intrusive policing. It is also the freedom to take one’s children to fireworks, to sit in a café, to travel on a train, to attend a concert, to worship in peace. When those ordinary acts become recurring targets, liberty has already been diminished. Security is not the opposite of freedom; it is one of freedom’s preconditions.

That means France and its allies need a more forceful and coherent counterterrorism strategy. The Islamic State’s territory in Syria and Iraq matters, because territory gives jihadists money, training grounds, prestige and propaganda. Its online networks matter, because they inspire or direct people who may need little more than a vehicle or a knife. Prisons matter, because they have too often served as incubators of radicalization. Borders and intelligence sharing matter, because European governments have repeatedly discovered after attacks that known extremists moved too easily or were lost in bureaucratic gaps.

None of this requires abandoning the rule of law. Surveillance should be supervised by courts. Emergency powers should be reviewed by legislatures. Police should be punished when they abuse authority. But democracies can be lawful and tough at the same time. Closing institutions that openly preach violence, deporting noncitizens who support terrorist organizations, monitoring returned fighters, expanding human intelligence and using military force against organized terrorist sanctuaries are not betrayals of liberal society. They are ways of defending it.

There is a moral problem, too, with telling people simply to adjust. The burden of “living with terrorism” falls unevenly. It is easy for officials behind security details to praise stoicism. It is harder for families who have buried children, for Jewish communities guarding schools, for Muslims facing backlash, and for police officers asked to stand between crowds and killers.

The goal should not be to eradicate every risk; that is impossible. But the goal should be to make terrorist activity rarer, harder, more costly and less glamorous. Resilience is necessary after an attack. Before the next one, complacency is not resilience. A free society should refuse both panic and fatalism.
