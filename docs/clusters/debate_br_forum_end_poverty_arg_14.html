"While technology's impact is contingent on human and institutional capacity, development policy should focus on identifying specific contexts where the marginal return of information technology exceeds other investments rather than dismissing it based on average failures."

As an economist, I read Kentaro Toyama’s essay as a useful attack on average thinking. The average telecenter may fail. The average laptop program may disappoint. The average poor household may use a phone for social calls rather than productivity. But development policy should not ask whether “technology” works on average. It should ask where the marginal return to a particular information technology exceeds the marginal return to the next best use of funds.

Toyama’s magnifier thesis can be translated into economic language: information technology is complementary to human capital, organizational capital, and institutional quality. Where complements are missing, returns are low. Where complements are present, returns may be high. This is not unique to ICT. Fertilizer has low returns without water, roads, credit, and agronomic knowledge. Schools have low returns without teachers and labor-market opportunities. Roads have lower returns if markets are monopolized or insecurity prevents travel. The existence of complementarities is not a reason to avoid investment; it is a reason to sequence and bundle it intelligently.

The telecenter boom violated this principle. Many projects confused a fixed asset with a service. A rural kiosk with a computer was expected to generate demand, provide public services, educate children, improve agriculture, and become financially self-sustaining. That is a heroic burden for a small business in a poor village. The result was predictable: insufficient revenue, elite capture, entertainment use, maintenance failures, and donor fatigue. Toyama’s field observations are consistent with what an economist would expect from a poorly specified business model serving customers with limited purchasing power.

Mobile phones are different. They have spread because they solve a problem people already recognize: communication over distance. The welfare gains may be dispersed and difficult to measure, but they are real. Aker’s work on grain markets in Niger and Jensen’s work on fisheries in Kerala suggest that when price dispersion reflects information frictions, phones can improve market efficiency. These are not miracles; they are reductions in search costs. But reductions in search costs are central to economic life. If a farmer avoids one wasted trip, a trader locates a buyer, or a worker hears of a job, the gain may be modest individually but large in aggregate.

Toyama is right, however, that private willingness to pay is not identical to social value. Poor households may spend scarce income on ringtones, gossip, or status goods. Firms will sell what is profitable, not what is developmentally optimal. But this is a general feature of markets, not a special defect of ICT. People also spend money on festivals, alcohol, cosmetics, and television. Development policy cannot be built on the premise that the poor should consume only what outsiders deem productive. It can, however, subsidize services with positive externalities—health information, market information, education, emergency communication—while taxing or regulating abuses where appropriate.

The comparison with deworming is important but incomplete. Deworming is extraordinarily cost-effective for a specific health and schooling problem. It should be funded. But the existence of one high-return intervention does not imply that all other interventions are wasteful. Governments must invest in public goods that support broad economic transformation: roads, electricity, ports, legal systems, schools, sanitation, and increasingly telecommunications. The question is not whether Internet access is as basic as water in a biological sense. It is whether communications infrastructure has public-good characteristics and productivity spillovers that justify public support. In many cases it does.

The danger lies in using public money to subsidize private gadgets without addressing market failures. A universal laptop program is hard to justify if teachers are absent and children lack textbooks. A rural broadband backbone may be easier to justify if it lowers costs for schools, clinics, businesses, and government offices. A mobile payment system may be valuable if it reduces transaction costs and leakage in welfare payments. A database may improve microfinance operations if the institution is already competent. Technology investments should be evaluated like other investments: expected benefits, distributional effects, complementarities, fiscal sustainability, and opportunity cost.

Distribution matters especially. Toyama argues that technology can widen inequality because the better educated and better connected extract more value from it. This is plausible and often observed. But inequality effects are not predetermined. If mobile coverage reaches traders before farmers, traders may gain. If farmers receive reliable price information and can coordinate transport, they may gain bargaining power. If digital government services require English and bribes to intermediaries, elites gain. If they are delivered through accountable local institutions in local languages, poorer citizens may gain. The same technology can shift surplus in different directions depending on rules and organization.

A sensible ICT4D agenda in 2010 would be neither boosterish nor dismissive. First, stop funding stand-alone hardware deployments whose theory of change is “access produces development.” Second, invest in rigorous evaluation, not just anecdotes about saved okra crops. Third, focus on high-frequency, high-value information problems: prices, weather, remittances, health follow-up, job matching, logistics, and public payments. Fourth, build on institutions that have incentives to use the technology well. Fifth, treat maintenance and training as recurring costs, not implementation details.

Toyama’s essay performs a valuable service by exposing the opportunity costs of technological fashion. But his magnifier metaphor should not become a veto. Complements can be built. Information frictions can be large. Small efficiency gains can matter to poor households. The correct conclusion is not “technology cannot end poverty.” It is: technology is an input, and like all inputs, its productivity depends on where and how it is used.

  2. 02The failure of telecenters was a result of poorly specified business models, whereas mobile phones have succeeded by addressing the pre-existing, universal need for distance communication.
  3. 03While private spending on technology may be 'frivolous,' governments should still subsidize ICT services that provide positive social externalities like health and market information.
  4. 04The potential for technology to widen inequality is not an inherent trait of the tools themselves but depends on the rules, languages, and institutional accountability governing their deployment.
  5. 05Technology should be evaluated as a standard economic input, focusing on high-value information problems and building upon institutions that already have the incentive to use tools effectively.
