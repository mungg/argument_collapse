"While the Democratic Party has successfully shifted toward an ambitious economic agenda, its electoral success is undermined by a cultural brand and institutional style that alienate non-college-educated voters."

Hacker and Pierson are right to push back against the claim that the Democratic Party has abandoned economics. The Biden administration’s agenda was plainly more ambitious on spending, industrial policy, labor standards, antitrust, and climate investment than anything Democrats had offered in decades. Anyone who says the party has talked only about pronouns and diversity trainings has not looked seriously at what Democrats tried to pass in 2021.

But the authors go too far in the other direction. They treat “wokeness” largely as a caricature broadcast by Republicans and conservative media. That misses why the charge has had political force. Cultural politics is not merely an invention of the right; it is a terrain on which many Americans encounter institutions governed by Democrats or by Democratic-aligned professionals. Schools, universities, nonprofits, HR departments, media organizations, foundations, public health agencies, and city governments all became sites of intense conflict over race, gender, policing, speech, immigration, and authority. The national Democratic Party may have foregrounded economics, but many voters experience “the Democrats” through these institutions.

This distinction matters. Parties do not control all the symbols associated with them. Republican politicians have often benefited from conservative churches, gun groups, talk radio, and local sheriffs even when they do not formally speak for the party. Democrats likewise inherit the cultural signals emitted by universities, advocacy organizations, progressive city councils, teachers’ unions, philanthropic networks, and highly educated activists. If those institutions adopt language and norms that many non-college voters experience as alienating or punitive, Democratic candidates cannot simply say, “Look at our infrastructure bill.”

The school controversies after 2020 are a case in point. Debates over pandemic closures, masking, curriculum, gifted programs, admissions, gender identity, and parental authority did not come from nowhere. Some were cynically amplified by conservative operatives, but many reflected genuine frustration. Parents saw children fall behind during prolonged school closures. Others worried that elite educational debates had become contemptuous of merit, discipline, or ordinary patriotism. Still others objected to bureaucratic language about race and identity that seemed to sort children into moral categories. Democrats can argue that Republicans distorted these issues. Often they did. But distortion works best when it has raw material.

Crime followed a similar pattern. Trump’s 2020 warnings about “crime and chaos” were crude and racially loaded, as Hacker and Pierson note. Yet rising homicide rates in many cities during the pandemic period were real. Disorder in some urban neighborhoods was real. Concerns about transit safety, retail theft, open-air drug use, and homelessness were real, even when exaggerated by right-wing media. The slogan “defund the police” was never the Biden agenda, and many Democratic officials rejected it. But the slogan became nationally associated with the party because prominent activists and some local officials embraced it at a moment when many voters wanted both police accountability and public safety.

The authors’ own evidence that Democratic elites put immigration and criminal justice reform on the backburner suggests they know these issues are dangerous. But avoiding them legislatively does not make them disappear politically. Republicans are eager to define them, and progressive activists are not going to stop talking about them. In a polarized media environment, the national party’s agenda is only one input into the party’s brand.

This is especially important for the white working-class voters Democrats hope to regain, but it is not only about white voters. Recent elections have shown signs of Republican improvement among some Latino voters, especially men and voters without college degrees, and among some Asian American communities concerned about education, public safety, or small business regulation. Black voters remain overwhelmingly Democratic, but even there Democrats cannot assume unlimited loyalty, particularly among younger men. A party that interprets all cultural backlash as white reaction will misunderstand the heterogeneity of its own multiracial working-class base.

Many working-class voters are economically moderate-to-left and culturally moderate-to-conservative. They may support higher minimum wages, infrastructure spending, health care subsidies, and taxing the rich while also disliking elite progressive discourse on immigration, policing, gender, or race. That combination is not irrational. It is common. The challenge for Democrats is not simply to emphasize economics more, but to develop a cultural style that does not constantly signal upper-middle-class educational status.

Hacker and Pierson’s “Brahmin left” discussion focuses on policy preferences, but the Brahmin problem is also about manners, language, and authority. College-educated progressives often speak in a moralized institutional dialect: “center marginalized voices,” “do the work,” “structural harm,” “equity lens,” “white privilege,” “Latinx,” “birthing people,” “intersectionality.” Some of these terms have analytic value in specific contexts. As mass politics, they often fail. Worse, they can communicate that politics is a seminar in which ordinary people are always one sentence away from being corrected.

Economic populism can coexist with cultural condescension, and when it does, the condescension often wins. A voter who thinks Democrats look down on him may not be persuaded by a tax credit. Respect is also material, or at least it shapes whether material appeals are trusted. This is one reason Biden personally performed better among some culturally moderate voters than many other Democrats might have. His political persona is old-fashioned, Catholic, union-friendly, patriotic, and less fluent in activist jargon. He could advance a more progressive economic agenda partly because he did not sound like the faculty lounge stereotype Republicans were selling.

That lesson should not be mistaken for a call to abandon civil rights, abortion rights, LGBT rights, or racial equality. The Democratic Party should defend pluralism and equal citizenship. It should not chase voters by validating bigotry. But there is a difference between defending rights and adopting every rhetorical and institutional fashion of the progressive professional class. There is a difference between saying police should be accountable and suggesting policing itself is illegitimate. There is a difference between teaching the full history of slavery and segregation and turning classrooms into ideological battlegrounds. There is a difference between welcoming immigrants and appearing indifferent to border management. There is a difference between respecting transgender Americans and refusing to acknowledge difficult tradeoffs in sports, prisons, or youth medicine.

On these issues, voters often detect evasiveness. They hear Democrats say Republicans are manufacturing a culture war. Sometimes that is true. But they also see Democrats reluctant to state clear limits. That reluctance allows the right to portray the party by its most unpopular activist edge.

The authors are persuasive that Democrats have used broad economic policy to avoid activating internal divisions over race and class. But avoidance is not a long-term strategy. The coalition they describe contains affluent social liberals, working-class voters of color, young progressives, moderate suburban parents, union households, immigrants, secular professionals, and religious minorities. These groups do not share a single cultural worldview. A party that lets its cultural image be shaped by its most educated and activist members will eventually strain that coalition.

The same is true locally. Hacker and Pierson mention opportunity hoarding, but the cultural dimension is equally important. Affluent blue suburbs often combine symbolic progressivism with exclusionary material politics. They may put up signs celebrating diversity while blocking housing, defending school boundaries, and using procedural liberalism to preserve privilege. Working-class voters notice this hypocrisy. So do voters of color priced out of high-opportunity neighborhoods. A politics that is radical in vocabulary and conservative in local practice is easy to ridicule because it deserves ridicule.

What should Democrats do? First, they should continue the economic turn. It is substantively right and politically necessary. A party that cannot improve living standards will not win durable trust. Second, they should build a sharper distinction between liberal egalitarian principles and elite progressive subculture. Defend rights; drop the jargon. Fight discrimination; avoid moral exhibitionism. Support public safety and police reform together. Teach history honestly without treating patriotism as embarrassing. Manage immigration humanely but visibly. Respect parents without surrendering schools to censorship campaigns. Make room for religious and culturally moderate Democrats.

Third, they should recognize that class is cultural. Rebuilding working-class support is not only about manufacturing jobs or wage subsidies. It is about whether people feel the party understands their lives, honors their work, shares their attachments, and speaks plainly. Franklin Roosevelt was an aristocrat, but he did not sound like he was asking voters to pass a graduate seminar. Democrats today too often do.

Hacker and Pierson have corrected an important misconception: the Democratic Party’s governing agenda has been much more economically ambitious than its critics admit. But politics is not only governing agenda. It is also identity, association, trust, and style. The new blue divide is not merely between affluent and struggling voters. It is between an economic program that could appeal broadly and a cultural brand that often narrows its reach. Democrats will not solve that problem by denying it exists.
