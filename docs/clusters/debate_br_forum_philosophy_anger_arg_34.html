"The philosophical debate over anger should move beyond the dichotomy of suppression versus endorsement by adopting a Buddhist-inspired model of 're-perception' that transforms anger into grief."

Callard's essay lands in the middle of a global crisis, and I cannot read it without feeling the weight of that context. In April 2020, millions of people are trapped at home, many of them in unsafe homes, facing economic devastation, illness, and death. The conditions for anger are abundant. The question of how to relate to that anger — whether to suppress it, express it, or transmute it — is not abstract.

I want to offer a practical intervention into Callard's framework. She is primarily a philosopher, and she thinks like one: her method is to identify hidden assumptions, trace their logical consequences, and reveal the crisis that follows when those consequences are confronted honestly. This is admirable work. But it produces a picture of anger that is static — anger as a condition you are in, rather than a process you are engaged in. And I think this static picture misses something important about how anger actually functions in human lives.

The Buddhist tradition — which Callard discusses only through the lens of Flanagan's appropriation of it — offers a different model. Śāntideva, whom she mentions, does indeed counsel the elimination of anger's seeds. But the Buddhist analysis of anger is not simply Stoic. It does not say that anger is irrational and should be replaced by reason. It says something more subtle: that anger arises in response to a misperception of causality. You are angry because you believe that a person or event is the sole and sufficient cause of your suffering, and that this cause deserves to be punished. The Buddhist correction is not Stoic detachment; it is a re-perception of conditions. When you see the chains of causation that produced the harmful act — the trauma, the ignorance, the structural forces — your anger does not dissolve into acceptance. It dissolves into something more like grief, which is also a moral response but one that does not require an enemy.

This is not the same as the Stoic move Callard critiques. The Stoic says: This event does not concern your inner self, so you should not be angry. The Buddhist says: This event concerns many selves, none of which is the simple author of the harm, so your anger is pointed at the wrong target. The first is a retreat from engagement; the second is a reorientation of it.

I find this reorientation practically useful in a way that Callard's stark dichotomy — between corruption through anger and corruption through acquiescence — is not. The pandemic context makes this concrete. I am angry about the failures of government response, the disparities in who bears the burden of illness and economic loss, the failures of public health infrastructure that were entirely foreseeable. Callard would say my anger is rational, that it is tied to grudges and vengeance, and that it constitutes a form of moral corruption. This may all be true. But the Buddhist framing gives me a way to hold the anger without being consumed by it — to recognize the structural conditions that produce injustice without requiring a villain, and to act from a sense of moral urgency that does not depend on the kind of adversarial intimacy Callard describes.

The grief model is not acquiescence. It is not the false serenity of someone who has decided not to be angry. It is a transformation of the emotional object of moral engagement — from the person who wronged you to the conditions that produced the wrong. This is a genuinely different moral phenomenology, and it is one that the philosophical debate Callard describes has not adequately considered. The Stoics want to eliminate anger; the sentimentalists want to endorse it; the Buddhists want to re-perceive it. That third option is closer to what most of us actually need.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Callard’s analysis treats anger as a static condition rather than a dynamic process, missing how the emotion actually functions in human lives.
  2. 02Anger arises from a misperception of causality that falsely identifies a single person or event as the sole author of suffering.
  3. 03By recognizing the complex chains of trauma and structural forces behind a harm, one can maintain moral urgency without the moral corruption of vengeful 'adversarial intimacy.'
  4. 04Transforming anger into grief allows for a moral response that focuses on changing the conditions that produced a wrong rather than simply punishing a villain.
  5. 05The 're-perception' model offers a practical middle path that avoids both the retreat of Stoic detachment and the moral damage of Callard’s 'corruption through anger' framework.
