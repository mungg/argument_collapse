"While a comprehensive postmortem of the COVID-19 response is necessary to address institutional failures like prolonged school closures and the suppression of dissent, the critique must avoid overcorrecting into a simplistic narrative that dismisses all non-pharmaceutical interventions as ineffective or purely products of elite panic."

Stephen Macedo and Frances Lee have written the kind of essay that liberal democracies need after a crisis: serious, uncomfortable, and unwilling to accept institutional self-protection as a substitute for accountability. They are right that the United States has not conducted a sufficiently searching COVID-19 postmortem. They are right that school closures lasted too long in many places and imposed unequal harms. They are right that public officials too often blurred the difference between scientific evidence and political judgment. And they are right that stigmatizing dissent, especially from credentialed experts, corroded trust.

Yet their essay overcorrects. In trying to rescue suppressed questions, it risks substituting one retrospective orthodoxy for another: that non-pharmaceutical interventions were largely unsupported, probably ineffective, and mainly a story of elite panic. That conclusion is too confident. It underplays the genuine uncertainty of March 2020, relies too heavily on crude comparisons among U.S. states, and does not adequately distinguish between interventions, time periods, goals, and implementation. A democratic postmortem should be unsparing, but it should not flatten the pandemic into a morality tale about “lockdowns.”

Start with the central empirical question. Macedo and Lee argue that more restrictive states did not have lower mortality before vaccines and that differences in mortality emerged mainly after vaccination. That is an important observation. But state-level comparisons are a weak instrument for measuring the effects of policies during a fast-moving epidemic.

Pandemic outcomes depended on when the virus arrived, where it seeded, household structure, nursing home exposure, population density, weather, age, comorbidities, prior immunity, health care capacity, workplace patterns, voluntary behavior, and chance. New York and New Jersey suffered catastrophic early mortality before clinicians understood the disease, before testing was adequate, before masking was common, and before policymakers had much time to act. Some less restrictive states were spared early not because they had discovered better policy but because the virus had not yet struck them with the same force. Later, many places with fewer formal restrictions still experienced behavioral changes: people canceled trips, avoided restaurants, worked from home, wore masks, or limited gatherings without being legally required to do so. If residents in a Republican state voluntarily reduced contacts while residents in a Democratic state complied only partially with stricter rules, the measured effect of legal stringency will be muddy.

This does not mean mandates worked. It means the evidence Macedo and Lee cite cannot bear all the weight they place on it. The question “Did NPIs work?” is too broad. Which NPIs? Against which variant? At what level of community spread? For what duration? With what compliance? To achieve what goal—eradication, delay, hospital preservation, mortality reduction, protection of nursing homes, or time until vaccination?

Some interventions were plainly more plausible than others. Closing beaches and parks was generally foolish. Outdoor masking mandates were mostly theater. Curfews often seemed arbitrary. Prolonged school closures were harmful and, after the first wave, increasingly difficult to justify. But reducing large indoor gatherings during explosive spread, improving ventilation, encouraging high-quality masks in crowded indoor spaces, isolating infected people when paid leave made that feasible, and temporarily limiting high-risk indoor venues during hospital crises were not irrational imitations of authoritarian China. They were attempts—imperfect, sometimes clumsy—to reduce transmission of an airborne pathogen before vaccines.

Macedo and Lee emphasize pre-pandemic planning documents that warned against sweeping NPIs. Those documents are relevant, but they were not tablets from Sinai. Many were written with influenza in mind. SARS-CoV-2 differed in crucial ways: substantial presymptomatic and asymptomatic transmission, overdispersion events, a steep age gradient, severe pressure on hospitals, and initially no population immunity. Pre-COVID plans rightly warned that quarantine and border closures often fail. But “often fail” is not the same as “all contact reduction is useless.” A democratic society should have taken those warnings seriously; it also had to adapt to the pathogen in front of it.

The Imperial College report has become a symbol of modeling overreach. Some criticism is justified. Models can create a false aura of precision, and their assumptions should be transparent. But it is also worth remembering the specific fear in March 2020: not simply deaths over a long horizon, but the collapse of health care capacity over weeks. If hospitals are overwhelmed, mortality rises not only from the virus but from ordinary emergencies. The goal of temporary restrictions was partly to buy time: for hospitals to expand capacity, for protective equipment to be produced, for clinicians to learn, for testing to improve, and, eventually, for vaccines and therapeutics to arrive. Buying time is not nothing.

Nor is “focused protection” the easy alternative it later appeared to be. The Great Barrington Declaration was right about the age gradient and right that children and young adults should not bear indefinite burdens to protect lower-risk adults. But protecting the vulnerable while allowing widespread transmission among the non-vulnerable was far harder than the phrase suggested. Older people do not live on islands. They live with adult children, receive care from low-wage workers, undergo dialysis, share nursing home staff, rely on hospitals, and participate in communities. Many working-age adults were also vulnerable because of diabetes, obesity, immune suppression, pregnancy, or occupational exposure. The United States struggled to protect nursing home residents even while suppressing transmission broadly; it is not obvious that it could have protected them better amid much higher community spread.

That said, critics of the Great Barrington Declaration often behaved shamefully. Francis Collins’s “fringe epidemiologists” email was revealing not because the declaration was above criticism, but because it showed a preference for reputational destruction over public argument. The proper response would have been: here is where the declaration is right, here is where it is vague, here are the practical obstacles, here are the ethical risks. Instead, many institutions treated disagreement itself as a danger. That was a serious failure.

The same distinction applies to speech platforms. A pandemic creates real misinformation problems. False claims that vaccines magnetize people, that hospitals are fabricating deaths, or that drinking bleach cures COVID-19 are not brave dissent. Platforms had reason to reduce the spread of harmful falsehoods. But defining misinformation as whatever contradicted the WHO or local health authorities was indefensible. Public health authorities were learning, revising, and sometimes wrong. Lab-leak discussion should not have been suppressed. Debate over school closures, cloth masks, and natural immunity should have been permitted. The cure for uncertainty is not enforced unanimity.

Where Macedo and Lee are strongest is on the moral economy of pandemic policy. Restrictions did not fall equally. Professionals could work on laptops while delivery drivers, grocery clerks, nursing aides, meatpacking workers, bus drivers, and warehouse employees kept society running. Many affluent families experienced lockdown as inconvenience; many poor families experienced it as unemployment, crowding, missed services, isolation, or danger. Children in private schools often returned sooner than those in public schools. The people most enthusiastic about extended restrictions were often least exposed to their costs.

Still, one should be careful not to suggest that popular support for restrictions was simply elite class interest. In 2020 many people supported restrictions because they were frightened, because they had lost relatives, because hospitals were full, because they wanted to protect elderly parents, or because they saw temporary sacrifice as civic solidarity. Some were wrong about particular policies. But they were not merely hysterical or authoritarian. A good postmortem should grant good faith even when judging decisions harshly.

The school issue deserves special condemnation because the evidence changed and policy did not change quickly enough. By late 2020 and certainly after teachers had access to vaccines, the balance shifted strongly toward reopening schools with mitigation. Other countries managed more in-person schooling. Many American districts did not. Teachers’ unions, cautious administrators, political leaders, and public health agencies all share responsibility. The CDC’s school guidance was at times too slow, too complex, and too deferential to zero-risk thinking. Children paid for adult institutional failure.

But even here, the pandemic-versus-policy distinction can be overdrawn. Some learning loss occurred because of illness, bereavement, fear, family disruption, parental job loss, and voluntary absenteeism. The pandemic itself destabilized childhood. School closures worsened that destabilization dramatically, but they were not the only cause. Good accounting should apportion responsibility carefully.

What should the next pandemic doctrine be? First, humility. Officials should communicate in probabilities, not commandments disguised as science. “We do not yet know” must become an acceptable sentence. Second, disaggregation. Never again should we speak of “lockdowns” as one policy. Closing an indoor nightclub during a hospital surge, closing an elementary school for a year, and banning solitary outdoor recreation are morally and empirically different acts. Third, priority for children. Schools should be among the last institutions to close and the first to reopen. Closures should require clear evidence, legislative authorization if prolonged, and compensatory plans. Fourth, ventilation and infrastructure. The United States spent vast sums on income support, much of it necessary, but far too little on making indoor air safer in schools, nursing homes, prisons, and workplaces. Fifth, social support. Isolation and quarantine cannot work humanely if people lose wages or housing when they comply. Paid sick leave is a public health tool.

Finally, we need institutional pluralism. Public health agencies should not be expected to decide all social values. Economists, educators, pediatricians, mental health experts, ethicists, disability advocates, labor representatives, and civil libertarians should be built into emergency decision-making from the start. But pluralism also requires accepting that decisions will remain tragic. In a pandemic, delay kills; action harms; uncertainty is unavoidable.

Macedo and Lee are right that institutions must earn trust through candor. But candor cuts both ways. It requires acknowledging the damage caused by overbroad restrictions and the damage that uncontrolled spread would have caused. It requires admitting that some dissenters were mistreated and that some dissenters were wrong. It requires recognizing that vaccines were a triumph, school closures a failure, censorship a blunder, and early temporary restrictions a defensible response to terrifying conditions.

The reckoning we need is not an acquittal. It is also not a conviction on all counts. It is a disciplined effort to learn which actions helped, which harmed, which were unknowable at the time, and which were maintained after evidence no longer supported them. If we replace “follow the science” with “the science proved lockdowns failed,” we will have learned too little. The better lesson is harder: in a crisis, democracy needs expertise, but expertise must be transparent, contested, limited, and answerable to the people whose lives it governs.
