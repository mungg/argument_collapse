"The Yasusada hoax was primarily a failure of editorial diligence and institutional responsibility rather than a purely philosophical or theoretical problem of authorship."

The Yasusada affair looks, from one angle, like a philosophical puzzle about authorship. From another, it looks like a practical editorial failure. I want to emphasize the second angle, not because the first is unimportant, but because literary culture often uses theory to excuse negligence. Whatever we think about pseudonyms, Orientalism, postmodern authorship, or the ethics of representing trauma, the immediate fact is plain: journals published work under false documentary pretenses without adequate verification.

Editors cannot verify everything. Little magazines are understaffed, underfunded, and built on trust. They depend on networks of poets, translators, and scholars who send work in good faith. To demand legalistic documentation for every poem would suffocate the very culture we want to preserve. But translation is a special case. When an editor publishes translated work, the editor implicitly vouches not only for literary quality but for a chain of transmission: author, source language, translator, context. If the author is unknown, the archive unpublished, and the biography historically charged, the obligation increases.

In the Yasusada case, ordinary diligence would have raised alarms. A Japanese scholar could have noted the problems with names. A historian of Japanese education could have questioned Hiroshima University in the 1920s. A reader familiar with Barthes and Celan would have seen chronological impossibilities. A specialist in modern Japanese poetry might have recognized that the work sounded uncannily tailored to American avant-garde taste. None of these checks required a vast bureaucracy. They required one or two phone calls, letters, or photocopies sent to competent readers.

Why did that not happen? Partly because editors were seduced by the archive. The notebooks, letters, English assignments, translator notes, and biographical apparatus created an effect of density. The more documentation appeared, the less need there seemed to be for documents. This is a familiar problem. False archives often persuade because they imitate the clutter of reality: misspellings, footnotes, minor confusions, marginal figures, dates. The reader mistakes texture for proof.

But the deeper issue is prestige. The Yasusada materials came framed by references that mattered to their likely editors: renga, Spicer, Barthes, Celan, Silliman, the new sentence, Zen, Hiroshima. They appeared not as raw foreign material needing mediation but as material already conversant with the editor’s world. The poems were strange enough to seem international, familiar enough to seem brilliant. They were, in editorial terms, irresistible.

Marjorie Perloff’s essay rightly exposes this desire. She also rightly resists the overstatement that a literary hoax is a “criminal act.” Yet I would put more pressure than she does on institutional responsibility. Once editors discovered the deception, indignation was understandable, but indignation cannot substitute for self-examination. To say “we were defrauded” is true only up to a point. Editors are gatekeepers. Gatekeeping is not merely selection; it is care.

What standards should govern future cases? First, editors should distinguish clearly between translation, adaptation, and invention. If a poem is “after” Basho, say so. If it is a free imitation from a literal crib, say so. If it is a fictional translation, say so—or at least be prepared to defend the fiction as fiction. The romance of “from the notebooks of” should not be used casually.

Second, source texts should be available to the editor, even if not printed. This is not an unreasonable burden. Translators of living or recently dead poets should be able to provide originals, publication history, permissions, or contact with literary executors. In cases of newly discovered manuscripts, editors should ask where the manuscripts are, who owns them, who has examined them, and whether independent scholars can inspect copies.

Third, biographical claims involving major historical trauma should be checked. This is not because victims require certification before they may speak; rather, it is because the category of witness carries special public trust. A poem by a Hiroshima survivor, a camp survivor, a political prisoner, or a torture victim does not become valuable solely because of that status, but the status changes reception. Editors know this. Therefore they must verify it.

Fourth, journals should cultivate broader advisory circles. Perloff’s point about the neglect of contemporary Japanese poetry is crucial. If American editors had regular relations with Japanese poets, critics, and translators, Yasusada’s oddities might have been caught sooner. The solution to hoaxes is not suspicion alone but relationship. A culture that knows only the Japan of Zen masters and bomb victims is easily fooled by a clever imitation of both.

Fifth, when errors occur, corrections should be honest and informative. American Poetry Review’s apology was necessary, but apologies can become rituals of purification. Better would be a dossier: what was submitted, what was checked, what was missed, what scholars now say, how future submissions will be handled. Readers deserve to learn not only that they were misled but how literary credibility is manufactured.

None of this requires hostility to pseudonymous art. There is a place for masks, heteronyms, invented translators, and false documents. But such works should enter the literary field with an awareness of the contracts they manipulate. A pseudonym printed as a pseudonym is one thing. A heteronym in a book by Pessoa is one thing. A fabricated Hiroshima survivor presented in translation is another. The difference lies not in metaphysics but in editorial framing.

Perloff suggests that Yasusada may ultimately perform a service by forcing readers back to actual Japanese writing and actual Hiroshima testimony. I hope so. But that service will be accidental unless institutions change their habits. The avant-garde likes to imagine itself immune to the sentimentalities of the mainstream. The Yasusada episode shows otherwise. Experimental editors can be just as vulnerable to kitsch when the kitsch arrives in avant-garde clothing.

Trust is the lifeblood of small literary communities. It should not be replaced by paperwork, but neither should it be treated as an excuse for credulity. The lesson of Yasusada is not “never believe.” It is “know enough, and know whom to ask.”

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Editors have a heightened obligation to verify the chain of transmission and historical context when publishing translations of archives involving major historical trauma.
  2. 02The hoax succeeded because editors were seduced by a dense biographical apparatus and a prestige-laden framing that mirrored their own avant-garde tastes.
  3. 03Basic fact-checking, such as consulting Japanese scholars or verifying historical dates, would have easily exposed the chronological and linguistic impossibilities of the Yasusada persona.
  4. 04To prevent future deceptions, journals must require access to source texts and cultivate broader international relationships with contemporary literary communities.
  5. 05The incident reveals that experimental literary circles are just as susceptible to sentimental kitsch as the mainstream when it is presented in a familiar intellectual vocabulary.
  6. 06When a hoax is exposed, institutions should provide transparent dossiers on their editorial failures rather than relying on simple apologies to restore credibility.
