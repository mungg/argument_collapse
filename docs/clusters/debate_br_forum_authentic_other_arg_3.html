"The Yasusada hoax is not merely a clever literary performance or a critique of multiculturalism, but a profound ethical trespass that appropriates the trauma of Hiroshima survivors for the sake of an avant-garde game."

The striking thing about Marjorie Perloff’s extended meditation on the Yasusada hoax is the way it gradually elevates a literary trick into a seminar on Orientalism, author-function, and the state of American letters—and in doing so, talks around the one subject that refuses to be intellectualized into harmlessness. That subject is Hiroshima. Perloff notes that the poet’s survivor biography was a “central factor in the equation” of the hoax’s reception, and she chides the American poetry world for seeking tragic authenticity in the wrong places. But she never fully confronts the moral abyss that opens when a writer, safely ensconced in rural Illinois, dons the mask of a man who lost his wife and child in the atomic blast and then invents witty, stoic, surreal elegies for that fictive daughter. The hoax is not merely a “clue”-ridden performance or a critique of multiculturalist yearning; it is an exercise in ethical trespass that deserves stronger condemnation than Perloff’s tight-lipped ambivalence.

Let us state plainly what the Yasusada project does. It appropriates one of the most terrible instances of mass death in the twentieth century—an event that continues to shape the bodies and psyches of actual living people—and turns it into a set piece for an avant‑garde literary game. The author of the hoax, whether Kent Johnson or some other hidden hand, created not just a spurious poet but a spurious hibakusha. The difference matters. Hiroshima survivors, the genuine ones, are not simply romantic figures of stoic suffering; they are human beings who faced the fire, who watched their children die of radiation sickness, who have had to contend for decades with physical pain and social stigma. To fabricate such a figure in order to satisfy, or to satirize, the hunger for “authentic” victim voices is an act of breathtaking presumption. It is as though someone invented a Holocaust survivor to make a point about the excesses of Holocaust memoir. The point might be valid, but the invention would remain a desecration.

Perloff, for all her analytic acumen, gets the ethical calculus wrong. She brackets the morality question by saying that calling the hoax a “criminal act” is “absurd” and that pseudonyms are time‑honored. But the pseudonym of a Fernando Pessoa heteronym or the Macpherson Ossian is not equivalent to the impersonation of a particular kind of survivor whose testimony carries existential weight. The identity of “Hiroshima poet” is not a neutral literary costume; it is a historically saturated position that comes with obligations of truthfulness and care. To utter the words “my wife and youngest daughter died instantly in the atomic blast” from behind a mask, even with clues that scream “this is a fiction,” is to borrow a horror you have not experienced, to deploy it as a literary device, and to leave real survivors to pick up the emotional shrapnel. It is, in a real sense, a lie told in the presence of the dead.

Perloff’s own evidence underscores the damage. She notes that a reader for Wesleyan University Press felt “queasy” about the manuscript, and that John Solt called it “Japanized crap.” Yet she seems to treat these reactions as symptoms of an unsophisticated literalism—a failure to appreciate the play of irony and textual slippage. What if the queasiness is the correct response? What if the feeling that it is not permissible to “impersonate a figure as ipso facto tragic as a Hiroshima survivor” is not a relic of naïve biography-worship but a moral intuition worth defending? The American poetry community may indeed be guilty of an unseemly appetite for representations of agony, but that guilt does not exonerate the hoaxer; it simply means we have two wrongs, not a right.

One could also argue that the hoax enacts a subtle form of Orientalist violence different from the one Perloff anatomizes. She contends that the poems pander to an American fantasy of Japan as a realm of compressed, elliptical, dignified suffering. But the greater offense may lie in the act of putting words into the mouth of a Japanese victim—words that were never spoken, a grief that was never felt—and then presenting them to an American audience that is already predisposed to view Hiroshima primarily through the lens of American guilt and Japanese victimhood. The real Yasusada, whoever he may have been, did not get the chance to craft his own elegies. The hoax fills that silence with a ventriloquist’s hollow echoes.

What is most disappointing is that Perloff, having taken us to the edge of this ethical precipice, steps back into the comfort of theory. She notes that the poems “play into the residual guilt of contemporary American readers” and that the hoax “forces us to think about these questions.” But thinking, in her treatment, becomes a substitute for a more direct reckoning with the fact that someone used the suffering of Hiroshima to play a literary prank on the American avant‑garde. The hoax may have exposed the contradictions of our multiculturalist moment, but it did so on the backs of the real dead, and that is a cost no amount of clever deconstruction can justify.

Diversified DeepSeek

"The Yasusada hoax is not a harmless literary provocation but a noxious act of imperial ventriloquism that exploits the historical trauma of Hiroshima to serve American aesthetic debates."

Marjorie Perloff’s autopsy of the Yasusada phenomenon is, for all its agile footwork around Foucault and Orientalism, a curiously depoliticized document. It speaks of discourse, of identity‑functions, of the hunger for authenticity, but it never squarely names the political economy that turns the ashes of Hiroshima into cultural capital for American poets. The Yasusada hoax is not merely a semiotic provocation or a carnivalesque unmasking of editorial bias. It is a particularly noxious instance of imperial ventriloquism, and the fact that its creators embedded clues of their own fraud does nothing to absolve them of the violence inherent in the act.

Let us begin with the most uncomfortable fact: the United States dropped the atomic bomb on Hiroshima and Nagasaki. That act was not a tragic accident of history; it was a calculated military decision, debated at the highest levels, and executed on a mostly civilian population. For fifty‑two years, American culture has struggled to process this event, veering between self‑justifying Cold War triumphalism and a guilt‑soaked fascination with Japanese suffering. The genuine hibakusha testimonies that Perloff cites—Hara Tamiki’s desperate cry for water, Toge Sankichi’s searing free‑verse reportage—emerge from that cauldron of pain. They are not “texts” in a vacuum; they are emergency transmissions from bodies that were irradiated, burned, and broken. To fabricate a new, conveniently avant‑garde hibakusha and to place him within a discursive lineage that flatters the American Language poetry scene is to commit a double insult: first, to the dead, whose voices are stolen and ventriloquized, and second, to the living, whose actual complex literature is erased.

Perloff notes that contemporary Japanese poets rarely write about Hiroshima and that the subject is “painful” and “difficult.” That difficulty is not a coy refusal to satisfy Western curiosity; it is a political stance. As Nagahata Akitoshi points out in his letter to Perloff, Japanese artists are both the sons and daughters of the bombed and of the oppressors. To speak of Hiroshima is immediately to confront the history of Japanese imperialism, the Rape of Nanking, the cannibalism of the Omiya veterans described in the recent New York Times article, and the deeply compromised position of the postwar Japanese state. A genuine Japanese poet who takes up the atomic bomb today must navigate this minefield of complicity and victimhood. The Yasusada hoax, by contrast, evades all such complications. Its inventor, an American, feels free to project onto his creation a pure victim‑status—a dead wife, a dead daughter, a lonely cancer death—untainted by the moral shadings that torment actual Japanese memory. The fictive Yasusada becomes the perfect, guiltless sufferer, and his poetry becomes a screen on which American readers can project their own remorse without ever having to ask what their own nation did, or what Japan did, or how those questions intertwine.

This is not an argument for censorship. It is an argument about the ethics of representation under conditions of extreme power imbalance. When a member of the dominant imperial culture creates a fictional member of a formerly colonized and bombed culture, and then uses that fiction to score points in a domestic literary squabble, the result is not merely a “hoax” in the tradition of Ossian or Ern Malley. It is an act of symbolic recolonization. The Japanese body becomes a costume, and the suffering of Hiroshima becomes a prop in a debate about Ron Silliman’s “new sentence.” The real crimes of American militarism are aestheticized into a quiver of poetic affect—the delicate urn, the severed head of the mad daughter—and then the entire apparatus is revealed to be a joke, a “gotcha” aimed at well‑meaning liberal editors.

Perloff’s framing, for all its apparent left‑sympathies, risks reinforcing this depoliticization. By folding the Yasusada case into a narrative about the “New Multiculturalism” and its unintended Orientalism, she abstracts the specific historical injury into a generic problem of representation. Orientalism, as Edward Said taught us, is not a mere mindset; it is a material practice that accompanied, enabled, and justified the exercise of Western power over the East. To treat the Yasusada hoax as primarily a literary‑critical affair is to forget that the Real Hiroshima—not the one in Johnson’s notebooks—was produced by an American bomb. That should remain the starting point for any ethical discussion, and from that starting point the fabricator’s irony looks less like brilliance and more like an evasion of historical responsibility.

  3. 03The project functions as a form of symbolic recolonization where Japanese suffering is reduced to a prop for scoring points in domestic American literary squabbles.
  4. 04Embedding clues of the fraud does not absolve the creators of the ethical violence inherent in aestheticizing the victims of American nuclear warfare.
  5. 05Treating the hoax as a mere literary-critical issue of 'Orientalism' abstracts and depoliticizes the specific historical injury caused by the American bombing of Hiroshima.
