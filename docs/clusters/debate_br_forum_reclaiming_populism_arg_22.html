Diversified Gemini

"The American left should abandon the project of reclaiming populism in favor of the Black Radical Tradition and abolitionist frameworks, as the populist tradition is fundamentally rooted in racial capitalism and settler colonialism."

In “Reclaiming Populism,” Adom Getachew touches upon the efforts of left organizers to forge a “multiracial working class alignment,” navigating the wreckage of the Bernie Sanders campaign and the terrifying new reality of the COVID-19 pandemic. Getachew rightfully centers the persistent barrier of white supremacy in these efforts. Yet, the very premise of the symposium she introduces—that the left should seek to "reclaim" populism—deserves rigorous scrutiny from the perspective of the Black Radical Tradition. To speak of reclaiming populism is to invoke a nostalgia for a past that was never built for, nor inclusive of, Black, Brown, and Indigenous people. The American populist tradition is not a neutral vessel that can simply be emptied of its reactionary elements and refilled with multiracial solidarity; it is deeply infected by the logics of racial capitalism and settler colonialism. We do not need to reclaim populism; we need to transcend it.

The historical referent for American populism is the agrarian Populist Party of the late 19th century. Leftist historians often romanticize this period as a missed opportunity for cross-racial class solidarity, pointing to moments when white and Black farmers briefly collaborated against the predations of the railroads and the banks. But this narrative glosses over how quickly white populists jettisoned their Black allies the moment it became politically expedient, culminating in figures like Tom Watson, who began as an advocate for biracial class solidarity only to become one of the most virulent white supremacists and anti-Semites in the South.

Similarly, the great triumph of 20th-century social democratic "populism"—the New Deal—was explicitly built on a racial compromise. To pass his transformative economic agenda, Franklin D. Roosevelt capitulated to Southern Dixiecrats, intentionally designing programs like Social Security and labor protections to exclude agricultural and domestic workers, the primary occupations of Black Americans at the time. The very foundation of the American middle class was constructed through redlining and GI Bill benefits that were systematically denied to Black veterans.

When today's left-populists, even well-meaning ones, call for a return to the spirit of the New Deal or emphasize a "universal" economic message that supposedly transcends race, they are invoking a political tradition that has always treated Black liberation as secondary, a distraction, or a bargaining chip to be traded away for white working-class support. The failure of the Sanders campaign to win over older Black voters in the South was not simply due to the "establishment" pulling strings, as some populists defensively claimed. It was a reflection of a deep, historically grounded skepticism among Black communities toward political movements that promise sweeping, class-based revolutions while failing to adequately center the specific, lethal mechanics of anti-Black racism.

The COVID-19 pandemic, as Getachew points out, makes the impossibility of a colorblind, class-reductionist populism undeniably clear. The virus is spreading along the fault lines of racial capitalism. In Chicago, Milwaukee, Louisiana, and New York, Black and Brown people are dying at rates drastically disproportionate to their population. This is not merely because they are disproportionately poor or working-class. It is the result of generations of medical racism, environmental racism (placing toxic facilities in Black neighborhoods, leading to high rates of asthma), and the hyper-policing and mass incarceration that have created incubators for the virus in jails and prisons. You cannot solve these interlocking crises with a universal basic income or free college alone. You must actively dismantle the architectures of white supremacy.

Instead of trying to squeeze the square peg of Black liberation into the round hole of traditional populism, the left should look to the framework of Abolition and the Black Radical Tradition. Movements like the Movement for Black Lives (M4BL) offer a far more robust, transformative vision than the nostalgia of left-populism. Abolitionist organizers are not simply asking for a fairer distribution of wealth within the current system; they are demanding the dismantling of the carceral state—police, prisons, and detention centers—which serves as the violently coercive arm of racial capitalism.

Getachew highlights the inspiring work of Jeanette Taylor and the Chicago Teachers Union. It is vital to recognize that the CTU's radicalism stems precisely from their refusal to adhere to traditional, narrow bread-and-butter unionism. Under the leadership of the Caucus of Rank-and-File Educators (CORE), the CTU explicitly adopted an anti-racist framework, striking not just for pay, but for affordable housing, sanctuary protections for undocumented students, and an end to the school-to-prison pipeline. They understand that for their Black and Brown students, the classroom cannot be separated from the violently policed streets or the threat of eviction. This is "Bargaining for the Common Good," and it is rooted in intersectional organizing, not colorblind populism.

As we face an economic depression and a public health catastrophe overseen by a white nationalist administration and weakly opposed by a corporatist Democratic nominee, the task is not to resurrect the ghost of Eugene Debs or FDR. The task is to build a mass movement guided by the insights of those who have always been the most marginalized by both capital and the state. If we are to survive this crisis and build a just future, we must stop trying to reclaim a flawed populist history and commit instead to the radical, unprecedented work of building an abolitionist, anti-colonial democracy.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Historical American populist movements, from the 19th-century agrarian party to the New Deal, consistently marginalized or sacrificed Black interests to secure white political support.
  2. 02Modern left-populism’s emphasis on universal economic messages fails to address the specific, lethal mechanics of anti-Black racism that have caused disproportionate suffering during the COVID-19 pandemic.
  3. 03The skepticism of Black voters toward class-based revolutions is a rational response to a political tradition that treats racial liberation as secondary or a distraction.
  4. 04True social transformation requires dismantling the carceral state and the architectures of white supremacy rather than merely redistributing wealth within the existing system.
  5. 05Successful contemporary movements, such as the Chicago Teachers Union, derive their power from intersectional, anti-racist organizing rather than traditional colorblind populism.
