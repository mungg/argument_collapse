"While student activists must address real trauma, they must resist adopting a therapeutic, psychological framework that allows neoliberal universities to co-opt their movement and avoid structural economic and political transformation."

Kelley’s forum essay is a masterclass in navigating the dialectic between reform and revolution, and it arrives at precisely the right moment. The momentum from Mizzou and other campus uprisings could easily be co-opted into a new, university-managed discourse on “diversity and inclusion” that leaves the underlying economic and carceral structures completely untouched. We have seen this movie before. The 1960s campus revolts gave way to Black Studies departments, which over decades were defanged, deradicalized, and absorbed into the very institutional logic they were created to combat. Kelley implores us not to let history repeat, and central to his warning is a fierce critique of how the movement frames its grievances through the language of personal trauma.

This is Kelley’s most uncomfortable and most crucial argument. He is not denying that trauma is real or that mental health support is needed. What he identifies is a political danger: the substitution of a psychological framework for a structural, economic one. When students press for “triggers” to be avoided and for “inclusion and belonging training,” they are—perhaps unwittingly—shifting the solution to racism from the realm of power and political economy to the realm of human resources and individual therapy. This is not a failure of the students themselves; it is a testament to the thorough success of what David Theo Goldberg calls the postracial, neoliberal moment, which transforms systemic injustices into personal pathologies and technical problems to be managed by the right consultant.

But I want to push Kelley’s insight even further, beyond the university gates. The problem with the trauma frame is not just that it personalizes the political, but that it is increasingly incompatible with the kind of political subject required for the revolutionary transformation he and I both desire. A politics grounded primarily in vulnerability and the insistence on safety from harm struggles to develop the fortitude for the ruthless, long-term criticism Marx described. Building a new world is inherently unsafe work. It involves confrontation with state power, internal conflict, and constant failure. If our primary political identity is that of the wounded, we risk constructing a movement in which the greatest moral authority is granted to whoever can lay claim to the most exquisite pain, rather than whoever develops the sharpest strategy or the most compelling vision of liberation.

Kelley points us toward an alternative tradition by recovering the models of the Mt. Vernon black feminists and the Mississippi Freedom Schools, where the goal was to build “soul things” and collective power, not just to heal individual wounds. These spaces did not ignore pain; they contextualized it within a system and organized people out of it. Crucially, they treated the people involved not as bundles of trauma needing safe management, but as thinkers, critics, and agents capable of “ruthless criticism.” The twelve-year-olds writing essays in Lessons from the Damned were not being given trigger warnings; they were being given analytical tools to understand why they were poor and what to do about it. That is a profound act of love and respect for their intellectual capacity, a love Kelley rightly places at the center of our work.

The real challenge for today’s student activists is not to choose between demanding a counselor and demanding an end to sweated labor. One can fight for both. The challenge is to be vigilant about how the university will inevitably attempt to grant the first demand in order to neutralize the second. An administrator will much more readily hire a new diversity dean than pay all workers a $25 minimum wage. When we accept and center the therapeutic language the university offers us, we do the administration’s work for them, willingly stepping into the manageable box of “students in distress” rather than the threatening role of “workers and organizers in motion.”

Kelley’s essay is a loving, rigorous act of political education in itself. It asks students to do the hardest thing: to refuse the identity of the victim even while acknowledging the reality of the violence, and to insist, as Baldwin did, that the goal is not to be accepted into a burning house but to build a new one. That work starts by reclaiming our language from the clinic and returning it to the streets, the study circle, and the picket line.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The language of personal trauma and 'inclusion' shifts the focus of activism from systemic power and political economy to the realm of human resources and individual therapy.
  2. 02A political identity grounded primarily in vulnerability and safety is incompatible with the fortitude and confrontation required for long-term revolutionary struggle.
  3. 03University administrators prioritize granting therapeutic demands, such as mental health services or diversity training, as a strategy to neutralize more radical demands for economic justice.
  4. 04Historical models like the Mississippi Freedom Schools demonstrate that true liberation comes from treating the oppressed as intellectual agents capable of ruthless criticism rather than as victims needing management.
  5. 05Activists must reclaim their language from clinical settings and return it to collective spaces like study circles and picket lines to avoid being absorbed into the institutional logic they oppose.
