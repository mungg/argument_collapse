"The abortion debate should shift from abstract philosophical and metaphysical arguments about rights toward practical social policies that support women's real-world needs and reduce the necessity of abortion."

Reading Judith Jarvis Thomson’s meticulous philosophical argument, I kept thinking: where are the women? Not the abstract women whose liberty is constrained, but the real, flesh-and-blood women who face unintended pregnancies. For them, abortion is not primarily a problem of rights theory or metaphysical status; it is a concrete, solitary, often frightening decision. What they need most is not a philosophical vindication of their liberty, though that matters, but a society that makes it possible for them to bear children with dignity or to end a pregnancy safely and without shame. The abortion debate has become a hostage to metaphysical abstractions, and Thomson’s essay, for all its brilliance, remains largely on that terrain. I want to redirect the conversation toward what we can actually do to help women and reduce the toll of abortion—both the number of abortions and the harm that flows from making them harder to obtain.

Thomson is right that the Court’s protection of abortion access has been essential. But at the grassroots, the real battle is not over fertilized eggs versus paintings; it is over insurance coverage, medication like RU-486, sex education, contraception funding, and social support for mothers. The encyclical and the placards are symptoms of a deeper societal failure: the failure to value both women and children enough to provide the resources that would make abortion less necessary. When a woman faces an unplanned pregnancy, her reasons are the weighty ones Thomson lists—poverty, violence, career disruption, health risks. Those reasons won’t disappear because a philosopher shows that a zygote has no right to life. They will, however, be mitigated by a living wage, universal health care, affordable child care, and genuine gender equality.

The religious opposition Thomson criticizes often does speak from a deep commitment to the sanctity of life. But too often that commitment is selective, focusing solely on the unborn while ignoring the lives of poor women and children. Instead of trading arguments about moral status, we should be forging coalitions around concrete measures that reduce the demand for abortion. We know from experience that countries with strong social safety nets and easy access to contraception have lower abortion rates. We also know that making abortion illegal does not eliminate it; it only makes it deadly. The public health argument for legal, safe abortion is overwhelming. That argument gets short shrift in Thomson’s rights-based framework, but it is the one that resonates most with the public and that, in the long run, may do more to secure access.

I am not suggesting we abandon the philosophical defense of abortion rights; Thomson’s work is vital. But as a matter of political strategy and human empathy, we need to pivot from winning a metaphysical debate to attending to the real-world conditions that lead women to seek abortions. When a scared teenager walks into a clinic past shouting protesters, she does not care whether the embryo is a threshold person; she cares about her future. We should meet her there, with compassion, resources, and yes, the freedom Thomson rightly defends. But that freedom must be part of a broader commitment to women’s full flourishing, not just an abstract right protected by a 1973 court decision.

In the end, the abortion wars will not be won by philosophers trading arguments about the nature of interests. They will be won or lost in the realm of practical policy and cultural change. Let us turn our energies to making this country a place where every child is wanted and every mother supported. That is the common ground that transcends the rights debate, and it is high time we occupied it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Thomson’s focus on the metaphysical status of the fetus neglects the concrete social and economic realities, such as poverty and lack of healthcare, that drive women to seek abortions.

  3. 03Forging coalitions around contraception access and sex education is a more effective strategy for reducing abortion rates than trading arguments over moral status.
  4. 04The public health argument—that legal abortion prevents maternal mortality—is a more resonant and effective justification for access than abstract rights-based frameworks.
  5. 05The abortion conflict will ultimately be resolved through practical policy and cultural changes that ensure every child is wanted and every mother is supported.
