Diversified Gemini

"While Governor Roemer correctly identifies that campaign finance corruption is the root cause of political failure, his proposed solutions are too weak to counter the massive influx of corporate cash authorized by the Citizens United ruling."

Governor Charles “Buddy” Roemer’s diagnosis of our broken political system is as devastating as it is accurate. His narrative of Wall Street’s deregulation, the subsequent economic collapse, and the staggering bailouts provided to the very architects of the crisis perfectly encapsulates the profound corruption at the heart of Washington. When he notes that the financial sector was “unpunished, undisciplined, unregulated, unchanged,” he speaks for millions of Americans who watched in horror as Main Street was sold out to protect the bonuses of Goldman Sachs executives. Roemer is entirely correct that the root cause of this systemic failure is the campaign finance system. However, while his diagnosis is flawless, his proposed cure is woefully inadequate for the post-*Citizens United* reality.

Roemer envisions a system where candidates voluntarily limit themselves to $100 contributions and refuse Political Action Committee (PAC) money, perhaps supplemented by a system of tax-return vouchers. He explicitly rejects the Fair Elections Now Act and other models of robust public financing, arguing that his free-market, small-donor approach is sufficient. In the political landscape of July 2011, this approach is little more than bringing a knife to a bazooka fight.

Following the Supreme Court’s disastrous 2010 decision in *Citizens United v. FEC*, and the subsequent *SpeechNow.org* ruling, the floodgates for unlimited, untraceable corporate cash have been thrown wide open. We are witnessing the birth of the “Super PAC”—independent expenditure-only committees that can raise and spend limitless sums to elect or defeat candidates. If a principled candidate like Roemer limits himself to $100 donations, he may indeed build a respectable grassroots war chest. But what happens when a Super PAC funded by a handful of Wall Street billionaires decides to spend fifty million dollars blanketing the airwaves with attack ads against him? A self-imposed $100 limit will leave that candidate utterly defenseless against the tidal wave of outside money.

Roemer’s dismissal of the Fair Elections Now Act is particularly baffling. That legislation proposes a voluntary system where candidates who raise a certain number of small, grassroots contributions receive a public match. This is not, as Roemer claims, “bureaucrats” determining the resources available; it is a multiplier effect for grassroots support. If we want candidates to spend their time governing rather than groveling, we must provide a viable alternative funding mechanism that is robust enough to compete with special interest money. Small-donor vouchers alone simply cannot generate the volume of capital necessary to run a modern, competitive campaign in a major media market, especially when the opposition is backed by unlimited corporate independent expenditures.

Furthermore, Roemer’s assertion that we must simply “proceed in the light of the rules that the Supreme Court has given us” is an unacceptable surrender. The Supreme Court’s doctrine that money equates to speech, and that corporations possess the same First Amendment rights as human beings, is a radical and relatively recent misinterpretation of the Constitution. If the rules of the game make democratic self-governance impossible, then we must change the rules. We cannot afford to merely tinker around the edges with $100 limits and tax vouchers. We must build a movement for a constitutional amendment to overturn *Citizens United* and clearly establish that Congress and the states have the authority to regulate campaign spending.

Roemer is right that a president ought to represent 100 percent of the American people, not just the 1 percent who give. He is right that the legislative process has been reduced to an endless shakedown for campaign cash. But defeating an entrenched, multi-billion-dollar corporate oligarchy requires heavier weaponry than voluntary pledges and tax rebates. We need comprehensive public financing to liberate our representatives, and we need the courage to challenge the Supreme Court’s fundamentally flawed jurisprudence. Only then can we ensure that our government is truly dependent on the people alone.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A voluntary $100 contribution limit leaves candidates defenseless against the unlimited spending power of modern Super PACs.
  2. 02Robust public financing, such as the Fair Elections Now Act, is necessary to provide candidates with enough capital to compete with special interest money.
  3. 03Small-donor vouchers and tax rebates alone cannot generate the volume of funding required for modern, large-scale political campaigns.
  4. 04Rather than accepting the Supreme Court's current rules, we must pursue a constitutional amendment to overturn the doctrine that equates corporate spending with protected speech.
Diversified Gemini

"While Governor Buddy Roemer correctly identifies that corporate money has corrupted American governance, his proposed campaign finance tweaks are insufficient to dismantle a multi-trillion-dollar oligarchy that requires a mass grassroots political revolution to overthrow."

Governor Buddy Roemer is sounding an alarm that resonates deeply with Americans living outside the wealthy enclaves of the Acela corridor. His speech captures the raw, visceral anger of a nation betrayed by its own leadership. When he points out that Wall Street executives engineered the greatest destruction of household wealth since the Great Depression, only to be rewarded with multi-billion-dollar bailouts and record bonuses, he is describing a system that is not merely flawed, but fundamentally illegitimate. Roemer proves that the disgust with crony capitalism is not just a progressive or conservative issue; it is an American issue. However, while his outrage is precisely what this moment demands, his proposed solutions are far too timid for the crisis at hand.

Roemer suggests that the path to salvation lies in politicians voluntarily capping contributions at $100, refusing PAC money, and perhaps utilizing a system of tax-return democracy vouchers. He believes that if we can just tweak the financial plumbing of campaigns, Congress will suddenly revert to a deliberative body of independent statesmen. This is a technocratic fantasy that fundamentally misunderstands the nature of power.

Wall Street did not capture Washington simply because of a loophole in campaign finance law. They captured it through a sustained, decades-long ideological and financial siege. The deregulation of derivatives, the repeal of Glass-Steagall, and the massive bailouts were not passive mistakes; they were the active results of a corporate ruling class that views the federal government as its own private risk-management firm. You cannot dismantle a multi-trillion-dollar oligarchy by asking politicians to politely decline their checks. The system will not reform itself from the inside, because the people on the inside are the beneficiaries of the corruption.

Real change in American history has never come from elite-driven tax vouchers or voluntary pledges. It comes from mass mobilization and grassroots pressure that makes the status quo politically and socially toxic. When the trusts dominated the American economy during the Gilded Age, it was a ferocious populist movement that forced the creation of antitrust laws. When workers were being exploited in factories, it was the labor movement—striking, organizing, and bleeding in the streets—that won the weekend and the eight-hour day.

Right now, we are seeing the early rumblings of this kind of grassroots awakening. The Tea Party, despite its co-optation by certain corporate elements, began as a genuine roar of anger against the bank bailouts and a government that ignores the people. On the progressive side, there is a deep, simmering frustration with an administration that promised hope and change but delivered Timothy Geithner and Larry Summers. The American people are waking up to the fact that the two-party system is often just a duopoly managed by the same corporate board of directors.

What we need is not just a new way to fund campaigns, but a cultural shift in how we engage with power. We need sustained, organized pressure on every member of Congress who takes a dime from the big banks. We need to make the phrase "too big to fail" a political death sentence for any representative who supports it. The $100 pledge is a nice symbolic gesture for a presidential candidate, but it must be backed by a massive, mobilized citizenry willing to march, protest, and primary out the politicians who serve Wall Street.

Roemer is right that "leadership not free to lead does not lead." But politicians will only break free of their corporate masters when the fear of the people outweighs the allure of the lobbyists’ cash. We do not need a polite, voucher-funded debate. We need a political revolution that returns power to the communities, the workers, and the taxpayers who actually build this country. Until we build a grassroots movement loud enough to drown out the millions spent by the financial sector, the boomtown of Washington will continue to thrive while the rest of the nation struggles to survive.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Roemer’s focus on voluntary contribution caps and tax-return vouchers is a technocratic fantasy that fails to address the deep-seated ideological and financial siege of Washington by the corporate ruling class.
  2. 02The political system is incapable of reforming itself from the inside because the current leadership consists of the primary beneficiaries of the existing corruption.
  3. 03Historical progress in America, such as antitrust laws and labor rights, has always been achieved through mass mobilization and disruptive grassroots pressure rather than elite-driven policy adjustments.
  4. 04Meaningful change requires a cultural shift where organized citizens make the status quo politically toxic for any representative who serves Wall Street interests.
  5. 05Politicians will only prioritize the public interest over lobbyist cash when the fear of a mobilized and angry electorate outweighs the financial allure of special interests.
