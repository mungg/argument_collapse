The lead essay asks what can be done to bring American institutions into better alignment with American principles. My answer begins with a warning: no movement can repair democracy while standing aloof from the institutions through which democratic decisions are made. Occupy Wall Street has changed the national conversation, but unless that change enters elections, legislation, administrative rulemaking, and party politics, it will be remembered as a dramatic season rather than a turning point.

This is not an argument for taming Occupy or turning every encampment into a precinct operation. Movements need independence. They need the ability to criticize allies, embarrass the complacent, and speak in moral terms broader than the next bill. But the history invoked by the lead essay—abolition, suffrage, civil rights—does not support the fantasy that outsider protest alone wins. Each of those struggles required translation into law, and law required politicians, votes, appointments, enforcement, and courts. The civil rights movement had sit-ins and marches; it also had the Civil Rights Act and Voting Rights Act. Without both, moral witness would not have become institutional change.

The immediate danger is that Occupy will mistake attention for power. Media coverage, viral images, and sympathetic polls matter, but they do not by themselves alter tax rates, foreclosure procedures, labor law, or bank capital requirements. Meanwhile, organized interests know how to wait. Banks can lobby quietly through the winter. Wealthy donors can shape the 2012 campaign. State legislatures can pass voter ID laws and weaken unions. A movement that refuses to engage the machinery of politics may preserve its purity while losing the country.

What would engagement look like? First, voter registration and protection should be central. If the movement believes that everyone has a right to be heard, it should fight laws that make voting harder for students, the poor, the elderly, and minorities. The right to vote is not a symbolic issue; it is the floor of democratic equality. Occupy groups could partner with civil rights organizations, unions, campus groups, and churches to make 2012 a referendum not only on candidates but on democratic access itself.

Second, Occupy should identify winnable campaigns at local, state, and national levels. If the goal is majoritarian reform, then people who attend church, run small businesses, serve in the military, work two jobs, or live far from big cities must see themselves in the movement. The lead essay’s emphasis on principles can help here. The principle is not envy; it is equal citizenship. The problem is not that some people are rich; it is that wealth has become a form of political power that undermines opportunity and voice. That claim can unite liberals, many moderates, some libertarians, and disaffected conservatives who oppose crony capitalism. But unity will not happen automatically. It requires disciplined communication and strategic patience.

There is a romance in refusing politics as usual. After the bank bailouts, the Senate filibusters, the debt-ceiling debacle, and the endless fundraising rituals of Washington, that romance is understandable. Occupy has given America a new moral vocabulary. It has made inequality speakable and plutocracy visible. Now it must decide whether it wants influence commensurate with its insight. To occupy the future is not only to hold space; it is to contest power where power is exercised. Parks matter because they gather citizens. Polling places, council chambers, regulatory hearings, union halls, and legislatures matter because they govern them.

It can criticize bailouts without embracing a general suspicion of markets. If Occupy wants to contribute to the future rather than merely express discontent, it will need to distinguish between fair criticism and indiscriminate resentment.

Start with inequality. The United States has indeed experienced a dramatic rise in income concentration at the top. Executive compensation, financial-sector profits, globalization, technological change, tax policy, and weaker unions have all played roles. But inequality is not one thing. Some fortunes are made by creating goods and services that improve lives. Some are made through financial engineering, regulatory arbitrage, or political favoritism. Some wage stagnation reflects weakened bargaining power; some reflects competition from abroad and changes in skills demanded by the economy. A serious movement would sort these causes rather than treating the top 1 percent as a single class of villains.

The same is true of the financial crisis. There is no doubt that major financial institutions behaved recklessly and that government rescue efforts created a justified sense of outrage. But responsibility was widely distributed: banks, mortgage brokers, rating agencies, regulators, government-sponsored enterprises, investors, and borrowers all participated in a housing bubble sustained by the belief that prices could only rise. To say this is not to excuse Wall Street. It is to insist that durable reform requires accuracy. If we misdiagnose the crisis as simply greed, we will miss the institutional incentives that made greed catastrophic.

Occupy’s camps and general assemblies may be interesting experiments, but they are not substitutes for representative democracy. In some cities, encampments have created real burdens for local residents, small businesses, sanitation workers, and police. Public space belongs to everyone, not only to those able and willing to camp in it indefinitely. A movement that claims to speak for the 99 percent should not dismiss the concerns of the many citizens who support reform but dislike disorder.

Meetings dominated by those with time, stamina, and ideological confidence are not necessarily more representative than elected bodies. The challenge is not to romanticize process but to build institutions that combine participation with responsibility.

Higher capital requirements, credible resolution authority, and limits on risky activities backed by public guarantees are essential. There is room to raise revenue from those who have benefited most, but taxation should be designed for growth as well as fairness.

Many Americans oppose larger government not because they have been duped by billionaires, but because they distrust bureaucracy, worry about dependency, or believe markets usually allocate resources better than politicians. Movements can expand imagination; they can also evade responsibility.

If it matures into a reform movement—empirical, disciplined, open to disagreement—it could help renew American democracy. **The Numbers Behind the Slogan**

The Boston Review's framing essay asks whether we're entering a moment when the gap between our principles and our institutions has been "cast into especially sharp relief." For anyone who has been paying attention to the economic data over the past three decades, the answer is plainly yes—and Occupy deserves credit for finally forcing that data into political conversation.

Consider what Emmanuel Saez and Thomas Piketty's work on income concentration has shown. The share of national income accruing to the top one percent of American households has roughly doubled since the late 1970s, from around ten percent to over twenty percent. The top 0.1 percent has done even better. Meanwhile, real median wages have essentially stagnated since 1973, even as productivity has continued to climb. The Congressional Budget Office's October 2011 report, "Trends in the Distribution of Household Income Between 1979 and 2007," confirmed the picture from a different angle: after-tax income for the top one percent grew by 275 percent over that period; for the middle three quintiles, by under 40 percent.

These are not abstractions. They translate into concrete differences in life expectancy, educational attainment, civic participation, and—crucially—political voice. Martin Gilens's research on policy responsiveness suggests that when the preferences of the affluent diverge from those of the middle class and the poor, it is overwhelmingly the affluent whose preferences prevail in legislation. This is the empirical core of the "we are the 99%" slogan, and it is what makes the slogan more than a rhetorical flourish.

I would push the Boston Review's lead essay to be more specific, though. The language of "principles" and "practices" is appealing but also slightly evasive. The American story is not only one of principles slowly catching up to practice; it is also one of organized economic interests actively rewriting the rules to their advantage. The decline of unionization (from roughly a third of private-sector workers in the 1950s to under seven percent today), the deregulation of finance culminating in the repeal of Glass-Steagall, the shift to a tax code that treats carried interest as capital gains, the explosion of corporate money in elections following *Citizens United*—none of these are accidents of cultural drift. They are the result of decades of concerted effort by well-funded actors.

If Occupy is to translate moral energy into durable change, it needs to be at least as concrete about its diagnosis as about its grievances. The good news is that the policy menu is not mysterious. Restore meaningful progressivity to the tax code. Tax financial transactions. Break up institutions that are too big to fail. Reverse *Citizens United* by constitutional amendment if necessary. Strengthen the right to organize. Expand the Earned Income Tax Credit. Invest in infrastructure and public higher education at a scale appropriate to a wealthy nation. None of this is utopian; most of it was mainstream American policy within living memory.

The objection one hears constantly—that Occupy "has no demands"—is partly disingenuous (the demands are not hard to infer) and partly an artifact of the movement's deliberate horizontality. But I think it is also a real weakness. Social movements that have changed America—abolition, women's suffrage, the labor movement, civil rights—combined moral witness with specific legislative and institutional targets. The March on Washington was for "Jobs and Freedom," with concrete demands attached. The suffragists wanted the Nineteenth Amendment, not a vague reordering of gender relations. Occupy will eventually have to make analogous choices about what, exactly, it is asking the political system to do.

This is not a counsel of compromise so much as a counsel of leverage. Public attention is a scarce resource, and movements that fail to convert attention into institutional outcomes tend to dissipate. The Tea Party, whatever one thinks of its politics, understood this; it ran candidates, took over primaries, and reshaped a major political party in the span of two election cycles. Occupy has more diffuse goals and more skepticism about electoral politics, but the underlying logic is the same.

The most encouraging thing about the moment is that the diagnostic conversation has finally shifted. A year ago, the dominant frame in Washington was deficit hysteria; the threat to American prosperity was supposedly Social Security and Medicare, not concentrated wealth or unemployment. Today, polling shows that majorities across the political spectrum think inequality is too high and that the wealthy do not pay their fair share. That is a genuine political opening.

Whether the opening becomes a turning point depends on whether the energy in Zuccotti Park and a thousand smaller encampments can find institutional expression. The Boston Review's series will be valuable if it helps think through how. The principles are not really in dispute. The question is whether we have the political will and organizational creativity to make them operative again.

**The Money Problem**

If I had to identify the single institutional failure that most clearly drives the gap between American principles and American practice—the gap the Boston Review's lead essay rightly identifies—it would be the role of money in our political system. Almost every other grievance Occupy has raised, from financial deregulation to tax inequity to the housing crisis to student debt, traces back in significant measure to the fact that organized money exerts disproportionate influence over American policymaking. Without addressing this, no other reform agenda is sustainable.

The empirical case is overwhelming. Political scientists have spent the last decade documenting what political donors and lobbyists have known forever: policy outcomes in the United States are far more responsive to the preferences of affluent citizens and organized interests than to the preferences of ordinary citizens. Larry Bartels's *Unequal Democracy*, Jacob Hacker and Paul Pierson's *Winner-Take-All Politics*, Martin Gilens's work on policy responsiveness, Kay Lehman Schlozman and collaborators on inequality of political voice—the literature converges on a clear finding. When the preferences of upper-income Americans diverge from those of middle- and lower-income Americans, Congress votes with the upper-income preferences. This is true across both parties and across most issue domains.

How did we get here? Two trajectories converge. The first is the steady increase in the cost of political campaigns, driven by media expenses, professional consulting, and the lengthening of the campaign season. A House race that cost $87,000 on average in 1976 now costs well over $1.4 million for an incumbent. Senate races routinely cost tens of millions. To finance these campaigns, members of Congress now spend a startling portion of their working hours on the phone with donors. The structural pressure to attend to the concerns of high-dollar contributors is built into the daily schedule of legislators.

The second is the legal architecture surrounding political money, which the Supreme Court has progressively dismantled. *Buckley v. Valeo* (1976) established the doctrine that money is speech, limiting Congress's ability to cap independent expenditures. *Citizens United v. FEC* (2010) extended this logic to corporations and unions, holding that independent political expenditures by these entities cannot be limited. *SpeechNow v. FEC* (2010), decided in the wake of *Citizens United*, gave rise to Super PACs, which can accept unlimited contributions from any source as long as they do not formally coordinate with campaigns. The 2012 election cycle, now underway, is the first to fully feel the effects of this architecture, and the spending projections are unprecedented.

The result is a system in which the formal equality of citizens—one person, one vote—coexists with an extraordinary inequality in the political voice that money can purchase. The Boston Review essay invokes the principle that "everyone, not just the rich, should have a right to be heard when our country makes decisions." Under the current rules, this principle is not even approximately satisfied.

What is to be done? Several reform agendas are worth taking seriously.

The most direct response is a constitutional amendment to overturn *Citizens United* and clarify that Congress and states have authority to regulate political spending. This is a long project—amendments are difficult by design—but it is not hopeless. Public support for overturning *Citizens United* polls high across party lines. Several state legislatures have already passed resolutions calling for an amendment. Movements like Move to Amend are building the infrastructure for a sustained campaign.

Short of an amendment, public financing of elections offers the most promising path. Programs like the one in Connecticut, or the matching funds system in New York City, demonstrate that small-donor democracy is possible at scale. The Fair Elections Now Act, which has been introduced repeatedly in Congress, would extend a similar model to federal elections. Such systems do not eliminate private money, but they reduce candidates' dependence on it and open the door to candidates who lack access to wealthy networks.

Disclosure is another front. The DISCLOSE Act, which would require organizations engaging in political spending to identify their donors, has been blocked repeatedly but remains a key short-term reform. Voters cannot meaningfully evaluate political speech when they do not know who is funding it.

Reforming lobbying is harder but essential. The revolving door between Congress, executive agencies, and lobbying firms creates structural incentives for regulatory capture. Stronger restrictions on post-employment lobbying, more transparency about lobbying contacts, and meaningful funding for legislative staff capacity (so that legislators do not need to rely on lobbyists for substantive expertise) would all help.

**A Cautionary Note from Civil Rights History**

The Boston Review's lead essay places Occupy in a lineage with abolition, women's suffrage, and the civil rights movement. As a historian of the last of these, I want to offer both encouragement and caution about that comparison.

The encouragement first. American social movements have repeatedly forced the country to confront the gap between its founding language—equality, liberty, consent of the governed—and the realities experienced by significant portions of its population. The movements that succeeded did so by combining moral clarity with organizational sophistication, and by sustaining their efforts over years and decades, not weeks and months. Occupy is showing the beginnings of moral clarity. Whether it will develop the other capacities remains to be seen.

The caution is this: nothing about the civil rights movement was as spontaneous, leaderless, or improvisational as it sometimes appears in retrospect. The Montgomery bus boycott, often remembered as a moment when an ordinary woman refused to move, was in fact carefully planned by an experienced network of activists who had been preparing for the right test case for years. Rosa Parks was a trained organizer and the secretary of the Montgomery NAACP. E. D. Nixon had been working on bus desegregation since the 1940s. Martin Luther King Jr. was selected as the boycott's spokesperson in part because, as a newcomer, he had not yet accumulated enemies. The boycott itself required a sophisticated alternative transportation system, sustained over more than a year, requiring fundraising, dispatching, mechanical maintenance, and constant negotiation.

Behind the public moments stood institutions. The black churches provided meeting space, communication networks, financial infrastructure, and moral authority. The NAACP provided legal expertise. The Highlander Folk School trained activists. The Southern Christian Leadership Conference, the Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee, the Congress of Racial Equality each brought distinct strategic capacities. Even when these organizations disagreed sharply—and they did, often bitterly—they constituted an ecology of mutually reinforcing institutions that could absorb setbacks, sustain morale, train new participants, and translate moral energy into legislative and judicial outcomes.

I emphasize this institutional reality because contemporary social movements, including Occupy, often define themselves in opposition to formal organization. The general assemblies, the consensus process, the refusal to designate leaders—these are presented as principled commitments to horizontal democracy. They have undeniable virtues: they make participation accessible, they distribute moral risk, they resist co-optation by ambitious individuals. But they also have costs. Movements without designated leaders cannot negotiate. Movements without institutional memory cannot learn from defeat. Movements without sustained organizational capacity cannot outlast the news cycle.

The civil rights movement was not afraid of leaders, and it was not afraid of institutions. It produced King, Bayard Rustin, Ella Baker, Fannie Lou Hamer, John Lewis, Diane Nash, James Lawson, Septima Clark, and many others. Each of them played distinct roles within a movement that had factions, debates, hierarchies, and chains of command. The movement also produced specific legislative demands—the Civil Rights Act of 1964, the Voting Rights Act of 1965—and organized methodically to achieve them, including by maintaining relationships with sympathetic figures inside the Democratic Party and the federal executive.

A second caution. The civil rights movement was successful in significant part because it was disciplined about violence, and disciplined about its public image. Nonviolence was not merely a tactic; it was a strategic commitment that required intensive training. SNCC's workshops drilled participants in how to absorb blows without responding, how to protect one another, how to maintain dignity under provocation. The result was a series of confrontations—Birmingham, Selma—in which the contrast between disciplined protesters and brutal repression created the moral conditions for federal intervention.

Occupy has not yet been tested in quite this way. There has been confrontation with police, sometimes brutal, and to its credit the movement has largely maintained nonviolent discipline. But it has not invested in the kind of systematic training that sustained the civil rights movement, and there are visible factions—particularly around tactics of property destruction in some West Coast actions—that could easily compromise the movement's broader appeal. Sustaining nonviolent discipline at scale is not automatic. It requires teaching, modeling, and sometimes the exclusion of those who refuse to abide by it.

Third, the civil rights movement was a long project. The Montgomery boycott was in 1955–56. The Civil Rights Act was in 1964, nine years later. The Voting Rights Act was in 1965. Real implementation of school desegregation, fair housing, and voting rights protections stretched well into the 1970s and beyond. The activists who started the movement could not know whether their efforts would yield results in five years or fifty. They committed anyway, building careers and lives around the work.

Occupy is two months old. It would be premature to render any verdict, positive or negative. What I would urge, from the vantage of the earlier movement, is patience, institution-building, and a willingness to learn from movements that came before. The principles invoked by the Boston Review's lead are correct. The hard work is in the institutions.

The Boston Review's lead essay asks whether we are entering a moment of dramatic reform, as the disjuncture between principles and institutions has been "cast into especially sharp relief." As someone who is sympathetic to the Occupy movement's critique but cautious about predictions of imminent transformation, I want to offer a moderate assessment of the movement's prospects.

The movement has accomplished something important: it has made inequality and democratic participation legitimate topics of public debate in a way they had not been for a generation. Before September 2011, serious people could debate tax rates and social programs, but the underlying assumption was that the market economy was essentially sound and that the main challenges were macroeconomic rather than distributional. Occupy challenged this assumption and forced a conversation about who benefits from the current system and who pays the costs.

This is no small achievement. But movements need more than a critique; they need a program. The Occupy movement has been notably vague about what specific policies it supports. Without clear answers to such questions, sympathetic politicians and editorial writers have little to work with.

The great reform movements of American history all had leaders, organizations, and decision-making processes that could translate popular energy into institutional change.

The Boston Review's lead essay situates the Occupy movement within a long tradition of American reform movements that sought to close the gap between principles and practices. This historical framing is instructive, but it also raises questions about what kinds of movements actually succeed in producing lasting change—and what conditions enable such success.

The great reform movements in American history—abolitionism, the labor movement, the women's suffrage campaign, the Civil Rights Movement—shared certain features that distinguished them from mere expressions of discontent. They developed institutional structures that could sustain themselves beyond the initial energy of protest. They formulated clear demands that could be translated into legislative or legal terms. And they built coalitions that stretched across geographic, racial, and class lines to create electoral majorities capable of rewriting the rules of political life.

Consider the labor movement. In the early twentieth century, labor organizers faced violent opposition from both private industry and public authorities. The Haymarket affair, the Pullman strike, the deaths of miners at Homestead—these episodes showed that reform could be costly and that the forces of resistance were formidable. Yet the movement persisted because it had organizational depth: unions, newspapers, political parties (the Progressives and later the New Dealers), and a coherent vision of what economic democracy required. By the mid-twentieth century, labor had secured the right to collective bargaining, minimum wages, maximum hours, and workplace safety regulations that transformed the relationship between capital and labor.

The Occupy movement has energy but lacks institutional depth. Its spontaneous gatherings in public spaces capture the imagination, but can they survive the winter, the inevitable dispersal of participants to jobs and families, the slow erosion of public attention? Without organizational infrastructure—staff, funding, clear decision-making processes—the movement risks burning bright and disappearing, leaving behind sentiment but not institutions.

There is also the question of demands. The great reform movements were able to point to specific changes: the end of slavery, voting rights for women, desegregation, the right to organize. Occupy has offered critiques but not alternative policies. What tax rates would satisfy the movement? What regulations on financial institutions? Without concrete proposals, sympathetic politicians and editorial boards have little to work with.

This is not to dismiss the movement's potential. The Arab Spring demonstrated that social media could enable rapid mobilization, but it also showed that transitions from protest to governance are treacherous and that movements without organizational infrastructure often fail to consolidate gains. Egypt's military has reasserted control; Tunisia's democracy remains fragile. The lesson is that streets are not enough.

I hope the Occupy movement finds the organizational discipline to translate its moral energy into lasting institutional change. American history suggests this is possible, but it requires more than occupation.

The lead essay rightly identifies the Occupy movement as a profound moral reckoning, one that places our soaring economic inequality at the center of the American democratic project. Vanilla Claude

"While the Occupy movement successfully diagnosed a structural crisis in American equality, it must transition from moral witness to a specific legislative agenda to achieve the historic reforms seen in previous social movements."

The editors are right to frame Occupy as part of a long American tradition of measuring institutions against principles. That tradition runs from the abolitionists to the suffragists to the civil rights movement, and it has always depended on a particular rhetorical move: insisting that the country honor the commitments it has already publicly avowed. Occupy belongs in this lineage. But the framing in the lead essay, while generous, may understate how much harder this iteration of the work will be — because the gap that has opened between our principles and our practices is not only material but constitutional, in the small-c sense of how our common life is structured.

Consider what we now know about the distribution of income. The Congressional Budget Office's October 2011 report found that, between 1979 and 2007, after-tax household income for the top 1 percent grew by 275 percent, while the middle three quintiles grew by under 40 percent and the bottom quintile by just 18. The work of Piketty and Saez places the top 1 percent's share of national income back at levels not seen since the late 1920s. Median household income has barely budged for a decade and has fallen during the recovery. Roughly one in six Americans is in poverty by the new Supplemental Poverty Measure; long-term unemployment is at post-war highs; nearly four million homes have been lost to foreclosure since 2007. These are not the byproducts of a temporarily off-kilter economy. They are the contours of a different economy than the one most Americans believe they live in.

If that were the whole problem, the lead essay's optimism would be well placed. Reform movements have repeatedly closed material gaps when they could mobilize political voice. What is distinctive — and dangerous — about this moment is that the channels through which voice ordinarily travels are themselves being warped by the same inequality. Citizens United, decided less than two years ago, did not invent the role of money in politics, but it removed a major guardrail at precisely the moment when concentrated wealth had reached historic levels. The result is visible in the early shape of the 2012 cycle: the rise of super PACs, the willingness of single donors to sustain entire presidential campaigns, the bipartisan deference to financial-sector preferences in everything from Dodd-Frank's implementation to the foreclosure settlement negotiations. Larry Bartels and Martin Gilens have already shown in their work that, on contested questions, the policy preferences of low-income Americans bear almost no statistical relationship to what Congress actually does. The principle of equal political voice is not merely unrealized; it is being actively eroded.

This is why I think Occupy's most important contribution so far has been diagnostic rather than programmatic. The slogan "We are the 99%" did something that years of careful policy writing could not: it gave ordinary people a vocabulary for naming a structural fact about American life. Before September, "inequality" was a wonk's word. It is now a kitchen-table word. Pew, Gallup, and the major networks have all registered a sharp uptick in public concern about the gap between rich and poor in the weeks since Zuccotti Park filled. That is a real accomplishment, and one that the clearing of the encampments on November 15 has not undone.

But diagnosis is not cure, and here I want to push gently against the lead essay. The civil rights movement that the editors invoke did not succeed only by exposing the disjuncture between principle and practice. It succeeded because it coupled moral witness to a concrete legislative agenda: desegregation, the Voting Rights Act, fair housing. The suffragists wanted the vote. The abolitionists wanted emancipation. Occupy has, so far, deliberately resisted that kind of specificity, partly out of a justified fear of being co-opted, partly out of an admirable commitment to horizontal deliberation. The risk is that a movement which refuses to name what it wants leaves the naming to others — to sympathetic politicians who will translate its energy into the narrow vocabulary of their existing agendas, or to opponents who will fill the vacuum with caricature.

The questions the editors pose at the end of their statement — whether we are entering a new reform moment, whether we can build an open and successful movement — are therefore not rhetorical. They depend on choices yet to be made. A reform agenda adequate to the diagnosis would have to address campaign finance, the tax treatment of capital, the governance of finance itself, the bargaining power of workers, and the conditions of access to higher education and health care. None of these is beyond the reach of ordinary democratic politics. But reaching them will require Occupy, or some successor formation, to translate moral clarity into legislative specificity — and to do so before the encampments fade from memory and the country's attention drifts elsewhere.

  3. 03Unlike the civil rights or suffrage movements, Occupy has resisted the concrete programmatic specificity necessary to prevent its energy from being co-opted or caricatured.
