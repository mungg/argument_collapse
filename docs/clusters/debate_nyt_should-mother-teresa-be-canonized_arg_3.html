"Mother Teresa should not be canonized because her legacy involves a morally mixed record that should not be elevated to an untouchable universal model of holiness."

Mother Teresa should not be canonized, at least not now. The issue is not whether she was sincere, hardworking, or personally austere. She plainly was. The issue is whether the Church should elevate her as a universal model of holiness while serious questions remain about the kind of care her order gave to people who were almost entirely powerless.

The standard defense is that her homes were not hospitals but places where the abandoned could die with dignity. That answer is incomplete. Dignity is not only a clean bed and a prayer. It includes competent diagnosis, pain relief, safe needles, trained staff, and referral to medical facilities when treatment is possible. Critics, including physicians and former volunteers, have alleged that the Missionaries of Charity often fell short on these basics. The concern is not that Mother Teresa failed to create the Mayo Clinic in Calcutta. It is that an organization receiving enormous global admiration and donations sometimes seemed to make a virtue of minimal care.

Her theology also matters. Mother Teresa spoke movingly of seeing Christ in the poor, but she also appeared to treat suffering as spiritually valuable in ways that can become dangerous when applied to other people’s bodies. Choosing austerity for oneself is admirable. Accepting avoidable pain for the destitute is something else.

There are broader moral problems. Her relentless opposition to contraception was not a harmless doctrinal footnote in a world where women’s health, family size and poverty are closely connected. Her willingness to accept money and praise from dubious political and financial figures also deserves more scrutiny than the hagiographic version allows.

Canonization is not merely an internal Catholic housekeeping matter. Mother Teresa is one of the most recognizable religious figures of the twentieth century. Declaring her a saint will shape public memory. It will tell millions that her methods, not just her compassion, are beyond serious reproach.

A better course would be gratitude without canonization. Honor the sisters who bathed wounds and held hands. Honor the impulse to serve the abandoned. But do not turn a morally mixed record into an untouchable icon. The poor deserve love, but they also deserve medicine, accountability and rights. Sainthood should not obscure that.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The Missionaries of Charity frequently failed to provide basic medical standards such as competent diagnosis, pain relief, and safe equipment despite having the resources to do so.
  2. 02Mother Teresa’s theology problematically romanticized the suffering of the poor as spiritually valuable rather than seeking to alleviate avoidable pain.
  3. 03Her dogmatic opposition to contraception ignored the practical link between family planning and the improvement of women's health and poverty.
  4. 04She demonstrated a troubling lack of discernment by accepting financial support and public praise from questionable political and financial figures.
  5. 05Canonization would prematurely validate her controversial methods and potentially obscure the right of the poor to receive accountability and modern medical care.
