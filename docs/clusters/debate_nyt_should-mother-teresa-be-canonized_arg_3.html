"Pope Francis should delay Mother Teresa's canonization until there is a thorough investigation into the discrepancies between her public image and her actual practices."

Pope Francis should reconsider this canonization until serious questions about Mother Teresa's actual practices receive honest examination. Approving sainthood carries symbolic weight—it declares this person worthy of emulation. If the evidence suggests otherwise, rushing forward dishonors the process.

The care provided at Teresa's Homes of Charity has been documented as substandard by visitors who observed patients left unwashed, facilities lacking basic medical supplies, and insufficient pain management. Defenders claim poverty excuses this, but Teresa raised hundreds of millions of dollars. Where did that money go? She famously held it in bank accounts rather than spending it on improvements. This is not poverty—it is choice.

Her alignment with authoritarian figures demands scrutiny. She maintained relationships with Haiti's Duvalier regime, Albania's Enver Hoxha (who persecuted Catholics), and Sri Lankan leaders during civil conflict. She courted these associations while accepting their favors. This pattern suggests prioritization of institutional relationships over consistent moral witness.

Most troublingly, Teresa publicly opposed contraception and abortion in nations where women desperately needed access to family planning. Her political advocacy in this area contributed to preventable suffering on a massive scale. When powerful people use their moral authority to restrict reproductive options for poor women, the harm ripples across generations.

Canonization should await genuine accountability. The Church might consider whether someone who raised millions while allowing her beneficiaries to suffer is truly an "heroic virtue" exemplar. If Teresa's canonization proceeds unchanged, the Church signals that results matter less than intentions—or perhaps less than celebrity.
