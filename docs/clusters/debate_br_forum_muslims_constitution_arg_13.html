"While the government must guard against anti-Muslim prejudice and executive overreach, it has a legitimate duty to distinguish between peaceful citizens and violent ideological movements to ensure national security."

Akbar and Theoharis describe a real danger: government power aimed at a religious minority can become abusive, self-justifying, and blind to its own premises. The travel ban was drafted recklessly, rolled out incompetently, and defended with rhetoric that made many Muslims understandably fear that campaign prejudice had become governing policy. Conservatives should not dismiss those fears. Religious liberty is not a partisan ornament. It is central to the American experiment.

Yet the lead essay goes much further. It portrays the post-9/11 counterterrorism system as an interlocking regime of anti-Muslim suspicion and treats the distinction between “radical” and “acceptable” Islam as itself suspect. That framing is too sweeping. The United States faces actual enemies who justify violence in Islamic terms. Recognizing that fact is not the same as putting Islam on trial. A democratic government has the duty to distinguish between peaceful Muslim citizens and violent ideological movements. The alternative—refusing to make distinctions at all—would be disastrous both for national security and for Muslim Americans, who have the greatest stake in defeating extremists who claim to speak for their faith.

The phrase “radical Islamic terrorism” is contested, and reasonable people disagree about its usefulness. President Obama avoided it, arguing that it risked giving terrorists religious legitimacy and alienating allies. President Trump embraces it, sometimes in ways that sound less analytical than accusatory. But the underlying problem does not disappear because officials choose different words. Al Qaeda, ISIS, Boko Haram, al-Shabaab, and similar groups draw from particular extremist interpretations, networks, grievances, and ambitions. They recruit by appealing to religious obligation. They kill Muslims in large numbers as well as Christians, Jews, Yazidis, secularists, and Westerners. A security policy that cannot speak honestly about ideology will be weaker.

The lead essay rightly warns against collective suspicion. But at times it seems to imply that focusing counterterrorism resources on jihadist networks is inherently discriminatory. That cannot be right. Law enforcement routinely allocates attention based on threat patterns. In the 1990s, the federal government devoted attention to militias and white supremacists after Oklahoma City. After 9/11, it focused on jihadist terrorism because jihadists had just carried out the deadliest attack on American soil and continued to plot against the country. One may criticize specific tactics without denying that prioritization can be rational.

That said, conservatives should be candid about failures. The No Fly List needs due process reform. A citizen or lawful resident should not be trapped in bureaucratic limbo, unable to fly and unable to learn how to clear his name. Border questioning must not become harassment for its own sake. Informants should not be rewarded for nudging unstable people toward plots they would never have conceived or executed. Mosques should not be treated as suspicious merely because they are mosques. And if Countering Violent Extremism programs become a euphemism for monitoring ordinary religious conservatism, they will backfire.

But the lead essay’s account underplays why preventive tools exist. Terrorism is not ordinary crime. The whole point is to stop mass casualty attacks before they happen. Waiting until a bomb is built or a truck is driven into a crowd is not an acceptable law-enforcement posture. Material support laws, sting operations, intelligence gathering, and watch lists are attempts—imperfect, sometimes overbroad attempts—to disrupt networks early. The challenge is not to abolish prevention but to discipline it.

Material support law illustrates the tension. Akbar and Theoharis describe it as criminalizing association, opinion, travel, and charity. There have indeed been hard cases, especially where humanitarian work intersects with designated organizations that also control territory. But terrorist groups rely on money, logistics, legitimacy, communications, and recruitment. Aid is fungible. If an organization runs both social services and suicide bombings, support for one side may free resources for the other. The government must have authority to prevent Americans from strengthening such groups. The better reform is to clarify intent requirements and protect independent advocacy, not to dismantle the law.

The essay’s foreign-policy argument is also one-sided. The United States has bombed several countries on the travel-ban list. That fact is morally relevant, but it does not settle the immigration question. Some of those countries are failed or failing states where reliable vetting is difficult. Syria in 2017 is not Canada with different cuisine; it is a war zone involving ISIS, al-Qaeda affiliates, Assad’s regime, Iranian-backed militias, Russian intervention, Kurdish forces, rebels of varying character, and massive population displacement. A temporary pause to review procedures might be defensible if carefully drafted, religiously neutral, and attentive to lawful residents and allies. Trump’s order failed those tests in important ways. But the principle that unstable states pose vetting challenges is not bigotry.

Nor can U.S. military action be reduced to hostility toward Muslims. American forces have fought alongside Muslims, protected Muslims, and intervened in conflicts where Muslims were victims. One may debate the wisdom of Iraq, Libya, drone strikes, or support for Saudi Arabia. I have grave doubts about some of these policies. But the United States is not simply waging war on Islam. In Iraq and Syria, American power has been used against ISIS, an organization that enslaves, beheads, massacres, and terrorizes Muslim populations. In Afghanistan, the Taliban’s return would be catastrophic for many Afghans, especially women and dissidents. Foreign policy requires prudence, not slogans.

The lead essay’s treatment of Khizr Khan is revealing. It asks why a Muslim family’s sacrifice was required for public celebration. That is a fair question. No citizen should have to lose a son in war to be recognized as American. But Khan’s speech was not merely an example of conditional belonging. It was a dignified rebuke to a candidate who had attacked his community. Military sacrifice has always had a special place in civic argument because it dramatizes commitment to the common good. When a Muslim father of a fallen American soldier speaks, he does not validate a loyalty test; he refutes a slander. We should not let academic suspicion drain that moment of its moral force.

There is another danger in the “Islamophobia” frame as deployed here. Anti-Muslim prejudice is real and rising. Mosques have been vandalized, women in hijab harassed, children bullied, and politicians have made disgraceful generalizations. But if Islamophobia is defined so broadly that it includes nearly any effort to investigate jihadist ideology, designate Islamist organizations, or question foreign funding of religious institutions, the term will lose precision. Bigotry against Muslims must be fought. So must Islamist extremism. Treating these concerns as mutually exclusive helps the extremes: anti-Muslim demagogues on one side and Islamist apologists on the other.

A sound policy would begin with several commitments. First, no religious bans. Immigration rules should be based on nationality, state capacity, individual risk, and statutory authority, not on theology. Second, robust refugee screening with adequate resources, because refugees are both vulnerable and politically easy to scapegoat. Third, due process for watch lists and no-fly determinations. Fourth, aggressive investigation of genuine terrorist recruitment, financing, and plotting, under judicial oversight. Fifth, equal attention to non-jihadist terrorism, including white supremacist and anti-government violence. Sixth, partnership with Muslim communities that respects them as citizens, not as suspect populations.

Partnership is crucial. Many Muslim Americans have cooperated with law enforcement not because they accept a loyalty test but because they do not want their children recruited by fanatics or their mosques infiltrated by extremists. If government treats them as potential enemies, cooperation will collapse. If activists treat any cooperation as capitulation to Islamophobia, they will isolate communities that are trying to navigate real threats. The state must earn trust, and civil society must allow room for Muslims who see counterterrorism as partly their own concern.

Trump’s presidency heightens every risk. His rhetoric has been careless at best and inflammatory at worst. Advisers who speak of Islam as a civilizational enemy should not shape policy. Conservatives who care about religious liberty should say so plainly. But resisting Trump should not require embracing a theory in which American counterterrorism is primarily a racial project and the category of radical Islam is merely a mask for prejudice. The United States must be able to defend itself while remaining faithful to its constitutional principles.

The lead essay calls us to scrutinize power. That call is welcome. But scrutiny should produce reform, not paralysis; precision, not denial. Muslims should never be placed on trial for being Muslim. Radical movements that preach and practice murder must be. The hard work of a free society is to know the difference—and to build laws that can act on that difference without betraying it.
