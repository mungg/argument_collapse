"A rule-based, automatic amnesty system based solely on time is practically unworkable and morally inconsistent; instead, legalization should be handled through a structured, individualized adjudication process that acknowledges the necessity of discretion."

I want to focus on a feature of Carens' argument that he treats as a virtue but that I think is actually a weakness: the proposal that legalization should follow automatically from a fixed period of residence, with no discretionary evaluation of the individual migrant.

Carens defends this on two grounds. First, efficiency: individualized evaluation is costly. Second, anti-discrimination: discretionary evaluation tends to be applied in biased ways. Both points have some force. But they together produce a proposal that is, in practical terms, both rigid and naive about how immigration systems actually work.

Consider what "five to seven years of settled residence without criminal convictions" means as an administrable rule. To qualify, a migrant must prove (a) length of residence, (b) the absence of criminal convictions, and (c) presumably some kind of identity. Each of these is a non-trivial evidentiary question for a population that has spent the relevant time precisely trying to avoid documentation.

Length of residence: How does Miguel Sanchez prove he has been in the United States for five years? Carens acknowledges that proving residence "can be a problem" but gestures vaguely at French practice. In fact, the proof problem is substantial. The 1986 IRCA legalization required documentation of continuous residence and produced a vast industry of falsified documents — pay stubs, utility bills, affidavits — many of which were of questionable authenticity. Adjudicators had to make individual judgments about credibility, which is exactly the kind of discretionary evaluation Carens' framework supposedly avoids. The choice is not between rule-based and discretionary; it is between admitting the discretion explicitly or pretending it does not exist while exercising it implicitly.

Criminal convictions: Carens specifies "without any criminal convictions," which sounds clean but is not. American criminal law is vast and inconsistently enforced. A migrant might have a conviction for driving without a license — which, in the case of irregular migrants, is often a direct consequence of irregular status, since most states do not issue licenses to undocumented residents. Should this disqualify? A conviction for use of false documents to obtain employment — same issue. A misdemeanor conviction for marijuana possession from ten years ago — counts or not? A juvenile adjudication? An expunged conviction? Each of these questions requires judgment. The rule-based appearance of Carens' proposal conceals a great deal of necessary discretion.

Identity: How does the system know which Miguel Sanchez is in front of it? Multiple people use the same names, the same fragmentary documents, the same social security numbers. Establishing identity for purposes of legalization is itself a significant administrative project. Some kind of biometric registration is almost certainly required, which raises its own civil liberties and political questions.

These are not mere implementation details. They are the substance of any actual legalization program, and they shape what kind of program it can be. A program with strict documentation requirements will exclude many people whom Carens' moral argument says should be included, because they cannot produce the required evidence. A program with loose requirements will admit people whom the political community would not want to admit, and will be vulnerable to fraud that will undermine its political support. The 1986 program made the documentation requirements loose, which produced massive fraud (especially in the agricultural worker program), which contributed to political backlash that has made subsequent legalization much harder.

There is also a question about Carens' "no criminal convictions" criterion that goes deeper than administrability. He treats this as a relatively minor side condition, but it is doing a lot of moral work. Why should a five-year resident with a clean record qualify but a five-year resident with a single drunk-driving conviction not? Carens' main argument is about membership: the long-resident is a member, and removing her is disproportionate to the wrong of irregular entry. But the long-resident with a drunk-driving conviction is no less a member; the harm of removing her is no less great. The criminal-conviction criterion sneaks in a different normative principle — that legalization is conditional on good behavior — that Carens does not really argue for. He needs the criterion to make his proposal politically viable, but it sits uneasily with his moral framework.

I am not raising these issues to oppose legalization. I support it. I am raising them to push back against Carens' suggestion that a rule-based, time-only approach is somehow cleaner or more principled than a discretionary approach. The rule-based approach has its own problems: it conceals the discretion that is in fact being exercised; it produces fraud; it draws arbitrary lines (five years, not four; not seven) that are presented as principled; it relies on documentation requirements that exclude many of the people the moral argument is meant to protect.

A more honest framework would acknowledge that any legalization program will involve substantial discretionary judgment, that the discretion should be exercised under transparent and reviewable standards, and that the goal is not to eliminate discretion but to constrain and correct it. This is what most administrative agencies do in most domains. There is no reason to think immigration legalization can or should be different.

What I would propose instead is a framework that takes the substance of Carens' argument — the moral importance of accumulated ties, the disproportion between removal and the original wrong — and translates it into a structured set of considerations to be weighed by adjudicators, with appellate review, transparency, and case-by-case judgment. Length of residence would be one factor. Family ties would be another. Workforce participation, community integration, criminal record, age at entry, conditions in the country of origin — all of these would be factors. The decision would not be automatic, but it would be principled, and it would be defensible in a way that an arbitrary time-cutoff is not.

Carens worries that discretion produces discrimination. He is right that this is a risk. But the answer is to constrain discretion, not eliminate it. Administrative law has a substantial body of doctrine on how to do this: notice-and-comment rulemaking, standards of review, equal protection scrutiny, anti-arbitrariness norms. We know how to make discretionary systems more or less fair. We do not have to choose between brittle rules and unconstrained discretion.

Carens has the moral argument right. The institutional proposal is too simple. The case for amnesty is the case for a humane, structured, individualized adjudication of claims, not the case for an automatic rule. Getting the institutional design wrong will undermine the moral case in practice, no matter how clear it is in principle.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Establishing objective proof for length of residence, criminal history, and identity is a complex evidentiary task that inevitably requires the very discretionary judgments the author seeks to avoid.
  2. 02Strict documentation requirements in a rule-based system would unfairly exclude many migrants who lack formal records but possess the moral claims to membership the author defends.
  3. 03The 'no criminal convictions' criterion contradicts the author's core membership argument, as a criminal record does not diminish a person's social ties or the harm caused by their deportation.

  5. 05A more effective policy would weigh multiple factors—such as family ties, workforce participation, and age at entry—rather than relying on an arbitrary and brittle time-cutoff.
