"The primary obstacle to redirecting AI toward social benefit is the unprecedented concentration of monopoly power among a few tech giants, which necessitates aggressive antitrust enforcement rather than just policy or norm shifts."

Daron Acemoglu’s warning about AI-induced inequality and democratic decay lands with great force, and his call to redirect artificial intelligence is an essential starting point. However, his essay repeatedly dances around the central structural problem without fully confronting it: monopoly power. The current dystopian trajectory is not primarily the result of poorly designed tax incentives, unenlightened researcher norms, or absent democratic oversight, though all those matter. It is the direct consequence of a few corporations having achieved an unprecedented concentration of control over data, platforms, and AI talent. Until we break up the tech giants, all attempts at redirection will be cosmetic.

Acemoglu himself provides the evidence. He notes that Google, Facebook, Amazon, Microsoft, and their Chinese counterparts account for the vast majority of private AI investment and that their influence shapes academic curricula, researcher career paths, and the policy conversation in Washington. He describes a “revolving door” between universities and big tech. He laments that the market is not selecting the most socially beneficial technologies. But the market is not a neutral referee; it is an arena dominated by a handful of players who set the rules. When a few firms control the digital infrastructure, the data, and the capital, they inevitably channel innovation in directions that preserve and extend their dominance — namely, automation that reduces labor costs and surveillance that intensifies the data-extraction flywheel on which their business models depend.

Acemoglu’s proposed remedies, however, fall short of an antitrust reckoning. He calls for government funding to support human-complementing AI, changes in norms among researchers, and greater democratic oversight. But government funding, while valuable, will be captured or marginalized by the same corporations that already dominate AI. Big tech firms can outspend public initiatives, attract the best talent with astronomical salaries, and use their lobbying power to ensure that any publicly funded “ethical AI” remains safely confined to academic pilots and never threatens their core markets. Norms change, similarly, cannot survive when the material incentives all point toward working for, selling to, or emulating the dominant firms. A researcher who wants to build a privacy-preserving social-media alternative faces a near-insurmountable barrier because the network effects of Facebook and Google make competition on quality nearly impossible. The problem is not a lack of good ideas; it is a lack of market space for them to grow.

Even Acemoglu’s own historical reference to the breakup of early-twentieth-century conglomerates and the antitrust action against AT&T points directly to the missing piece. The postwar era’s competitive environment, which he credits with fueling a ferocious appetite for worker-productivity-enhancing technologies, was a direct product of antitrust enforcement. Breaking up Standard Oil and the railroad trusts, and later breaking up AT&T, did not destroy innovation; it unleashed it. The same logic applies to today’s AI landscape. Separating Amazon’s marketplace from its cloud-services division, unwinding Facebook’s acquisitions of Instagram and WhatsApp, and imposing structural separation on Google’s advertising and search businesses would create a genuinely competitive market for AI applications. New firms would then have the incentive and the opportunity to develop AI tools that serve users and workers rather than simply extracting value for a monopolistic platform.

A competitive market would also make it possible to enforce labor market policies that favor good jobs. As long as a small number of firms wield monopsony power over both product markets and labor, they can impose low wages and fragile employment arrangements regardless of what technology they deploy. Breaking their hold would allow the minimum wage, collective bargaining, and sectoral labor agreements to regain their bite, creating the mutual reinforcement between worker-friendly technology and worker-protecting institutions that Acemoglu so rightly celebrates.

On the surveillance front, antitrust is equally critical. The drive to collect ever more personal data is fueled by the network effects of social media and search. A competitive market in social networking, for example, would force companies to compete on privacy and user experience rather than simply extracting maximum data from a captive audience. The current push for “data portability” and “interoperability” — which Acemoglu does not mention — is a form of antitrust philosophy, aiming to reduce switching costs and break the stranglehold of incumbents. Strong data-portability mandates and public-interest digital platforms are complements to, not substitutes for, structural breakup.

Acemoglu concludes that “government policy, funding, and leadership are critical,” and he is right. But that leadership must be willing to use the heaviest tool in the regulatory toolbox: antitrust law. Without it, the redirection he envisions will remain a beautiful blueprint, drawn again and again while the corporations that own the drafting table continue building the dystopia he fears. The first act of democratic reassertion over AI must be to shatter the monopolies that have stolen our technological future.
