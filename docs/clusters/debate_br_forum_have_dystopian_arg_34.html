Diversified Gemini

"Instead of trying to redirect AI to create new labor tasks for humans, we should embrace automation as a means to liberate humanity from the necessity of work through a Universal Basic Income."

Daron Acemoglu correctly identifies the economic and political rot at the heart of our current technological paradigm, but his proposed solution—a return to the "good jobs" consensus of the mid-twentieth century—is both historically impossible and conceptually uninspired. Acemoglu wants to steer AI development toward creating "new tasks" so that humans can continue to labor productively and feel a sense of social purpose. This is a profound failure of imagination. If artificial intelligence possesses the capability to automate vast swaths of human toil, our goal should not be to artificially invent new ways for humans to work. Our goal should be to liberate humanity from the necessity of labor altogether. The future of AI doesn’t have to be dystopian, but avoiding dystopia means abandoning the fetishization of work.

The mid-century compact that Acemoglu idolizes was an aberration, a specific historical moment built on the ashes of World War II, massive industrial expansion, and an exclusionary social order. It was also an era characterized by what anthropologist David Graeber aptly called "bullshit jobs"—employment that is entirely pointless, yet kept intact by a societal belief that one must work to deserve a living. Acemoglu’s plea to redirect AI toward creating new tasks is essentially a plea to digitally engineer a new generation of bullshit jobs. Why should we use AI to create new "adaptive teaching tasks" or "integrated design tasks" just to keep people on the clock? The insistence that human dignity requires forty hours a week of wage labor is a capitalist construct, not a biological imperative.

We are currently witnessing the greatest productive capacity in human history. The algorithms powering supply chains, the robotic automation of warehouses, and the early stages of generative AI are capable of producing immense abundance. The problem is not the automation; it is the system of property rights and wealth distribution that demands people sell their labor to survive in a world where their labor is increasingly redundant. Acemoglu worries that building shared prosperity based predominantly on redistribution is a "fantasy." But what is truly fantastical is the belief that we can somehow force private companies, engaged in fierce global competition, to voluntarily adopt less efficient, more labor-intensive AI simply because a shifting "norm" suggests it is the right thing to do.

Instead of fighting a rearguard action to save the concept of the "job," we should be demanding a Universal Basic Income (UBI) funded by the taxation of automated capital and data extraction. Acemoglu dismisses UBI as an insufficient supporting pillar, arguing that jobs provide vital social purpose. This is a patronizing view of human nature. People derive purpose from their families, their communities, their art, their civic engagement, and their passions. The idea that meaning is exclusively conferred by a manager in a workplace is a modern tragedy. If AI can automate the production of our basic needs, a robust UBI would decouple survival from wage labor, granting millions the freedom to find their own purpose.

Furthermore, Acemoglu’s focus on the labor market blinds him to the broader emancipatory potential of AI. He warns of AI's threat to democracy via surveillance and polarization, which is a very real danger. But true democratic participation requires time—time that is currently stolen by the exhaustion of the modern workday. A society where AI handles the drudgery and humans are supported by UBI would be a society with the civic bandwidth to engage in meaningful democratic deliberation.

The COVID-19 pandemic has already ruptured the traditional boundaries of work, revealing to millions the arbitrary nature of the daily grind. As we stand in May 2021, looking at an economy forever changed by lockdowns and remote work, we should not be asking how to use technology to force people back into the labor market. We should be asking how to use technology to permanently free them from it. Let AI automate the economy; let humans inherit the resulting abundance.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The mid-twentieth-century 'good jobs' consensus was a historical aberration that relied on the creation of 'bullshit jobs' and should not be the model for our future.

  3. 03It is unrealistic to expect private companies in a competitive global market to voluntarily adopt less efficient, labor-intensive AI technologies based on social norms.
  4. 04A Universal Basic Income funded by taxes on automated capital is the only viable way to distribute the abundance created by AI.
  5. 05Decoupling survival from labor would provide citizens with the necessary time and 'civic bandwidth' to engage in meaningful democratic deliberation.
