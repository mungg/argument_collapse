"Summer reading should be a balanced pursuit of both challenging and pleasurable books rather than a performative exercise in aspirational virtue."

Let us be honest: the true summer reading list is often a fantasy of the self we wish to be by Labor Day. We pack “Middlemarch,” “The Magic Mountain,” a biography of Lincoln and perhaps a slim volume of poetry, then spend the weekend reading restaurant reviews and the first thirty pages of a thriller.

I do not say this to scold. Aspirational reading has its charms. Books are not only objects we consume; they are signals we send to ourselves. The heavy novel on the bedside table says: I am still a person with an interior life. I may answer email at midnight and know too much about cable news, but I have not surrendered completely.

Still, the honest list is better than the virtuous one. Bring one book that stretches you and one that catches you. The stretching book may be a classic, a work in translation, a history of a place you barely understand. The catching book should be shamelessly readable, the sort of thing that makes you ignore the chips and the mosquitoes. Alternate them. Reward the difficult chapters with the fast ones; let the fast ones teach you something about momentum.

The division between serious and pleasurable books is exaggerated anyway. The best books tend to be both. Jane Austen is a comic novelist. “The Odyssey” is an adventure story. “Beloved” is devastating, but also possessed by narrative force. Even Proust, allegedly the patron saint of unread books, is full of gossip, jealousy, comedy and social snobbery.

Summer is not a test. It is an opening. The question is not whether the reading is easy, but whether the reader feels free.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Aspirational reading lists serve as a psychological signal that we still possess an interior life despite the distractions of modern technology.
  2. 02Readers should alternate between 'stretching' books that challenge them and 'catching' books that provide effortless engagement.
  3. 03The perceived dichotomy between serious literature and pleasurable reading is false, as many classics are inherently entertaining and driven by narrative force.
  4. 04The ultimate goal of summer reading is to experience a sense of intellectual freedom rather than to pass a test of academic rigor.
