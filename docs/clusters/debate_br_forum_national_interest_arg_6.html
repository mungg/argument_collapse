"The United States should abandon the pursuit of global primacy in favor of a grand strategy rooted in demilitarization, global justice, and adherence to international law."

Stephen Walt’s essay is a critique of American arrogance written from inside the architecture of American dominance. For that reason it is both useful and insufficient. He sees clearly that preventive war, permanent garrisons, unconditional alliances, and imperial dreams are damaging the United States. But his goal is to preserve American primacy “for as long as possible.” That is precisely the premise that should be questioned. The problem is not merely that the United States has misused primacy. The problem is primacy itself.

A world organized around one state’s overwhelming military superiority is inherently unstable and undemocratic. Even when the hegemon behaves politely, others know that it can choose not to. They know that international rules are applied unevenly. They know that Washington can invoke sovereignty in one case and humanitarian necessity in another, nonproliferation in one case and nuclear privilege in another, democracy in one case and strategic partnership with dictatorship in another. Walt wants America to reassure others that it will use power wisely. But why should the world depend on American wisdom? A decent international order should not require trust in the self-restraint of any single empire, even a liberal one.

Walt’s realism usefully strips away some illusions. He admits that America’s support for autocrats, its one-sided Middle East policy, and its war in Iraq have fueled anti-Americanism. He recognizes that Iraq had nothing to do with September 11 and that the occupation has become a costly disaster. He rejects the fantasy that democracy can be delivered by invasion. These are important truths, especially in a political climate where dissent has too often been equated with weakness.

But offshore balancing is not a peace strategy. It is an imperial management strategy with a lighter footprint. The United States would still decide which regions matter, which balances must be preserved, which states may dominate, which weapons are permissible, and when intervention is “absolutely necessary.” It would intervene from over the horizon rather than from permanent bases, but the hierarchy remains. To people on the receiving end of cruise missiles or covert operations, the distinction may not feel morally profound.

Consider the Middle East. Walt wants the United States to return to its earlier role as offshore balancer in the Persian Gulf. But that earlier role was hardly benign. It included support for the Shah of Iran, whose secret police helped create the resentments that exploded in 1979. It included tilting toward Saddam Hussein during the Iran-Iraq War, even as Iraq used chemical weapons. It included deep ties to Saudi Arabia and other monarchies that deny their citizens basic rights. It included sanctions on Iraq in the 1990s that inflicted suffering on civilians while strengthening the regime’s control. If this is the golden age of balancing, we should be wary of nostalgia.

The deeper issue is oil. The United States has treated the Persian Gulf as a strategic prize because its economy and the global economy depend on fossil fuels. A truly forward-looking grand strategy would reduce that dependence radically. Instead of spending hundreds of billions on wars, bases, and military technologies to police energy supplies, the United States should invest in conservation, public transportation, renewable energy, and international climate cooperation. Walt mentions Kyoto as a missed opportunity for legitimacy, but climate policy is not merely public diplomacy. It is security policy. Oil dependence entangles America with repressive regimes, funds extremism, and threatens the planet. The national interest should be redefined accordingly.

On nuclear weapons, Walt comes closer to challenging hierarchy. His proposed grand bargain—deep U.S. reductions, no new nuclear weapons, stronger nonproliferation, and security assurances—is sensible. But why stop at a few hundred warheads as a permanent entitlement? If nuclear weapons are too dangerous for others, they are too dangerous for us as well. The United States cannot credibly lead a nonproliferation regime while insisting that its own security requires the ability to annihilate cities. The long-term goal should be abolition, pursued carefully and multilaterally, not indefinite American nuclear privilege.

The same double standard appears in Walt’s discussion of legitimacy. He wants America to be seen as legitimate so that it can maintain primacy. But legitimacy should arise from democratic consent, international law, and equal rules, not from better messaging. Public diplomacy cannot solve the fact that the United States reserves rights it denies to others. It refuses the International Criminal Court while demanding accountability abroad. It lectures on human rights while sending detainees into legal black holes. It promotes free trade while protecting favored industries and imposing harsh intellectual-property rules on poor countries. The world is not fooled because it lacks Arabic-language websites. It is angry because it sees hypocrisy.

Walt is right that the Israeli–Palestinian conflict is central to America’s loss of standing in the Arab and Islamic world. Here his willingness to confront domestic taboos is admirable. The United States should stop financing occupation and settlement expansion. It should insist on equal political rights for Palestinians, whether through a viable two-state settlement or, if that becomes impossible, through a democratic framework in which all people under effective Israeli control have rights. But even here, Walt frames the issue mainly as a cost to American security. Palestinians deserve freedom not because their oppression helps recruit terrorists, but because they are human beings.

An alternative grand strategy would begin not with primacy but with demilitarization and global justice. Close unnecessary overseas bases. End the occupation of Iraq as rapidly and responsibly as possible, under international auspices rather than permanent American control. Stop supporting dictatorships because they are “moderate.” Redirect military spending toward public health, energy transition, education, and poverty reduction. Strengthen international law even when it constrains Washington. Support debt relief, fairer trade rules, and development without ideological coercion. Treat terrorism as a grave criminal and political problem, not as a blank check for war.

Critics will call this naïve. They will say that power abhors a vacuum and that if America steps back, worse powers will step forward. But this assumes that the only alternative to U.S. dominance is chaos or tyranny. In reality, many countries would welcome a United States that cooperates rather than commands. Europe, India, Brazil, South Africa, Japan, and others can share responsibility in a more plural order. Regional organizations can play larger roles. The United Nations can be strengthened. None of this will be easy, but neither is the permanent management of global resentment.

The United States should defend itself against real threats. Al Qaeda murdered thousands and must be dismantled. Nuclear materials must be secured. Police, intelligence, financial tracking, and carefully targeted force all have roles. But war should be exceptional, not organizing principle. Security built on domination breeds insecurity for others, and eventually for oneself.

Walt asks how America can preserve primacy by making it more acceptable. The better question is how America can help build a world that no longer depends on primacy. A republic should not aspire to be an empire, even an offshore one. It should aspire to be a powerful democracy among other democracies, bound by the same rules it asks others to obey. The path away from Iraq’s folly does not lead back to balance-of-power management. It leads toward equality, law, and a humbler understanding of what security means.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A world organized around the military superiority of a single state is inherently unstable, undemocratic, and prone to hypocritical double standards.
  2. 02Offshore balancing is merely a form of imperial management that maintains an unjust global hierarchy and relies on a history of supporting repressive regimes.
  3. 03True national security requires a radical reduction in oil dependence to end entanglements with dictatorships and address the existential threat of climate change.
  4. 04The United States cannot credibly lead nonproliferation efforts while maintaining its own nuclear privilege; it should instead pursue the long-term goal of global abolition.
  5. 05International legitimacy must be earned through equal application of international law and democratic consent rather than improved public diplomacy or 'messaging.'
  6. 06Foreign policy should prioritize human rights and global equality—such as Palestinian freedom—as moral imperatives rather than as mere tools for enhancing American security.
  7. 07The U.S. should redirect its massive military budget toward public health, education, and poverty reduction while transitioning to a pluralistic world order where responsibility is shared among many nations.
