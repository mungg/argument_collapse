"The United States must maintain a limited, realistic military presence in Afghanistan to prevent the reestablishment of terrorist sanctuaries, rather than abandoning the mission due to the current flaws of the Afghan government and security forces."

Nir Rosen’s essay arrives at a moment of high national ambivalence about the Afghan war, and it powerfully reinforces the case for pulling back. But the essay is oddly coy about the real alternative it implies. It mocks the pretensions of counterinsurgency, exposes the corruption of the Afghan government, and suggests that the al Qaeda threat is overblown, yet it never quite says what the United States should actually do tomorrow. That evasion is telling, because the strategic logic that originally took us to Afghanistan—and that keeps us there—remains compelling, even after eight frustrating years. We are in Afghanistan not to build a Scandinavian-style social democracy, but to prevent the reestablishment of a terrorist sanctuary in a region vital to global security. That mission is still necessary, and it remains achievable if we pursue it with realism and limits.

Rosen’s critique is strongest when it describes the yawning gap between the rhetoric of population-centric COIN and the reality of undermanned, under-resourced operations like the one he embedded with. He is right that the Afghan police are a mess, that the Karzai government is grotesquely corrupt, and that the Taliban are a tenacious and adaptive enemy. Where he goes wrong is in treating these facts as permanent and decisive. The history of irregular warfare is, in large part, the history of external powers learning to work with flawed local partners under terrible conditions. The early years of the U.S. involvement in Vietnam saw similar absurdities and worse, but by the time of the 1968 Tet Offensive, the South Vietnamese army had improved markedly and was fighting hard—the U.S. still lost the war for political reasons, not because the local forces were inherently incapable. In El Salvador in the 1980s, a similarly corrupt and brutal government eventually reformed its military and held off a guerrilla insurgency, with sustained but limited American support. The point is not that Afghanistan is Vietnam or El Salvador, but that the narrative of inevitable failure is a convenient intellectual alibi, not a historical law.

The more original part of Rosen’s essay is his direct challenge to the threat assessment that undergirds the whole war. He argues that al Qaeda is not a grave danger to a “vigilant superpower,” that the Taliban have no global jihadist ambitions, and that a Taliban government in Kabul would not necessarily host al Qaeda camps. This is a breathtaking exercise in wishful thinking. On September 11, 2001, we learned that a small group of men with basic flight training and a safe haven could kill thousands of Americans in a single morning. In the years since, al Qaeda has attempted mass-casualty attacks using commercial aircraft, sought chemical, biological, and radiological weapons, and inspired a global network of affiliates from London to Jakarta. The idea that a few hundred extremists cannot pose a grave danger is refuted by the entire course of modern counterterrorism. And while the Afghan Taliban are indeed nationalistic in their outlook, their leadership has repeatedly refused to break with al Qaeda even when doing so would have relieved international pressure. The alliance is not merely tactical; it is forged in a shared Islamist ideology and a shared history of struggle. If the Taliban regain control of substantial Afghan territory, al Qaeda will surely return, and this time they will be far more sophisticated in concealing their presence. Drones and occasional special forces raids are no substitute for denying them a territorial base.

The domestic political argument is also important. President Obama ran on a platform that called Afghanistan the “war of necessity,” in contrast to Iraq. He appointed General McChrystal precisely to shift the strategy in a more population-focused direction, and in December 2009 he announced a surge of forces coupled with a commitment to begin transitioning to Afghan responsibility in mid-2011. Critics like Rosen see the time-limited commitment as a contradictory signal, but it is better understood as a necessary compromise between the demands of the military and the patience of the American people. An open-ended occupation is politically unsustainable, but a complete withdrawal would be a strategic catastrophe. The middle path—surge, then transition—is the only politically viable way to keep the core mission on track.

What that mission should not be is an attempt to build a modern centralized state in a country that has never had one. Here Rosen is right to mock the maximalist rhetoric that sometimes escapes from official Washington. The goal in Afghanistan need not be the construction of something from nothing; it can be more modest: to build an Afghan state that is strong enough to hold the major cities, control the key highways, and prevent the return of international terrorist camps, while allowing a great deal of local autonomy in the countryside. That is roughly the equilibrium that existed in Afghanistan for most of the twentieth century before the Soviet invasion shattered it. The Taliban do not control every valley because the people love them; they control them because the government is absent or predatory. A focused COIN campaign that prioritizes key population centers and lines of communication, combined with a political strategy that draws in elements of the insurgency willing to reconcile, could achieve that limited but essential goal.

Rosen’s essay is an invaluable record of the war’s ugly surface. But its implied conclusion—that we should despair and cut our losses—is unsupported by his own evidence. The question is not whether the war is going badly; it is whether we can recover. The answer, with the right strategy and sustained commitment, is yes. We owe it to those who have died, and to our own future security, to try.
