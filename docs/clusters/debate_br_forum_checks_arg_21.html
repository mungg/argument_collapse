"Meaningful democratic renewal requires the painstaking work of civic repair and the reconstruction of a deliberative public sphere rather than just the removal of institutional veto points."

Lisa L. Miller’s essay rightly identifies a profound legitimacy crisis in American governance. Her catalog of thwarted popular policies—from universal health care to paid leave—will resonate with anyone who has watched Congress endlessly dither while ordinary people suffer. Yet there is a ghost haunting her entire analysis, a profound absence she never fully addresses: the condition of the American public itself. For all her faith in mass mobilization and majoritarian responsiveness, she says remarkably little about the citizens who would supposedly power this democratic renewal. Institutions are not autonomous machines; they are inhabited and operated by human beings, and those human beings have, over recent decades, become less civically competent, less engaged in local deliberation, and more susceptible to demagoguery and misinformation. Any serious plan to revitalize American democracy must begin not with institutional redesign, but with the painstaking work of civic repair.

Miller treats the public largely as a reservoir of progressive preferences waiting to be unblocked by elite veto points. She points to polls showing support for minimum wage increases, background checks, and equal treatment as evidence that the system is thwarting a naturally majoritarian public. But this view flattens a far messier reality. Public opinion is often shallow, contradictory, and highly malleable. The same polls that show support for universal health care also show significant resistance to the tax increases and regulatory changes it would require. Americans are simultaneously in favor of smaller government and more services, hostile to Congress but fond of their own representative. This is not an electorate ready to take the reins of power; it is a distracted, polarized, and often ill-informed citizenry that has been systematically deprived of the spaces and skills necessary for genuine democratic deliberation.

That deprivation is not accidental. It is the result of a decades-long decay of civil society—the civic associations, unions, churches, community groups, and local media that once nurtured democratic habits and provided platforms for cross-class, cross-partisan discussion. Robert Putnam documented this erosion a quarter century ago, and the trends have only accelerated. Social media has not filled the void; it has intensified polarization, shortened attention spans, and rewarded outrage over reason. The isolation that breeds extremism is not just economic; it is social and cognitive. In such a context, simply removing veto points so that majority sentiment can be translated more efficiently into law is a recipe for mob rule, not enlightened democracy. The mob, after all, is not just a myth invented by elite propaganda; it is a real possibility in any polity where the public sphere has collapsed.

Miller’s solution—constitutional amendments and mass mobilization—is thus built on sand. She cites historical movements like abolitionism and the civil rights struggle as evidence that mass politics can overcome institutional obstacles. But those movements were successful precisely because they combined mass mobilization with deep, long-term investment in civic education, moral persuasion, and institution-building within communities. They changed not only laws but hearts and minds, creating a moral consensus that made institutional reform durable. The work of Freedom Summer was as much about teaching literacy and citizenship as it was about registering voters. The labor movement built halls, newspapers, and cultural institutions alongside picket lines. Today’s progressives, by and large, lack such infrastructure. They have mastered Twitter and campaign analytics, but they have not built the dense, face-to-face networks that can sustain democratic engagement over the long haul.

This is not an argument for quietism or pure gradualism. The threat of authoritarianism is real, and Miller is right to call for resistance. But the form of resistance matters. A premature push for constitutional overhaul, especially in the absence of a revitalized civic culture, risks unleashing a backlash that could make things worse. A national referendum on, say, abolishing the Electoral College would almost certainly be seized upon by populist demagogues on both left and right to inflame regional divisions and attack the legitimacy of any outcome they dislike. The constitutional amendment process itself, as Miller acknowledges, is controlled by the very institutions she wants to reform. What she fails to reckon with is that these institutions are embedded in a society that has lost much of its capacity for reasoned, collective self-rule.

The most promising path, then, runs through the kind of local, deliberative experiments that Miller mentions only in passing: citizens’ assemblies, participatory budgeting, community-led planning. But these should not be seen merely as a means to a national constitutional end. They should be the end itself—the gradual reconstruction of democratic culture from the ground up. Imagine, for instance, a decade-long national commitment to expanding civic education in schools, supporting local journalism with public funds, and creating a network of local assemblies where ordinary citizens, chosen by lottery and given time, resources, and expert testimony, can deliberate on public issues and feed their conclusions into the formal legislative process. This is not a substitute for institutional reform; it is the precondition for making any such reform meaningful and durable.

Miller is correct that the checks and balances narrative serves elite interests by hiding the reality of minority veto power. But changing the narrative without changing the citizenry will only substitute one elite-serving myth for another. The myth of the wise, restrained Constitution might be replaced by the myth of the inherently virtuous majority, ready to govern if only the procedural obstacles were cleared. Both myths obscure the hard truth that democracy requires constant, unglamorous work—education, deliberation, compromise, trust—from millions of ordinary people, not just the periodic exercise of a vote or the occasional protest march. Our institutions are indeed broken, but they reflect a broken public sphere. We need to fix both, simultaneously and patiently, or we will fix neither.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The American public currently lacks the civic competence, social infrastructure, and deliberative habits necessary to effectively exercise majoritarian power.
  2. 02Public opinion is often too shallow, contradictory, and susceptible to demagoguery to serve as a stable foundation for direct institutional overhaul.
  3. 03The historical movements cited as models for mass mobilization succeeded because they were rooted in deep civic education and long-term community institution-building that today's movements lack.
  4. 04Removing constitutional checks without first revitalizing civic culture risks replacing elite dysfunction with mob rule and further polarizing the nation.
  5. 05Democratic reform should prioritize local, deliberative experiments like citizens' assemblies to rebuild democratic culture from the ground up as a precondition for national institutional change.
