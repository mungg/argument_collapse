"Reducing jail populations by ending wealth-based detention would increase public safety by allowing resources to be redirected toward proactive crime prevention."

The debate over jail populations often centers on the false premise that our current system is designed to maximize public safety. In reality, the American jail system functions primarily as a debt collection mechanism and a tool for the criminalization of poverty. We would absolutely be safer if fewer people were jailed, because ending the wealth-based detention system would allow us to reallocate immense resources toward actual crime prevention.

Currently, the primary determinant of whether a legally innocent person stays in jail before trial is not their risk to the community, but the size of their bank account. Under the commercial cash bail system, a wealthy defendant accused of a serious, violent crime can purchase their freedom and walk the streets. Meanwhile, a poor defendant accused of shoplifting or a minor drug offense is forced to languish in a cell for weeks or months. This is a profound miscarriage of justice that has nothing to do with keeping the public safe.

The financial toll of this system is staggering. Local governments spend billions of dollars every year to house, feed, and guard nonviolent individuals who simply cannot afford bail. These are funds that could be invested in public schools, after-school programs, job training, and community-based violence interruption initiatives—investments that have been proven to reduce crime over the long term.

Furthermore, the pressure of pre-trial detention frequently coerces innocent people into accepting plea bargains just so they can go home. This undermines the integrity of our judicial system and burdens people with criminal records that limit their future employment prospects, thereby perpetuating the cycle of poverty and crime.

We can safely reduce the use of jails by abolishing the cash bail system entirely. By moving to a system based on individualized risk assessments rather than wealth, we can ensure that only those who pose a demonstrable flight risk or a genuine threat to physical safety are detained. Emptying jails of the poor makes the system fairer and leaves us all safer.

  4. 04Replacing cash bail with individualized risk assessments would ensure that detention is reserved only for those who pose a genuine threat to public safety.
