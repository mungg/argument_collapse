"Trials for highly publicized crimes should remain in the district where the offense occurred unless it is demonstrably proven that an impartial jury cannot be seated through rigorous screening."

Courts should be cautious before moving a trial simply because a crime horrified a community. If that were the rule, the most serious offenses would routinely be tried far from where they occurred, and the constitutional promise of a jury drawn from the state and district would be weakened precisely when public accountability matters most.

The Boston Marathon bombing received intense publicity, but publicity is not the same as prejudice. By January 2015, any major American city would contain people who had seen the photographs, watched the manhunt, and heard the allegations. Moving the trial to New York, Chicago, Washington or Philadelphia would not place it before citizens who knew nothing about the case. It would simply trade one pool of informed jurors for another.

The proper tool is searching voir dire. Judges can question prospective jurors about what they have read, what opinions they have formed, whether they know victims or first responders, whether they attended memorial events, and whether they can follow instructions on guilt and punishment. Jurors who cannot do so must be excused. That process is imperfect, but it is more precise than assuming that millions of Massachusetts residents are incapable of fairness.

The Supreme Court has set a high bar for presumed prejudice, and for good reason. In cases like Skilling, the Court emphasized the size and diversity of the jury pool, the passage of time, and the ability of careful questioning to identify bias. Massachusetts is not a small town dominated by a single narrative. The federal district can draw from a broad population, not merely from spectators at the finish line.

A defendant charged with a notorious crime is still entitled to due process, not to anonymity. The question is not whether jurors have heard of the bombing. Of course they have. The question is whether twelve can be found who will decide the case on evidence and law. Unless that effort demonstrably fails, the trial should remain where the crime occurred.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Moving a trial to a different city is ineffective because modern media ensures that any major jury pool will be equally informed about notorious cases.
  2. 02The constitutional right to a jury from the local district should be upheld to ensure public accountability in the community most affected by the crime.
  3. 03A thorough voir dire process is a more precise tool for identifying individual bias than the broad assumption that an entire regional population is prejudiced.
  4. 04Large and diverse jury pools, such as the federal district of Massachusetts, provide a sufficient variety of perspectives to overcome localized trauma.
  5. 05Due process guarantees a defendant a fair trial based on evidence and law, but it does not guarantee a jury that is entirely ignorant of the crime.

"Courts should replace impressionistic venue debates with a structured, evidence-based framework to determine if a highly publicized trial must be moved to ensure a fair jury."

The usual debate over venue is too impressionistic. One side points to newspaper headlines and civic trauma; the other invokes the magic of voir dire. Courts need a more structured method for deciding when local juries will not do.

A sensible standard would examine at least five factors. First, the size and diversity of the jury pool: a sensational crime in a small community poses a different problem from one in a large metropolitan district. Second, the nature of the publicity: factual reporting is less dangerous than inflammatory or officially endorsed claims of guilt. Third, the role of the crime in local identity: an attack on a courthouse, school, church or civic celebration may implicate jurors personally even if they were not direct victims. Fourth, the passage of time and whether public ceremonies have kept the event fresh. Fifth, the stakes of the proceeding, especially whether the death penalty is sought.

Applied to the Tsarnaev case, these factors cut in mixed directions but create a serious argument for transfer. Massachusetts offers a large jury pool, and national coverage means no venue would be pristine. Yet the Marathon is uniquely local, the bombing and manhunt touched the region broadly, and the prospective penalty makes the margin for error very small. A court need not conclude that Bostonians are vengeful or irrational to recognize that the community was a participant in the trauma.

The answer should not be automatic transfer, however. Before ordering a move, the judge should require evidence: public opinion surveys, media analysis and an initial round of juror questionnaires. If the responses show that large numbers of qualified jurors have fixed opinions, personal connections to the event, or difficulty considering the full range of punishments, then the case should be moved. If not, it can remain.

Venue decisions should be transparent and evidence-based, not symbolic. The public should be able to see why the court trusted a local jury or why it concluded that distance was necessary. That approach would protect both the defendant’s rights and the community’s confidence in the verdict.

  2. 02The Tsarnaev case presents a strong argument for transfer because the Boston Marathon is a uniquely local event and the death penalty stakes leave a very small margin for error.
  3. 03Judges should require empirical evidence, such as public opinion surveys and juror questionnaires, rather than making automatic transfer decisions.
  4. 04Transparent and evidence-based venue decisions protect both the defendant's constitutional rights and the public's confidence in the judicial verdict.

"Criminal trials should remain in the community where the crime occurred unless it is proven through the jury selection process that an impartial local jury cannot be seated."

There is an important democratic reason not to move a criminal trial too readily: crimes occur in communities, and communities have a legitimate role in the administration of justice. A local jury is not an accident of geography. It reflects the idea that the people, not only legal professionals, judge whether the government has proved its case.

That principle should apply even in painful cases. The Boston Marathon bombing wounded Boston, Cambridge, Watertown and the surrounding region. But to say that the community was wounded is not to say that it is incapable of fairness. Citizens are often asked to set aside anger, sympathy and fear when serving on juries. They do it in murder cases, police shooting cases, child abuse cases and gang cases. The law cannot operate on the assumption that emotional exposure makes civic judgment impossible.

Indeed, removing a trial can carry its own message: that local citizens are too damaged, too passionate or too provincial to honor the Constitution. That is a serious claim, and it should require more than notoriety. The better presumption is that people can rise to the role, provided the court gives them careful instructions and excludes those with direct connections or fixed views.

The victims also matter, though not because they are entitled to a conviction. They are entitled to a public process that is accessible and intelligible. Moving a trial hundreds of miles away imposes burdens on survivors, families, witnesses and local residents who have followed the case from the beginning. Justice should not be converted into a distant proceeding unless fairness truly demands it.

The line should be drawn where evidence shows that an impartial jury cannot be seated after serious effort. If voir dire reveals pervasive disqualification, then transfer becomes necessary. But courts should not presume failure before testing the pool. The legitimacy of a verdict depends on fairness to the accused, but also on public confidence that the community can administer justice rather than merely suffer harm. In Boston, that confidence should not be abandoned lightly.

  4. 04Relocating a trial creates an undue burden on victims, witnesses, and families who have a right to an accessible and intelligible public process.
  5. 05The legitimacy of a verdict is strengthened when the affected community demonstrates it can administer justice fairly rather than being treated as merely a collection of victims.

"Courts should prioritize rigorous jury screening and procedural safeguards within the local district before resorting to a change of venue in high-profile cases."

The venue debate should begin with a humbling fact: in modern mass-media cases, there may be no untainted venue. The Boston Marathon bombing was not a local news story. It was broadcast nationally and internationally, accompanied by dramatic images, days of speculation, a citywide manhunt and continuing discussion of terrorism. Moving the trial outside Massachusetts might change the emotional temperature, but it would not produce jurors who know nothing or have no opinions.

That does not mean venue is irrelevant. Local exposure can be different in kind. A person in Boston may have stood near the finish line in prior years, known someone running, sheltered during the lockdown, or passed memorials every day. Those experiences can shape judgment in ways a television viewer’s knowledge does not. But the law needs remedies more tailored than simply packing the case off to another district.

Judges should use layered safeguards. Detailed written questionnaires can identify personal connections, media consumption and preliminary views without forcing jurors to confess bias in open court. Individual voir dire can probe sensitive issues. The court can grant additional peremptory challenges, sequester jurors if necessary, instruct them repeatedly about outside information, and select from a broad geographic cross-section within the district. If those tools fail, then the case should be moved. But transfer should come after diagnosis, not as an act of ritual purification.

Courts also should avoid the fiction that ignorance is the measure of impartiality. A juror who has heard of the case but understands the presumption of innocence may be better than a juror who claims total unfamiliarity in an implausible way. The central question is not prior knowledge; it is open-mindedness and obedience to the law.

In Tsarnaev’s case, the argument for moving the trial is substantial because the bombing struck at a civic symbol and because the government seeks death. Still, the wisest rule is sequential: try first to seat an impartial jury through rigorous screening; if the process shows that local trauma overwhelms impartiality, transfer then. Fairness requires vigilance, not automatic exile.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01In the modern mass-media era, moving a trial is unlikely to find a venue where jurors have no prior knowledge or opinions of a major crime.
  2. 02While local exposure can create a unique emotional impact, judges can effectively mitigate bias through detailed questionnaires, individual voir dire, and additional peremptory challenges.
  3. 03Impartiality should be measured by a juror's open-mindedness and obedience to the law rather than a total lack of prior knowledge about the case.
  4. 04A change of venue should only be granted as a last resort after a sequential process proves that local trauma has made seating an impartial jury impossible.

"High-profile criminal trials should remain in the district where the crime occurred unless jury selection proves it is impossible to empanel an impartial jury."

The presumption ought to favor trying defendants where the crime occurred. The Sixth Amendment guarantees trial "by an impartial jury of the State and district wherein the crime shall have been committed," and this language is not accidental. Local juries embody the community's stake in seeing justice done — and seeing it done in public, in the very place wounded by the offense. Moving high-profile trials elsewhere risks treating the affected community as too emotional or too parochial to render justice, which is itself a kind of insult.

The Tsarnaev case is a clean test of this principle. Yes, Boston was traumatized. But "traumatized" is not the legal standard. The question is whether twelve jurors can be found who will decide the case on the evidence presented in court. Eastern Massachusetts has about five million residents. The notion that not a dozen of them can set aside what they've read and follow a judge's instructions is implausible — and insulting to the millions of citizens who serve faithfully on juries every year in cases they know something about.

We should reserve venue changes for the rare case in which voir dire actually fails to produce a workable jury pool. The Oklahoma City bombing trial of Timothy McVeigh was moved to Denver, and one can debate whether that was necessary; but at least the decision came after careful examination. In modern America, where saturation media coverage extends to every corner of the country, the premise that moving a trial 200 miles meaningfully reduces juror exposure is doubtful at best. Anyone in Springfield, Hartford, or Albany who owns a television knows about the marathon bombing.

Judges should decide venue questions by looking at the actual results of jury selection, not at pretrial assumptions about community feeling. If qualified jurors can be empaneled, the trial belongs at home. Boston has earned the right to render this verdict.

"Courts should adopt a staged, evidence-based approach that attempts to seat a local jury through rigorous voir dire before considering a change of venue as a last resort."

Courts should stop treating change of venue as a binary, all-or-nothing decision. The proper approach is a staged, evidence-based process that tests whether a fair jury can be seated before assuming it can’t. In Tsarnaev’s case, the trial judge took exactly this route: he ordered and oversaw a meticulous, months-long jury selection process in Boston, using detailed questionnaires and individual questioning. Only if that process had demonstrably failed to produce an impartial panel would a move have been necessary.

This “try first, move if needed” method respects both the defendant’s right to an unbiased jury and the community’s profound interest in hosting the trial. It avoids reflexive relocations based on pretrial publicity alone. Modern high-profile cases always generate massive coverage, but empirical research shows that exposure to news does not automatically translate into an inability to render a fair verdict. What matters is the depth of that exposure, the juror’s ability to set aside preconceptions, and the effectiveness of the voir dire. A local population is not a monolith; within it are people who have avoided the saturation coverage, who can distinguish between media narratives and courtroom evidence, and who take their oath seriously.

Of course, there are extreme cases where the community’s trauma is so pervasive—think of a small town after a horrific school shooting—that attempting voir dire locally would be futile and retraumatizing. But Boston is a large, diverse metropolitan area. The marathon bombing affected it deeply, but it also contains millions of people with varying degrees of connection to the event. The court’s refusal to move the trial before jury selection even began was not a denial of Tsarnaev’s rights; it was a wise decision to let the actual evidence of bias, not fearful speculation, drive the outcome. Other courts confronting future cases should adopt the same orderly, case-specific approach: start locally, use extraordinary voir dire tools, and change venue only as a last resort when a fair jury proves unattainable.

"In the digital age, courts should prioritize rigorous jury selection and trial management over changing the trial's physical location, as national media saturation has made geographic venue changes largely obsolete."

In the digital age, the change of venue for high-profile crimes is becoming a legal relic. The internet, national cable news, and social media have dissolved the boundaries that once made a different physical location mean a different informational environment. When everyone with a smartphone can follow the manhunt, watch the same cellphone footage of a pressure-cooker bomb, and read the same criminal complaint on Twitter, there is no truly untainted jury pool anywhere in the country. Moving the Tsarnaev trial from Boston to, say, Denver would simply shift the proceedings to a city whose residents experienced the same saturation coverage from a slightly greater physical distance.

Given this reality, the focus of courts should shift from geography to the tools of jury selection and trial management. Extensive questionnaires, individual voir dire, vigorous use of challenges for cause, and careful instructions from the judge are far more important than the zip code of the courthouse. If a juror in Boston can honestly say they can weigh the evidence impartially, they are no more suspect than a juror in Ohio who has seen the same CNN segments. Sequestration of the jury during the trial—already likely in a case of this magnitude—further insulates them from local pressures.

Moreover, the alternative of venue-shopping offers no clean solution. Jurors in a distant city are not blank slates; they may carry their own biases about Boston, terrorism, or Islam. The Sixth Amendment does not demand a perfect jury, only an imperfect one that can render a verdict based on the evidence. In a nationally notorious case, where the crime is known from coast to coast, the best—and perhaps only—defense of impartiality lies in the rigor of the selection process, not in a fruitless search for some virgin territory of ignorance. The Tsarnaev court recognized this, and future courts should follow suit.

  3. 03Procedural tools such as extensive voir dire, juror questionnaires, and judicial instructions are more effective at ensuring impartiality than changing the courthouse zip code.
  4. 04Jurors in distant locations are not necessarily more objective, as they may harbor their own external biases regarding the nature of the crime or the defendant.
  5. 05The Sixth Amendment requires a jury capable of weighing evidence impartially rather than a jury that is entirely ignorant of the case.

"The justice system should replace subjective standards for venue changes with an objective, data-driven metric to ensure fair trials in highly publicized cases."

The federal appeals court’s decision to keep the Dzhokhar Tsarnaev trial in Boston highlights a glaring flaw in how our justice system handles changes of venue. Currently, courts rely on vague, subjective standards about "presumed prejudice" that give judges enormous leeway to deny venue changes regardless of the facts on the ground. We need an objective, quantifiable metric for moving trials in the modern age.

When a defense team requests a venue change, courts should apply a "saturation and proximity" test. This test would require an independent analysis of two specific data points. First, the court must measure the density of direct victimization. In the Tsarnaev case, this is not just the hundreds injured at the finish line, but the thousands of medical personnel, police, and witnesses directly involved, plus their immediate family members. If the percentage of the jury pool with less than two degrees of separation from the crime exceeds a certain threshold—say, 15 percent—the venue must be changed.

Second, courts must look at media consumption analytics. Rather than asking jurors if they have "formed an opinion," courts should examine market data on how thoroughly the local media consumed the public attention compared to a control city. In Boston, the local media saturation during the manhunt was absolute.

Relying on jurors to self-report their own biases during *voir dire* is an exercise in psychological denial. People are notoriously bad at recognizing their own deeply ingrained prejudices, especially when influenced by a unifying civic slogan like "Boston Strong." By adopting a strict, data-driven threshold for venue changes, courts can remove the guesswork and protect the integrity of the Sixth Amendment from the overwhelming pressure of local sentiment.

  1. 01Courts should implement a 'saturation and proximity' test that mandates a venue change if a specific percentage of the jury pool has direct personal connections to the crime.
  2. 02Venue decisions should be based on objective media consumption analytics rather than relying on jurors' unreliable self-assessments of their own biases.
  3. 03A strict, quantifiable threshold for moving trials is necessary to protect the Sixth Amendment from the influence of powerful local sentiment and civic unity.

"Courts should prioritize rigorous jury selection processes over geographic venue changes, as modern media makes it impossible to find a jury pool unaware of high-profile crimes."

We should be honest about what venue change really accomplishes in the modern era: very little. The Boston Marathon bombing was a national, indeed international, news story. There is no federal district in America whose residents are unaware of it. Moving the trial to Manhattan or Washington or San Francisco would produce a jury pool that consumed essentially the same news coverage as a Boston pool, minus the direct personal experience of the lockdown.

Is that direct personal experience legally significant? Sometimes. A juror who was running the marathon, or who lost a relative, would be struck for cause anywhere. But the marginal Boston juror — someone who watched the events on TV, like Americans everywhere — is no more biased than a marginal juror in another city who also watched on TV. Voir dire is the right tool to sort out the genuinely conflicted from the merely informed.

The deeper problem is that our jurisprudence on venue is rooted in a pre-broadcast world, when local newspapers actually shaped a juror's information environment in ways that distant newspapers did not. That world is gone. Today, the relevant question is less geographic than psychological: which prospective jurors have formed firm opinions that they cannot set aside? That inquiry can be done in Boston as well as anywhere else.

So my standard would be a procedural one. Trial courts should conduct extended, individualized voir dire in high-profile cases; allow generous use of cause challenges; and consider venue change only if the actual results of jury selection demonstrate that an impartial panel cannot be seated. Pretrial speculation about community sentiment, fueled by defense surveys and prosecutorial counter-surveys, is a poor substitute for seeing whether a jury can in fact be assembled. In Tsarnaev's case, Judge O'Toole's decision to test the waters through voir dire before ruling on venue is exactly the right approach.

"Courts should decide whether to move a trial based on a disciplined, evidence-driven inquiry into whether the local jury pool has been exposed to influences that ordinary jury selection cannot cure."

Courts should decide venue motions with a more disciplined inquiry than the usual debate over whether publicity is “too much.” The key question is not how many news stories have appeared, but whether the local jury pool has been exposed to influences that ordinary voir dire cannot cure.

Several factors should matter. First is the size and diversity of the community. A sensational murder in a small county is different from a sensational crime in a metropolitan federal district. Second is the percentage of prospective jurors with personal ties to the event: victims, relatives of victims, first responders, hospital workers, people who were evacuated or locked down, and people whose daily lives were directly affected. Third is the character of publicity. Straight reporting is less troubling than repeated exposure to inadmissible confessions, leaked evidence, inflammatory editorials, or descriptions that turn the defendant into a symbol rather than an accused person.

Timing matters too. Passions cool, though not always quickly. Courts should ask whether the publicity is continuing and whether public commemorations or anniversaries keep the case emotionally fresh. Public opinion surveys may help, but only if they are carefully designed; a poll showing that people have heard of the case proves very little. More useful is data showing how many people have fixed opinions, how strongly held those opinions are, and whether they can fairly consider both guilt and punishment.

Voir dire should then test the predictions. If a large share of prospective jurors must be excused because they cannot presume innocence, cannot consider a life sentence, or have personal connections to the attack, that is strong evidence that transfer is required. If, after searching questioning, the court can seat a jury that appears genuinely open, transfer may be unnecessary.

Applied to the Tsarnaev case, the answer should not rest on Boston’s pain alone or on judicial confidence alone. The judge should make explicit findings based on questionnaires, individual questioning and the content of local publicity. A venue change is a drastic remedy, but so is trying a capital case before a jury pool that has effectively lived inside the crime. The standard should be evidence-driven, transparent, and revisable as jury selection unfolds.

  3. 03The timing of the trial and the persistence of public commemorations should be evaluated to determine if local passions have sufficiently cooled.
  4. 04Public opinion surveys are only useful if they measure the strength and fixity of opinions rather than mere awareness of the case.
  5. 05The results of the voir dire process itself serve as critical evidence; if a large portion of the pool cannot presume innocence or consider all sentencing options, a transfer is required.
  6. 06Judges should make explicit findings based on questionnaires and individual questioning rather than relying on general judicial confidence or the community's emotional state.
