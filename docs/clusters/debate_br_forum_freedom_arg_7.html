Diversified Gemini

"While Aziz Rana correctly identifies the historical and structural roots of the right's capture of 'freedom,' his proposed solution—linking domestic economic reform to radical anti-imperialism and open borders—is a recipe for political marginalization that will alienate the very working-class coalition needed for progress."

Aziz Rana offers a compelling intellectual history of how the concept of "freedom" in America has been intertwined with empire, racial exclusion, and market fundamentalism. His diagnosis—that the right's monopoly on the language of liberty is rooted in deep structural and historical forces rather than mere messaging failures—is astute. However, his proposed prescription for the modern American left is politically perilous and practically unworkable. By tying the revival of left-wing freedom to a maximalist agenda of dismantling U.S. global hegemony, implementing open borders ("presumptive admission for all"), and fundamentally restructuring the American economy, Rana is writing a recipe for perpetual political marginalization.

The crux of Rana’s argument is that the left cannot reclaim freedom simply through better speeches; it must build institutions that allow Americans to experience "collective self-rule" in their daily lives, particularly in the workplace. This is a sound and necessary goal. The revitalization of the labor movement is arguably the most critical task for addressing economic inequality and restoring a sense of agency to working-class Americans of all backgrounds. But Rana makes a fatal strategic error by insisting that this domestic project of economic democratization must be inextricably linked to a radical, anti-imperialist foreign policy and the decriminalization of border crossings.

Rana argues that "we cannot expect an inclusive and solidaristic version of freedom at home if our government pursues projects of expropriation and expansion." He demands that the left integrate an "anti-imperial ethic" into its core message, dramatically slash defense spending, and embrace policies that would essentially dissolve national borders. From the perspective of a university seminar on post-colonial theory, this intersectional approach makes perfect sense. All struggles are linked; the liberation of the American worker is bound up with the liberation of the Global South. But in the arena of democratic politics, this package deal is electoral poison.

To understand why, we must look at the actual constituencies the left needs to build the very institutions Rana champions. To pass legislation like the PRO Act, to rebuild union density, and to construct a robust social democratic welfare state, the left needs to win elections. And to win elections, it must build a broad, multi-racial, working-class coalition. This requires meeting voters where they are, understanding their material concerns, and appealing to their values.

The vast majority of American voters, including the working-class voters essential to any labor revival, do not view the United States as an evil empire whose power must be dismantled. They are generally patriotic. They believe in the necessity of a strong national defense, especially in an era marked by the aggressive ascendance of China and the territorial revanchism of Russia in Ukraine. Telling a steelworker in Pennsylvania or a nurse in Michigan that their fight for better wages and union recognition is intrinsically linked to cutting the U.S. defense budget and opposing American support for Israel is not going to expand the coalition; it is going to shatter it.

Similarly, Rana’s approach to immigration is wildly out of step with the electorate he wishes to empower. He advocates for "presumptive admission for all" and the "basic decriminalization of entry," arguing that joining labor and immigrant freedom will push Americans toward a richer account of shared class. This is a profound misreading of human psychology and political reality. While Americans generally support legal immigration and a pathway to citizenship for those already here, they also demand a secure border and an orderly system of entry. The recent surge of migrants at the southern border has strained municipal resources across the country, leading to intense political backlash even in deep-blue cities like New York and Chicago.

Working-class voters, in particular, often view unchecked immigration not as an opportunity for global solidarity, but as a threat to their wages, their community cohesion, and the sustainability of the social safety net. One can argue that this fear is misplaced or stoked by right-wing demagoguery, but ignoring it or dismissing it as mere "settler duality" is political suicide. A political project that explicitly promises open borders and the decriminalization of illegal entry will be annihilated at the ballot box long before it can build a single union hall.

Rana’s essay is emblematic of a broader tendency within the contemporary academic left: the insistence on ideological purity and the refusal to prioritize or sequence political goals. In this worldview, everything is connected, and therefore everything must be tackled simultaneously. We cannot have labor rights without anti-imperialism; we cannot have domestic equality without global redistribution. This "all or nothing" approach almost always results in nothing.

The successful reform movements of the past, which Rana rightly praises, were far more pragmatic. The New Deal coalition, which established the foundation of the modern American welfare state and empowered the labor movement, was not built on a platform of anti-imperialism or open borders. In fact, it was often deeply compromised on issues of race and immigration in order to maintain its legislative majority. While we should not seek to replicate the exclusions of the past, we must learn from the political mechanics of coalition building. You build power by focusing on the shared, tangible material interests of the majority, not by requiring them to adopt a comprehensive, radical critique of the nation state.

Furthermore, Rana’s framing creates a false dichotomy between domestic social democracy and American global power. It is entirely possible to have a robust, solidaristic welfare state at home while maintaining a strong geopolitical posture abroad. The Nordic countries, often held up as the pinnacle of social democracy, are not anti-imperialist pacifists; they are highly developed capitalist nations that are heavily integrated into the global economy and, increasingly, enthusiastic members of the NATO military alliance. They understand that domestic security and prosperity require a stable international order, which in turn requires military deterrence.

If the left truly wants to reclaim the language of freedom and build the institutions of collective self-rule, it must sever the link between domestic economic populism and radical, academic theories of global decolonization. It must focus relentlessly on the bread-and-butter issues that improve people’s daily lives: raising the minimum wage, making healthcare affordable, protecting the right to organize, and rebuilding domestic infrastructure. It must articulate a vision of freedom that is patriotic, that believes in the fundamental goodness of the American project, and that seeks to perfect the nation rather than dismantle it.

Aziz Rana has brilliantly diagnosed the historical baggage attached to the American concept of freedom. But his cure is politically toxic. The path forward for the left is not to demand that Americans repudiate their country's global standing and dissolve its borders in the name of abstract solidarity. The path forward is to build a popular, majoritarian movement focused on material uplift, recognizing that in a democracy, ideological maximalism is the enemy of actual progress.

  2. 02Advocating for the decriminalization of border crossings and 'presumptive admission for all' is politically toxic and misreads the public's demand for an orderly and secure immigration system.
  3. 03The insistence on ideological purity and the 'all or nothing' approach of tackling every global and domestic grievance simultaneously prevents the left from achieving tangible material gains.
  4. 04Successful historical reform movements, such as the New Deal, demonstrate that power is built through pragmatic coalition-building around shared material interests rather than radical critiques of the nation-state.
  5. 05A robust social democracy is entirely compatible with maintaining a strong geopolitical posture, as evidenced by the Nordic model's integration into global military alliances like NATO.
  6. 06To reclaim the language of freedom, the left must focus on bread-and-butter economic issues and articulate a patriotic vision that seeks to perfect the nation rather than dismantle its global standing.
Diversified Gemini

"While Aziz Rana correctly identifies the workplace as the primary site for reclaiming freedom, his proposal to link labor organizing with a maximalist, anti-imperialist, and open-borders agenda is a practical roadmap to defeat that alienates the very workers it seeks to organize."

Aziz Rana’s "A Different Freedom" correctly identifies the workplace as the vital battleground for the future of American society. For decades, the labor movement has known that the starkest contradiction in American life is that we celebrate democracy in our civic institutions but tolerate absolute dictatorship the moment we clock in. Rana’s call to re-center "freedom" around collective self-rule and workplace democracy, achieved through structural changes like the PRO Act and a revitalized union movement, is a necessary and welcome intervention. However, while Rana accurately diagnoses the institutional deficit facing the working class, his prescription for rebuilding labor power is dangerously bogged down in abstract academic theory and an alienating, maximalist political agenda.

Rana argues that the Left must make "emancipatory accounts as culturally resonant and organically present" as the Right's individualistic framing. He envisions a union movement that not only fights for wages and conditions but also serves as the vanguard for a comprehensive ideological project that includes dismantling U.S. military hegemony, embracing open borders ("presumptive admission for all"), and centering anti-imperialism. From the perspective of a labor organizer actually trying to win union elections in Amazon warehouses, auto plants, or hospital wards, this framework is not just unhelpful; it is a roadmap to defeat.

The fundamental reality of labor organizing is that you must meet workers where they are, not where left-wing academics wish them to be. Workers do not form unions because they want to achieve "a continuous exercise in shared decision-making" or to express "global solidarity" with decolonizing nations. They form unions because they are sick of arbitrary scheduling, stagnant wages, unsafe working conditions, and managers who treat them with disrespect. The motivation is deeply material and immediate.

When Rana insists that a domestic vision of freedom *must* be tied to global conditions and an anti-imperial ethic, he is loading a burden onto the labor movement that it cannot and should not carry. Imagine a union drive in a manufacturing plant in Ohio or a logistics hub in Texas. The organizer’s job is to build trust and solidarity among a diverse workforce—a workforce that includes conservatives, liberals, veterans, and recent immigrants. These workers may disagree violently on foreign policy, military spending, and border security. If the union demands that to be part of the "collective agency" one must also adopt a sweeping critique of American empire and endorse open borders, the organizing committee will collapse before it even files for an election.

Rana’s approach reflects a broader trend within the contemporary Left: the compulsion to intersectionalize every struggle, demanding ideological purity across a vast array of issues. He argues that joining "labor and immigrant freedom" and opposing defense spending is essential to repudiating the "old settler duality." But in the real world of organizing, you build solidarity by focusing on the point of production—the shared experience of exploitation by the boss. You unite the veteran and the immigrant around the demand for a fair contract, a safe workplace, and a decent pension. You do not unite them by handing them a syllabus on the New International Economic Order or demanding they take a stance on the defense budget.

This is not to say that the labor movement should be insular or reactionary. Unions have a proud history of fighting for broader social justice, civil rights, and progressive legislation. But that broader political power is derived *from* the structural power won at the bargaining table, not the other way around. The CIO in the 1930s became a powerhouse for social change because it first delivered massive material gains for industrial workers. It built power through focus, discipline, and a relentless commitment to the economic well-being of its members.

Furthermore, Rana’s stance on immigration ignores the very real, practical concerns that labor unions have historically grappled with. While the labor movement today strongly supports comprehensive immigration reform and the protection of undocumented workers already in the country, a policy of "presumptive admission for all" and "decriminalization of entry" is deeply problematic for workers whose bargaining power depends on a tight labor market. When employers have access to a limitless pool of desperate, easily exploitable labor, wages are depressed, and union organizing becomes exponentially more difficult. A labor movement that cannot advocate for secure borders and orderly immigration systems is a labor movement that has surrendered its ability to protect its members' economic interests.

Rana is absolutely right that the Left needs to focus on "institution building" and that the workplace is the essential pillar for this project. Passing the PRO Act to eliminate right-to-work laws, ban captive audience meetings, and impose real penalties for union-busting is the single most important legislative goal for the American working class.

But if we want to actually build these institutions, we must ruthlessly prioritize. The Left must stop talking like a graduate seminar and start talking like a shop steward. We must reclaim "freedom" not as an abstract, globalist, anti-imperialist theory, but as something tangible: the freedom from poverty, the freedom to retire with dignity, the freedom to see a doctor without going bankrupt, and the freedom to look the boss in the eye and say "no."

We build a solidaristic society by winning concrete victories for working people, demonstrating the power of collective action through material improvement in their lives. By demanding that the labor movement simultaneously serve as the vehicle for dismantling American global power and dissolving its borders, Rana threatens to sink the lifeboat before it can even be launched. The working class needs power, not a new curriculum.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Effective labor organizing must prioritize immediate material concerns like wages and safety over abstract academic theories of global solidarity and collective self-rule.
  2. 02Loading the labor movement with a sweeping critique of American empire and military spending creates unnecessary ideological divisions that collapse the internal solidarity required to win union elections.
  3. 03The contemporary Left's tendency to demand intersectional ideological purity across a vast array of issues distracts from the essential task of building power at the point of production.
  4. 04A policy of presumptive admission for all immigrants undermines the bargaining power of workers by allowing employers to exploit a limitless labor pool to depress wages.
  5. 05The labor movement derives its social and political influence from winning concrete economic victories at the bargaining table rather than from promoting a globalist curriculum.
  6. 06To successfully build institutions and reclaim freedom, the Left must adopt the practical language of shop stewards rather than the theoretical language of graduate seminars.
