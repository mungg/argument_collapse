"The rise of closed information appliances like the iPhone should be viewed as a valid response to consumer demands for reliability and security rather than a threat to innovation, provided that the broader digital ecology maintains open spaces for tinkering and data portability."

The iPhone is too easy a villain. Jonathan Zittrain presents Steve Jobs’s new device as a symbol of the road away from generativity: beautiful, useful, controlled, and therefore dangerous. The contrast with the Apple II is rhetorically powerful. One machine awaited instructions from anyone; the other accepts only what Apple permits. But this comparison risks mistaking one kind of innovation for the whole of innovation. Appliances are not simply failed PCs. They are products designed around different values, and those values deserve more respect.

Most people do not experience computers as canvases for creativity. They experience them as unreliable household infrastructure. They want to send mail, read news, manage photos, write documents, talk to friends, listen to music, and perhaps shop or bank. When a PC slows down under the weight of spyware, when a driver conflict breaks printing, when a child installs a file-sharing program that brings pop-ups and legal risk, generativity does not feel emancipatory. It feels like bad design. The appliance model answers a real demand: make the device coherent, dependable, and supported.

Zittrain acknowledges this demand but treats it mainly as a threat to the ecology that produced the Web, VisiCalc, Wikipedia, and countless other inventions. The worry is fair: if every device requires permission from a platform owner, many unforeseen uses will never arise. But it does not follow that closed or semi-closed platforms are sterile. Innovation can occur within constraints. Game consoles have generated remarkable creative industries despite certification requirements. The iPod transformed music listening not because users could rewrite its firmware, but because hardware, software, store, and design worked together. BlackBerry became indispensable to many professionals because it solved a specific problem extremely well. The iPhone’s browser, interface, and integration may bring mobile Internet use to people who would never tolerate the awkwardness of earlier smartphones.

Generativity is not the only public value. Reliability, safety, accessibility, battery life, privacy, and accountability matter too. A device that allows arbitrary background processes may be more open, but it may also drain power, expose location, crash calls, or confuse users. Jobs’s remark that people do not want their phone to stop working because they loaded three applications is not merely paternalism. A phone is not a desktop computer. It is also an emergency device, a social lifeline, and a radio operating on networks shared with others. The case for tighter control is stronger in a pocket device than on a hobbyist workstation.

The better question is not whether appliances should exist, but what conditions make them compatible with a generative culture. Here Zittrain’s proposals on data portability and application neutrality are useful, though they should be separated from hostility to appliances as such. If I buy a phone, store photos, messages, contacts, and notes on it, I should be able to export them. If I entrust mail or family pictures to an online service, I should be able to leave without abandoning my own life. Portability reduces the cost of choosing convenience. It lets consumers enjoy managed systems without becoming captives.

Disclosure also matters. If a device is locked, say so plainly. If the manufacturer may remove functions remotely, say so. If applications require approval, describe the criteria. If a carrier restricts uses such as voice over IP, tethering, or third-party messaging, that too should be visible at purchase. The wrong is not that every product has boundaries. All products do. The wrong is hiding those boundaries until after users, developers, and businesses have invested.

Zittrain’s “appliance neutrality” principle is strongest when a company invites outside developers and then changes the rules opportunistically. A closed platform from the start is one thing. A platform that cultivates an ecosystem of developers, gains market power from their investment, and then discriminates against them is another. Law should be wary of bait-and-switch generativity. Still, mandates must be cautious. If imposed too early or too broadly, they may discourage companies from opening platforms at all. A firm considering a software development kit for a phone might decide not to release one if doing so creates open-ended legal duties. The law should protect settled expectations without punishing experimentation.

The same balance applies to security. Zittrain favors community-based tools for assessing code. That may work on PCs, but appliances can contribute to the broader ecosystem in another way: by narrowing the attack surface for users who do not want to administer computers. If millions of people shift some everyday tasks from vulnerable Windows machines to better-managed devices, botnets may have fewer opportunities. This is not a complete solution, and closed devices can have their own flaws. But diversity of platforms is itself a security benefit. A monoculture of general-purpose PCs is a tempting target.

One might answer that appliances depend on the prior existence of open PCs. Today’s iPhone, Xbox, and Web services were created by people who learned, tinkered, and built on open systems. If future generations grow up only as consumers of sealed devices, where will the next inventors come from? This is Zittrain’s most serious challenge. The answer is to preserve abundant spaces for tinkering, not to insist that every device be equally open. Schools, libraries, universities, and households should have access to programmable computers. Development tools should be cheap or free. Open-source systems should flourish. Networks should allow new applications. Children should be able to move from using a device to programming one when curiosity strikes.

In other words, the ecology needs both gardens and workshops. A garden is curated, bounded, and maintained. A workshop is messy, flexible, and dangerous in small ways. Civilization is not improved by turning every garden into a machine shop. Nor is it improved by abolishing workshops because someone might cut a finger. The policy goal should be to keep the paths between them open.

That means protecting the neutral Internet more vigorously than protecting the general-purpose PC as the universal endpoint. If appliances can attach to an open network, if users can export their data, if developers have some route to reach willing users, and if open computers remain available and affordable, then the rise of appliances need not end generativity. It may simply relocate some of it. The Web browser itself, even on a controlled device, can be a surprisingly generative environment, especially as Web applications become richer. Facebook applications and Google mash-ups are tethered, as Zittrain notes, but they also show that creativity often adapts to new constraints.

We should not be naïve about corporate control. Apple, Microsoft, carriers, and service providers will seek advantage. They may block competitors, impose rents, or narrow user freedom. Law has a role in preventing deception, preserving portability, and policing monopoly abuses. But neither should we be naïve about openness. For many users, the open PC has become a source of anxiety and expense. A world with more appliances may be more usable, more secure, and more inviting.

The task is not to save the PC from the iPhone. It is to ensure that the iPhone’s convenience does not become the only model of digital life. Let appliances be appliances. Let PCs be PCs. Keep the network open, the data movable, the rules honest, and the tools of creation within reach. Generativity will survive not by winning every design battle, but by remaining available wherever imagination needs it.

  3. 03The policy focus should shift from preserving the PC as the universal endpoint to ensuring data portability and transparency regarding device boundaries.
