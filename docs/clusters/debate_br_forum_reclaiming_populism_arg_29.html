"Reclaiming populism as a strategic framework is counterproductive for the left because the term is ideologically burdened and fails to address the structural fragmentation of the modern working class."

In her compelling essay, Adom Getachew and her collaborators have identified what may be the central strategic question facing the American left: can a populist framework—a politics that appeals to "the people" as a unified collective against entrenched elites—serve as the foundation for a durable, multiracial progressive coalition? The Chicago Teachers Union's remarkable trajectory from striking in 2012 to helping elect democratic socialists to city council in 2019 offers one model. The Sanders campaign offered another, more expansive vision. And the COVID-19 pandemic, as Getachew rightly observes, has revealed both the desperate need for and the profound obstacles to a revitalized progressive populism. Yet in my view, the essays in this forum underestimate the extent to which the very framework they champion—the reclamation of populism as a descriptor for left politics—may be strategically counterproductive. The term "populism" carries too much ideological baggage, obscures more than it illuminates about the actual mechanics of coalition-building, and risks committing the left to a politics of affect and identity that cannot deliver the structural transformations its proponents seek.

To be clear, I share the authors' frustration with the lazy consensus that treats populism as inherently reactionary. The equation of populism with Trump, with Bolsonaro, with Orbán has become a truism in both academic and popular discourse, and it obscures the ways that appeals to "the people" have animated some of the most consequential movements for social justice in American history. The Populist movement of the late nineteenth century, for all its internal contradictions and explicit racism, articulated a fundamental critique of the concentrated economic power that continues to structure American life. The civil rights movement, in its most insurgent moments, framed its demands in precisely the populist language of reclaiming a democracy corrupted by white supremacy and corporate interests. The New Deal coalition, however imperfect, represented a successful effort to build a governing majority around populist appeals to ordinary Americans against economic royalists.

But the question before us is not whether populism has been unfairly maligned. It is whether a deliberate effort to claim the label and build explicitly populist political formations can succeed in the current conjuncture. Here I am considerably more skeptical than my colleagues in this forum. The contemporary political-economic terrain differs profoundly from the contexts in which populist appeals have historically succeeded. The transformations in labor markets, the collapse of private-sector unionization, the financialization of the economy, and the rise of winner-take-all dynamics within virtually every industry have fundamentally atomized the working class that populism presupposes. You cannot appeal to "the people" as a coherent political subject when the people have been disaggregated into gig workers, contingent faculty, app-based laborers, and a shrinking permanent workforce defined more by their precariousness than by any shared material interest.

The Sanders campaign offers the clearest test case. Whatever its considerable achievements in mobilizing young voters and shifting the terms of political debate, it ultimately failed to generate the multiracial working-class coalition that Getachew and her collaborators envision. The reasons for this failure are disputed, but I would emphasize a structural factor that the essays underemphasize: the inability of a populist message to overcome the fragmented, segmented character of the contemporary working class. Sanders did remarkably well among service workers, hospitality industry employees, and other precariously employed people—but these constituencies lack the institutional presence, the neighborhood concentration, and the cultural coherence that would allow them to function as a political base in the way that industrial workers did in the New Deal era. Populist appeals require a certain kind of political morphology to be effective, and that morphology has been largely destroyed.

This does not mean that progressive politics is doomed. The CTU's successes demonstrate that movement-building around specific, concrete demands—better pay, smaller class sizes, more librarians and nurses—can achieve victories even in the absence of a broader populist narrative. The socialist caucus in Chicago's city council exists because organizers built relationships, won specific battles, and accumulated credibility over years. This is a different model: not the top-down mobilization of a charismatic leader around a populist message, but the bottom-up construction of institutional power through patient, strategic organizing. It is precisely the kind of politics that Ernesto Laclau's influential work on populism tends to abstract away in favor of the dramatic moment of "the people" constituting itself against its enemies.

If this analysis is correct, then the project of "reclaiming populism" may be a distraction from the more fundamental task of building organizational capacity in communities, workplaces, and institutions. The label matters less than the substance. What the left needs is not a new populist discourse but a serious engagement with the question of how to rebuild the institutions—the unions, the mutual aid societies, the community organizations—that can aggregate interests, train leaders, and sustain pressure over the long term. The COVID-19 pandemic has revealed, as nothing else could, the catastrophic consequences of the decline of these institutions. The workers who are now deemed "essential" but are left without protection are, overwhelmingly, the workers least likely to be organized, least likely to have voice in their workplaces and communities, and least likely to be reached by any populist message that presupposes a coherent "people" to be addressed.

I do not mean to suggest that the essays in this forum are wrong about everything. The emphasis on internationalism, on the need to link domestic struggles with global solidarity, is exactly right. The recognition that white supremacy remains a profound obstacle to coalition-building is essential. And the insistence that the pandemic's political implications are not predetermined—that it could deepen rightward trends or bolster progressive causes depending on political agency—is a healthy corrective to both utopian and dystopian simplifications. But the framework of populism, however thoughtfully rehabilitated, risks misleading us about the tasks ahead. The left does not need to reclaim a word. It needs to rebuild a movement.
