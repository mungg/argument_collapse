"A post-neoliberal economic framework for inclusive prosperity must move beyond universalism to center racial justice and the repair of historical racial extraction."

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman offer a bracing correction to the idea that economics is synonymous with market fundamentalism. But their account of inclusive prosperity gives too little attention to the oldest and most durable exclusions in American economic life: racial domination and the racial wealth gap. A post-neoliberal economics that does not put race at the center will reproduce too much of the old framework, even if it adopts more progressive policies.

The lead essay mentions racial disparities in wealth and well-being, but race largely disappears from the policy discussion that follows. This is a missed opportunity. The United States did not merely develop an unequal capitalism that happened to contain racial disparities. American capitalism was built through slavery, land seizure, racial exclusion, segregated labor markets, discriminatory housing policy, unequal education, and criminalization. Markets were not neutral mechanisms later distorted by prejudice; they were structured by law and violence to distribute property, opportunity, and risk along racial lines.

The racial wealth gap is the clearest example. Wealth is cumulative. It reflects past income, inheritance, homeownership, access to credit, tax subsidies, neighborhood appreciation, education, and protection from predation. White households have benefited from policies that built assets: land grants, homesteading, subsidized mortgages, the GI Bill, favorable tax treatment of homeownership and retirement savings, public infrastructure, and schools funded by property wealth. Black households were often excluded from these channels or included on inferior terms. Redlining, restrictive covenants, contract buying, urban renewal, employment discrimination, and predatory lending extracted wealth from Black communities. The result cannot be solved by general growth alone.

The authors’ institutional approach should make them especially attentive to this history. If markets are embedded in institutions, then racially stratified institutions produce racially stratified markets. Property rights, contract enforcement, credit scoring, zoning, school finance, policing, and local taxation are not race-neutral in their origins or effects. An inclusive prosperity agenda must therefore be race-conscious in design, not merely universal in aspiration.

Universal social programs are important. Health care, child allowances, unemployment insurance, stronger labor standards, public education, and progressive taxation would disproportionately help many Black and Latino families because they are overrepresented among those harmed by low wages and insecurity. But universalism alone can leave racial gaps intact. If everyone moves forward at the same rate, historical gaps remain. If benefits are delivered through institutions with racial bias, unequal treatment persists. If programs depend on homeownership, formal employment, or local property taxes, they may reinforce existing disparities.

Consider labor policy. Stronger unions and wage boards could reduce racial wage inequality, especially in low-wage service sectors where workers of color are concentrated. Yet the history of American labor is mixed. Some unions fought for interracial solidarity; others excluded Black workers or defended white job privileges. New labor institutions must be deliberately inclusive: protecting domestic workers, agricultural workers, gig workers, formerly incarcerated workers, and undocumented workers; ensuring representation of women and people of color in bargaining structures; enforcing anti-discrimination rules; and addressing harassment and scheduling, not just wages.

Trade policy also has racial dimensions. The public debate often centers on white manufacturing communities in the Midwest, but deindustrialization also devastated Black workers in cities such as Detroit, Cleveland, Chicago, Baltimore, and St. Louis. Public narratives that treat economic dislocation as newly visible only when it affects white workers distort policy priorities. A serious adjustment policy must include urban Black communities, tribal communities, Puerto Rico, and regions long treated as expendable.

Financial regulation must also confront racial extraction. The 2008 crisis was not merely a story of excessive leverage and systemic risk. It was also a story of predatory lending targeted at Black and Latino borrowers, who were steered into costly subprime loans even when some qualified for better terms. The destruction of housing wealth in communities of color was catastrophic. Macroprudential regulation is necessary, but so are fair lending enforcement, postal banking or public banking options, down payment assistance, anti-predatory rules, and mechanisms to rebuild stolen wealth.

Education policy cannot be separated from housing segregation. Economists often argue for human capital investment, and rightly so. But schools reflect neighborhood wealth, racialized zoning, municipal boundaries, and political power. A child’s opportunity is shaped by whether her family can afford to live in a high-resource district. Inclusive prosperity requires fair housing enforcement, integration efforts, school finance reform, public transit, and affordable housing in opportunity-rich areas. It also requires investing in historically neglected neighborhoods without displacing residents.

The authors’ discussion of property rights opens the door to a more transformative racial agenda. If property rights are socially constructed, then we can ask how past unjust property arrangements should be remedied. This raises the question of reparations. By 2019, reparations had moved from the margins toward the center of public debate, not because they are easy but because the scale of racial theft is impossible to ignore. Economists have tools that could help estimate losses from slavery, discrimination, housing exclusion, and unequal public investment. But reparations are not only a calculation; they are a democratic and moral reckoning.

Some will object that race-conscious policy is politically divisive. But race-neutral policy in a racially unequal society is not neutral. Moreover, reactionary politics has long used racial resentment to undermine universal social provision. The New Deal and Great Society were limited and attacked through racial politics. Welfare was racialized. Public housing was racialized. Crime policy was racialized. If inclusive prosperity avoids race to preserve coalition unity, it may leave the field open to those who weaponize racial fear.

A better approach is targeted universalism: set universal goals, then design policies attentive to the different barriers facing different groups. If the goal is secure retirement, address the fact that Black workers have less inherited wealth and more physically taxing jobs. If the goal is full employment, address discrimination in hiring and criminal records. If the goal is affordable housing, address segregation, appraisal bias, and exclusionary zoning. If the goal is democratic participation, address felony disenfranchisement, voter ID laws, polling place closures, and the political underrepresentation of poor communities of color.

Economics after neoliberalism must also expand what it measures. Life expectancy decline, maternal mortality, environmental racism, incarceration, exposure to violence, and stress from discrimination are economic issues. They affect human flourishing, labor market outcomes, family stability, and democratic participation. A narrow focus on income misses the ways racial hierarchy is embodied and spatialized. Lead in water, asthma from pollution, long commutes from segregated housing, court fines and fees, and cash bail are part of political economy.

The authors want economists to persuade non-economists that economics can be part of the solution. On race, persuasion will require humility. Economists should learn from historians of slavery and capitalism, sociologists of segregation, legal scholars of discrimination, public health researchers, and organizers in affected communities. They should not arrive with models that translate racial injustice into a few variables and declare the matter solved. But they can contribute: by documenting disparities, evaluating policies, exposing rent extraction, designing transfers, and showing how racism damages both justice and prosperity.

The post-neoliberal era cannot merely be a return to mid-century social democracy. That era itself was racially exclusionary. Nor can it be a colorblind welfare state that assumes equal starting points. Inclusive prosperity must be reparative prosperity. It must ask not only how to distribute future growth, but how to repair accumulated deprivation and dismantle institutions that continue to sort Americans by race.

The lead essay gives us a valuable framework: markets are made, power matters, institutions differ, and economics need not serve the powerful. The next step is to recognize that in the United States, power has always been racialized. Economics after neoliberalism must be economics after racial capitalism.
