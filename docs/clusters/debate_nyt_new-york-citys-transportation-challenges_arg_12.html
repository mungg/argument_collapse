"The next mayor of New York City must implement congestion pricing to reduce traffic gridlock and provide essential capital funding for the city's transit network."

If the next mayor is serious about improving commute times and funding our desperately overstretched transit network, they must revive the political courage to fight for congestion pricing. We can debate the merits of bike lanes and pedestrian plazas all day, but these are localized interventions. The macro-level crisis facing New York City is that our streets are choking on traffic while our subways and commuter rails are starved for capital funding.

The bridge-and-tunnel crowd, along with outer-borough residents who drive into Manhattan, currently face paralyzing gridlock because the East River bridges remain toll-free. This creates perverse incentives, encouraging drivers to route through densely populated neighborhoods in Brooklyn and Queens to avoid the tolled tunnels. By implementing a rational pricing system that tolls vehicles entering the central business district, the city could instantly reduce traffic volumes. This would dramatically speed up commute times not just for the drivers who choose to pay the toll, but for the commercial freight that keeps our economy running and the surface transit vehicles trapped in the same gridlock.

More importantly, the billions of dollars in revenue generated by congestion pricing could be lockboxed for transit improvements. Following the devastation of Superstorm Sandy, the MTA’s capital needs are more urgent than ever. We need signal upgrades to run trains closer together, expanded regional rail capacity, and heavy investments in outer-borough transit hubs. Mayor Bloomberg attempted to pass congestion pricing in 2007 and was rebuffed by Albany. The next mayor cannot afford to take no for an answer. Without pricing our roads correctly, we will never have the money or the physical space to solve our regional transportation challenges.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Tolling vehicles entering the central business district would eliminate the incentive for drivers to clog outer-borough neighborhoods to avoid existing tolls.
  2. 02Reducing traffic volume through pricing would significantly improve commute times for commercial freight and surface transit vehicles.
  3. 03The revenue generated from congestion pricing is necessary to fund critical MTA capital projects, such as signal upgrades and expanded regional rail capacity.
  4. 04Congestion pricing addresses the macro-level transportation crisis in a way that localized interventions like bike lanes and pedestrian plazas cannot.
