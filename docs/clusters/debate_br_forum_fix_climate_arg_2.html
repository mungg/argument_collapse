Diversified Gemini

"The Paris Agreement should be viewed as a successful and necessary experimentalist framework that provides the essential overarching architecture for global climate action, rather than an inflexible top-down relic that should be discarded in favor of disjointed sectoral agreements."

In their essay "How to Fix the Climate," David G. Victor and Charles Sabel construct a compelling narrative about the failures of global climate diplomacy. They paint the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC) and the Paris Agreement as relics of an inflexible, top-down era, arguing instead for a fragmented, sector-by-sector approach they call "experimentalist governance." While their exploration of sectoral innovation is valuable, their dismissal of the Paris Agreement is premature and relies on a fundamental mischaracterization of how the international climate regime has evolved.

Far from being the antithesis of experimentalist governance, the Paris Agreement is, in fact, the largest and most ambitious experimentalist framework ever created. Victor and Sabel’s critique might have been devastating if directed at the Kyoto Protocol of 1997, which genuinely attempted to impose rigid, top-down, legally binding emissions targets on a subset of industrialized nations. But Paris was explicitly designed to correct the failures of Kyoto by embracing the very principles of bottom-up initiative, periodic review, and decentralized action that Victor and Sabel champion.

The authors claim that Paris is "hamstrung by its reliance on sovereign NDCs [Nationally Determined Contributions]" and a rulebook that makes intrusive review impossible. This betrays a misunderstanding of how NDCs function in practice. An NDC is not merely a diplomatic pledge; it is an umbrella under which thousands of domestic "experiments" take place. When a country submits an NDC, it is forced to engage its domestic industries, cities, and regulatory agencies to figure out what is technologically and politically feasible. The NDC aggregates these local and sectoral experiments into a national framework.

Furthermore, the Paris Agreement’s "ratchet mechanism"—the requirement that countries return every five years with progressively more ambitious targets, subject to a Global Stocktake—is the exact structural equivalent of the periodic review panels Victor and Sabel praise in the Montreal Protocol. The Global Stocktake assesses aggregate progress, identifies technological and policy breakthroughs, and uses that shared knowledge to pressure nations to tighten their goals. It is a learning institution built for uncertainty.

Victor and Sabel argue that the "real work" gets done in economic sectors (like shipping or steel) rather than within sovereign states. But this creates a false dichotomy. Sectoral innovation and national climate targets are deeply symbiotic. The International Maritime Organization (IMO) or networks of global steel manufacturers do not operate in a political vacuum. As the authors themselves concede late in their essay, the Paris Agreement is "the most legitimate institution in global politics where climate change is discussed," and it provides the ultimate "penalty default" by authorizing activists, investors, and governments to punish laggards.

But Paris does much more than just provide a legitimate banner for protesters. It coordinates the global financial system. The signals sent by the Paris Agreement have catalyzed a massive shift in capital markets. Major asset managers, sovereign wealth funds, and central banks are aligning their portfolios with the goals of Paris. This macroeconomic alignment is what funds the sectoral experimentation Victor and Sabel so value. A Swedish steelmaker is willing to bet on zero-carbon steel not just because they are talking to a German steelmaker in a sectoral committee, but because the Paris Agreement has convinced global capital that there will be a future market for green steel.

If we were to abandon the UNFCCC framework in favor of a patchwork of disjointed sectoral agreements, as the authors seem to suggest, we would lose the vital overarching architecture that holds the global effort together. Climate change, unlike ozone depletion, is not a problem of isolated industrial sectors; it is an economy-wide crisis that requires interconnected solutions. How do we ensure that the electrification of the auto sector aligns with the decarbonization of the power grid? How do we balance agricultural emissions with land-use policies and forestry? These cross-sectoral challenges cannot be solved in isolated committees; they require national governments setting comprehensive, economy-wide strategies.

As we approach the end of 2020, with the Biden administration preparing to re-enter the international climate arena, the focus must be on leveraging the Paris architecture, not discarding it. We should integrate Victor and Sabel’s insights on sectoral innovation into the NDC process, using international forums to accelerate technology transfer. But we must not mistake the slow, grinding reality of international consensus-building for structural failure. The Paris Agreement has provided the global scaffolding; it is now up to governments, industries, and civil society to build the experimentalist engine within it.
