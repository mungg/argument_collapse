"While Walter Johnson’s critique of racial capitalism is powerful, it must be supplemented by an attention to the specific cultural and political formations of the enslaved to avoid losing the moral heart of the historical enterprise."

Walter Johnson's essay is a powerful synthesis of the Du Boisian tradition in the study of slavery and capitalism, and it deserves credit for bringing that tradition to a wider audience. Yet in its emphasis on the global and structural dimensions of racial capitalism, it risks losing sight of the lived experience of the enslaved—and with it, the moral heart of the historical enterprise.

Johnson's critique of the "dehumanization" framework is insightful in its diagnosis of the ideological work done by certain formulations of this concept. He is right that historians who frame their work as a "proof" of enslaved people's humanity are participating in a logic that renders that humanity intellectually probationary. If black humanity must be proven again and again, it is never fully established; it remains always under suspicion, always subject to challenge.

Yet Johnson's alternative is not entirely satisfactory. He suggests that we should attend to the historical parameters and cultural determinants of enslaved people's actions, but this leaves unclear what exactly we should see when we look at the lives of the enslaved. The emphasis on "independent will and volition" that Johnson critiques is problematic not because it is wrong but because it is incomplete. Enslaved people did exercise agency, but their agency was exercised under conditions of radical constraint, and it took forms that cannot be reduced to the liberal categories of choice and self-determination.

A more adequate framework is offered by Du Bois's concept of "double consciousness." This concept does not reduce enslaved people's experience to the assertion of humanity or the exercise of liberal agency. It captures something more complex: the way that enslaved people were forced to see themselves through the eyes of their oppressors while also developing their own independent sense of self. Double consciousness was not merely a psychological condition; it was a cultural and political formation, a way of navigating the conditions of slavery while maintaining a critique of it.

The spirituals are the clearest expression of double consciousness in the cultural life of the enslaved. They drew on the imagery and concepts of Christianity—the God who created all humans equal, the suffering servant who would be vindicated, the Exodus that promised liberation—to make sense of the conditions of slavery. They did not assert "humanity" in the abstract; they articulated a specific vision of justice grounded in the resources of a specific cultural tradition.

This is what Johnson means when he says that we should not collapse the actions of enslaved people "down to a single anachronistic and essentially liberal moral." The spirituals were not liberal manifestos; they were expressions of a religious culture that addressed the specific conditions of slavery. And in addressing those conditions, they revealed the contradictions of the system that claimed to treat human beings as property.

The same point can be made about resistance. Enslaved people resisted in many ways—through sabotage, flight, rebellion, and the preservation of family and cultural practices. These forms of resistance cannot be reduced to the assertion of liberal agency; they were specific responses to specific conditions. And in understanding them as such, we gain insight not only into the history of slavery but into the possibilities of resistance under conditions of radical domination.

Johnson's argument about racial capitalism is powerful, but it should be supplemented by this attention to the cultural and political formations of the enslaved. The structure of racial capitalism is important, but it is not the whole story. What matters also is how enslaved people lived within and against that structure—how they made lives, made meaning, and made demands for justice under conditions not of their choosing.

This supplement does not undermine Johnson's argument; it deepens it. The critique of racial capitalism is not only a matter of understanding the system but of understanding how it was lived—and in understanding how it was lived, we find resources for critiquing and transforming the present. The enslaved were not only victims of racial capitalism; they were its critics, and their critique remains vital today.
