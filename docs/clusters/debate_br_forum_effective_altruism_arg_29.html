"While effective altruism offers valuable tools for maximizing the impact of resources, its purely rational and utilitarian framework overlooks the essential spiritual, relational, and transformative dimensions of charity."

Peter Singer’s effective altruism asks us to think clearly about how to do the most good with our resources, and that is a worthy aspiration. But as a Christian, I find the movement’s vision of charity incomplete. Singer treats giving primarily as a problem of rational optimization: measure outcomes, compare cost-effectiveness, and direct funds accordingly. What this approach overlooks is the spiritual dimension of charity—the understanding that giving is not only about achieving results but about the transformation of the giver’s heart and the cultivation of a loving community. An ethic of charity that lacks a soul may be efficient, but it is not fully human.

Scripture repeatedly calls us to give generously and cheerfully, not out of cold calculation but out of love for God and neighbor. The story of the widow’s mite reminds us that it is not the amount that matters but the sacrificial spirit. The Good Samaritan does not pause to compare the marginal utility of helping the injured traveler against a donation to a distant leper colony; he acts out of compassion for the person in front of him. This is not “warm glow giver” irrationality. It is the recognition that moral life unfolds in the concrete encounters God places before us. When we reduce these encounters to data points in a global optimization problem, we risk losing the very capacity for mercy that makes charity virtuous in the first place.

Singer is right that many donations are made thoughtlessly, and that some charities are fraudulent or ineffective. Christians should certainly exercise prudence and avoid enabling waste or harm. But prudence is not the same as treating GiveWell’s top picks as the only legitimate outlets for generosity. My parish church runs a food pantry that serves a few dozen families. The pantry is not the most cost-effective way to alleviate hunger—probably a larger organization could do more with the same dollar. Yet the pantry does something that a Direct Relief shipment cannot: it creates a space where neighbors encounter one another face to face, where the hungry are not anonymous recipients but members of a community who are prayed for by name. That fellowship is itself a good, one that resists quantification. Effective altruism, with its emphasis on scalable impact, tends to flatten out these local, relational goods as inefficiencies.

Moreover, the Christian tradition insists that the moral worth of an action depends partly on the intention behind it. If I donate to an anti-malaria charity simply because the math works out, but my heart remains untouched by love for the children who will sleep under those nets, my act is ethically diminished. Love transforms giving from a transaction into a participation in God’s own care for the world. Singer might reply that what matters is the suffering prevented, not the state of my soul. But that is a philosophical commitment, not an obvious truth. For Christians, the ultimate purpose of life is not to maximize utility but to grow in holiness and love. Charity is a means to that end, both for the giver and the receiver.

This is not to deny that effective altruism does immense good. The movement has saved countless lives, and that is cause for gratitude. Christians can learn from its rigor and its refusal to let good intentions substitute for genuine impact. But a purely secular, utilitarian framework cannot sustain the deepest motives for altruism. It invites a kind of moral bureaucracy in which we outsource our compassion to a spreadsheet. By contrast, a faith-shaped charity asks us to give not only our money but our attention, our prayer, and our presence. It recognizes that effectiveness is not the only measure of love.

Singer closes his essay with the hope that reason can expand our moral horizons. I share that hope but would add that reason alone is insufficient. The great saints of the Christian tradition—Francis, Teresa of Calcutta—did not embrace poverty and service because they calculated that it would maximize QALYs. They were seized by a love that overflowed the boundaries of prudence. Effective altruism can tell us how to give, but it cannot tell us why. For that, we need a source of meaning that transcends utility, a wellspring of charity that is not exhausted by any human accounting. And that, finally, is a matter of faith.
