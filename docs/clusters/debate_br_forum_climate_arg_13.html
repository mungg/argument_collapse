"The defeat of fossil capital must be inextricably linked to a broader ecosocialist rejection of the capitalist growth imperative, as an energy transition that preserves infinite accumulation will merely replace one extractive regime with another."

Táíwò is right that fossil capital must be defeated, but he understates the degree to which fossil capital is not an aberrant faction of capitalism. It is the concentrated expression of a deeper imperative: endless accumulation. A climate politics that first dethrones fossil capital and only later asks whether growth, commodification, militarism, and imperial consumption can continue may find that the throne has been refilled by another extractive regime.

The problem is not merely that oil, gas, and coal companies have too much power. It is that modern economies are organized around expansion: more production, more consumption, more infrastructure, more extraction, more throughput, more returns on investment. Fossil fuels powered this expansion because they were abundant, dense, and profitable. Renewable energy can power many necessary and desirable activities, but it cannot make infinite growth ecologically harmless. Solar panels, wind turbines, batteries, transmission lines, electric vehicles, data centers, and heat pumps all require land, minerals, labor, and waste sinks. If the goal is to preserve high-consumption societies with cleaner inputs, the transition will generate new sacrifice zones.

This is where degrowth and ecosocialist arguments are not optional add-ons but central. Táíwò mentions them favorably, yet his two-step frame could be read as: first achieve the energy transition, then transform everything else. That sequencing is dangerous because the shape of the energy transition depends on the social order it is meant to serve. A transition designed to maintain luxury consumption, suburban sprawl, planned obsolescence, military dominance, and profit-driven production will look very different from one designed around sufficiency, care, repair, public services, and global equality.

Consider transportation. A carbon-reductionist approach replaces gasoline cars with electric cars. That is better than gasoline cars, but it leaves intact car dependency, dangerous streets, mineral-intensive manufacturing, long commutes, and land use organized around highways and parking. An ecosocialist approach asks how much mobility people need, how it can be provided with less material throughput, and who controls the infrastructure. It prioritizes public transit, walking, cycling, dense affordable housing, regional rail, and shorter workweeks. Electrification is part of the answer, but the goal is not a one-for-one substitution of machines.

Or consider housing. A green-growth approach retrofits some buildings and electrifies appliances while real estate remains a speculative asset. An ecosocialist approach treats housing as a right, invests in public and cooperative housing, reduces energy demand through insulation and design, and prevents landlords from capturing the benefits. Again, the issue is not technology versus politics. It is whether technology serves sufficiency or accumulation.

The same applies globally. Wealthy societies cannot claim an equal right to continue material expansion while poorer societies still lack adequate housing, electricity, sanitation, health care, and transport. Climate justice requires convergence: more energy and material use for those denied dignified lives, less wasteful use by the affluent. That is not simply fossil phaseout. It is planned downscaling of destructive sectors in rich economies: private aviation, arms production, advertising, fast fashion, SUVs, industrial meat, disposable goods, and perhaps significant parts of finance. Without this, “clean growth” will remain a promise that the whole world can live like the rich without ecological consequence. It cannot.

Táíwò’s analogy to earlier two-step strategies is illuminating but should make us cautious. Twentieth-century movements that seized states often inherited developmentalist assumptions: build industry, increase production, catch up with the West, master nature. Some achieved real gains in literacy, health, and sovereignty; many also reproduced hierarchy and ecological damage. The tragedy was not only that leaders betrayed ideals. It was that the model of liberation was too often tied to productivism. Climate politics must not repeat this by imagining that a green developmental state can deliver justice while leaving the growth imperative intact.

The state is especially ambivalent here. States seek legitimacy through growth. They fear unemployment, capital flight, fiscal crisis, and geopolitical weakness. Even progressive governments often measure success by GDP, exports, investment, and competitiveness. A state may build renewables not to reduce throughput but to power new industries, attract data centers, expand mining, and increase military capacity. “Energy security” can easily mean more energy for the same destructive purposes. Public ownership does not automatically solve this. A publicly owned enterprise can still be productivist; a national plan can still be ecologically irrational if its objective is expansion.

This is why climate politics must democratize the definition of need. What production is socially necessary? What should be reduced? What should be expanded? Who decides? Markets answer according to purchasing power; capitalist states often answer according to growth and national competition. Ecosocialism answers through democratic planning rooted in equality and ecological limits. That sounds ambitious because it is. But the alternative is to let investment decisions be made by profitability, then wonder why emissions and extraction persist.

A serious degrowth politics does not mean recession or generalized deprivation. Recession is chaotic contraction that hurts the poor first. Degrowth, properly understood, is planned reduction of energy and material throughput in wealthy economies while improving well-being through redistribution and public goods. It means universal basic services, shorter working hours, job guarantees in care and ecological restoration, repair economies, agroecology, and limits on luxury consumption. It means replacing private affluence with public abundance: libraries, parks, transit, clinics, schools, clean water, cultural life, and free time.

This politics can strengthen the fight against fossil capital. Fossil companies thrive not only because they lobby but because societies remain energy-hungry in ways that make their product seem indispensable. Reducing unnecessary demand weakens them. If cities become less car-dependent, oil loses power. If buildings are efficient and electrified, gas utilities lose power. If industrial agriculture shrinks and food systems localize where appropriate, fossil inputs decline. If militaries are reduced, one of the world’s most carbon-intensive institutional complexes shrinks. Demand reduction is not moralistic individual sacrifice; it is structural disarmament of fossil dependence.

There is also a temporal argument. Building clean supply fast enough is difficult. Reducing wasteful demand can make the task easier. A grid serving efficient homes, transit-oriented cities, and less wasteful industry requires fewer mines, fewer transmission conflicts, fewer batteries, and less land. This matters politically because the transition is already producing conflicts over siting and minerals. The more energy rich societies insist on consuming, the more they will impose extraction elsewhere. Sufficiency is therefore a condition of global solidarity.

The lead essay rightly criticizes climate politics that confuses moral parity with temporal priority. But we should be wary of separating fossil defeat from broader transformation too sharply. Racism, colonialism, patriarchy, and class exploitation are not merely issues that become winnable after fossil capital falls. They structure the transition itself. Which neighborhoods get trees and cooling centers? Which lands are mined? Which workers are exposed to heat? Which migrants are criminalized after climate disasters? Which households can afford electrification? Which countries are told to remain poor? If these questions are postponed, the first step will be unjust and politically unstable.

What would an ecosocialist two-step look like? First, confront fossil capital through phaseout mandates, public ownership where useful, subsidy removal, liability, and bans on new infrastructure—but simultaneously reduce energy demand in rich sectors through public investment and regulation. Second, build democratic planning institutions that allocate resources according to need within ecological limits. The overlap is crucial. The first step must prefigure the second, or else it will build institutions hostile to it.

This means climate movements should not be embarrassed to talk about limits. The right will call any serious climate policy austerity or tyranny. Avoiding the language of limits will not prevent that. The better answer is to distinguish democratic sufficiency from elite excess. People do not need billionaire space flights, mega-yachts, private jets, or endless advertising-driven consumption. They do need cooling, heating, food, water, housing, care, mobility, education, and joy. A politics that guarantees the latter while restricting the former can be popular if it is honest and redistributive.

Táíwò ends by saying enough steps in the right direction get you where you are going. True, but only if they are steps on the right road. A transition that builds renewable energy for an economy still organized around accumulation may reduce emissions too slowly, deepen extraction, and entrench new inequalities. Dethroning fossil capital is necessary. But the crown belongs not only to oil executives. It belongs to growth as a social command. Climate justice must depose both.
