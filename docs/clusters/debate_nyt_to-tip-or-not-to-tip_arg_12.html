Diversified Gemini

"The only effective way to move past the exploitative tipping model is through legislative action to abolish the subminimum wage for tipped workers rather than relying on voluntary changes by individual restaurants."

Sushi Yasuda’s shift away from tipping is an admirable gesture, but framing this as a choice for individual restaurants misses the broader structural problem. We cannot rely on the goodwill of high-end sushi bars to fix a deeply exploitative labor system. The only way to move past the tipping model in America is through legislative action to abolish the subminimum wage for tipped workers.

In 2013, the federal tipped minimum wage sits at an appalling $2.13 an hour, a rate that has not budged since 1991. The restaurant industry lobby, led by the National Restaurant Association, has fought tooth and nail to keep this dual-wage system in place, shifting the burden of paying their employees directly onto the consumer.

Expecting individual restaurants to voluntarily end tipping while their competitors continue to exploit the $2.13 loophole is terrible public policy. If a neighborhood restaurant tries to do the right thing—paying their staff a flat, living wage and raising menu prices to cover it—they are put at a massive competitive disadvantage. Their burgers will look significantly more expensive than the joint down the street that pays its staff practically nothing.

Voluntary bans on tipping will only ever be a niche luxury trend. To create real change, we need to mandate a level playing field. Organizations like the Restaurant Opportunities Centers (ROC) United are right to push for legislative solutions rather than relying on consumer boycotts or rogue restaurateurs.

States like California and Oregon have already eliminated the tipped subminimum wage, requiring employers to pay the standard state minimum wage before tips. This is the crucial first step. Once all restaurants are legally required to pay their staff a real, living base wage, the reliance on tipping will naturally recede. Until the law changes, however, noble experiments like Sushi Yasuda’s will remain exceptions to a fundamentally broken rule.
