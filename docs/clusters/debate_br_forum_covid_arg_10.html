The Censorship Was the Story

Stephen Macedo and Frances Lee have written a careful, restrained essay. It is so careful, in fact, that it understates what is arguably the most disturbing legacy of the pandemic response: the systematic suppression of dissenting expert opinion by an alliance of federal officials, social media platforms, and the prestige press. The authors mention this in passing—the YouTube takedown of Ioannidis, Meta's 27 million removed posts, Francis Collins's "quick and devastating takedown" email, the Stanford Faculty Senate's censure of Scott Atlas—but they treat these as illustrations of a broader epistemic failure. They are more than that. They are the central scandal, because they explain why the broader epistemic failure was so durable and so total.

Consider what the lead essay establishes. Before March 2020, the dominant view among pandemic planners—WHO, Johns Hopkins, the UK Department of Health, D.A. Henderson and his coauthors, the Institute of Medicine—was that population-wide NPIs were of uncertain effectiveness, that border closures and contact tracing were not recommended, that quarantine of the healthy was perhaps the least likely measure to work, and that the social costs of prolonged restrictions would be devastating. This was not a fringe position. It was the considered judgment of the world's leading public health institutions, rendered in writing, repeatedly, over more than a decade. Anthony Fauci himself voiced it in late January 2020.

Now ask: between January and March 2020, what new evidence emerged that justified abandoning this consensus? Essentially none. The Imperial College model was a model, not evidence. China's claimed success in Wuhan was reported by an authoritarian regime with strong incentives to project competence and was, in any case, not externally validated. What changed was not the evidence but the political and emotional climate. The previously mainstream view became, almost overnight, "misinformation."

This is where the censorship apparatus matters. If the Great Barrington Declaration had been published in 2018 as a planning document, its core claims—age-stratified risk, focused protection of the vulnerable, skepticism of population-wide restrictions—would have been unremarkable. Published in October 2020, the same claims were treated as a public health emergency in themselves, with the NIH director personally directing a "devastating takedown" and platforms suppressing discussion. The Fifth Circuit's findings in Missouri v. Biden (later Murthy v. Missouri at the Supreme Court) established that federal officials coerced platforms with "intimidating messages and threats of adverse consequences." That is not a footnote. It is a constitutional event.

Macedo and Lee are right to note the chilling effect on debate, but they don't quite name what was lost. What was lost was the ordinary scientific process. Science advances through public, contested, often messy disagreement. Steven Goodman's comment—that public debate among scientists can "confuse people and undermine consistent messaging"—captures the alternative epistemology that displaced ordinary science during the pandemic: a managerial model in which a designated authority (the WHO, the CDC, Fauci) decides what is true and the role of dissenters is to be silent or be silenced. Under this model, the proper response to Jay Bhattacharya, Sunetra Gupta, and Martin Kulldorff was not to engage their arguments but to delegitimize them. That is precisely what happened. And it worked, in the sense that for roughly two years it foreclosed the public discussion that might have changed policy.

The authors are too gentle about the press in this story. The New York Times, the Washington Post, the Atlantic, NPR—these outlets did not merely fail to interrogate official claims. They actively amplified the delegitimization. The Atlantic called Georgia's reopening "an experiment in human sacrifice"; the Times ran the doomsaying models on the front page without serious counter-modeling; the Post quoted Collins calling distinguished epidemiologists "fringe" without noting that their position was, in fact, the pre-pandemic mainstream. When the lab leak hypothesis—endorsed in private by senior virologists from the earliest weeks, as we now know from released emails—was labeled "misinformation," major papers parroted that label rather than reporting the underlying scientific disagreement. Zeynep Tufekci's "we were badly misled" column this March was a striking admission, but it should have appeared in 2021, and the misleading was not passive.

Why does any of this matter five years later, when the emergency is over? Three reasons.

First, the infrastructure of suppression remains. The pressure channels between federal agencies and platforms have been formalized, not dismantled. The legal questions about whether such pressure constitutes state action under the First Amendment remain unresolved. The next emergency—whether a pandemic, a climate event, a geopolitical conflict—will find these mechanisms ready to use, and there is little institutional learning to suggest they will be used with more restraint.

Second, the credentialing institutions that misfired have largely escaped accountability. Francis Collins retired with honors. The Stanford Faculty Senate has not, to my knowledge, withdrawn its censure of Atlas, even though Atlas's substantive positions on masks and school closures look considerably better in retrospect than the positions of those who censured him. Universities have not investigated the suppression of their own faculty. Journals have not retracted clearly mistaken commentary. Without accountability there is no learning.

Third—and this is where Macedo and Lee are entirely correct—the populist suspicion of expertise that Donald Trump exploits in 2025 is partly the predictable harvest of these failures. When public health authorities tell the public that schools must close based on "the science" and the science turns out to have been a contested model dressed in consensus clothing; when the same authorities denounce a lab leak as conspiracy theory and then quietly concede its plausibility; when platforms remove posts at government direction and the government denies doing so until courts force disclosure—the rational response of citizens is to discount future expert pronouncements. Robert F. Kennedy Jr.'s confirmation as Secretary of Health and Human Services, Jay Bhattacharya's elevation to lead the NIH, the broader collapse of vaccine confidence: these are not bolts from the blue. They are responses, however overshooting, to an institutional betrayal.

Here I want to push past the lead essay's framing in one more respect. Macedo and Lee describe the failure as "tunnel vision" and "class bias" among elites. These descriptions are accurate but insufficiently structural. What we witnessed was the emergence of a coalition—federal health bureaucracies, large platforms, prestige media, university administrations, and aligned NGOs—that exhibited a remarkable capacity for coordinated message discipline. This was not a conspiracy; nobody had to be in a room together. It was something more troubling: a set of institutions that, having internalized similar political commitments and similar professional norms, converged on a single permissible position and treated departures from it as moral as well as factual errors. Call this what you will—the cathedral, the credentialed class, the laptop class. The pandemic revealed its existence and its power.

A serious reckoning with COVID-19 therefore cannot be only a retrospective on NPI effectiveness, though that work is essential and Macedo and Lee summarize the relevant findings well. It must include institutional reforms: legal limits on agency pressure of platforms, transparency requirements for "trust and safety" communications between government and industry, structural protections for academic dissenters, and—perhaps most importantly—a recovery within journalism of the habit of treating official sources as sources rather than as authorities.

I would add one further reform. We need a formal, independent national commission on the pandemic response, modeled on the 9/11 Commission. The United Kingdom has its COVID Inquiry; Sweden conducted one; Scotland commissioned the report Macedo and Lee cite. The United States, the country that spent more and disrupted more lives than any peer democracy, has nothing remotely comparable. The Senate HELP minority report and a handful of House select subcommittee reports do not substitute for a comprehensive, bipartisan, public-facing inquiry with subpoena power. That such a commission has not been established—five years on, under three different congressional configurations—is itself evidence of the elite consensus to move on. We should not move on.

One worry about my framing: doesn't emphasizing censorship and institutional malfeasance play into exactly the populist anti-expert narrative that, as Macedo and Lee note, the authors of this critique should be wary of strengthening? I do not think so. The strongest defense of expertise is honesty about its failures. Expertise that cannot be criticized is not expertise but authority, and authority claiming the prestige of expertise is precisely what corrodes public trust. The way back to a healthy relationship between scientific institutions and democratic publics runs through, not around, the painful work of acknowledging what went wrong.

Macedo and Lee have begun that work. Their essay deserves the broad public debate they call for. But the debate should not stop at whether the NPIs were effective—the empirical question. It should extend to how a society organized around free inquiry came to suppress inquiry on its most consequential policy question in living memory, and what institutional changes can prevent a recurrence. That is the postmortem we have not yet had.
