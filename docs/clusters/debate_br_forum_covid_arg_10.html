The Cost of Neglecting the Vulnerable

Macedo and Lee have written a sweeping indictment of the U.S. pandemic response, and their core charge—that expert institutions succumbed to tunnel vision, moralized dissent, and refused to weigh tradeoffs—contains substantial truth. But as I read their essay, a quiet discomfort grows. Their argument, for all its empirical sophistication, ultimately rests on a frame that is more congenial to the right than they might wish to admit: that the big mistake was government overreach, that restrictions were ineffective, and that the main victims were those harmed by the lockdowns, not those ravaged by the virus itself. A very different story can be told, and it is one that centers race, class, and structural inequality rather than the epistemological sins of public health officials. From that vantage, the real failure was not that we did too much, but that we did not do nearly enough for those on the receiving end of a social order that was lethal long before COVID-19 arrived.

Let’s start with the premise that NPIs did not save lives. The lead essay’s Figure 2—showing virtually identical mortality across red and blue states through April 2021—is striking, but the interpretation is trickier than it seems. The virus did not respect state lines. Many of the deadliest early outbreaks happened in densely packed, lower-income communities in New York City, Newark, and other urban centers that were locked down hard but already had high seeding before restrictions took effect. Meanwhile, rural, predominantly white communities in states with loose policies often had less population density and fewer international travelers, which may have delayed their surges but not prevented them. The counterfactual we need—what would have happened if no state had locked down at all—cannot be read from these graphs. We do know, however, that in the absence of any restrictions, the virus disproportionately sickened and killed essential workers: grocery clerks, bus drivers, meatpacking plant employees, home health aides. These were not the “laptop class” that the lead essay correctly identifies as insulated from hardship; they were the very working-class people the essay says were most harmed by policy. But they were harmed by a virus that flourished where workplaces remained open and protections were absent. The decision to shutter restaurants and offices while keeping logistics hubs and factories running was a tragic compromise, but it was driven by economic necessity, not by a scientific consensus. And it was that compromise—the designation of some workers as sacrificial—that produced the stark racial and class disparities in infection and death we saw throughout 2020. The lead essay notes that Black Americans experienced the largest rise in all-cause mortality, but it attributes much of this to policy side effects. In truth, many of those excess deaths were directly from COVID-19, and they occurred among people who could not afford to stay home and whose jobs were deemed “essential” without offering adequate safety nets.

This brings me to the Great Barrington Declaration. The lead essay treats the declaration’s authors as unjustly persecuted truth-tellers, and I agree that the personal vitriol they faced was unacceptable. But the substance of their proposal deserves far more scrutiny than the authors give it. “Focused protection” is a seductive phrase, but in the United States it was a mirage. How do you protect an elderly parent who lives in a multigenerational household in a one-bedroom apartment in Queens? How do you protect a dialysis patient who relies on public transportation and a low-wage job to survive? The declaration’s premise was that society could be neatly divided into the “vulnerable” and the “resilient,” and that the latter could resume normal life while the former were shielded. But vulnerability is not a discrete category; it is a gradient produced by poverty, by racism, by the very structures of American life. The only way to make focused protection work would have been to provide guaranteed income, safe housing, and comprehensive healthcare to every high-risk person and their families for the duration. That would have required precisely the kind of massive government intervention that the declaration’s supporters—many affiliated with free-market think tanks—have spent decades opposing. The proposal was thus either naïve or disingenuous. In practice, letting the virus rip would have meant that the same working-class, minority communities that the lead essay champions as lockdown’s losers would have faced a tidal wave of sickness and death with even less support, because the public health apparatus would have effectively stood down.

Which is not to say that school closures were harmless. They were devastating. But the lead essay’s framing—that closures were an unforced error by elites—misses the agency of educators, parents, and communities themselves. In many urban districts serving large Black and Latino populations, families were deeply reluctant to send children back into aging buildings with poor ventilation, no testing, and crowded classrooms. Teachers’ unions, however one judges their motives, raised legitimate safety concerns that were never adequately addressed. The better alternative was not to fling open school doors in September 2020 with no mitigations; it was to invest billions in ventilation upgrades, rapid testing, and smaller class sizes so that in-person learning could resume safely. The federal government did some of this, but not nearly enough, and far too late. And the same story holds for other NPIs. The problem was not that we imposed restrictions; it was that we imposed them without building the social infrastructure to make them bearable. European countries that funded furlough schemes and paid people to isolate achieved higher voluntary compliance and lower economic scarring. The United States instead sent one-time checks to everyone regardless of need, left unemployment systems to buckle, and allowed landlords to evict millions. That is not a failure of science; it is a failure of solidarity.

The lead essay’s argument about censorship deserves similar rebalancing. Yes, social media platforms were heavy-handed; the removal of the Ioannidis video and the suppression of lab-leak hypotheses were real injuries to open debate. But the authors gloss over the scale of the misinformation problem. In a country where the sitting president promoted bleach injections and fake cures, and where right-wing media relentlessly undermined vaccines even as they became available, a purely laissez-faire approach to content moderation would have cost lives. The line between protecting public health and stifling dissent is difficult, and we got it wrong in many cases. But to treat the pandemic as a story of censored contrarians versus a monolithic official narrative is to forget the millions of Americans who were radicalized by algorithmic rabbit holes into refusing vaccines, leading directly to the excess Republican mortality the authors document in the post-2021 period. The vaccine hesitancy that ultimately drove the divergence in state death rates was itself a product of the same epistemic chaos the lead essay describes, but that chaos did not arise from the CDC’s muddled messaging alone; it arose from a decades-long erosion of trust fueled by economic precarity, elite disregard, and a political party that found profit in stoking resentment.

So what should a true reckoning look like? It must start by acknowledging that the deepest failure was not that we followed the science too credulously, but that we refused to see science and policy as embedded in a social and economic context. A serious public health response to a pandemic requires more than epidemiologists’ models; it requires a robust welfare state that can cushion the blows of necessary restrictions, that ensures everyone can afford to miss work, that invests in health systems before they collapse, and that communicates with humility and respect for the public’s lived experience. The lead essay’s call for an honest postmortem is correct, but as long as that postmortem focuses narrowly on the intellectual honesty of public health agencies and skirts the deeper question of why the United States tolerated so much death and suffering across all policies—restrictions and reopening alike—it will be an elite debate about elite failures. The people who died in excess numbers, whether from COVID-19, from delayed cancer treatment, or from drug overdoses, were largely the same people: the poor, the racially marginalized, the working class. They were not failed by a particular set of non-pharmaceutical interventions; they were failed by a society that had already decided their lives mattered less. Until that changes, no amount of scientific candor will make us prepared for the next emergency.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The claim that lockdowns were ineffective ignores the fact that high mortality in urban centers was often due to high initial viral seeding and the lack of protections for essential workers who could not afford to stay home.
  2. 02The 'focused protection' strategy proposed by the Great Barrington Declaration was a 'mirage' because it failed to account for the reality of multigenerational housing and the economic impossibility of shielding the poor without massive government intervention.
  3. 03The harm caused by school closures resulted from a failure to invest in necessary safety infrastructure, such as ventilation and testing, rather than a simple error in elite judgment.
  4. 04The negative outcomes of the pandemic response were a 'failure of solidarity' caused by the absence of a welfare state capable of providing the financial and medical support needed to make public health restrictions bearable.
  5. 05While social media censorship was problematic, the authors downplay the lethal impact of widespread misinformation and the decades-long erosion of trust in institutions that fueled vaccine hesitancy.
  6. 06A true postmortem must look beyond the intellectual honesty of experts to address why the U.S. social and economic order consistently treats the lives of the poor and racially marginalized as expendable.
