"Creationism, defined as the rejection of mainstream science in favor of a literalist biblical interpretation, should be controversial because it creates a false and damaging conflict between faith and intellectual integrity."

Creationism should be controversial most of all among religious believers. I do not mean the broad belief that God created the world. I mean the modern insistence that faith requires rejecting mainstream geology, cosmology and evolutionary biology. That insistence has done real damage to religion.

It teaches believers to fear evidence. A child raised to think that Christianity stands or falls with a young earth may eventually encounter radiometric dating, fossils, genetics or astronomy and conclude that the faith has collapsed. But what has collapsed is not necessarily Christianity. It may be a brittle interpretation that confused the authority of Scripture with a particular modern reading of Scripture.

Genesis is profound without being a biology textbook. It proclaims that the world is not divine, that creation is good, that human beings bear God’s image, that work and rest both matter, and that moral disobedience fractures human life. These are not trivial claims. They are also not claims about allele frequencies or the branching pattern of primate ancestry. When churches make Genesis answer questions it was not written to answer, they set up needless conflict.

There is a further problem: anti-evolution creationism often makes God smaller, not greater. It imagines divine action chiefly as interruption, as if God is present only where natural explanations run out. But a deeper doctrine of creation can see God’s providence in ordinary processes as well as extraordinary events. If life developed over immense ages through mechanisms science can study, that need not mean God is absent. It may mean creation is more intricate than we first imagined.

Secular critics should recognize that believers are drawn to Genesis because it gives a morally ordered universe. But believers should recognize that many people are drawn to science not because they hate God, but because they love truth and evidence. If all truth is God’s truth, then Christians have no reason to fear honest investigation.

The controversy, then, should not be “Bible versus science.” It should be within religious communities over whether we will pass on a faith strong enough to face the world as it is. Creationism, narrowly understood as denial of well-established science, deserves criticism not only from scientists but from pastors, theologians and parents who do not want their children forced into a false choice between intellectual integrity and belief in God.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Teaching children that faith depends on a young-earth interpretation makes their religious belief vulnerable to collapse when they encounter scientific evidence.
  2. 02Treating Genesis as a scientific textbook misinterprets the text's original purpose, which was to provide theological and moral truths rather than biological or geological data.
  3. 03Anti-evolution creationism promotes a limited 'God of the gaps' theology that ignores divine providence within natural, ordinary processes.
  4. 04A narrow denial of well-established science forces believers into an unnecessary choice between their religious identity and the pursuit of truth.
