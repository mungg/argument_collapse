"While Deborah Meier’s vision of intimate, community-driven schooling is appealing, her rejection of standardized accountability in favor of local control ignores the historical reality that local autonomy has frequently served as a cover for systemic inequality and the neglect of marginalized students."

Deborah Meier writes with the eloquence and deep humanity that has long made her one of our most indispensable voices in public education. Her portrait of the Mission Hill school, where learning is intimate, community-driven, and intensely personal, is undeniably attractive. Who wouldn’t want their child to attend a school that feels less like a factory and more like a warm, intellectually vibrant family? But as a blueprint for national educational policy in 1999, Meier’s defense of “local control” and her rejection of standardized accountability rests on a deeply flawed and historically amnesiac understanding of what local control has actually meant for the majority of America’s most vulnerable children.

Meier argues that in fundamental questions of education, “experts should be subservient to citizens,” and she champions the power of local communities to forge their own standards. But for African American, Latino, and low-income students, the romanticized ideal of “local control” has almost always been the polite political cover for structural racism, systemic neglect, and unequal funding. Historically, local control meant that a white school board on one side of town could funnel the lion’s share of property tax revenues to its own gleaming facilities while relegating Black children to crumbling, under-resourced buildings. Local control meant that expectations for minority children were systematically lowered by the “ordinary human judgments” of educators who believed that poor kids were simply incapable of complex intellectual work.

The movement for standards-based reform did not emerge solely from the boardrooms of corporate America or from a paranoid misreading of economic indicators, as Meier implies. It also emerged from the civil rights community. For decades, minority parents watched their children receive high school diplomas that were virtually worthless because the local schools had passed them along from grade to grade, a practice born of a paternalistic refusal to demand real academic achievement. Social promotion is not a myth invented by conservative think tanks; it is a lived reality in marginalized communities where schools have historically functioned more as containment facilities than as engines of social mobility.

When a state imposes a single, rigorous standard and requires every student to take the same test, it is doing something profoundly democratic: it is asserting that every child, regardless of their zip code, their skin color, or their parents' income, is entitled to learn the same core academic skills. Standardization, in this sense, is an instrument of equity. It forces the system to make its failures visible. Before the advent of disaggregated testing data, it was entirely too easy for a “good” suburban school to hide the fact that its minority students were failing, masking their struggles behind a high overall average. Standardized tests strip away that veil. They tell us exactly who is being served and who is being left behind.

Meier dismisses this as a simplistic pursuit of “a few more right answers on a standardized test.” But literacy and numeracy are not trivial pursuits, nor are they merely the building blocks of corporate productivity. They are the prerequisites for civic participation and personal liberation. A child who cannot read fluently or do basic algebra by the eighth grade is a child whose choices in life are already drastically curtailed. If we allow each small school to determine its own definition of a "good education," we risk formalizing a tiered system where privileged children learn the language of power, while disadvantaged children are given a “differentiated” curriculum that looks more like a self-esteem exercise than rigorous academic preparation.

To be sure, the punitive use of tests—such as holding children back based on a single score—is a blunt and often destructive instrument. Meier is entirely correct that sanctions should be handled with far more care and humanity than we see in places like Chicago. But the solution to the misuse of testing is better policy, not the abandonment of objective measurement. Meier suggests that we rely on portfolios and the judgments of local educators, a process she compares to a "doctoral dissertation oral exam." That is a beautiful model for a small, heavily subsidized pilot school with a hand-picked staff of extraordinary progressive educators. But how does it scale to a nation of 50 million public school students? The sheer subjectivity of portfolio assessment is a fatal flaw when applied to a massive, unequal system. Who evaluates the portfolios in a struggling, underfunded urban district? If the local standards are low, the portfolio assessments will merely validate those low expectations.

Meier asks us to throw away the factory model and embrace the "messy" reality of democracy. But democracy also requires a social contract. It requires a guarantee that the state will not abandon its children to the varied and often prejudiced whims of local authorities. We have tried leaving education solely to the discretion of local communities. For millions of marginalized students, that experiment was a disaster. Standards and assessments are imperfect tools, but they are the only tools we currently have that force the entire system to look those children in the eye and take responsibility for their futures.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Historically, local control has allowed for the inequitable distribution of resources and the lowering of academic expectations for minority and low-income children.
  2. 02The standards-based reform movement is rooted in civil rights concerns, aiming to end the paternalistic practice of social promotion that devalues the diplomas of disadvantaged students.
  3. 03Standardized testing acts as an instrument of equity by making educational failures visible and ensuring that every child is entitled to the same core academic skills regardless of their background.
  4. 04Basic literacy and numeracy are essential prerequisites for civic participation and personal liberation, making objective measurement of these skills a democratic necessity.
  5. 05Relying on subjective local assessments like portfolios is unscalable and risks validating low expectations in underfunded districts where oversight is most needed.
  6. 06A democratic social contract requires state-mandated standards to protect children from the prejudiced whims or varied quality of local authorities.
