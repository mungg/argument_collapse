"While technology cannot replace institutional reform or human capacity, it should be integrated into education as a tool for exploration and equity rather than being dismissed due to the failures of poorly implemented distribution programs."

Kentaro Toyama’s criticism of One Laptop Per Child and similar educational technology projects deserves to be taken seriously, especially by those of us who believe computers can matter for learning. The weakest argument for laptops is the one most often made in their defense: children are naturally curious, computers are naturally engaging, therefore learning will naturally happen. Anyone who has spent time in a classroom knows that “naturally” is doing too much work.

But Toyama’s essay risks conflating bad laptop policy with the deeper idea that children, including poor children, can use computers as instruments of exploration and expression. There is a difference between dropping machines into weak schools and building a learning environment in which children program, write, draw, record, simulate, communicate, and solve problems. The latter is difficult. It requires teachers or mentors, curriculum, maintenance, language support, time, and a culture that values inquiry. Yet difficulty is not futility.

The educational debate around computers has always contained two visions. In one, the computer is a tutor: it delivers lessons, drills arithmetic, and replaces scarce teacher attention. In the other, associated with Seymour Papert and constructionist learning, the computer is a material for thinking: children make things with it and learn through making. OLPC has often borrowed the language of the second vision while being implemented by governments tempted by the first or by the symbolism of distribution. Toyama is right to criticize the slippage. A laptop by itself is not a pedagogy.

Still, the failures of existing school systems should not automatically push computers to the back of the line. In many poor countries, classrooms are overcrowded, teachers underprepared, textbooks scarce, and rote learning dominant. Saying “invest in teachers first” is sound, but not simple. Teacher training systems are often weak; supervision is politicized; absenteeism is common; curricula are outdated. Computers cannot solve these problems, but they may be part of strategies to address them. For example, they can provide teachers with lesson resources, allow children to practice at their own pace, support local-language materials, and make student work visible to parents and administrators.

Toyama asks whether we would choose PC-based education for our own children. As a replacement for good teachers, no. As part of a rich environment, absolutely. Middle-class children increasingly grow up with computers at home and school. They learn not only formal skills but confidence: the sense that digital tools are theirs to command. If poor children are denied that experience until every school system is fixed, inequality may deepen. The children of the affluent will use computers to create, search, network, and apply for opportunities, while poor children are told to wait for institutional reform.

The opportunity-cost concern remains. A hundred-dollar laptop is not cheap in an education budget where teachers, toilets, roofs, blackboards, and deworming are underfunded. But education is not a single-outcome enterprise. Deworming improves attendance; it does not teach writing, mathematics, or scientific reasoning. Textbooks are essential; they do not teach children to debug a program or produce a multimedia report. The right comparison is not always laptop versus pill. It may be laptop program versus other efforts to improve learning, motivation, teacher support, or digital inclusion. Many laptop programs will lose that comparison. Some may not.

The design principles are clear enough. First, do not buy hardware without a pedagogical plan. Second, do not pretend children can maintain systems without adult support. Third, do not impose English-only software in multilingual societies. Fourth, do not let procurement drive policy. Fifth, do not scale before learning from small implementations. Sixth, do not measure success by distribution numbers. Measure attendance, literacy, numeracy, student projects, teacher practices, repair rates, equity of use, and long-term continuation.

Most importantly, involve teachers rather than treating them as obstacles. OLPC’s rhetoric has sometimes implied that teachers are unnecessary or even harmful relics. That is both politically foolish and educationally wrong. Good teachers orchestrate attention, ask questions, diagnose confusion, and create expectations. In poor communities, a teacher may also be the adult who legitimizes girls’ use of computers or persuades parents that a machine is more than a toy. If teachers are weak, strengthen them. If they are absent, fix incentives and accountability. But do not design around their disappearance.

Toyama’s magnifier thesis applies well here: computers magnify the learning culture around them. In an exam-obsessed rote classroom, they may become expensive notebooks or distractions. In a classroom organized around inquiry, they can expand what children imagine and produce. The policy challenge is to create more of the latter, not to expect hardware to do it automatically.

I also worry about the symbolic meaning of telling poor children that “few of us would choose PC-based education for our own children.” The affluent rarely choose one thing. They choose books and teachers and museums and sports and computers and safe homes. Poor children are too often offered a sequence of false choices: food or school, textbooks or toilets, teachers or technology. Of course budgets require priorities. But the long-term aim should be abundance in learning environments, not austerity justified as realism.

Technology cannot end poverty, and laptops cannot rescue education from neglect. On that Toyama is right. Yet education is partly about giving children access to the tools through which their society thinks, works, and communicates. In 2010, the computer is one of those tools. The lesson from failed projects should be humility, not retreat. Put laptops where there is a serious plan for learning; withhold them where there is only a plan for distribution. But do not confuse the emptiness of a slogan with the poverty of the idea that children everywhere deserve powerful tools.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The failure of many ICT4D projects stems from a 'drop and maintain' approach to hardware rather than a failure of the underlying idea that children can use computers for creative problem-solving.
  2. 02Computers should not be viewed as substitutes for teachers but as resources that can help strengthen weak school systems by providing lesson materials and supporting local-language content.
  3. 03Denying poor children access to digital tools until institutional reforms are complete risks deepening global inequality, as affluent children continue to gain digital fluency and confidence.
  4. 04The opportunity cost of technology should be weighed against other learning interventions rather than basic health needs like deworming, as different investments serve distinct educational goals.
  5. 05Successful technology integration requires a shift from measuring success by distribution numbers to focusing on pedagogical plans, teacher involvement, and long-term learning outcomes.
  6. 06The goal for impoverished learning environments should be to move toward the same abundance of resources enjoyed by the affluent rather than accepting a permanent state of austerity.
