"While Zittrain’s focus on civic virtue and community-based tools is valuable, preserving the generative Internet requires a more robust legal framework that shifts the burden of security from individual users to the commercial actors and intermediaries best positioned to mitigate risks."

Jonathan Zittrain’s essay is most persuasive when it describes the Internet’s predicament as a tragedy of success. The same openness that enabled unexpected invention now enables industrial-scale abuse. But his proposed cure leans too heavily on civic virtue and too lightly on political economy. The problem is not simply that users lack tools or that communities have not yet been mobilized. The problem is that many of the actors best positioned to improve security do not bear the costs of insecurity, while those who do bear the costs often lack the power to fix anything.

Consider the ordinary compromised home computer. The user suffers sluggish performance, stolen credentials, or embarrassment. Other users suffer spam or attacks. Banks and merchants suffer fraud. Network operators suffer congestion and complaints. But the software vendor that shipped a vulnerable product may not pay directly. The broadband provider may profit from selling connectivity while treating infected customers as support burdens. The maker of bundled adware may earn revenue while imposing cleanup costs on everyone else. The criminal, if abroad or well hidden, may escape entirely. This is a classic incentive problem.

Zittrain’s generative proposals—virtual machines, easy reversion, code reputation, StopBadware, data portability—are valuable. But without a sharper allocation of responsibility, they may become optional patches on a structurally careless market. We should ask: who can prevent harm at lowest cost? Who profits from risky design? Who has information users lack? Who can spread the cost of safer systems across millions of customers? The answers point toward vendors, service providers, and large intermediaries, not individual users.

This does not mean we should conscript intermediaries into becoming “Net police.” That phrase rightly alarms anyone who values free expression. But there is a middle ground between deputizing every ISP as censor and allowing every firm to disclaim responsibility. Law often creates duties of care without dictating every design choice. Restaurants must meet health standards; landlords must maintain basic safety; automakers must recall dangerous defects. Software and online services should not be categorically exempt from similar expectations merely because code is complex.

A reasonable software liability regime would begin modestly. First, vendors should be held to their promises. If a product claims to be “spyware-free,” it should not install undisclosed components that alter browsers, resist removal, or transmit data in ways users would not expect. Deceptive badware is low-hanging fruit for consumer protection law. Second, companies handling sensitive information should have enforceable duties to use reasonable security practices. A firm that stores financial or medical data in reckless ways should face consequences when predictable breaches occur. Third, vendors that knowingly leave widely exploited vulnerabilities unaddressed while continuing to sell or support products should not enjoy unlimited immunity.

The hard case is ordinary buggy software. Strict liability for every vulnerability would be disastrous. It would favor large companies with legal departments, punish open-source projects, and discourage publication of useful tools. But negligence standards, safe harbors, and sector-specific duties could reduce the worst behavior. Noncommercial developers who act in good faith and respond to notices should receive broad protection. Firms distributing software at scale, especially software that runs with high privileges or updates automatically, should meet higher standards.

Zittrain’s discussion of free software and intellectual property is important in this regard. The law currently threatens collaborative coding with copyright and patent risks while doing too little to deter insecure or deceptive code. That is backwards. We should reduce legal uncertainty for open development—through notice-and-cure rules for inadvertent copyright contamination, patent reform, and shorter windows for sandbagging claims—while increasing accountability for commercial actors who impose security risks on the public. Generativity is not helped by a legal system that makes sharing code legally perilous but shipping careless code commercially routine.

Internet service providers also require a clearer role. Zittrain suggests that strict end-to-end neutrality may need to yield to limited ISP security measures. That is true if carefully bounded. ISPs can identify customers whose machines are sending massive spam, participating in denial-of-service attacks, or scanning the network aggressively. They can notify, assist, and, in serious cases, quarantine. But such measures should be governed by public rules: notice to users, explanation of the behavior detected, a path to restoration, protection against content discrimination, and reporting requirements. Security intervention must be auditable. Otherwise, anti-malware filtering will become a precedent for anti-competitive blocking or political censorship.

The same principle applies to search engines and warning systems. Google’s “This site may harm your computer” label creates powerful incentives for webmasters to clean infected sites. That is a useful private governance mechanism. But because the penalty is severe—traffic can disappear overnight—there must be due process appropriate to the context. Site owners need timely notice, clear criteria, and a realistic path to review. A public-interest partner such as StopBadware can help, but scale matters. If thousands of sites are labeled, overwhelmed reviewers can become a bottleneck. Private security governance must not become arbitrary simply because it is well intentioned.

Zittrain’s most inspiring claim is that the Internet can use generative means to solve the problems of generativity. Sometimes it can. Wikipedia’s community practices are a real achievement. But Wikipedia is not a general model for all Internet governance. It is a project with a defined mission, a visible record of edits, and a community of contributors who share enough norms to make repair possible. The broader software ecosystem is more opaque, more commercial, more adversarial, and more technically complex. Civic tools will help, but they will not eliminate the need for enforceable rules.

The appliance trend should be understood in this economic light. Consumers are not abandoning openness because they have lost civic virtue. They are responding to risk, confusion, and the absence of reliable accountability. A locked-down device offers a clear promise: someone else is responsible. That promise may be incomplete or self-serving, but it is attractive because the open PC world often tells users, “You are on your own.” If defenders of generativity want to compete, they must offer responsibility without monopoly control.

This suggests a policy agenda. Require data portability for hosted services and tethered devices. Protect open-source and amateur developers from disproportionate intellectual-property liability. Impose consumer-protection rules against deceptive software. Create security duties for firms handling sensitive data. Encourage automatic updates and safer defaults. Permit narrowly tailored ISP action against clearly compromised machines, subject to transparency and appeal. Support public-interest measurement projects, but do not expect them to substitute for law. Preserve network neutrality against economic discrimination even while allowing well-defined security practices.

The deeper point is that generativity is not a natural state that survives if government stays away. It is an achievement supported by law, norms, markets, and architecture. The early Internet benefited from public funding, academic culture, open standards, and a small community of skilled users. Today’s Internet is a mass environment embedded in commerce and crime. Its openness will endure only if the burdens of keeping it safe are fairly distributed.

Zittrain is right that we should not wreck the Internet in order to protect it. But we may also wreck it by asking ordinary users and volunteers to absorb costs that powerful institutions could reduce. A generative Internet needs generosity of spirit. It also needs duties of care. Without both, the future will belong either to criminals who exploit openness or to platform owners who sell safety as control.
