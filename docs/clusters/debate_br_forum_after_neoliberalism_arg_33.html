The Limits of the Credibility Revolution

The EfIP manifesto leans heavily on a specific story about contemporary economics: that the discipline has become much more empirical since the 1990s, that systematic evidence has displaced ideological priors, and that the new evidence supports a substantially more interventionist policy stance than the old conventional wisdom. I want to push on each of these claims as someone who works inside this empirical tradition.

The empirical turn is real. The share of empirical papers in top journals has risen substantially, and the methodological standards for credible causal identification have tightened. Where once a regression with a few control variables passed as analysis, the modern paper requires natural experiments, instrumental variables, regression discontinuity designs, or randomized controlled trials. This is progress. Many findings that survived for decades in the textbooks—negative employment effects of moderate minimum wages, large growth effects of trade liberalization, neutral distributional effects of capital account opening—have not held up under more rigorous scrutiny. The authors are right to celebrate this.

But the celebration should be more guarded than the essay allows. The credibility revolution has its own pathologies, and they matter for the political project the authors describe.

First, the credibility revolution biases the discipline toward questions that admit of clean identification, often at the expense of larger and more important questions. We have a great deal of careful evidence about the effects of small changes in policy parameters at the margin of existing institutional arrangements. We have much less evidence about the effects of large institutional changes, because such changes do not produce the kind of variation that credibility-focused designs require. The minimum wage literature is illustrative: we know a great deal about the local effects of $1-$2 minimum wage hikes from neighboring-county designs. We know much less about what a $15 federal minimum wage would do, because such a change is outside the support of the available data. The EfIP project includes proposals (wage boards, contingent debt contracts) that are similarly outside the support of clean U.S. evidence. We can extrapolate, we can use Australian or German comparisons, but the confidence the empirical literature affords for marginal interventions does not carry over to structural ones.

Second, the credibility revolution has a partial-equilibrium bias. The cleanest identification strategies isolate local effects, often by exploiting the fact that one place got a policy and a similar place didn't. But many of the most important effects of policy are general-equilibrium effects that wash out in local comparisons. A minimum wage that raises wages in restaurants might lower wages in agriculture as displaced workers flow elsewhere; a local design won't catch this. A trade liberalization that decimates manufacturing communities might still raise national income on net through gains in other sectors; a place-based design will document the losses but not the offsetting gains. The empirical literature is good at the parts of policy questions that admit of local variation and worse at the parts that don't. The authors lean on the empirical literature without acknowledging that the parts of their proposals that are well-supported empirically are often not the parts that matter most.

Third, the credibility revolution has done less than it claims to discipline ideological priors. The same methods can be applied with different framings, different samples, and different control variables. Studies of the same question often reach divergent conclusions. The minimum wage literature is again illustrative: there is now a large body of credible studies, and they continue to disagree. The Card-Krueger tradition finds small or zero employment effects. The Neumark-Wascher tradition finds meaningful negative effects. Both use modern methods. The disagreement is not resolved by better identification; it is reproduced inside better identification. The authors' confidence that the empirical evidence has moved the field is, in part, a selection effect: they cite the findings consistent with their politics and elide the rest.

Fourth, the credibility revolution has been less successful at the macroeconomic questions that matter most for the EfIP project than at microeconomic questions. We do not have good causal estimates of the effects of capital account liberalization, or of corporate tax cuts, or of public investment, because the relevant variation is at the country-year level and there are not many countries. The authors' confidence that "financial liberalization produces crises rather than faster economic growth" rests on findings that, while suggestive, do not have the identification credentials of the minimum wage or trade literatures. This is not a fatal problem—we do the best we can with the data we have—but it is a reason for more caution than the essay exhibits.

I want to be clear: I support the EfIP project. I think the proposals are mostly good. I think the empirical literature, properly handled, leans in the direction of more interventionist policy than the conventional wisdom of the 1990s. But the rhetorical move the authors make—from "economics has become more empirical" to "economics now supports our policy program"—is faster than the evidence warrants. The empirical literature is a tool, not a verdict. It supports some of the EfIP proposals more than others, and it leaves many of the most important questions open.

The honest version of the empirical case for inclusive prosperity would acknowledge the gaps. It would distinguish between proposals that are well-supported by clean evidence (Dube on local minimum wage variation, perhaps), proposals that are supported by reasonable extrapolation (wage boards informed by Australian evidence), and proposals that rest mostly on theory and analogy (Korinek on directing technological change). All of these can be defended, but the defenses look different. Treating the whole portfolio as if it is uniformly underwritten by the credibility revolution does the project a disservice and gives critics an easy target.

If economics is to play the role the authors hope for it, it needs to be honest about what it knows and doesn't. The credibility revolution is a powerful tool but it does not magically deliver progressive policy conclusions. It delivers, mostly, more cautious and more local conclusions than the old confident generalities. Sometimes those are progressive, sometimes they are not, and sometimes they leave the big questions unresolved. EfIP should embrace this uncertainty rather than paper over it.
