"The Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) agenda fails to truly move beyond neoliberalism because it merely applies new empirical topics to a fundamentally flawed and outdated macroeconomic theoretical framework."

The premise of “Economics After Neoliberalism” is that mainstream economics has already cured itself of its neoliberal disease. Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman argue that the public’s hostility toward economics is based on outdated stereotypes. Because empirical economists in top departments now study inequality, monopsony, and market failures, the authors claim the discipline is fully equipped to guide us toward "inclusive prosperity."

From a Post-Keynesian perspective, this is a profound misdiagnosis. The authors are mistaking a change in the *topics* mainstream economists study for a change in the *core theoretical paradigm* of the discipline. Economics has indeed become more empirical, and it is welcome that labor economists are finally acknowledging monopsony. But the macroeconomic foundations of the discipline—the models that actually dictate fiscal and monetary policy—remain trapped in a pre-2008 fantasy world.

The authors state that the typical macroeconomics course focuses on how governments can solve unemployment and instability rather than relying on self-adjusting classical models. This is highly misleading. The dominant framework in mainstream macroeconomics remains the Dynamic Stochastic General Equilibrium (DSGE) model. These models are still fundamentally built on "methodological individualism" (which the authors admit is still dominant) and "rational expectations." They view the economy as inherently tending toward equilibrium, with crises modeled as exogenous "shocks" rather than the endogenous result of capitalist dynamics.

More glaringly, mainstream macroeconomics still completely misunderstands money and banking. The authors cite Anat Admati and Atif Mian for their work on capital markets and banking regulation. While Mian's empirical work on household debt is excellent, the mainstream theoretical framework still largely treats banks as mere intermediaries that match savers with borrowers. This "loanable funds" fallacy obfusces the reality that banks *create* money ex nihilo through lending.

By failing to incorporate endogenous money and radical uncertainty into their core models, mainstream economists remain blind to the inherent instability of financialized capitalism. Hyman Minsky warned us decades ago that "stability is destabilizing," yet mainstream macroeconomics still treats financial crises as black swan events rather than structural inevitabilities.

Furthermore, the EfIP agenda remains surprisingly timid on fiscal policy. Writing in 2019, amid a sluggish global recovery and persistent secular stagnation, the authors talk about "macroeconomic stability" but offer no bold vision for fiscal dominance. There is no mention of a Job Guarantee, no recognition that sovereign currency issuers cannot go bankrupt, and no call to permanently abandon the paralyzing fear of deficits. Instead, they rely on complex regulatory tweaks (like Mian’s contingent credit contracts) or Zucman’s tax reforms. These are microeconomic solutions to macroeconomic problems.

The authors quote Carlos Diaz-Alejandro saying that a bright graduate student can produce a model yielding any policy recommendation. They offer this as proof of the discipline's flexibility. But this is exactly the problem! If the core theoretical machinery of mainstream economics is so vacuous that it can justify *anything* simply by tweaking an assumption, then it is not a science; it is a sophisticated language game used to confer authority on pre-determined political preferences.

The EfIP network has done good work exposing the flaws of market fundamentalism. But you cannot build a new house on a rotten foundation. Moving "beyond neoliberalism" requires more than adding friction to neoclassical models. It requires abandoning the utility-maximizing *homo economicus*, discarding equilibrium models that ignore financial reality, and embracing a heterodox approach that understands capitalism as a complex, dynamic, and inherently unstable monetary production economy. Until mainstream economists are willing to throw out their cherished DSGE models, their "creative ferment" is just old wine in new bottles.
