"The current disconnect between American principles and institutions is caused not by market inequality, but by the corrupt alliance between big government and the corporate class which stifles individual liberty."

The Boston Review asks whether we are entering a moment of sharp relief between our nation’s principles and its institutions. There is merit in that question, but it leads us down a narrow path. Our founding principles were never simply about equality of opportunity or a voice in a mythical town square; they were, fundamentally, about the right of the individual to be left alone, to pursue his own path without a distant ruler’s permission. The disjuncture we face today is not that the rich have too much, but that government has grown so enormous and so intermingled with the corporate class that the individual—rich, poor, or in between—is increasingly a supplicant. Occupy Wall Street, despite its anti-corporate veneer, has accidentally gathered near the truth: the problem is not Wall Street per se, but the unholy marriage between Wall Street and Washington.

What is the source of extreme inequality? Not the free market, which is a relentless engine for raising the standard of living of the common person, but the systematic rigging of the market by state power. The financial crisis that sparked this unrest was not a product of laissez-faire; it was the direct result of government interventions—the Federal Reserve’s easy money, the implicit “too big to fail” guarantees, the government-sponsored housing enterprises that distorted the mortgage market. When the crash came, the state bailed out the creditor class, socializing losses while preserving private gains. That is crony capitalism, and it is as distant from genuine free enterprise as a commissar’s fiat.

Occupy’s diagnosis is therefore tragically incomplete. The protesters denounce the 1 percent but do not ask how they came to be so entrenched. The financiers have not succeeded by competing in an open market; they have thrived by capturing the regulatory apparatus, by lobbying for rules that crush small competitors, by securing patent monopolies, and by relying on a central bank that protects them from the consequences of their own folly. The answer to inequality is not to expand government in pursuit of some re-distributive ideal. It is to dismantle the machinery of privilege. End the Fed, repeal the regulations that require an army of compliance lawyers only the big players can afford, abolish corporate welfare, and stop using the tax code to engineer social outcomes. Only then will the 99 percent encounter a genuine meritocracy, where wealth is tied to value creation rather than political connection.

The editors’ eloquent language about “bringing our institutions into better alignment” with principles of participation and meaningful opportunity would, if applied honestly, require a radical decentralization of power. It would mean challenging the two-party duopoly, the permanent bureaucratic state, and the intellectual guilds that smother debate. But the Occupy movement, with its general assemblies and consensus process, ironically mirrors the centralizing impulse it decries. A true movement for liberty and opportunity would champion the individual over the collective, voluntary cooperation over coercion, and local self-government over mass democracy. That is the American tradition worth occupying—the one distilled by Thoreau, not Marx.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The financial crisis and subsequent inequality were driven by government interventions and crony capitalism rather than the failures of a free market.
  2. 02The wealthy elite maintain their power by capturing regulatory systems and securing state-sponsored privileges that eliminate competition from smaller players.
  3. 03True reform requires dismantling the machinery of privilege, such as corporate welfare and central banking, rather than expanding government through redistribution.
  4. 04Restoring American ideals necessitates a radical decentralization of power that prioritizes individual autonomy and voluntary cooperation over collective state action.
