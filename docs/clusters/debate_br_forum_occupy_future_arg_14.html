"The systemic injustices identified by the Occupy movement are caused by political corruption of the market rather than capitalism itself, and the solution lies in reducing state power to eliminate the privileges and barriers that favor the well-connected."

Occupy Wall Street is right about one thing that many defenders of capitalism prefer to ignore: the present system is not a free market. It is a dense web of subsidies, guarantees, tax preferences, licensing regimes, bailouts, zoning rules, monetary interventions, and political favors. But the lead essay draws the wrong lesson from this fact. The problem is not that America has too much capitalism and too little democracy. The problem is that political power has been used to corrupt markets, protect incumbents, and transfer risk from the well-connected to everyone else.

If “everyone, not just the rich, should have the opportunity to get ahead,” then we should be most concerned about barriers to entry. Large banks benefit from implicit guarantees because creditors believe the government will not let them fail. Established corporations hire lawyers and lobbyists to shape regulations that smaller competitors cannot navigate. Occupational licensing protects insiders from competition. Agricultural subsidies benefit politically organized producers. The tax code rewards those able to exploit complexity. Land-use restrictions make housing more expensive. Public schools are often assigned by residence, tying opportunity to real estate wealth. These are not failures of markets; they are failures of politics.

The bailouts remain the great moral scandal of our time. Libertarians warned long before 2008 that “too big to fail” would encourage reckless behavior. If profits belong to private actors but losses fall on taxpayers, the result is not capitalism but corporatism. Many Occupy protesters understand this instinctively. Their anger at bank rescues, Federal Reserve interventions, and revolving-door politics overlaps with the Tea Party’s anger more than either side likes to admit. Both see a governing class that preaches responsibility to ordinary citizens and provides rescue to insiders.

Where I part ways with Occupy is in the assumption that the cure is more state power directed by better people. Concentrated government power is precisely what the wealthy are best positioned to capture. If campaign contributions and lobbying distort policy now, why should we expect a larger regulatory and redistributive apparatus to be immune? The more decisions Washington makes about credit, energy, health care, housing, and industry, the more valuable political influence becomes. A movement against plutocracy should be wary of strengthening the prize plutocrats are competing to control.

This does not mean doing nothing. It means choosing reforms that reduce privilege rather than merely redirecting it. End all bailouts, explicitly and credibly. Break up or shrink institutions whose failure would threaten the system, or require such high capital levels that creditors, not taxpayers, bear the risk. Stop subsidizing mortgage debt through policies that inflate housing prices and encourage leverage. Simplify the tax code by eliminating deductions and preferences that benefit those with accountants. Let failing firms fail through bankruptcy instead of ad hoc rescue. Reduce regulatory complexity that protects incumbents while maintaining clear rules against fraud, force, and deception.

Campaign finance reform is less promising than many imagine. Money will find channels into politics as long as politics controls so much economic value. Disclosure is reasonable; censorship of political speech is dangerous. The better way to reduce the power of money is to reduce the number of favors government can dispense. A simpler, more limited state is harder to game.

Occupy’s emphasis on inequality also needs refinement. Inequality produced by innovation, thrift, or superior service is not an injustice. Inequality produced by privilege, fraud, subsidy, or coercion is. A society in which entrepreneurs can become rich by creating value is different from one in which bankers become rich by exploiting guarantees and complexity. The moral distinction is essential. Condemning “the rich” as a class obscures it.

Opportunity, too, requires more than redistribution. The poor are harmed by failing schools, unsafe neighborhoods, expensive housing, and regulations that make it difficult to start businesses or enter trades. A young person who wants to braid hair, operate a food truck, drive a taxi, or open a small shop should not need permission from entrenched competitors. A family should not be trapped in a bad school because it cannot afford a different ZIP code. A worker should not pay higher prices because tariffs or subsidies protect politically favored industries. These are opportunity issues, and they deserve a place in any honest democratic conversation.

There is also a cultural dimension that neither left nor right should ignore. Debt-fueled consumption, speculative housing expectations, and the belief that prosperity can be separated from production were not confined to Wall Street. Government encouraged borrowing; lenders promoted it; consumers accepted it. Rebuilding a healthy economy requires prudence at every level. That includes banks, households, and governments that have promised more than they can sustainably deliver.

The lead essay asks whether America can build an open, democratic, successful movement to realize its ideals. Yes—but openness must include those who think liberty is one of those ideals. Democracy is not simply collective control over more of life. It is also the protection of individuals and associations from domination, including domination by political majorities. The right to be heard is important; so is the right to act without asking permission from bureaucracies or incumbents.

Occupy has given voice to a real grievance: the game is rigged. But a rigged game is not fixed by handing more tools to the riggers. The future should not belong to Wall Street, but neither should it belong to Washington. It should belong to citizens free to build, trade, save, organize, criticize, and cooperate under rules that are general, transparent, and not for sale.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current economic system is a form of corporatism where political power is used to protect incumbents through subsidies, bailouts, and complex regulations that smaller competitors cannot navigate.
  2. 02Expanding government power and redistribution would only increase the incentive for the wealthy to capture and control the state apparatus for their own benefit.
  3. 03True reform requires eliminating 'too big to fail' guarantees and simplifying the tax and regulatory codes so that creditors and failing firms, rather than taxpayers, bear the risk of loss.
  4. 04The moral focus should be on eliminating inequality produced by privilege and coercion rather than condemning wealth generated through innovation and value creation.
  5. 05Expanding opportunity for the poor requires removing government-imposed barriers to entry, such as occupational licensing and restrictive land-use rules, rather than simply increasing redistribution.
  6. 06A healthy economy requires a cultural return to prudence and production across all levels of society, including households and government, to move away from debt-fueled consumption.
