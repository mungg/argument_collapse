"To minimize the risks of the Internet of Things, connectivity should be treated as a functional necessity rather than an inherent virtue, with a focus on data subtraction and the right to remain offline."

The first rule of the Internet of Things should be: do not connect things unless there is a real reason. A networked heart monitor may be invaluable. A networked bridge sensor may save lives. A networked irrigation system may conserve water. But a dishwasher that wants an account, a toothbrush that uploads brushing habits and a toy that sends a child’s voice to a server are not automatically progress.

Connectivity often serves the manufacturer more than the customer. It creates a continuing relationship after the sale, a stream of data, opportunities for advertising, and sometimes a way to sell subscriptions or supplies. It also creates new ways for products to fail. A simple appliance can last for decades. A “smart” appliance may depend on software updates, remote servers and a company that may lose interest long before the machine wears out.

There are genuine possibilities here. Sensors can help older people remain at home safely. Smart meters can make the electric grid more efficient. Devices that track exercise or sleep can help some people understand their health. But we should stop treating connection as a virtue in itself. Every sensor is also a possible leak. Every wireless feature is also a possible attack surface. Every database is also a temptation.

The best privacy protection is often subtraction. Collect less. Transmit less. Store less. Put a physical switch on microphones and cameras. Let basic functions work without registration. Do not punish customers who choose not to create an online account. Make “offline mode” a consumer right, not a hidden option. If a product stops receiving security updates, consumers should be told plainly, just as they are told about expiration dates on food or medicine.

Regulators should require honesty about what a connected product needs in order to function. If the cloud service is optional, say so. If the device becomes useless without it, say that too. Consumers should ask a simple question before buying: what problem does the network connection solve for me?

The Internet of Things will be healthiest if it includes the right not to participate. Sometimes the smartest object is the one that stays dumb.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Many connected products prioritize manufacturer data collection and advertising over genuine consumer utility.
  2. 02Smart devices introduce new points of failure, such as dependency on remote servers and software updates that may not outlast the physical hardware.
  3. 03Every networked feature increases the security risk by creating potential attack surfaces and data leaks.
  4. 04Privacy should be protected by minimizing data collection and ensuring that basic device functions work without internet registration or accounts.
  5. 05Consumers should have a legal right to an 'offline mode' and be clearly informed about the lifespan of security updates.
  6. 06Regulators should mandate transparency regarding whether a product's functionality is dependent on cloud services.
