"While global class injustices and the plight of migrants are significant, a humane migration policy must also respect the moral reality of national belonging and the democratic right of citizens to maintain a bounded political home."

Lea Ypi’s essay is eloquent, humane, and in important respects correct. It is indefensible that rich democracies sell access to the wealthy while treating desperate migrants with performative harshness. It is shameful when politicians use immigrants as scapegoats for housing shortages, wage stagnation, crime, or public disorder they have failed to address. It is true that many people move because war, poverty, corruption, climate, and global economic forces leave them little real choice.

But Ypi’s argument suffers from a familiar cosmopolitan impatience with the moral reality of national belonging. She asks us to see migration primarily through class and global injustice. Those lenses reveal much. They do not reveal everything. Human beings are not only workers in a global economy. They are also members of particular communities, shaped by language, law, memory, religion, custom, and shared institutions. A border is not only a capitalist device for sorting labor. It is also the boundary of a political home.

That phrase, “political home,” will make some readers nervous. It should not. Homes can be hospitable or cruel, open or fearful, generous or selfish. But a home that belongs to no one in particular becomes difficult to govern democratically. Citizens are not wrong to think they have a special obligation to one another. They pay into common systems, inherit common debts, maintain common institutions, and pass on a civic culture. They vote not as abstract humans but as participants in a historically formed polity.

Ypi invokes Nathan the Wise against civilizational arrogance. Fair enough. No nation has a monopoly on virtue. But rejecting superiority does not require denying difference. Cultures vary in their assumptions about religion and law, men and women, speech and offense, family and individual autonomy, authority and dissent. Liberal democracy depends on habits that are not automatic: tolerance, trust, compromise, respect for law, acceptance of electoral defeat, and willingness to treat strangers as fellow citizens. These habits can be learned by newcomers—millions have done so. But they can also be weakened if a society refuses to name and transmit them.

Progressives often speak as if integration is an insulting demand imposed on migrants. It need not be. Integration is the process by which a society renews itself without dissolving. It asks newcomers to join a common civic project and asks the host society to make room for them as equals. That requires language learning, work, schooling, respect for constitutional norms, and social contact. It also requires limits. A country that receives people faster than it can integrate them may produce parallel societies, resentment, and political backlash.

This is not a fantasy. Across Europe, many citizens have watched neighborhoods change rapidly while governments insisted that only bigots noticed. Some concerns were racist; many were not. Parents worried about schools struggling with multiple languages. Women worried about imported patriarchal practices. Secular liberals worried about religious separatism. Workers worried about labor competition. Jews, Christians, Muslims, and atheists alike worried about communal tensions imported from conflicts abroad. These issues are not solved by saying “class.”

Nor is the welfare state merely a mask for exclusion. It is a moral achievement of bounded solidarity. People support high taxes and redistribution when they believe the system is reciprocal and controlled. If they think access is unlimited or rules are unenforced, support erodes. Ypi argues that the liberal democratic state has often been exclusionary. True. But the answer is not to weaken membership; it is to make membership more inclusive and meaningful.

A serious migration policy would start from duties in concentric circles. We have duties to all human beings: not to abuse them, not to return them to persecution, not to exploit them, not to treat them as vermin. We have stronger duties to refugees whose danger is immediate and severe. We have duties of repair when our own wars or policies have contributed to displacement. But we also have special duties to fellow citizens, especially the poor, elderly, disabled, and young. A government that neglects those duties in the name of universal benevolence will not remain democratic for long.

Ypi worries that accepting restrictionist premises leads progressives down a slippery slope toward persecution. The warning is not baseless. History is full of minorities made into targets during crisis. But there is another slippery slope: when mainstream institutions refuse to regulate migration credibly, citizens turn to parties that will regulate it cruelly. Humane control is not the enemy of decency. It may be its precondition.

What would humane control mean? First, no sale of citizenship. Membership should not be auctioned to investors. Second, strong asylum protection for genuine refugees, with fast decisions and safe legal routes. Third, deportation after due process for those without a right to remain, carried out without humiliation or family cruelty. Fourth, labor migration at levels set democratically, tied to housing, infrastructure, wages, and training. Fifth, equal labor rights for migrants so employers cannot use them to undercut citizens. Sixth, serious integration policy: language, civics, employment, anti-discrimination enforcement, and expectations of loyalty to constitutional norms. Seventh, a foreign policy that reduces displacement rather than moralizing after the fact.

This approach rejects both nativism and borderless moralism. It says that nations may choose whom to admit, but must do so justly. It says citizens may care about cultural continuity, but not racial purity. It says migrants are human beings with rights, not instruments of GDP growth or symbols in elite morality plays. It says democracy requires consent.

The hardest question for Ypi is this: if citizens vote repeatedly for lower immigration, on what democratic grounds may their preference be overridden? One can say they are misinformed. Sometimes they are. One can say elites have manipulated them. Often they have. But democracy cannot mean that voters are sovereign only when they affirm cosmopolitan conclusions. Political leadership should challenge prejudice, but it must also respect durable public judgments about the scale and pace of change.

The right, for its part, must answer its own hard question: will it defend borders without dehumanizing those outside them? Too often it has failed. Too often “control” becomes cruelty, and “culture” becomes ethnicity. That failure does not vindicate Ypi’s cosmopolitanism. It demands a better conservatism: one that knows the difference between a nation and a tribe, between prudence and fear, between a border and a barricade.

The real border crisis is a crisis of trust. Citizens do not trust governments to tell the truth about numbers, costs, crime, integration, or labor markets. Migrants do not trust states to treat them fairly. Poor citizens do not trust elites who celebrate diversity while insulating themselves from its pressures. Rich countries do not trust poor countries to govern well; poor countries do not trust rich countries to act justly. Into this distrust step smugglers, demagogues, and profiteers.

Ypi is right that we need courage. But courage is not only the courage to affirm universal equality. It is also the courage to say that particular loyalties matter, that democratic consent matters, and that a humane border is still a border. A world in which nobody is forced to leave home is a noble aspiration. Until it exists, political communities must decide how many people they can welcome, under what conditions, and with what obligations on all sides. That decision should be generous. It should not be unlimited. And it should belong, finally, to the people whose common life is at stake.

"While the lead essay correctly identifies the class biases and injustices of current border regimes, it wrongly dismisses the importance of national membership, which is essential for maintaining the solidarity and institutions required for a functioning democratic welfare state."

Lea Ypi is right to condemn the cruelty, hypocrisy, and class bias of contemporary border regimes. She is right that rich people often experience borders as inconveniences while poor people experience them as walls. She is right that selling residence to millionaires while chaining deportees is a grotesque picture of our age. She is also right that migration debates are too often conducted in cultural shorthand, with “integration” serving as a polite substitute for suspicion.

But Ypi’s essay makes one large mistake: it treats the political salience of membership as something progressives should overcome rather than something they must democratize and defend. The nation-state is not merely an exclusionary machine. It is also the most effective institution yet devised for making solidarity legally enforceable. Welfare states, labor law, public education, health systems, pensions, social housing, democratic accountability, and redistribution have all depended on bounded political communities whose members believe they owe one another more than market exchange.

This is not an argument for ethnic nationalism. It is an argument for democratic membership. The distinction matters. A polity can define membership in civic, inclusive, and pluralist terms while still maintaining rules about admission. It can reject racism and xenophobia while recognizing that borders are not morally irrelevant. It can welcome refugees and immigrants while insisting that the pace and composition of migration be subject to democratic consent.

Ypi’s critique of “left restrictionism” is powerful when aimed at politicians who borrow the language of the far right. When a social democratic leader speaks of an “island of strangers,” something has gone badly wrong. But not every concern about migration is a surrender to ethnocentrism. Some concerns arise from the same egalitarian commitments Ypi invokes.

Take housing. If a country has failed for decades to build enough homes, additional population growth will increase competition for scarce rentals. That does not make migrants responsible for the housing crisis; landlords, planning failures, speculative finance, and austerity bear far more blame. But politics occurs under real constraints. A low-income citizen facing eviction does not experience “global capitalism” in the abstract. She experiences twenty people bidding for one flat. If progressives tell her that migration has no bearing on her situation, they appear dishonest.

The same is true of public services. In a well-governed country, migration can strengthen the tax base, staff hospitals, revive towns, and support aging societies. In a badly governed one, where schools are full, local authorities are broke, and health systems are overstretched, rapid population change can intensify pressure. The correct answer is investment, not scapegoating. But investment requires time, capacity, and political legitimacy. Democratic governments must be able to regulate inflows so that institutions can adapt.

Ypi might reply that these shortages are produced by austerity and oligarchic capture, not migration. Often they are. But this does not settle the question. A welfare state must make choices about sequencing. To say that migration interacts with scarcity is not to blame migrants for scarcity. It is to acknowledge that solidarity is easier to sustain when citizens trust that the state can manage change.

The word “trust” is unfashionable in some radical circles because it can sound like a euphemism for cultural conformity. Yet trust is not a trivial ornament of democracy. People support redistribution when they believe rules are fair, contributions are broadly shared, and institutions are not being gamed by the powerful. If they come to believe that borders are uncontrolled, asylum rules are unenforced, or labor markets are being deliberately flooded to lower wages, they will withdraw support not only for immigration but for government itself. The beneficiaries will not be migrants. They will be the anti-tax right.

That is why humane enforcement matters. A system that cannot distinguish between refugees, students, workers, family members, tourists, trafficked persons, and people with no legal claim will eventually lose public consent. The answer is not brutality; it is competence. Asylum claims should be processed quickly and fairly. People with strong claims should receive protection and integration support. Those without a right to remain should be returned with dignity and legal safeguards. Labor migration should be transparent and tied to enforceable standards. Family unity should be respected. Long-term residents should have a realistic path to citizenship. None of this is inconsistent with democratic control.

Indeed, a state that refuses to enforce any boundary often empowers the worst actors. Smugglers thrive when legal pathways are inadequate and procedures are chaotic. Employers benefit when undocumented workers are present but afraid. Far-right parties grow when citizens see a gap between official rules and practical reality. A progressive migration policy must reduce that gap, not widen it.

Ypi is also too quick to describe migration primarily as forced. Many people move under constraint, but constraint comes in degrees. Some flee war or persecution. Some escape destitution. Some seek higher wages, education, adventure, family reunion, or a better future for children. Their aspirations are legitimate. But if every serious economic motive becomes a form of compulsion for which receiving states are responsible, the distinction between refugee protection and general migration collapses. That collapse may sound morally generous, but it risks weakening the special obligations owed to those fleeing persecution and violence.

Nor is it enough to say that rich countries helped create the conditions people flee. Sometimes they did: through war, exploitation, emissions, debt regimes, or support for corrupt allies. But causal responsibility varies. The world is full of bad governments, civil conflicts, demographic pressures, climate disasters, and domestic policy failures for which no particular receiving state is chiefly responsible. International justice demands aid, fairer trade, climate finance, peacebuilding, and refugee resettlement. It does not require any one democracy to accept unlimited immigration.

This is where the language of “all subjected to coercive power” becomes difficult. Migrants outside a territory are affected by border laws, but they are not governed in the same way as residents. If being affected by a law were sufficient to confer full democratic standing, then almost everyone would have claims on almost every state, because trade, climate, financial regulation, sanctions, military alliances, and monetary policy all have cross-border effects. That insight should push us toward stronger international institutions. It should not erase the special obligations citizens have to co-members with whom they share a coercive legal order.

Still, the defenders of borders must confront Ypi’s strongest challenge: contemporary systems often regulate poor migrants harshly while courting wealthy ones. A democratic membership view should reject that bargain. Citizenship should not be for sale. Investor visas that amount to passport markets corrode the civic meaning of membership and inflate assets in ways that hurt ordinary residents. If a country needs investment, it should tax wealth, borrow for public purposes, or build industrial policy—not auction belonging.

A social democratic border policy would therefore look different from both neoliberal openness and nationalist closure. It would end golden passports. It would expand refugee resettlement and safe humanitarian routes. It would set labor migration levels through democratic planning connected to housing, training, wages, and public investment. It would require equal pay and rights for migrant workers so they cannot be used to undercut domestic labor. It would regularize long-term undocumented residents who have built lives in the country. It would permit local voting rights after a period of residence. It would invest heavily in integration: language, schools, civic education, anti-discrimination enforcement, and pathways to citizenship. And it would maintain credible rules about admission and return.

This position will satisfy neither open-borders cosmopolitans nor hard restrictionists. But it has one advantage: it takes democracy seriously. Democracy is not only a universal aspiration; it is also a practice of collective self-government among people who must live with the consequences of decisions. If citizens believe that migration policy is removed from their control, they will not become more cosmopolitan. They will become more resentful.

Ypi closes with Enlightenment universalism. I share the aspiration. But the Enlightenment also gave us constitutional government, popular sovereignty, and the idea that legitimate law requires the consent of the governed. Universal values do not float above institutions; they must be housed somewhere. For now, the democratic welfare state remains the best house we have. It needs renovation, expansion, and protection from oligarchy. It should be generous to newcomers and honest about global injustice. But it cannot survive if membership is treated only as a prejudice to be overcome.

The real border crisis is indeed a crisis of liberal democracy. Yet liberal democracy will not be restored by denying the moral and practical importance of borders. It will be restored by making them lawful, humane, egalitarian, and democratically accountable.

"While class analysis is essential to understanding the border crisis, a viable progressive politics must also address the practical necessity of democratic border governance and institutional capacity to maintain public trust."

Lea Ypi is right to insist that what is usually called a “border crisis” is not, at bottom, a crisis caused by migrants. The spectacular images—the boats, the fences, the encampments, the deportation flights—invite us to mistake a symptom for a cause. People do not risk the Darién Gap, the Mediterranean, the Channel, the Sahara, or the Sonoran Desert because mobility is a lifestyle preference. They move because violence, poverty, repression, family separation, ecological stress, or sheer blocked possibility has made staying intolerable. And they arrive in states whose political classes have spent decades weakening public housing, labor protections, asylum administration, and social insurance, only to blame newcomers for the scarcity that follows. On this, Ypi’s argument is powerful and necessary.

She is also right that the class character of contemporary mobility is obscene. The world has become increasingly open to capital, credentials, and inherited advantage, while becoming increasingly punitive toward those who travel with nothing but need. The same states that criminalize the poor migrant court the investor migrant; the same governments that warn of “queue jumping” create premium queues for the rich. Golden visas, citizenship-by-investment programs, and fast-track residence for the highly skilled expose the fiction that restrictive migration policy is simply about preserving civic solidarity. Often it is about sorting human beings by market value.

But if Ypi’s essay clarifies what is morally corrupt in the current order, it risks understating a different problem: democratic border politics cannot be reconstructed by class analysis alone. Class is indispensable, but it is not exhaustive. The fact that anti-migrant politics is frequently manipulated by elites does not mean that all concerns about scale, pace, institutional capacity, or civic trust are mere mystification. Nor does the fact that borders are unjustly administered mean that borders as such have no democratic function. The challenge is to defend migrants without pretending that membership, consent, and public capacity are politically irrelevant.

A humane politics has to hold two truths together. First, affluent democracies have far greater obligations to migrants and refugees than they typically admit. Their wealth is entangled with colonial extraction, carbon emissions, military interventions, unequal trade, and international financial rules that have constrained poorer states’ choices. They also need migrants: demographically, economically, culturally, and morally. Second, even just societies would need institutions to regulate entry, allocate resources, protect labor standards, and sustain the confidence that makes redistribution possible. The alternative to cruel borders is not no governance; it is better governance.

This distinction matters because state capacity is not a right-wing fantasy. When asylum systems are backlogged for years, everyone suffers: refugees remain in limbo, receiving communities lose trust, and opportunistic politicians turn administrative failure into civilizational panic. When cities receive large numbers of newcomers without housing, school places, health services, language support, or work authorization, the result is not solidarity but competition under conditions of scarcity. That competition is not the fault of migrants, but neither is it imaginary. A low-wage citizen waiting for social housing may be wrong to blame a refugee family, but she is not wrong to notice that the state has failed both of them.

Ypi warns that the left should not accept the right’s framing. I agree. But there is a difference between accepting xenophobic premises and acknowledging institutional constraints. If progressives respond to every concern about migration levels as racism in disguise, they abandon the terrain on which democratic legitimacy is built. Voters can be misled, but they are not simply raw material for enlightenment by better theorists. Democratic politics requires persuasion, and persuasion begins by taking seriously the lived experience of people who feel that decisions affecting their neighborhoods, wages, schools, and public services are made elsewhere.

The stronger progressive claim is not that borders do not matter. It is that the current border regime produces disorder by design. In the United States, decades of congressional failure have created a system in which employers rely on unauthorized labor while politicians denounce the workers who supply it. In Europe, safe legal routes remain narrow, asylum processing is uneven, and responsibility is pushed onto border states or poorer neighbors. The 2024 EU Pact on Migration and Asylum promised order, but much of its energy went into accelerated procedures, detention-like border facilities, and externalization. Britain’s Rwanda scheme became a symbol of performative deterrence: expensive, legally contested, and morally degrading. These policies do not restore control; they dramatize control while deepening vulnerability.

The central labor-market point in Ypi’s essay is exactly right: employers do not simply want migrants; they want migrants with fewer rights. A worker tied to a single employer, afraid of deportation, excluded from welfare, or unable to unionize is easier to exploit. That exploitation then undercuts standards for everyone. The answer is not to exclude the worker but to remove the vulnerability: broad work authorization, strong wage enforcement, firewalls between labor inspection and immigration enforcement, portable visas, sectoral bargaining, and serious penalties for employers who use status as a weapon. If undocumented workers can report wage theft without risking removal, they become less useful as tools of discipline against citizen workers.

Still, the politics of solidarity cannot be reduced to shared material interest. People also ask who “we” are, what obligations we owe one another, and how common institutions can survive disagreement. These questions can be answered badly—with blood-and-soil nationalism, religious exclusion, or racial hierarchy. But they cannot be avoided. The postwar welfare states Ypi invokes were never purely class machines; they were also national projects, often built around narratives of shared sacrifice, public contribution, and mutual obligation. Their exclusions were real and often shameful. Yet their solidarities were real too. The task is not to pretend that democratic belonging is obsolete, but to widen and revise it.

There are examples of such widening. The reception of Ukrainians after Russia’s full-scale invasion showed that European states can move quickly when they choose: temporary protection, work rights, school access, and public sympathy were mobilized at scale. The contrast with Syrians, Afghans, Sudanese, Eritreans, or Palestinians revealed racial and geopolitical double standards. But it also demonstrated a practical lesson: when legal status is clear, work is permitted, and local authorities are supported, reception is less chaotic. The lesson should be universalized, not resented. Protection should not depend on being European, Christian, or geopolitically convenient.

A just migration politics would therefore begin with administrative seriousness. Asylum claims should be decided quickly, fairly, and with legal representation—not through years of destitution followed by sudden removal, and not through sham “safe third country” arrangements. People with valid claims should receive stable status and family reunification. People without valid claims should not be brutalized, but a system that never reaches decisions will lose public support and empower authoritarians. Regularization should be available for long-settled residents, especially those with families and work histories. At the same time, future pathways must be realistic enough that irregularity is not the only accessible route.

Second, receiving states must invest where migration is experienced. National governments often reap the macroeconomic benefits of immigration while municipalities bear the immediate costs. Housing, schools, clinics, translation services, and legal aid are not sentimental extras; they are the infrastructure of integration. If the public sees newcomers housed only by displacing the already insecure, the far right wins. If new arrivals are matched with visible investment that benefits existing residents too, solidarity becomes materially plausible.

Third, the hierarchy of mobility should be attacked at the top as well as the bottom. Golden passports and investor visas should be abolished or radically curtailed. Universities and employers should not be allowed to use international students and migrant workers as revenue streams or disposable labor. Points systems that select only the already advantaged should be balanced by humanitarian corridors, family migration, climate-related protection, and pathways for essential workers whose social value is not captured by salary thresholds. Care workers, farmworkers, cleaners, drivers, and construction workers sustain wealthy societies; admitting them as permanently subordinate guests is indefensible.

Fourth, the external causes of displacement must be addressed without pretending that “development” is a magic substitute for migration. Rich states should expand climate finance, debt relief, public-health investment, and fair trade rules; they should stop exporting arms into conflicts while lamenting refugees from those conflicts; they should support regional refugee hosts, who continue to bear a disproportionate share of responsibility. But development can initially increase mobility by giving people the resources to move. The goal should not be to trap people where they are. It should be to make staying and moving both genuinely free choices.

Ypi’s deepest insight is that freedom of movement cannot be separated from freedom from domination. A world in which the poor are immobilized by visa regimes, displaced by economic restructuring, then criminalized when they move is not a liberal world in any meaningful sense. Yet freedom also requires institutions capable of commanding trust. The border crisis is real not because migrants are invading, but because our systems are hypocritical, underbuilt, and unjust. They promise universal rights while rationing them by passport, wealth, and race; they promise control while producing illegality; they promise solidarity while starving the public realm.

The left should refuse the lie that migrants are the enemy. But it should also refuse the temptation to answer border cruelty with border abstraction. The humane alternative is neither fortress nor fantasy: it is a politics that expands legal mobility, protects refugees, empowers all workers, funds integration, democratizes membership, and confronts the global inequalities that make movement desperate. Such a politics would not abolish every border tomorrow. It would make borders less like instruments of class rule and more like accountable institutions constrained by justice. That may fall short of cosmopolitan redemption, but it would be a beginning worthy of Nathan’s wager on the future.

"While Lea Ypi correctly identifies the economic drivers of migration, her class-based analysis fails by dismissing the legitimate cultural and social attachments that are essential for building political solidarity and successful integration."

Lea Ypi's essay is strongest in its historical contextualization of how migration came to be framed as a crisis and in its tracing of this shift to changes in global economic governance. Her account of neoliberal restructuring, the Washington Consensus, and the resulting displacement of smallholder farmers and public sector workers in the Global South is compelling and largely accurate. However, I want to challenge her dismissal of cultural and social considerations in understanding migration's political impact.

Ypi writes that borders are a problem "not because of any civilizational puzzle to solve prior to establishing who belongs and who doesn't. Nor because there is a problem of cultural adaptation by some groups compared to others." This dismissal is too quick. Even if we accept that migration patterns are driven primarily by economic and political forces rather than cultural traits, the question of how societies integrate migrants and how integration affects social trust remains politically significant.

The argument is not that some cultures are superior to others or that migration necessarily creates conflict. The argument is that meaningful solidarity—the mutual recognition that Ypi correctly identifies as central to democratic life—does not emerge automatically from shared material interests. Workers share interests across borders, but they also develop identities, expectations, and forms of life that shape how they understand their relationship to political communities.

This is not mere tribalism to be overcome. When people value particular ways of life, particular forms of social organization, particular relationships between generations and communities, this is not simply false consciousness or manipulated ideology. These values often reflect legitimate attachments to practices, relationships, and institutions that give meaning to people's lives. A progressive politics that dismisses these attachments as mere prejudice will not succeed in building the solidarity it seeks.

The "trade-off" between openness and solidarity that Ypi discusses is not simply a conservative talking point. There are genuine tensions between maintaining generous welfare states and maintaining political community open to unlimited migration. Ypi's argument that we should expand representation and transform citizenship to resolve these tensions is correct but incomplete. It does not tell us how to do this in societies where significant portions of the population hold attachments that cannot be reduced to economic interest.

I do not mean to suggest that these attachments justify exclusionary policies. Far from it. But effective progressive politics must engage with them rather than dismissing them as false consciousness or right-wing manipulation. When Ypi describes Keir Starmer's "island of strangers" rhetoric as capitulation, she is right. But understanding why that rhetoric resonates requires more than class analysis.

Consider the communities left behind by deindustrialization that Ypi correctly identifies as key to the rise of anti-immigrant sentiment. These communities did not simply lose jobs; they lost the social infrastructure—the factories, unions, churches, and neighborhood institutions—that organized collective life. Rebuilding this infrastructure is not simply a matter of economic redistribution. It requires forms of social organization that give people meaningful roles and relationships.

Migrants can be part of rebuilding this social fabric, but not if they are introduced into communities that have already lost the organizational capacity to integrate anyone. This suggests that the sequence matters: social infrastructure must be rebuilt before or alongside migration, not assumed to follow automatically from economic growth.

Ypi is correct that the left has too often capitulated to exclusionary discourse. But the alternative is not simply to articulate a more sophisticated class analysis. It is to develop a politics that can honor both the universal claims of human dignity that she rightly emphasizes and the particular attachments that give people reasons to build common life together.

"While Lea Ypi correctly identifies migration as a symptom of global class conflict and neoliberal failure, her proposal to abandon the framework of national political membership is an impractical strategy that ignores the moral and institutional importance of the democratic state."

Lea Ypi’s incisive essay rightly names the real border crisis as a crisis of liberal democracy itself, not of migrant numbers. Her diagnosis of a global order in which class conflict, neoliberal restructuring, and the commodification of citizenship drive forced displacement is a necessary corrective to the culturalist hysteria dominating public debate. Ypi’s linking of the Washington Consensus’s wreckage, the selling of Golden Visas to the wealthy, and the criminalisation of poor migrants is an indictment that progressives ignore at their peril. Yet for all its analytical power, the essay’s prescriptive horizon—a class-first internationalism that rejects the membership frame entirely—underestimates the immediate political terrain the left actually inhabits and the moral weight that legitimate, bounded political communities retain even for those who deplore the current order.

The strength of Ypi’s argument lies in its refusal to treat migration as a problem of cultural maladaptation. She demonstrates that anti-immigrant sentiment rises even as numbers fall, revealing a political and economic malaise manipulated by elites. Her account of citizenship becoming a luxury commodity—Trump’s $5 million “Platinum Card” juxtaposed with shackled deportees—captures the obscene asymmetry of a system that turns human mobility into a class privilege. And she is correct that social-democratic parties that mimic nativism will never outbid the far right; Keir Starmer’s “island of strangers” rhetoric is as electorally shortsighted as it is morally bankrupt.

However, Ypi’s reconstruction of the Enlightenment ideal through Lessing’s Nathan the Wise, while moving, also reveals the limits of her approach. She celebrates Nathan’s forward-looking cosmopolitanism, his refusal to define himself by nation or creed. But note: Nathan returns home. He is no permanent nomad; his rootedness in Jerusalem, his particular relationships and obligations, are not incidental to his humanity. The play itself does not ask us to transcend all membership, only to refuse its descent into tribal supremacism. A democratic politics that simply dismisses the emotional and institutional reality of political membership—the fact that people understand their moral obligations as running first to those with whom they share a scheme of social cooperation—risks floating free of the very communities whose solidarity it needs. Ypi’s argument that the moral weight of the citizen–migrant distinction is “merely contingent” and parasitic on an arbitrary territorial association is philosophically bold, but it overlooks why, for many, that contingency has felt morally significant for generations: because democratic states, however imperfect, have been the primary arenas in which ordinary people have fought for and won rights, built public goods, and forged bonds of mutual responsibility. Dismissing that history as a capitalist ruse demobilises the very working-class agents Ypi wants to recruit.

A second difficulty is the article’s treatment of culture. Ypi is right that the “clash of civilisations” frame is a dangerous fiction, and that class cuts across national lines. Yet her insistence that all political conflicts be read as class conflicts between domestic and international elites and workers flattens an awkward reality. In many receiving societies, tensions over migration are not solely manufactured by capital; they also reflect genuine clashes over liberal norms—gender equality, religious freedom, sexual orientation—that cannot be reduced to economic grievance. When a social-democratic politician in Germany worries that parallel societies harbour illiberal attitudes towards women or LGBTQ people, the response cannot simply be that this is a distraction from class war. The working-class voters who express those anxieties are not only dupes of right-wing propaganda; they often live in the neighbourhoods where such tensions are most acutely felt. An effective left politics must offer a language that addresses both economic precarity and the defence of liberal democratic culture, without ceding the latter to chauvinists. Ypi’s framework, by framing any acknowledgment of cultural friction as a capitulation to the membership frame, leaves the left ill-equipped for this task.

The most pressing challenge, however, concerns electoral strategy and institutional reality. Ypi concedes that rejecting dominant exclusionary discourses “even at the price of a short-term loss” may be necessary. But the repeated short-term losses of left parties across Europe over the past decade have not been mere blips; they have delivered power to ethnonationalist governments that have devastated asylum systems, dismantled welfare states, and shredded labour protections far more aggressively than any cautious social democrat. Telling the left to abandon the electoral arena of parliamentary democracy and shift to “counter-hegemonic projects” beyond narrow cycles sounds principled, but it neglects the fact that state power—control over borders, labour law, public investment—is exercised now. Refusing to engage with border politics from inside the state means ceding that power entirely to the right. The alternative cannot be a purely outsidist politics of transnational worker solidarity which, while inspiring, remains fragmentary. The “Make Amazon Pay” campaign exemplifies coordination across borders, but it is a far cry from transforming the global migration regime, and its achievements depend precisely on the legal frameworks of the states where strikes occur. Without a strategy to win and wield state power, the left’s cosmopolitanism risks becoming, to borrow Craig Calhoun’s phrase, the class consciousness of frequent-travelling activists rather than a politics of the majority.

What might a more plausible left response look like? It would take Ypi’s class analysis seriously without jettisoning the democratic state as an irredeemable site of capitalist control. It would defend the right to asylum and legal pathways for migration while acknowledging that any viable welfare state requires some capacity to plan public services and housing, which in turn implies a managed migration system—one that is transparent, humane, and decoupled from the cruelty of deportation spectacle, but still a system. It would fight against the commodification of citizenship while recognising that political membership can, in better democratic conditions, serve as a tool for inclusion rather than exclusion. And it would invest massive resources in integration, in language training, in anti-discrimination enforcement, and in rebuilding public infrastructure so that higher immigration does not become a zero-sum competition for scarce social goods. This is not Ypi’s vision of a world where nobody is forced to leave their home; that remains a distant horizon. In the meantime, the left must govern the borders we have, while relentlessly attacking the global economic injustices that make those borders lethal.

Ypi’s essay performs an essential task by exposing the hypocrisy of liberal states that preach universality while erecting fortress walls for the poor and open gangplanks for the rich. Her call to see migration as a symptom of class war is a vital counterweight to nativist panic. Yet the left cannot simply stand on the right side of history and wait for the long run. A politics that insists on the total overcoming of the membership frame risks making the perfect the enemy of the good—and in that interval, the very vulnerable people she seeks to defend will continue to drown in the Adriatic, as Ben the Lame did, while liberal democracies drift further into oligarchy. The real border crisis is indeed a crisis of democracy, but the democratic repair must begin where people actually are: inside states, with their imperfect but real collective identities, their constrained but still-useable institutions, and their desperate need for a politics that offers both security and solidarity.

"The essay advocates for a "civic integrationist" approach to migration that balances material labor market realities with the legitimate cultural and national attachments of citizens, rejecting both radical internationalism and nativist exclusion."

Lea Ypi has written a powerful and often persuasive polemic that forces a long overdue reckoning with the class dimensions of migration. She is right that the selling of golden visas and the brutal treatment of poor migrants are linked, that left parties have often betrayed their internationalist principles, and that the neoliberal order has immiserated millions. But her essay, in its drive for theoretical purity, ends up offering a false choice: either class politics as she defines it, or capitulation to right-wing culturalism. There is a third way, one that takes seriously both the material realities of labour markets and the legitimate attachments people have to place, language, and national community. This alternative, which we might call civic integrationism, does not require a choice between class and culture; it demands that we integrate both into a pragmatic, humane, and democratic migration policy.

Ypi’s intellectual hero, Nathan the Wise, is a figure of reconciliation who refuses to claim superiority for any one tradition. But Nathan’s wisdom is not just in his cosmopolitan outlook; it is in his patience and his willingness to engage with the concrete relationships around him. He does not demand that the sultan abolish all religious distinctions; he suggests that the proof of a tradition’s value lies in the long future of how people live together. That is a practical wisdom, not a revolutionary one. Applied to migration, it suggests that we should be less concerned with abolishing borders than with building the institutions and social practices that can make diverse societies work over time. That means accepting that borders will exist for the foreseeable future, but insisting that they be governed by principles of justice, transparency, and respect for human dignity.

The central empirical claim of Ypi’s essay is that migration is “rarely the realization of free choice” but a consequence of a broken order. This is partly true, but it treats the world as a static system of push and pull factors rather than a dynamic web of individual aspirations and social networks. Many migrants from the same village, facing similar economic conditions, will stay while others will go. The ones who go are not mere products of their class position; they are people with particular life plans, family obligations, and personal courage. A just policy must make room for that human variety. The current asylum system is indeed broken, and the distinction between “economic migrants” and “genuine refugees” is often cruelly and arbitrarily administered. But the solution is not to abolish the distinction; it is to reform the system so that it can make fair, timely, and individualised assessments, and so that it includes robust legal pathways for labour migration that respect the agency of those who want to improve their lives through work.

The welfare state objection is not as easily dismissed as Ypi suggests. It is one thing to note that most migrants are net contributors over their lifetimes, and that remittances exceed foreign aid—these are important correctives to nativist claims. But the aggregate net fiscal impact says little about the distributional effects in particular sectors and localities. In the UK, for example, the sharp rise in net migration in 2022–23, while overall beneficial for the economy, did put pressure on rental markets and primary school places in certain towns. A responsible government cannot simply wave this away with macro statistics; it must be seen to manage the pace of change and to invest in public services accordingly. The failure to do so has been a key driver of anti-immigration sentiment, and telling voters that their concerns are merely “false consciousness” is a recipe for a resentful backlash. The left does not need to validate xenophobia to acknowledge that rapid demographic change can strain social cohesion and that managing it is a legitimate function of a democratic state.

Ypi’s class-first framework also overlooks the reality that integration is a two-way street that requires mutual adaptation. She is scathing about language and civic knowledge tests, calling them “insurmountable obstacles,” but requiring a basic competence in the common language of a country and an understanding of its laws and democratic norms is not an act of oppression; it is an investment in the individual’s ability to participate fully in society. The Nordic countries, often held up as paragons of social democracy, maintain just such requirements alongside generous welfare states. The goal is not to create “second-class members” but to ensure that everyone has the tools to exercise voice and to negotiate the society they have joined. To abandon integration policy entirely would be to abandon migrant workers to the very isolation and exploitation that Ypi decries.

A genuine alternative to both the Ypi’s maximalist internationalism and the nativist right would include several elements. First, it would combine an opening of legal migration channels with a commitment to invest in the public square—housing, health care, education—so that the benefits of migration are widely shared. This means taxes on high incomes and wealth to fund services, and it means cracking down on employers who undercut wages by exploiting irregular workers. Second, it would reform international trade and investment agreements to include enforceable labour and environmental standards, reducing the “race to the bottom” that displaces workers in both sending and receiving countries. Third, it would maintain an asylum system rooted in international law but substantially increase resettlement and community sponsorship programmes so that the burden is not borne disproportionately by neighbouring countries. Fourth, it would reject the commodification of citizenship—golden visas must go—and instead create earned pathways to citizenship that reward time, work, and civic commitment. Finally, it would foster a national narrative based on shared civic values rather than ethnic sameness, an inclusive patriotism that can coexist with cultural diversity.

Ypi’s own concluding vision—a world where nobody is forced to leave—is a distant horizon. In the meantime, we must govern the world as it is, with all its imperfect institutions and stubborn human loyalties. That means building a broad coalition that includes not only organised labour but also the many voters who are neither xenophobes nor radical cosmopolitans, people who want fair rules, humane treatment of refugees, and a sense that their own communities are not being left behind. Such a coalition is not built by lecturing people about class consciousness or by dismissing their cultural attachments as the product of right-wing propaganda. It is built by listening, by offering practical solutions that make a tangible difference in their lives, and by showing that a well-managed migration system is not a threat to national community but a precondition for its renewal in a global age. That, in the end, may be the truest expression of Nathan’s wisdom: not the grand refusal of identities, but the slow, patient work of making those identities capacious enough to include the stranger.

"While Lea Ypi correctly identifies the class-based cruelty of current border regimes, her rejection of bounded political membership is philosophically and politically flawed because democratic citizenship is the necessary precondition for disciplining capital and achieving social justice."

Lea Ypi's essay does something rare and valuable: it refuses the terms of debate that both the populist right and the center-left have settled into. The juxtaposition she draws out — Trump's chained deportees broadcast on social media alongside his "Platinum Card" auctioning citizenship for five million dollars — captures something essential about the present moment that neither the immigration-skeptical left nor the humanitarian liberal quite manages to name. The border is not a wall; it is a sieve calibrated by class. The cruelty visited on the destitute traveler and the welcome extended to the wealthy investor are not separate phenomena but two faces of the same regime. Any honest analysis of contemporary migration politics has to start there, and Ypi is right to insist on it.

I also think she is correct, against a certain tendency on the Wagenknecht-Starmer axis of European politics, that the "trade-off thesis" — solidarity for natives requires restriction for foreigners — rests on an idealized picture of the welfare state that the welfare state itself does not deliver. The actually existing British, German, or Italian state is not a Bernsteinian cooperative being undermined by foreign arrivals; it is an entity that has spent four decades transferring risk downward and rents upward, and whose redistributive capacities were hollowed out long before the small-boat crossings became politically salient. The migrant is not the agent of austerity. Anyone who has watched a hospital trust be sold to private equity, or a council estate demolished to free land for luxury flats, knows where the welfare state actually went.

Where I want to press on Ypi's argument is not at the diagnosis but at the inference she draws from it. From the (true) claim that the class character of border regimes is obscene, and the (also true) claim that capital benefits from rightless workers, she slides toward the conclusion that the proper progressive stance is to reject "the reduction of democracy to membership" tout court — to treat any account of bounded political community as already a concession to ethnonationalism. This is, I think, both philosophically too quick and politically self-defeating.

Consider the historical record Ypi herself invokes. The struggles she celebrates — workers' movements for universal suffrage, women's struggles for political rights, anticolonial independence, civil rights — were not struggles against the form of bounded citizenship. They were struggles to be included in it. The suffragettes did not want to abolish the franchise; they wanted to be inside it. Anticolonial movements did not demand the dissolution of political community into cosmopolitan flux; they demanded sovereign states of their own. The Indian or Algerian or Ghanaian who fought against empire was, in the most precise sense, demanding a border — a line that marked where one people would govern itself rather than be governed by another. Ypi's own beautiful memoir of growing up in Albania describes a country whose people fought, at terrible cost, for the right to constitute themselves as a free political community. To treat membership as such as the enemy is to misread the emancipatory tradition she wants to recover.

Bernstein's wager, which Ypi cites approvingly, was precisely that democratic citizenship could discipline capital because it bounded a demos that could act collectively. The mid-twentieth-century welfare states — the "rare and fortunate historical episodes" she mentions in passing — were not accidents. They depended on the capacity of a political community to tax its capital, conscript its young, and distribute its surplus, and that capacity in turn depended on a relatively settled answer to the question of who "we" were. One can dislike this fact; one cannot wish it away. The cosmopolitanism of Nathan the Wise is morally luminous, but Nathan's Jerusalem is not a polity that redistributes income through progressive taxation. The institutions we actually have for converting class struggle into social rights are national institutions, and they work, when they work, through the very membership Ypi treats as the obstacle.

This matters because Ypi's prescription — "fighting for an expansion of political representation beyond inherited citizenship," refusing to "separate problems of admission from problems of integration" — has, in the world as it is, a predictable consequence: it concedes the question of who decides to the right. The far right's electoral success in Europe and North America rests not principally on racism (though racism is present) but on a perception, widespread among voters who are not themselves racist, that mainstream parties have stopped treating the polity as something the polity controls. When Starmer's "island of strangers" rhetoric is the only alternative to a position that appears to refuse any account of legitimate border policy whatsoever, voters who would prefer neither are forced into a choice. Ypi acknowledges this in passing — "in a contest on ethnocentrism and immigrant scapegoating, the left will never win against the right" — but the conclusion she draws, that the left should therefore change the subject to class, underestimates how much the demand for democratic control over membership is itself a class demand. Working-class voters are not confused when they ask who lives here and on what terms; they are asking whether the political community is still theirs to shape.

A more powerful version of Ypi's argument would, I think, distinguish more sharply between the form of bounded citizenship and its current substance. The form — that there exists a political community with the right to determine its own composition and to extend rights to those it admits — is not dispensable; it is the precondition for any project that wants to "shift the costs of economic decisions from workers to capital." The substance — what kinds of admission, on what criteria, with what protections, against what background of international obligation — is precisely what is up for political contestation. On substance, almost everything Ypi says is right: golden visas are an obscenity; integration regimes that condemn people to permanent second-class status are cruel and corrosive; the EU's externalization of migration control to Libyan militias is indefensible; the structural sources of displacement (debt, austerity, climate, the wars she rightly names from the Balkans to Iraq to Libya) demand redress at source. A left that argued all of this while also taking seriously the demand for democratic regulation of admission — including against the wealthy, whose mobility is far more politically corrosive than the poor's — would not be conceding to the right. It would be doing what the right pretends to do and never does: reclaiming the polity from capital.

Ypi ends with the warning that "first they will come for irregular migrants, then for resident noncitizens, then for citizens whose names are Mohamed and Abdallah." She is right to issue it. The historical pattern is real, and on this point I would not soften her language by a syllable. But the answer to that pattern is not the dissolution of the demos into cosmopolitan abstraction. It is the construction of a demos that includes Mohamed and Abdallah as full members, that taxes the man with the Platinum Card, and that knows the difference. That is harder than either open borders or closed ones. It is also, I suspect, the only proposition that can actually win.

"While Lea Ypi correctly identifies the global economic roots of the migration crisis, her proposal to reject national membership in favor of borderless class solidarity is a mistake because it undermines the democratic nation-state, which is the only effective institutional mechanism for constraining capital and providing a welfare state."

Lea Ypi’s sweeping essay offers a profound and necessary corrective to the dominant narratives surrounding global migration. By shifting the focus away from the fabricated culture wars of the right and the defensive, apologetic triangulations of the center-left, Ypi rightly identifies the "border crisis" not as a clash of civilizations, but as a symptom of a violently unequal global economic order. She is entirely correct that the same neoliberal consensus that demands the uninhibited movement of capital also produces the very crises—deindustrialization, debt traps, and the dismantling of the public sector—that force the most vulnerable people in the Global South to abandon their homes. Furthermore, her indictment of the gross hypocrisy of contemporary citizenship—where a desperately poor asylum seeker is chained and deported while a billionaire can purchase a "Platinum Card" from Donald Trump or a European "golden visa" for a few million dollars—is devastatingly accurate. We are witnessing the unabashed commodification of political belonging.

However, while Ypi’s diagnosis of the ravages of global capitalism is impeccable, her proposed prescription—a wholesale rejection of national membership in favor of borderless, transnational class solidarity—founders on the very political realities she urges us to confront. In her noble attempt to reclaim the cosmopolitan spirit of the Enlightenment, Ypi dismisses the bounded nation-state as little more than an instrument of capitalist exclusion. In doing so, she strips the left of the only institutional mechanism that has ever successfully constrained the excesses of capital: the democratic, territorial welfare state. Ypi argues that to restrict immigration is to pit vulnerable domestic workers against vulnerable foreign workers, thereby doing the bidding of ruling elites. But this framework ignores the deeply institutional, material, and democratic prerequisites of social democracy.

The central flaw in Ypi’s argument lies in her characterization of the left’s concern over migration as mere ideological capitulation. She targets politicians like Germany’s Sahra Wagenknecht, accusing them of idealizing the liberal state and falsely claiming a "trade-off" between openness to immigration and support for the welfare state. For Ypi, this trade-off is an illusion propagated by an oligarchic system looking to divide the working class. But the tension between porous borders and a robust welfare state is not a right-wing myth; it is an enduring institutional reality.

Welfare states are not magical wellsprings of infinite resources. They are historically contingent, politically fragile systems of mutual insurance that require deep civic solidarity, high levels of taxation, and a clearly defined pool of contributors and beneficiaries. Universal healthcare, public housing, and comprehensive unemployment benefits rely on a social contract in which citizens agree to pool their resources under the assumption of reciprocal obligation. When population flows are rapid, unmanaged, and asymmetric, they place acute, localized pressures on public services that have been scaled and funded for a specific demographic baseline. To acknowledge that sudden surges in population can strain municipal budgets, exacerbate severe housing shortages, and depress wages in the most precarious sectors of the labor market is not to succumb to xenophobia. It is to acknowledge the material reality of governing.

By dismissing these pressures as mere artifacts of elite manipulation, Ypi effectively abandons the working-class citizens whose neighborhoods and public services bear the immediate brunt of rapid demographic change. When progressives refuse to acknowledge the spatial and material limits of local infrastructure, they do not foster transnational class consciousness; they simply drive their traditional constituents into the arms of the resurgent far-right. UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer’s rhetoric may be uninspiring to the cosmopolitan ear, but the fundamental electoral imperative remains: left-wing parties cannot build an egalitarian society if they are structurally locked out of state power by voters who feel their material anxieties are being derided as false consciousness.

Furthermore, Ypi’s vision fundamentally misconstrues the relationship between democracy and borders. She urges us to decouple the concept of democracy from "legally recognized membership," advocating instead for a political space of contestation where all those subjected to coercion have a voice. This is a beautiful normative ideal, but democracy, as a functional system of self-rule, requires boundaries. Without a defined "we," the concept of collective self-determination dissolves. To tax, to regulate, and to redistribute wealth requires an authoritative jurisdiction.

Historically, the capitalist class has always preferred a borderless world—not for the sake of human rights, but to escape regulation, taxation, and labor organizing. The nation-state, for all its historic crimes and exclusions, has served as the primary fortress within which the working class has been able to mobilize, vote, and rewrite the rules of the economy. The labor movement achieved the eight-hour workday, child labor laws, and workplace safety standards by capturing the legislative apparatus of the bounded state. Ypi asserts that employers favor "the movement of people without rights," which is true. But the way to ensure people have rights is not to dismantle the concept of membership; it is to strictly enforce labor standards within the state's jurisdiction while managing the influx of new entrants so that the regulatory capacity of the state is not overwhelmed.

Ypi points to transnational labor campaigns, such as the 2023-24 "Make Amazon Pay" strikes, as the blueprint for a new, borderless political solidarity. Such movements are indeed vital and inspiring. Cross-border union coordination is a crucial tool against multinational monopolies. Yet, we must be ruthlessly realistic about the limits of this approach. A coordinated strike can win a modest wage increase from a single employer, but a transnational union cannot levy a wealth tax, build public housing, mandate universal healthcare, or enforce environmental regulations. Only the coercive power of a democratic state can achieve these systemic transformations. By suggesting that the left should prioritize abstract transnational alliances over the defense and capture of national political institutions, Ypi asks the most vulnerable members of society to trade the tangible protections of the welfare state for the utopian promise of a global workers' revolution that has been perpetually delayed for a century and a half.

None of this is to say that Ypi is wrong about the grotesque nature of our current border regimes. The fact that the Global North extracts immense wealth from the Global South while violently repelling the very people displaced by that extraction is a moral atrocity. The systemic underdevelopment of poorer nations through structural adjustment programs, unfair trade regimes, and the looming specter of climate change—driven overwhelmingly by Western consumption—constitutes an ongoing crisis of justice. Ypi is entirely right to demand that we stop viewing migrants as invaders and start viewing them as casualties of a broken global order.

But how should a responsible, progressive politics respond to this reality? If the answer is not the false binary of either capitulating to right-wing nationalism or embracing open-border utopianism, what is the alternative?

A genuine left alternative must operate on two tracks: domestic realism and international radicalism. Domestically, progressive parties must unapologetically defend the integrity of the welfare state. This means defending managed migration systems that align the intake of newcomers with the state’s capacity to provide housing, healthcare, and education. It means decoupling migration policy from the demands of corporate lobbies that seek cheap, exploitable labor to undermine domestic bargaining power. It also means cracking down fiercely on the commodification of citizenship. The "golden visas" and "Platinum Cards" Ypi rightfully excoriates must be abolished. Citizenship must be a status of equal democratic participation, not a luxury asset traded on the global market. At the same time, the state must grant immediate, robust labor protections to all residents within its borders, regardless of legal status, to prevent employers from using irregular migrants as a tool to undercut native workers.

Internationally, however, the left must embrace the radicalism Ypi advocates. The solution to forced displacement is not to simply absorb the displaced into Western welfare states—a mathematical and political impossibility—but to stop generating the conditions that force people to flee in the first place. This requires a fundamental overhaul of foreign policy and international economic institutions. It means abandoning the neoliberal trade regimes that destroy local agriculture in the Global South. It means massive, unconditional debt forgiveness for developing nations so they can build their own sovereign welfare states. It means aggressive technology transfers and climate reparations to help vulnerable nations adapt to a warming world. It means stopping the arms exports and imperial proxy wars that destabilize entire regions.

In short, the left must fight for a world where people have the "right to stay," rather than merely treating the "right to move" as the highest expression of freedom. The goal of international solidarity should not be the abolition of borders, but the equalization of the conditions on either side of them.

Let us return to Lessing’s *Nathan the Wise*, as Ypi does in her conclusion. It is true that Nathan’s cosmopolitanism asks us to look to the future, to build a world based on universal reason rather than inherited tribalism. But the Enlightenment was also an era of immense practical state-building. The philosophers of the Enlightenment understood that liberty and equality could not exist in the ether; they had to be institutionalized. They required constitutions, laws, and republics capable of defending themselves against the arbitrary power of monarchs and oligarchs.

Today, the arbitrary power that threatens us is global capital. To resist it, we cannot simply rely on the moral superiority of a borderless ideal. We must wield the tools of democratic sovereignty. We must recognize that the national political community, while imperfect and historically exclusive, remains the most potent engine for social justice yet devised. To protect the universal values of freedom, equality, and solidarity, we must have the courage to govern the real world, rather than waiting for a perfect one. Defending the democratic state and its borders is not a betrayal of the migrant; it is the preservation of the only sanctuary capable of giving those Enlightenment values enduring, material life.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The tension between porous borders and a robust welfare state is a material reality, as social safety nets rely on a defined pool of contributors and a stable social contract that can be strained by unmanaged population flows.
  2. 02Dismissing the material pressures of migration on local infrastructure and public services as 'elite manipulation' alienates working-class citizens and drives them toward far-right politics.
  3. 03Functional democracy requires a defined jurisdiction and a bounded 'we' to exercise collective self-determination, tax, and redistribute wealth effectively.
  4. 04Historically, the capitalist class—not the working class—benefits most from a borderless world, as it allows them to escape the regulations and labor standards enforced by sovereign states.
  5. 05Transnational labor movements, while valuable, lack the coercive power of a state to implement systemic transformations like universal healthcare or wealth taxation.
  6. 06A responsible progressive alternative should focus on 'domestic realism' by managing migration to protect the welfare state and 'international radicalism' by ending the global economic conditions that force people to flee their homes.
