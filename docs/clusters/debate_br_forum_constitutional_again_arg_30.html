Diversified Gemini

"To effectively operationalize an anti-oligarchy constitutionalism, progressives must move beyond public rhetoric and legislative battles to embed the "democracy-of-opportunity" tradition within the administrative state's regulatory and enforcement actions."

In their compelling manifesto, “Make Progressive Politics Constitutional Again,” Joseph Fishkin, William E. Forbath, and their co-authors identify a critical failure of the modern liberal project: the surrender of the Constitution to the conservative movement. The authors rightly argue that progressives must revive the “democracy-of-opportunity” tradition, insisting that the Constitution requires a political economy free from oligarchy. While their focus on public rhetoric, legislative battles, and eventual judicial transformation is essential, there is a missing strategic pillar in their account. If we are to operationalize this anti-oligarchy constitutionalism today, the immediate battleground is not just in the halls of Congress or the court of public opinion, but deep within the administrative state.

For decades, liberals have viewed the administrative state—agencies like the Federal Trade Commission (FTC), the Department of Justice (DOJ), the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB), and the Securities and Exchange Commission (SEC)—through a purely technocratic lens. As the authors note, we left "economic questions to the economists." This technocratic retreat allowed conservative, neoliberal assumptions—such as the consumer welfare standard in antitrust or shareholder primacy in corporate law—to become the default operating systems of these powerful agencies.

But administrative agencies are not merely neutral executors of statutory text; they are sites of profound constitutional interpretation. What scholars call "administrative constitutionalism" recognizes that agencies routinely interpret their mandates in light of broader constitutional values. If progressives want to revive a constitutional political economy, they must empower agency leaders to explicitly reject neoliberal technocracy and embrace the democracy-of-opportunity tradition in their rulemakings and enforcement actions.

Take the realm of antitrust, which the authors correctly identify as crucial for preserving a democratic constitutional order. The transformation of antitrust in the 1980s under the influence of Robert Bork and the Chicago School was not achieved primarily by amending the Sherman Act. It was achieved by changing how the DOJ and the FTC interpreted their enforcement mandates, which courts eventually adopted. The neo-Brandeisian movement today, championed by figures like Lina Khan (currently chairing the FTC), understands that the agencies must take the lead in reversing this disaster.

When the FTC moves to block a mega-merger or regulate noncompete clauses, it should not merely issue a dry economic analysis predicting a marginal increase in consumer prices. It should explicitly ground its actions in the structural, constitutional imperatives of its founding statutes. The FTC was created in 1914 precisely to be an administrative bulwark against the political power of monopolies. By articulating an anti-oligarchy vision in its enforcement guidelines and administrative rulings, the FTC can force the courts to engage with a progressive theory of political economy. Even if conservative courts push back, the agency’s framing shapes the legal and public discourse, validating the idea that extreme market concentration is a threat to democratic governance.

The same logic applies to the NLRB. The authors brilliantly dissect how the conservative Supreme Court has weaponized the First Amendment against labor unions in cases like *Janus*. But before these issues reach the Supreme Court, the NLRB shapes the landscape of labor rights. A progressive NLRB can aggressively interpret the National Labor Relations Act (NLRA) to expand the definition of "employee" in the gig economy, protect secondary boycotts where the statute allows, and aggressively sanction union-busting employers. In doing so, the NLRB should revive the rhetoric of its New Deal architects, explicitly arguing that its aggressive enforcement is necessary to secure the "freedom of association" and the countervailing power required by a democratic republic.

The SEC provides another vital arena. The authors note the shift in corporate law toward shareholder primacy and Elizabeth Warren’s proposal for federal corporate chartering. While awaiting such sweeping legislation, the SEC has immense power to shape corporate behavior through disclosure requirements. When the SEC mandates disclosures regarding climate risk, political spending, or the ratio of CEO-to-worker pay, it faces fierce opposition from corporate lobbies who argue the agency is exceeding its statutory authority. The SEC must defend these rules not just as technical protections for investors, but as essential mechanisms for ensuring that large, publicly traded corporations remain accountable to the public and compatible with a democratic society.

Embracing administrative constitutionalism is particularly urgent now because the conservative Supreme Court is actively mobilizing to dismantle the administrative state. The Court’s conservative majority is rapidly expanding the "Major Questions Doctrine" (MQD)—a judicially invented rule stating that agencies cannot issue regulations of vast "economic and political significance" without highly specific, explicit congressional authorization. The MQD is the conservative legal movement’s newest weapon of constitutional political economy; it is designed to paralyze the executive branch’s ability to respond to modern crises like climate change or gig-economy exploitation, thereby protecting corporate power.

If progressive agencies continue to defend their actions merely through narrow, technocratic interpretations of ambiguous statutes, they will be easily swatted down by the MQD. To fight back, agencies must meet constitutional arguments with constitutional arguments. They must argue that Congress purposefully delegated broad authority to these agencies to prevent the exact kinds of economic crises and concentrations of power that the democracy-of-opportunity tradition abhors. They must assert that paralyzing the administrative state in the face of oligarchic corporate power violates the foundational constitutional imperative to maintain a functional, responsive democratic government.

The authors warn that the progressive fear of "re-constitutionalizing" claims cedes power to the courts. They are right. But failing to constitutionalize the work of the administrative state cedes power to both the courts and the corporations. The executive branch has an independent duty to interpret and defend the Constitution. By embedding the anti-oligarchy vision into the day-to-day regulatory actions, guidelines, and enforcement priorities of the federal government, progressives can create facts on the ground.

When an agency protects workers or breaks up monopolies, it provides material benefits to citizens, demonstrating the tangible value of a progressive political economy. This material success, in turn, builds the popular political support necessary to sustain the broader legislative and rhetorical movement the authors advocate. Fishkin and his colleagues have provided the historical and theoretical map. Now, progressives must use the levers of the state they currently control to start marching the territory.
