Angell Doesn't Go Nearly Far Enough

Marcia Angell has spent twenty years documenting the corruption of American medicine by the pharmaceutical industry, and her essay is a useful summary of what she has learned. But her proposed reforms — no personal payments to investigators, no gifts to doctors, cheap licensing of academic patents — are pitifully inadequate to the scale of the problem she has herself described. She is calling for the painters to use a different brand of paint while the house burns down.

Consider what Angell's own evidence implies. The pharmaceutical industry's profits exceed its R&D spending. Marketing budgets are twice R&D budgets. The FDA approves drugs based on company-controlled trials. Most "new" drugs are me-too variants. Advisory panels and guideline committees are populated by industry-paid experts. Medical education is industry marketing. The biggest companies routinely pay multibillion-dollar fines for criminal misconduct and treat the fines as a cost of doing business. This is not a system with corruption around the edges. This is a system whose central function is the extraction of rents from the sick, with academic medicine as the credentialing arm.

Tinkering with conflict-of-interest disclosures will not change this. The only adequate response is to remove drug development from the profit-maximizing private sector and place it under public control.

This sounds radical only because we have forgotten that almost every component of drug development is already publicly funded or could easily be. Basic biomedical research is publicly funded through NIH — about thirty billion dollars a year. The training of the scientists who do that research is publicly funded. The infrastructure of teaching hospitals where clinical trials are conducted is largely publicly funded, directly or through Medicare and Medicaid. The patent system that gives drug companies their exclusivity is a public creation. The FDA, which determines what can be sold, is a public agency. The only piece that is not currently public is the late-stage development, manufacturing, and marketing of drugs, and that is precisely the piece that generates the abuses Angell catalogs.

A public drug-development institute, funded at perhaps fifty billion dollars a year — less than the current marketing budgets of the major firms — could conduct pivotal trials on candidate compounds selected on the basis of medical need rather than market potential. Compounds that succeeded could be manufactured by generic manufacturers under contract, at cost plus a regulated margin. There would be no patents because the development was publicly funded; there would be no marketing because there would be nothing to market against; there would be no me-too drugs because there would be no patent races to win. Antibiotics for resistant infections would be developed because they are medically needed, regardless of return on investment. Vaccines for diseases of the global poor would be developed for the same reason.

I know what the objections are. The objections always come in the same order, and they are all wrong.

"The public sector can't do innovation." This is the most easily refuted objection because the public sector already does most of medicine's actual innovation. The molecular biology underlying every important drug class of the past forty years — recombinant proteins, monoclonal antibodies, tyrosine kinase inhibitors, statins, antiretrovirals — was worked out in publicly funded labs. Private firms have added development capacity, not basic creativity. Angell makes this point and it deserves to be repeated.

"A public system would be bureaucratic and slow." The current system is also bureaucratic and slow; it just hides its bureaucracy inside private firms whose operations we cannot see. The FDA approval process is the rate-limiting step in most drug development, and a public developer would face the same FDA, perhaps with a more streamlined relationship.

"You would destroy the venture capital ecosystem that funds biotech." Yes. That is the point. The venture-capital biotech ecosystem is a mechanism for converting publicly funded basic research into private financial returns. We have no national interest in maintaining it.

"Other countries free-ride on American R&D, and your proposal would end that." Other countries pay much less for drugs and do not appear to have worse medical outcomes than we do. The free-rider story is a talking point, not an argument.

Angell will not endorse anything like this proposal. She is too institutionally embedded to do so, and too committed to the framing of a fundamentally legitimate enterprise corrupted at its edges. But the logical implication of her own evidence is that the enterprise itself is the problem, not its edges. A profit-maximizing industry will always produce the abuses she describes, in some form, because they are profitable. Disclosure reforms, gift bans, and CME firewalls will be evaded, weakened, or coexist with new forms of the same dynamic.

The historical analogy I would invoke is utility regulation in the early twentieth century. Reformers spent decades trying to discipline private electric utilities through disclosure requirements, anti-trust actions, and rate regulation, with limited success. The eventual solution in much of the world was public ownership or near-public-utility regulation with cost-plus pricing. The pharmaceutical industry today resembles the unregulated private utilities of 1910, with one critical difference: it kills more people. Vioxx, Avandia, Bextra, and the antidepressant marketing scandals are not anomalies; they are the predictable byproducts of a system that asks profit-seeking firms to generate the evidence base for medical practice.

Angell wants to clean up academic medicine. I want to clean up pharmaceutical capitalism. Until we do the latter, the former will be impossible.
