"National parks should not restrict access because doing so violates the democratic principle that public lands belong to everyone and ignores the underlying issue of inadequate funding."

The proposal to restrict access to our National Parks in response to overcrowding is a dangerous pivot away from the democratic ideals upon which public lands were founded. National parks are often called "America's Best Idea" precisely because they belong to everyone, not just a privileged few. Establishing quotas, lotteries, and strict reservation systems would inevitably turn our most majestic landscapes into gated communities, locking out the very people the Park Service is currently trying to reach.

Consider who is most impacted by a strict reservation system. Securing a slot months in advance requires a level of predictability in work schedules and vacation time that working-class Americans often do not possess. Furthermore, the Park Service is currently striving to diversify its visitor base, which has historically been older, wealthier, and predominantly white. Telling lower-income families or spontaneous weekend travelers that the park is "full" will undo years of outreach and reinforce the narrative that the outdoors are an exclusive club.

The overcrowding we are seeing this centennial year is not a sign that there are too many people; it is a sign of a massive failure in funding and infrastructure. Congress has allowed a $12 billion deferred maintenance backlog to accumulate. Instead of capping visitors, we should be demanding that our lawmakers adequately fund the agencies that manage these lands.

With proper funding, the NPS could expand infrastructure, open up new entry points, and hire more rangers to manage crowds and educate the public on Leave No Trace principles. If the parks are feeling the strain of "too many people," the solution is not to arbitrarily shrink the public's right to access them. The solution is to expand the capacity of the parks to welcome the American people to their own land.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Implementing quotas and reservation systems disproportionately excludes working-class Americans and lower-income families who lack the schedule predictability required for long-term planning.
  2. 02Restricting access undermines the National Park Service's efforts to diversify its visitor base and reinforces the perception of the outdoors as an exclusive club.
  3. 03The current strain on parks is a result of a massive failure in federal funding and a multi-billion dollar maintenance backlog rather than an inherent surplus of visitors.
  4. 04The solution to overcrowding is to expand infrastructure and increase staffing to accommodate more people rather than arbitrarily limiting the public's right to access their own land.
