Vanilla Claude

"The United States should establish a standardized, federally backed compensation system for victims of all mass violence to replace the current inconsistent and unfair patchwork of private donations and ad hoc funds."

The instinct to "do something" for victims is generous, but it has produced an inconsistent patchwork that ends up being deeply unfair. The 9/11 fund paid families an average of over $2 million each; the families of the 26 killed at Sandy Hook had to fight over donations from a charity, and survivors of everyday gun violence in Chicago or New Orleans get essentially nothing beyond state crime-victim funds that cap payouts in the tens of thousands. If we believe victims deserve compensation as a matter of justice, we cannot keep distributing it by the emotional gravity of the news cycle.

There is a principled case for a permanent federal compensation program, but only if it covers all victims of mass violence — terrorism, school shootings, workplace rampages — on roughly equal terms. Ken Feinberg, who administered both the 9/11 fund and the One Fund Boston, has himself acknowledged that the 9/11 model was a one-off born of a specific fear: airline bankruptcy. Treating Boston survivors generously while ignoring the dozens shot in a single weekend in any major city sends an indefensible message about whose suffering counts.

Lawsuits are a poor substitute. Terrorists are usually dead or judgment-proof; suing gun manufacturers is largely barred by the 2005 PLCAA; suing landlords or schools for inadequate security produces erratic results and punishes the wrong parties. Private donations, meanwhile, are driven by media attention and produce both windfalls and bitter disputes over allocation, as we saw painfully at Newtown.

The better path is to expand and standardize state crime-victim compensation, federally backstopped, with a supplemental tier triggered by federally designated mass-casualty events. Compensation should focus on documented economic losses — medical bills, lost wages, long-term care — rather than speculative valuations of a life. That approach treats victims as citizens entitled to a safety net, not as lottery winners whose payout depends on how telegenic their tragedy was.

"The United States should establish a standing national framework to provide prompt, rule-based federal compensation for victims of mass violence rather than relying on ad hoc private donations or slow litigation."

The first principle should be that compensation is not the same as charity, tort liability or a memorial. It is a public acknowledgment that, when people are killed or maimed in attacks aimed at a community, the injury is partly civic. That does not mean every victim can be “made whole,” or that grief can be priced. But it does mean we should not leave assistance to the accident of where a donation drive catches fire or whether a solvent defendant can be sued.

The United States already has pieces of a better system. State crime-victim compensation programs, supported in part by the federal Crime Victims Fund, help with medical bills, funeral costs and lost wages. After Sept. 11, Congress created a much broader fund, partly to stabilize the airline industry by limiting litigation. After Boston, private giving was impressively swift, but the distribution of millions of dollars rested largely on ad hoc judgments by an administrator. Newtown showed the opposite problem: donations can be fragmented, delayed and disputed. These cases differ, but the moral claim of the badly injured child, runner, office worker or teacher should not depend so heavily on improvisation.

A sensible approach would create a standing national framework for mass violence: limited, prompt federal compensation for death, catastrophic injury, serious physical injury, and documented economic losses, available whether the perpetrator is a foreign terrorist, a domestic extremist or a mass shooter. The threshold should be high enough to avoid turning every crime into a federal compensation case, but not so narrow that victims of politically undefined atrocities are excluded. Payments should be transparent, with published categories and procedures, and coordinated with state programs, insurance and workers’ compensation to avoid duplication.

Private donations should remain welcome, but they should supplement rather than substitute for a public baseline. Donors may want to help with counseling, scholarships, community rebuilding or needs that formulas miss. Lawsuits should also remain available when there is genuine negligence or misconduct — for example, against a security provider, gun seller or institution that violated a legal duty. But litigation is slow, uncertain and often mismatched to urgent needs. It also distributes money according to provable fault, not necessarily according to harm.

The Sept. 11 fund was justified in part by exceptional economic concerns, so it should not become an unlimited precedent. Still, the lesson should not be that only some tragedies merit national responsibility. The better lesson is that compassion needs rules before the next attack, not after it.

Vanilla DeepSeek

"The federal government should establish a permanent, standardized compensation fund for victims of terrorism and mass shootings to ensure reliable and equitable relief."

The impulse to help victims of mass violence is as immediate as it is noble, yet the way we channel that compassion reveals deep-seated tensions over fairness, responsibility, and the role of the state. The patchwork of responses after 9/11, Newtown, and the Boston Marathon bombing shows that ad hoc charity and litigation are poor substitutes for a clear, principled system. The federal government should establish a permanent compensation fund for victims of terrorism and mass shootings, not as an exclusive remedy, but as a reliable floor beneath which no victim should fall.

Private donations, however generous, are inherently arbitrary. After the Sandy Hook shootings, more than $11 million in donations sat undistributed for months while committees debated eligibility, angering families who had immediate funeral costs and medical bills. Some funds went directly to the school district for a memorial, not to the families who lost children. In Boston, the One Fund was more streamlined, but it still relied on the subjective judgments of a single administrator—Kenneth Feinberg—who had to weigh the worth of a lost limb against a lost life, a calculus that no private citizen should be forced to make alone. Charitable giving also follows media attention; victims of less photogenic tragedies are routinely left behind.

Lawsuits offer equally uneven justice. The 9/11 Victim Compensation Fund was created partly to shield airlines from crippling litigation, but it also guaranteed swift, no-fault payments to families who would otherwise have faced years of legal wrangling with uncertain outcomes. Those with skilled lawyers or sympathetic juries might win massive awards, while others—especially the uninsured or low-income victims—would get little. Litigation channels public grief into adversarial contests that deepen trauma rather than heal it.

A government fund, by contrast, can provide immediate, standardized relief based on clear criteria: loss of life, permanent disability, medical expenses, and lost income. It ensures that compensation reflects societal solidarity, not the luck of the charity draw or the lottery of litigation. This is not a radical idea; many nations already operate victim compensation schemes for violent crime, recognizing that the state’s core duty is to protect its citizens. When that protection fails in the face of terrorism or mass violence, the state has a moral obligation to respond directly.

To be sure, a federal fund should not become the sole avenue for redress. Private donations can still cover gaps—scholarship funds for children, mental health services not covered by insurance—and lawsuits should remain an option when government negligence demonstrably contributed to the tragedy, as in cases of intelligence failures or security lapses. But the baseline must be public, predictable, and insulated from the whims of donors and the capriciousness of the courts. As we mark another year since Boston and twelve years since Newtown, the question is no longer whether the government should act, but why it hasn’t yet created a permanent mechanism to do so. The patchwork has failed; we owe survivors more than a lottery.

  4. 04The state has a moral obligation to provide direct compensation when it fails in its core duty to protect citizens from mass violence.

Whenever tragedy strikes, Americans reflexively open their wallets. But as the stark disparities between the aftermath of 9/11, Newtown, and the Boston Marathon bombing demonstrate, our ad-hoc approach to compensating victims of mass violence is fundamentally inequitable. Relying on the unpredictable winds of public attention or the grueling grind of the civil justice system leaves too many victims behind. To ensure fairness, the United States needs a standardized, federally backed compensation framework for victims of terrorism.

Lawsuits are an agonizingly slow and uncertain remedy. Victims and their families can spend years in court fighting well-funded corporate or institutional defendants over liability, often compounding their trauma without any guarantee of financial relief. Private donations, while noble, are similarly flawed as a primary safety net. The One Fund Boston successfully distributed tens of millions of dollars, largely because the bombing occurred in a major media market during a beloved international event. Lesser-known tragedies simply do not generate the same philanthropic outpouring, creating an unequal hierarchy of victimhood where compensation depends heavily on media coverage rather than actual human need. Furthermore, as the aftermath of the Newtown shootings showed, delegating distribution to disparate nonprofits can lead to severe bureaucratic delays, lack of transparency, and bitter community disputes.

A permanent federal compensation fund, triggered by official declarations of terrorism or mass violence, would resolve these deep inequities. The September 11th Victim Compensation Fund proved that a centralized administrative process, guided by a neutral special master, can distribute aid swiftly and fairly. While the 2001 fund was uniquely designed to protect the airline industry from financial ruin, its core mechanism—offering guaranteed, rapid financial support in exchange for waivers of certain litigation rights—should be adapted as a permanent model.

Private philanthropy will always play a vital role in national healing, and citizens should absolutely continue to give. But charity cannot replace sound public policy. When American citizens are targeted in acts of terrorism or mass violence, their physical and financial recovery should not depend on the whims of the 24-hour news cycle or the endurance to survive a decade-long lawsuit. The government has a duty to provide a predictable, equitable baseline of support.

Vanilla MiniMax

"The U.S. government should establish a standing framework that provides a baseline of immediate compensation for victims of mass violence to ensure consistency and fairness that private charity and litigation cannot guarantee."

The question of how to compensate victims of mass violence admits no single right answer, but the historical record suggests that a government role—even a limited one—offers advantages that private mechanisms alone cannot replicate.

Pure private charity, as seen in Newtown, produces inconsistent results. Distribution depends on which organizations receive donations, how efficiently they operate, and often on which victims attract media attention. Families who suffered equally but quietly sometimes received far less. This arbitrary distribution is not merely unfair; it compounds trauma by forcing victims into competition for sympathy.

Lawsuits provide compensation but only for those with the resources and patience to litigate, and often only against parties with deep pockets. After 9/11, lawsuits against airlines threatened the industry's survival while providing uncertain, delayed recovery to families. Tort litigation also creates perverse incentives: plaintiffs seek defendants with insurance, not necessarily those most responsible.

The 9/11 Victim Compensation Fund demonstrated what government action can accomplish. It provided guaranteed, relatively quick payments without forcing families into adversarial litigation. The tradeoff—limiting lawsuits against airlines—reflected a policy judgment that broader economic interests justified streamlined compensation.

Going forward, a tiered approach makes sense. Government should provide a baseline of immediate support—medical care, funeral expenses, basic living assistance—through emergency funds established in advance of disasters. This removes the chaos of ad hoc fundraising and ensures that no victim is forgotten for lack of media attention.

Private donations and civil litigation should supplement but not replace this foundation. Victims retain the right to sue responsible parties, but initial compensation comes from public sources designed to deliver it quickly and fairly. This acknowledges that mass casualties are not merely private misfortunes but events that strain communities and implicate public interests in security and recovery.

What we should avoid is the current pattern of improvisation. Each tragedy produces a new debate about how to help, leaving victims uncertain and officials reacting instead of responding. A standing framework—with clear government responsibilities and defined roles for private action—would serve both efficiency and justice.

The federal government should compensate victims of terrorism and comparable mass violence, but it should do so through a standing, rules-based program rather than improvising after each horror.

Lawsuits are no better as the primary vehicle. They are slow, adversarial and often hinge on whether a defendant with money can be found. The criteria can be defined in advance: terrorist attacks, mass shootings and other intentional mass-casualty events meeting a threshold of deaths or severe injuries.

Money cannot restore the dead or erase trauma. But a fair, predictable public system can pay hospital bills, replace lost income, and signal that victims were not abandoned to accident, charity or litigation. That is what a civilized country owes.

**Create a Permanent Federal Fund — and Stop Improvising**

The pattern is by now familiar and embarrassing. A tragedy occurs, the nation grieves, and then we scramble to invent a compensation scheme from scratch. After 9/11, Congress hurriedly stood up a special master's fund. After Newtown, a confused patchwork of nonprofits collected money that some families never saw. After Boston, Ken Feinberg was pulled out of semi-retirement to do once again what he had done before. Each time, victims and donors are left wondering whether their case will be treated like the last one or invented anew.

This is no way to run a civilized response to mass violence. The United States should establish a permanent federal compensation program for victims of terrorist attacks and mass shootings, with clear eligibility rules, a published benefits schedule, and a standing administrator. Australia, the United Kingdom and Israel all have versions of this. We have one too, technically — every state runs a Crime Victim Compensation program funded partly by the federal Victims of Crime Act — but the caps are absurdly low (often $25,000 or less) and few people know it exists.

A permanent federal fund would do three things at once. It would give victims certainty: they would know in the chaotic days after an attack that medical bills, lost wages, and funeral costs will be covered, without having to wait for a charity drive or a lawsuit. It would protect against the inequities that emerge whenever ad hoc funds divide donations: the Boston fund paid amputees who lost one leg differently from those who lost two, generating heartbreak and resentment. And it would remove the political pressure that distorts each new crisis, where the size of the payout depends on how telegenic the tragedy was.

The standard objection is fairness: why should a person killed by a terrorist receive more than a person killed by a drunk driver? It is a serious question, but the answer is that mass violence is different in kind — it is an attack on the polity itself, an event for which the state bears some responsibility for prevention. Compensating its victims is not charity; it is an acknowledgment that they were harmed in our place. We should institutionalize that acknowledgment rather than reinvent it after every funeral.

The worst way to compensate victims of mass violence is to invent a new system while the sirens are still echoing. That is essentially what the United States has done: a congressional fund after Sept. 11, a jumble of charities after Newtown, and the One Fund after Boston. The federal government should create a standing compensation program for victims of terrorism and mass-casualty attacks. The reason is not that these victims suffer more than other crime victims. If a defendant is later found responsible, the government can seek reimbursement or offset duplicative payments.

Private donations should still be welcome, and lawsuits should still play their role in uncovering wrongdoing. But neither is a reliable foundation for justice. Charity follows publicity. Litigation follows deep pockets. A standing federal fund would say something simpler and better: when Americans are attacked in public places, the country will not leave the injured and bereaved to pass the hat.
