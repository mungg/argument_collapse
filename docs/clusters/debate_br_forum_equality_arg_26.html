"While Sypnowich correctly identifies the limitations of equal opportunity, her proposed focus on equal flourishing risks imposing a universalist, top-down definition of the 'good life' that may entrench existing social hierarchies and silence marginalized voices."

Christine Sypnowich’s lead essay mounts an ambitious argument that the ideal of equal opportunity is insufficient and that a genuine egalitarianism must aim for equal flourishing—outcomes, not just chances. Her emphasis on the multidimensional nature of disadvantage and her call for a community committed to mutual care resonate with feminist concerns. Yet her framework, for all its talk of pluralism, risks reinstating a universalist, top-down conception of “flourishing” that can silence marginalized voices and obscure the specific ways that race, gender, sexuality, and disability shape both experience and aspiration. An adequate egalitarianism must not only redistribute goods but also transform the processes by which we define what a good life entails, centering the perspective of those who have been historically excluded from that definition.

Sypnowich rightly criticizes the liberal neutralist paradigm for pretending that the state can be agnostic about values while in fact promoting market-friendly individualism. She calls for a society that actively promotes valuable ways of life and discourages harmful ones, and she suggests that this can be done through democratic deliberation. But her account of deliberation remains abstract; she does not grapple with the power asymmetries that pervade any actually existing democracy. Whose voices will dominate that deliberation? In societies marked by patriarchy, white supremacy, and ableism, it is all too likely that the “valuable” will end up reflecting the norms of the already advantaged. Historically, white, able-bodied, heterosexual men have set the standards for what it means to flourish—being a productive worker, a breadwinner, a rational decision-maker—and these standards have been used to stigmatize and discipline those who deviate: the stay-at-home mother, the disabled person who cannot work a conventional job, the queer individual whose intimacies fall outside the nuclear family.

Sypnowich’s own examples betray an unexamined orientation toward conventional notions of achievement and culture. Music education, for instance, is a classic middle-class aspiration; but is playing an instrument truly a universal component of flourishing? For some, flourishing might involve storytelling, spiritual practice, care networks, or manual craft traditions that have been marginalized by official culture. To make a state project of “incentives and supporting institutions that offer pursuits of value” is to risk a new kind of cultural imperialism, even if the intent is benevolent. The line between encouraging and imposing is not as sharp as Sypnowich claims. Education policy that mandates classical music lessons while ignoring hip-hop or indigenous music is a case in point.

Feminist theory has long warned against abstract universalism. Iris Marion Young, in her critique of distributive justice, argued that the model of the state as a distributor of goods among a homogeneous citizenry erases the structural differences that constitute women’s and minorities’ oppression. What is needed is not a single metric of flourishing but a democratic politics of difference in which marginal groups have the power to define their own needs and aspirations. Sypnowich’s call for “socialist community” is welcome, but it must be a community that recognizes internal heterogeneity and cultivates autonomy for its sub-groups, not a monolithic collective that dispenses resources and approval.

This is not to say that material redistribution is unimportant—far from it. Feminists have long shown that economic inequality is gendered and racialized, and Sypnowich is right to insist that we must tackle unequal resources to enable flourishing. But her framework seems to assume that once material conditions are equalized, people will be equally situated to pursue the good. This neglects the way that symbolic and identity-based hierarchies persist even after redistribution. For example, even in a social-democratic state with generous parental leave and childcare, mothers may still bear the “mental load” and face cultural pressure to be primary caregivers, a role that limits their flourishing in other domains. That is an outcome of social norms, not just economic opportunity, and addressing it requires a cultural struggle that Sypnowich’s state-led promotion of the valuable might either ignore or, worse, entrench, if it channels women into “flourishing” as caregivers because that is deemed valuable.

Additionally, Sypnowich’s embrace of responsibility softened by social context still retains a troubling judgmental edge. She condemns “fatalism” and “low expectations” as things to be corrected. But many marginalized people adopt such outlooks as rational adaptations to a society that systematically devalues them. Labeling these adaptations as deficient and seeking to “correct” them through state action without first dismantling the structures that produce them is a recipe for victim-blaming. A better approach is to follow the lead of disability justice and reproductive justice movements, which assert that flourishing means having the resources and supports to live a dignified life as one is, not being reshaped into someone else’s ideal of a flourishing person.

What might an alternative look like? An egalitarian society must invest in democratic, grassroots processes that allow diverse communities to articulate their own visions of the good life. It must provide universal material security and access to a rich array of cultural resources, but it must not use that provision as a lever to steer people toward state-approved ends. The state’s role is to ensure that no vision of the good life can be pursued at the expense of others’ equal freedom, and to counteract structural inequities that silence some voices. That means, in practice, targeted policies that address the specific barriers faced by women of color, LGBTQ+ people, and disabled individuals—not a one-size-fits-all outcome metric.

Sypnowich’s essay is a valuable provocation, but its outcome-focus is insufficiently intersectional. To truly achieve equality, we must listen as much as we redistribute, and ensure that the definition of flourishing remains an ongoing, contested, and bottom-up process, never a fixed target set by even a well-meaning state.
