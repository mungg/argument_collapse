Diversified Gemini

"Instead of relying on indirect job creation through sector subsidies as Robert Pollin suggests, the federal government should achieve full employment by directly guaranteeing a job to anyone who wants one through an Employer of Last Resort (ELR) program."

Robert Pollin’s vision for a full-employment economy is an admirable intervention in a political climate completely obsessed with austerity. His reminder that the U.S. government has the capacity to banish involuntary unemployment is exactly what the left needs to be articulating. But while his heart is in the right place, Pollin’s reliance on indirect job creation through sector subsidies represents a timidity that has hobbled progressive economics for decades. If we want full employment, we should not hope that clean-energy subsidies "induce" it; the government should guarantee it directly.

Pollin takes great pains to outline how shifting $330 billion from the military and fossil fuels to clean energy and education will create 4.8 million jobs through direct, indirect, and induced multipliers. This is the classic Keynesian approach: nudge the private sector and local governments, provide funding, and hope that the money trickles down into decent employment. But why rely on this labyrinthine process, especially when private capital has proven so adept at capturing government subsidies without passing on the benefits to workers?

Instead of relying on multipliers, the United States should institute an Employer of Last Resort (ELR) program, effectively a modern Works Progress Administration (WPA). If there are 15 million people looking for work, the federal government should offer a job at a living wage to anyone who wants one.

An ELR program cuts through the capitalist blackmail that Pollin describes so well in his invocation of Marx and Kalecki. Pollin rightly notes that under capitalism, a "reserve army of the unemployed" is used to discipline labor and keep wages down. But his solution—subsidizing green energy companies so they hire more people—still leaves the power in the hands of the private employer. If the government instead guarantees a job at, say, $15 an hour with basic benefits, it instantly sets a true floor for the labor market. No private employer could offer worse pay or conditions than the ELR standard, because workers would simply leave for the public option.

Direct public employment is also superior because it can be targeted exactly where it is needed. We don't have to wait for a solar-panel manufacturer to decide a neighborhood is profitable enough to open a plant. An ELR program can put people to work immediately in their own communities: repairing local infrastructure, expanding eldercare, cleaning up parks, and weatherizing homes.

Pollin limits his imagination to tweaking the federal budget to create a "favorable environment" for job growth. But the Great Recession proved that the private sector cannot be trusted with the foundation of our society. We need structural transformation, not just better subsidies. A federal job guarantee achieves full employment by definition, completely eliminating involuntary unemployment on day one, and finally democratizing the right to a livelihood.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Direct public employment is more efficient than the classic Keynesian approach because it avoids the risk of private capital capturing subsidies without passing benefits on to workers.
  2. 02A federal job guarantee creates a true labor market floor that forces private employers to match public wages and conditions, effectively ending the use of unemployment to discipline labor.
  3. 03An ELR program allows for immediate, targeted community improvements—such as infrastructure repair and eldercare—without waiting for private sector investment to become profitable.
  4. 04A direct job guarantee eliminates involuntary unemployment by definition and provides a more stable foundation for society than the untrustworthy private sector.
