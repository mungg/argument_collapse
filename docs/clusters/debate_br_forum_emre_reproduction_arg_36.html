"While the lead essay correctly deconstructs the myth of 'natural' reproduction, its solution relies too heavily on a techno-rationalist, rights-based framework that fails to address the alienation and fragmentation inherent in the commodified fertility industry."

“All Reproduction Is Assisted” attempts to do two things at once: to deconstruct the distinction between natural and artificial reproduction, and to advance a positive vision of reproductive justice grounded in something like a right to have children with dignity. The first move succeeds brilliantly; the second, I fear, is weakened by an undertheorized conception of autonomy. The essay’s protagonists—S, B, N, K, and cárdenas—are all depicted as navigating a landscape of constrained choices. They long for children; they confront biological, economic, and social obstacles; they turn to technology. The essay’s moral appears to be that by universalizing access to assisted reproduction, we can correct the injustices that make these stories so painful. But there is a deeper current in the narratives that pushes against this conclusion. What haunts these women is not just the difficulty of achieving pregnancy but the experience of fragmentation: the splitting of reproduction into a series of discrete, commodified steps that feel alienating even when they are freely chosen. The inadequacy of the technological fix suggests that what is needed is less a right to assistive services and more an ethic of care that reintegrates reproduction into the fabric of communal life.

Take S’s egg freezing. She undergoes a rigorous, painful protocol to harvest fifteen viable eggs, which she calls an “insurance policy.” Yet she feels “shackled to the specter of the natural.” The language is telling: rather than expanding her sense of freedom, the technology intensifies a feeling of being watched and judged. The problem is not that she lacks access; her employer covers two rounds. The problem is that the technology does not, and perhaps cannot, answer the existential questions that the natural once foreclosed—What kind of mother do I want to be? What kind of family do I belong to? She is left alone with a freezer full of gametes and a future as uncertain as before. The essay attributes this to “internalized” norms, and certainly there is truth in that. But another reading is possible: that reproduction always involves a surrender to forces beyond individual control, and that the attempt to master biology through contract, schedule, and optimization produces its own form of distress.

B’s story similarly reveals the limits of a rights‑based approach. She chooses IVF after a traumatic miscarriage because she wants “gradual attrition” rather than sudden loss. But the attrition turns out to be brutal in its own right. She tracks the dwindling number of viable embryos until she is left with three. The embryo is screened, graded, and delayed until her employer’s leave policy allows. At every step, the process demands that she manage risk, make decisions under conditions of profound uncertainty, and treat her own body as a project to be managed. The essay frames her situation as a failure of social policy—lack of paid leave and universal health care—and it is. But it is also a failure of the surrounding culture to support her not just as a rights‑bearer but as a person in need of care. She borrows money from friends, but where is the community that would sit with her during the two‑week wait, that would grieve with her, that would treat her desire for a child not as an individual project but as a shared undertaking of a social world?

The glaring absence in the essay is any sustained discussion of midwifery, doula care, mutual‑aid networks, or the long history of community‑based reproductive assistance that predates and often resists the high‑tech clinic. The essay’s historical narrative jumps from the incubator to Firestone to egg‑freezing, bypassing entirely the feminist health movement of the 1970s that produced *Our Bodies, Ourselves* and the alternative birth center movement. Those movements were not, as the essay’s caricature of essentialism might suggest, simply nostalgic for the natural. They were trying to wrest reproductive knowledge away from a patriarchal medical establishment and return it to women in community with one another. Their vision was not anti‑technology but anti‑authoritarian. A doula assisting a hospital birth is offering a form of assistance that is no less technological for being low‑tech; the monitoring, the breathing, the advocacy are all skills that embody the claim that reproduction is never solitary. The difference is that the doula’s assistance is oriented toward supporting the laboring person’s agency and dignity within a relational context, rather than toward maximizing a quantifiable outcome.

The contemporary fertility industry, for all its talk of empowerment, often works against this relational ethos. Patients report being processed through assembly‑line clinics, rarely seeing the same doctor twice, receiving calls from automated systems. The technology fragments the experience into units—follicle counts, blood draws, trigger shots—that can feel disconnected from the emotional arc of trying to become a parent. The essay’s subjects cope with this fragmentation largely by turning to online communities: #ttcsisters, a queer TTC Facebook group. These communities provide real solace, but they are also constrained by the platform’s logic of likes and shares, and they can quickly become spaces of “madly projecting grief,” as K puts it. They are no substitute for the face‑to‑face, long‑term relationships of care that a truly just reproductive order would nurture.

What would it mean to ask not “How can we get more women access to IVF?” but “How can we make the process of family‑making a site of genuine care?” The answer would include, certainly, the structural reforms the essay advocates: universal health care, paid leave, protections for LGBT families. But it would also require a cultural shift that the essay’s techno‑rationalist idiom cannot quite reach. It would mean clinics that are organized around continuity of relationship rather than throughput; community‑based support groups that are funded and embedded in neighborhoods; fertility preservation that is not a career‑management perk but an offering made in the context of a holistic conversation about life, love, and work. It would mean honoring the foster parent’s training not as “a rehearsal for loss” but as a profound education in the ethics of care that all parents, biological or not, would benefit from.

The lead essay closes with the hope that acknowledging positive rights to reproduction could “make assisted reproduction the preferred strategy” and close the gap between women. I am less sanguine. Making assisted reproduction the preferred strategy may simply embed an instrumental, product‑oriented model of family‑making more deeply into our lives. What I want is to make care the preferred frame. When we see reproduction as an act of care rather than production, the question of whether assistance is natural or artificial fades. The crucial matter becomes: Are we helping each other to love and raise the children who come, through whatever path they arrive? That is the assistance that matters most, and it is one that no insurance policy will ever provide.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The subjects in the lead essay experience distress not just from a lack of access, but from the optimization and clinical isolation of reproductive technologies that treat the body as a project to be managed.
  2. 02A rights-based approach to reproductive justice is insufficient if it does not challenge the assembly-line nature of modern clinics that prioritize quantifiable outcomes over relational dignity.
  3. 03The lead essay overlooks the historical and practical importance of low-tech, community-based assistance—such as midwifery and doulas—which centers the agency of the individual within a social fabric.
  4. 04True reproductive justice requires moving beyond the 'production' of children toward an 'ethic of care' that reintegrates family-making into communal life rather than just expanding insurance coverage.
  5. 05The focus on universalizing high-tech assisted reproduction risks further embedding an instrumental, product-oriented model of kinship into society.
