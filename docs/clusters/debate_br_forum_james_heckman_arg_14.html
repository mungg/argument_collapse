"While early childhood intervention is a critical foundation for social mobility, public policy must not succumb to a 'developmental determinism' that neglects the essential role of K-12 schooling, adolescent programs, and career pathways for those already past the preschool years."

James Heckman’s emphasis on early childhood is persuasive, but his essay risks encouraging a new fatalism about everything that happens afterward. He rejects genetic determinism, only to flirt with developmental determinism: if the decisive gaps are already visible at age six, if adolescent remediation has low returns, if schools play only a minor role in creating or reducing inequality, then the policy imagination naturally migrates almost entirely to the preschool years. That would be a mistake.

The evidence that early childhood matters is overwhelming. Children arrive at kindergarten with very different vocabularies, habits, health profiles, and emotional resources. The Perry and Abecedarian results are important. Home visiting can help parents. Socio-emotional skills matter. None of this should be disputed. But it does not follow that K-12 schooling, adolescent intervention, and postsecondary pathways are secondary concerns. For millions of children already past early childhood, they are the only concerns available.

Public policy cannot be built only for newborns. On November 9, 2012, the United States contains a generation of children who are eight, twelve, sixteen, and twenty. Telling them that the highest-return intervention would have occurred before they could tie their shoes is analytically interesting and morally insufficient. A mobility agenda must ask not only where the production function is steepest, but what obligations we have to those who missed the optimal window.

Heckman is right that many conventional later interventions disappoint. The GED has been oversold. Adult job training often produces weak returns. Remedial education in community colleges is frequently a graveyard of aspiration. But “later intervention” is too broad a category to dismiss. Some charter schools serving low-income students—KIPP is the best-known example—have produced large achievement gains, especially when they combine more time, strong school culture, tutoring, and high expectations. The evidence is not uniform, and charter schools are no universal cure. Still, their existence suggests that adolescent learning environments can alter trajectories.

Teacher quality also matters. The difference between a strong and weak teacher is not trivial, especially for disadvantaged students. A child who has had a poor early environment may be particularly dependent on school adults who provide structure, feedback, and encouragement. If we conclude that schools do not explain much of the initial gap, we may neglect the fact that good schools can prevent gaps from widening and can sometimes narrow them. The question is not whether schools caused all inequality. The question is whether they can be made powerful enough to compensate for some of it.

The same is true of adolescence. Heckman himself acknowledges that personality and social skills remain malleable into the early twenties. That concession should be expanded. Adolescence is a period of identity formation, peer influence, risk-taking, and sensitivity to adult recognition. Programs that connect young people to work, apprenticeships, military-style discipline without criminalization, arts, sports, and mentoring can matter. The problem is that many such programs are fragmented, underfunded, and weakly linked to actual opportunity. We should improve them, not merely contrast them unfavorably with preschool.

Career and technical education deserves special attention. The American high school has too often offered a false choice between a college-prep track that many students experience as abstract and a vocational track stigmatized as second-class. Other advanced economies do better at connecting school and work. The United States needs stronger pathways from high school to skilled employment: apprenticeships, employer partnerships, industry-recognized credentials, and community college programs tied to labor market demand. For a young person with weak academic motivation, the discovery that algebra is connected to a wage, a machine, a credential, and an adult role can itself build non-cognitive skills.

Heckman’s critique of the GED is especially relevant here. The lesson should not simply be that GED recipients lack perseverance. It should be that institutions have offered too many young people thin credentials instead of structured pathways. Completing high school matters partly because it requires attendance, relationships, deadlines, and persistence. A better career pathway would cultivate those same traits in a context that feels real to students. The alternative to credentialism is not resignation; it is building institutions that make accomplishment substantive.

There is also a political danger in framing early childhood as the uniquely efficient investment. Policymakers love high-return claims, but they also love excuses to cut less fashionable programs. If early childhood becomes the “smart” investment, then funding for high-poverty schools, youth employment, community colleges, and reentry programs may be portrayed as wasteful compassion. That would deepen inequality for those who are already behind. Efficiency analysis must be joined to a theory of civic obligation.

A balanced approach would use Heckman’s developmental insight as a guide to sequencing and reinforcement. Begin with prenatal care, home visiting, and high-quality early childhood education. Follow with excellent elementary instruction in literacy and numeracy. Add social-emotional supports without turning them into another testing regime. Prevent summer learning loss. In middle and high school, provide tutoring, mentoring, extended learning time, and pathways to work. In late adolescence and early adulthood, offer apprenticeships, community college programs that actually lead somewhere, and second-chance institutions for those leaving prison or dropping out.

The phrase “skill begets skill” should be understood dynamically. Early skills make later learning easier, but later institutions still determine whether early gains are sustained. Head Start fadeout debates have taught us that children can gain in preschool and then lose ground in weak elementary schools. Conversely, a strong school can sometimes awaken capacities that were dormant. Human development is path-dependent, not path-locked.

Heckman has done a great service by pushing the debate earlier and by challenging the obsession with test scores. But the next step is not to rank stages of life against one another in a zero-sum contest. It is to build a developmental state—or, if one prefers, a developmental civil society—that supports human growth from birth through young adulthood. Early childhood is the foundation. But a foundation without walls, wiring, and a roof is not a house. Children need the whole structure.
