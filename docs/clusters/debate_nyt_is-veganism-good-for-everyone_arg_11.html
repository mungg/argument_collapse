"Veganism is not universally beneficial because it poses significant health risks to vulnerable populations like infants, children, and the elderly."

While the conversation around veganism often focuses on healthy adults deciding to change their habits, the narrative shifts dramatically when we consider vulnerable populations. Is veganism good for everyone? When "everyone" includes pregnant women, developing infants, and the elderly, applying a rigid, exclusionary dietary dogma becomes a dangerous game.

The nutritional demands of human growth and development are intense and unforgiving. While an adult body can draw on stored reserves of vitamins and minerals for years before a deficiency becomes clinically apparent, an infant or a fetus has no such buffer. The most critical nutrient of concern is Vitamin B12, which is vital for neurological development and is found naturally only in animal products.

Medical literature contains tragic, documented cases of breastfed infants of vegan mothers who suffered severe, irreversible brain damage and developmental delays due to B12 deficiency. Even if a mother supplements, the margins for error are terrifyingly thin. Furthermore, growing children require high-quality, easily digestible protein and calorie-dense foods to fuel their rapid development. Because plant foods are highly fibrous and low in calories, a child can easily become full before they have consumed enough calories and nutrients to thrive, leading to stunted growth.

For the elderly, the challenges are different but equally concerning. As we age, our ability to absorb nutrients, particularly B12 and protein, diminishes. Sarcopenia—the age-related loss of muscle mass—is a major cause of frailty, falls, and mortality in seniors. Combatting it requires high biological-value protein, most easily obtained from eggs, dairy, and meat.

Adults have the right to experiment with their bodies. If a 30-year-old wishes to navigate the complexities of a vegan diet to lower their cholesterol, they can safely do so with monitoring. But we must be incredibly cautious about presenting a vegan diet as universally safe and beneficial. Imposing an inherently restrictive diet on developing bodies or fading metabolisms introduces severe risks that simply do not exist with a balanced, omnivorous approach.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Developing fetuses and infants are at high risk for irreversible neurological damage due to the lack of naturally occurring Vitamin B12 in plant-based diets.
  2. 02The high fiber and low calorie density of plant foods can lead to stunted growth in children who may feel full before meeting their nutritional and caloric needs.
  3. 03Elderly individuals require high biological-value proteins from animal sources to combat sarcopenia and the natural decline in nutrient absorption.
  4. 04Unlike healthy adults who can manage dietary restrictions, vulnerable groups lack the physical reserves to safely navigate the margins of error inherent in a vegan diet.
