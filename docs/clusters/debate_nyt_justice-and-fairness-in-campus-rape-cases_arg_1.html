The way out of the false choice is to distinguish care from adjudication. A student who reports rape should receive immediate medical help, counseling, academic accommodations, housing changes and protection from retaliation without first having to “prove” anything. The collapse of the Rolling Stone story is a setback for survivors, but it shouldn't obscure what we already know: campus rape is common, badly underreported, and badly handled when it is reported. The right response is not to choose sides between believing women and protecting the accused — it is to build a process serious enough to do both.

That starts with separating the support function from the adjudicative one. A student who reports an assault needs immediate access to medical care, counseling, academic accommodations, and a confidential advocate. None of those services should depend on whether her account is ultimately substantiated, and none of them require the university to have pre-judged the case. The mistake schools keep making is conflating compassionate treatment of a complainant with a finding against the respondent.

The adjudication itself should look more like a real investigation and less like a faculty committee improvising. That means trained, full-time investigators (ideally drawn from outside the campus disciplinary office), prompt collection of physical and digital evidence, written findings, and parallel cooperation with police when the complainant chooses to go that route. The accused should receive timely written notice of the allegations, access to the evidence, the right to an adviser, and a genuine opportunity to respond. The preponderance standard mandated by the 2011 Dear Colleague letter is defensible for a school proceeding that can expel but not imprison — but it only holds up morally if procedures are otherwise rigorous. Cross-examination through the panel, rather than direct confrontation, can protect complainants without gutting the respondent's ability to test the case.

Finally, universities should stop pretending they are courts. Their job is to decide whether a student may remain enrolled, not to pronounce on guilt. Honest framing of that limited question — paired with real support services and competent investigation — would do more for both accusers and accused than the current oscillation between credulity and backlash.

"Colleges must move beyond a false choice between supporting survivors and protecting the accused by establishing institutional processes that are trauma-informed, procedurally fair, and rigorously independent."

The unravelling of a high-profile magazine story has exposed a painful truth: the current system too often forces a false choice between supporting those who report sexual assault and safeguarding the rights of the accused. We can and must do both. The path forward lies not in taking sides, but in building institutional processes that are trauma-informed, procedurally fair, and rigorously independent.

First, support and investigation must be structurally separated. A reporting student should immediately be connected to a confidential victim advocate—someone who is not a college employee tasked with fact-finding—who can explain options, arrange counseling and academic accommodations, and provide support without triggering a formal inquiry. The advocate’s role is to listen, not to weigh evidence. Only when the student chooses to proceed does a formal investigation begin, preserving the complainant’s agency while avoiding the impression that the institution is reflexively adversarial.

For that investigation to be fair, colleges need trained, impartial investigators who are not embedded in the dean’s office or campus security. The 2011 “Dear Colleague” letter rightly demanded prompt and equitable response, but it also inadvertently encouraged an overcorrection: some schools adopted truncated processes that denied accused students meaningful notice of the allegations or the chance to present witnesses. A fair system gives both parties timely, written notice of the charges, access to the full evidence file, and the opportunity to suggest questions for witnesses. Cross-examination need not be a live confrontation—trauma-informed models allow each student to submit questions through an intermediary, preserving dignity while testing consistency.

Crucially, these proceedings should be governed by a clear, consistent standard of proof, and the preponderance-of-evidence standard remains appropriate for educational discipline. What must change is the perception that this means a presumption of guilt. The investigator’s report should be reviewed by a panel that includes faculty and trained community members, not students alone, to insulate decisions from campus politics. Both sides deserve written findings that articulate the reasoning, and both should have a right to appeal on procedural grounds.

This is not a zero-sum contest. When institutions invest in skilled investigators, transparent protocols, and separate advocacy resources, they can honor the pain of survivors without abandoning the fundamental principle that a disciplinary finding must rest on evidence, not sympathy or suspicion. Fairness and sensitivity are not opponents; they are the twin pillars of justice.
