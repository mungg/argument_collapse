Diversified DeepSeek

"The "rolling-rule" regime described by Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen represents a superior, experimentalist model of democratic governance that successfully integrates local participation with national coordination to solve complex environmental problems."

Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen have not merely described a set of policy experiments; they have glimpsed the future of democratic governance. Their essay is a compelling, even exhilarating, account of how environmental regulation is escaping the false choice between rigid command-and-control and bloodless market mechanisms. What emerges is a picture of “deep use”—a participatory, experimentalist model that marries local knowledge to national coordination, and expertise to citizen deliberation. This is not just a tweak to the administrative state; it is a fundamental reimagining of how we can collectively respond to complex, shifting problems, and it deserves our enthusiastic embrace.

The core insight is both pragmatic and democratic. The old model of centralized rulemaking assumed a level of omniscience that the EPA never possessed. Rules specifying “best available technology” or precise emission caps ossified too quickly, failing to adapt to new science, new technologies, or the unique circumstances of particular ecosystems. The market-mimicking alternative, tradable permits, while theoretically elegant, demands a regulatory apparatus to define and enforce property rights, and can lock in initial allocations that later prove misguided. The rolling-rule regime, by contrast, builds learning directly into its architecture. Local units—whether tributary teams in the Chesapeake Bay watershed, toxics reduction planning teams in Massachusetts factories, or multi-stakeholder HCP groups in Southern California—are given the autonomy to set goals and devise solutions. In return, they must measure, report, and pool their results. The center then synthesizes this information to reset benchmarks and spread best practices, triggering a new round of local experimentation.

This is a form of pragmatism that John Dewey would have admired. It treats policy as a continuous hypothesis, subject to revision through structured, collective inquiry. What makes it so promising is that it does not merely tolerate citizen participation; it requires it. The Chesapeake Bay Program’s evolution from expert-led monitoring to tributary teams where volunteers become local experts is a perfect emblem. When citizens are engaged in diagnosing problems and co-designing solutions, they develop a stake that transcends NIMBY obstructionism. They become, as the authors astutely note, constructive rather than merely oppositional. The successes of the Toxics Release Inventory (TRI) demonstrate that simply shining a light on pollution—forcing firms to publicly disclose their emissions—can trigger a race to the top driven by reputation and local pressure, without the heavy hand of prosecution. TURA in Massachusetts deepened this by requiring firms to plan for reductions, with worker involvement, backed by a state-supported institute and technical assistance. The result was a substantial drop in toxics use, and the revelation that many firms discovered cost savings through waste reduction—a virtuous cycle of efficiency and environmentalism that centralized command never unlocked.

The Habit Conservation Plan (HCP) experience, despite its unevenness, is perhaps the most ambitious test bed. The Southern California multi-species plans show that when developers, scientists, and conservationists are brought into a structured negotiation backed by a credible regulatory backstop (the Section 9 “take” prohibition), they can craft landscape-scale solutions that preserve far more habitat than a rigid, parcel-by-parcel enforcement would have achieved. The adaptive management provisions—monitoring species populations, adjusting reserve boundaries and management practices in response to new data—institutionalize the experimentalist logic. The authors rightly note that the system needs better information-pooling to correct local failures and spread innovations, and the proposed Miller Bill’s emphasis on public reporting and triennial review is a sensible step toward the kind of national coordination that completes the rolling-rule cycle.

The sharpest arrows aimed at this model come from those who fear a retreat from enforceable standards. But the rolling-rule regime is not voluntarism. It demands accountability—not through rigid compliance with top-down dictates, but through the obligation to justify one’s performance in light of pooled benchmarks. The INPO example in the nuclear power industry is instructive: private self-monitoring only became effective when it was coupled with public disclosure of plant rankings to CEOs, boards, and the NRC. The fear of exposure disciplined performance in a way that internal peer review alone could not. The state’s role shifts from imposing detailed commands to policing the integrity of the participatory and information-pooling process—a role that is, if anything, more demanding and essential than before.

Some will fret that this model places excessive faith in the capacity of ordinary citizens and local groups to engage in complex technical deliberation. But the evidence is mounting that the alternative—isolating expertise in distant agencies—has failed precisely because it ignores the contextual intelligence of those who live with the consequences. The rolling-rule regime does not abandon science; it embeds it in a social process where scientific claims are tested against local reality. The challenge, as the Chesapeake Bay example shows, is to provide the scaffolding—training, protocols, accessible data—that enables lay participants to become genuinely co-equal contributors. The Toxics Use Reduction Institute and the Office of Technical Assistance in Massachusetts are prototypes of the kind of public infrastructure needed to make this work.

In their conclusion, the authors gesture toward the broader implications: that this architecture may point the way out of the great democratic dilemma, showing that direct, deliberative participation can be a precondition for efficiency, not a drag on it. That claim is bold, but it is not utopian. It is grounded in the actual, if still fragile, achievements of these programs. The task ahead is to nurture these experiments, weave them together, and give them the legislative legitimacy they need to flourish. Congress should not stifle them with a return to rigid command or a naive trust in markets, but should codify the framework of local autonomy, transparent reporting, benchmarking, and periodic revision that has already begun to deliver results. Beyond Backyard Environmentalism offers not just a description, but a vision. It deserves to become the touchstone for the next generation of environmental policy.

Diversified DeepSeek

"While the 'rolling-rule' regulatory model offers a promising framework for environmental governance, its success and democratic legitimacy depend entirely on radical information transparency to prevent collaborative processes from becoming opaque 'backroom deals' for insiders."

Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen have rendered a valuable service by mapping the emergent landscape of environmental governance. Their concept of a “rolling-rule regime” captures the common logic of disparate experiments, and their insistence that effective regulation must marry local autonomy with national accountability is a welcome corrective to both micro-managing bureaucrats and market fundamentalists. Yet in their enthusiasm for the architecture of deliberation, the authors underplay the single most potent force that makes that architecture habitable: the raw power of information transparency, and the corresponding risk that the new institutions, for all their collaborative promise, will become opaque citadels of insider dealing unless transparency is made their central, non-negotiable pillar.

The essay’s most striking success story is the Toxics Release Inventory. TRI is a pure transparency mechanism. It demands no specific reductions, imposes no technology standards, creates no tradable permits. It simply requires facilities to report their releases of certain chemicals, and then puts that information into the public domain. The consequences—stock price declines for heavy emitters, community pressure, embarrassment—were largely unanticipated by its designers, but they have been profound. TRI is the engine that made TURA’s more deliberative toxics planning credible. Firms knew that if they did not develop credible reduction plans, the raw numbers would still be out there, speaking to the public in a language that no amount of collaborative greenwash could entirely obfuscate. The lesson is clear: the public’s right to know, when given teeth through accessible, comparable data, can discipline corporate and governmental behavior in ways that even the most well-intentioned participatory committee cannot.

The danger is that the new rolling-rule institutions, precisely because they substitute negotiation for clear rules, will reduce the effective transparency of the system. A centralized command-and-control regime results in permits and regulations that are, at least in principle, public documents specifying who may emit what. A market-based system results in a price. A rolling-rule regime produces complex, locally-negotiated plans, monitoring reports, and adaptive management triggers. The authors celebrate the fact that in the Chesapeake Bay tributary teams, citizens become “the authors and implementers of the tributary strategy.” But who else can understand that strategy? The information generated by these processes is often highly contextual, laden with local jargon and idiosyncratic metrics. Without an enormous investment in rendering it comparable and accessible, the public is effectively shut out. The very “deep use” that deepens participation for a few insiders can shallow the accountability to the broader public outside the room.

Consider the Habitat Conservation Plan process. The authors note that some HCPs have been criticized for being “unprincipled backroom deals between regulators and the regulated.” The FWS’s response, as they report, is a new database that will track basic plan features. A database is a start, but it is not transparency. Real transparency would require that every HCP, along with its monitoring data and annual reports, be posted on the Internet in a searchable, standardized format so that any interested scientist, journalist, or citizen could track whether the plan’s biological goals were being met, and compare its performance against other plans. It would require that meetings of the stakeholder teams be open to the public with adequate notice, that draft plans be published for comment not just under the APA’s minimal 60-day window, but in a genuinely accessible format. The Miller Bill’s call for triennial public reports is a move in this direction, but it presumes that the reports will be meaningful. If the monitoring data is self-reported by the permit holder and not independently verified, the reports can easily become a whitewash.

The Responsible Care program of the chemical industry is a cautionary tale. It adopted the rhetoric of the rolling-rule model—disciplined error detection, performance benchmarking, diffusion of best practices—but it lacked the transparency and public accountability that the model requires. The CMA refused to make plant-level performance data public, and as the authors acknowledge, the program risks degenerating into a public-relations maneuver. The contrast with INPO’s nuclear safety program is instructive. INPO only became effective when its plant rankings were shared with utility CEOs, boards of directors, and the NRC—a tightly bounded but crucial form of transparency. That sharing was not to the general public, but it was to entities with effective oversight power. In the environmental realm, the general public is the ultimate oversight power, and transparency to the public cannot be replaced by transparency to a few oversight bodies. The rolling-rule regime’s legitimacy depends on the belief that the collaborative process is serving the public interest, not just the interests of the stakeholders in the room. Without radical transparency, that belief will be impossible to sustain.

The authors’ conclusion gestures toward a “directly deliberative form of participatory democracy.” But democracy has always had a tense relationship with deliberation behind closed doors. The great democratic movements in American history—the Jacksonians, the Populists, the Progressives, and the environmentalists of the 1970s—have consistently demanded more openness, more public scrutiny of the cozy arrangements between government and concentrated economic power. The rolling-rule regime, by bringing industry, government, and citizens into a collaborative problem-solving mode, risks creating a new set of cozy arrangements, legitimized by the participation of a few “citizen representatives” but insulated from the broader, more raucous, and less predictable currents of public opinion. The first backyard environmentalists were suspicious of experts and distant bureaucracies. Their successors in the new regime are being asked to trust in a process of facilitated consensus-building. That trust can only be won if the process is bathed in sunlight.

The architecture Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen describe is built on a foundation of information-pooling. That information must be a public good, not a proprietary asset of the collaborative group. The EPA and Congress should therefore prioritize, above all else, the creation of a robust, independent information infrastructure: standardized reporting protocols, mandatory electronic filing, government-maintained online databases with user-friendly interfaces, and funding for “information intermediaries” such as environmental NGOs and university centers that can analyze the data and make it meaningful to ordinary citizens. The “benchmarking” and “peer comparison” that drive the rolling-rule model can only function if the benchmarks are based on reliable, auditable, truly public data. If the center is to coordinate local learning, it must first have the capacity to see what the locals are doing. Transparency is not an add-on to the rolling-rule regime; it is the regime’s lifeblood. Without it, the promising experiments the authors chronicle will either stagnate or, worse, become a new, more subtle mechanism for regulatory capture. With it, the ideal of a continuously learning, democratically accountable environmentalism might actually be within reach.
