"To successfully dethrone fossil capital on a global scale, climate politics must expand its focus beyond emissions reduction to dismantle the broader 'fossil-creditor order' of debt, unequal trade, and colonial extraction that forces the Global South into extractive dependence."

The call to “dethrone fossil capital” is compelling, but from much of the Global South it also sounds incomplete. Fossil capital should indeed be defeated. Yet the political economy of fossil dependence is not only a story of oil companies blocking enlightened climate policy. It is also a story of debt, unequal exchange, development hierarchy, trade rules, currency dependence, and the long afterlife of colonial extraction. If the twenty-first-century two-step is to be more than a slogan devised in the North, its first step must be broader: dethrone the fossil-creditor order.

Táíwò gestures toward this when he invokes Samir Amin, debt cancellation, and the decolonization of the atmosphere. Those gestures should be moved to the center. For many states, fossil extraction is not merely the result of elite corruption or ideological backwardness. It is one of the few ways to obtain hard currency, service debt, finance imports, and maintain a minimal fiscal base under conditions not of their own choosing. A country may know that oil development is ecologically dangerous and politically distorting and still face a brutal balance-of-payments problem. It may be told by international lenders to attract foreign investment, expand exports, and stabilize its currency. Then, when it discovers oil or gas, wealthy countries and private companies arrive with finance, expertise, and contracts. Later the same wealthy world lectures it about emissions.

This does not absolve Southern elites. Many have enriched themselves through extractive deals, repressed communities, and used oil and gas rents to avoid democratic accountability. But a serious climate politics cannot pretend that all fossil producers occupy the same position. ExxonMobil, the U.S. government, Guyana, Mozambique, Nigeria, Brazil, Norway, Saudi Arabia, and the Democratic Republic of Congo are not interchangeable actors in a morality play. They inhabit an unequal world system.

The language of “phaseout” can therefore mean very different things depending on who is speaking and who is being addressed. When directed at wealthy countries that grew rich through coal, oil, gas, colonial plunder, and atmospheric overuse, phaseout is a minimal requirement of justice. The United States, Canada, Australia, Norway, and the United Kingdom have no defensible claim to continued fossil expansion. Their resources, technology, and historic responsibility obligate them to move first and fastest. When directed at poorer countries still struggling with electrification, public health, food systems, and industrial development, phaseout must be paired with finance, technology, and trade transformation. Otherwise it becomes climate austerity.

This is the weakness of some Northern climate discourse. It says: keep it in the ground. Often that is right. But people then ask: who will pay the wages not earned, the hospitals not built, the debt not serviced, the energy not generated, the fertilizer not imported, the adaptation infrastructure not constructed? Too often the answer is silence, philanthropy, or private green investment structured to protect investors first. That is not a transition. It is a moral command without a development program.

Táíwò is correct that the state remains indispensable. But the relevant state is often a fiscally constrained state, a debtor state, a postcolonial state whose policy space is narrower than formal sovereignty suggests. A minister may wish to build renewable energy but face high borrowing costs. A utility may wish to expand the grid but be insolvent. A government may wish to subsidize clean cooking but be under pressure to cut public spending. A country may have excellent solar resources but lack storage, transmission, manufacturing capacity, or affordable finance. Meanwhile a gas export project promises royalties and foreign exchange. That promise may be exaggerated, but it is not politically imaginary.

Thus, if fossil capital is to be dethroned globally, rich countries must do more than decarbonize themselves. They must make fossil development economically unnecessary for poorer countries. That means large-scale public climate finance on concessional terms, not mostly loans that deepen debt. It means debt cancellation, especially where climate disasters have already made repayment a form of extraction. It means technology sharing and relaxation of intellectual property barriers where they impede transition. It means support for public grids, not just bankable private projects. It means fair prices and labor standards in transition mineral supply chains. It means ending the hypocrisy of financing gas abroad while claiming climate leadership at home.

The question of transition minerals is especially important. A world moving away from fossil fuels will need lithium, copper, nickel, cobalt, graphite, manganese, rare earths, and many other materials. If governed by the same extractive logic, the green transition will reproduce colonial patterns: raw materials leave poor regions; value-added manufacturing, patents, and profits accumulate elsewhere; local communities receive pollution, displacement, and militarization. Dethroning fossil capital cannot mean crowning battery capital, mining capital, or asset managers as the new sovereigns. Countries that possess critical minerals should have the right to move up value chains, regulate extraction, require local benefits, protect communities, and refuse projects that violate rights. Rich countries cannot demand secure mineral access while denying industrial policy to the states and peoples on whose lands those minerals lie.

This is where Táíwò’s attraction to public power should become an internationalist program. Public renewables in New York or Los Angeles are valuable, but public power on a planetary scale would require new institutions of financing and production. Imagine climate finance treated not as aid but as repayment of ecological debt. Imagine a global public investment program capitalized by wealthy states, taxes on fossil profits, financial transaction taxes, and redirected military spending. Imagine regional manufacturing hubs for solar panels, batteries, heat pumps, and grid equipment in Africa, Latin America, South Asia, and the Caribbean, governed by labor and environmental standards. Imagine debt contracts with hurricane clauses, or better, widespread cancellation for countries whose fiscal capacity is being consumed by disasters they did little to cause. These are not utopian embellishments; they are what make fossil phaseout politically plausible.

The lead essay also raises the problem of climate change eroding “social capacities.” This is already visible. Cyclones, droughts, floods, heat waves, and crop failures do not merely damage infrastructure. They consume political attention, exhaust public budgets, displace communities, and create openings for militarized and xenophobic politics. In such contexts, telling states to prioritize mitigation without funding adaptation is unrealistic and unjust. For many vulnerable countries, seawalls, drainage systems, climate-resilient agriculture, public health, and relocation planning are immediate matters of survival. If rich countries want a global fossil phaseout, they must help create the conditions under which vulnerable countries are not forced to choose between adaptation and decarbonization.

There is another issue: sovereignty. Climate politics sometimes treats sovereignty as an obstacle, because states defend fossil development in the name of national interest. But for formerly colonized peoples, sovereignty remains a hard-won shield, however imperfect, against external control. A just climate strategy must not become a new mandate system in green clothing. Fossil fuel nonproliferation, for example, is a promising idea only if it embeds equity: wealthy producers move first; poorer producers receive compensation and development alternatives; affected communities participate; and enforcement does not become another tool used selectively by powerful states.

Similarly, carbon border adjustment policies in Europe and elsewhere may encourage cleaner production, but they can also function as protectionism if poorer countries lack finance to decarbonize industry. Trade rules must be redesigned to enable green industrialization in the South, not simply punish carbon-intensive exports from countries denied the means to transform. Climate justice cannot be reduced to emissions accounting at the border. It must ask who controls technology, who captures value, who bears risk, and who has room to develop.

Táíwò’s essay is at its best when it rejects carbon reductionism. I would extend that rejection to “transition reductionism.” The goal is not simply to replace fossil energy with renewable energy. It is to transform the global relations that made fossil dependence rational for some and profitable for others. A solar panel assembled under coercive labor conditions, financed by predatory debt, installed on grabbed land, and used to power an export enclave is not climate justice. It may reduce emissions, and that matters, but it does not answer the political question.

What, then, should the two steps be? First, dismantle the power of fossil capital together with the debt and trade regimes that sustain extractive dependence. Second, build a democratic, reparative, low-carbon development order. These steps overlap, but the distinction matters. Without the first, the second will be blocked. Without the second, the first will look to many countries like a demand for permanent subordination.

The climate movement in wealthy countries has a special responsibility. It must attack its own fossil companies, banks, militaries, consumption patterns, and governments. It must oppose new drilling and export infrastructure at home. It must also fight for international obligations that are large enough to be credible. A politics that demands sacrifice from the poor while protecting Northern affluence will fail morally and strategically. It will strengthen the very forces Táíwò fears: authoritarian nationalism, climate denial, and extractive populism.

Dethroning fossil capital is necessary. But fossil capital sits on a larger throne built by empire, debt, and unequal development. Climate justice must bring the whole structure down.

  6. 06The 'two-step' strategy must simultaneously dismantle the global debt and trade regimes while building a democratic, reparative development order to prevent climate policy from becoming a form of 'climate austerity' for the poor.
