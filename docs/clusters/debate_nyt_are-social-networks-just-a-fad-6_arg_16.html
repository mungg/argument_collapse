"While social media usage is a permanent shift in human behavior, the massive financial valuations and business models built on monetizing that behavior are a fragile fad."

If we are asking whether social networks are a fad, we must separate the user behavior from the business model. The behavior—sharing photos, updating statuses, messaging friends—is likely a permanent addition to the human repertoire. But the multi-billion-dollar business models built entirely on the premise of monetizing that behavior? That is a massive, incredibly fragile fad, and Facebook’s disastrous IPO is the first tremor of a bursting bubble.

Right before Facebook went public, General Motors made headlines by pulling its entire $10 million advertising budget from the platform, stating simply that the ads didn't work. This is the elephant in the room for the entire Web 2.0 economy. Consumers use social media to interact with their friends, not to be sold cars or shampoo. Unlike a Google search, where a user is actively declaring an intent to find a product or service, a social media user is in a passive, conversational state.

Furthermore, the entire internet is migrating to mobile. Facebook is desperately trying to figure out how to cram profitable advertisements onto a four-inch smartphone screen without alienating its user base. Thus far, nobody has solved the mobile monetization puzzle for social networking.

So, will people stop using social networks? No. But the era of viewing these platforms as trillion-dollar economic titans that will replace traditional media and advertising is coming to an end. Social media will endure as a communication utility, much like email. But nobody expects their email provider to be the most valuable company on Earth. The fad is not the network; the fad is the valuation.
