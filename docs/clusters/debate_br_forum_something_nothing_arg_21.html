Diversified Claude

"While Nir Rosen correctly identifies the strategic failures of the Afghanistan war, his critique is too narrow because it fails to challenge the underlying imperial nature of the conflict and the illegitimate premise of American military intervention."

Nir Rosen has produced the most damning portrait of the Afghanistan war I have read in any mainstream American publication this year, and yet I find myself wanting to argue that he has not gone far enough. His essay treats the failure of counterinsurgency in Afghanistan as a tragedy of mistaken strategy, of bad lessons drawn from Iraq, of military culture not up to the task. I want to suggest something harsher: that the project was never going to succeed because it was never really about what it claimed to be about, and that the framework Rosen uses — strategic, technical, evaluative — flatters a war that does not deserve to be evaluated on its own terms.

Look at the structure of Rosen's argument. He grants the legitimacy of the question "how do we win in Afghanistan?" and then patiently shows that we cannot. He grants the legitimacy of the question "is al Qaeda a serious threat?" and then suggests, gently, that perhaps it has been overstated. He grants the legitimacy of the question "did the Surge work in Iraq?" and then explains that it worked for reasons other than the ones credited. Each of these moves is correct as far as it goes. But they all leave intact the underlying premise that American military force is a tool reasonably deployed against problems in the world, and that the only question is which problems, and how.

The eight years of the Afghan war have killed tens of thousands of Afghans, displaced hundreds of thousands more, and produced exactly the warlord-restored, opium-soaked, election-rigged client state Rosen describes. None of this was an accident or a deviation from the plan. The 2001 invasion was a punitive expedition launched without serious deliberation, supported by a population whose grief was being channeled by a media that had spent a decade preparing the ground for it. The Bonn Conference excluded the actual losing party in the civil war, the Taliban, ensuring an unstable settlement. The early years of the occupation enthusiastically empowered the Northern Alliance warlords whose abuses had created the constituency for the Taliban in the first place. Karzai was chosen, not elected, in any meaningful sense. The 2009 ballot was the predictable outcome of a system designed to legitimate, not to choose.

When Rosen describes Sergeant Dyer's contempt for the Afghan police, he is documenting something more than a personality clash. He is documenting the texture of empire. The "Haji" slur, the casual references to "man jammies," the certainty that Afghans "suck at shooting," the language of "gangbangers" applied to a foreign police force — this is the lingua franca of every occupying army in history. It is not a defect in COIN implementation. It is what occupation sounds like from the inside. Rosen seems to expect that with better leadership, longer rotations, or more anthropological training, this could be different. I do not believe it could. The structural relationship — armed foreigners with vastly superior firepower, operating under their own legal regime, attempting to remake another people's politics — produces this culture every time, regardless of the personal virtues of the participants.

And what is the goal? Rosen accepts, more or less, that the goal is preventing al Qaeda from re-establishing safe havens. But the actual operative goal, eight years in, is the preservation of the prestige and credibility of the United States military and political establishments. This is why withdrawal is unthinkable even when continuation is also unthinkable. This is why the bureaucracy produces ever more elaborate justifications for staying — protecting women, building schools, denying sanctuary, training police — that shift as each previous justification collapses under scrutiny. This is why both the Bush and Obama administrations have committed more force to a country whose strategic significance to the United States is, candidly, minimal. The war exists because ending it would be a political defeat for the people who started it and the people who inherited it.

Rosen's closing paragraphs gesture in this direction. His observation that al Qaeda was a response to American imperial excesses, not a freestanding evil, points toward a real political analysis. His point that "addressing these problems at their roots would do much more — for Afghans and for us — than sending in the military once more to do the work of decent politics" is exactly right. But he says it too softly, almost as an afterthought, after seven thousand words of detailed argument that take the strategy debate on its own terms. The strategy debate is the problem. As long as the only acceptable critique is one that engages with troop levels, COIN doctrine, and rules of engagement, the underlying question — what is the United States doing with a military empire of nearly a thousand foreign bases, and why does it imagine it has the right to remake the politics of nations on the other side of the planet — never comes up for honest consideration.

The cult of celebrity that Rosen lampoons in the figures of Petraeus and McChrystal is a symptom of this deeper pathology. A serious republic does not produce hagiographies of its generals during a war the public has barely noticed. A serious republic does not let a cadre of think-tank pundits — Kagan, Biddle, O'Hanlon, Cordesman — float from one war's advocacy to the next without ever being held to account for the consequences of their previous advice. A serious republic does not allow a sitting president to inherit a war, conduct a "review," and emerge with a recommendation that essentially ratifies what the military requested. The Obama process Rosen describes — three months of policy review culminating in a near-total adoption of McChrystal's framing — is not democratic civilian control of the military. It is its absence dressed in the costume of deliberation.

I do not write this from a position of pacifism. I write it from a position of having watched, like Rosen, what eight years of this war have done. The American left, what remains of it, has largely abandoned the antiwar position it briefly occupied during the Iraq debate. The election of Obama drained the energy from the streets, and the careful liberal hawks reasserted themselves over the policy discussion. Rosen's essay, for all its sharpness, is a product of that consensus. He is writing the most aggressive critique the consensus permits. I want a critique the consensus does not permit: that this war should never have been fought, that the larger imperial project of which it is a part should be dismantled, and that the lives of Afghan farmers and American soldiers alike have been spent for nothing more elevated than the convenience of a political class that cannot bring itself to admit a mistake.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The failure of the Afghan state and the rise of the Taliban are not accidental strategic errors but the predictable results of an occupation that empowered corrupt warlords from the start.
  2. 02The racism and contempt displayed by American soldiers toward Afghans are inherent features of any foreign occupation rather than defects in counterinsurgency training or leadership.
  3. 03The war continues primarily to preserve the prestige and political credibility of the U.S. military and political establishments rather than to achieve genuine security goals like stopping al Qaeda.
  4. 04Engaging in technical debates over troop levels and counterinsurgency doctrine distracts from the more fundamental question of whether the United States has the right to forcibly remake other nations' politics.
  5. 05The lack of accountability for pro-war pundits and the military's ability to dictate policy to the president signal a breakdown of democratic civilian control in the United States.
