"The state cannot legitimately ban abortion because it lacks a public justification strong enough to override a woman's autonomy, as the claim that a fetus is a person from conception is not a reason that all reasonable citizens are compelled to accept."

Judith Jarvis Thomson has done something more useful than simply repeat the familiar defense of abortion as a matter of “privacy.” She has asked what kind of reason a constitutional democracy may use when it imposes a grave burden on citizens. That is the right question. The abortion debate is not merely a contest between two metaphysical assertions, one about fetal personhood and one about women’s autonomy, with the state free to pick either. It is a debate about coercion.

This point is often lost. Opponents of abortion say: “You cannot prove that the fetus is not a person.” Supporters answer: “You cannot prove that it is.” Then someone concludes that the matter is evenly balanced and should be settled by majority vote. Thomson rightly denies the symmetry. If the state bans abortion, it does not merely express a moral opinion. It forces a woman to remain pregnant, to undergo childbirth, and to assume all the risks, indignities, and life consequences that follow. The citizen who demands such a law is demanding state power over another person’s body. That demand requires a public justification of unusual strength.

The most important sentence in Thomson’s essay is her claim that “what the supporters want is a license to impose force; what the deniers want is a license to be free of it.” That does not by itself settle every question about abortion. But it does establish where the burden lies. Those who would make abortion criminal must show not merely that their view is sincere, or religiously grounded, or emotionally compelling, but that women are unreasonable in rejecting it. On the question whether a fertilized egg has the moral status of a child, that showing has not been made.

Indeed, it is striking how much anti-abortion argument depends on sliding between images. At one moment we are asked to contemplate a newborn baby; at the next we are told that the same moral conclusion applies to a zygote. But the emotional force of the first image cannot do the argumentative work required for the second. A baby has a face, cries, suffers, responds, is already enmeshed in human relations. A fertilized egg has human DNA and, under certain conditions, may develop into such a being. That difference matters to many reasonable people. It is not moral blindness to think so.

Thomson is also right to resist Ronald Dworkin’s too-comfortable interpretation of abortion opponents. Many of them really do believe abortion is murder. It is condescending, and politically foolish, to say they cannot mean what they say. But taking them seriously is not the same as granting them legal authority. A citizen may believe, with complete sincerity, that divorce is sinful, that war is always murder, or that eating animals is a grave evil. In a pluralist republic, sincerity is not enough to justify legal compulsion.

Where Thomson’s essay could be strengthened is in acknowledging that some regulations need not rest on the claim that the fetus is a rights-bearing person from conception. One may think that later fetal development has moral significance, or that the state has some legitimate interest in ensuring that decisions are informed, without embracing the absolutism of the encyclical. The danger is that “informed consent” and waiting periods often become devices of humiliation rather than information. Still, a liberal defense of abortion rights should not imply that all moral concern for fetal life is irrational or oppressive. The public may reasonably distinguish early abortion from late abortion, contraception from abortion, and medical necessity from elective procedure.

But those distinctions strengthen rather than weaken the case for the core right. Most abortions occur early. The strongest anti-abortion claim—that from conception there exists a person with a right to life equal to that of a born child—is precisely the claim least available as a basis for coercive law in a society of free and equal citizens. Casey’s preservation of Roe’s “central holding” therefore remains morally defensible, even if its “undue burden” standard will invite endless litigation.

Finally, Thomson’s opening reminder of violence matters. To call abortion “murder” in ordinary political speech is not the same as pulling a trigger. Responsibility for murder belongs to murderers. But words shape moral permission. If doctors are repeatedly described as baby-killers, if women are portrayed as accomplices in slaughter, if laws permitting abortion are said to have no moral validity, then some unstable adherents will draw the conclusion that killing providers is rescue. Leaders who use such language cannot wash their hands entirely of its foreseeable effects.

The right to abortion is not secure merely because the Supreme Court has spoken. Rights require institutions, doctors, clinics, public courage, and a moral vocabulary adequate to defend them. Thomson supplies part of that vocabulary. A society that cannot justify forcing women to remain pregnant has no right to do so.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The abortion debate is fundamentally about the state's use of coercion, placing a heavy burden of proof on those who wish to use state power to force a woman to remain pregnant.
  2. 02The moral status of a fertilized egg is not equivalent to that of a newborn baby, and it is not unreasonable for citizens to distinguish between the two.
  3. 03Sincere moral or religious belief, such as the conviction that abortion is murder, is insufficient to justify legal compulsion in a pluralist republic.
  4. 04While the state may have a legitimate interest in regulating later-term abortions or ensuring informed consent, these interests do not justify an absolute ban on the core right to abortion.
  5. 05The inflammatory rhetoric used by abortion opponents, such as labeling the procedure as murder, creates a moral climate that foreseeably leads to violence against providers.
