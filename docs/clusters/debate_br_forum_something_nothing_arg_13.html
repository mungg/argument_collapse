"The failure of the U.S. strategy in Afghanistan is rooted in treating the conflict as a self-contained counterinsurgency rather than addressing the regional dynamics, particularly the role of Pakistan and other neighboring powers."

Rosen’s account of Helmand is vivid, but the center of gravity in the Afghan war may not be in Helmand at all. It may be in Pakistan: in Quetta, North and South Waziristan, the settled cities where militants raise money and recruit, and the headquarters of a Pakistani security establishment that has long distinguished between militants who threaten Pakistan and militants useful for influence in Afghanistan. Any strategy that treats Afghanistan as a self-contained counterinsurgency is likely to fail, no matter how many Marines patrol Highway 601.

This does not invalidate Rosen’s critique. On the contrary, it strengthens it. If the Taliban can retreat, refit, and receive support across a border the United States cannot seal and Pakistan will not fully police, then population-centric counterinsurgency in southern Afghanistan faces a structural disadvantage. American forces may clear a district, Afghan police may man checkpoints, aid projects may begin, and still the insurgent leadership can wait, intimidate at night, and return when foreign attention shifts. A fish may need water, as Mao’s phrase has it, but it also benefits from a neighboring pond.

Pakistan’s role is not simple. The Pakistani state has suffered terribly from militancy. The Pakistani Taliban have attacked soldiers, police, markets, mosques, and civilians. The army’s operations in Swat and South Waziristan show that Islamabad recognizes some jihadist groups as existential threats. Yet Pakistan’s policy has been selective. Afghan Taliban networks, especially those seen as providing “strategic depth” against India, have often been treated differently from groups attacking the Pakistani state. This distinction may be morally indefensible and strategically dangerous, but it is rooted in Pakistan’s history: fear of encirclement by India, desire for influence in Kabul, resentment of Pashtun nationalism, and distrust of American staying power.

Rosen criticizes the assumption that failing to build a unified Afghan state will lead to al Qaeda’s return. He is right that al Qaeda is already better protected in Pakistan than it would be in Afghanistan. But that fact should lead to a broader argument: the Afghan war cannot be won by Afghan policy alone. If al Qaeda and Taliban leaders enjoy sanctuary in Pakistan, then American troops in Afghanistan are addressing symptoms. If Pakistan believes the United States will eventually leave and India will gain influence in Kabul, then elements of Pakistan’s establishment will hedge by preserving ties to Afghan insurgents. If India and Pakistan remain locked in hostility, Afghanistan will remain an arena for proxy competition. Counterinsurgency doctrine has little to say about this.

Obama’s decision to send more troops may buy time, but time for what? If the time is used merely to train Afghan forces and pressure Karzai, it will be wasted. If it is used to construct a regional settlement, it may have value. The United States should define success not as defeating every Taliban unit but as changing the incentives of Afghanistan’s neighbors.

The first requirement is a more candid bargain with Pakistan. Washington has provided billions in military and economic assistance since 2001, yet anti-Americanism remains high and cooperation remains uneven. Public threats can backfire, feeding Pakistani nationalism and conspiracy theories. Blank checks also fail, reinforcing the belief that Pakistan can extract rents while hedging. Aid should be sustained but conditional in specific ways: action against identifiable al Qaeda and Afghan Taliban leadership networks, tighter controls on militant fundraising, intelligence cooperation, and transparency in the use of military assistance. At the same time, the United States must acknowledge Pakistan’s legitimate security concerns without endorsing its destructive methods. A stable Afghanistan should not be a platform for Indian encirclement; nor should it be a Pakistani client.

Second, India must be part of the conversation, even if indirectly. India’s development work in Afghanistan has been popular among many Afghans and should not be sacrificed to Pakistani paranoia. But New Delhi should understand that visible Indian security involvement in Afghanistan can intensify Pakistani fears and strengthen hardliners. Confidence-building between India and Pakistan—trade, talks on Kashmir, crisis communication, limits on proxy activity—may do more for Afghan stability than another battalion in Helmand. The United States cannot impose peace between nuclear rivals, but it can use diplomatic capital to reduce incentives for competition in Afghanistan.

Third, Iran should not be excluded. Tehran opposed the Taliban in the 1990s and cooperated with the United States in the early stages after 2001 before being labeled part of an “axis of evil.” Iran has interests in western Afghanistan, narcotics control, refugee flows, and preventing extremist Sunni domination. U.S.-Iran relations are poisoned by the nuclear issue and domestic politics on both sides, especially after Iran’s disputed 2009 election and repression. Still, Afghanistan is an area where limited cooperation is conceivable. Refusing to talk to Iran because of other disputes only narrows American options.

Fourth, Saudi Arabia and Gulf donors matter because money and religious networks matter. Private funding streams have supported radical institutions across the region. Diplomatic pressure on Gulf states to monitor charities and donors is less dramatic than a military offensive but may be more relevant to the long-term struggle against jihadist networks. Likewise, reconciliation efforts may require mediators with Islamic legitimacy, though not at the cost of handing Afghan politics to outside patrons.

Fifth, Russia, China, and the Central Asian states have stakes in narcotics, extremism, and supply routes. Their interests do not align perfectly with America’s, but none benefits from uncontrolled jihadist sanctuaries. A regional framework should include them where practical. The alternative is a war supplied through fragile routes and contested by neighbors pursuing separate agendas.

Rosen’s essay gives little confidence that the Afghan government can become legitimate under current conditions. Regional diplomacy cannot solve that problem, but it can reduce the external fuel feeding it. Afghanistan’s internal factions have always been shaped by outside support. The anti-Soviet jihad was internationalized; the civil war drew in Pakistan, Iran, Saudi Arabia, India, Russia, and others; the Taliban’s rise was inseparable from Pakistani sanctuary and support. To imagine that today’s insurgency can be defeated solely by protecting Afghan villages is ahistorical.

A regional approach also requires reducing American maximalism. Pakistan will not help the United States build a strong Afghan state aligned with India. Iran will not help consolidate a permanent American military presence on its eastern border. Russia will not welcome an open-ended NATO project in Central Asia. China will not contribute much to a strategy defined by American primacy. But these states may cooperate around narrower goals: no al Qaeda sanctuary, no Afghan civil war spilling across borders, no monopoly of power by a faction that threatens neighbors, and limits on narcotics and refugee destabilization.

This suggests a political settlement in Afghanistan that may be messy and decentralized. Some Taliban elements may enter local or national arrangements. Former Northern Alliance factions will retain influence. Kabul’s authority may remain limited. Women’s rights and democratic reforms may be uneven and contested. This is not the Afghanistan Americans hoped for in 2001. But it may be the Afghanistan that regional powers can tolerate and Afghans can inhabit without perpetual war.

Rosen’s anger at counterinsurgency fashion is justified. Washington loves doctrines that make distant societies appear manageable. But the opposite error is to focus so closely on the village that one misses the region. The frightened elder on Highway 601 is affected by decisions in Kabul, Quetta, Rawalpindi, New Delhi, Tehran, Riyadh, and Washington. His fear cannot be understood only by asking whether Team Prowler practices good COIN.

If Obama’s escalation is to serve any purpose, it should create leverage for diplomacy, not an illusion of military victory. The United States should tell Pakistan and Afghanistan’s neighbors that it will not occupy Afghanistan indefinitely, but neither will it permit the country to become a base for transnational terrorism. It should seek a settlement that separates Afghan insurgents from al Qaeda, reduces proxy warfare, and accepts Afghanistan’s plural political reality. Without that regional bargain, Rosen’s Highway 601 will remain what it is now: a dangerous road on which local men die while the larger war regenerates beyond the horizon.
