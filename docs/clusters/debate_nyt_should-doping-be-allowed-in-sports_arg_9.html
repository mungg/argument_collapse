"Doping should be permitted in sports because athletes are consenting adults who deserve bodily autonomy and the right to pursue excellence through science."

Alex Schwazer and an unnamed judoka are the latest casualties of a paternalistic and intrusive regime that treats elite athletes as children incapable of making their own choices. The question is not whether doping is “dangerous” or “unfair”—elite sport is inherently dangerous and profoundly unfair—but whether the state has the right to dictate what consenting adults put into their bodies in pursuit of excellence. The answer is no. Doping should be allowed, not because it is safe, but because personal liberty demands it. However, this liberty must be paired with a radical transparency that restores honesty to competition.

The bodily autonomy argument is straightforward. Every year, athletes undergo surgeries, injections, and therapies that carry long-term risks, all in the name of performance. We praise a gymnast who competes on a broken ankle with the help of painkillers, yet demonize a swimmer who uses a controlled substance to recover faster from training. The only relevant difference is a moralistic line drawn by sports bureaucrats. If an informed athlete decides that the glory of a gold medal is worth a small chance of future health problems, that is their value judgment to make. It is not for the World Anti-Doping Agency to play the role of a nanny state.

What about fairness? As the system stands, doping is already a secret privilege of the best-connected. Legalizing it—with a requirement of full public disclosure—would actually be fairer. Every athlete would list their pharmaceutical regimen, just as they list their coaches and equipment. Fans and sponsors could then make their own assessments. If someone wins with a disclosed course of EPO, the record books would note it, and the public could decide how to value that achievement. The current hypocrisy, where athletes are quietly using and only the unlucky are punished, is far more corrosive to the sport’s integrity than open acknowledgment.

The only acceptable limit is informed consent and safety standards: bans on substances for juniors, mandatory medical oversight, and prohibiting practices that cause immediate, irreparable harm. But the core principal should be that an athlete’s body is their own laboratory. Let them decide, let us know, and let the games be a truthful reflection of human ambition, powered by whatever science can offer.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current ban on doping is a paternalistic overreach that denies athletes the right to make their own value judgments regarding the risks they take for their careers.
  2. 02It is hypocritical to demonize performance-enhancing drugs while praising athletes for undergoing dangerous surgeries and competing through severe physical injuries.
  3. 03Legalizing doping with a requirement for full public disclosure would be fairer than the current system, where only the best-connected athletes can dope in secret.
  4. 04Replacing the current prohibition with a regime of radical transparency would restore honesty to competition and allow the public to accurately value athletic achievements.
  5. 05The focus of sports regulation should shift from prohibition to ensuring informed consent, medical oversight, and the protection of minors.
