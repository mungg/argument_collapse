"To effectively curb prescription drug abuse, the pharmaceutical industry must be held accountable for its role in promoting medications and must be required to help fund and implement public health solutions."

Any serious answer must include the pharmaceutical industry. Prescription drug abuse did not arise only because patients deceived doctors or doctors lacked willpower. It also grew in an environment in which powerful medications were aggressively promoted, risks were minimized, and a pill-based model of care became extraordinarily profitable.

For years, opioids were marketed to physicians with the message that pain was being undertreated and that addiction risk was small when the drugs were used for legitimate pain. That message found a receptive audience because undertreated pain was indeed a problem. But the resulting prescribing culture often outran the evidence, especially for long-term use in chronic non-cancer pain. Meanwhile, newer formulations and brand-name drugs generated enormous revenue, while communities were left to absorb the costs of dependency, overdose and diversion.

Manufacturers should be required to prove not just that a drug can relieve pain in controlled settings, but that its long-term use is supported by evidence for the populations being targeted. Labels and educational materials should be clear about addiction, tolerance, withdrawal and overdose risk, especially when combined with alcohol, benzodiazepines or sleep medications. Continuing medical education funded by companies that profit from prescribing deserves far more scrutiny. Doctors need independent education, not marketing disguised as science.

Regulators should also demand stronger post-marketing surveillance. If a drug is being diverted widely or associated with rising overdose deaths, that information should lead to rapid changes in labeling, prescribing guidance and risk management. Abuse-deterrent formulations may have a role, but they are not a magic answer; people can switch substances, and “abuse-deterrent” can become another marketing slogan if not carefully evaluated.

Finally, companies that profit from controlled substances should help pay for the public health response: prescription monitoring systems, addiction treatment, safe disposal programs and prescriber education. This is not a call to demonize medicines that many patients genuinely need. It is a call to align responsibility with profit.

We have seen this pattern before in public health: a legal product, promoted as normal and safe, produces harms that are then framed as individual failure. Individual choices matter, but they occur in a market shaped by advertising, regulation and medical culture. To curb prescription drug abuse, we must address that market. If the industry helps create demand for powerful drugs, it must also be held accountable for preventing their misuse and treating the damage.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Pharmaceutical companies aggressively marketed opioids while minimizing addiction risks, leading to a prescribing culture that prioritized profit over evidence-based care.
  2. 02Manufacturers should be required to provide long-term evidence for drug efficacy in target populations and ensure that educational materials clearly communicate the risks of addiction and overdose.
  3. 03Medical education for doctors should be independent and scrutinized to prevent marketing from being disguised as scientific guidance.
  4. 04Regulators must demand stronger post-marketing surveillance to ensure that rising rates of diversion or overdose lead to rapid changes in prescribing guidance.
  5. 05Companies that profit from controlled substances should be financially responsible for supporting prescription monitoring systems, addiction treatment, and safe disposal programs.
