"Daron Acemoglu’s proposal to redirect AI toward human-complementing tasks is a flawed strategy because it relies on an outdated historical analogy and underestimates the transformative, uncontrollable nature of AI technology."

Daron Acemoglu’s essay leans heavily on a particular historical analogy in which the post–World War II settlement serves as proof that technology can be made to serve broad-based prosperity and democracy. But that analogy is strained to the breaking point. The era he describes was a unique convergence of circumstances — the destruction of rival industrial powers, the threat of communism, the unprecedented state capacity built during total war — that cannot be replicated. Worse, the analogy distracts from a more fundamental shift in the nature of technology. AI represents not just another tool in a long line of automation but a qualitative leap toward systems that can learn, adapt, and make decisions in ways that erode the very category of “human task” on which Acemoglu’s entire scaffolding rests. His redirection proposal, however well-intentioned, is like trying to steer an avalanche with a traffic sign.

Acemoglu’s central concept is the distinction between automation (replacing human tasks) and the creation of new tasks for humans. This distinction, borrowed from his own important academic work, assumes that there will always be a frontier of tasks where humans have a comparative advantage, and that policy can tilt the balance toward generating more such tasks. But AI increasingly attacks the general capabilities — pattern recognition, language understanding, strategic reasoning — that underpin a vast range of human tasks. It is not obvious that there is a permanent, non-automatable domain of “human” work once AI surpasses narrow applications and moves toward more general abilities. Acemoglu dismisses superintelligence as something that won’t “create superintelligence anytime soon,” but the timeframe is irrelevant to the structural argument. Even if AGI is decades away, the progression of AI systems into managerial, creative, and caregiving domains suggests that the pool of tasks that can be meaningfully redesigned for humans rather than replaced may shrink far faster than we create new ones. The lesson of the twentieth century — that new tasks always emerge — was contingent on the nature of the technologies then in play. Extrapolating that pattern into an era of machine learning is an act of faith, not science.

The democratic side of Acemoglu’s argument is similarly vulnerable. He treats the weakening of democracy as a historical accident to be reversed, but AI fundamentally changes the conditions for democratic deliberation. A democracy presupposes citizens capable of forming independent judgments and a public sphere in which those judgments can be contested and aggregated. AI-powered information environments manage attention, polarize opinion, and micro-target messages with a precision that undermines the very notion of a shared reality. Acemoglu acknowledges this, but then posits that we can use AI to “protect privacy and freedom” through things like adversarial neural cryptography. This is a category error. You cannot use the same underlying logic of algorithmically mediated sociality to repair the epistemic fabric that algorithmic mediation is shredding. The problem is not a lack of technical fixes; it is that the medium itself has become a machine for producing fractured publics.

Given these structural shifts, the project of redirecting AI through government funding, norm changes, and democratic oversight becomes tragically inadequate. It assumes that the political institutions of a pre-AI world — representative democracy, regulatory agencies, public-interest science — remain capable of governing the new machine. But those institutions are themselves being hollowed out by the very technologies they are supposed to govern. The regulatory state works through notice-and-comment rulemaking, bureaucratic expertise, and judicial review; all of these depend on stable, transparent information flows that AI-driven complex systems inherently disrupt. Asking the 1970s-era EPA to regulate climate change was difficult; asking the 2021 Congress to redirect AI research toward “social benefit” while in thrall to tech lobbyists and algorithmic disinformation is a joke in poor taste.

What, then, should we do? A more honest approach would begin by accepting that we are in an era of deep technological disruption that existing political categories cannot easily master. This does not mean fatalism. It means focusing on resilience rather than direction. Instead of trying to steer AI toward pre-defined human-complementing tasks, we should build robust social institutions that can absorb whatever shocks come: a strong welfare floor, decentralized energy and food systems, local democratic assemblies that are less reliant on digital media, and legal frameworks that subordinate algorithmic decision-making to human rights courts. The goal should be to insulate human dignity from technological turbulence, not to micromanage the turbulence itself. Acemoglu’s vision of a “three-pronged approach” implicitly assumes a vantage point from which an enlightened elite can see the whole field and adjust the dials. That vantage point no longer exists, if it ever did. The better metaphor is gardening in a storm: we cannot control the weather, but we can deepen the roots and build the shelters. That modest, unheroic labor may be the only genuinely democratic response left to us.
