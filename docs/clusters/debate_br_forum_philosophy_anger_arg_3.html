"While anger correctly identifies that a wrong has occurred, forgiveness offers a necessary alternative to the cycle of grudges and revenge by refusing to let a wrongdoer’s evil dictate the victim's future will."

Agnes Callard’s essay ends with a sentence that sounds like a verdict: “We can’t be good in a bad world.” It is a severe claim, and in one sense a deeply religious one, though Callard does not present it that way. It says that evil does not merely injure bodies or institutions; it infects the moral atmosphere in which victims and perpetrators alike must breathe. The wrongdoer sins by doing wrong. The victim is tempted, perhaps forced, into anger, hatred, revenge, or else acquiescence. Innocence disappears.

I want to answer from the standpoint of forgiveness—not the cheap forgiveness that tells victims to hurry up and make everyone comfortable, but the older, harder practice that remembers wrong without returning it. Forgiveness is often caricatured as forgetting, excusing, or abandoning justice. If that were forgiveness, Callard would be right to dismiss it. But genuine forgiveness is neither amnesia nor moral weakness. It is a refusal to let the wrongdoer’s evil determine the final form of one’s own will.

Callard’s Argument for Grudges begins with an undeniable point: what happened cannot be made not to have happened. If I betray you, apologize, and repair what I can, it remains true forever that I betrayed you. Therefore, she says, your reason for anger remains. But forgiveness also begins from the irreversibility of the past. Only the unforgivable-in-the-sense-of-real can be forgiven. If the wrong were erased, there would be nothing to forgive.

The question is what fidelity to the past requires. Callard assumes that anger is the faithful custodian of the fact that the wrong occurred. But there are other custodians: lament, testimony, mourning, judgment, repentance, and covenantal memory. The Psalms, for example, are full of complaint against enemies and against God; they do not deny rage, but they bring rage into speech before a judge beyond the self. The point is not to pretend that the wound is gone. It is to prevent the wound from becoming sovereign.

A grudge is a form of memory, but not all memory is grudge. A grudge keeps the wrong present as a claim against the wrongdoer. It says: you are the one who did this, and I will hold you there. Some wrongdoers need to be held there, at least for a time, because they deny, evade, or repeat. But a grudge can also imprison the victim with the offender. Callard describes this intimacy brilliantly: the enemy colonizes fantasy, perception, conversation. Anyone who has hated knows the accuracy of this account. The hated person becomes a dark companion.

Forgiveness is not the denial that one has reason for anger. It is the decision, sometimes made long before the feeling obeys, not to organize one’s life around that reason. We often have real reasons that we rightly decline to enthrone. I may have reason to fear, but act courageously. I may have reason to despair, but hope. I may have reason to condemn, but forgive. The reason does not vanish; it is subordinated to another good.

Callard’s Argument for Revenge is more radical. She says revenge holds the wrongdoer responsible by making his principle govern the relationship: he made my bad his good, so I make his bad mine. But forgiveness rejects the premise that the wrongdoer’s act should legislate the future. If you lie, I need not answer in the language of lies. If you abandon me, I need not make abandonment my law. If you crucify, I need not become crucifier.

This does not mean there are no consequences. Forgiveness is compatible with punishment, restitution, separation, and public truth. A battered woman may forgive and still leave. A community may forgive and still demand reparations. A state may punish without hatred, at least as an aspiration. Forgiveness concerns the will’s relation to the offender’s good. It refuses to take the offender’s destruction as the object in which justice is fulfilled.

The Christian tradition speaks of vengeance as belonging to God not because wrongdoing does not matter, but because it matters too much to be entrusted to the injured human heart. This idea can be abused; it has been used to pacify the oppressed. But at its best it means that judgment is real and that I am not its final owner. I may testify, resist, protect, and demand repair. I may not need to become the mirror image of the one who harmed me.

Callard’s essay is powerful because she sees that anger’s revenge is mimetic. It copies the wrongdoer’s valuation. René Girard, whom she cites, would recognize this: enemies become doubles. Each claims to oppose the other while increasingly resembling him. Forgiveness interrupts mimetic escalation. It says: the wrong stops here, not because it is insignificant, but because its significance must not be allowed to reproduce itself endlessly.

This interruption is not natural. It can feel like death. Anyone who has tried to forgive a serious wrong knows that the self resists. Anger offers energy, identity, and the bitter dignity of refusal. Forgiveness may feel like surrender, especially when the wrongdoer is unrepentant. That is why forgiveness cannot be demanded from outside. Commanded forgiveness is often another violation. It must be free, and it may be impossible for a long time.

But the fact that forgiveness cannot be demanded does not mean it cannot be praised. We are rightly cautious about praising victims for forgiving, because such praise can become pressure. Still, a culture that loses the ability to admire forgiveness loses one of the few practices capable of answering Callard’s tragedy. Without forgiveness, the options narrow to forgetting, acquiescing, punishing, or revenging. Forgiveness creates another possibility: truthful release.

The phrase “release” needs care. It does not mean releasing the offender from accountability. It means releasing the victim from the obligation to make the offender’s bad her good. The offender may still owe apology, restitution, confession, transformed conduct. Society may still owe protection and repair. But the victim need not carry hatred as proof that the wrong mattered. The wrong mattered whether or not hatred continues.

Callard’s discussion of her grandmother shows why this is so difficult. A Holocaust survivor’s anger at a German child cannot be judged from a safe moral distance. The grandmother’s world had been destroyed. Her anger carried the dead, and perhaps protected the living. To tell her simply to forgive would be obscene. Yet if no forgiveness, or at least no transformation of memory, is possible, then the Nazis continue to govern even the granddaughter’s play. The fence remains, and the child across it remains “one of Them.” That is another victory for the perpetrators.

The moral miracle would be a memory strong enough to say both: never forget what was done, and do not make this child bear it. Such memory is not innocence. It is costly grace. It may require rituals, education, public justice, and time. It may require the survivor to say, “I cannot cross this fence, but perhaps you can.” Forgiveness often begins not as feeling but as the hope that the next generation will not be wholly conscripted into the last generation’s war.

As I write in April 2020, amid a pandemic, public anger is rising. Some of it is just. There will be failures to investigate, lies to expose, workers to defend, deaths to mourn. Forgiveness must not become a way of evading accountability for decisions that cost lives. But we will also need mercy: for frightened neighbors, exhausted nurses, mistaken officials, grieving families, and ourselves. A society that remembers only blame will not be able to mourn well. A society that forgives too cheaply will not be able to repair. We need both judgment and mercy, neither collapsed into the other.

Callard is right that we should abandon fantasies of purity. Forgiveness is not purity. It is practiced by impure people with mixed motives, recurring anger, and incomplete understanding. It often must be practiced again and again because the grudge returns. Nor does forgiveness make us good in a simple way. It may coexist with rage, fear, and the desire for vindication. But it names a direction: away from revenge, toward a good not defined by the enemy’s pain.

Can we be good in a bad world? Not if goodness means untouched innocence. But perhaps goodness has always meant something else: not the absence of wounds, but the refusal to let wounds become the whole law of one’s being. Anger tells the truth that evil has occurred. Forgiveness tells the further truth that evil need not have the last creative word.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Forgiveness is not a form of amnesia or moral weakness, but a difficult practice that remembers the irreversibility of a wrong without allowing that wrong to become the sovereign ruler of one's life.
  2. 02Fidelity to the past and the preservation of moral truth can be maintained through custodians other than anger, such as lament, testimony, and covenantal memory.
  3. 03A grudge often imprisons the victim with the offender, whereas forgiveness is a decision to stop organizing one's life around the reasons for anger.
  4. 04Forgiveness rejects the logic of revenge by denying that a wrongdoer's actions should legislate the victim's future conduct or turn the victim into a mirror image of the perpetrator.
  5. 05Forgiveness is compatible with justice, punishment, and restitution because it concerns the victim's internal relation to the offender's good rather than the removal of external accountability.
  6. 06By interrupting mimetic escalation, forgiveness prevents the significance of an evil act from endlessly reproducing itself through the next generation.
