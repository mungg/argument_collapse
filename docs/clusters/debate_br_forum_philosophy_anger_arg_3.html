"Agnes Callard’s conclusion that anger necessitates moral corruption is based on a flawed, absolutist definition of rationality that ignores proportionality, human flourishing, and the capacity for evaluative judgments to change over time."

Agnes Callard’s essay presents a compelling, if bleak, taxonomy of human anger, ultimately concluding that moral responsiveness requires a degree of moral corruption. She argues that the reasons for anger are permanent because the past is fixed (the Argument for Grudges) and that accountability intrinsically relies on vengeance (the Argument for Revenge). While her critique of the sanitized, academic versions of anger is refreshing, her ultimate conclusions are derailed by a flawed, absolutist conception of rationality. Callard’s argument succeeds only if we accept a definition of rationality that is entirely divorced from human well-being, proportionality, and the temporal nature of human life. From an Aristotelian or virtue ethics perspective, Callard completely misses the concept of the mean, confusing the *existence* of a reason with the *proportionality* of a response.

Let us examine the Argument for Grudges. Callard states that because the theft on Monday will always be a fact, the reason for your anger on Tuesday remains perfectly intact on Thursday, even after a sincere apology and full restitution. "Once you have a reason to be angry, you have a reason to be angry forever."

This is a logical sleight of hand. It conflates a historical truth with an emotional justification. To use an analogy: if I am walking outside and it starts pouring rain, I have a rational reason to open my umbrella. The fact that it rained on Monday will remain eternally true. But if the sun comes out on Wednesday, is it rational for me to keep my umbrella open on Thursday? By Callard’s logic, the historical fact of the rain gives me a reason to hold the umbrella forever.

In the moral sphere, a sincere apology, restitution, and a change in the offender's character are the "sun coming out." They change the present environment. Emotions are not merely backward-looking recorders of historical facts; they are evaluative judgments about our current standing in the world. Anger evaluates a present threat or a present lack of respect. When the offender alters their stance toward the victim through genuine repair, the threat is neutralized. To maintain the anger at its original intensity is not "perfectly rational"—it is a failure to update one's evaluative judgments based on new empirical evidence. It is, by definition, an irrational rigidity.

Callard's Argument for Revenge suffers from a similar absolutism. She claims that revenge is rational because it is the way we hold others accountable: "you make my bad your good." But this ignores the concept of proportionality, which is central to any rational moral framework. Aristotle taught that the virtuous person gets angry at the right things, toward the right people, and—crucially—for the right amount of time and to the right degree.

If someone steals from you, a proportional response of anger motivates you to demand restitution and reestablish moral boundaries. That is accountability. Callard, however, leaps from this initial boundary-setting directly to limitless, adversarial vengeance. She conflates the legitimate demand that an offender bear the cost of repair with a malicious desire to see the offender suffer for suffering's sake. The former is a rational requirement of justice; the latter is a vice.

Callard is acutely aware of the danger of limitless violence, citing Girard and Foucault. She asks, "Perhaps the simple explanation for the neglect of these arguments is that we do not want to acknowledge the possibility that morally righteous anger provides rational grounds for limitless violence."

But morally righteous anger does *not* provide rational grounds for limitless violence, precisely because human rationality encompasses more than just retrospective ledger-keeping. Rationality includes the pursuit of eudaimonia—human flourishing. A life consumed by an eternal grudge and a relentless pursuit of vengeance is antithetical to flourishing. It destroys relationships, consumes cognitive resources, and isolates the individual. As Callard herself admits, "educating me in this way is not easy on you... you divert yourself away from what would otherwise be good for you."

If an action actively diverts you from what is good for you, destroys your peace, and risks limitless violence, on what grounds can Callard call it "perfectly rational"? It is only rational if we define rationality in a vacuum, completely severed from the human organism's biological and social need to thrive.

Callard’s Grandmother example is deeply touching, illustrating the heavy toll of historical trauma. The grandmother was indeed full of anger, and she was not "innocent" in her prejudice. But to suggest that her trauma-induced prejudice is the logical, rational endpoint of moral responsiveness is a profound error. Trauma is precisely the disruption of healthy rationality; it locks the sufferer in the past. We should have immense compassion for victims of such horrific oppression, recognizing that their psychological wounds may never fully heal. But we do not honor their suffering by elevating the symptoms of their trauma to the status of a rational moral ideal. We can acknowledge the darkness of the world without surrendering our capacity for measured, forward-looking, and proportionate moral repair.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The 'Argument for Grudges' incorrectly conflates the eternal historical truth of a past wrong with a permanent emotional justification for anger, failing to account for how repair and apology change the present moral environment.
  2. 02Callard’s 'Argument for Revenge' fails by ignoring the concept of proportionality, conflating the rational demand for justice and accountability with the irrational vice of limitless, malicious vengeance.
  3. 03A response cannot be considered 'perfectly rational' if it actively destroys the individual's peace and prevents human flourishing (eudaimonia).
  4. 04The essay mistakenly elevates the symptoms of trauma and psychological rigidity to the status of a rational moral ideal rather than recognizing them as disruptions of healthy moral reasoning.
Diversified Gemini

"Agnes Callard’s claim that anger is both rational and inescapably corrupting is flawed because it relies on a static, ahistorical view of rationality that ignores the transformative power of moral repair and human growth."

Agnes Callard’s essay on the moral corruption inherent in anger presents a fascinating, if deeply pessimistic, view of human moral psychology. Her central thesis—that anger is both completely correct and inescapably corrupting—rests on two provocative arguments: the Argument for Grudges and the Argument for Revenge. By arguing that the reasons for anger are permanent because the past is unchangeable, and that revenge is the mechanism by which we enforce moral accountability, Callard concludes that to be a victim of injustice is to be morally compromised. We are forced to choose between the moral failure of acquiescence and the moral corruption of vengeful anger. However, this bleak picture relies on a fundamental misunderstanding of the relationship between time, emotion, and rationality. Callard’s Argument for Grudges treats human beings as ahistorical ledgers of moral debt, ignoring the deeply transformative nature of moral repair and the rational capacity for emotional evolution.

To understand where Callard goes wrong, we must first examine her Argument for Grudges. She posits a scenario where a person is stolen from on Monday. On Wednesday, the thief returns the item, overcompensates the victim, apologizes sincerely, and promises never to steal again. Callard asks whether it could be rational for the victim to remain just as angry on Thursday as they were on Tuesday. Her answer is yes, because the original reason for the anger—the fact that the theft occurred and was wrong—has not changed. The apology and restitution do not erase the historical fact of the theft. Therefore, Callard argues, the reason for anger remains forever, making the eternal grudge a perfectly rational response.

This argument conflates the historical fact of a wrong with the present-tense rationality of an emotional state. It is true that the past cannot be changed. The statement "you stole from me on Monday" remains eternally true. However, emotions are not merely truth-tracking cognitive states directed at isolated historical propositions; they are dynamic, relational responses to our environment and our social standing. The purpose of anger is not merely to "grasp the fact that something is broken," as Callard suggests, but to register a breach in the moral relationship and to demand its repair.

When the thief apologizes, returns the item with interest, and sincerely commits to future good behavior, the historical fact of the theft does not disappear, but the *meaning* of that fact within the ongoing relationship is fundamentally transformed. The thief has repudiated the action, realigned their values with the victim’s, and re-established the terms of mutual respect. To remain just as angry on Thursday as on Tuesday is not rational; it is profoundly irrational because it ignores new, highly relevant moral data—namely, the thief’s repentance and the restoration of the victim's moral standing. Callard's rationality is strangely static, frozen in the moment of the infraction, entirely blind to the temporal reality of human interaction.

Furthermore, Callard’s dismissal of the Stoic and Buddhist traditions is far too quick. She characterizes the anti-anger position as advocating for the eradication of anger because it is "infected with a backward-looking 'payback wish.'" But the deeper Buddhist critique of anger, for instance, is not just that it causes harm, but that it is epistemically flawed because it relies on a rigid, unchanging conception of the self and the other. Śāntideva does not merely say anger is harmful; he argues it is rooted in ignorance of impermanence and dependent origination.

When Callard argues that you have a reason to be angry forever because the past action remains forever diverged from what ought to have been done, she assumes that the "you" who was wronged and the "I" who wronged you are static entities. But the person who sincerely apologizes and makes restitution on Wednesday is not the exact same moral agent who stole on Monday. Their moral character has shifted. To hold an eternal grudge is to interact with a phantom—to punish the present self for the sins of a past self that has already been disavowed. The rationality of letting go is not akin to amnesia; it is a profound recognition of human growth and the fluidity of identity.

Callard’s Grandmother narrative is deeply moving, but it actually illustrates the tragedy of being trapped in a static moral framework rather than the "complete correctness" of eternal anger. Her grandmother, traumatized by the Holocaust, could not distinguish between an innocent ten-year-old German girl in the 1980s and the architects of the genocide. Callard interprets this as proof that the oppressed are morally corrupted by their oppression, concluding that her grandmother "wasn't innocent" and was full of anger.

But diagnosing the psychological scars of severe trauma as a *rational moral response* to injustice is a categorical error. Trauma freezes time; it makes the past perpetually present. This is a recognized psychological phenomenon, not a standard for philosophical rationality. We should view the grandmother’s inability to let go of her anger not as a valid "Argument for Grudges," but as a tragic symptom of extreme suffering. To elevate this trauma-induced rigidity to the status of a rational moral ideal is to do a disservice both to philosophy and to the victims of profound injustice. We can recognize the psychological inevitability of such anger without conceding that it is rationally justified or morally correct.

In conclusion, we are not forced into Callard’s dilemma. We can be good in a bad world, provided we understand that being good does not require erasing the past, but integrating it. Anger has its place as the initial alarm bell of injustice. But alarms are meant to be turned off once the fire is extinguished. By failing to account for the moral weight of apology, restitution, and time, Callard traps us in a burning building of her own theoretical construction.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Callard wrongly conflates the unchangeable historical fact of a past wrong with the present-tense rationality of an emotional state, which should instead respond to new moral data like apologies and restitution.
  2. 02The purpose of anger is not merely to register a past infraction but to demand a repair of the moral relationship; once that repair occurs, the meaning of the past event is transformed.
  3. 03Callard’s 'Argument for Grudges' fails to account for the fluidity of identity, mistakenly punishing a present person for the actions of a past self that they have since disavowed through repentance.
  4. 04The inability to let go of anger, as seen in Callard’s grandmother, should be understood as a tragic psychological symptom of trauma rather than a rationally justified moral response.
  5. 05True moral goodness involves integrating the past through emotional evolution rather than being trapped in a perpetual state of vengeful anger.

"Agnes Callard’s defense of the eternal grudge is unconvincing because it relies on a flawed, atomistic view of human action that ignores how moral repair and apologies fundamentally alter the normative meaning of past wrongs."

Agnes Callard’s "The Reason to Be Angry Forever" offers a startling defense of the eternal grudge. Her argument fundamentally challenges the Strawsonian consensus in contemporary moral philosophy, which holds that while negative reactive attitudes like resentment are essential to holding others responsible, they are also sensitive to moral repair. Callard argues that because the past cannot be changed, the reason for anger remains forever. Thus, to hold a grudge is perfectly rational, and the philosophical attempt to separate the moral value of anger from the irrationality of grudges is a failure. While Callard’s critique of the "purification" of anger is incisive, her Argument for Grudges is ultimately unconvincing because it misconstrues the nature of moral reasons and the ontology of human action.

According to Callard, the reason you are angry on Tuesday about a theft on Monday is the fact that the theft occurred and that it was wrong. When the thief apologizes and pays you back on Wednesday, Callard asserts that "Apologies, restitution, and all the rest do nothing to cancel or alter the fact that I stole, nor the fact that I ought not to have stolen. Those facts were your reasons to be angry." Therefore, the reason persists, and the anger should persist.

This argument relies on a highly localized and atomistic view of human action. It treats the "theft on Monday" as a hermetically sealed event, eternally discrete and unalterable. But human actions, especially in the context of interpersonal relationships, are not discrete physical events like a rock falling; they are highly context-dependent, socially embedded performances whose meanings are subject to retrospective negotiation.

When an offender offers a sincere apology, undergoes remorse, and makes restitution, they are not changing the *physical* past, but they are altering the *normative* past. As philosopher Pamela Hieronymi has argued (whom Callard mentions but whose specific arguments on forgiveness she bypasses), an offense constitutes a lingering threat: it stands as a claim by the offender that the victim can be treated in this degrading way. Resentment is a protest against that ongoing claim.

When the offender sincerely apologizes, they retract the claim. They join the victim in condemning the past act. The apology changes the moral significance of the past event. The event is no longer an active assertion of disrespect; it is a shared point of moral failure that has been disavowed. Therefore, Callard is simply incorrect when she says the reasons for anger have not changed. The reason for anger was not just the physical movement of the stolen goods; it was the expressive, insulting meaning of the theft. The apology extinguishes that expressive meaning. Once the threat is withdrawn, the rational basis for the protest—the resentment—dissolves.

To maintain the anger on Thursday, as Callard suggests is rational, is actually an epistemic failure. It is a refusal to recognize the moral reality of the apology. The eternal grudge does not track the truth; it tracks a phantom. It insists on interpreting the offender’s present self entirely through the lens of a past action that the offender has themselves repudiated.

Callard might respond, using her Argument for Revenge, that holding onto the theft is how we hold the person accountable. "You hold me accountable by holding onto my theft, refusing to forget it, turning its one-off opposition between our interests into a rule to which I am now subject." But this conflates accountability with punishment. Accountability is the demand for a moral response; it is the drawing of the offender into the space of reasons to answer for their conduct. Once they have answered—via apology and restitution—the work of accountability is complete. To continue to enforce the "rule" of opposition is no longer accountability; it is cruelty.

Callard’s reliance on Nietzsche to undergird her dark view of morality is telling. Nietzsche indeed saw morality as rooted in *ressentiment* and bloodlust. But Peter Strawson’s great contribution in "Freedom and Resentment" was to show that our reactive attitudes, while sometimes ugly, are fundamentally expressions of our vulnerability to and care for one another. We resent because we care about how others regard us. When that regard is restored through reconciliation, the resentment naturally and rationally abates.

We do not need to invent a "philosopher's fiction" of pure transition anger to avoid Callard’s trap. We merely need to recognize that human beings are temporal creatures capable of changing their minds and their moral stances. The past is fixed in its physical details, but its moral meaning is always subject to revision through the hard work of repair. Callard’s universe is one of terrifying stasis, where every wrong is an eternal stain and every victim is condemned to endless, rational rage. But in the real world, we possess the remarkable technology of forgiveness, a deeply rational process that allows us to be good, and to see others as good, even after they have been bad.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Human actions are not discrete physical events but socially embedded performances whose moral significance is subject to retrospective negotiation and revision.
  2. 02A sincere apology functions as a retraction of the offender's claim of disrespect, thereby extinguishing the expressive meaning of the wrong that served as the original reason for anger.
  3. 03Maintaining anger after an apology is an epistemic failure that refuses to recognize the moral reality of the offender's present self and their repudiation of the past act.
  4. 04Callard conflates accountability with cruelty, as true accountability is a demand for a moral response that is satisfied once the offender offers apology and restitution.
  5. 05Forgiveness is a rational technology that allows temporal creatures to revise the moral meaning of the past and restore mutual regard, avoiding the 'terrifying stasis' of eternal rage.
