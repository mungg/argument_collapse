Diversified DeepSeek

"While social endurance and epistemic humility are vital for solidarity, they must be supported by robust institutional structures and formal organizational practices rather than relying on individual moral commitment alone."

Mie Inouye’s “Solidarity Now” is an intricate, self-aware argument that moves gracefully between lived experience and political theory. I am particularly drawn to her engagement with the paradox of solidarity: that we need alliances across lines of domination to defeat those very systems, yet those systems themselves distort our interests and perceptions, making solidarity seem impossible. Inouye’s proposed solution—social endurance, underwritten by epistemic humility and an embrace of productive conflict—is innovative and, I think, fundamentally correct. But I want to take up her reading of Nathan DuFord’s work and push it a step further, because I believe the temporal dimension she highlights—the need to stick together long enough for conflict to transform us—requires a deeper institutional and structural analysis than she provides.

DuFord, as Inouye summarizes, argues that democratic solidarity is premised on disunity, that conflict within organizations is a crucial check on domination. They also argue that these organizations are not meant to be permanent. Inouye qualifies this: they must endure “for a while.” But what constitutes “a while”? And what institutional forms can sustain conflict without either dissolving or becoming repressive? Inouye’s answer seems to be a kind of virtue ethic: we must cultivate the capacity to show up. This is an individual temperament, a moral muscle. But institutions shape our capacities, and the current institutional landscape of the left—largely informal networks, social media–mediated groups, short-term campaigns—is not designed to foster endurance. It is designed for rapid mobilization and equally rapid dispersal.

The Mormon Church succeeded in instilling social endurance because it was a total institution that structured its members’ lives from childhood. It had buildings, a professional clergy (of sorts), a dense calendar of rituals, and a theology that made endurance a path to salvation. The secular left has nothing comparable. Our meetings are held in borrowed spaces, our organizations often run on shoestring budgets with volunteer labor, and our members are pulled in a hundred directions by the demands of precarious work and family life. Asking individuals to just “endure” in such a context is like telling someone to swim against a current without a boat. We need to build the boat: more robust, membership-based organizations that provide not just a place to meet but material support, mutual aid, and a sense of collective identity that can hold people through the hard times.

Inouye’s invocation of the Rainbow Coalition is instructive. The Black Panther Party, the Young Lords, and the Young Patriots were not just gatherings; they were organizations with offices, programs, and a public presence. They provided survival services—free breakfast, health clinics, legal aid—that gave people a concrete reason to join and to stay. The ideological work of forging a shared vision was embedded in these day-to-day material practices. Today’s left has some echoes of this: community fridges, bail funds, union drives. But these are often disconnected from a larger political organization that can coordinate and escalate them. Without that infrastructure, the endurance Inouye desires will remain an individual moral project, accessible mostly to those with the time and cultural capital to pursue it.

Moreover, the temporal dimension of solidarity requires us to think about life cycles and stages. A young person with few obligations may find it easier to “show up” than a middle-aged parent. Organizations that rely on endurance as a primary virtue will inevitably filter out those who cannot sustain that level of commitment, reproducing the very demographic exclusions Inouye wants to avoid. A better approach is to design organizations that can accommodate varying levels of commitment, with a strong core of dedicated cadre and a broader periphery of supporters who can be mobilized at key moments. That way, endurance is not an individual test but an organizational feature, distributed across many shoulders.

I also want to complicate Inouye’s use of conflict. She rightly notes that some conflicts are unproductive, and that the neoliberal style of conflict can atomize rather than transform. DuFord’s attempt to distinguish realistic from unrealistic conflict is inadequate, she says, because personality and strategy are often entangled. I agree. But we might look to the tradition of popular education, influenced by Paulo Freire, for a method of conflict that is structured and generative. In popular education, disagreement is channeled through processes that help participants identify the social roots of their individual experiences. The goal is not to resolve conflict but to politicize it—to move from “I am angry at you” to “we are both angry at the system, but we express it differently.” This requires skilled facilitation and a shared commitment to a pedagogical process. It is not enough to simply put people in a room and hope they fight productively; we must build the facilitation capacity to guide that conflict toward political clarity.

Ultimately, I am more optimistic than Inouye about the possibility of enduring solidarity. The 2020 uprisings did not produce a sustained movement against policing and mass incarceration, but they did produce a new generation of activists who are now deep in the long, unglamorous work of building tenant unions, workplace committees, and community defense networks. These are the seedbeds of social endurance. Inouye’s essay contributes to this work by giving it a language and a philosophical grounding. But we must supplement her virtue ethics with an institutional realism. We need both the heart to endure and the structures to make endurance a collective, sustainable practice.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The current institutional landscape of the left is too informal and precarious to foster the long-term social endurance necessary for transformative solidarity.
  2. 02To sustain commitment across different life stages and social locations, organizations must provide material support and mutual aid rather than treating endurance as an individual test of character.
  3. 03Productive conflict requires structured pedagogical methods and skilled facilitation, such as those found in popular education, to prevent disagreements from becoming atomizing or purely personal.
  4. 04Solidarity should be viewed as an organizational feature distributed across a membership base with varying levels of commitment rather than a virtue ethics accessible only to those with significant cultural capital.
