Diversified DeepSeek

"While Marcia Angell correctly identifies serious ethical scandals in medicine, her proposed solutions are overly broad and risk dismantling the essential collaborations between academia and industry that drive medical innovation."

Marcia Angell’s essay is a powerful piece of advocacy, marshaled with the authority of her years at the helm of the New England Journal of Medicine. Her moral passion is undeniable, and many of the anecdotes she recounts are genuinely disturbing. Yet a careful reading suggests that she has oversimplified a complex landscape, overstated the prevalence of systematic bias, and proposed remedies that are at once draconian and, in important respects, counterproductive. Before we dismantle the productive collaborations between academia and industry, we owe the public a more measured assessment of the evidence.

Angell’s argument rests heavily on the claim that industry-funded research is “far more likely to be favorable to the sponsors’ products.” As an epidemiological generalization, that claim has some support. But the causal interpretation is far less clear than she implies. Many of the most rigorous meta-analyses in the literature find that when you control for methodological quality, the funding effect diminishes considerably and sometimes disappears. Industry-sponsored trials are often larger, better-designed, and more closely monitored than many publicly funded ones, precisely because they must withstand FDA scrutiny. The bias Angell sees may, in part, reflect a tendency for companies to pursue only the most promising compounds—a selection effect rather than a manipulation of results. Moreover, the peer-review and editorial systems, imperfect though they are, do act as a filter. Angell herself acknowledges that the NEJM sometimes published biased studies unwittingly, but that is an argument for strengthening peer review and for requiring independent statistical analyses of clinical trial data, not for severing the financial ties that make large-scale trials possible.

The case of the FDA advisory panel on COX-2 inhibitors, which she presents as a smoking gun, deserves a closer look. After Vioxx was withdrawn, the combined panel voted on a range of options, and the final tally did include members with ties. However, the vote was advisory, and the FDA, as it was designed to do, weighed the totality of the evidence and issued a more restrictive decision. Moreover, the agency has long had rules allowing it to grant waivers for experts with financial ties when their expertise is essential, precisely because the pool of truly disinterested, qualified specialists is finite. The problem is not the existence of ties per se, but the lack of adequate safeguards—such as excluding conflicted members from voting, as some have proposed. The recent Physician Payments Sunshine Act, which will soon make all such ties transparent, may do more to clean up the process than a blanket prohibition ever could.

Angell’s assault on the Bayh-Dole Act also overreaches. She asserts that medical research was “every bit as productive before Bayh-Dole as it is now.” That is simply not the historical record. Before 1980, the fruits of billions of dollars of NIH-funded research languished because no company would risk development capital on unpatented discoveries. The biotechnology industry, which has given us monoclonal antibodies, targeted cancer therapies, and countless diagnostic tools, is an almost direct outgrowth of Bayh-Dole. Angell’s call for non-exclusive licensing of all university patents would, in practice, make it impossible for small biotech firms to attract the venture capital they need to survive the “valley of death” between laboratory discovery and clinical proof-of-concept. The result would be less innovation, not more, and it would further concentrate drug development in the hands of a few giant companies.

Finally, her proposal to ban all personal payments to clinical researchers beyond grant support would have the unintended consequence of draining academic medical centers of their most talented clinician-scientists. Many of these individuals can command far higher compensation in industry; they remain in academia partly because consulting and speaking fees allow them to supplement modest university salaries. If we make such engagement impossible, we risk losing their expertise entirely—or worse, driving it behind closed corporate doors where there is no peer review at all.

None of this is to deny that serious problems exist. Ghostwriting, selective publication, and the misuse of CME as marketing are genuine scandals that demand a response. But the response should be a scalpel, not an axe. Mandatory registration of all clinical trials, independent data monitoring committees, strict limits on consulting fees combined with full disclosure, and a firewall between marketing and education—all pursued with vigor—would address the core abuses while preserving the engine of therapeutic progress on which millions of patients depend.

Diversified DeepSeek

"Marcia Angell’s proposed reforms to sever the ties between academic medicine and the pharmaceutical industry would dismantle the collaborative ecosystem that makes the United States the global leader in biomedical innovation."

There is a reason that patients from Europe, Asia, and the Middle East flock to the United States for cutting-edge medical treatments. American biomedical innovation leads the world, and it does so in no small part because of the dense, collaborative web that links university laboratories, teaching hospitals, and the pharmaceutical industry. Marcia Angell’s sweeping critique of these relationships, while raising legitimate concerns, threatens to undermine the very ecosystem that has made the U.S. the engine of global drug development. In our eagerness to police conflicts, we must be careful not to hand our competitive advantage to other nations.

The global pharmaceutical landscape in 2010 is rapidly shifting. China, India, and Singapore are investing billions in building their own biotech industries, often with far fewer regulatory constraints and ethical compunctions than we impose on ourselves. If American universities sever their ties with industry—if top researchers can no longer consult, if patents are given away, and if clinical trials become mired in bureaucracy—the center of gravity will simply move elsewhere. The result will not be a more ethical world; it will be one in which Americans have slower access to new therapies and in which the standards for research integrity are set by countries with no tradition of independent oversight.

Angell’s proposal that academic researchers refuse all personal payments beyond grant support ignores the reality that the U.S. leads precisely because it attracts the best minds. Those individuals are mobile. A blanket prohibition would accelerate the brain drain into industry, where their expertise would be hidden behind proprietary walls, or to countries offering a more hospitable environment. Moreover, the FDA’s advisory committee system already includes safeguards; the Vioxx episode she cites ultimately resulted in a more restrictive decision after public scrutiny—a sign that the system, while imperfect, can self-correct without radical surgery.

The true crisis in American medicine is not that drug companies market their products—that is their job—but that the cost of developing and approving new drugs has become so astronomically high that it invites the very corner-cutting Angell deplores. Streamlining the FDA’s review process, allowing reciprocal approvals with trusted foreign regulators, and permitting drug reimportation would do far more to lower prices and reduce the financial pressure to fudge data than would dismantling the university-industry partnerships that drive discovery.

Angell’s moral clarity is admirable, but morality without a strategy for maintaining American leadership is a luxury patients cannot afford. We need to reform the excesses, not torch the engine. The rest of the world is watching, and it is ready to fill any vacuum we create.

  3. 03The existing regulatory system, such as the FDA’s advisory committees, already possesses the transparency and oversight necessary to self-correct without radical restructuring.
  4. 04The financial pressures in medicine are better addressed by streamlining the FDA review process and allowing drug reimportation rather than by dismantling the current research model.
  5. 05Maintaining American leadership in medical discovery is a strategic necessity that outweighs the moral benefits of Angell's proposed isolation of academic medicine.
Diversified DeepSeek

"While Marcia Angell correctly identifies the corrupting influence of the pharmaceutical industry on academic medicine, her proposed solution of near-total financial separation is an impractical and purist approach that would stifle medical innovation and drive research into less accountable private sectors."

Marcia Angell has performed an invaluable service by cataloging the ways in which drug companies have insinuated themselves into the heart of academic medicine. Her diagnosis of a profession awash in conflicts of interest, of research that is subtly and not-so-subtly bent to serve commercial ends, and of medical education turned into a marketing carnival is largely correct. But when she turns to remedies, her prescription is so purist and so indifferent to the actual machinery of drug development that it risks killing the patient in the name of curing the disease.

Angell’s central proposal—that members of medical school faculties who conduct clinical trials should accept no payments from drug companies except grant support, with no strings attached and no personal consulting fees—sounds noble. But it ignores the brutal economics of modern drug development. Bringing a single new medicine to market now costs well over $800 million, and the overwhelming majority of candidates fail, often late in development. The National Institutes of Health, with its flat budget, cannot possibly fill the gap if industry retreats. If we insist on a world in which academic researchers can only work at arm’s length, uncompensated except by their universities, we will simply accelerate the exodus of the most talented clinical investigators into the private sector or into contract research organizations, where no one even pretends to be independent. The result will not be cleaner research; it will be less publicly accountable research.

Moreover, Angell’s nostalgia for the pre-Bayh-Dole era is dangerously ahistorical. The Bayh-Dole Act of 1980 was not a sellout; it was a response to a real problem. Before the law, inventions arising from federally funded research often gathered dust because no company had the incentive to invest the enormous sums required for development without some exclusive position. The biotech revolution—which gave us recombinant insulin, monoclonal antibody therapies like Herceptin, and a host of targeted cancer drugs—was made possible by the marriage of university discovery and private risk capital that Bayh-Dole enabled. Angell points to Stanley Cohen and Herbert Boyer’s non-exclusive licensing of recombinant DNA technology as a model. It is indeed a shining example, but it is also an exception that proves the rule: that technology was a platform tool, not a specific therapeutic product. Most university discoveries are far narrower and require enormous follow-on investment that no company will make without a period of exclusivity. Her suggestion that all academic discoveries should be placed in the public domain or licensed non-exclusively would starve biotech start-ups of the oxygen they need and return us to the era of “benign neglect” she romanticizes.

Angell is also too dismissive of the power of transparency. The recent health care reform law includes the Physician Payments Sunshine Act, which will soon require public disclosure of almost all payments from drug and device companies to physicians and teaching hospitals. That is not a panacea, but it is a genuine step forward. Sunlight, combined with rigorous conflict-of-interest management—including independent oversight of data analysis and publication, mandatory clinical trial registration, and strict limits on consulting arrangements—can address many of the abuses she identifies without severing the productive connections between the laboratory and the clinic. The alternative she envisions, a near-total financial separation, would leave academic medicine poorer in both resources and talent and would ultimately slow the delivery of new treatments to the patients she rightly wants to protect.

Angell is right that the pharmaceutical industry has overreached, and the profession has been far too supine. But the answer is not a return to a mythical golden age of pure, disinterested science. It is smarter regulation that harnesses the profit motive while guarding against its worst excesses. Her critique should be the beginning of a serious conversation about redesigning incentives, not the end of a debate in which only one side is heard.
