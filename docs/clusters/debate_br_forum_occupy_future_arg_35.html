"While the Occupy movement's moral outrage against inequality is justified, it will fail to achieve lasting reform unless it abandons its marginalizing tactics in favor of pragmatic electoral politics and broad coalition-building."

I share the Boston Review’s alarm at the growing chasm between the promise of American life and the lived experience of millions. Extreme inequality is not just an economic problem; it is a moral corrosion and a direct threat to democratic governance. The anger that fuels Occupy Wall Street is, at bottom, righteous. But I cannot join the chorus that acclaims the movement as the harbinger of democratic renewal, because so much of its current practice seems designed to alienate precisely the broad middle of the country that any successful reform movement must attract. The occupation tactic, the general assembly radicalism, and the reluctance to translate moral claims into electoral reality risk turning a moment of genuine possibility into a marginal, self-indulgent episode.

The American public is capable of great change. The Progressive Era, the New Deal, and the civil rights revolution all succeeded because they combined moral passion with a relentless, pragmatic focus on building durable majorities and changing the law. The key to those movements was not just encampments or general assemblies; it was the patient work of organizing, compromising, and voting. The Senate, the House, the presidency—these are contested terrain. To dismiss electoral politics as thoroughly corrupted, as many in Occupy do, is to abandon the only mechanism through which the legal and fiscal architecture of inequality can be reconstructed. The Dodd-Frank financial reform was weak, but it passed. The Affordable Care Act, for all its flaws, extends coverage to thirty million people. These are the fruits of messy, coalition-based politics, and they should be built upon, not scorned.

Occupy’s tactics also carry the risk of repelling the moderate sensibilities on which broad coalitions depend. The drum circles, the vagueness of the declaration, and the occasional flare-ups of property destruction have been a gift to a conservative media eager to depict the movement as a carnival of slackers. This is profoundly unfair as a characterization of the movement’s core, but fairness has never governed political messaging. What the moment demands is a disciplined, clear-eyed push for concrete institutional reforms: a new Glass-Steagall Act to separate commercial and investment banking, public financing of elections to break the grip of money on politics, a higher minimum wage indexed to inflation, and a serious program of job creation through infrastructure spending. These are ambitious but achievable goals, grounded in the principles of fair reward and equal voice. They can win the support of suburban moderates who are deeply worried about the country’s direction but who will never pitch a tent in a park.

The movement’s moral energy is a precious resource, but it is being dissipated. The clearing of the parks this month could be a clarifying moment. My hope is that the energy of Occupy is rechanneled into a sustained, federally directed campaign—perhaps one that emulates the Tea Party’s focus on primary elections, but from the left. Imagine Occupy-linked candidates running on a platform of clean elections and economic populism, winning local office, and building a bench for higher stations. That would bring our institutions into alignment with our principles through the slow, hard graft of democratic procedure. Moral witness is indispensable, but it is not a substitute for political power.

  5. 05The energy of the movement should be rechanneled into a sustained effort to win political power by running candidates for office and engaging in the democratic process.
