"The federal government should maintain its current holiday calendar to preserve shared national traditions, with the single addition of Lincoln’s Birthday to honor the nation's moral struggle for emancipation."

The current federal holiday roster is less a product of deliberate design than of historical sedimentation. Christmas arrived with the culture; Washington’s Birthday honored the first president; later, Memorial Day, Labor Day and Martin Luther King Jr. Day were layered on. The result is a calendar that imperfectly tells the national story but that has, over decades, acquired the weight of tradition and a rough‑hewn coherence. Before we rush to tear it up in the name of inclusion or secular purity, we should consider what would be lost.

The existing holidays serve a subtle but vital civic function: they provide a recurring rhythm of shared days off that people of all backgrounds can experience together. Christmas, despite its Christian origins, has become so thoroughly secularized in its public celebration—with gift‑giving, family gatherings and year‑end reflection—that it functions much like New Year’s Day as a winter festival that transcends any single creed. Thanksgiving, too, morphed long ago from a Puritan prayer day into a broadly ecumenical harvest meal. These holidays are not endorsements of theology; they are cultural habits that bind a vast and fractious country in common, low‑stakes rituals. Turning them into battlegrounds over religious neutrality risks destroying that quiet binding without gaining anything of comparable value.

That does not mean the calendar is perfect. There is one glaring historical omission that deserves correction: Lincoln’s Birthday. While the federal government officially marks Washington’s Birthday, the president who preserved the Union and ended slavery has never received the same national recognition. Creating a distinct federal holiday for Lincoln, perhaps on February 12, would rectify a symbolic imbalance without opening the door to an unmanageable cascade of demands. It would also enshrine the principle that the nation’s deepest moral struggle—emancipation—merits its own day of remembrance, separate from the generic Presidents’ Day that has commercially swallowed both Washington and Lincoln.

The temptation to add holidays for every major cultural and religious community is well‑meaning but misguided. It would turn the federal calendar into a patchwork of particularisms, inviting endless contention over which groups are represented and which are left out. Such decisions are best left to local communities and private institutions, where they can reflect actual demographic concentrations without imposing a fraught, top‑down scheme on the whole nation. A federal holiday should say something about the shared political and historical experience of all Americans, not about the identity of one subset.

We have a calendar that, for all its quirks, works. The Christmas‑Washington’s Birthday rhythm connects us to the deep past of the republic and its evolving culture. Adding Lincoln’s Birthday would complete the portrait of our defining century. Beyond that, the wisest course is to resist the urge to engineer the calendar and let the slow, organic process that gave us what we have continue on its own terms.
