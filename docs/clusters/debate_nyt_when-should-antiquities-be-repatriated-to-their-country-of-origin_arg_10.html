"Antiquities should be repatriated when they were looted or are essential to a monument's context, but complex disputes should be resolved through negotiated stewardship and shared custody rather than binary ownership."

The best answer to repatriation is not a slogan but a process. Antiquities should be returned when they were looted, when they are essential parts of a monument or sacred tradition, or when their continued retention depends on secrecy and legal technicalities. But many disputes cannot be solved by simply asking where an object was first dug up. They require negotiated stewardship.

Start with the easy cases. The thousands of Italian antiquities found in Switzerland should be returned if they were illicitly excavated or exported. Their value is not only aesthetic; it is archaeological. Looting destroys information about burial practices, trade, chronology and daily life. A museum that buys such an object after it has been ripped from the ground becomes part of the destruction, even if the gallery label is elegant. Repatriation in these cases is both justice and deterrence.

Then consider the hard cases. The Elgin marbles raise questions of legality, empire, preservation, access and identity. Greece is not wrong to argue that the sculptures belong with the Parthenon and can now be displayed properly in Athens. The British Museum is not wrong to say that the marbles have acquired a global history in London and are seen by millions as part of a comparative collection. Both claims have weight because cultural property is not ordinary property.

That is why the remedy should often be shared custody rather than winner-take-all ownership. Museums and source countries could negotiate renewable long-term loans, joint labels, traveling exhibitions, shared conservation teams and agreements that recognize origin without necessarily requiring permanent physical transfer in every case. In some disputes, ownership could be transferred while the object remains on loan abroad; in others, the object could return for a generation and later travel. Such arrangements would reduce the fear that any concession opens the floodgates.

There also must be attention to preservation. Returning antiquities to a war zone, a corrupt customs system or an underfunded storeroom may not serve the object or the public. But “we can take better care of it” should be used cautiously; it has too often been the language of paternalism. If wealthy museums believe in preservation, they should help build conservation capacity abroad rather than use inequality as a title deed.

Repatriation should occur when return repairs a specific wrong, restores lost context, or reconnects an object with a community for whom it has continuing meaning. Where claims are mixed, the answer should be partnership. The past is shared, but sharing cannot be built on permanent possession by the powerful.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Illicitly excavated or exported items must be returned to provide justice and deter the destruction of archaeological information caused by looting.
  2. 02Objects that are essential parts of a monument or a living sacred tradition should be reunited with their original context.
  3. 03In cases where both the source country and the museum have legitimate claims, shared custody and long-term loans offer a middle ground that recognizes origin without requiring permanent transfer.
  4. 04Repatriation decisions must consider the safety and preservation of the objects, though wealthy museums should help build conservation capacity in source countries rather than using lack of resources as an excuse to keep items.
  5. 05The resolution of cultural property disputes should prioritize repairing specific wrongs and reconnecting objects with communities for whom they hold continuing meaning.
