"While Mother Teresa’s personal devotion is undeniable, her canonization risks enshrining a model of charity that prioritizes sentimental proximity to suffering over the systemic pursuit of justice."

For non-Catholics, the question “Should Mother Teresa be canonized?” can sound like asking outsiders to vote on a family ritual. Canonization is, formally, a Catholic act: the pope declaring that a person is in heaven and may be venerated by the faithful. On those terms, the Church will decide according to its own theology of sanctity, miracles and heroic virtue. But Mother Teresa is not only a Catholic figure. She is one of the 20th century’s most famous moral celebrities. Her canonization will shape public ideas about poverty, charity and goodness. That is why the controversy matters.

The danger is that sainthood will reinforce a sentimental politics of poverty. Mother Teresa became famous not by challenging the systems that produce slums, caste exclusion, inadequate health care or gendered vulnerability, but by offering intimate care within those systems. There is nobility in that. Someone has to wash the body in front of them; someone has to feed the abandoned person tonight. Yet charity can also become a substitute for justice. It can make the giver feel holy while leaving the structures of suffering intact.

Her admirers say this critique is unfair because she never claimed to be an economist or politician. Perhaps. But she did make public interventions on abortion, contraception and family life; she did meet the powerful; she did attract enormous donations and global prestige. Once a religious figure becomes a worldwide symbol, scrutiny is not optional.

The most honest position may be to separate three questions. Was Mother Teresa personally courageous and devoted? Clearly yes. Were many of the people she served treated with more human warmth than they would otherwise have received? Yes. Should her approach be treated as the highest model of service to the poor? No.

If the Vatican canonizes her, as it is now poised to do, the rest of us need not respond with either sneering or uncritical applause. We can honor the impulse to go near suffering while insisting that the poor need more than proximity and compassion. They need competent medicine, political voice, sanitation, education, legal protection and economic opportunity. A saint of charity should not become an excuse to neglect the harder work of justice.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Mother Teresa's approach focused on providing intimate care within existing systems of poverty rather than challenging the structures that create those conditions.
  2. 02The global prestige and public influence Mother Teresa wielded make her subject to scrutiny regarding her impact on public ideas about goodness and charity.
  3. 03Charity can serve as a substitute for justice, allowing the giver to feel holy while leaving the root causes of suffering unaddressed.
  4. 04The poor require competent medicine, political voice, and economic opportunity rather than just the compassion and proximity offered by Mother Teresa’s model of service.
