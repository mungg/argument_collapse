"The Port Huron Statement’s vision of participatory democracy remains an urgent and necessary ethic for modern activism because it empowers ordinary people to reclaim agency from failing, corporate-aligned institutions."

Tom Hayden is right to begin with ghosts. The Port Huron Statement was not merely a document; it was a way of recognizing one another. Its power lay in naming a hunger that many Americans felt but could not yet describe: the desire not simply to be represented, managed, polled, or mobilized, but to participate in shaping the conditions of common life.

That hunger is still with us. Indeed, it may be stronger now because so many of the institutions that once claimed to mediate democratic life have lost credibility. Congress is widely distrusted. The parties are treated by many citizens as fundraising machines and branding operations. Universities increasingly speak the language of markets, rankings, debt, and “human capital.” News organizations, even when doing important work, operate within a fragmented media ecology in which citizens suspect manipulation from every direction. Corporations are not merely economic actors but political actors of enormous scale. In such a world, the Port Huron insistence that ordinary people must be makers of history rather than spectators is not antiquarian. It is urgent.

Hayden’s examples are well chosen. The undocumented students who risk deportation by speaking publicly for the Dream Act embody a kind of democratic courage that cannot be reduced to lobbying. They are refusing invisibility. They are saying, in effect, that laws written about them without them lack moral legitimacy. Likewise, the students protesting tuition hikes are not only complaining about prices. They are contesting a transformation in the meaning of public education. If college becomes a debt-financed private investment rather than a public good, then students become customers rather than citizens in formation. The sit-ins and occupations at campuses are attempts, however imperfect, to recover a democratic claim on institutions that increasingly behave like corporations.

Occupy Wall Street has made the same claim in a broader idiom. Critics complain that Occupy lacks demands, leaders, and legislative strategy. Some of those criticisms are fair. But they miss what the encampments accomplished at their best. They created public spaces in which people practiced a different political grammar. In the shadow of banks, courthouses, and city halls, citizens debated debt, inequality, policing, foreclosure, war, unemployment, and the capture of government by money. They did not wait for permission from professional politics. They disrupted the habits of resignation.

The genius of participatory democracy is that it joins means and ends. If the goal is a society in which people have more control over the decisions that shape their lives, then movements must themselves create spaces where people learn to speak, listen, deliberate, and decide. This was true in the civil rights movement’s mass meetings and freedom schools. It was true in the women’s consciousness-raising groups that transformed private pain into public analysis. It is true in immigrant youth organizing, where young people once trained to hide now train one another to testify. Democratic capacity is not innate in the abstract; it is cultivated by doing.

Yet Hayden’s celebration also invites a caution. Participatory democracy is not magic. It does not automatically overcome inequality inside movements. Meetings can be dominated by those with more time, education, confidence, or cultural capital. Consensus can become a veto for the stubborn or a cover for informal hierarchy. The language of openness can conceal who set the agenda before the meeting began. Anyone who has attended activist gatherings knows that the absence of official leaders does not mean the absence of power. The Port Huron tradition is strongest when it recognizes this and treats democracy as a discipline rather than a mood.

Nor should participatory democracy be opposed too quickly to representative institutions. Hayden is careful on this point, and his inside-outside strategy is essential. Movements need legislators, lawyers, administrators, and policy experts, just as they need marchers and jail-goers. The Americans with Disabilities Act did not pass because moral witness alone floated into the statute books; it passed because activists combined disruption with negotiation, technical knowledge, and political leverage. The civil rights victories of the 1960s required sit-ins and freedom rides, but also federal courts, congressional votes, and executive enforcement. Democracy is impoverished when it is reduced to elections, but it is also weakened when it disdains the institutional work that converts aspiration into durable rights.

The most powerful contemporary movements understand this. The campaign for marriage equality, whatever one thinks of its priorities within the broader struggle for gay and lesbian liberation, has combined personal testimony, legal strategy, electoral work, cultural change, and direct action. The immigrant-rights movement has combined mass marches, local organizing, student activism, litigation, and legislative pressure. Climate activism is beginning to discover that moral urgency must be matched by institutional design: carbon rules, transit systems, energy policy, and international agreements do not emerge from passion alone.

Hayden also rightly emphasizes the Internet, but we should be careful not to confuse communication with democracy. Social media can spread information, expose abuse, and lower barriers to participation. It can also accelerate rumor, reward performance over reflection, and create the illusion that clicking is organizing. The Port Huron tradition asks more of us than expression. It asks for responsibility in common. A democratic movement is not simply a network of voices; it is a community capable of making commitments.

Still, the new tools matter. The young activists of today inherit a world in which surveillance, corporate media, and state power remain formidable, but they also possess means of connection unavailable to earlier generations. The images of pepper-sprayed students at UC Davis traveled instantly across the country because witnesses could document and distribute them. Police power, once hidden in local darkness, now more often meets a national audience. That does not guarantee justice, but it changes the terrain.

What is most moving in Hayden’s essay is his refusal to let the 1960s become either museum or myth. Nostalgia is a danger for veterans of great movements, and so is condescension toward the young. Hayden avoids both. He acknowledges that Port Huron was limited by its moment. It did not foresee, and in some cases did not adequately include, the movements that would later transform the meaning of democracy itself. But he sees continuity where others see only fragmentation.

That continuity is not ideological uniformity. Participatory democracy is not a platform plank. It is a democratic ethic: those affected by decisions should have a meaningful role in making them; public life should develop human capacities rather than merely aggregate preferences; institutions should be judged by whether they enlarge or diminish people’s power to act together.

At fifty, the Port Huron Statement remains alive because the problems it addressed remain alive. We are still surrounded by systems that ask for our obedience, consumption, debt, and silence. We are still told that experts, markets, donors, generals, and managers know best. Against that, participatory democracy says that ordinary people are not raw material for history. They are its authors. That idea is still radical. It is also still necessary.
