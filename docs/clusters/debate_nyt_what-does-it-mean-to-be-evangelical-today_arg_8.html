"The term “evangelical” should be defined by its global, multiethnic, and theological foundations rather than being reduced to the narrow political identity of white conservative voters in the United States."

The question “What does it mean to be evangelical today?” is often answered as though evangelicals were simply white Republican voters in a presidential primary. That answer erases too many people. It erases black evangelicals whose churches preach conversion and biblical authority but do not identify with the religious right. It erases Latino and Asian immigrant congregations growing in American cities and suburbs. It erases Pentecostals in storefront churches, campus ministries serving international students, and millions of believers in Africa, Latin America and Asia for whom “evangelical” has little to do with Iowa, South Carolina or Super Tuesday.

American evangelicalism is in crisis partly because its public image is too narrow. The movement’s center of gravity has shifted globally, while its media representation remains stubbornly tied to white conservative politics. Around the world, evangelicals are planting churches, translating Scripture, caring for orphans, opposing corruption, serving refugees and debating how to live faithfully under poverty, persecution or unstable governments. In the United States, many evangelicals are working on immigration reform, racial reconciliation, anti-trafficking efforts, prison ministry and poverty, even while also holding traditional views on abortion and marriage.

Trump’s evangelical support matters. It says something troubling about fear, race, celebrity and the hunger for power among many white conservative Christians. But it should not be allowed to define the whole family. The term “evangelical” comes from evangel, good news. If the public face of the movement becomes bad news for immigrants, Muslims, the poor or political opponents, then the movement has contradicted its name.

The way forward is not merely rebranding. Calling oneself a “gospel Christian” will not heal racial division or repair public witness. Evangelicals need a wider table and a humbler posture. White evangelicals in particular should listen to believers who share their theology but not their political instincts. They should ask why the same Bible produces different political priorities in communities marked by slavery, migration, poverty or minority status.

To be evangelical today should mean belonging to a global, multiethnic people formed by Scripture and sent into the world with good news. If it means only defending one tribe’s lost cultural dominance, then the word deserves the suspicion it now receives.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Defining evangelicalism solely through white Republican voting patterns erases the diverse experiences of Black, Latino, Asian, and international believers.
  2. 02The movement's public image is in crisis because its media representation fails to reflect its global shift and its focus on humanitarian work like refugee service and poverty relief.
  3. 03The political support for Donald Trump among white conservatives reflects a troubling pursuit of power that contradicts the 'good news' inherent in the word's etymology.
  4. 04Rebranding with new labels is insufficient; instead, white evangelicals must listen to the diverse political priorities of believers from different racial and socioeconomic backgrounds.
  5. 05The future of the movement depends on prioritizing a humble, multiethnic identity over the defense of a single tribe's cultural dominance.
