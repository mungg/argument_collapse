"The 'Economics for Inclusive Prosperity' framework fails to achieve true inclusivity because it ignores the unpaid care work and social reproduction that form the essential foundation of the economy."

In "Economics After Neoliberalism," Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman declare their intention to redesign the discipline of economics to foster "inclusive prosperity." They correctly identify the failures of market fundamentalism and the devastating consequences of rising inequality. They rightfully call for an economics that acknowledges power asymmetries, market failures, and the institutional embeddedness of markets. Yet, for an essay that uses the word "inclusive" so prominently, their vision remains glaringly exclusive of a massive portion of the economy: the unpaid care work and social reproduction that makes all other economic activity possible.

The authors propose a "broad public discussion of new policy ideas" spanning labor markets, international trade, finance, and taxation. But reading their manifesto in 2019, one is struck by the total absence of gender as a category of analysis. The "Economics for Inclusive Prosperity" (EfIP) network seems to operate under the assumption that tweaking the formal, monetized sectors of the economy will automatically trickle down to resolve the deep-seated gender inequalities that structure our society. This is the very same blind spot that characterized the neoliberal economics they seek to depose.

Feminist economists have spent decades pointing out that the traditional economic model—whether neoclassical or the "second-best" empirical model favored by EfIP—relies on the invisible subsidy of women's unpaid labor. When the authors talk about "labor markets," they are talking about the productive economy. But what about the reproductive economy? The raising of children, the care of the elderly, the maintenance of households—this work is not a footnote to the "real" economy; it is its foundation.

Consider the EfIP proposals. The authors champion Arindrajit Dube’s wage boards and Suresh Naidu’s ideas for revitalizing labor unions. These are important for formal sector workers. But how do wage boards help the millions of women who drop out of the labor force because the United States lacks universal childcare? How does unionization reach the deeply atomized, informal, and often undocumented domestic workers who scrub floors and change diapers?

Sandra Black and Jesse Rothstein are cited as providing a "contemporary restatement of an old idea: government should provide public goods and social insurance." They mention unemployment insurance, social security, health insurance, and education. But where is the social insurance for caregiving? The US is the only advanced economy without paid family leave. The crisis of care in 2019 is arguably as severe as the crisis of financialization, yet it merits not a single mention in this blueprint for our economic future.

The authors state that economics supplies the tools to lay out trade-offs, mentioning the distribution of returns between "workers, employers, and the government." They omit the household. Intra-household bargaining is a site of immense power asymmetry, precisely the kind of power dynamic the authors claim economics must now study rigorously. The assumption that the household is a unitary, harmonious unit has been thoroughly debunked by feminist economics, yet the EfIP agenda proceeds as if macro-level institutional tweaks will land neutrally across genders.

Furthermore, Anton Korinek’s discussion of technology and AI focuses on how innovators might "overestimate the social cost of labor," urging policies that steer AI to augment cognitive abilities. But what about the emotional and physical labor of care, which AI cannot easily replace, but which the market systematically undervalues? An economics for inclusive prosperity would not just worry about manufacturing workers displaced by robots; it would fundamentally revalue the care professions that represent the fastest-growing, lowest-paid sector of the US labor market.

Rodrik, Naidu, and Zucman are right that economics must abandon its obsession with the "competitive equilibrium model." But shifting from a model of perfect competition to a model of market failure is insufficient if both models systematically ignore half the human experience. If "inclusive prosperity" is to mean anything, it must redefine prosperity to include human well-being generated outside the cash nexus, and it must redefine "inclusive" to mean dismantling the patriarchal structures that mainstream economics has long naturalized. Until the EfIP network integrates the insights of feminist economics, their "after neoliberalism" will look remarkably like the status quo for women.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The authors' policy proposals focus exclusively on the formal, monetized sectors of the economy while neglecting the reproductive economy and gender as a category of analysis.
  2. 02Labor market interventions like wage boards and unionization do not address the needs of women excluded from the workforce due to a lack of universal childcare or those in atomized domestic work.
  3. 03The essay’s vision for social insurance and public goods overlooks the urgent need for paid family leave and support for caregiving.
  4. 04By omitting the household as a site of power asymmetry and intra-household bargaining, the authors fail to account for how macro-level institutional changes affect genders differently.
  5. 05A truly inclusive economics must revalue care professions and well-being generated outside of market transactions rather than just focusing on technological displacement in the productive sector.
