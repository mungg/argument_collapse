"Stephen Walt’s realist framework of offshore balancing and strategic restraint offers a necessary and analytically rigorous path to preserving American primacy by correcting the self-defeating overreach of current foreign policy."

Stephen Walt's essay represents the best of American realist thinking: analytically rigorous, historically informed, and grounded in a clear understanding of how great powers actually behave. While I have reservations about some specific recommendations, the overall framework deserves broad support.

The core insight—that American power provokes resistance when misused—underlies much of Walt's analysis. The United States is strong enough to dominate but not strong enough to dominate permanently; overreach generates counterbalancing that erodes the very primacy policymakers seek to preserve. This is not a novel insight, but Walt articulates it with unusual clarity and supports it with compelling evidence from the Iraq experience.

The critique of preventive war deserves particular attention. Walt distinguishes preemption (the use of force to prevent an imminent attack) from preventive war (the use of force to forestall future threats regardless of imminence). The Bush administration's 2002 National Security Strategy embraced preventive war, abandoning the traditional legal basis for anticipatory self-defense. This shift was not merely a change in strategy but a transformation in how the United States understood its rights under international law. Walt is right to condemn this shift: it set dangerous precedents, alarmed allies, and justified a war that had no legitimate basis.

The proposal for nuclear reductions is also compelling. American nuclear weapons policy has become a liability rather than an asset. Thousands of warheads are unnecessary for deterrence, provoke other states to pursue their own arsenals, and undermine American credibility in nonproliferation diplomacy. Walt's proposal for a grand bargain—reductions accompanied by verifiable denuclearization by Iran and North Korea—sets the right objectives even if the diplomatic path to achieve them remains unclear.

The analysis of Israeli-Palestinian policy is where Walt is most likely to face criticism, and where his analysis is most incisive. American unconditional support for Israel has been a disaster for both countries. It has not made Israel safer; it has encouraged Israeli maximalism, enabled settlement expansion, and removed incentives for compromise. It has also made America hated across the Islamic world, providing recruiting ammunition for terrorist movements and diplomatic leverage for American adversaries. Walt's proposal to condition aid on Israeli policy changes is not anti-Semitic; it is a recognition that unconditional support serves no one's long-term interests.

The greatest weakness of the essay is its optimism about the prospects for change. Walt assumes that American policymakers will eventually recognize the errors of their ways and adopt a more restrained approach. This assumption may be overly optimistic. The foreign policy establishment has strong interests in maintaining the status quo, and the domestic political constraints on policy change are formidable. Walt's essay provides an intellectual framework for reform, but translating that framework into practice requires sustained political effort.

This is a debate that matters, and Walt's contribution advances it significantly. The United States cannot afford to continue on its current path; the costs are already visible in reduced standing, alienated allies, and strategic overextension. A different approach is possible, and Walt's essay points the way.

  3. 03Ending unconditional support for Israel and conditioning aid on policy changes is a strategic necessity to reduce anti-Americanism and curb regional extremism.
  4. 04American primacy is inherently fragile because overreach triggers counterbalancing by other states, making self-restraint the only way to maintain long-term influence.
  5. 05While Walt provides a compelling intellectual framework for reform, the primary obstacle to its implementation is the entrenched status quo of the foreign policy establishment and domestic political constraints.

"While Stephen Walt’s strategy of offshore balancing is the most effective way to preserve American primacy through restraint, its success depends on embedding this strategy in durable multilateral frameworks and overcoming significant domestic political obstacles."

Stephen Walt's essay represents the most sophisticated articulation of offshore balancing currently available, and on the substance, I find myself in substantial agreement. The argument that American primacy is best preserved through restraint rather than overextension is not merely analytically sound but practically achievable. Where I wish to extend Walt's analysis is in the domain of institutional design—specifically, how the United States can embed its offshore balancing strategy in durable multilateral frameworks that survive changes in administration.

Walt correctly identifies the core dilemma of American primacy: overwhelming power breeds fear, and fear generates resistance. The Bush administration's response—insisting that others simply accept American hegemony and trust in benevolence—failed because it asked the impossible. No great power voluntarily cedes autonomy to another, regardless of that power's stated intentions. This is not anti-Americanism; it is the fundamental logic of international politics. Walt's solution is essentially correct: the United States must signal restraint credibly and repeatedly, not merely assert it.

The most compelling section of Walt's essay concerns the Iraqi quagmire. The failure to find weapons of mass destruction was not simply a matter of faulty intelligence; it reflected a deeper pathology of preventive war doctrine that treats military action as a policy tool rather than a last resort. The lesson Walt draws—that preventive war will rarely be viable—is well-supported by both theory and history. Empires that overextend themselves to impose values rather than defend interests inevitably decline. The British understood this better than the Americans have; the United States should take notes.

Where I would push back is on Walt's treatment of alliance relationships. The essay correctly notes that allies should be encouraged to do more for themselves, but it understates the risk of alliance atrophy. If American allies come to believe that the United States will disengage regardless of their cooperation, they will hedge against American unreliability. This means acquiring nuclear weapons (as Japan or South Korea might if they lost confidence in American security guarantees), developing independent military capabilities, or accommodating potential adversaries. Offshore balancing requires careful calibration: enough American presence to deter free-riding, but not so much that the United States bears disproportionate costs.

The proposal for a "grand bargain" on nuclear policy deserves particular attention. Walt is right that American hypocrisy undermines nonproliferation efforts. The United States cannot credibly demand that other states forgo nuclear weapons while maintaining thousands of warheads and developing new ones. But the grand bargain Walt proposes—trading American nuclear reductions for verified Iranian and North Korean denuclearization—requires an degree of diplomatic trust that does not currently exist. Iran and North Korea have strong incentives to cheat, and American credibility has been damaged by the Iraq experience. Walt's proposal is directionally correct, but the sequencing and verification mechanisms need more development.

On the Israeli-Palestinian question, Walt's argument is both courageous and correct. American unconditional support for Israel has not made Israel safer; it has instead provided ammunition to extremists and undermined America's standing across the Arab and Islamic world. The proposal to pressure Israel toward a two-state solution based on the 1967 borders is not anti-Semitic—it is a recognition that permanent occupation will eventually destroy Israel as a Jewish and democratic state. American policymakers who claim to support Israel should be supporting its long-term survival, not its short-term maximalism.

The essay's weakness lies in underestimating domestic political constraints. Walt's recommendations—pressure on Israel, nuclear reductions, multilateral consultation—face fierce opposition from well-organized domestic constituencies. The foreign policy establishment that Walt critiques is not merely ignorant; it is often captured by interests that profit from the status quo. Changing course requires not just intellectual argument but also political mobilization. Walt's essay is an excellent starting point for that conversation, but the political work is only beginning.

"While Stephen Walt’s proposal for offshore balancing and nuclear diplomacy requires refinement, his core argument that American primacy is best preserved through strategic restraint and international legitimacy is a vital corrective to current foreign policy."

Stephen Walt has written a genuinely important essay that deserves careful engagement rather than reflexive dismissal. While I disagree with some of his specific recommendations, his core argument—that American primacy is best preserved through restraint rather than overextension—deserves serious consideration from policymakers and citizens alike.

The critique of preventive war is the essay's strongest section. Walt correctly distinguishes between preemption (legitimate under international law) and preventive war (a policy of using force to forestall future threats regardless of imminence). The Bush administration's embrace of preventive war doctrine was both legally dubious and strategically counterproductive. It alarmed allies who could not be certain they would not become American targets, justified American actions in ways that invited reciprocal claims, and—most importantly—led the United States into a costly and unnecessary war in Iraq. Walt is right to call for repudiating this doctrine.

The analysis of Israeli-Palestinian policy is equally compelling, if politically controversial. Walt correctly identifies unconditional American support for Israel as a source of anti-Americanism throughout the Islamic world. This support has not made Israel safer; it has instead provided ammunition to extremists who portray America as an enemy of Arab and Muslim peoples. American pressure for a genuine two-state solution—based on the 1967 borders, with mutually agreed modifications—is not only consistent with American values but also with American strategic interests. A resolution of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict would remove a potent source of anti-American grievance and make it easier to build coalitions against genuine threats like Iran and al-Qaeda.

The proposal for offshore balancing is directionally correct but needs refinement. Walt is right that the United States has been too willing to deploy military forces overseas in pursuit of objectives that do not directly threaten American security. The Cold War footprint is no longer appropriate for a unipolar world. But offshore balancing requires capable regional partners willing to assume security responsibilities that the United States no longer wishes to bear. Some regions—the Persian Gulf, for example—lack such partners, and American withdrawal could lead to the very hegemony that Walt seeks to prevent.

The nuclear grand bargain is the essay's weakest section. Walt proposes that the United States reduce its arsenal and offer nonaggression guarantees to potential proliferators in exchange for their nuclear abandonment. This proposal fails to account for the motivations of states like Iran and North Korea. These regimes seek nuclear weapons not because they fear American aggression but because nuclear weapons provide status, deterrence, and leverage. No American concession will satisfy them because their goals are inherently incompatible with American interests. Negotiation is appropriate, but Walt sets the bar too high for what American diplomacy can achieve.

Despite these reservations, Walt's essay represents a genuine contribution to the foreign policy debate. It correctly diagnoses the pathologies of current policy and points toward a more sustainable approach. The United States cannot impose its values by force; it can only lead by example. This is not weakness but wisdom—the recognition that lasting influence requires legitimacy, and legitimacy requires respect for the interests and opinions of others. Walt's essay deserves a wide audience.

Sub-arguments

  2. 02Ending unconditional support for Israel and pursuing a genuine two-state solution is essential to removing a primary source of anti-American grievance in the Islamic world.
  3. 03The United States should reduce its global military footprint and transition toward offshore balancing, provided it can find capable regional partners to maintain stability.
  4. 04Walt’s proposed 'nuclear grand bargain' is overly optimistic because it underestimates the status-seeking and deterrent motivations of regimes like Iran and North Korea.
  5. 05Lasting international influence depends on leading by example and respecting the interests of others rather than attempting to impose American values through military force.

"Stephen Walt’s proposal for 'offshore balancing' provides a sophisticated and rigorous strategic framework for preserving American primacy by replacing aggressive global hegemony with a policy of restraint."

Stephen Walt has produced an essay that will be appreciated by some as wisdom and condemned by others as weakness. In truth, it is neither; it is a sophisticated attempt to think through the strategic logic of American primacy in a unipolar world. The answer it reaches—that primacy is best preserved through restraint—is not comfortable for those who believe American power should be used to remake the world, but it may be correct.

The central argument is straightforward: American power provokes resistance, and resistance erodes the primacy that American policymakers seek to preserve. This is not a novel insight, but Walt develops it with unusual rigor. He shows that global hegemony is infeasible, that selective engagement was better but not sufficiently selective, and that offshore balancing offers the best framework for preserving American power while reducing the resistance that power generates. Each step in the argument is supported by historical evidence and strategic logic.

The critique of preventive war is the essay's most valuable contribution. Walt correctly distinguishes preemption from preventive war and shows how the Bush administration's embrace of preventive war doctrine damaged American credibility without enhancing security. The Iraq war provides the most compelling evidence: a preventive war justified by disputed intelligence, executed without international authorization, and resulting in a costly occupation that has produced no commensurate strategic benefit. Walt's conclusion—that preventive war will rarely be a viable policy option—is well-supported by both theory and evidence.

The proposal for a nuclear grand bargain is directionally correct but needs further development. Walt is right that American nuclear hypocrisy undermines nonproliferation, and that the United States should reduce its arsenal to set a better example. But the bargain he proposes—American reductions in exchange for Iranian and North Korean denuclearization—requires verification mechanisms that do not currently exist and diplomatic trust that has been eroded by recent American behavior. Walt identifies the problem correctly but underestimates its difficulty.

The analysis of Israeli-Palestinian policy will generate the most controversy, and Walt handles it well. He correctly identifies unconditional American support for Israel as a source of anti-Americanism, and he proposes conditioning aid on Israeli policy changes. This proposal is not anti-Semitic; it is a recognition that Israel's long-term survival depends on ending the occupation, and that American leverage is most effectively used to encourage rather than enable maximalism. Walt's willingness to state this plainly deserves credit even if the proposal faces formidable political obstacles.

The greatest limitation of the essay is its underestimation of the domestic political constraints on policy change. American foreign policy is not made by strategic thinkers in isolation; it is made by politicians responding to interest groups, media, and public opinion. Walt's recommendations face powerful opposition from those who profit from the status quo. The essay provides an intellectual framework for change, but the political work of building support for that change is beyond its scope.

This is a valuable contribution to an important debate. The United States faces genuine threats that require sustained attention and substantial resources. But it also faces the risk of overreach—using American power in ways that generate resistance, provoke counterbalancing, and erode the primacy that makes American security possible. Walt's essay offers a framework for navigating between these risks. Whether policymakers will heed its advice is another question, but the essay deserves to be read and engaged seriously.
