Diversified DeepSeek

"While offshore balancing is the correct strategic direction, the United States must pursue a more radical and consistent policy of military disengagement than Stephen Walt proposes to truly avoid the pitfalls of imperial overreach."

Stephen Walt’s call for offshore balancing is a welcome antidote to the imperial overreach of the Bush years. His diagnosis of the ways American primacy provokes fear, resistance, and anti-Americanism is largely correct, and his insistence that the United States husband its power rather than squander it in preventive wars is a lesson the nation has paid for in Iraq with blood and treasure. And yet, for all its wisdom, Walt’s offshore balancing remains too timid. He still clings to a forward military presence in Asia, retains NATO as a shell, and imagines interventions to contain China or to halt genocide when conditions allow. If we take the logic of his argument seriously—that American power is most effective when it is least provocative, and that local powers will balance on their own unless we remove their incentives to do so—the conclusion must be a far more sweeping disengagement.

Walt’s mistake is to preserve too many of the reflexes of global leadership. He would keep “a significant military presence in Asia,” primarily air and naval forces, to reassure allies and lay a foundation for containing China. But that same logic of playing hard-to-get that he applies so lucidly to the Persian Gulf applies equally to East Asia. Japan, South Korea, and Australia are wealthy, technically advanced democracies fully capable of defending themselves and offsetting Chinese ambitions without permanent American garrisons. If they sometimes fear abandonment, that fear is healthy: it spurs them to do more for their own security and to make their relationships with the United States truly reciprocal. The Bush administration has already begun to rearrange forces globally; offshore balancing should complete that process by withdrawing virtually all forward-based American troops in Asia, leaving only naval stations that can be quickly surged in an emergency, not a permanent tripwire.

The same logic demands a final, honest reckoning with NATO. Walt’s proposal—small training contingents but no leading security role—is a half-measure that perpetuates the illusion of an alliance that has outlived its purpose. Europe faces no plausible conventional military threat. The modest security challenges it might confront, from Balkan instability to terrorism, are manageable with European resources. Keeping the transatlantic security architecture on life support distorts European defense budgets and encourages free-riding while simultaneously giving the United States a stake in squabbles that do not touch its vital interests. The United States should declare that the mutual defense clause of Article V is no longer operative in the post-Soviet environment and that European security is a European responsibility. Such candor would be a shock, but it would be a productive one, much as the Nixon Doctrine encouraged regional self-reliance in Asia.

Walt also leaves the door ajar for humanitarian interventions, even as he acknowledges that offshore balancing would make them easier. This is a dangerous temptation. The recent history of such interventions—from Somalia to Kosovo—shows that even well-intentioned missions drag the United States into messy nation-building that inflames nationalism and drains resources without clear strategic benefit. The genocide in Darfur, heartbreaking as it is, does not threaten the security of the United States. We have no obligation, and usually no capacity, to fix all the world’s horrors. Offshore balancing should be understood as a strategy of strategic restraint, not a justification for wars of altruism that bluntly instrumentalize American power. Walt’s own argument that nationalism defeats outside occupations applies just as forcefully to peacekeeping and humanitarian occupation as it does to democracy-by-gun.

Even more, offshore balancing as Walt presents it underestimates the economic drain of a global presence. Maintaining air and naval supremacy across the world’s oceans, even without large ground forces, still requires staggering defense budgets that compete with domestic investment and undercut the long-term vitality of American power. A genuine strategy of offshore balancing would draw down military spending significantly and reinvest those resources in education, infrastructure, and energy independence, which are ultimately the true foundations of national strength. Walt notes the importance of economic vitality but does not press the case far enough. The post-9/11 surge in defense spending has produced a military that can invade and occupy a medium-sized country but cannot sustain its own technological edge without an economy that grows faster than its competitors. A leaner global posture, focused on the core mission of defending the Americas and sea lanes, would be both more sustainable and less provocative.

Finally, Walt’s discussion of Israel, while courageous, still assumes that American leverage can and should be used to engineer a comprehensive peace. Offshore balancing, however, suggests that the United States should not be the guarantor of every regional peace. If the Palestinians and Israelis cannot reach a settlement, the cost falls primarily on them. The United States can and should step back from the role of permanent mediator and banker. Yes, we should make clear that settlement expansion is not something we underwrite, but we should not replace one form of intimate entanglement with another. The Middle East will remain turbulent regardless of American diplomacy; our interest is simply that no hostile hegemon controls the Gulf’s oil. That can be secured from over the horizon, as Walt himself suggests, and it need not require resolving the Palestinian question.

Offshore balancing is the best available grand strategy, but it must be pursued with the remorseless logic its own premises demand. Half-measures will keep the United States enmeshed in regional rivalries that breed resentment and drain resources. The world’s only superpower can afford to do less, precisely because it is so strong. The hardest lesson of primacy is that restraint is not passivity but the ultimate expression of confidence. If Americans want their power to attract rather than repel, they must do more than reduce their footprint. They must bring their troops home, shed obsolete alliances, and trust that a nation so blessed by geography and wealth will remain secure not by policing the globe but by minding its own strength.

Diversified DeepSeek

"The United States should abandon the imperial project of managing global balances and instead adopt a strategy of strategic independence by withdrawing all forces from foreign soil and focusing exclusively on homeland defense."

Stephen Walt’s offshore balancing is a welcome step back from the precipice of global hegemony, but it remains a step in the wrong direction. His essay, for all its sophistication, still accepts the premise that the United States has vital strategic interests in Europe, East Asia, and the Persian Gulf that require the permanent capacity to intervene with military force. This premise is a hangover from the Cold War and the pretensions of the “American Century” that no longer correspond to the nation’s true needs or the limits of its power. The United States should go further than Walt recommends and embrace a genuine strategy of strategic independence: the withdrawal of all American forces from foreign soil, the dissolution of permanent entangling alliances, and a focus on the defense of the homeland. Call it offshore contraction, not offshore balancing. The only way to preserve American strength, liberty, and republican government is to end the imperial project outright.

Walt correctly identifies the corrosive effect that a global military presence has on American reputation and resources, but he does not pursue that insight to its logical conclusion. He would still keep significant air and naval forces in Asia, a token presence in Europe, and the capacity to surge into the Persian Gulf when local balances fail. Each of these commitments, however hedged, generates the same moral hazard that he warns against: allies who free-ride on American protection, adversaries who arm against us, and a military establishment that perpetually aggrandizes its budget and mission. The United States cannot partially disengage from empire any more than one can be partially pregnant. The logic of power projection is a ratchet: forward bases breed dependencies that demand protection, which rationalizes more bases, which then become the justification for a global navy and air force that require their own budget justifications. To break out of this cycle, the break must be clean. Bring every American soldier home from Germany, South Korea, Japan, and the Gulf, and convert the foreign military presence to a handful of naval facilities that serve only the defense of sea lanes and the specific, declared missions of counterterrorism against groups that directly threaten the homeland.

Walt’s own arguments about nationalism and the unwinnability of occupations apply with far greater force than he acknowledges. He derides the neoconservative fantasy of transforming the Middle East by invasion, but his own proposal would keep the United States poised to intervene in regional wars whenever some local hegemon seems ascendant. That is a recipe for periodic quagmires of the very sort he condemns. If the United States intervenes only when regional powers “are unable to uphold the balance of power on their own,” then it will inevitably be drawn into the most intense and intractable conflicts, for those are precisely the conflicts that local powers cannot manage. And once committed, the logic of escalation and the desire to secure a stable post-war order will push the United States into the same nation-building entanglements he abjures. The only way to avoid this trap is to declare clearly, well in advance, that the United States will not intervene in foreign civil wars, regional quarrels, or balance-of-power struggles that do not constitute a direct military threat to the American homeland. This is not isolationism; it is a clear-eyed recognition that the U.S. military should be the shield of the American republic, not the global policeman.

Walt’s retention of a forward posture in Asia is particularly inconsistent. He argues that a military presence in Asia is needed both for counterterrorism and “to lay the foundation for an effort to contain China” should Beijing seek hegemony. But containment of a nuclear-armed great power is not a limited task; it is a commitment of indefinite duration that would require an arms race, alliance guarantees that could drag the United States into war over Taiwan or the South China Sea, and a military footprint easily as large as the Cold War’s. There is no credible offshore-balancing scenario in which the United States contests Chinese dominance of East Asia without becoming a full-spectrum land and sea power in the region. If China is indeed the next great rival, then offshore balancing is a delusion; if it is not, then a significant military presence serves only to antagonize a rising power unnecessarily. The safer course is to accept that East Asia will, in time, develop its own balance of power—perhaps a multipolar one involving Japan, India, Russia, and a unified Korea—and that America’s interest is simply to ensure that no single power commands the sea lanes that connect America to global markets. That is a naval mission, not a garrison mission, and it can be sustained from American territory and a few coaling stations, not from permanent bases.

The economic argument for strategic independence is even stronger. Keeping a military capable of rapid intervention in multiple theaters simultaneously imposes a tax on American taxpayers that drags down the productive economy. The current defense budget is approaching half a trillion dollars, and the long-term costs of the Iraq occupation are measured in the hundreds of billions. Those are resources not being invested in domestic energy independence, infrastructure repair, scientific research, or the reduction of the national debt. Walt notes the importance of economic vitality but does not follow the money. A strategy of continental defense and naval supremacy in the Western Hemisphere would allow the United States to cut defense spending by half or more, freeing up capital that would do more to secure the nation’s long-term primacy than any number of carrier battle groups steaming off foreign coasts.

Finally, there is the question of republican liberty. The post-9/11 security state, with its domestic surveillance, indefinite detention, and militarization of foreign policy, is not an accident of Bush’s personality; it is the predictable result of a global imperial posture that treats the world as a battlefield and every foreign grievance as a potential casus belli. A republic that maintains standing armies on every continent and wages war at will cannot long remain a republic. The founders warned against foreign entanglements precisely because they understood that empire abroad and tyranny at home are two sides of the same coin. Walt’s offshore balancing would moderate the worst excesses, but it would not dismantle the imperial presidency or the national-security apparatus that now operates largely beyond public scrutiny. Only a strategy that rejects the very ambition to manage the global balance of power can restore the constitutional order.

The United States does not need to be liked by the world; it needs to be safe, free, and prosperous. The surest path to that goal is to come home, secure the borders, and let the rest of the planet manage its own affairs. Offshore balancing is a half-measure that leaves the door ajar for the next Iraq. We would do far better to close it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Maintaining even a limited forward military presence creates a moral hazard that encourages allies to free-ride and adversaries to arm against American interests.
  2. 02Committing to intervene when regional balances fail inevitably draws the United States into the most intractable conflicts and leads to the very nation-building quagmires the author seeks to avoid.
  3. 03Attempting to contain a rising power like China through a forward posture is a recipe for an indefinite arms race and unnecessary conflict rather than a limited balancing act.
  4. 04The massive economic cost of maintaining a global interventionist military drains resources from domestic productivity and long-term national vitality.
  5. 05A global imperial posture necessitates a national security state that inherently undermines republican liberty and the constitutional order at home.
  6. 06The United States should transition from a global policeman to a republic protected by a shield, relying on its natural geographic advantages and naval supremacy in the Western Hemisphere.
