"Doping in professional sports should be regulated and medically supervised rather than prohibited to ensure transparency, athlete safety, and fairness."

The honest answer is uncomfortable: many forms of doping should be brought out of the shadows and regulated rather than prohibited. The current system pretends to preserve purity, but in practice it produces secrecy, hypocrisy and selective punishment.

Elite sport is already an exercise in artificial enhancement. Athletes benefit from altitude tents, specialized diets, legal supplements, advanced surgery, high-tech swimsuits until they are banned, and national training systems that ordinary people could never access. A runner born with a naturally high hematocrit is celebrated; a runner who medically raises oxygen-carrying capacity is disgraced. The line between natural talent, technology and pharmacology is not as philosophically clean as anti-doping rhetoric suggests.

Nor is prohibition obviously protecting athletes. When substances are banned, athletes who choose to use them must rely on clandestine doctors, rumor and concealment. They may avoid medical supervision precisely because detection is the greater fear. A regulated model could set health limits, require physician oversight, monitor blood values and punish endangerment rather than chemistry itself. If EPO is dangerous when abused, then the goal should be to prevent dangerous hematocrit levels, not to maintain a symbolic war that sophisticated dopers may evade.

The fairness objection is serious, but prohibition has not created fairness. It may simply favor athletes with the best access to undetectable methods, corrupt officials or expert pharmacology. Poorer athletes and countries are more likely to be caught using cruder means. Meanwhile, every astonishing performance becomes suspect, as with the whispers around an “unbelievable” swim. A sport in which extraordinary achievement automatically invites accusation is not morally healthy either.

Legalization need not mean an anything-goes pharmaceutical arms race. Sports could adopt medically supervised enhancement categories, publish permissible ranges and ban methods that create extreme risk. Minors should receive special protection. Coercive team or state doping should be treated harshly. But adult professionals should not be forced into a pretense that their bodies are untouched by science.

The anti-doping regime asks us to believe in a natural athlete who scarcely exists at the elite level. Rather than chase that myth at enormous cost, sport should choose transparency, medical control and honesty about what high performance has become.

"Doping should remain prohibited to protect athletes and fair competition, but enforcement must be disciplined and evidence-based to prevent a corrosive culture of unfounded suspicion."

The worst response to suspicious performances is to let suspicion become evidence. Doping should remain prohibited where it clearly enhances performance and endangers athletes, but the current climate of accusation is becoming corrosive. A young swimmer posts a startling time and almost immediately the whispers begin. A champion from a country viewed with distrust is asked to prove not only that she won, but that she deserves to be believed.

That is not a healthy foundation for international sport. The Olympics depend on rules, but they also depend on the presumption that achievement is possible. If every exceptional result is treated as a disguised crime, the Games become a theater of resentment. The history of doping gives people reason to be skeptical; many celebrated champions have later been exposed. Yet skepticism must not become a substitute for due process, especially when it attaches unevenly to nationality, race or geopolitical rivalry.

Anti-doping enforcement should continue, but it must be disciplined. Testing should be rigorous, scientifically grounded and independent. Athletes should have clear rights to challenge results. Public officials and commentators should avoid insinuations unsupported by evidence. A failed EPO test, such as the one reported for Alex Schwazer, is a concrete matter. Raised eyebrows about an “unbelievable” swim are not the same thing.

There is also a danger in pretending that doping is the only explanation for excellence. Athletes improve because of coaching, physiology, technique, nutrition, psychology and generational change. Records often look impossible until they are broken. In swimming especially, changes in training methods, body type, starts, turns and race strategy can produce leaps. None of this proves that any given performance is clean. It does mean that amazement is not an indictment.

The anti-doping system should aim to protect athletes from both cheaters and witch hunts. That means focusing on substances with real performance effects, improving out-of-competition testing, storing samples for retesting when science advances, and maintaining confidentiality until violations are established. It also means recognizing the human cost of reckless suspicion.

Sport needs clean competition, but it also needs wonder. If we cannot watch a brilliant race without immediately reaching for an accusation, doping has damaged us even when no one has doped.

"Doping should not be universally allowed, but the anti-doping system must be reformed to distinguish between performance-enhancing substances, health risks, and recreational drugs to maintain its credibility."

The question “Should doping be allowed?” is too broad. The better question is: which substances threaten the integrity of competition, which threaten health, and which merely offend the sensibilities of sports administrators? Treating all violations as the same is one reason the anti-doping system loses credibility.

Consider the examples from the London Olympics. A judo athlete expelled for marijuana and a race walker expelled for EPO are not morally or competitively identical cases. EPO is a blood booster with a direct and powerful relevance to endurance performance. It can help an athlete train harder and compete longer; it also carries real medical risks if abused. It belongs at the center of anti-doping enforcement. Marijuana, by contrast, is not generally thought of as a performance enhancer in a combat sport. It may violate a code of conduct or national law, but expelling an Olympian for it sends the message that anti-doping is as much about policing lifestyle as protecting fair play.

That confusion matters. Rules gain authority when athletes and spectators can see their purpose. A banned list that includes anabolic steroids, EPO, masking agents and recreational drugs under one moral umbrella invites cynicism. It also gives defenders of legalization an easy target: if the system is foolish about marijuana, they say, perhaps it is foolish about everything. That conclusion does not follow. It means the system needs pruning.

A sensible policy would divide prohibited conduct into categories. Performance-enhancing substances with significant competitive effects should remain banned and vigorously tested. Medical risks should be monitored independently of whether a substance improves performance. Recreational drugs should generally be handled outside the anti-doping framework unless used in competition in ways that create safety concerns. Therapeutic exemptions should be transparent enough to prevent abuse but humane enough not to punish illness.

Sport will never be perfectly natural or perfectly fair. Genetics, money, coaching and technology all matter. But the existence of inequality is not a reason to allow pharmaceutical manipulation without limits. It is a reason to write rules that target the real harms. If anti-doping agencies want public confidence, they should stop pretending that every banned substance represents the same kind of cheating. The future of enforcement depends on making distinctions.

The case for ending the charade of anti-doping testing grows stronger with every Olympics. We are watching a system that catches a handful of athletes — often the unlucky or the careless — while the sophisticated cheats sail through. Marion Jones passed hundreds of tests. Lance Armstrong, currently under federal scrutiny, has never failed one in competition. The judo fighter expelled this week tested positive for marijuana, a substance that gives no competitive advantage in any meaningful sense. Meanwhile, the chemists are always a step ahead of the labs.

If we accept that elite sport is already a pharmacological arms race conducted in secret, regulated doping under medical supervision would be safer than the current black market. Athletes today inject substances of unknown purity, calibrated by rogue doctors or, worse, by themselves. East German records from the 1970s and 80s reveal the horrors of unsupervised state doping. A transparent system, with disclosure requirements and medical monitoring, would at least protect athletes' long-term health.

There is also a fairness argument that cuts against the current regime. Wealthy federations and well-funded athletes can afford the designer drugs, the masking agents, and the expert advice to evade detection. Athletes from poorer countries, or those without access to sophisticated medical teams, are the ones who get caught — or who genuinely compete clean and lose. The system entrenches inequality while pretending to police it.

None of this is to celebrate doping. The ideal of clean sport is beautiful. But ideals must be measured against reality, and the reality is that testing catches a tiny fraction of users, ruins reputations selectively, and forces athletes into dangerous underground regimens. We already accept enormous training advantages from altitude tents, hyperbaric chambers, customized nutrition, and cutting-edge sports medicine. The line between "natural" and "artificial" enhancement is already incoherent.

A regulated approach — perhaps permitting certain substances above a safety threshold, with full disclosure — would acknowledge what fans already half-suspect when they watch a world record fall. It would be more honest than our current ritual of moral outrage every two years, in which we feign shock that athletes pushed to superhuman performance turn out to be using superhuman means.

They understand that their careers are short, that injury is always possible, that their bodies will eventually fail them regardless of what they do. Demanding they refuse any pharmaceutical assistance to gain a few extra years of competitive performance is paternalistic.

I am not suggesting we encourage doping among amateurs or young athletes. But in the professional ranks, where informed adults compete for contracts and championships, regulation is more honest and more fair than prohibition.

"Instead of choosing between a total ban or total permissiveness, sports organizations should adopt a principled, science-based anti-doping policy that focuses on athlete safety and genuine competitive fairness."

The doping debate is usually framed as a binary — ban everything versus allow everything — but the more honest question is: where do we draw the line, and on what principle?

Consider the substances at issue this week. Marijuana is on the prohibited list, and a judo fighter has lost his Olympic dreams over it. Yet marijuana confers no athletic advantage and may even impair performance. Its presence on the list reflects moralism, not sports science. EPO, on the other hand, dramatically enhances endurance performance and carries serious cardiovascular risks. Lumping these together under the banner of "doping" is intellectually lazy and corrodes the legitimacy of the whole enterprise.

A coherent anti-doping policy would be guided by two clear principles: athlete safety and meaningful competitive fairness. Substances that pose significant health risks at performance-enhancing doses — EPO, anabolic steroids, human growth hormone — should remain banned, and aggressively tested for. Recreational drugs that don't enhance performance should be a matter for athletes' national federations and personal lives, not Olympic expulsion. Caffeine, once banned, was sensibly removed from the list years ago; the same logic should apply to marijuana.

We should also be more thoughtful about the gray areas. Athletes routinely sleep in altitude tents to boost red blood cell production — a physiological effect identical to mild EPO use, but achieved "naturally." Cortisone injections, therapeutic use exemptions for ADHD medications, hypoxic training: these are accepted enhancements. The line between training innovation and cheating is blurrier than the rhetoric admits.

What this means in practice is that we should continue and strengthen testing for genuinely dangerous performance enhancers, while pruning the prohibited list of substances that don't belong there. We should invest in the biological passport approach, which catches doping patterns rather than relying on point-in-time tests that elite cheaters can evade. And we should be transparent about therapeutic use exemptions, which currently function as a loophole the wealthy and well-connected can exploit.

The choice isn't between prohibition and permissiveness. It's between a principled, science-based regime and the incoherent mess we have now.

The question before us—"should doping be allowed?"—is poorly framed, and answering it requires us to distinguish between different categories of substances and different contexts of use.

Performance-enhancing drugs exist on a spectrum. At one end are caffeine and creatine, substances that almost no one questions and that are present in the bodies of most elite athletes. At the other end are drugs like EPO that carry genuine mortality risks and that have been linked to athlete deaths. The blanket term "doping" obscures this spectrum and prevents the nuanced policy discussions we actually need.

What matters most is not whether athletes use substances but whether their use is safe and whether it preserves meaningful competition. If a drug carries minimal health risks and can be used by all competitors equally, it is hard to see why we should prohibit it. Competition is not about returning to some imagined state of nature; it is about human excellence, however achieved.

But we should be skeptical of anyone who claims that all doping should be permitted. Certain substances do pose serious health risks that even informed adults should not be permitted to assume, particularly when those adults may be under pressure from coaches, sponsors, or national systems that profit from their performance. We also have legitimate interests in preserving sport as a domain where talent and training, not pharmaceutical portfolios, determine outcomes.

The answer, then, is not simple prohibition or simple permission. It is a regulatory framework that distinguishes between substances by risk, that ensures medical supervision, and that preserves the core uncertainty that makes competition watchable and meaningful.

The timing of this debate, set against the London Olympics, presents an opportunity to reflect on what we want from international sport and whether our current anti-doping framework serves those goals.

Substances that fail on all three counts should be banned; substances that pass on all three should be permitted; the middle cases require careful judgment.

This would not eliminate controversy—reasonable people will disagree about how to weight each factor. But it would make our anti-doping regime more transparent and more defensible than the current system, which too often appears arbitrary and politically motivated rather than principled.

The expelled judo competitor and the EPO-using race walker may both have violated the rules, but whether their violations merit the same moral weight is a question worth taking seriously.

Testing for that kind of doping is necessary.

Marijuana is different. In most Olympic events, it is hard to see it as a meaningful performance enhancer. In some sports it may pose a safety issue; in others it may be more relevant to public image than competition. Testing positive for cannabis metabolites can also reveal prior use rather than intoxication during the event. That does not mean federations must ignore it, but expulsion from the Olympics should be reserved for conduct that seriously threatens fairness, safety or both.

A sensible anti-doping policy would be narrower and tougher. It would focus intensely on substances and methods that enhance strength, endurance, recovery, pain tolerance in dangerous ways, or the ability to conceal other drugs. It would include out-of-competition testing, because serious doping is often done during training, not on race day. It would pursue doctors, coaches and suppliers, not merely the athlete who gets caught at the end of the chain.

At the same time, it would stop pretending that every banned substance is equally corrupting. Recreational drug use should be handled, when necessary, through rules about impairment, safety and athlete conduct. Medical use should be judged carefully through therapeutic exemptions. Penalties should be proportionate.

This distinction matters because anti-doping depends on public trust. When authorities appear to punish a cannabis case with the same symbolic force as blood manipulation, many people conclude the whole system is arbitrary. That skepticism helps the real dopers. If sport wants to defend fair competition, it should draw sharper lines: strict enforcement against genuine performance fraud, restraint where the connection to competition is weak.

  3. 03Marijuana use rarely functions as a performance enhancer and should be managed through safety and conduct rules rather than Olympic expulsion.
  4. 04Effective enforcement requires focusing on out-of-competition testing and targeting the support networks of doctors and coaches rather than just the athletes.
  5. 05Treating all banned substances with equal severity undermines public trust and creates a skepticism that ultimately protects those committing genuine performance fraud.
