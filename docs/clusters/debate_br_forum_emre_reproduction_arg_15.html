Egg Freezing Is Not a Perk

I want to focus on the part of Merve Emre's essay that has gotten the least attention in feminist debate and that, to my mind, deserves the most: the moment when S explains, with no apparent irony, that her frozen eggs are a "happy product of Silicon Valley's marriage of capitalist competition and social justice." That sentence should stop us cold. It is a near-perfect example of how the language of inclusion has been absorbed by, and made indispensable to, the very forms of corporate power feminism once defined itself against.

Let's recover the basic facts about the egg-freezing benefit. When Facebook, Apple, and the rest announced these policies in 2014–2015, they were lavishly praised in the business press as forward-looking pro-woman gestures. But the demographic context was unmissable. Tech firms were under intense public pressure over their dismal gender ratios, their pipeline rhetoric, and their well-documented hostility to women in their thirties — the age at which engineers begin having children and at which female engineers, in particular, leave the industry in droves. The egg-freezing benefit did not address that hostility. It restructured it. It told women: the problem with your career is your fertility, and we will pay to delay your fertility, so you can keep working.

Compare what these companies could have offered instead. They could have offered, as Suzanne McGee pointed out in the Guardian piece Emre dismisses, on-site childcare, generous parental leave, lactation rooms, schedule flexibility, the ability to take a year off without career suicide. These benefits would have made it possible for women to have children when they actually wanted to and remain in the workforce. They would also have cost more, structurally, because they would have required the company to accommodate parents as parents, not as freelancers with extracted ovaries. Egg freezing, by contrast, is a one-time medical reimbursement. It privatizes the cost of having children later and externalizes the risk — physical, emotional, financial — onto the worker herself.

Emre is too quick, I think, to wave away McGee's critique as misdirected at women's choices rather than at corporate policy. McGee is plainly criticizing the policy. And what is being criticized is not whether women should make use of available technology, but whether the technology — at this price, in this institutional configuration — actually represents the choice it is sold as. S is sympathetic and articulate and her sense of agency is real. But her account is precisely the account a successful corporate program of this kind would produce. She does not regret the choice. She also tells us, in detail, about the bruises, the weight gain, the mood swings, the OHSS risk, the anxiety of the daily call, the prohibition on exercise and sex, the not-thinking-about whether the procedure will fail. This is a description of labor — bodily, gendered, unpaid labor — undertaken on behalf of an employer who has bought a future option on her reproductive timeline. It is exactly the kind of labor Firestone wanted to abolish.

The LGBT-inclusion framing of the benefit is, I think, the most ideologically clever part of the package, and Emre does the reader a disservice by accepting it on its own terms. Yes, lesbian and gay employees were locked out of insurance-mandated infertility coverage because they could not produce a diagnosis. Yes, the company's LGBT association pushed for inclusion, and yes, that pressure helped extend the benefit. But the benefit that resulted is not, in the main, a benefit for queer family formation; it is an egg-freezing benefit that disproportionately serves straight women postponing childbearing. The queer-justice framing is, in effect, the rhetorical engine that made an expensive, image-burnishing perk politically saleable. This is the standard logic of contemporary "diversity" politics within capital: take a real, legitimate grievance from a marginalized constituency, redesign the response so that its primary beneficiaries are not that constituency, and then describe the result as social justice. Google and LinkedIn did not start covering elective egg freezing because lesbians needed it. They started covering it because they were competing for talent in a market that demanded such signals.

There is a longer history here, and feminists of color have been telling it for decades. Dorothy Roberts, whom Emre invokes at the end, has documented how the reproductive choices made available to differently situated women have always reflected the labor and population-management needs of capital and the state. Wealthy white women got the pill and abortion rights, often before poor women and women of color did; poor women and women of color got coerced sterilization, Norplant mandates, and family-cap welfare rules. Egg freezing fits comfortably in that genealogy. It is a technology that helps a thin layer of high-skill professional women time their childbearing to the rhythms of their employers' demand cycles. It is not a technology that has, so far, done much for the women who clean those employers' offices or care for those employers' children.

I am not arguing against egg freezing as such. Like Emre, I think the technology has real uses, and I am sympathetic to S and to anyone who avails herself of what is available. I am arguing against the seductive framing in which the corporate version of the procedure is celebrated as feminist progress. To accept that framing is to accept that the appropriate scale of feminist victory is the employer-sponsored benefit package — that the relevant unit of analysis is the individual woman optimizing her reproductive options within an institutional structure she does not control and is not invited to change.

What Emre's essay accomplishes, in spite of itself, is a quiet exposure of the costs of that bargain. The most haunting line in the S section is not S's, but Emre's own observation: "She has greater control over her reproductive future than ever before, yet she seems even more shackled to the specter of the natural now that the choice is hers to make." I would put it differently. S is not haunted by the natural. She is haunted by the fact that an enormous technological apparatus has been mobilized to defer a decision that, in a society organized differently, could simply have been made. The unmade child whose timing she is now responsible for is also the future child whose support — housing, schooling, healthcare, time with parents — is not, and will not be, socially guaranteed.

Firestone wanted technology to free women from gestation. What Silicon Valley has given them is technology to time their gestation around quarterly earnings. These are not the same thing. Feminism needs the discernment to tell them apart.

  6. 06True reproductive liberation requires changing the institutional structures of society rather than merely providing technological tools to time gestation around corporate earnings cycles.
