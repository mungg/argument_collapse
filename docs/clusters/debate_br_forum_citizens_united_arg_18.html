History’s Lesson: This Has Happened Before, and We Fixed It

Lawrence Lessig’s diagnosis of institutional corruption arrives at a moment of genuine crisis, and his moral clarity is welcome. But to understand both the depth of the problem and the possibilities for reform, we need to place our current predicament in historical context. The Gilded Age of the late nineteenth century confronted Americans with a political system even more thoroughly captured by concentrated wealth than anything we see today. Then, as now, critics decried the power of money over legislatures, the corruption of public trust, and the seeming impotence of ordinary voters. And then, as I believe will happen again, a sustained democratic movement reshaped the rules of political life and restored a measure of popular control.

In the decades after the Civil War, industrial corporations and railroad barons wielded immense influence over Congress and state legislatures. Campaigns were financed through assessments on officeholders and direct corporate contributions. The Senate was so beholden to the trusts that it was called the “Millionaires’ Club.” The system was not merely an “economy of influence” in Lessig’s sense; it was a naked fusion of economic and political power. Yet from that nadir emerged the Progressive movement, which demanded the direct election of senators, the secret ballot, voter initiative and referendum, and — critically — the first federal campaign finance laws. The Tillman Act of 1907 banned corporate contributions to federal candidates, and subsequent legislation extended disclosure requirements and spending limits. These reforms did not end the influence of money, but they fundamentally altered the rules of engagement, establishing the principle that the people’s representatives could act to protect the integrity of elections.

Lessig’s “dependency corruption” framework would have resonated with the Progressives. They understood that corruption was not simply a matter of crooked politicians stuffing envelopes with cash; it was a structural condition in which the institutions of government had become dependent upon the very interests they were supposed to regulate. Their response was not to accept that dependence as an inevitable feature of human nature, but to redesign the institutions. They changed the Constitution (the Seventeenth Amendment), enacted sweeping statutes, and — crucially — they mobilized a social movement that shifted the norms of acceptable political behavior.

The Supreme Court’s decision in Citizens United is a dramatic setback, but it is not the end of the story. In fact, it may prove to be the galvanizing event that the campaign finance reform movement has needed. The public backlash against the decision has been swift and intense, cutting across party lines. A constitutional amendment to overturn Citizens United is already being debated in Congress and in state legislatures. And even without an amendment, Congress can pursue the kind of small-donor matching systems that Lessig mentions, which would change the material incentives without running afoul of current First Amendment doctrine. The DISCLOSE Act, though it failed by a single vote in the Senate, demonstrated that there is a legislative majority for greater transparency.

History teaches us that democratic renewal is neither quick nor guaranteed. The Progressives fought for decades to achieve their reforms, and they faced a Supreme Court that, in cases like Lochner, was often hostile. But they prevailed because they built a broad coalition grounded in the lived experience of citizens who felt their government had been stolen. Today’s tea party activists, progressive netroots, and good-government organizations are, in their different ways, tapping into the same deep well of frustration. If Lessig’s framework can help unite these disparate groups around a common diagnosis — that the system itself has become institutionally corrupt, without any need to demonize individual politicians — then we may be at the start of a new Progressive era. The past is not a perfect guide, but it reminds us that seemingly immovable structures of power can, under the right conditions, be transformed.
