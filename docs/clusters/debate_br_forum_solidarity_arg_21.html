"While social endurance is a vital component of solidarity, it must be redefined through the lens of disability justice to ensure that the physical and logistical demands of 'showing up' do not exclude those with variable bodily and caregiving needs."

Mie Inouye’s call for “social endurance” is compelling, but it raises a question that disability politics has long pressed on every theory of solidarity: who is able to keep showing up, and at what cost?

The essay’s central image is the meeting—the fundraiser, the church gathering, the classroom, the Young Patriots’ room where Bob Lee asks, “What do you want?” Inouye argues that solidarity grows when people repeatedly enter such rooms, encounter those unlike themselves, and remain through annoyance and conflict. There is wisdom here. But for many disabled, chronically ill, neurodivergent, immunocompromised, and caregiving people, the demand to show up physically and regularly has always been complicated. After COVID, it is more complicated still.

The pandemic appears in Inouye’s essay through the death of Uncle Gerald and the isolation that preceded the 2020 protests. She writes movingly about people entering the streets because they were “dying of being alone” and needed to be pressed against other bodies. That was true for many. But for others, those same crowds were dangerous or inaccessible. Immunocompromised people, high-risk elders, disabled people dependent on care networks, and those living with vulnerable family members often could not join without risking illness or death. Some watched as movements committed to collective liberation treated masking, ventilation, hybrid participation, and remote access as secondary or temporary concerns.

This is not a minor addendum to solidarity. It goes to the heart of it. If solidarity requires bodily co-presence, then those whose bodies cannot safely or easily be present become peripheral. If endurance means sitting through long meetings in inaccessible rooms, people with pain, fatigue, sensory sensitivities, mobility impairments, or caregiving responsibilities are marked as insufficiently committed. If conflict is imagined as a face-to-face intensity one must withstand, those whose nervous systems respond differently may be excluded from the transformative process.

Disability justice offers a different starting point: bodies and minds are interdependent, variable, and vulnerable. No political community can assume a standard participant who can attend at night, sit still for hours, process rapid debate, speak publicly, risk arrest, march long distances, or recover quickly from emotional strain. The “room” of solidarity must be built for bodyminds that differ.

This does not negate Inouye’s argument; it deepens it. Social endurance should not mean heroic individual tolerance. It should mean collective arrangements that make sustained participation possible for more people. A ramp is social endurance. So is child care. So is a hybrid meeting link that actually allows remote participants to speak and vote. So is a culture in which people can step outside during conflict without being treated as avoidant. So is a schedule that recognizes fatigue. So is masking when vulnerable comrades ask for it. So is not organizing every bonding activity around loud bars.

The left often treats access as a logistical matter to be handled after the “real” political questions are settled. But access is political knowledge. A group that asks who cannot enter the room will understand power better than one that equates visibility with commitment. A campaign that notices who is missing will ask better questions about work schedules, transit, policing, language, digital divides, family obligations, and health. Disability access is therefore not a niche concern. It is a method for uncovering the hidden assumptions of political life.

Inouye’s critique of deference politics can be rethought from this angle. Disability movements popularized the demand “nothing about us without us,” which is a form of deference: those most affected must shape decisions. Yet disability communities also know that self-knowledge is not simple. People internalize ableism. Needs conflict. One person needs low stimulation; another needs emotional expressiveness. One person needs remote access; another cannot use digital tools. One person’s accommodation may create labor for another. There is no pure standpoint from which all answers follow.

What follows is not the abandonment of deference but a practice of access-centered negotiation. Instead of assuming either that the most affected person is always right or that everyone’s preference has equal weight, the group asks: what conditions allow each person to participate with dignity? What burdens are being shifted, and onto whom? Which needs are urgent, which are flexible, and which reveal a deeper design failure? This is epistemic humility made concrete.

The concept of endurance also changes when viewed through chronic illness. Many activists valorize stamina: the all-night meeting, the jail stint, the march in heat, the rapid response, the constant availability. Such practices can be necessary in moments of crisis, but they easily become ableist measures of worth. Disabled organizers have long insisted that rest is not retreat, slowness is not apathy, and sustainability is not bourgeois softness. Movements that burn through bodies reproduce the very disposability they oppose.

This is especially relevant after the initial years of COVID. By September 2023, many institutions have declared a return to normal, even as long COVID, ongoing transmission, and medical vulnerability remain real. The political meaning of “showing up” has changed. To insist on pre-pandemic forms of togetherness without mitigation is to decide whose risk counts. A solidaristic organization must be willing to ask whether the desire for unmasked togetherness is being purchased at the expense of those for whom infection could be catastrophic.

At the same time, isolation has its own harms. Inouye is right that embodied presence can transform people in ways digital communication often cannot. Online spaces intensify misinterpretation, encourage performance, and make exit too easy. The task is not to replace physical organizing with remote life but to multiply forms of presence. Some meetings should be in person; some hybrid; some asynchronous; some one-on-one by phone; some outdoors; some centered on material aid rather than discussion. A movement with only one mode of participation will mistake a subset of people for “the people.”

Bob Lee’s tactile organizing in American Revolution 2—his hands in hair, on shoulders, grasping hands—also reads differently after disability and consent politics. Inouye’s students find that physicality unimaginable, and she sees in their reaction a loss of capacity to bump up against others. Perhaps. But some of the reluctance also reflects political learning. Touch can convey care and authority; it can also violate boundaries. People with trauma histories, autistic people, disabled people, and many others may experience unsolicited touch not as solidarity but as threat. The lesson should not be that contemporary distance is simply neoliberal atomization. It is also an attempt, however imperfect, to respect bodily autonomy.

Still, autonomy alone is insufficient. Disability justice does not imagine independent individuals politely leaving one another alone. It insists on interdependence. The question is how to practice interdependence without coercion. Consent is one answer. Access intimacy is another: the sometimes wordless trust that others understand and honor your needs. Such trust develops over time, through precisely the repeated contact Inouye values. But it develops best when people are not forced to choose between belonging and self-protection.

Uncle Gerald’s story also invites a disability analysis. His refusal of hospital care, his self-treatment, his maskless visit to a sick friend, and his death reflect not only ideology but a culture of masculine invulnerability. Many communities teach people, especially men, to deny dependency until it is too late. Disability politics challenges that denial. It says that needing care is not failure, that bodies are porous, that health is collective, and that freedom cannot mean the right to expose others to harm. A solidarity capable of saving Uncle Gerald would have had to contest his libertarianism, but also the deeper shame attached to vulnerability.

Inouye’s essay asks what kind of organization might have helped her family identify racial capitalism as the root of both internment and premature death. I would add: what kind of organization might have taught them to speak openly about dependency, illness, fear, and care? What kind of solidarity makes it easier for a rural doctor to say, “I need help”? What kind makes it obvious that protecting others from infection is an act of love rather than submission?

Social endurance remains a vital idea, but it must be freed from any single image of the meeting, the march, or the crowd. The point is not simply to keep showing up in the same way no matter what. It is to build forms of collective life capacious enough that people can show up differently and still belong. Solidarity now means staying with one another through conflict, yes. It also means redesigning the room.
