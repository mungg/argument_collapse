Diversified DeepSeek

"The presidential primary system should replace the current Iowa and New Hampshire openers with a simultaneous 'First Four' primary consisting of diverse states that collectively represent the nation's demographic and economic breadth."

The problem isn’t that Iowa and New Hampshire go first; it’s that they go first alone. No single state can possibly represent the nation’s demographic, economic, and cultural breadth. But we need not choose between a tiny, unrepresentative opener and a national primary free-for-all. The answer is a carefully constructed “First Four”—a collection of states that, when their results are read together, look like America.

Imagine a joint first primary day featuring four states, each selected for the demographic balance they collectively provide: New Jersey for its racial diversity and urban density, Missouri for its Midwestern agricultural and manufacturing mix, Arizona for its growing Latino population and border-state identity, and North Carolina for its large African American community and fast-changing suburbs. Together, these states are not a perfect statistical replica, but they present a far more complete electorate than two overwhelmingly white, rural states. Candidates would have to assemble a coalition that bridges North Jersey commuters, St. Louis union members, Phoenix retirees, and Raleigh tech workers all at once.

This format preserves the virtues of the current system. The states are still small enough in total delegate share to allow a delayed national verdict if the race remains unsettled, and their media markets are not so astronomically expensive as to lock out all but the wealthy. Yet by forcing candidates to engage with multiple regional cultures simultaneously, the First Four would eliminate the myopic pandering to ethanol, maple syrup, or any other single-interest monoculture. It would also silence the perennial complaint that black and brown voters are taken for granted until late in the calendar. By making diversity a prerequisite for an early victory, this plan transforms the first contest from a parochial gatekeeper into a genuine national bellwether.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A multi-state opening day featuring New Jersey, Missouri, Arizona, and North Carolina would force candidates to build broad coalitions across different racial, regional, and industrial groups.
  2. 02This model preserves the benefits of small-state retail politics and manageable media costs while preventing any single state from acting as an unrepresentative gatekeeper.
  3. 03Moving diverse states to the front of the calendar ensures that the concerns of urban, minority, and suburban voters are addressed at the start of the campaign rather than as an afterthought.
  4. 04A simultaneous regional mix eliminates the disproportionate influence of single-interest monocultures like ethanol or maple syrup that dominate the current early states.
