Diversified Gemini

"The responding essay argues that intellectuals must reject Robin D. G. Kelley’s call for militant partisanship and instead uphold their fundamental responsibility to defend objective truth and empirical rigor as the only effective means of resisting authoritarianism."

Robin D. G. Kelley’s essay, “The Responsibility of Intellectuals in the Age of Fascism and Genocide,” is a masterclass in the mobilization of apocalyptic rhetoric. Driven by a palpable sense of moral emergency, Kelley argues that the traditional Chomskyan role of the intellectual—speaking truth to power and exposing state lies—is obsolete. In an era he broadly and persistently defines as an age of "fascism and genocide," Kelley insists that intellectuals must discard the pursuit of objective truth in favor of militant partisanship. They must become "organic intellectuals," choosing a side and embedding themselves within an insurgent struggle. While Kelley’s outrage at the civilian death toll in Gaza and his concern over authoritarian overreach in the United States are entirely justified, his underlying epistemological argument is disastrous. By embracing massive rhetorical inflation and explicitly downgrading the importance of empirical truth in favor of ideological warfare, Kelley surrenders the only ground upon which authoritarianism can actually be defeated.

The most glaring flaw in Kelley’s argument is his absolute, unquestioning reliance on the terms "fascism" and "genocide" to describe the current American political landscape and the conflict in the Middle East. These words are not merely descriptive for Kelley; they are structural load-bearers for his entire thesis. If we are truly living in 1933 Germany, then all normal democratic procedures, academic norms, and commitments to objective truth must logically be suspended in favor of desperate resistance. But are we?

To describe the Trump administration and the MAGA movement as "fascist" is to engage in a profound historical flattening. Trumpism is certainly a dangerous, xenophobic, and illiberal populist movement with strong authoritarian tendencies. But fascism, as understood by historians like Robert Paxton or Stanley Payne, requires specific elements that are notably absent here: the total subordination of the individual to a totalitarian state, the destruction of all independent civil society, the militarization of the ruling party, and an expansionist, imperialist drive for *Lebensraum*. MAGA, with its chaotic deregulation, its deep suspicion of state power (the "deep state"), and its isolationist foreign policy, is a very different phenomenon. Similarly, applying the term "genocide" to the horrific and devastating urban warfare in Gaza—without engaging with the complex reality of Hamas’s embedded military infrastructure, its stated goal of destroying Israel, and the events of October 7th (which Kelley mentions only in passing as the date when "repression escalated")—strips the word of its legal and historical specificity, reducing it to a synonym for "massive civilian casualties."

Why does this terminological inflation matter? Because Kelley uses this self-declared state of apocalypse to justify a radical departure from intellectual rigor. He argues that "whatever power truth-telling might have had against fascism has been radically diminished." He notes that the right wing makes up its own stories, ignores evidence, and relies on emotion. His conclusion, implicitly, is that the left must do the same—or at least, that the left must prioritize "building power" and "choosing sides" over the quaint, liberal obsession with facts.

This is a catastrophic concession. If the intellectual abandons the Chomskyan commitment to exposing lies through rigorous, empirical evidence, what replaces it? The answer is simple: the will to power. If truth is merely a construct used by different classes to advance their interests—a vulgarization of Gramsci that Kelley flirts with—then there is no way to adjudicate between the claims of the MAGA right and the insurgent left other than brute force. When Kelley scoffs at the "myth of the liberal university, of the transcendent intellectual, of the power of reason," he is adopting the exact epistemological cynicism of the far-right.

Stephen Bannon and Christopher Rufo, whom Kelley rightly identifies as the organic intellectuals of the MAGA movement, would entirely agree with Kelley’s premise. They too believe that the university is just a site for ideological indoctrination, that objective truth is a liberal myth, and that the only thing that matters is raw power. When left-wing intellectuals adopt this identical framework, declaring that their responsibility is to "choose a side and fight" rather than to seek the truth, they validate the right’s most paranoid accusations. The right can easily point to such declarations and say to the public: "See? They admit they aren't teaching your children how to think; they are training them to be insurgents. We must defund them."

Furthermore, Kelley’s demand for militant solidarity destroys the capacity for necessary internal critique. If the primary duty of the intellectual is to stand in unquestioning solidarity with the "subaltern" or the "insurgent class," then intellectuals must turn a blind eye to the contradictions, failures, and moral compromises of their own movements. We saw this vividly in the aftermath of October 7th. A genuinely independent intellectual must be able to condemn the brutal occupation and the devastating bombardment of Gaza while simultaneously, and unequivocally, condemning the mass slaughter of Israeli civilians by Hamas. But in the partisan framework Kelley advocates, such nuance is often derided as liberal both-sides-ism or a betrayal of the oppressed. When solidarity eclipses truth, intellectuals become apologists, rationalizing the indefensible because it was committed by "their side."

Chomsky’s 1967 essay remains vital precisely because it rejected this tribalism. Chomsky did not ask intellectuals to become political commissars; he asked them to use their privilege, their access to information, and their training to pierce through the fog of state propaganda. He believed that facts matter. He believed that demonstrating the illegality of the Vietnam War, or the falsehoods of the War on Terror, could shift public consciousness. Kelley dismisses this because "there is ample scholarship proving that immigrants do not take jobs... that the 2020 election wasn’t stolen," and yet the right persists in its lies.

But the fact that truth does not immediately vanquish falsehood does not mean truth is impotent. It means the struggle is hard. The reason the election denialism of 2020 failed to overturn the republic was precisely because journalists, judges, and independent intellectuals insisted on the evidentiary truth. They did not respond to Trump’s lies by constructing a left-wing counter-mythology; they responded with affidavits, vote counts, and empirical reality.

Kelley envisions the university as a "contested terrain" where activists should seize power and "expand governance" to transform the world. This fundamentally misunderstands the purpose of the academy. The university is not a parliament, nor is it a vanguard party. It is a fragile, historically unique institution designed to foster the skeptical, rigorous, and often slow pursuit of knowledge. It requires a certain distance from the immediate political passions of the day to function. When professors and students transform the campus into a staging ground for global geopolitical struggles, demanding that the institution itself adopt foreign policy positions or boycott specific nations, they invite the destruction of their own sanctuary.

In a post-truth era, where algorithms reward outrage and political factions retreat into alternate realities, the most radical act an intellectual can perform is not to join the fray as a partisan combatant. The most radical act is to stubbornly, relentlessly defend the existence of objective reality. It is to insist that words have specific meanings, that historical analogies must be precise, and that evidence must dictate conclusions, even when those conclusions are politically inconvenient for one's own side.

Robin D. G. Kelley is right that we face grave threats, and he is right to praise the courage of those who put their bodies on the line. But courage without a commitment to truth is merely zealotry. If intellectuals abandon their responsibility to reason and evidence in the name of fighting fascism, they will find that they have not defeated the darkness, but simply joined it in a different uniform. The defense of civilization requires those who will stand apart from the mob—whichever side the mob is on—and insist on the truth.
