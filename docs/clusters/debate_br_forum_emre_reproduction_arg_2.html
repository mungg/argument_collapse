Diversified DeepSeek

"While the lead essay correctly identifies that all reproduction is assisted, it fails to recognize that technological access alone is an insufficient, market-driven solution that ignores the need for fundamental social and structural transformation."

The lead essay makes a welcome and overdue intervention: it insists that what we call “natural” reproduction is already thoroughly assisted, and it uses a series of beautifully reported personal stories to show how reproductive technologies reveal the hidden scaffolding of a profoundly unequal world. I share the essay’s impatience with the cult of the natural—that bougie fantasy of birthing in Eden while managing a $500 hypnosis tutorial—and its recognition that for many queer, trans, single, and otherwise non-normative people, assisted reproduction is not a lifestyle choice but the only path to parenthood. Yet the essay, for all its narrative richness, stops short of the political conclusion its own logic demands. It tells us that all reproduction is assisted, but it fails to tell us what kind of assistance we should be fighting for. The danger is that, in defaulting to a celebration of technological access, we settle for a Silicon Valley version of reproductive justice: egg‑freezing perks, expanded IVF coverage, and an ever‑widening menu of individual solutions to what are fundamentally collective problems.

The essay’s own stories inadvertently illustrate the limits of this approach. S, the biotech employee with twenty‑five eggs on ice, is held up as a beneficiary of corporate largesse. But her privilege is precisely the problem. Her company’s $20,000 lifetime cap for elective oocyte cryopreservation was not a victory achieved by a militant union of women demanding that care work be socialized; it was a competitive market move meant to attract highly skilled workers while leaving the underlying structures of American work and family life untouched. Notice what S does not receive: guaranteed paid parental leave of adequate length, truly affordable on‑site childcare, a reduced‑hours arrangement without career penalty, or, for that matter, a healthcare system that would make her egg‑freezing unnecessary by relieving the pressure to postpone childbearing to build a career first. The perk is a Band‑Aid on a gushing wound. When the essay tells us that S’s frozen eggs are “a happy product of Silicon Valley’s marriage of capitalist competition and social justice,” we should recoil. This is not a marriage; it is a one‑night stand in which social justice is always the party that gets left in the morning.

B’s story sharpens the point. A single woman financing IVF through small loans from friends, she is paradigmatic of the precarity that the essay’s techno‑optimism papers over. She has to time her embryo transfer around a new employer’s twelve‑month waiting period for paid maternity leave—a policy that is absurd on its face, but that in the United States counts as a “generous” benefit compared to having no leave at all. Telling B that she should just embrace her reproductive technology as liberation is a cruel joke when the state makes it nearly impossible for her to raise a child without falling into poverty. The same liberal feminism that once gave us “lean in” is now giving us “freeze your eggs and stop complaining.” Neither addresses the fact that the workplace was never designed for a person who performs care work, and no number of transvaginal ultrasounds will redesign it.

These criticisms are not a dismissal of reproductive technology itself. I am not suggesting we return to some pre‑lapsarian naturalism, nor that technology is inherently oppressive. But technology is not a neutral tool that simply expands human freedom. It inherits and often intensifies the inequalities of the society that produces it. ART in the United States remains a luxury good available to those with top‑tier insurance, generous employers, or access to family wealth. For every S, there are legions of women—particularly women of color, poor women, and transgender women—for whom IVF and egg freezing are as remote as a moon landing. The essay nods to this when it invokes Dorothy Roberts, but it does not let Roberts’s reproductive justice framework restructure the analysis. A true reproductive justice politics would start not with the question “How can more women get access to technology?” but with the question “What material conditions would allow all women to form and sustain families with dignity?” That means fighting for universal healthcare, a living wage, affordable housing, paid family leave, and an end to the carceral and immigration regimes that tear families apart.

The essay’s most haunting line comes from micha cárdenas: “I could be dead anytime. There is a real sense of urgency.” That urgency is the product not of a biological clock but of social murder—the transphobic violence that makes survival a daily uncertainty. The appropriate response to that urgency cannot be simply to help trans women bank sperm before they die. It must be to organize against the conditions that make them fear for their lives. In a just world, the desire for a child would not have to wrestle so intimately with the fear of death. Until we address that wider injustice, all the reproductive technology in the world will remain, for too many, an expensive consolation prize handed out while the game is still rigged.

So yes, all reproduction is assisted. The question is: assisted by what, and for whom? If the assistance comes only in the form of expensive medical interventions while the state assists the rich with tax cuts and the corporations with deregulation; if it comes without any guarantee that the child you work so hard to conceive will have a safe school, clean water, or a fair chance at life beyond the delivery room—then we have not liberated anybody. We have just updated an old fairy tale: the one where, if you want something badly enough and work hard, you can have it all, as long as you don’t ask who is cleaning the lab and rocking the babies in the nursery while you’re at your standing desk. The necessary reply to that fairy tale is not a manifesto for more technology, but a demand for a society that cares for everyone, regardless of how they came into the world.

Diversified DeepSeek

"While reproductive technologies offer potential for assistance, they cannot truly liberate women as long as they remain embedded in a capitalist framework that commodifies the body and prioritizes workplace optimization over genuine care."

When Shulamith Firestone proclaimed that artificial reproduction would liberate women, she was, the lead essay implies, unfairly maligned. Yet there was a reason so many radical feminists of her era—women like Mary Daly and many socialist feminists—recoiled from her technophilia. Their caution was not simply a kneejerk return to nature worship; it was rooted in a dark intuition about the patriarchal and capitalist armature on which reproductive technology would inevitably be hung. “All Reproduction Is Assisted” is a brilliant piece of narrative journalism that, perhaps despite itself, provides all the evidence needed for a more skeptical conclusion: as long as reproduction is embedded in capitalist social relations, technological enhancement will not liberate us but will extend the reach of commodification into the deepest recesses of human life.

Consider S, the biotech employee whose employer covers elective egg freezing. The essay describes this as a “happy product” of competition and social justice. But the happiness is precarious. S’s frozen eggs are an insurance policy, a hedge against professional risk. The company’s message is clear: we will help you postpone fertility so that you can dedicate your most productive years to our quarterly earnings reports. This is not reproductive justice; it is workplace optimization dressed in the language of choice. The same corporate logic that once offered free pizza to keep coders coding all night now offers egg freezing to keep women’s career trajectories uninterrupted. The technology does not loosen the demands of the workplace; it simply shifts the burden onto a different set of bodily practices—daily injections, transvaginal ultrasounds, and the emotional labor of managing the possibility of failure. S’s sigh that the eggs “should be liberating” is the lament of a worker who has been sold a product that doesn’t deliver on its packaging.

The essay’s other stories deepen this critique. B finances her IVF through small loans from friends, a form of informal debt peonage that mirrors the larger student‑loan and medical‑debt crises plaguing younger generations. K and N undergo treatment while N’s brother and sister‑in‑law, presumably heterosexual and more conventionally insured, enjoy full coverage. The state, through insurance mandates that require a diagnosis of infertility defined by intercourse, structures the flow of resources along heteronormative lines, leaving queer people to pay out of pocket or to beg from family. The market for sperm banks, genetic screening, and storage generates profits for a growing biomedical industry, while the emotional costs are borne by individuals. In every account, the reproductive technology is embedded in circuits of money, debt, and differential access. The technology doesn’t float free of capitalism; it is capitalism made flesh.

What the essay celebrates as “assistance” can, from another angle, look like the progressive deskilling and alienation of reproductive work. Firestone’s liberatory vision drew on Marx’s analysis of the division of labor: just as industrial machinery de‑skilled artisanal production, artificial wombs would de‑skill gestation. But Marx also taught us that under capitalism, the machine is not owned by the worker; it is used by the capitalist to extract surplus value and discipline labor. An artificial womb owned by a corporation or a state would not necessarily be a tool of women’s liberation; it could become a site of new forms of exploitation—embryo factories, gestational piecework, a market for commodified wombs that mimics the market for everything else under neoliberalism. The drive to externalize gestation is, in other words, entirely compatible with the further marketization of women’s bodies.

This is not to say that we must reject reproductive technology tout court. A socialist‑feminist politics would embrace technologies of assistance only to the extent that they are yoked to a broader transformation of social reproduction. That means decommodifying care entirely: making child‑rearing a social responsibility, funded by progressive taxation, with robust public services and a guaranteed basic income that makes paid work optional rather than compulsory. In such a context, egg freezing might be a genuine expansion of freedom rather than a career‑management tool. But as long as we live in a world where your access to food, shelter, and healthcare depends on your employment status, reproductive technology will be captured by the employer, the insurer, and the clinic’s bottom line.

The lead essay’s own ending points toward this larger vision when it invokes Dorothy Roberts’s reproductive justice framework: “not just a woman’s right not to have a child, but her right to have children and raise them with dignity in safe, healthy, and supportive environments.” That’s a fine sentence, but it requires a material foundation that the essay’s narratives never quite confront. Raising children with dignity demands more than a successful embryo transfer. It requires a living wage, affordable housing, a non‑discriminatory education system, and a planet that isn’t on fire. Reproductive technology, no matter how sophisticated, cannot deliver any of these things. By focusing so intently on the moment of conception and gestation, the essay risks replicating the very narrowness it seeks to correct: it expands the frame from natural to assisted, but it still keeps the frame trained on the production of the baby rather than on the lifelong labor of care.

Perhaps the most honest moment in the essay is when the author admits, “It is not a question that has a rational answer. Like sexual desire, reproductive desire seems fundamentally irrational.” There is profound political humility in that admission. It suggests that the longing for a child cannot be fully analyzed in terms of interests or rights; it belongs to the realm of the non‑rational, the passionate, the existential. Capitalism has a way of colonizing even that realm, turning it into a market for aspirational consumption. A feminism that wants to be more than a cheerleader for biotechnology must insist that some goods should never be produced as commodities. Caring for the next generation is one of them. The appropriate response to the infertility that the lead essay so vividly documents is not to make ART cheaper or more universally available within the existing market framework. It is to build a society that values care work so highly that the question “Why would you put yourself through this?” is met not with individual anguish but with an answer that is social, collective, and finally unalienated: because the community we live in will embrace whatever child arrives, however she arrives, and will sustain both of you for the whole of your lives.

"The lead essay’s focus on expanding access to reproductive technology fails to address the underlying capitalist structures that privatize social reproduction and make dignified family-making impossible for all but the wealthy."

One of the most provocative lines in “All Reproduction Is Assisted” is buried in a parenthesis: “the right to have a child is not a right that is equally distributed.” That single clause encapsulates the political tragedy of the assisted reproductive technology (ART) landscape, a tragedy the essay powerfully documents but ultimately cannot resolve on its own terms. For all its narrative nuance and its correct insistence that the natural is a mystification, the essay’s analytic framework is torn between two competing impulses: a liberal celebration of expanding access to technology, and a radical inkling that access alone will never be enough. I want to sharpen the radical inkling. The problem with reproduction in the United States in 2018 is not just that the technologies of assistance are unevenly distributed; it is that the entire domain of social reproduction has been privatized, commodified, and subjected to a regime of market discipline that makes dignified family‑making nearly impossible for all but the wealthy. A politics that doesn’t name that regime by its right name—capitalism—will keep offering egg freezing as a “perk” while the world burns.

The essay’s subjects are a biotech worker, a writer‑lecturer, two graduate‑student‑adjacent queer women, and a trans artist‑academic. These are not the oligarchy, but they are, let’s be honest, members of the professional‑managerial class. Their struggles are real, but they are struggles of people who have some access to cultural and financial capital, who can navigate medical bureaucracies, who have friends with spare cash, who can write Facebook posts that will find a sympathetic audience. The essay tells us almost nothing about the 23‑year‑old Black woman on Medicaid in Mississippi whose fertility is systematically threatened by environmental racism and whose desire for a child is met not with egg‑freezing perks but with the constant suspicion of the state. It doesn’t tell us about the undocumented mother who is terrified to seek prenatal care, or the incarcerated woman who is shackled during labor. Until these stories occupy the center of our reproductive politics, we are not doing reproductive justice; we are doing lifestyle feminism for the college‑educated.

Dorothy Roberts’s work, which the essay invokes, makes this point with devastating clarity. Roberts argues that the mainstream reproductive rights movement, by focusing narrowly on abortion, has served the interests of white, middle‑class women while ignoring the ways that Black women have historically been subjected to coerced sterilization, welfare caps that penalize childbearing, and the removal of children by a predatory foster system. The right to not have children has been framed as liberty; the right to have children and raise them with dignity has been treated as a private, individual responsibility. The lead essay quotes Roberts approvingly, but its own storytelling falls back into the same trap. The story of B, for example, frames her desire for paid maternity leave as a personal obstacle to IVF timing, not as a systemic injustice that the state has deliberately refused to remedy. She “just has to move forward,” adjusting her individual timeline. The structural critique never quite breaks the surface of the narrative.

One could imagine a very different essay built from the same materials. Instead of threading individual stories into the larger argument that all reproduction is assisted, it could have threaded them into an argument that all reproduction is exploited. And indeed, the essay’s historical genealogy gives us all the tools we need for that argument. The artificial womb in the steamer trunk was not developed to liberate women but to save the premature daughter of a millionaire. The Swedish doctors with their “goldfish bowl” brought back babies legally aborted, a prospect that rightly horrified. Research into artificial wombs sputtered when its dystopian potential became clear. In every case, the technology was shaped by the priorities of wealthy men, national prestige, and medical paternalism. Why would contemporary egg freezing and IVF be any different?

The answer is that they are not. The ART industry today is a multi‑billion‑dollar enterprise, tied to finance capital, dependent on a precarious workforce of lab technicians and, increasingly, surrogates in low‑income countries. The same logic of “optimization” that leads B to screen her embryos for chromosomal abnormalities is also the logic that leads corporations to extract maximum productivity from workers like S before allowing them a brief, often unpaid, leave to actually raise the child they spent tens of thousands of dollars to conceive. The family is being reshaped not according to the dreams of Firestone or Haraway, but according to the imperatives of a system that needs workers to be flexible, mobile, and permanently anxious about their biological clocks.

What would a genuinely emancipatory reproductive politics look like in this context? It would start not with expanding insurance coverage for ART, but with a collective refusal of the terms on which reproduction currently takes place. It would demand that care work—gestation, nursing, the daily grind of raising a child—be recognized as socially necessary labor and be compensated with a universal basic income and publicly funded, high‑quality childcare and eldercare. It would take the profit out of fertility medicine, treating reproductive assistance as a public good like education or firefighting. It would protect surrogates and egg donors with strong labor law and international agreements, not leave them to the mercies of the global market. And it would insist that a livable planet is a precondition for any reproductive choice, because the most important assistance any child needs is a biosphere that can sustain her.

These demands are not anti‑technology; they are anti‑capitalist. Firestone’s great insight was that the biological family is a unit of oppression, but her great error was to think that technology alone could demolish it. As the essay’s own stories remind us again and again, technology without a transformed social order only produces new forms of dependence and new layers of guilt. Until every person who wants a child can have one without bankrupting themselves, without risking their job, and without being made to feel like a failure for needing help, we have not yet begun to take Firestone’s manifesto seriously. The lead essay asks us to imagine a world in which reproduction is so thoroughly assisted that the natural “no longer looms so large.” I would rather imagine a world in which the market no longer looms so large, and in which the care of the next generation is a public joy rather than a private anxiety.
