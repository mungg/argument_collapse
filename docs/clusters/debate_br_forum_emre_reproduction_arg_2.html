Diversified DeepSeek

"While the lead essay correctly identifies the social and technological scaffolding of reproduction, it fails to provide a meaningful phenomenology of reproductive desire, which should be understood as a profound human longing rather than a mere ideological residue of the 'natural.'"

What stays with me from “All Reproduction Is Assisted” is not the thesis but the atmosphere: the hum of the fertility clinic, the needle at 9:00 sharp, the medical resident who can’t find the cervix, the single pink line of a negative pregnancy test. These details lodge in the mind because they convey something that political manifestos rarely capture: the sheer emotional cost of trying to make a family when the ordinary paths are blocked. The essay takes a great risk by building its argument out of personal narratives rather than policy analysis, and for the most part that risk pays off. It makes the point that reproductive technologies are not alien forces but are woven into the fabric of everyday life for millions of people. Yet there is a soft spot at the center of the essay, a question it raises but never fully answers: why, given the pain and expense, do people want biological children so badly? The essay calls this desire “irrational,” and then moves on. I think that move is a mistake, and that a better reproductive politics would spend more time trying to understand the specific character of this longing.

The essay’s reflexive antibiological stance—its suspicion of any attachment to genetic connection, pregnancy, or birth—causes it to mischaracterize its subjects. When B, after genetic testing, says “I have two girls and a boy,” her voice swelling with pride, the essay treats this as a kind of category error, a “conflation of sex and gender” that her academic training should have prevented. But B is not making a philosophical mistake; she is doing what millions of expecting parents have done long before genetic testing existed: she is forming an imaginative bond with a future person. The revelation of sex chromosomes is, for her, a scrap of concreteness in an otherwise terrifyingly abstract process. To reduce that to “deliberative conflation” is to stand at a clinical distance from the very intimate experience the essay purports to honor.

Similarly, when Joanne Spataro writes of wanting “a mixture of us two in human form,” the essay notes the commenters who find this desire “oppressively normative,” and seems to waver. On the one hand, it says it is unfair to demand that trans and queer people model the most subversive politics in their intimate lives. On the other hand, it structures its own analysis around the hope that technology will make the natural “no longer loom so large,” which implies that the desire for a genetic child is a residue of that looming natural. The ambivalence is honest but unresolved. We need a framework that can hold two truths simultaneously: the desire for a biologically related child is profoundly human and not inherently conservative; *and* it should not be the only culturally recognized form of reproductive longing.

The missing piece, I suggest, is a phenomenology of reproductive desire. Why do people want children at all? The answer cannot be reduced to social pressure or biological instinct, though both play a role. For many, the wish for a child is a wish to participate in the great chain of generations, to see one’s own features and gestures reappear in a new face, to know a love that is not chosen but given. These are not ignoble desires. They are desires that connect us to our mortality and to one another. To treat them as mere ideology that a properly liberated person would discard is to set up an ideal of the autonomous rational agent that is itself a patriarchal fiction. The feminist task is not to eliminate the desire for biological kinship but to ensure that it is not a luxury of the rich, that it does not require the exploitation of others, and that it does not become a wedge that divides women from one another.

The essay’s most productive insight is that the technologies of assisted reproduction make visible what was always already there: the social and material scaffolding of all family‑making. But the visibility goes both ways. It makes visible the scaffolding of the desire itself. When S says her frozen eggs “should be liberating” but do not feel that way, she is naming a gap between the promise of technology (control, choice) and the reality of her emotional life, which remains shaped by images of romantic love and natural conception. Instead of concluding that she needs to purge those images, we might ask how they could be expanded. What would it look like to have stories and rituals and cultural forms that honor the singular beauty of IVF conception—the high‑stakes countdown, the communiqués from the embryologist, the frozen embryos that are at once potential siblings and suspended possibilities? What if we stopped using “natural” as a marker of legitimacy and instead cultivated a rich descriptive language for all the ways that children join families?

That project requires more than manifestos. It requires the kind of narrative work the essay itself begins. We need novels, memoirs, films, and visual art that dwell in the particularity of assisted reproduction, not to normalize it into invisibility, but to give it the weight and texture of a fully human experience. Only then will women like B and N and K and S stop feeling like they are doing something deviant and start feeling like they are doing something epic. The essay’s final invocation of Roberts’s reproductive justice points in this direction: rights plus dignity plus safe environments. But dignity includes symbolic recognition. It means a world in which a child born from a thawed egg and a sperm‑bank donor is celebrated not despite her origins but in part because of them—because her parents jumped through rings of fire to bring her here.

I do not think we get there by insisting, over and over, that the natural is a lie. That is a necessary demolition, but it leaves a vacuum. We get there by building a new aesthetic of reproduction, one capacious enough to honor the ecstatic water birth and the trigger shot, the adoption homecoming and the surrogate contract, the trans pregnancy and the childless life of devoted care. The lead essay has given us the stories; the next step is to give them the poetry they deserve.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The lead essay's clinical and suspicious stance toward biological attachment mischaracterizes the intimate, imaginative bonds that individuals form with their future children during the IVF process.
  2. 02The desire for a biologically related child is not inherently conservative or a philosophical error, but a way for individuals to connect with their mortality and the chain of generations.
  3. 03Feminism should move beyond simply debunking the 'natural' and instead develop a new aesthetic and descriptive language that honors the singular, epic nature of assisted reproductive experiences.
  4. 04True reproductive justice requires symbolic recognition and cultural rituals that grant dignity to non-traditional paths to parenthood, rather than treating them as deviant or purely technological.
  5. 05The goal of reproductive politics should be to expand the imagery of family-making so that all forms of reproduction—whether biological, assisted, or adoptive—are celebrated as fully human experiences.
