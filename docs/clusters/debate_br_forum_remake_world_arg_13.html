"While Walter Johnson correctly identifies the centrality of slavery to modern capitalism, his framework of 'racial capitalism' risks oversimplifying history by collapsing essential distinctions between different labor regimes and moral concepts."

Walter Johnson has written a powerful essay, but its power depends in part on a set of overstatements that historians should resist. His moral instincts are often sound: slavery was central to the modern world; enslaved people must never disappear behind balance sheets; race and capitalism have been deeply intertwined; reparative justice deserves serious consideration. Yet Johnson’s larger formulation—especially the claim that “there was no such thing as capitalism without slavery”—risks replacing one simplification with another.

The history of slavery and capitalism has been distorted for a long time by those who imagined slavery as archaic, inefficient, premodern, or external to the development of industrial capitalism. That view cannot survive serious scrutiny. The antebellum South was not a feudal backwater. It was commercially sophisticated, export-oriented, financially entangled, technologically adaptive, and violently productive. Cotton was indispensable to British textile manufacturing. Enslaved people were used as collateral. Southern planters bought on credit, sold into world markets, speculated in land and slaves, and participated in modern finance. Northern merchants, insurers, shipbuilders, bankers, and manufacturers profited from slavery. Any history of American capitalism that treats slavery as marginal is indefensible.

But to say slavery was central to capitalism’s history is not the same as saying capitalism and slavery are analytically inseparable, or that capitalism could not have developed without slavery. Johnson dismisses counterfactual questions as “science fiction,” but all causal reasoning contains counterfactual assumptions. To say slavery caused or accelerated industrialization is to imply that, absent slavery, industrialization would have been different. One can debate how different, and one must be disciplined in doing so. But historians cannot avoid counterfactuals simply by denouncing them. If we say that British cotton mills depended on slave-grown cotton, we are also asking what would have happened had cotton been grown by free labor, or had wool, linen, or other fibers remained more important, or had industrialization proceeded at a slower pace. These questions are not attempts to absolve Europe. They are attempts to specify causation.

Johnson’s rhetorical move is understandable. He wants to prevent the old story in which Europe gets capitalism, Africa gets slavery, and the plantation appears only as raw material for the real history of modernity. Yet his corrective sometimes implies that the only alternative to Eurocentric abstraction is to make slavery the master key to capitalism itself. That is not necessary. We can put Mississippi, Manhattan, and Manchester in the same system while still asking how their social relations differed. We can recognize that slave labor and wage labor were connected without concluding that the distinction between them was secondary.

The distinction mattered. Enslaved people knew it mattered. Abolitionists knew it mattered. Workers knew it mattered. Slaveholders knew it mattered. The law of property in persons was not merely an extreme form of labor exploitation; it was a distinctive regime of power. The enslaved could be bought and sold apart from family members. Their children inherited their condition. Their labor, movement, sexuality, and reproduction were governed by owners. They could not quit, bargain, sue, testify freely, vote, or claim wages. Wage workers, however exploited, generally possessed a legal self-ownership that allowed forms of mobility, organization, family integrity, and political action unavailable to slaves. That legal self-ownership could be compromised by poverty, debt, gender subordination, racial exclusion, and employer power. But compromised freedom is not slavery.

The danger of a capacious “racial capitalism” framework is that it can flatten these differences. Johnson insists that it need not do so, and in principle he is right. A good history of racial capitalism would examine functional differentiation: how plantations, factories, colonies, farms, counting houses, and households interacted through different labor regimes. But in practice the term often becomes too elastic. If racial capitalism includes slavery, wage labor, colonialism, nativism, personal service, pornography, prison labor, environmental extraction, and white working-class resentment, then we need to know what it excludes. A concept that explains too much may explain too little.

One way to preserve its usefulness is to treat racial capitalism not as a synonym for capitalism but as a historically recurring pattern in which capitalist accumulation is organized through racial hierarchy. That formulation allows us to ask empirical questions. When and where has race been central to accumulation? How has race interacted with class, gender, nationality, and religion? What labor regimes did racial classification enable? How were profits distributed? How did states enforce racialized property and labor systems? How did workers and enslaved people resist? This approach is more fruitful than declaring capitalism racial in its essence and then fitting all evidence into that frame.

Johnson’s discussion of “dehumanization” likewise contains both insight and exaggeration. He is right that slavery did not depend on a failure to recognize enslaved people’s humanity. Torture works because the victim can suffer. Threats to sell children work because parents love them. Rules against literacy work because owners fear intelligence. Slavery exploited human capacities. But it does not follow that “dehumanization” is a misleading or harmful term in all ordinary uses. Slaveholders developed ideologies that likened black people to animals, children, machines, and commodities. The law treated enslaved people as property even while punishing them as moral agents. To call this dehumanization is not to say the enslaved lost their humanity; it is to say that a society organized itself around denying the moral consequences of that humanity.

Johnson is also too quick to criticize historians who emphasize enslaved people’s agency. There is a real danger in writing as if black humanity must be proven through resistance, creativity, or will. But agency scholarship emerged for important reasons. Earlier histories often portrayed enslaved people as passive victims or faceless labor units. Recovering their choices, communities, cultures, and politics was not a liberal vanity; it was an evidentiary and moral correction. The problem is not the concept of agency itself but its careless use. Enslaved people’s humanity did not depend on agency, but their agency remains historically significant.

Johnson’s essay is most compelling when he insists that histories of slavery must include enslaved people’s own worlds. Ironically, some of the recent scholarship he criticizes for focusing on capitalism and slavery is animated by the very desire to show how slavery shaped modern institutions. But Johnson’s warning is necessary: if the enslaved appear only as units of labor productivity, collateral value, or demographic reproduction, then a new history of capitalism can reproduce an old violence at the level of method. Economic history must not become a second auction block.

Yet economic history also must not be subordinated to moral testimony alone. We need ledgers, shipping records, insurance contracts, bank archives, plantation accounts, court records, and commodity flows precisely because slavery was a material system. To understand how slaveholders ruled, we must understand how credit worked. To understand expansion into the Southwest, we must understand land speculation and federal policy. To understand the Civil War, we must understand cotton markets and political economy. The challenge is not to choose between the enslaved person’s experience and the merchant’s ledger. It is to read them together.

On justice, Johnson’s argument for reparative thinking is important but underdeveloped. If present distributions of wealth and vulnerability are tied to slavery, segregation, and dispossession, then merely proclaiming formal equality cannot suffice. Reparations deserve a place in public debate. But reparations require specificity. Who owes what to whom, through which institutions, for which harms, according to what measure, and toward what end? If the answer is “racial capitalism” in general, the politics becomes grand but vague. If the answer identifies concrete injuries—unpaid labor, stolen land, discriminatory housing policy, exclusion from public benefits, racial terror, school inequality—then remedies can be debated and implemented.

Here again, distinctions matter. The reparative claims arising from Native dispossession are not identical to those arising from African slavery, though they intersect. The claims arising from Jim Crow are not identical to those arising from the slave trade, though they are historically connected. The claims against federal, state, local, corporate, religious, and educational institutions differ. A politics of repair must be historically granular if it is to be more than a moral posture.

Johnson’s criticism of human rights is also only partly persuasive. Liberal rights are insufficient, but they have been essential. The enslaved wanted freedom from ownership, bodily security, family integrity, education, mobility, property, wages, and political power. These demands can be described in rights language without reducing the freedpeople to bourgeois abstractions. The problem was not that rights were too liberal, but that they were denied, narrowed, and abandoned. Reconstruction failed not because rights were meaningless, but because white supremacist violence and Northern retreat destroyed the possibility of enforcing them in conjunction with land and labor reform.

The same is true today. Voting rights, due process, equal protection, labor rights, reproductive rights, and protections against discrimination remain vital. They do not solve racial capitalism, if one accepts the term, but their erosion would make every emancipatory project weaker. In the first days of the Trump administration, with immigrants, Muslims, black communities, and dissenters facing renewed threat, it would be strange to treat rights primarily as a bourgeois distraction. People under attack need enforceable protections.

Johnson’s essay should be read as an intervention against complacency, not as a final theory. It reminds us that slavery belongs at the center of modern history. It reminds us that capitalism was built not only in factories but also in fields, ports, courts, ships, banks, and slave markets. It reminds us that race is not an afterthought to political economy. It reminds us that justice must be historical and material.

But we should resist the temptation to answer old reductionisms with new ones. Not everything in capitalism is reducible to slavery. Not every invocation of humanity is liberal mystification. Not every use of “dehumanization” diminishes the enslaved. Not every counterfactual is an evasion. Not every distinction between slave and wage labor is an apology for capitalism. Historical clarity requires connection and differentiation at once.

The world Johnson wants us to remake will not be remade by slogans, however morally charged. It will require exact knowledge: of who suffered, who profited, how institutions transmitted advantage, how racial ideas organized labor and property, and how people fought back. Racial capitalism can be a useful frame if it sharpens those inquiries. It becomes a problem when it settles them in advance.
