"While historians should heed Walter Johnson’s warning against using 'dehumanization' and 'agency' to prove black humanity, they must still study enslaved people's agency as a means of understanding them as historical actors whose decisions and interpretations shaped the development of racial capitalism."

Walter Johnson’s challenge to the language of “dehumanization” should be welcomed by historians of slavery, even by those of us who have long written about enslaved people’s agency, resistance, and culture. His warning is bracing: do not make black humanity a problem to be solved by scholarly demonstration. Do not write as if the enslaved must sing, rebel, love, strategize, or create in order to become human in our eyes. Their humanity is not a conclusion. It is the premise.

That warning is needed. The historiography of slavery has often moved through pendulum swings. One generation emphasized the brutal power of the plantation and, at times, portrayed enslaved people as crushed by it. Another generation recovered community, culture, religion, family, resistance, and autonomy within constraint. Later work explored paternalism, negotiation, violence, capitalism, gender, sexuality, memory, and the archive’s silences. Each turn corrected something, and each produced its own excesses. “Agency,” once a necessary intervention, sometimes became a password. If enslaved people altered a work pace, broke a tool, told a story, ran away, prayed, stole food, or maintained kinship, historians could present the act as proof that slavery had failed to dehumanize them. Johnson is right to ask: why was proof required?

Yet I would defend the study of agency, provided we understand it differently. Agency should not mean liberal self-sovereignty. It should not mean that enslaved people stood outside structures of domination choosing freely among options. It should not mean that every act of survival was resistance, or that the historian’s task is to find uplift in every corner of the archive. Agency means that enslaved people were historical actors whose decisions, interpretations, conflicts, and aspirations mattered, even when made under conditions of extreme coercion. To study agency is not to prove humanity; it is to understand history.

Johnson worries that “independent will and volition” smuggles in the bourgeois subject. That worry is justified when historians reduce enslaved life to the universal drama of the individual seeking freedom as modern liberalism defines it. But enslaved people’s actions were often embedded in collective, spiritual, familial, and ecological worlds that do not fit liberal categories. A mother hiding a child, a driver mediating between field hands and an overseer, a conjurer healing or harming, a preacher interpreting Exodus, a woman choosing a partner despite legal nonrecognition, a man slowing his work rhythm, a group planning flight, an elder remembering Africa—these are not simply instances of individual will. They are practices of meaning within social worlds.

The difficulty is that the archive of slavery was largely produced by those who owned, governed, bought, sold, insured, punished, and observed enslaved people. To write enslaved people into history, scholars have often had to read against the grain. The concept of agency helped make that possible. It directed attention away from the master’s intention and toward the enslaved person’s maneuver. It asked what black people were trying to do, not merely what was done to them. Without that question, we risk returning to histories in which slaveholders are the only actors and enslaved people appear as labor inputs, collateral, demographic units, or victims.

Johnson himself shares this concern when he criticizes histories of slavery written without enslaved people. The new history of capitalism has produced important insights into credit, finance, accounting, commodification, and global trade. But it can make enslaved people disappear into numbers. A slave becomes a price, an appreciation rate, a mortgage instrument, a productivity figure, a line in a ledger. Such work may expose the capitalist brutality of slavery, but it can also replicate the slaveholder’s abstraction if not joined to histories of experience. We need to know what it meant to be collateral. We need to know how it felt to be appraised, how people responded to sale, how families understood debt, how rumors of market panic traveled through quarters, how enslaved people interpreted the difference between a resident master and an absentee creditor.

Agency matters especially because slavery was not a static system imposed on inert subjects. Enslaved people’s actions shaped slavery’s development and destruction. Runaways forced legal and political crises. Everyday resistance affected plantation management. Revolts and rumors terrified slave societies. Black sailors, preachers, soldiers, domestics, artisans, and fugitives moved information. During the Civil War, enslaved people’s flight to Union lines helped make emancipation a military and political necessity. Reconstruction politics cannot be understood without freedpeople’s demands for land, schools, churches, family recognition, labor control, and political office. These were not mere expressions of humanity. They were causes of historical change.

At the same time, we should avoid romanticizing agency. The enslaved were not always unified. They disagreed, competed, betrayed, accommodated, and made painful compromises. Some gained limited privileges by supervising others. Some acts of survival harmed fellow slaves. Some people resisted openly; others could not. Some believed escape possible; others had children, age, disability, geography, surveillance, or fear binding them to place. To demand resistance as the measure of dignity is cruel. A person who endured without visible rebellion was no less human, no less worthy of remembrance.

This is where Johnson’s critique of “preserving humanity” is most valuable. Enslaved people did not preserve humanity as if it were a candle flickering in a storm. They preserved relationships, memories, skills, beliefs, bodies, languages, songs, names, and hopes. Their humanity was not the fragile object preserved; it was the condition of preservation. The historian should not write, “This act shows that the slave remained human.” The historian should write, “Because this person was human, this act had meaning.”

Johnson’s account of racial capitalism also changes how we should study agency. If slavery was central to modern capitalism, then enslaved people’s actions belong to the history of capitalism, not only the history of race or the South. When field hands slowed cotton picking, they intervened in commodity production. When women resisted reproductive exploitation, they challenged capital accumulation. When families tried to prevent sale, they confronted credit systems secured by human property. When fugitives crossed borders, they exposed the dependence of property rights on state power. When freedpeople demanded land, they challenged the distribution of capital itself.

But racial capitalism should not absorb the whole texture of enslaved life. Enslaved people did not experience themselves only as workers in a global economy. They were parents, children, lovers, rivals, Christians, Muslims, herbalists, musicians, storytellers, mourners, and dreamers. Their worlds included markets but were not exhausted by markets. Some cultivated provision grounds and sold produce. Some hired out their time. Some learned trades. Some worshipped in secret. Some negotiated holidays, marriages, and burial practices. Some interpreted illness and death through cosmologies not reducible to political economy. A history that begins and ends with capitalism may miss what enslaved people thought life was for.

Johnson’s suspicion of human rights also requires balance. It is true that abstract rights can distort enslaved experience by measuring it against a liberal ideal. But enslaved and freed people made rights claims in their own idioms. They demanded the right to marry legally because slavery had denied family integrity. They demanded schools because literacy had been criminalized. They demanded wages because labor had been stolen. They demanded arms because white violence threatened freedom. They demanded land because without it emancipation could become dependency. Rights talk was not imposed from above; it was appropriated from below.

Perhaps the problem is not rights but thin rights. The right to contract meant little if planters controlled land and credit. The right to vote meant little if night riders could murder with impunity. The right to marry meant little if poverty and labor coercion separated families. Enslaved people’s own political thought often joined rights to material conditions. They did not ask merely to be recognized as abstract citizens. They asked for the means to live as free people.

A historiography adequate to Johnson’s challenge would have several commitments. First, it would never pose black humanity as a question. Second, it would study violence without making suffering the essence of enslaved life. Third, it would study agency without making heroism a requirement. Fourth, it would connect plantations to capitalism without reducing people to commodities. Fifth, it would take gender, sexuality, reproduction, and childhood as central. Sixth, it would attend to the archive’s silences and the ethical limits of historical reconstruction. Seventh, it would understand enslaved people as thinkers, not only actors.

This last point is crucial. Johnson invokes Du Bois’s phrase “the philosophy of life and action which slavery bred in the souls of black folk.” That phrase should guide us. Enslaved people interpreted their condition. They debated divine justice, freedom, Africa, America, family, death, vengeance, patience, and escape. Their thought appeared in sermons, songs, stories, naming practices, healing rituals, work routines, and political claims after emancipation. Not all philosophy is written in treatises. The slave quarters, brush arbor, praise house, field, swamp, kitchen, and Union camp were sites of theory.

The ethical stakes are high in 2017. Public arguments about slavery remain trapped between denial, sentimentality, and appropriation. Some want to minimize slavery’s centrality. Others want to honor enslaved people by celebrating resilience in ways that make brutality easier to consume. Still others treat slavery as an origin story for present injustice but skip over the actual lives of the enslaved. Historians must resist all three. The enslaved were not props in a morality play. They were people living under a system designed to exploit every dimension of their humanity.

Johnson has done a service by making us uneasy with our habits. We should be uneasy when we say “dehumanization.” We should be uneasy when we say “agency.” We should be uneasy when we say “capitalism.” Uneasiness is not paralysis. It is the beginning of more careful history. The goal is not to find the perfect word that frees us from implication. The goal is to write in ways that neither repeat the master’s categories nor comfort the reader with easy redemption.

Enslaved people do not need historians to restore their humanity. They need historians to tell the truth about the worlds that violated it, the worlds they made within and against that violation, and the unfinished claims those worlds place upon ours.
