"While Lawrence Lessig correctly identifies that the current campaign finance system creates a corrupting dependency on money, a truly conservative solution must address the problem by both reforming funding mechanisms and limiting the expansive government power that creates the market for political influence."

Conservatives should not be too quick to celebrate Citizens United, and liberals should not be too quick to imagine that the problem begins with corporations. Lawrence Lessig’s essay is valuable because it identifies a corruption that ordinary Americans across the political spectrum already sense: Washington is dependent on money in ways that make it less dependent on the people. But his account would be stronger if it were less selectively alarmed about corporate spending and more attentive to the many ways government itself creates the market for influence.

The first conservative point to make is that the Supreme Court was dealing with a real First Amendment problem. A statute that prevents a nonprofit corporation from distributing a film critical of Hillary Clinton near an election is not a trivial regulation. It is a restriction on political advocacy at the moment citizens are most likely to care. Conservatives are right to distrust government power over such speech. Incumbents write campaign-finance laws, and incumbents have a natural desire to protect themselves from attack. The line between reform and self-protection is not always bright.

Yet the second conservative point is that constitutional permission is not the same as civic health. The fact that corporations, unions, and wealthy individuals may have a right to spend independently does not mean the resulting system is admirable. A republic can be damaged by conduct that cannot wisely be prohibited. Lessig’s notion of institutional corruption captures something real: members of Congress spend too much time raising money, listen too carefully to organized interests, and often produce legislation that no coherent political philosophy can defend. When free-market Republicans defend sugar quotas and dairy supports, they are not reading Hayek. When Democrats carve out favored industries from regulations they claim are necessary to protect consumers, they are not channeling Jeffersonian democracy. They are responding to pressure.

But pressure comes from government power as much as from private wealth. If Congress did less, there would be less reason to purchase access to Congress. The modern federal government allocates subsidies, tax credits, loan guarantees, procurement contracts, regulatory exemptions, licenses, mandates, tariffs, and bailouts. Every one of these is an invitation to organize politically. The larger the discretionary state, the greater the return on lobbying and campaign spending. Public choice economists have long warned that concentrated benefits and dispersed costs produce rent-seeking. Lessig’s institutional corruption is, in part, the predictable result of a government with vast power to pick winners and losers.

Consider the financial crisis. It is tempting to describe it simply as a product of Wall Street influence. That influence was real. But the underlying political economy included federal housing policy, government-sponsored enterprises, ratings-agency regulation, monetary policy, bank-capital rules, and the expectation that large institutions would not be allowed to fail. When government policy can make or break industries, industries will invest in shaping government policy. Campaign finance is one expression of that investment. The root is the prize.

This does not mean we can abolish influence by abolishing government. A modern nation needs laws, courts, defense, infrastructure, environmental rules, financial rules, and a safety net. But conservatives are right to insist that complexity and discretion multiply opportunities for corruption. A 2,000-page bill filled with exceptions, waivers, phase-ins, pilot programs, and delegated authority will attract lobbyists the way sugar attracts ants. Simpler laws, lower and broader tax rates, fewer targeted subsidies, and less industrial policy would reduce the payoff from political spending. They would not cure dependency corruption, but they would shrink the market in which it thrives.

Lessig’s proposed remedies—small-dollar public financing and democracy vouchers—deserve serious consideration. Conservatives often recoil at public financing, seeing it as welfare for politicians. But a voluntary system that encourages candidates to raise small donations from constituents may be preferable to the current model, in which candidates spend endless hours courting large donors and PACs. If public financing can be designed without protecting incumbents, without subsidizing fringe candidacies automatically, and without penalizing private speech, it might strengthen republican accountability. A voucher system is intriguing because it decentralizes public financing: citizens, not bureaucrats, decide where the money goes.

Still, reformers should be modest. Public financing will not eliminate dependency. Candidates will depend on party activists, ideological groups, media attention, and organized blocs of voters. They may become more dependent on small-dollar passions, which can reward polarization and theatrical outrage. A member who relies on thousands of ideologically intense donors from around the country may be no more attentive to the median constituent than one who relies on business PACs. Lessig notes that John Kerry received much money from outside Massachusetts; under Internet fundraising, that pattern could become common for small donors too. The problem is not only big money; it is non-constituent money.

For that reason, one reform worth considering is a stronger connection between campaign money and the relevant electorate. If candidates seek to represent a district or state, perhaps public matching funds should be limited to donations from that district or state. Private citizens elsewhere would remain free to speak independently, but the publicly supported campaign treasury would reflect the people to be represented. This would align Lessig’s “dependence upon the People” with federalism and representation. It would also reduce the nationalization of every race, a trend that has weakened local accountability.

Disclosure should be robust and rapid. Conservatives who defend political spending should not fear voters knowing who is speaking. If a union, corporation, trade association, or wealthy individual wants to influence an election, it should do so under its own name, not through misleadingly titled committees. Disclosure is not censorship. It allows citizens to judge credibility and motives. At the same time, disclosure rules should be written with care to protect genuine harassment concerns for small donors and unpopular causes. The secret ballot exists for a reason; so does anonymous pamphleteering. But large institutional spending in candidate elections is different from a citizen giving twenty dollars to an unpopular group.

Congress also needs internal reform. Earmarks, leadership PACs, committee fundraising, and the revolving door all reinforce dependency. A member who knows that a committee assignment will attract industry contributions has incentives that distort judgment. Former members and staffers who become lobbyists monetize relationships built in public service. Longer cooling-off periods, clearer earmark rules, and more transparent committee processes could help. Term limits are often proposed, but they may worsen dependence by empowering lobbyists and staff who know the system better than short-term legislators. The better conservative reform is not amateurism; it is accountability and simplicity.

Citizens United itself should be understood as both a vindication of speech and a warning about politics. The Court was right that government may not silence political advocacy because the speaker is a corporation. But the decision will likely intensify public suspicion that Washington is for sale. The answer is not to amend the First Amendment into a permission slip for incumbents to regulate critics. The answer is to make government less rentable, campaigns less dependent on large funders, and political spending more transparent.

Lessig’s essay challenges conservatives to recover an older republican vocabulary. The framers feared dependency, patronage, and corruption not only because they were moralists, but because they understood human nature. Ambition must counteract ambition; institutions must be designed for ordinary people, not angels. That insight is compatible with free markets, limited government, and strong speech rights. Indeed, it may require them. A limited government reduces the rewards of rent-seeking; a strong First Amendment prevents incumbents from suppressing challengers; a better campaign-finance system can reconnect candidates to constituents.

The worst outcome would be partisan complacency: Republicans cheering corporate spending while denouncing union spending, Democrats doing the reverse, and both sides preserving a system that benefits incumbents and organized interests. Lessig is right that dependency corruption is not a left-right problem. The conservative contribution should be to add that dependency will persist so long as Washington has so many favors to sell. Reform democracy, yes. But also reform the state that money is trying to buy.

  2. 02Institutional corruption is driven as much by the size and complexity of the discretionary state as by private wealth, as government power to pick winners and losers incentivizes rent-seeking.
  3. 03Reducing the 'market for influence' requires simplifying laws, lowering tax rates, and eliminating targeted subsidies to make the government less 'rentable' to special interests.
  4. 04Voluntary small-dollar public financing and democracy vouchers are worth considering because they could strengthen republican accountability by decentralizing political funding.
  5. 05Campaign finance reform should prioritize reconnecting candidates to their specific geographic constituents by limiting public matching funds to donations from within the relevant district or state.
  6. 06Robust and rapid disclosure of large institutional spending is essential for civic transparency and does not constitute censorship.
  7. 07Internal congressional reforms, such as stricter rules on earmarks and the 'revolving door' for lobbyists, are necessary to reduce the monetization of public service.
