"Tennis should modernize its etiquette by replacing exclusionary, class-based formalities with inclusive rules that prioritize accessibility and mutual respect over tradition."

The etiquette tennis most needs to update is not about decibels. It is about whom the sport makes comfortable. Tennis still carries too much of the country-club assumption that the “right” fan or player already knows how to dress, when to sit, when to clap, what not to say, and how not to look out of place. That code may seem harmless to insiders, but to newcomers it can feel like a velvet rope.

Start with clothing. At the recreational level, clubs should stop treating all-white rules, collared-shirt requirements and fussy footwear policies as if they were the guardians of civilization. Of course shoes must protect courts, and professional tournaments can have brand identities. Wimbledon’s white clothing rule is part of its theater. But when dress codes become a way to signal class rather than facilitate play, they keep people out. The sport should be eager for children in public parks, immigrants, city kids, and adults who did not grow up with lessons. It should not ask them first to learn the costume.

Spectator etiquette needs the same rethinking. Tennis can keep quiet during points while being less severe around the edges. Explain the customs on tickets, scoreboards and apps rather than relying on ushers to scold. Create family sections where a restless child is not treated as an act of vandalism. Let fans enter at more frequent natural breaks on large courts. Encourage applause for good defense, not just obvious winners, so new fans learn the game’s subtleties.

Technology also requires a calmer code. Phones are now cameras, scoreboards and lifelines. Banning them outright is unrealistic, but flash, ringtones and filming that blocks another fan’s view should be policed. The rule should be courtesy, not nostalgia.

Finally, players and officials have obligations too. It is strange to demand monk-like discipline from spectators while tolerating endless towel requests, slow play, gamesmanship over medical timeouts, or abuse of ball kids and umpires. Etiquette should not run only downhill from the club to the cheap seats.

Tennis should remain a game of manners. But manners at their best are not barriers; they are tools that allow strangers to share a space. The sport should trade exclusionary formality for generous, clearly explained respect.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Recreational dress codes should be relaxed to ensure that clothing requirements do not act as a socioeconomic barrier to entry for new players.
  2. 02Spectator etiquette should be more accommodating and educational, moving away from scolding fans toward creating family-friendly environments and more frequent entry points.
  3. 03Technology policies should be governed by modern courtesy and the prevention of actual disruption rather than a nostalgic desire to ban devices entirely.
  4. 04Etiquette standards must be applied consistently to players and officials, ending the double standard where fans are strictly policed while players are permitted to engage in gamesmanship or poor behavior.
  5. 05The sport should transition from using manners as a tool for gatekeeping to using them as a means of making diverse newcomers feel welcome and comfortable.

"Tennis should update its etiquette by replacing exclusionary social gatekeeping and unwritten tribal rituals with clear, accessible norms that welcome a more diverse audience."

The question is not whether tennis should become rowdy. The question is whose behavior tennis has historically labeled “rowdy.” A polite cough from a corporate box is treated differently from an excited outburst by a teenager in the cheap seats. A seasoned club member who knows when to sit and when to clap belongs; a newcomer who cheers at the wrong moment is made to feel foolish. That is the etiquette tennis most needs to update.

The sport has benefited enormously from players who did not fit old country-club expectations. The Williams sisters, Rafael Nadal’s fist-pumping physicality, Novak Djokovic’s theatrical edge, and the global crowds at the U.S. Open have all widened the game’s emotional range. Yet the audience code still often feels designed for an earlier, narrower tennis culture: sit still, speak softly, know the rituals before you arrive, and never disturb the paying calm.

Some rules should stay. No one should shout during a serve, call balls from the stands, or abuse a player. But tournaments should stop treating every breach as a lapse in breeding. Put clear etiquette instructions on tickets, scoreboards and broadcasts: cheer after points, remain quiet during play, silence phones, no flash photography, move only at designated times unless in upper-deck areas where quiet movement is reasonable. Make the rules legible rather than tribal.

Tennis should also examine the etiquette embedded in dress codes, club access and event presentation. Wimbledon’s all-white tradition is charming to some and fussy to others, but at local clubs “proper attire” rules can become a way to signal who belongs. If the sport wants new fans and players, especially from public courts and urban programs, it should distinguish between respect for the game and respectability politics.

The best tennis atmosphere is not silent; it is attentive. It has gasps, laughter, partisan roars, nervous murmurs and then quiet when the ball is in play. Updating etiquette should mean replacing social gatekeeping with shared norms. The goal is not to make tennis less respectful. It is to make respect easier for everyone to join.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Current tennis etiquette often functions as a way to distinguish between established insiders and newcomers rather than simply maintaining order.
  2. 02The sport has been significantly enriched by players and fans who challenge traditional country-club expectations and bring a wider emotional range to the game.
  3. 03Tournaments should provide explicit, legible instructions on behavior to ensure that respect for the game is easy for everyone to follow regardless of their background.
  4. 04Tennis organizations must re-examine dress codes and club access rules to ensure they do not serve as tools for respectability politics that alienate potential fans and players.
  5. 05The ideal tennis atmosphere should prioritize genuine attentiveness and shared passion over the enforcement of a silent, narrow, and elitist culture.
