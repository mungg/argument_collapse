**Hirschmann Doesn't Go Far Enough**

I want to register a friendly amendment to Nancy Hirschmann's essay: it doesn't go far enough. She has finally said out loud what many of us who came up through second-wave feminism have been muttering for years—that the celebration of care, the romance of mothering, and the chic of "opting out" have served as ideological cover for women's wholesale retreat from public life. She is right. But she keeps softening her conclusion with hedges. Linda Hirshman, whom Hirschmann cites approvingly, was clearer: if you're a woman with talent, education, and resources, and you spend your life as the unpaid domestic appendage of a husband, you are betraying both yourself and the women who will come after you.

Let me put the hard version of the argument plainly. Educated women in the United States in 2010 have a society-wide responsibility. That society made enormous investments in them: public schools, scholarships, university subsidies, anti-discrimination law, the painfully won infrastructure of Title IX. To take all of that and pour it into a single household, polishing two children for the Ivy League, is not a private choice. It is a withdrawal of public resources from the public, justified by an ideology of motherhood that conveniently aligns with husband's preferences and patriarchal expectations. The choice may feel personal. Its consequences are not.

Hirschmann is too gentle on her students. When women in an elite feminist theory course tell their professor that they will be stay-at-home mothers because mothering is "the most important job anyone could do," they are not engaging in feminist analysis. They are reciting an ideology that women in every generation have been told by men who benefited from it. The fact that they have learned to call it "care" rather than "duty" or "calling" is a marketing change, not a substantive one. The job description hasn't moved. The husband still gets the career, the income, the pension, the social standing, the friendships from work, and the option to leave. The wife still gets the children, the laundry, the dependency, and the option to be left.

This is why I find Hirschmann's gestures toward sympathy with care theory unsatisfying. She wants to keep the moral architecture of care—the idea that connection, attention to particulars, responsiveness to need are valuable orientations—while ditching the prescriptive consequence that women are the ones who provide them. But the moral architecture and the gendered division of labor are not so easily pried apart. Care theory was, from its inception, deeply ambivalent on this point. Gilligan said women speak in a different voice. Ruddick said maternal practice generates a particular form of thinking. Noddings centered the mother-child relation as paradigm. These framings were intellectually fertile and politically catastrophic. They told a generation of women that what they were already doing was philosophically important. They did not tell men to do it. The structure of the discourse made men outsiders to care; it made men's continued absence from caregiving compatible with their respect for care.

That is why the practical results have been so dismal. Hirschmann cites the numbers: women still doing two to three times the housework. Family-friendly policies used by men to bank early career advantage. Maternity leaves used as opportunities to write off the careers of women who take them. Tax codes that punish the second earner. These are not failures of implementation. They are the structural endpoints of an ideology that says care is socially valuable but assigns it to women in practice.

The way out is not more public recognition of care. It is the opposite. Public policy should aggressively encourage the equal sharing of paid and unpaid work between adults in every household. Tax the second earner equally, not more, by treating each adult as a separate filer. Mandate paternity leave that is use-it-or-lose-it, on the Scandinavian model—non-transferable, well-paid, of substantial duration. Remove the spousal benefits in Social Security that subsidize the one-earner household. Make divorce settlements presumptively equal in the absence of agreement. Treat childcare as essential public infrastructure, like roads. And, yes, end the cultural mythology that the highest calling of an educated woman is the cultivation of her own offspring.

I expect to hear that this is harsh, that it disrespects women's choices, that it sounds like Linda Hirshman, who was vilified for saying women shouldn't have children if they planned to mother them full-time. So be it. Vilification is what happens to feminists who refuse to make peace with the new gloss on the old arrangement. Each generation has had its accommodationists: those who said women should stay home but read more books while doing it; those who said women should work but only part-time; those who said women should "balance" everything; those who say now that women should "lean in" sometimes and "opt out" other times. Each accommodation has kept the basic structure in place.

Where I would push past Hirschmann is on the question of children themselves. She still treats childbearing as a settled good and worries only about how the labor is divided. I would ask the harder question: is the social expectation that women will have children, and that two-child families are the natural form of life, itself part of the structure of oppression? Many educated women do not want children. Many more would not want them if they were not constantly told they should. The "should I work?" debate has crowded out the "should I bear?" debate. Maybe educated women's caring "less"—as Hirschmann puts it—should include caring less about whether to have children at all.

Hirschmann ends with the suggestion that care should become more "tough love" and less empathic giving. I agree. But the tough love should start with feminists themselves, and with the temptation to keep dressing up the old division of labor in the new vocabulary of choice. Either feminism means a serious challenge to the gendered organization of social life, or it doesn't. Hirschmann is right that we have spent too long pretending it can mean both.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Educated women who choose full-time motherhood are effectively withdrawing public resources—such as their subsidized education and legal protections—from the society that invested in them to benefit a single private household.
  2. 02The 'ethic of care' is not a substantive feminist analysis but a rebranded version of traditional patriarchal duty that continues to leave women economically dependent and socially marginalized.
  3. 03The moral architecture of care theory is fundamentally inseparable from the gendered division of labor because it historically framed caregiving as a uniquely female practice while leaving men as outsiders.
  4. 04Public policy should move beyond merely recognizing care and instead aggressively dismantle the one-earner household model through separate tax filing, non-transferable paternity leave, and the elimination of spousal Social Security benefits.
  5. 05Feminism must challenge the social expectation of childbearing itself, as the pressure to have children is a core component of the structural oppression that limits women's lives.
  6. 06True gender equality requires feminists to stop validating 'choice' when those choices reinforce traditional gender hierarchies and instead demand a radical reorganization of social life.
