"The existing commercial and open-source AI ecosystem already facilitates the flexible, creative, and non-instrumental 'eolithic' intelligence that critics desire, making market-driven decentralization a better path for democratization than state-led alternatives."

Morozov’s essay is a masterful performance, but it fundamentally misreads the generative AI of 2024. He treats large language models as the latest projection of instrumental reason, a technology born of Cold War bureaucracy and now harnessed to neoliberal market logic. Yet his own example of building a language app betrays a far more interesting reality: the very flexibility and open-endedness he yearns for are already emergent within the commercial AI ecosystem. The market, for all its flaws, has produced a tool so plastic that it enables the eolithic flânerie he treasures. Instead of dreaming of a statist alternative, we should focus on making that tool more widely accessible, more energy-efficient, and more accountable to a diverse public—through the very market mechanisms and open-source collaborations that are already reshaping the field.

Consider the moment of discovery Morozov describes. He is browsing an AI service, notices a feature, and wonders if he can link it with two others. That playful recombination is precisely the kind of intelligence his essay lionizes. And it happened not inside a publicly funded research lab but in the messy, decentralized world of commercial APIs and subscription models. The tools were privately owned, but their interoperability and ease of use made spontaneous experiment possible. This is the promise of platform markets: they create ecosystems where users can combine modular services in ways never anticipated by their creators. It is, in effect, Storm’s stonefield, populated with digital eoliths that users pick up and repurpose.

Morozov recoils at the fees and the privatization, but these are contingent features, not necessary ones. Open-source models are proliferating. Just a few months before his essay, Meta released Llama 2 with an open license, and Mistral, a French startup, debuted powerful open-weight models. An entire cottage industry of fine-tuned, community-maintained models has sprung up, running on consumer hardware or affordable cloud instances. The cost of inference is dropping rapidly, and techniques like quantization and distillation are making models more efficient. The stonefield is becoming a commons, driven not by a centralized Humanity Lobby but by the distributed ingenuity of hackers, activists, and small firms. This is market-driven decentralization, not state planning, and it has already done more to democratize AI than any government program.

Morozov’s preference for public-sector alternatives is understandable, but it ignores the historical record. Government-led technology projects have often been captured by the very Efficiency Lobby he decries, from the military origins of the internet to the bureaucratic ossification of public IT systems. The Latin American experiments he admires were crushed precisely because they depended on fragile political regimes. By contrast, open-source communities, standards bodies, and competitive markets have shown a remarkable capacity to evolve and adapt over the long term. The coordination problem he identifies—how to avoid redundant, energy-hungry queries—is already being addressed by shared caches, semantic deduplication, and API design patterns that incentivize reuse. These solutions emerge from the interplay of self-interest and technical ingenuity, not from a master plan.

To be clear, this is not a Panglossian defense of the status quo. Market concentration is a real problem; OpenAI and Google wield enormous power. But the remedy is not wholesale abandonment of market mechanisms. It is vigorous antitrust enforcement, interoperability mandates, and public investment in open infrastructure—measures that preserve the dynamism of competition while curbing monopolistic excess. The AI Act in Europe, imperfect as it is, represents a step in this direction: it requires transparency and risk management without stifling innovation. Similarly, the growing movement for “public option” AI—models developed with public funds and made available as a commons—offers a market-friendly path to counterbalance corporate giants.

Morozov’s deeper worry is that the market frames our activities as consumers and entrepreneurs, colonizing even our idlest curiosity with a profit-seeking logic. But this grants too much power to the frame and too little to the framers. People can and do use market-provided tools for non-market ends all the time. The teenager generating fan fiction with ChatGPT, the activist summarizing legal documents with Claude, the community group brainstorming accessible event flyers with image generators—none of these activities are reducible to “arbitrage opportunities.” They are instances of vernacular creativity, the very kind Morozov wants to foster. The market does not determine the meaning of the stones; it merely supplies them.

If we take Storm’s eolithism seriously, we should celebrate the emergence of a technology that is so radically open to reinterpretation. LLMs are the closest thing we have to a universal eolith, a stone that can become almost anything. The task is not to replace the market with a single public alternative—which would inevitably reintroduce the rigidities of design—but to ensure that the stonefield remains fertile, diverse, and accessible to all. That means funding open research, protecting fair use for training data, and subsidizing access for marginalized communities. The AI we deserve is already being built, in bits and pieces, by millions of tinkerers. Our job is not to smother it with utopian blueprints but to clear the path.
