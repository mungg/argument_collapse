Diversified MiniMax

"The United States should adopt a strategic industrial policy that leverages Breznitz’s four-stage innovation framework to rebuild lost domestic capabilities and manage geopolitical risks without pursuing a total technological decoupling from China."

Breznitz's analysis has significant implications for the intensifying competition between the United States and China, and I want to explore how the four-stage framework illuminates the current geopolitical dynamics.

American policymakers have expressed growing concern about dependence on Taiwanese semiconductor manufacturing, epitomized by TSMC's dominance in advanced chip fabrication. The Trump administration's request that TSMC build fabrication facilities in the United States, and the Biden administration's efforts to accelerate this process, reflect a belated recognition that stage three capabilities have been lost. But rebuilding these capabilities is not simply a matter of inviting TSMC to open a plant; it requires the ecosystem of suppliers, technicians, and engineers that Taiwan has spent decades building.

China presents a more complex case. Breznitz correctly notes that the Pearl River Delta has become the world's leading hub of stage four innovation, with Shenzhen companies capable of manufacturing sophisticated products at unprecedented speed and scale. Chinese policymakers are not satisfied with stage four, however; they have made clear their ambition to move up the value chain into stages two and three, and eventually into stage one. The Made in China 2025 initiative and subsequent policies explicitly target the development of capabilities in semiconductor design and fabrication, advanced manufacturing, and ultimately frontier technologies like artificial intelligence and quantum computing.

The United States has responded to these ambitions with export controls, investment restrictions, and efforts to accelerate domestic production. These measures may slow Chinese advancement, but they also risk fragmenting the global innovation system that has generated enormous benefits for all participants. The semiconductor supply chain, in particular, has become deeply integrated across borders, with American chip designers working with Taiwanese fabricators and Chinese packaging companies. Breaking these connections will raise costs, reduce innovation incentives, and potentially trigger retaliation that harms American exporters.

What would a coherent policy look like? It would recognize that complete technological decoupling is neither feasible nor desirable—that the goal should be resilience, not autarky, and that resilience requires maintaining capabilities at multiple stages rather than concentrating production in low-cost locations abroad. It would invest in the stage three capabilities that have been lost, using the kind of industrial policy that the United States pioneered in the mid-twentieth century but has since abandoned. And it would work with allies to maintain shared standards and integrated supply chains that benefit all participants while reducing vulnerability to any single actor.

Breznitz's framework suggests that the United States should excel at stages one and two, where its research universities and design capabilities remain strong, and should rebuild capabilities at stage three, where it has lost ground. Stage four manufacturing will likely remain in Asia, but diversification across multiple Asian suppliers can reduce dependence on any single country. This is not the autarky that some American nationalists demand, nor the complete surrender implied by continued offshoring. It is a realistic strategy for maintaining innovation leadership while managing geopolitical risk.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Reclaiming stage three capabilities, such as semiconductor fabrication, requires more than just building factories; it necessitates recreating the entire ecosystem of suppliers and skilled labor that the U.S. has neglected for decades.
  2. 02China’s aggressive move from stage four assembly into stage one novelty and stage three component innovation represents a direct challenge to American technological leadership.
  3. 03Aggressive export controls and decoupling efforts risk damaging the global innovation system and increasing costs for American exporters due to the deeply integrated nature of modern supply chains.
  4. 04A coherent national policy should focus on maintaining dominance in stages one and two while diversifying stage four manufacturing across multiple Asian partners to ensure resilience rather than autarky.
  5. 05The U.S. government must return to the mid-twentieth-century model of industrial policy to support 'tech teens' and essential manufacturing stages that private markets currently overlook.
