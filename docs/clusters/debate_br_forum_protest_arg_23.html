Diversified Gemini

"The defense of campus encampments as a right to protest is legally and logically flawed because it ignores the fundamental role of property rights and the primary educational mission of the university."

Alex Gourevitch’s defense of the Gaza campus protests relies on a deliberate obfuscation of a very simple concept: property rights. In his exhaustive attempt to justify the chaotic encampments that paralyzed American universities, he casually dismisses the idea of trespass, arguing that universities betrayed their "educational mission" by asserting their right to control their own grounds.

This argument is legally and logically bankrupt. The First Amendment protects freedom of speech; it does not protect the right to commandeer land.

Whether an institution is public or private, it operates under the rule of law. A university quadrangle is designed for the use of the entire community—for transit, for studying, for recreation. When a faction of students (and outside agitators) erects a barricaded tent city, they are effectively privatizing shared space for their own political ends. They are denying the use of that space to everyone else.

Gourevitch acts as if enforcing trespass laws is a draconian suppression of ideas. It is nothing of the sort. The students were not arrested for *what* they were saying; they were arrested for *where* and *how* they were saying it. A student is free to hold a sign condemning Israel on the campus steps. They are not free to pitch a tent in the library, lock the doors, and refuse to leave.

Furthermore, Gourevitch's demand that we tolerate "disruptive acts" because democracy is "demanding" is an arrogant dismissal of the primary purpose of a university: education. Students pay tens of thousands of dollars to attend classes, access libraries, and complete degrees. They do not pay to be unwilling extras in a radical political psychodrama.

Order is the prerequisite for liberty. By romanticizing mob behavior and castigating administrators who finally found the spine to enforce their own codes of conduct, Gourevitch is not defending academic freedom. He is defending anarchy.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The First Amendment protects the freedom of speech but does not grant protesters the right to commandeer or privatize shared university land for their own political ends.
  2. 02Enforcing trespass laws is a neutral application of the rule of law regarding where and how speech occurs, rather than a suppression of the ideas being expressed.
  3. 03The encampments effectively denied the rest of the university community their right to use shared spaces for transit, study, and recreation.
  4. 04Prioritizing disruptive protest over the university's educational purpose unfairly burdens students who pay for access to classes and facilities.
  5. 05Maintaining institutional order is a necessary prerequisite for liberty, whereas romanticizing disruptive behavior leads to anarchy rather than academic freedom.
