"The most effective way to curb prescription drug abuse is to aggressively enforce laws against illegal diversion and rogue clinics rather than imposing restrictive monitoring requirements that hinder legitimate medical care."

Whitney Houston’s death, should it prove to be prescription-related, is undeniably tragic. Yet in our rush to prevent the next celebrity tragedy, we risk throwing legitimate pain patients under the bus. The best way to curb prescription drug abuse is not to erect ever-higher barriers between suffering Americans and their medicine, but to aggressively enforce existing laws against illegal diversion while fiercely protecting the doctor-patient relationship.

The majority of people dying from prescription drug overdoses are not the grandmother with arthritis who takes a steady dose of oxycodone as prescribed. The victims are overwhelmingly polydrug users who mix pharmaceuticals with alcohol or street drugs, or who crush and inject pills obtained on the black market. Lawmakers in several states, including Florida, have cracked down on the “pill mill” clinics that were little more than cash-for-scrips operations. That kind of targeted enforcement works: Florida saw a significant drop in oxycodone-related deaths after its 2011 reforms. We should replicate this surgical approach nationally—aggressive prosecution of rogue prescribers and interstate trafficking rings—rather than burdening every responsible physician with new monitoring requirements.

Unfortunately, the popular solution du jour—mandatory prescription drug monitoring databases—too often acts as a blunt instrument. Doctors, terrified of landing on a law enforcement radar, are starting to undertreat pain. Patients with complex conditions report being dropped from practices or forced to sign humiliating “pain contracts” that treat them like suspects. The chilling effect is real, and it falls heaviest on the poor, the elderly, and those without the resources to find a specialist willing to take a chance.

No one disputes that we have a problem with misuse. But the core of that problem is illegal behavior, not medical care. If we invest in law enforcement to stop trafficking and provide voluntary physician education about weaning and alternative therapies, we can reduce deaths without turning pain patients into collateral damage. The balance should always tip toward the suffering patient who has done nothing wrong.
