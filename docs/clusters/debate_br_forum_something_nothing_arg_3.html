"Rosen’s essay provides a vital, evidence-based critique of American strategy in Afghanistan by demonstrating that the military's attempt to build a functional state and security apparatus from nothing is a form of 'magical thinking' that ignores structural realities and institutional failures."

Rosen's essay achieves something rare in discussions of American foreign policy: genuine engagement with the realities of military operations as experienced by those who conduct them. His reporting from Helmand province provides insight that strategic documents and official assessments consistently obscure. This is valuable journalism, and its findings deserve attention from anyone seeking to understand why American strategy in Afghanistan has struggled to achieve its objectives.

The essay's central argument—that America is attempting "something from nothing" in Afghanistan—captures a fundamental truth about the limits of military power in deeply divided societies lacking institutional foundations. The assumption that sufficient resources and appropriate strategy can overcome these structural limitations represents a form of magical thinking that Rosen's reporting systematically challenges.

His account of the Afghan security forces reveals dysfunction that official reports consistently minimize. The drug test results, equipment shortages, and popular alienation that Rosen documents suggest not merely implementation failures but fundamental problems with the train-and-equip approach that underpins American exit strategy. If the Afghan police cannot be made competent with current methods, and if alternative methods are not being developed, then the entire framework of transition crumbles.

The comparison with Iraq produces sharp insight. Rosen correctly identifies that the Surge succeeded partly because conditions had already exhausted the insurgency's momentum before American reinforcements arrived. The Sunni Awakening reflected Sunni recognition that they had been defeated and saw American alliance as their best remaining option. This dynamic had no parallel in Afghanistan, where the Taliban had not been decisively defeated and where American operations continued to produce civilian casualties that fed insurgent recruitment.

Rosen's institutional critique of the American military deserves endorsement. The gap between COIN doctrine and practice reflects not individual failures but systemic dysfunction. The military's culture, training, and operational rhythms remain oriented toward conventional warfare despite years of counterinsurgency operations. Rosen's observation that soldiers joke about "doing that COIN shit" captures the distance between official doctrine and actual behavior.

The author's analysis of al-Qaeda's relationship with the Taliban deserves particular attention. If the Taliban's alliance with al-Qaeda reflects "convenience, not ideology," then strategic calculations require adjustment. The original justification for the Afghanistan war—denying al-Qaeda safe haven—has been stretched to include objectives that may not serve American interests and that certainly exceed the original mandate. Rosen's suggestion that American presence strengthens rather than weakens terrorist networks deserves serious engagement.

However, the essay's implicit recommendation of withdrawal requires development that Rosen does not provide. The author never explicitly argues for American departure, perhaps recognizing that such a position remains politically untenable. Yet his analysis points consistently in this direction, creating tension between what he argues and what he implies. A more honest essay would explicitly state its conclusions and defend them directly.

The author's passing suggestion that "addressing these problems at their roots"—meaning American imperial excess and support for Israel and Middle Eastern dictators—would do more for everyone than military intervention requires scrutiny. This argument conflates distinct issues and assumes policy changes in one domain would automatically affect outcomes in another. The assumption that terrorist movements are purely reactive to American policy ignores their own ideological commitments and strategic objectives.

Despite these limitations, Rosen's essay represents essential reading for anyone seeking to understand why American strategy in Afghanistan has failed to achieve its objectives. The specific failures he documents—corrupt police, civilian casualties, electoral fraud, institutional dysfunction—are real and require acknowledgment. Whatever policy direction one ultimately favors, this account demands engagement. The comfortable assumption that more resources, better strategy, and sufficient resolve will eventually produce success has no basis in the evidence Rosen presents.

"Nir Rosen’s assessment of the Afghanistan conflict demonstrates that the American strategy is a failure based on a fundamental disconnect between Washington's strategic ambitions and the operational realities on the ground."

After eight years of American involvement in Afghanistan, Nir Rosen has provided what may be the most honest assessment of that conflict's reality. His essay demonstrates why policy discussions in Washington so often seem disconnected from conditions on the ground, and why the gap between strategic ambition and operational reality has grown so dangerously wide.

The core of Rosen's argument is irresistible: the United States military is attempting to build a centralized state in Afghanistan when no such entity has ever existed there, using counterinsurgency methods developed for fundamentally different contexts, while relying on Afghan security forces that remain incapable of standing on their own. The mathematical impossibility of these combined objectives should be apparent to anyone willing to examine them honestly.

Rosen's account of the Afghan police reveals the hollowness at the heart of American strategy. When police commanders cannot read maps, when sixty percent of officers in some districts test positive for narcotics, when police are more feared by civilians than the Taliban themselves, the entire framework of "train and transfer" collapses. The American approach assumes that additional resources, better training, and more mentoring will produce effective security forces. Rosen's reporting suggests this assumption rests on fantasy rather than evidence.

The election fraud documented in the essay deserves particular emphasis. When turnout in the south falls below ten percent and systematic fraud renders the results meaningless, the assumption that legitimate governance can emerge from democratic processes proves false. Rosen is correct that "the failed elections were a message to Afghans that there was no hope of improvement or change." This message, more than any tactical development, will shape Afghanistan's future.

The author's analysis of COIN doctrine's institutional challenges within the American military deserves wide attention. The gap between what the counterinsurgency manual prescribes and what actually happens at the squad level reflects not individual failures but systemic dysfunction. When soldiers joke about "doing that COIN shit" while privately focusing on survival, the doctrine's political factors have no chance of achieving primacy.

The comparison with Iraq is particularly illuminating. Rosen correctly identifies that the Surge succeeded partly because conditions had already exhausted the insurgency's momentum. The Sunnis were defeated; they turned to Americans as the least worst option. No such exhaustion exists in Afghanistan, where the Taliban are not losing and where their nationalist appeal remains intact. Applying Iraq lessons to Afghanistan commits the classic error of assuming that historical analogies trump current conditions.

Perhaps most importantly, Rosen challenges the assumption that defeating the Taliban serves American interests. His argument that al-Qaeda is already established in Pakistan, that the Taliban are primarily nationalist rather than jihadist, and that American presence strengthens rather than weakens terrorist networks deserves serious engagement. The original justification for the Afghanistan war—the need to deny al-Qaeda safe havens—has been progressively stretched to include regime change, nation-building, and counterinsurgency, each additional justification diluting the original rationale.

The essay's implicit conclusion is that American withdrawal remains the only viable option. Rosen never states this directly, perhaps recognizing that such a recommendation remains politically impossible in early 2010. But his analysis points clearly in this direction. If the mission cannot succeed, if the costs outweigh the benefits, and if continued presence only strengthens the forces America seeks to defeat, then the rational choice is to leave.

The remaining question is whether withdrawal can be managed responsibly. Rosen's analysis suggests that a sudden departure would create chaos, but a gradual drawdown tied to verifiable Afghan capacity might create space for political accommodation. The Taliban are not ideologically committed to global jihad; they want foreign forces removed and Islamic governance established. These goals might be achievable through negotiation rather than military victory.

For American policymakers, Rosen's essay should prompt fundamental reassessment. The comfortable assumption that more resources, better strategy, and sufficient resolve will eventually produce success has no basis in evidence. The path forward requires either a dramatically different approach or an honest acknowledgment that the mission cannot be accomplished at acceptable cost.

"Nir Rosen’s essay correctly identifies that the U.S. counterinsurgency strategy in Afghanistan is a failure driven by 'magical thinking' and a fundamental misunderstanding of the country's structural and institutional realities."

Rosen's essay illuminates the dark corners of American strategy in Afghanistan that official discourse prefers to ignore. His on-the-ground reporting reveals the gap between what counterinsurgency doctrine promises and what American forces actually accomplish, between strategic objectives and operational realities. This is valuable journalism, and policymakers would do well to attend to its findings.

The essay's central insight—that America is attempting "something from nothing" in Afghanistan—captures a fundamental truth about the limits of military power in deeply divided societies lacking institutional foundations for centralized governance. The assumption that sufficient resources and correct strategy can overcome these structural limitations represents a form of magical thinking that Rosen's reporting systematically demolishes.

His account of the Afghan police deserves particular attention. Rosen documents a force that cannot read maps, that operates under corruption so pervasive it has become invisible to its participants, that is more feared by civilians than the insurgents it supposedly combats. The drug test results—sixty percent positive in some districts—suggest an institution that has not merely failed but collapsed entirely as a functioning organization. Against this backdrop, McChrystal's plan to expand Afghan security forces to 400,000 personnel appears not merely ambitious but delusional.

The comparison with Iraq proves Rosen's sharpest analytical point. The Surge succeeded not because American strategy was brilliant but because conditions had already exhausted the insurgency's momentum. The Sunni Awakening reflected Sunni recognition that they had been defeated in civil war, not American persuasion. The American military could position itself as a relatively benign actor because Shia militias had demonstrated their willingness to commit atrocities that dwarfed anything the Americans had done. No comparable dynamic exists in Afghanistan, where the Taliban have not been defeated and where American operations regularly produce civilian casualties that feed insurgent propaganda.

Rosen's institutional critique of the American military deserves endorsement. The gap between COIN doctrine and practice reflects not individual failures but systemic dysfunction. The military's culture, training, promotion systems, and operational rhythms remain oriented toward conventional warfare despite a decade of counterinsurgency operations. The bureaucracy that Rosen identifies as "dominated by old-school army thinking" has resisted adaptation not through malice but through institutional inertia that no amount of new manuals or strategic directives can overcome.

The author's analysis of al-Qaeda's relationship with the Taliban deserves careful consideration. If Rosen is correct that this alliance reflects "convenience, not ideology," then the strategic logic of American intervention requires reexamination. The original justification for the Afghanistan war was denying al-Qaeda safe haven, not defeating the Taliban regime. If al-Qaeda is already established in Pakistan, and if the Taliban are primarily concerned with national rather than international objectives, then the costs of American presence may exceed the benefits.

However, Rosen's essay would benefit from greater attention to what withdrawal would actually produce. The implicit assumption that American departure would create space for accommodation between Kabul and the Taliban ignores the Taliban's own strategic objectives and the likely behavior of regional powers in a post-American Afghanistan. Pakistan, with its historical support for Taliban forces and its ongoing security concerns, would likely fill any vacuum created by American withdrawal. Iran might similarly increase its involvement. The result might not be negotiated accommodation but intensified regional competition.

Rosen correctly identifies that "the failed elections were a message to Afghans that there was no hope of improvement or change." This message, combined with continuing civilian casualties from American operations, undermines whatever support for the Karzai government might have existed. But the solution to legitimacy failure is not American withdrawal alone but rather fundamental restructuring of American objectives and methods.

The essay's vision of what "addressing these problems at their roots" might entail remains vague. Rosen mentions American support for Israel and Middle Eastern dictators as grievances that fuel terrorist movements, but does not explain how changing these policies would affect strategic calculations in Afghanistan. The assumption that American retrenchment in the Middle East would reduce terrorist threats to American personnel in Afghanistan deserves explicit argument rather than passing reference.

Despite these limitations, Rosen's essay represents essential reading for anyone seeking to understand why American strategy in Afghanistan has failed to achieve its objectives. The official narratives of progress and momentum cannot survive contact with the realities he documents. Whatever policy direction one ultimately favors, this account demands engagement.
