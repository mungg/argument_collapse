"While the 'two-step' strategy of dethroning fossil capital is correct in principle, its success depends on the climate movement's ability to secure the support of the working class through massive, concrete material commitments and high-road labor standards."

**A Trade Unionist's Caution**

I appreciate Olúfẹ́mi Táíwò's essay as a serious effort to think about climate strategy from the left, and I appreciate especially his attention to the labor dimension—the gold-standard labor language in New York's Build Public Renewables Act, the references to unions and tenant organizations, the emphasis on public power as both ownership and ethos. These are the right starting points. But I want to register some concerns from the perspective of someone who has spent twenty years in the labor movement and who thinks the climate left often underestimates how difficult it is to bring workers into a transition strategy on terms they will accept.

The first thing to say is that the two-step framework, as Táíwò presents it, is much closer to actual labor traditions than the impatient maximalism that dominates much climate discourse. Trade unions have always thought in stages: contract by contract, sector by sector, beachhead by beachhead. We know that strategy means choosing what to fight for now and what to defer. We know that solidarity has to be constructed and that "the workers" are not a unified subject who will simply act on their abstract interests if those interests are correctly described. From this perspective, Táíwò's argument is welcome, because much of the climate movement has been working with a model of social change that bears no relation to how social change actually occurs—which is slowly, through institutions, through built relationships, through compromises that prefigurative politics finds unpalatable.

That said, I want to flag three problems that Táíwò's essay touches on but does not fully address.

The first is the actual position of fossil workers in the transition. The U.S. has roughly 150,000 direct workers in oil and gas extraction, plus another 500,000 or so in pipeline, refining, and distribution, plus several million in jobs adjacent to or dependent on fossil-energy systems—gas station attendants, truck mechanics, petrochemical workers, plastics workers. In coal country, the number is smaller but the geographic concentration is severe. These workers are not abstractions. They are union members in some cases, would-be union members in others, and they vote.

A "two-step" politics that proposes to dethrone fossil capital has to have a serious answer for what happens to these workers, their families, and their communities. The honest version of this answer is not "they will be retrained for jobs in solar," because solar jobs do not pay what oil jobs pay, are not located where oil jobs are located, and require different skills. The honest answer is some combination of (a) generous early retirement, (b) wage insurance for displaced workers that protects their living standards for a substantial period, (c) place-based investment in fossil-dependent communities that may or may not produce comparable employment, and (d) honest acknowledgment that some communities will not recover and that those communities deserve substantial transfers regardless of whether new economic activity materializes.

This is what a "just transition" actually requires, and it is expensive—much more expensive than the climate left typically budgets for. The German coal exit, for instance, included tens of billions of euros for affected regions. The U.S. equivalent would be in the hundreds of billions. Without this kind of commitment, the political math of fossil phaseout simply does not work, because the workers and communities affected will fight any phaseout with everything they have, and they have considerable political power, particularly in U.S. swing states. The history of trade liberalization tells us what happens when displaced workers are promised retraining and not given material security: they become the political base for reactionary movements, and the next progressive coalition has to spend a generation winning them back.

The second problem is the labor politics of the green sector itself. Táíwò celebrates the union jobs mandated in New York's Build Public Renewables Act, and rightly so. But the broader green economy in the U.S. is largely non-union and increasingly so. Solar installation, in particular, has been a notoriously low-wage, transient, dangerous occupation, with high turnover and contractor-based employment that defeats most organizing efforts. Battery and EV manufacturing is unionized at the Big Three but increasingly not at the new entrants—Tesla, Rivian, the Korean and Japanese plants in the South. The IRA's labor provisions are real but limited and contested.

If the green transition produces millions of new jobs but those jobs are worse than the fossil jobs they replace, the working-class politics of the transition will fail. Not because workers are short-sighted but because they correctly recognize a worsening of their material position. The "two-step" strategy has to confront this directly: the first step is not merely to dethrone fossil capital but to build the green economy on labor terms that make it materially preferable to the fossil economy, which means substantial public-sector employment, strong labor law reform, prevailing-wage requirements, and project labor agreements as norms rather than exceptions.

The third problem is the international dimension of green industrial policy. The IRA, the Inflation Reduction Act, has aspects I support and aspects I do not. I support its scale, its labor provisions, and its commitment to public investment. I am more skeptical of its protectionist features and what they mean for solidarity with workers in Mexico, Korea, China, and elsewhere. A climate strategy that succeeds in the U.S. by exporting the costs of decarbonization to workers in other countries is not a climate strategy that the international labor movement can endorse.

The trade union tradition has historically been internationalist in rhetoric and nationalist in practice. The climate crisis demands that we close that gap, and we are not close to doing so. Industrial policy that benefits American workers at the expense of Mexican workers is not the model. Industrial policy coordinated across North America—or, better, across the hemisphere—on shared labor and environmental standards, would be. We are not building toward that, and Táíwò's essay does not push us toward it.

Where does this leave the two-step strategy? Where any honest strategy leaves it: as a framework that has to be filled in with concrete commitments that the climate movement has not yet adequately made. The labor movement is ready to be a serious partner in this work, but readiness is conditional on the climate left's willingness to internalize what serious labor politics actually requires: massive material commitments to displaced workers, a non-negotiable commitment to high-wage union jobs in the new sectors, and international solidarity that does not collapse the moment domestic political pressures intensify.

Táíwò invokes the New York legislation as a model, and it is. But it is a model that took years of patient coalition work, that required compromises that purists in the climate movement opposed, and that even now is being implemented at a pace and scale that falls short of what the climate emergency demands. The "two-step" strategy is correct in principle. The labor movement's caution is about whether the climate left is prepared for the actual work of step one—which is not a clever slogan but the long, unglamorous labor of building a coalition that can survive the political reactions our victories will provoke.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A viable phaseout of fossil fuels requires an expensive and honest 'just transition' that provides material security, such as wage insurance and place-based investment, to workers whose livelihoods currently depend on the fossil fuel economy.
  2. 02The green transition will fail politically if the new jobs created are lower-wage, non-union, or more precarious than the fossil fuel jobs they replace.
  3. 03The first step of the strategy must include building a green economy based on public-sector employment, labor law reform, and prevailing-wage requirements to make it materially preferable to workers.
  4. 04Climate industrial policy must move beyond protectionist national frameworks toward international labor solidarity to prevent exporting the costs of decarbonization to workers in other countries.
  5. 05The climate movement must shift from 'impatient maximalism' to the long-term, unglamorous work of building institutional coalitions and accepting the compromises necessary for durable social change.
