"While humility is a valuable epistemic virtue, it is an insufficient solution to pandemic failures because the primary cause of the crisis was not a lack of modest planning, but a political economy and social structure that prioritizes wealth extraction over the protection of vulnerable populations."

Humility Without Power Is Just Another Sermon

Sheila Jasanoff has given us an elegant diagnosis of what went wrong with the world's pandemic preparedness, and a graceful prescription. Trade prediction's hubris for humility. Replace blueprints with technologies that listen, that map vulnerabilities, that pull "possible futures back to show us how to act in the present." Who could disagree? And there, precisely, is the trouble. As an ethic, humility is unobjectionable. As a politics, it is dangerously underspecified. The reason the United States lost more than 740,000 lives by October 2021 is not principally that its planners failed to think modestly about uncertainty. It is that the country is a class society organized to extract wealth from poor and racialized bodies, and that no spreadsheet of "indicators" — however humble — would have changed that without changing the underlying distribution of power.

Consider what Jasanoff lists, almost in passing, as the populations COVID-19 disproportionately killed: the poor, the elderly, Black and Hispanic Americans, residents of environmentally compromised neighborhoods, single mothers shouldering collapsed schools. She is right that none of this was unforeseeable. But the structural reasons it was foreseeable cannot be patched by better epistemic posture. They are baked into how Americans work, where they live, what air they breathe, whether they can afford to stay home, and whether the institutions that ration health care regard them as worth saving. Meatpacking workers at Smithfield and Tyson did not die at vastly elevated rates because the Global Health Security Index neglected "humility." They died because they were forced back to the line under federal protection while their employers received liability shields. A technology of humility might have mapped that vulnerability in advance; a politics of solidarity would have prevented it.

The same applies to vaccine hesitancy, which Jasanoff cites as a paradigm case of what humility could have illuminated. Ethnographers have indeed been writing for years about the Tuskegee echo, about the way medical institutions earned distrust the hard way among Black Americans, and about the libertarian and evangelical currents that turn immunization into a cultural battlefield. But the ethnography was there. The "map of power and powerlessness" was drawn. What was missing was not knowledge; it was the political will to act on it — to fund community health workers, pay people to get vaccinated, send trusted local messengers into neighborhoods, and decouple vaccine access from employer-based health systems. Calling for more humility risks suggesting that the failure was conceptual when in fact it was distributional.

I do not think Jasanoff would disagree with any of this in private. Her essay gestures repeatedly at justice, at marginalization, at "the perspectives of those who are acted upon." But the framework she offers — "technologies of humility" — keeps drifting back to epistemic virtues. It asks: how should planners think? How should expertise reckon with its blind spots? How should the framers of plans incorporate memory and experience? These are good questions for a graduate seminar at the Kennedy School. They are not the questions that decide whether a nursing aide in Queens lives or dies. Those are answered by labor law, housing policy, the structure of Medicaid, the bargaining power of unions, the political economy of the long-term care industry, and the willingness of the federal government to use coercive power against capital.

There is also a problem of agency. Whose humility, exactly, is Jasanoff calling for? The CDC's? The White House's? Mitch McConnell's? The Florida governor's, who actively sabotaged school mask mandates? The very architecture of American federalism that she correctly identifies as crippling the response is not going to be reformed by the cultivation of a more humble disposition among public health planners. A planner can be exquisitely sensitive to the limits of her own knowledge and still be powerless when a state legislature votes to strip her department of authority. Humility, in such cases, is not a policy lever; it is a mood.

Jay Kaufman, whom Jasanoff quotes, gets closer to the bone: "The U.S. Achilles's heel is not lack of humility; it is a toxic social ideology rooted in a blind obsession with individual freedom." I would add: and an even more toxic political economy that treats public goods as inefficiencies, social insurance as welfare, and labor as expendable. The pandemic did not catch the United States short on epistemology. It caught it short on solidarity, redistribution, and state capacity — the three things forty years of bipartisan neoliberalism have systematically corroded. By 2020, the country had a public health workforce a third smaller than in 2008. It had a hospital system in which a fifth of beds were owned by private equity. It had a labor force in which 30 million people had no paid sick leave. No "humble" planning document was going to compensate for those facts.

What would a more materialist alternative to Jasanoff's program look like? It would start by naming the actual obstacles. It would observe that "preparedness" failed not because experts were arrogant but because the political coalitions necessary to enact preparedness were systematically disorganized — by Citizens United, by the gutting of public unions, by the capture of regulatory agencies, by the destruction of local journalism that might have held leaders accountable. It would observe that the countries which fared best — Vietnam, South Korea, Taiwan, New Zealand, Cuba — did not necessarily have more "humble" planners; they had more capable states with greater legitimacy and, in some cases, stronger collectivist traditions. State capacity, it turns out, matters at least as much as epistemic virtue.

This is not a rejection of Jasanoff's project. The technologies of humility she sketches — citizen participation, attention to historical memory, structured engagement with marginalized perspectives — are valuable and overdue. But they need to be coupled to a frank account of who benefits from the current configuration of expertise and who pays. Without that coupling, "humility" risks becoming the latest in a long line of soft, ameliorative concepts — "resilience," "stakeholder engagement," "co-production" — that promise to humanize technocracy without disturbing its foundations. Each of those concepts, in turn, was absorbed into the institutional cultures it was meant to challenge and emerged thirty years later as boilerplate language in the executive summaries of consulting firms.

I worry that "technologies of humility" will travel the same route. Already one can imagine the slide deck: "Pillar 3: Humility. Stakeholder mapping. Vulnerability listening sessions. Equity dashboards." The form will be retained; the bite will be extracted. To prevent that, humility needs to be operationalized not only as a stance toward knowledge but as a transfer of decision-making power — over budgets, over priorities, over who lives near the freeway and who works without sick pay. Otherwise we will have a more reflective expert class presiding over the same body count.

The next pandemic will come. The microbes are patient. When it arrives, the question will not be whether American planners had read enough Foucault or convened enough citizen panels. It will be whether the country built, in the meantime, the kind of society in which collective sacrifice is reciprocated by collective protection. That is not a problem humility can solve. It is a problem only politics — argumentative, redistributive, sometimes confrontational politics — can solve.

  2. 02The failure to address vaccine hesitancy was not due to a lack of ethnographic knowledge or 'humble' mapping, but a lack of political will to fund community-based health infrastructure and decouple access from employment.

  5. 05Without a transfer of actual decision-making power and a commitment to redistributive politics, 'technologies of humility' risk being co-opted as empty, bureaucratic jargon that humanizes technocracy without changing its deadly outcomes.
