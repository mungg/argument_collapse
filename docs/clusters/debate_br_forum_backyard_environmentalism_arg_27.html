"The authors' dismissal of market-based environmental regulation is fundamentally misguided because it ignores the proven efficiency and informational advantages of cap-and-trade systems like the SO2 emissions trading program."

In “Beyond Backyard Environmentalism,” Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen advance a compelling and highly sophisticated theoretical model for the future of environmental regulation. Their concept of a “rolling-rule regime,” characterized by decentralized experimentation and centralized information pooling, captures a genuine shift in how some environmental policies are currently being implemented. However, as an economist observing the evolution of environmental policy, I find their casual dismissal of market-based mechanisms to be fundamentally misguided and rooted in a misunderstanding of how artificial markets actually function in practice.

The authors critique both centralized command-and-control regulation and market-simulating mechanisms (like tradable emissions permits) on the grounds that both demand “extraordinary quantities and varieties of information” that regulators cannot possibly satisfy. They argue that to create a market in pollution permits, regulators must possess an impossible degree of foresight regarding aggregate emissions, environmental harm, and feasible reductions. Therefore, they conclude, market mechanisms fail to deliver on their promise of orderly decentralization and must be replaced by the direct deliberative problem-solving of the rolling-rule regime.

This critique creates a straw man. It demands a level of perfection from market mechanisms that no economist expects, while simultaneously granting extraordinary leniency to the untried deliberative processes the authors prefer.

To see why the authors’ dismissal of markets is premature, we need only look at the most significant environmental policy innovation of this decade: the sulfur dioxide (SO2) emissions trading program established under Title IV of the 1990 Clean Air Act Amendments. The goal of this program was to combat acid rain by reducing SO2 emissions from power plants by 10 million tons from 1980 levels. Instead of telling every power plant exactly what technology to install (the command-and-control approach) or asking local citizens to form deliberative committees with utility managers to negotiate reductions (the authors’ preferred approach), Congress simply set a national cap on emissions, allocated allowances to utilities, and let them trade.

Has this market required “immodest omniscience” from the EPA? Not at all. The regulator did not need to know the abatement costs of every individual facility, nor did it need to predict technological breakthroughs. It only needed to establish the cap based on a broad scientific consensus about environmental harm, monitor the actual emissions (using continuous emissions monitors), and enforce strict penalties for exceeding allowances.

The results have been spectacularly successful, far outpacing the authors’ slow, consensus-building tributary teams. Emissions have dropped faster than required, and the costs of compliance have been a fraction of what both industry and government initially projected. Why? Because the market harnessed the decentralized knowledge of plant managers and engineers. The people closest to the problem figured out the cheapest ways to reduce emissions—whether by switching to low-sulfur coal, installing scrubbers, or improving efficiency—and profited by selling their excess allowances.

Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen argue that artificial markets are rigid because they depend on secure ownership rights, limiting post-hoc program corrections. But the SO2 program demonstrates that caps can be phased in and adjusted by legislation if necessary. More importantly, the certainty of the property right is exactly what incentivizes long-term investment in pollution-control technology. If a utility manager knows that a deliberative committee might change the rules next year based on “continuous learning,” why would they invest hundreds of millions of dollars in a new scrubber? The “rolling-rule regime” replaces the certainty of property rights with endless meetings and bureaucratic fluidity, a recipe for capital strike and delayed investment.

Furthermore, the authors completely ignore the transaction costs of their own model. They champion a system where citizens, experts, and managers continuously deliberate, set targets, monitor each other, pool data, and revise standards. The sheer amount of human capital, time, and administrative friction required to sustain such a regime across every factory and watershed in America is staggering. Markets, by contrast, coordinate behavior through the price mechanism. A rising price of a pollution permit transmits all the necessary information to a plant manager: it tells them that pollution is becoming scarcer and that they need to innovate. No “deep participation” or endless stakeholder meetings are required.

This is not to say that cap-and-trade works for every environmental problem. It is best suited for uniformly mixed pollutants, not localized toxic hotspots where community deliberation might indeed be more appropriate. But to wave away the proven success of tradable permits as just another failed attempt at “modest omniscience” is to ignore the greatest environmental success story of the 1990s. If we truly want to move beyond the inefficiencies of centralized command, we should be looking to expand the use of market incentives, not replacing them with a utopian vision of perpetual town-hall meetings.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The authors create a straw man by demanding a level of informational perfection from markets that is not required for them to function successfully in practice.
  2. 02The SO2 emissions trading program demonstrates that markets can achieve environmental goals faster and at a lower cost than projected by harnessing decentralized technical knowledge rather than centralized foresight.
  3. 03The certainty of property rights in market mechanisms provides the necessary incentives for long-term capital investment in pollution-control technology, which the authors' fluid 'rolling-rule' regime would undermine.
  4. 04The authors fail to account for the staggering transaction costs and administrative friction inherent in a system that requires continuous, deep participation from all stakeholders.
  5. 05The price mechanism in a market transmits necessary information to actors more efficiently than the authors' proposed model of perpetual deliberation and data pooling.
