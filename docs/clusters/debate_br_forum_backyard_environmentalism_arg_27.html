"The proposed 'rolling-rule regime' of collaborative, performance-based regulation is fundamentally flawed because it prioritizes the management and mitigation of economic growth over the necessary ability to prohibit ecologically destructive activities."

Fung, Sabel, and Karkkainen celebrate a transformation in environmental regulation: from rigid, top-down commands to a flexible, participatory “rolling-rule regime.” For them, the task is to institutionalize better ways of learning, to fuse expert and local knowledge in a continuous process of adjustment. It is a vision of pragmatist experimentalism that, they suggest, might even resolve the age-old democratic tension between efficiency and self-rule. But what is entirely missing from their analysis is any serious reckoning with the deeper, more uncomfortable question: What if the learning process leads us to the conclusion that certain economic activities, no matter how “civically co-managed,” are fundamentally incompatible with the long-term health of the biosphere? The rolling-rule regime is a framework for accommodating development; it may be incapable of saying “no” when no is what the earth demands.

The authors’ narrative arc traces a movement from NIMBY obstructionism to collaborative construction. The first backyard environmentalists, they write, “fought to keep harmful activity out of their neighborhoods.” The new generation moves “beyond” that to determine “what should occur in their backyards—what kinds of activity are productive, yet acceptably sustainable.” This framing implicitly delegitimizes the impulse to simply stop a harmful activity. The polluting factory, the sprawling subdivision, the toxic chemical—these are no longer adversaries to be banished, but partners to be brought into a deliberative process. The goal shifts from elimination to “acceptably sustainable” management. In the HCP process, a developer who would have been barred from “taking” an endangered species under Section 9 can now negotiate an “incidental take” permit by agreeing to set aside some land and implement adaptive management. The underlying assumption is that development will proceed; the only question is how to mitigate its impacts. But what if the accumulation of such mitigated developments, each deemed acceptable by its local tributary team or HCP stakeholder group, results in the slow, cumulative unraveling of ecosystems? The model has no mechanism for a collective, society-wide decision that certain forms of growth must be halted, not just managed.

This is not an abstract concern. The Chesapeake Bay Program is, as the authors note, a model of the new architecture. Yet after fifteen years of collaborative effort, the Bay’s living resources remain severely stressed. The targeted 40% nutrient reduction was supposed to be met by 2000; it will be missed by a wide margin, and even if it were met, some scientists now suggest it would be insufficient to fully restore the Bay’s submerged aquatic vegetation and oxygen levels. The tributary teams are now being tasked with developing even more localized plans. But the fundamental driver of nutrient pollution is the land-use pattern of the entire watershed: the suburban sprawl with its fertilized lawns, the intensive row-crop agriculture, the vast number of vehicles emitting nitrogen oxides. These are not problems that a small team of stakeholders can solve by tweaking “best management practices.” They are products of a growth machine that no amount of local collaborative tweaking can fundamentally redirect. The rolling-rule regime can optimize within a given growth trajectory, but it cannot challenge the trajectory itself. Indeed, by bringing developers and farmers to the table as legitimate partners, it may actively foreclose that challenge.

The Toxics Use Reduction Act in Massachusetts is often held up as an exemplar. Firms found “significant net benefits” from pollution prevention; toxics use dropped 20% in five years. But this is the low-hanging fruit. A 20% reduction from a 1990 baseline still leaves a massive toxic load coursing through the state’s factories, waste streams, and products. The deeper goal of a 50% cut by 1997 was not met, and the state’s authority to compel truly transformative change—through a ban on the most persistent bioaccumulative toxins, for example—is not part of the TURA framework. The process of planning and expert consultation works well for incremental process redesigns that save money. But when the next 30% reduction requires a complete overhaul of production chemistry, the collaborative, voluntary spirit may evaporate. And some chemicals, like endocrine disruptors, cause harm at such minute concentrations that no level of “acceptable” use and release may be safe. A rolling-rule system oriented around continuous reduction might never arrive at the only scientifically defensible target: zero discharge.

The deep ecology tradition, along with the more radical strands of the environmental justice movement, has long argued that reformist environmentalism, with its focus on regulation and management, serves to legitimize an unsustainable, growth-addicted economic system. The “rolling-rule regime” is the apotheosis of this reformist tendency. It promises a hyper-rational, inclusive process that replaces the blunt conflict between industry and environmentalists with a perpetual conversation. But that conversation occurs within a horizon that takes for granted the prerogatives of private property, the necessity of economic growth, and the legitimacy of corporate actors as partners in governance. It substitutes process for purpose. The Endangered Species Act, for all its flaws, at least articulated a clear, non-negotiable purpose: no species shall be pushed to extinction by American economic activity. It was a line in the sand. The HCP process, even in its most ambitious form, has replaced that line with a negotiating table. The Miller Bill would make the table mandatory, requiring “objective, measurable biological goals” and adaptive management. But who sets the goals? If the local collaborative body determines that the “recovery” of the gnatcatcher is consistent with 20% habitat loss, that becomes the scientifically-blessed target. The absolute character of the prohibition is dissolved into a flexible standard, to be bent by the very parties whose interests lie in bending it.

The authors point to the nuclear industry’s INPO as a success story of self-regulation backed by public transparency. But nuclear power is a technology that many environmentalists believe should be shut down entirely, not made marginally safer. The INPO model didn’t challenge the basic decision to operate reactors; it made their operation less prone to catastrophe. A world full of INPO-style rolling-rule institutions would be a world that manages risk ever more finely, but never asks whether the risk-producing activity itself should continue. The same logic applies to the HCPs and the Bay Program. They are devices for making an unsustainable economy seem tolerable, extending its life by polishing its roughest edges.

The alternative is not a return to pure command-and-control, which indeed had its own blindness. The alternative is to insist that the democratic debate over environmental governance must include the radical options: the prohibition of whole classes of chemicals, the large-scale de-intensification of agriculture, the downzoning and retreat from floodplains and fire-prone wildlands, the degrowth of energy- and materials-intensive consumption. These are not matters that can be devolved to local tributary teams or corporate toxics reduction teams; they require a national, indeed a global, political confrontation with the forces driving ecological destruction. The authors have given us a sophisticated toolkit for fine-tuning the machine. But they have not addressed the possibility that the machine itself is the problem.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The shift from 'backyard environmentalism' to collaborative construction implicitly delegitimizes the necessary impulse to stop harmful activities entirely in favor of 'acceptably sustainable' management.
  2. 02Local collaborative frameworks, such as the Chesapeake Bay Program, are incapable of addressing the systemic drivers of ecological decline, like suburban sprawl and intensive agriculture, which require challenging the trajectory of growth itself.
  3. 03Performance-based systems like TURA focus on incremental improvements and 'low-hanging fruit' but lack the authority to mandate the transformative changes or total bans required for long-term biosphere health.
  4. 04By replacing non-negotiable legal protections, such as the Endangered Species Act’s absolute prohibitions, with flexible negotiating tables, the new regime allows environmental standards to be compromised by the very parties interested in bending them.
  5. 05The rolling-rule model functions as a tool for risk management that ultimately serves to legitimize and extend the life of an unsustainable economic system rather than questioning the viability of the risk-producing activities themselves.
  6. 06True environmental governance requires a national political confrontation capable of enacting radical options—such as degrowth or the prohibition of certain chemicals—which cannot be devolved to local stakeholder teams.
