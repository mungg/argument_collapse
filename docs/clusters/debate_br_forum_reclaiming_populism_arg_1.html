Vanilla GPT

"A viable left-wing populism must move beyond mere rhetoric to build durable, multiracial, and internationalist democratic power through the patient construction of grassroots institutions."

Adom Getachew is right to insist that “populism” cannot be left as a synonym for demagoguery. In a democracy, politics always involves the making of a people: the selection of injuries that count as public, the narration of common interests, the naming of antagonists, and the invitation to act together. The question is not whether democratic politics will appeal to “the people,” but what kind of people it will imagine and organize. In the United States, where the language of “ordinary Americans” has so often been coded as white, native-born, male, and property-owning, a left populism that is explicitly multiracial is not merely one option among others. It is the only democratic answer to a right populism that turns social abandonment into racial resentment.

But reclaiming populism also requires acknowledging why many people have come to distrust the term. The danger is not simply that elites use “populism” to discredit redistribution. They do. The danger is also that appeals to “the people” can flatten real differences among those whom a movement hopes to unite. A multiracial working-class politics cannot be built by declaring class the universal solvent that dissolves race, gender, citizenship, disability, region, or religion. Nor can it be built by treating every particular demand as automatically compatible with every other. It has to be constructed through institutions and practices that make solidarity durable, not merely through rhetoric that makes it sound obvious.

The Chicago examples Getachew emphasizes are instructive precisely because they are not only rhetorical. The Chicago Teachers Union did not succeed in 2012, or again in 2019 alongside SEIU Local 73, because it discovered a better slogan. It succeeded because it had rebuilt itself internally, connected the workplace to the neighborhood, and framed teachers’ working conditions as students’ learning conditions. “The schools Chicago’s students deserve” was a populist claim, but it was also an institutional strategy. It linked wages to class size, privatization to racial inequality, and public education to the fate of the city. The union could speak credibly about nurses, librarians, social workers, and housing insecurity because its members had organized with parents and community groups in neighborhoods long treated as disposable by city government.

That model matters more than ever in the pandemic. COVID-19 has revealed the truth that austerity tried to hide: care work, logistics, sanitation, food distribution, public health, and education are the infrastructure of social life. The workers suddenly called “essential” have long been treated as expendable. Many are women; many are Black, Latino, immigrant, or undocumented; many lack adequate health insurance, paid sick leave, protective equipment, or bargaining power. A left populism worthy of the name must begin from this contradiction. It must ask why the people most necessary to collective survival are so often the least protected, and why those who profit from their labor are first in line for rescue.

Yet the pandemic also illustrates the limits of revelation. Crisis does not interpret itself. Mass unemployment, illness, and fear can produce demands for universal health care, rent suspension, decarceration, and a stronger public sector. They can also produce border closures, scapegoating, intensified policing, and a politics of sacrifice in which the elderly, disabled, imprisoned, undocumented, and poor are implicitly deemed expendable. The Trump administration’s insistence on calling COVID-19 a “Chinese virus,” its effort to suspend immigration, and its refusal to lift sanctions that obstruct medical access abroad are not distractions from the crisis. They are attempts to define “the people” through exclusion at the very moment when interdependence is most undeniable.

For this reason, the aspiration to build a “left-led, multiracial working class alignment” must resist two temptations. The first is nostalgia for a supposedly simpler New Deal populism. The New Deal built mass institutions, expanded the role of the state, and legitimated social provision, but it also accommodated segregation, excluded many agricultural and domestic workers, and left intact a racialized hierarchy of citizenship. The second temptation is a thin moralism that condemns the old exclusions without building the organizational forms capable of overcoming them. A multiracial populism cannot simply be a New Deal with better manners. It has to be more democratic in its internal structure, more capacious in its account of labor, and more internationalist in its horizon.

The defeat of Bernie Sanders’s presidential campaign has made these questions unavoidable. Sanders did more than any national candidate in decades to popularize demands for Medicare for All, tuition-free public college, a Green New Deal, and a direct confrontation with billionaire power. He also demonstrated that a campaign could be funded by small donors, supported by young people across racial lines, and tied to movements outside the Democratic Party’s usual consultant class. That is no small achievement. The language of class conflict has returned to American politics in part because Sanders refused the etiquette of neoliberal triangulation.

Still, the campaign’s limits should not be dismissed as merely the product of media hostility or establishment consolidation, though both were real. The left has to ask why a message that galvanized many young voters, Latinos, and working-class supporters did not consolidate a broader majority among Black voters, older voters, suburban voters, or union households in key states. Some of this had to do with fear: many Democratic voters judged electability through the trauma of 2016 and chose the candidate they imagined safest. Some of it reflected the power of Barack Obama’s legacy and Joe Biden’s association with it. Some reflected the weakness of left institutions in large parts of the country. If people encounter socialism primarily as a televised argument rather than as a union, tenant organization, church partnership, mutual aid network, or local elected official who has delivered something concrete, they may admire its moral clarity while doubting its capacity to govern.

That is why electoral campaigns, however insurgent, cannot substitute for organized constituencies. The Working Families Party, the Democratic Socialists of America, progressive unions, immigrant rights groups, prison abolitionists, climate justice organizations, and tenant unions all point toward a different political ecology. But much of this remains uneven, urban, and fragile. The right has spent decades building institutions that connect grievance to identity and identity to power: churches, media networks, legal organizations, donor structures, gun clubs, police unions, and local party committees. The left often surges during campaigns and protests, then struggles to sustain membership, leadership development, and discipline across electoral cycles. Reclaiming populism requires the patient work of organization, not only the dramatic work of mobilization.

It also requires a serious account of the state. Anti-institutionalism is often treated as a defect of populism, but the left’s problem in the United States is not too much hostility to institutions; it is that many existing institutions are organized to frustrate majorities. The Senate, the Electoral College, partisan gerrymandering, voter suppression, campaign finance, the courts, and fragmented federalism all distort democratic power. At the same time, the pandemic shows that we desperately need public institutions with capacity: health departments, hospitals, unemployment systems, schools, housing authorities, transit agencies, and agencies able to coordinate production and distribution. A democratic populism should therefore be anti-oligarchic, not anti-institutional. It should seek to democratize and rebuild the state rather than merely denounce it.

This distinction is crucial. Right populism attacks institutions in order to free executive power from accountability and to shield private domination from public constraint. Left populism should attack institutions when they entrench minority rule, racial hierarchy, or corporate veto power, but it should also defend the institutional preconditions of democratic life: voting rights, civil liberties, independent unions, public administration, local democracy, and social rights. In a pandemic, contempt for expertise can kill. But technocracy without democracy also kills, by treating communities as objects of management rather than participants in decision-making. The alternative is not rule by experts or rule by impulse, but public expertise made accountable to the people most affected.

Race is the central test of this project. The disproportionate deaths of Black residents in Chicago, Milwaukee, Detroit, and elsewhere are not biological facts; they are political facts embodied as illness. They reflect segregated housing, environmental exposure, unequal access to care, employment in high-risk jobs, higher rates of chronic disease produced by inequality, and the cumulative stress of racism. A populism that speaks of “the working class” while treating racialized death as a secondary issue will fail both morally and strategically. The working class is not an abstract white man in a factory. It is a Black nurse riding the bus to a hospital without adequate protective equipment; a Latina home health aide caring for elders; an incarcerated person unable to socially distance; a meatpacking worker; a grocery clerk; a laid-off restaurant worker; a public school parent without broadband; an undocumented worker excluded from relief.

But naming race is not enough. The right has become adept at presenting anti-racist claims as elite condescension, even when those claims arise from working-class communities themselves. The left has to show, in practice, that combating racism expands the horizon of democracy for everyone without pretending that everyone is harmed in the same way. Universal programs can help here, but only if designed with attention to unequal starting points. Medicare for All would be universal, but the struggle for health equity also requires hospitals in underserved areas, language access, reproductive justice, mental health care, and environmental protections. A jobs guarantee or Green New Deal would be universal, but it must prioritize communities devastated by deindustrialization, pollution, and racial exclusion. Universalism must be targeted in its construction if it is to be universal in fact.

The international dimension is equally indispensable. Getachew is right that the defeats of Morales’s MAS in Bolivia, Corbyn’s Labour Party, and Sanders in the United States should be considered together, though not collapsed into one story. Each faced distinct conditions: a contested election and military pressure in Bolivia; Brexit, media hostility, anti-Semitism controversies, and Labour’s regional collapse in Britain; the Democratic Party’s structure and anti-socialist anxieties in the United States. But all three reveal the difficulty of sustaining redistributive politics in a global order organized around capital mobility, debt discipline, extractive development, and nationalist backlash. The left cannot answer “America First” with a kinder nationalism. A politics adequate to the pandemic must see health, migration, climate, debt, sanctions, and supply chains as linked.

This is not a call for vague cosmopolitanism. Internationalism must be made concrete. It means opposing sanctions that block access to medicine. It means defending migrants and refugees as members of the communities in which they live and labor. It means recognizing that supply chains depend on workers whose rights cannot stop at the border. It means supporting global public health rather than treating vaccines, protective equipment, and ventilators as spoils in a bidding war. It means a climate politics that acknowledges the unequal responsibilities of wealthy countries and the unequal vulnerabilities of poorer ones. A multiracial working-class alignment in the United States will be incomplete if it defines its people only within national borders.

The great challenge is that the language of the people works best when it identifies an antagonist. For the left, that antagonist should be oligarchy: the concentration of wealth and power that allows a few to decide who receives care, who bears risk, who is disposable, and what forms of life are possible. But “the billionaire class” is not the only antagonist. White supremacy, patriarchy, xenophobia, carceral power, and imperial domination are not mere distractions from class rule; they are among the ways class rule is organized and lived. The art of left populism is to connect these structures without reducing them to one another.

This also suggests a different tone for democratic leadership. The charismatic leader will not disappear from mass politics, and it is foolish to pretend otherwise. Sanders’s campaigns benefited from his consistency and moral authority; Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez’s rise has shown the power of political communication that is both insurgent and pedagogical. But a left populism cannot depend on a single tribune who incarnates the people. Its leaders should multiply leaders. Its campaigns should leave behind organizations stronger than they found them. Its measure of success should not only be votes won, but capacities built: members recruited, shop stewards trained, tenants organized, local candidates developed, political education deepened, solidarities tested.

The pandemic has made the stakes brutally clear. For decades, Americans were told that there was no alternative to privatization, lean government, precarious work, employer-based health care, mass incarceration, militarized borders, and the rule of markets. In a matter of weeks, Congress authorized sums of money previously dismissed as fantastical when proposed for social needs. The question was never simply affordability. It was power: who can command public resources, and for whose benefit.

A reclaimed populism should begin there. It should say that the people are those whose labor and care make society possible, including those whom the nation has excluded, confined, deported, underpaid, or rendered invisible. It should say that democracy is not exhausted by elections, though elections matter profoundly. It should say that public goods are not charity but the material form of equal citizenship. It should say that no community is safe in a world organized around abandonment.

But if populism is to be reclaimed, it must be earned. The people are not waiting fully formed for the right slogan. They are made through conflict, persuasion, compromise, memory, and shared institutions. They are made when teachers strike for students, when nurses demand protective equipment for patients as well as themselves, when tenants refuse eviction, when prisoners are recognized as members of the public, when immigrants are defended not as guests but as neighbors, when unemployed workers are treated not as failures but as casualties of a broken order. The future of left populism depends less on whether we can rescue the word than on whether we can build the democratic power that would make the word true.

Vanilla Gemini

"To counter the rise of right-wing authoritarianism and the failures of technocratic liberalism, the left must reclaim populism by building a multiracial, working-class alignment rooted in local solidarity and radical internationalism."

Adom Getachew rightly identifies a crucial misstep in contemporary liberal discourse: the tendency to treat populism as an inherent pathology, a dangerous deviation from democratic norms that inevitably leads to authoritarianism and exclusion. By ceding the concept of populism to the right, the liberal center has effectively abandoned the most potent mechanism of democratic politics—the ability to define and mobilize “the people.” As Getachew points out, citing Michael Denning, every political force must constitute a people. Politics without populism is reduced to technocratic management, a sterile administration of the status quo that offers no compelling narrative to those who are suffering under it. In the terrifying and unprecedented context of April 2020, with a global pandemic ravaging our communities and the global economy in freefall, the necessity of reclaiming populism for the left has never been more urgent.

The immediate backdrop to this discussion is one of profound political defeat for the institutional left. As Getachew notes, the collapse of Bernie Sanders’s presidential campaign, preceded by the defeat of Jeremy Corbyn in the UK and the coup against Evo Morales in Bolivia, has forced a painful reckoning. The Sanders campaign represented the most viable attempt in modern American history to construct a left-populist project—a “multiracial working-class alignment” that sought to draw a sharp line of antagonism between the 99 percent and the billionaire class. Its failure to secure the Democratic nomination, culminating in the elevation of Joe Biden, suggests that the mechanics of building this alignment are far more complex than a straightforward economic appeal.

To reclaim populism successfully, the left must confront the specific reasons why right-wing populism currently holds the upper hand. Right-wing populism thrives because it provides a simple, albeit deeply toxic, narrative of exclusion. It defines “the people” through the familiar boundaries of the nation-state and the deeply entrenched hierarchies of white supremacy. When Donald Trump scapegoats immigrants or uses the pandemic to justify closing borders, he is tapping into a pre-existing ideological architecture. Right-wing populism protects the economic elite by directing the righteous anger of the masses downward, toward the marginalized, or outward, toward foreign adversaries.

Left populism, by contrast, must direct that anger upward, toward the structural forces of racial capitalism. But constructing “the people” along these lines is inherently difficult in a society fractured by racial disparities. Getachew highlights the horrifying reality of the COVID-19 pandemic in cities like Chicago and Milwaukee, where African Americans account for 70 to 80 percent of deaths despite making up a fraction of the population. This racialized death toll is not a tragic accident; it is the predictable outcome of decades of redlining, environmental racism, chronic disinvestment in public health, and the disproportionate concentration of Black and Brown workers in low-wage, “essential” jobs that offer no protective gear or hazard pay.

In this context, a left populism that relies solely on colorblind economic universalism will inevitably fail. The Sanders campaign struggled to win over older Black voters in the South not because those voters are inherently opposed to progressive economic policies, but because a populist project must build deep, localized trust to overcome the profound skepticism bred by centuries of structural racism. Left populism cannot simply assume the existence of a unified working class; it must actively forge that unity by acknowledging and fighting the specific, racialized ways that capitalism exploits different communities.

This is where Getachew’s invocation of the Chicago Teachers Union (CTU) is so instructive. The CTU’s strikes in 2012 and 2019 are not merely examples of traditional labor disputes; they are the blueprint for a successful left-populist politics. By engaging in “bargaining for the common good,” the CTU expanded the scope of their demands beyond wages and benefits for their own members. They demanded smaller class sizes, nurses, social workers, and sanctuary protections for undocumented students. In doing so, they bridged the gap between the producer (the teacher) and the consumer (the student and the parent). They effectively drew a populist frontier: on one side was the multiracial working class of Chicago, fighting for the survival of their communities; on the other side was a neoliberal political establishment intent on austerity, school closures, and privatization.

This model of local, rooted solidarity is the foundation upon which any national left-populist project must be built. The pandemic has laid bare the fundamental truth of the CTU’s approach: the well-being of the worker is inextricably linked to the well-being of the community. Today, we are seeing the seeds of this broader struggle in the wildcat strikes and walkouts at Amazon warehouses, Instacart, and meatpacking plants across the country. Workers are risking their livelihoods to demand basic safety protections, exposing the hypocrisy of a system that brands them “essential” while treating them as entirely disposable. A revitalized left populism must capture the energy of these workplace struggles and articulate them as part of a broader fight for a society that values human life over corporate profit.

However, as Getachew astutely observes, the challenge of reclaiming populism is not limited to the domestic sphere. The urgent need to link political realignment at home with a new internationalism presents perhaps the greatest theoretical and practical hurdle for left populism. Historically, populism is a nationalist phenomenon. The very act of defining “the people” usually requires a border. How, then, do we construct a left populism that does not fall into the trap of economic nationalism or xenophobia?

The global nature of the COVID-19 pandemic underscores the absolute necessity of solving this puzzle. The virus does not respect borders, yet the international response has devolved into a grim competition for scarce resources. The United States and other wealthy nations are engaged in a bidding war for ventilators and personal protective equipment, while the Trump administration maintains brutal economic sanctions on countries like Iran and Venezuela, intentionally crippling their ability to respond to the public health crisis.

Frighteningly, the liberal establishment has shown little interest in offering a genuine internationalist alternative. Getachew rightfully condemns the recent campaign ad released by Joe Biden, which attacked Trump from the right on China. By relying on anti-Chinese rhetoric, the presumptive Democratic nominee is validating the very nationalist framing that fuels right-wing populism. This xenophobic posturing not only endangers Asian Americans, who are facing a surge of hate crimes, but it also forecloses the possibility of global cooperation just when we need it most.

A left-populist internationalism must reject this paradigm entirely. It must define “the elite” not as a foreign nation-state, but as the transnational capitalist class—the pharmaceutical executives who fiercely guard intellectual property rights while the world begs for a vaccine; the fossil fuel executives who continue to pollute the planet; the architects of a global supply chain that exploits cheap labor in the Global South to deliver cheap goods to the North. The “people” in this populist equation is the global working class, united by a shared vulnerability to the predations of neoliberalism and the ravages of a warming, disease-stricken planet. Building this international solidarity requires concrete political demands: the immediate lifting of all economic sanctions, the suspension of intellectual property rules for medical supplies, and massive debt relief for the Global South.

Furthermore, the domestic response to the pandemic has permanently altered the terrain of economic debate, creating a unique opening for left-populist policies. For decades, the primary weapon deployed against the left’s vision for a robust welfare state was the disingenuous question: “How will you pay for it?” Whether the demand was Medicare for All, a Green New Deal, or universal higher education, the gatekeepers of neoliberal austerity insisted that the state simply could not afford it.

In the span of a few weeks in March and April 2020, that myth has been pulverized. Congress passed the CARES Act, injecting trillions of dollars into the economy virtually overnight. The Federal Reserve has promised unlimited quantitative easing to prop up the financial markets. The sudden, immense mobilization of state resources proves that the constraints on public spending were never economic; they were entirely political.

This is a dangerous and highly volatile moment. As Getachew notes, quoting Nikhil Singh, the pandemic will not automatically create the social transformation we seek; it only sets the terms. The right is highly adaptable. There is a very real danger of a right-wing drift toward "welfare chauvinism"—a system where a muscular state provides economic relief and social protections exclusively to a favored, racially defined segment of the population, while maintaining brutal exclusion at the borders and in the carceral system. We are already seeing glimpses of this in the way the CARES Act excluded undocumented taxpayers from receiving stimulus checks, effectively weaponizing state aid.

To combat this, the left must aggressively champion a populism of radical inclusion. If universal health care is an obvious reply to a public health crisis, then we must fight for a system that covers every single person within our borders, regardless of immigration status or employment. If mass incarceration is a public health catastrophe, as the exploding infection rates in jails and prisons demonstrate, then decarceration must become a central pillar of our public health strategy.

Ultimately, reclaiming populism is about having the courage to abandon the politics of polite consensus and embrace the necessity of political conflict. The liberal desire for a return to "normalcy" under a Biden administration is a profound misreading of the historical moment. Normalcy was the gig economy; normalcy was half a million people bankrupt every year from medical debt; normalcy was the systematic defunding of public schools and the hyper-militarization of police. Normalcy is what left us entirely defenseless in the face of this pandemic.

We cannot afford to go back. The task ahead is daunting, especially in the wake of recent electoral defeats and in the midst of staggering grief and economic anxiety. But the elements for a multiracial, working-class majority are present. They are visible in the teachers walking picket lines in Chicago, in the warehouse workers walking off the job in New York, and in the millions of people who cast their votes for a political revolution. To reclaim populism is to take those disparate struggles and forge them into a unified political force—one that is finally capable of wresting power from the few and delivering it to the many.
