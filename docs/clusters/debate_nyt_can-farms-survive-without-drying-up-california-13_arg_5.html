Don't Blame the Almond

It has become fashionable to point fingers at California farmers, and especially at almonds, as if a single nut were responsible for the state's predicament. This is convenient scapegoating that lets urban Californians off the hook and ignores the genuine value of what the Central Valley produces.

California grows roughly half of the fruits, vegetables, and nuts consumed in the United States. It is not optional infrastructure; it is the reason American families can afford fresh produce year-round. The same Mediterranean climate that draws people to live in coastal cities is what makes the valley agriculturally unique on the continent. If we drive farming out of California with punitive water policy, that production does not vanish — it moves to Chile, Mexico, or simply disappears from American plates at affordable prices.

The 80 percent figure that gets repeated endlessly is also misleading. It refers to "developed" water, excluding the roughly half of the state's water that flows to environmental purposes. Once you count environmental flows — much of which serves legitimate ecological goals but some of which arguably does not — agriculture's share looks closer to 40 percent. That changes the moral arithmetic considerably.

Farmers have, in fact, sacrificed. Hundreds of thousands of acres were fallowed last year. Surface water deliveries from the federal Central Valley Project to many farms were zero. Workers in Mendota and Huron are unemployed. Brown was not being soft on agriculture; he was acknowledging reality on the ground.

Yes, groundwater overdraft is a serious problem and the new groundwater law was overdue. Yes, drip irrigation should be universal. Yes, some crops are poorly matched to the climate we now have. But the answer is not to dismantle California agriculture in a panic. It is to invest in storage, recycling, and conveyance; to fund the technology transition; and to let farmers adapt as they have always done. The almond is not the enemy.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01California's Central Valley is a unique and essential agricultural resource that provides affordable, year-round produce for the entire United States.
  2. 02Driving farming out of California would not eliminate water demand but would instead shift production to other countries and increase food prices for American families.
  3. 03The claim that agriculture uses 80 percent of the state's water is misleading because it fails to account for the significant portion of water dedicated to environmental purposes.
  4. 04Farmers have already faced severe economic hardships, including zero surface water deliveries, massive land fallowing, and high unemployment in rural communities.
  5. 05The solution to the water crisis lies in investing in infrastructure, recycling, and technology rather than scapegoating specific crops like almonds.
