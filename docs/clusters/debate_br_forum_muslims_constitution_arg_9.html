"To prevent the national security state from being used as an instrument of religious exclusion, the United States must move beyond moral outrage and enact specific statutory and administrative reforms that constrain executive power."

Akbar and Theoharis offer a broad indictment of the national security state. I share much of their concern, but I want to translate the indictment into a more institutional question: what legal reforms would prevent a president such as Donald Trump from turning inherited tools into instruments of religious exclusion? Moral outrage is necessary, but without statutory and administrative redesign, the next crisis will find the same machinery waiting.

The travel ban has prompted many Americans to rediscover the importance of courts. Federal judges have asked whether the executive exceeded statutory authority, denied due process, or acted from unconstitutional religious animus. That scrutiny is welcome. But the lead essay correctly observes that courts have been far less skeptical of ordinary counterterrorism practices. When the government invokes national security, judges often defer. They defer on secrecy, standing, watch lists, prison conditions, material support, border authority, and surveillance. Trump’s crudity may provoke judicial resistance, but a system that depends on presidential subtlety is not a system of rights.

Start with watch lists. The No Fly List and broader terrorism screening databases impose serious burdens without adequate process. A person may miss weddings, funerals, jobs, or religious obligations. A person may be subjected to repeated secondary screening or reputational stigma. In some cases, according to civil liberties litigation, people have been pressured to become informants as the price of relief. This is precisely the kind of administrative power that should trouble both liberals and conservatives: secret criteria, secret evidence, limited notice, and limited correction.

Reform should not be impossible. The government can protect genuinely sensitive intelligence while providing meaningful process. It should notify U.S. persons and lawful permanent residents when they are denied boarding because of watch-list status, except in narrowly defined emergency circumstances. It should provide an unclassified summary of reasons sufficient to permit rebuttal. It should create an independent review body with authority to examine classified evidence and order removal. It should impose periodic review and data-retention limits. And it should prohibit using watch-list status as leverage to recruit informants absent judicial authorization.

Second, border searches and questioning require clearer limits. The border has always been an area of reduced Fourth Amendment protection, but “reduced” cannot mean nonexistent. In an age when phones and laptops contain the archive of a person’s life—religion, politics, medical records, intimate messages, legal work—the old analogy to luggage is obsolete. Customs and Border Protection should need reasonable suspicion for forensic searches of electronic devices and should be barred from questioning citizens and residents about religious belief or political opinion absent specific relevance to a lawful investigation. Congress should codify these limits rather than leaving them to agency discretion.

Third, material support law needs narrowing. The state has a legitimate interest in preventing money, personnel, training, and services from reaching organizations that commit terrorism. But the current framework risks sweeping too broadly, especially where speech and humanitarian work are concerned. Holder v. Humanitarian Law Project upheld restrictions on coordinated advocacy even when the plaintiffs wished to teach lawful conflict-resolution methods. That decision may be constitutional doctrine for now, but Congress can choose a better statute. It should require proof that a defendant intended to further unlawful violence or knew that the support would materially assist such violence. It should protect independent advocacy, journalism, academic research, legal representation, and bona fide humanitarian aid subject to licensing procedures that are timely and reviewable.

Fourth, undercover operations and informant practices need rules. Some stings disrupt real threats. Others appear to cultivate them. The difference matters. A lonely, mentally ill, or impoverished person who talks violently but lacks means may be transformed into a terrorism defendant by an informant who supplies ideology, encouragement, transportation, money, fake explosives, and a plan. Entrapment doctrine has not adequately addressed this problem because courts focus heavily on predisposition, and predisposition can be inferred from ugly speech. But in a free country, speech—even hateful or radical speech—cannot substitute for capacity and intent.

The Department of Justice should require high-level approval before terrorism stings proceed, including written findings that the target has demonstrated intent and steps toward violence independent of government inducement. Courts should permit broader discovery into informant compensation, prior cases, immigration benefits, criminal charges, and instructions. Juries should be told more clearly that government creation of opportunity is different from government creation of crime. Congress could require annual public reporting on informant use in terrorism cases, with classified annexes where necessary.

Fifth, Countering Violent Extremism programs should be rebuilt or ended. If the purpose is social services, fund social services without intelligence objectives. If the purpose is violence prevention, make the program ideology-neutral and evidence-based. A legitimate prevention model would address white supremacist violence, anti-government militias, and other threats alongside jihadist violence. It would avoid treating religious conservatism, foreign-policy dissent, or adolescent identity struggles as indicators. It would be voluntary, transparent, firewalled from criminal intelligence gathering, and governed by local oversight. If those conditions cannot be met, the program should not exist.

Sixth, prison conditions in terrorism cases deserve scrutiny. Special Administrative Measures, prolonged isolation, restrictive communication rules, and harsh pretrial confinement can impair defense preparation and pressure defendants to plead. Some restrictions may be necessary for prisoners who pose ongoing communication risks, but necessity should be proven, not presumed. Courts should conduct meaningful periodic review. Defense lawyers should be able to challenge restrictions without being forced to litigate blind. The Bureau of Prisons should publish aggregate data on the use and duration of such measures.

Seventh, immigration law must be disentangled from guilt by association. Immigration proceedings already provide fewer protections than criminal trials, and national security allegations can make them still weaker. Secret evidence, broad terrorism-related inadmissibility grounds, and discretionary waivers place enormous power in executive hands. Refugees and asylum seekers who paid ransom, lived under armed groups, or provided coerced support can be trapped by definitions designed for actual militants. Congress should refine these grounds to distinguish coercion, duress, humanitarian necessity, and minimal contact from genuine support for violence.

These reforms may sound technocratic compared with the lead essay’s sweeping critique of Islamophobia. But structure is where prejudice becomes durable. Trump can issue a discriminatory order because immigration law gives presidents vast authority. Agents can harass travelers because border doctrine is permissive. Prosecutors can threaten crushing sentences because material support law is broad. Agencies can stigmatize communities because grant programs lack safeguards. Judges can defer because Congress has not required transparency. Bigotry does not need to announce itself when institutions have already been designed to operate in darkness.

At the same time, reform must be guided by a principle broader than efficiency: Muslims are not a suspect class to be managed. This means rejecting the idea that rights depend on a person’s usefulness to American security objectives. The interpreter who served U.S. forces deserves protection. So does the imam who opposed the Iraq War. The student who studies conservative theology deserves protection. So does the activist who criticizes drones. A due-process system worthy of the name must protect the unsympathetic as well as the sympathetic.

The lead essay criticizes “innocence” as a limiting frame, and that criticism should shape legal advocacy. Courts often respond to plaintiffs who look blameless: infants detained at airports, decorated veterans, elderly grandparents, respected professors. Such plaintiffs are important, but doctrine built around their innocence may not help the next person, whose politics are unpopular or whose associations are complicated. The law should not ask whether the target is admirable. It should ask whether the government has evidence, lawful authority, and fair procedures.

There is one place where I would qualify Akbar and Theoharis’s argument. Government must retain capacity to prevent terrorism. A reform agenda that ignores the reality of political violence will not command public trust and may not deserve it. The point is not to blind the state. It is to make it see individuals rather than categories, evidence rather than atmospherics, conduct rather than identity. Prevention can be compatible with liberty only if it is bounded by law.

That requires legislative courage. Since September 11, Congress has too often preferred symbolic toughness to constitutional maintenance. Members denounce discrimination while praising watch lists when convenient. They criticize executive overreach when the other party controls the White House but expand authority when their own party does. The Trump presidency should end this irresponsibility. If lawmakers fear what Trump can do with inherited powers, they should reduce those powers.

It also requires public consistency. Many liberals rightly object to a Muslim ban but supported using the No Fly List to restrict gun purchases without first ensuring due process. Many conservatives distrust administrative power in economic regulation but tolerate it in national security. Both sides should recognize the pattern: rights erode when the government acts secretly against unpopular people in the name of safety.

The airport protests were a democratic alarm bell. But alarms are not architecture. The task now is to build legal structures that do not depend on benevolent presidents, restrained agents, or heroic judges. The United States needs transparent watch-list procedures, narrower material support laws, limits on digital border searches, regulated informant practices, non-stigmatizing prevention programs, humane confinement standards, and immigration rules that distinguish coercion from culpability.

Akbar and Theoharis ask us not merely to resist Trump’s ban but to confront the system that made it possible. The best answer is both principled and practical: dismantle the parts that cannot be reconciled with equal citizenship, and discipline the rest through law. A republic should not ask Muslims to trust that power will be used gently. It should deny government the power to make suspicion a way of life.

  5. 05Countering Violent Extremism programs should either be rebuilt to be ideology-neutral and firewalled from intelligence gathering or be abolished entirely.
  6. 06Prison conditions and immigration proceedings must be reformed to ensure that national security concerns do not bypass due process or rely on secret evidence and guilt by association.
  7. 07Legal protections must be applied universally to all individuals based on evidence and fair procedures rather than being contingent on a person's perceived 'innocence' or utility to the state.
