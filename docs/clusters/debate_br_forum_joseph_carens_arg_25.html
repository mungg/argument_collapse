Diversified Claude

"The moral case for granting amnesty to long-term irregular migrants is reinforced by existing American legal precedents and the practical necessity of a functional immigration system."

Joseph Carens has written the rare philosophical essay that actually changes the way one thinks about a familiar problem. By the time one finishes reading about Miguel Sanchez, Marguerite Grimmond, and Hiu Lui Ng, the burden of proof has shifted: it is no longer enough to invoke "the rule of law" to justify deportation. The defender of removal has to explain why the law's interest grows stronger as the human cost grows greater. He cannot do it.

I want to add one consideration that strengthens Carens' case in a way he does not quite emphasize: the constitutional logic of the United States already endorses the principle he defends. Plyler v. Doe (1982) held that the children of undocumented migrants could not be denied public education, and the Court's reasoning was precisely about social membership. Justice Brennan wrote that creating an "underclass" of people raised within American society, denied the means to participate in it, would offend basic constitutional values. The Court did not stop to inquire whether these children's parents had been law-abiding in their manner of entry. It treated the children's presence in the community as the morally salient fact. Carens' position generalizes the Plyler logic from children to long-resident adults.

The same logic runs through American statutory law in places we have forgotten. Carens mentions the Attorney General's old discretionary authority to suspend deportation for ten-year residents. There used to be more such doors. "Registry," still on the books, allows certain very long-resident noncitizens to apply for lawful status, although the cutoff date has been frozen at 1972 since 1986 and the provision has become a dead letter. The 1986 IRCA amnesty itself was premised on length of residence (continuous since January 1, 1982). The Nicaraguan Adjustment and Central American Relief Act of 1997, the Haitian Refugee Immigration Fairness Act of 1998, and the various Cuban adjustments all rest on the intuition that prolonged settlement transforms an irregular migrant's claim. America has done this many times. It works. The republic has not collapsed.

The objection that amnesty "rewards lawbreaking" deserves a sharper response than Carens gives it. The phrase smuggles in a particular theory of immigration violation — that crossing a border without permission is wrong in the way fraud or assault is wrong. But this theory is false. Crossing a border to take a job is malum prohibitum, not malum in se. There is no victim. The violation consists in the failure to obtain a permission slip from a state that, in most cases, would never have issued one. To call Miguel Sanchez a criminal is to elevate a bureaucratic infraction to a moral category it cannot bear. Carens hints at this with his traffic-law analogy, which I find apt. We do not refer to "illegal drivers." We do not say that speeding tickets reward lawbreaking when we let them lapse after a few years.

The "fairness to those waiting in line" argument is even weaker. As Carens points out, for most of the people we are talking about there is no line. The notion that millions of Mexican farm workers and Salvadoran cooks could simply have applied at the consulate and waited their turn is an American fairy tale. Our employment-based immigration system caps unskilled visas at a few thousand a year. The Diversity Visa lottery offers worse odds than most slot machines. To tell a man whose family is starving that he should "follow the rules" when the rules contain no rule for him is not a defense of fairness; it is a refusal of moral seriousness.

What I would add to Carens' argument is a structural point about the relationship between amnesty and enforcement. Carens treats amnesty as a constraint on enforcement, and so it is. But there is also a practical synergy: a workable enforcement regime requires a workable legalization mechanism. The reason American immigration enforcement is dysfunctional is that we have built a vast removal apparatus on top of an economy that depends on unauthorized labor and a legal system that offers most of those workers no path to status. Enforcement against eleven million people is impossible; selective enforcement is arbitrary and cruel; what we have is the worst of both. Establishing an automatic legalization track at five or seven years would not undermine enforcement. It would make enforcement possible, by reducing the population subject to it to a manageable size and by separating the people we actually want to remove (recent arrivals, criminals) from those we have effectively accepted.

One disagreement. Carens is too confident that the prospect of long-run legalization will not function as a "magnet." It probably does function as some kind of inducement, though probably a small one compared with the wage differential and the demand for labor. The honest answer is not that legalization has no incentive effect but that we should be willing to tolerate a modest incentive effect in exchange for not living in a country that deports grandfathers. Every just policy has costs. The question is whether the alternative — a permanent shadow population, perpetually deportable — is morally tolerable. It is not.

The Obama administration has now been in office for a hundred days. It has signaled that comprehensive immigration reform is on its second-term agenda but has continued, and in some respects accelerated, the worksite raids and detention practices of its predecessor. Hiu Lui Ng died in ICE custody less than a year ago. Postville's meatpacking workers were marched through a makeshift court in fast-food shackles last summer. These are not the practices of a country that has worked out the moral basics of immigration enforcement. Carens has written the essay that the next round of reform debate ought to start from. The principle he defends — that time creates rights — is not radical. It is recognizably American. We have only forgotten it.

  2. 02American statutory history, including the 1986 amnesty and various adjustment acts, demonstrates a long-standing tradition of recognizing that prolonged settlement transforms a migrant's status.
  3. 03The 'lawbreaking' objection is invalid because unauthorized entry is a bureaucratic infraction (malum prohibitum) rather than an inherently immoral act (malum in se).
  4. 04The argument that amnesty is unfair to those 'waiting in line' is a fallacy because no realistic legal path exists for the majority of unskilled migrant workers.
  5. 05Establishing an automatic legalization track would actually improve enforcement by allowing the state to focus its limited resources on recent arrivals and actual criminals rather than settled residents.
  6. 06While amnesty may act as a minor 'magnet' for future migration, this is a tolerable cost compared to the moral catastrophe of maintaining a permanent, deportable shadow population.
