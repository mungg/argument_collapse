"The United States should reject gender quotas and positive-rights mandates in favor of its traditional negative-rights framework, which protects individual liberty and voter choice while allowing women to achieve political progress through merit."

Jennifer Piscopo’s essay elegantly distills a century of feminist struggle into a call for positive action, but in doing so it sidesteps the foundational principle that has, however imperfectly, expanded liberty for all: the negative-rights framework is not a bug of the American constitutional order—it is its essential virtue. The argument that the United States must now adopt gender quotas because the right to vote did not automatically deliver the right to be elected confuses equality of opportunity with a guarantee of equal outcomes, and it asks us to abandon the very individualism that has allowed women to achieve historic breakthroughs without quotas.

Start with the facts on the ground. In August 2020, the Speaker of the U.S. House is a woman. A major party has just nominated a woman of color as its vice-presidential candidate, a few years after running the first woman at the top of the ticket. Record numbers of women sit in Congress, and the 2018 midterms were driven by female candidates and donors. None of this required a single legislative seat reservation or candidate quota. It was achieved within a system that treats candidacy as an individual right, and voters as free to choose whomever they wish. To argue that this progress is insufficient because the U.S. ranks seventy-sixth globally for women’s legislative representation is to mistake an international scorecard for a democratic principle. Many of the countries that outrank us—Rwanda, Mexico, Afghanistan—have achieved their numbers through top-down mandates that suppress genuine voter choice. Rwanda’s women-dominated parliament sits in a political system that is not free in any meaningful sense. That is not a model to emulate.

Piscopo contends that the right to candidacy, as interpreted by U.S. courts, is merely negative, and thus inadequate. But removing barriers is precisely the point. The Supreme Court in *Lubin v. Panish* struck down prohibitive filing fees; in *Williams v. Rhodes* it threw out onerous signature requirements. These decisions protect the outsider, the insurgent, the woman who does not have the backing of party bosses—the very people who might be shut out by a quota system that privileges party insiders. When we move from removing barriers to reserving places, we convert candidacy into a group entitlement. That logic, once accepted, does not stop at gender. If women deserve seats because they are 50 percent of the population, consistency demands quotas for every historically disadvantaged group, leading to a balkanized politics of identity that is antithetical to the ideal of a colorblind and gender-neutral public square.

The quota advocate’s fallback is instrumentalism: more women in office will produce better policy. Piscopo herself notes that this argument requires women to justify their inclusion in ways men never have. But the instrumental case is also empirically fragile. It elides the fact that women are not a monolithic voting bloc; female legislators span the ideological spectrum and frequently disagree on the very family-friendly and reproductive policies that quotas are supposed to deliver. A conservative woman representing a conservative district will not suddenly vote for abortion rights because she shares a gender with those who do. The case for quotas thus collapses into the very essentialism its proponents claim to reject: it assumes that gender identity, rather than ideas and constituency, drives representation. That is a thin reed on which to build a case for rewriting the rules of democratic competition.

Of course, structural sexism exists. Women face sexist media coverage, online harassment, and double binds around ambition and likability. Those are real and corrosive. But the appropriate response is not to hand women a different set of electoral rules; it is to vigorously enforce civil rights laws, hold social media platforms accountable for harassment, and continue the slow cultural work of changing attitudes. That work is underway, as the #MeToo movement and the unprecedented number of women of all backgrounds stepping forward to run testify. When women run, they win at equal rates to men—a fact that quota proponents themselves cite. That tells us the voters are ready; the remaining challenge is encouraging more women to run, not rigging the nomination process.

The centennial of the Nineteenth Amendment should remind us that the right to vote and the right to run are fundamentally individual liberties that belong to every citizen, not group privileges allocated by the state. The glass ceiling is cracking, election by election, and it is cracking precisely because women have claimed their rights in a system that, for all its flaws, prizes individual merit and voter choice over engineered outcomes. To abandon that framework for a quota regime would be to trade a long, hard road toward genuine equality for a shortcut that undermines the very democratic legitimacy that makes representation meaningful in the first place.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The recent historic breakthroughs of women in U.S. politics demonstrate that significant progress can be achieved within the existing individualistic system without top-down mandates.
  2. 02International rankings that favor countries with quotas are misleading because many of those high-ranking nations lack genuine democratic freedom and suppress voter choice.
  3. 03Shifting from removing barriers to reserving seats transforms candidacy into a group entitlement, which risks balkanizing politics into a system of identity-based quotas.
  4. 04The instrumental argument for quotas is flawed because it relies on gender essentialism and ignores the fact that women are not a monolithic ideological voting bloc.
  5. 05The solution to structural sexism should be found in enforcing civil rights and cultural change rather than rigging the electoral process with different rules for different genders.
  6. 06Quotas undermine democratic legitimacy by prioritizing engineered outcomes over the fundamental principle of individual merit.
