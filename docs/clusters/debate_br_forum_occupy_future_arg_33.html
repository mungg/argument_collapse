"To achieve tangible democratic reform and address extreme inequality, the Occupy movement must transition from public protest to organized electoral participation."

The Boston Review’s forum on Occupy the Future poses vital questions about how to translate the raw energy of the Occupy movement into tangible democratic reform. The disjuncture between our principles of fair play and the realities of extreme inequality is undeniably vast. However, if Occupy is to be remembered as anything more than a fleeting moment of public theater, it must quickly mature past its anti-institutional bias and learn to wield the only currency that ultimately matters in American politics: electoral power.

The lead essay asks how we can build a successful movement. The answer lies in looking at recent history, specifically the political right. Say what you will about the Tea Party, but they understood the mechanics of power. They did not simply camp out in parks holding signs and holding consensus-driven general assemblies. They organized, they mobilized voters, they primaried incumbent politicians, and they effectively took over the machinery of the Republican Party. Within a single election cycle in 2010, they dramatically altered the legislative agenda of the United States Congress.

By contrast, Occupy Wall Street has, to date, maintained a profound skepticism toward electoral politics. There is a romantic attachment to being "outside the system," a refusal to endorse candidates, and a hesitation to articulate specific legislative demands. While this purity may be emotionally satisfying, it is politically sterile.

The institutions that govern our lives—the tax code, financial regulations, labor laws, social safety nets—are not abstractions. They are written by men and women sitting in legislative chambers. If the 99% wants to change the rules of the game, they have to elect the people who write the rules.

Extreme inequality is not a natural disaster; it is a policy choice. The Bush tax cuts, the deregulation of derivatives, the defunding of the SEC—these were political victories won by the 1% because they ruthlessly employ lobbyists and fund campaigns. The only counterweight to organized money is organized people. But those people must be organized into a voting bloc that politicians fear to cross.

As we approach the 2012 elections, Occupy has a unique opportunity. They have successfully shifted the national narrative. President Obama and Democratic leaders are now adopting the rhetoric of inequality and the "fair share." But rhetoric is cheap. Occupy must transition into a durable political infrastructure. This means registering voters, supporting progressive primary challengers against corporate Democrats, and creating a scorecard to hold elected officials accountable on issues like Wall Street reform, student debt relief, and progressive taxation.

Re-democratizing American politics, as the editors suggest, requires participating in it. It requires the messy, compromising, and often frustrating work of building an electoral coalition. The tension between our principles and our practices will not be resolved by occupying parks, which are increasingly being cleared by mayors across the country anyway. It will be resolved by occupying school boards, city councils, statehouses, and the halls of Congress.

  1. 01The Tea Party's success demonstrates that movements only achieve legislative change by mobilizing voters and taking over party machinery.
  2. 02Maintaining a stance outside the political system and refusing to endorse candidates renders a movement politically sterile despite its emotional appeal.

  4. 04Organized people can only serve as a counterweight to organized money if they form a voting bloc that politicians fear to cross.
  5. 05The movement must build a durable political infrastructure focused on registering voters, supporting primary challengers, and holding elected officials accountable through scorecards.
