Diversified MiniMax

"While the Economics for Inclusive Prosperity (EfIP) project correctly distances economics from neoliberalism, it fails to sufficiently address the discipline's historical role in legitimizing inequality or provide a practical theory for how better economic ideas can overcome entrenched political power."

The authors make a strong case that economics is not synonymous with neoliberalism, and that mainstream economists have valuable contributions to make to inclusive prosperity. Their specific policy proposals are thoughtful and well-grounded in economic theory and empirical evidence. The EfIP project represents an important effort to redirect economic research toward questions of distribution and power.

But the essay could be more critical of the discipline's limitations. Economics has developed powerful tools for analyzing individual behavior, market outcomes, and policy trade-offs, but it has been less successful in addressing the structural sources of inequality and the political dynamics that sustain market-friendly policies. The authors acknowledge that "power asymmetries shape our contemporary economy," but they do not adequately explain how those asymmetries arose or how they might be corrected.

The role of economics in producing and legitimizing those asymmetries deserves more attention. The "first-best reasoning" the authors critique was not merely a methodological error; it was also a political resource that served the interests of those who benefited from market-friendly policies. When economists emphasized efficiency over distribution, when they assumed away complications and adverse implications, when they treated markets as natural phenomena rather than social constructions—they provided intellectual cover for policies that served the wealthy and powerful at the expense of everyone else.

This does not mean that economics is irredeemably reactionary. The behavioral revolution, the revival of economic history, and the renewed attention to distribution and power within mainstream economics all represent genuine progress. But the pace of change has been slow, and the institutional structures that shape economic research have proven resistant to transformation.

The authors write that "economics has never produced predetermined policy conclusions" and that "the answer to almost any question in economics is 'it depends.'" This is true, but it cuts both ways. If economics is inherently indeterminate, then it cannot provide the kind of clear guidance that policymakers need. The authors seem to believe that economists can help "lay out the trade-offs" for democratic deliberation, but they do not explain how that deliberation is to be organized or how its conclusions are to be implemented.

What is missing is a theory of change—a account of how better ideas will translate into better policies. The authors advocate for "democratic political economy" but do not explain how to move from the current situation, characterized by profound inequalities of influence and resources, to a political system in which "people's influence on policy is roughly equal." This is not merely an empirical question; it is also a normative one that requires engagement with questions of political philosophy that the authors largely avoid.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The authors underestimate how economic methodologies, such as 'first-best reasoning,' served as political tools to provide intellectual cover for policies benefiting the wealthy.
  2. 02The inherent indeterminacy of economic conclusions—the 'it depends' defense—limits the discipline's ability to provide the clear guidance required by policymakers.
  3. 03The essay lacks a theory of change that explains how academic policy proposals can be implemented within a political system currently defined by profound inequalities of influence.
  4. 04The authors avoid necessary engagement with normative political philosophy, which is required to define and achieve the 'democratic political economy' they advocate for.
