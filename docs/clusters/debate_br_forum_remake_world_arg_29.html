In Defense of "Dehumanization," Cautiously

Walter Johnson opens his essay with a striking claim: that the language of "dehumanization," as applied to slavery, is misleading, harmful, and worth resisting. The argument is provocative, and Johnson develops it with the care his readers have come to expect. But I think he is wrong, or at least more wrong than he is right, about this particular term — and I want to spend my response defending a chastened version of "dehumanization" against his critique.

Johnson's argument against the term has two main parts. First, it is misleading because slavery in fact depended on the human capacities of the enslaved — their labor, their sentience, their fear, their pain. Second, it is harmful because the language imposes on the enslaved a kind of secondary injury: it suggests they were alienated from their humanity, and it places observers in a paternalist position of judging when and whether that humanity has been restored. Both criticisms have force. But I think the force can be acknowledged without abandoning the term, and there are good reasons not to abandon it.

Consider the first criticism. Johnson is right that slavery did not require the enslaved to actually become non-human in any descriptive sense. The slaveholder needed the slave to be human in order to exploit, fear, manipulate, and torture her. But this is not what the term "dehumanization" denotes when philosophers and historians use it carefully. Dehumanization, in the philosophical literature, is typically not a descriptive claim about the actual ontological status of the dehumanized; it is a claim about the perceptual and ideological structure of the dehumanizer. To dehumanize is to perceive, or to ideologically construct, the other as less than fully human — even while continuing to depend on capacities that one's perceptual frame officially denies. The contradiction is internal to the perpetrator, not to the victim.

This is, I think, the structure of most serious accounts of dehumanization in the literature on mass violence — David Livingstone Smith's work, for example, or the older sociological literature on dehumanization in modern warfare. The dehumanizer is engaged in a kind of perceptual self-deception, holding the other as both human (in capacities) and sub-human (in moral standing). The contradiction is not resolved; it is sustained, and it is what allows the violence to proceed. To say that slavery dehumanized the enslaved, on this understanding, is not to say that the enslaved actually became less than human. It is to say that the slaveholder operated within a perceptual and ideological frame that classified them as less than human, even as it depended on their human capacities. This is closer to what historians like Philip Morgan are typically trying to say, even when they put it less precisely.

Now Johnson might respond that even this more carefully formulated version of dehumanization smuggles in a problematic normative claim — namely, that there is a stable category of "the human" against which dehumanization can be measured, and that the project of restoring or affirming the humanity of the enslaved presupposes our ability to occupy that category in a way the enslaved supposedly could not. This is the deeper version of his second criticism, and it is the one I want to engage with most seriously.

There is something right in the worry. The category of "the human" has been a fraught one in Western thought, repeatedly defined in ways that excluded those who did not fit a particular cultural or biological template. The history of "humanism" is, as Sylvia Wynter and others have argued, a history of differentiated humanity, in which "Man" — typically European, male, propertied, rational — has been positioned as the model against which all other humans are measured. To invoke "the human" as the standard against which dehumanization is to be measured is, on this view, already to be operating within a problematic framework.

But notice what happens if we follow this critique all the way down. We end up with a position in which there is no stable notion of the human from which moral claims about slavery can be made. Slavery cannot be called "inhuman" because there is no category of "the human" to contrast it with. The violations of slavery cannot be called violations of human capacity because the category of human capacity is itself ideological. This is, I think, an untenable position. The reason "dehumanization" has persisted as a category in spite of its difficulties is that it names something real and important: the construction of moral hierarchies that classify some human beings as less deserving of moral consideration than others. To abandon the term is to lose a vocabulary we cannot yet do without.

The question, then, is whether we can hold on to the term while addressing Johnson's legitimate concerns. I think we can. The first move is to distinguish carefully between dehumanization-as-description and dehumanization-as-perception. The enslaved were not, descriptively, dehumanized; they remained fully human throughout, in all the capacities that constitute humanity. But they were subjected to a system that perceived and treated them as less than fully human. The dehumanization is in the system, not in the persons. This is a subtle distinction but a crucial one, and it preserves the moral force of the term without imposing on the enslaved the secondary injury Johnson worries about.

The second move is to be careful about the relationship between dehumanization talk and the project of historical recovery. Johnson is right that there is something unsatisfying about a historiography that frames its central task as "discovering" the humanity of the enslaved, as if that humanity were ever in genuine question. But I do not think most contemporary historians are doing exactly this. What they are doing — what Morgan is doing in the passages Johnson quotes — is reconstructing the lifeworlds, agency, creativity, and moral seriousness of the enslaved against an archive that systematically obscured these things. The point is not to prove that the enslaved were human (which no one with any sense doubts) but to give weight, texture, and specificity to lives that the archive was designed to flatten. Johnson conflates these two projects, and the conflation does damage to his argument.

There is also a generational point worth making. The historiographic moves Johnson criticizes — the insistence on the agency and humanity of the enslaved — were developed in the 1960s and 1970s against an older historiography (Phillips, Elkins) that had genuinely treated the enslaved as objects of history rather than its subjects. The work of John Blassingame, Eugene Genovese, Lawrence Levine, and others was a corrective to a tradition that did need correcting. Johnson is in a sense complaining about the success of that corrective: now that the agency of the enslaved is established, perhaps the framework that established it has become a confinement. There is something to this, but it is worth noting that the framework remains contested in some quarters, and that its dismantling in academic discourse may not be received in the same way in public discourse, where the older paternalist framings retain considerable life.

Where I think Johnson is most powerful is in his observation about the implicit liberalism of the "agency" framework — the way it tends to assimilate enslaved action to the model of the bourgeois liberal subject choosing among options. Singing a spiritual, breaking a tool, fomenting a revolution: these can be read as expressions of liberal selfhood, and there is something thin and anachronistic about that reading. The cultural and religious idioms within which enslaved action took place do not reduce to the framework of intentional choice. A richer historiography would attend to the specific moral and cosmological universes the enslaved inhabited — and there has been important work in this direction, by scholars like Stephanie Camp, Walter Rucker, and others.

So my position is this: Johnson is right that the language of agency, humanity, and dehumanization has been deployed in ways that can flatten the specificity of enslaved experience and impose anachronistic frameworks. He is wrong that this requires abandoning the language. A more careful use of these terms — one that distinguishes perceptual from descriptive dehumanization, that does not frame the historian's task as proving humanity, and that attends to the specific cultural idioms of enslaved life — preserves what is valuable in the inherited vocabulary while addressing what is problematic.

There is one final point I want to register, which concerns the politics of vocabulary. Johnson's critique of "dehumanization" is being mounted at a moment when the actual political defense of the humanity of black people remains, in the United States and elsewhere, an ongoing struggle. The Movement for Black Lives takes its very name from an insistence on the basic moral status of black life that significant portions of the polity continue to deny. In this context, the philosophical refinement of our vocabulary needs to be calibrated against the political work that vocabulary continues to do. "Dehumanization" remains a useful word in public discourse because it names, with rhetorical force, a moral failure that is still being committed. To strip it from the vocabulary on philosophical grounds, however well-reasoned, is to risk disarming a discourse that needs the weapons it has.
