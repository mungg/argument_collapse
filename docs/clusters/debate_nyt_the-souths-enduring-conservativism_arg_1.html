"The Deep South's enduring conservatism is the result of a unique modernization process where historical, racial, religious, and cultural factors reinforce one another to create a distinct political identity."

The Deep South’s conservatism is not a historical accident that somehow survived modernization; it is partly a product of the way modernization occurred there. The region industrialized later, with weaker unions, lower wages, fewer large immigrant communities and a political economy built around low taxes, business recruitment and skepticism toward federal labor and welfare regulation. Those conditions did not produce the same white working-class politics that emerged in much of the North and Midwest.

Race remains central, though not always in crude or explicit form. For much of the 20th century, white Southern politics was organized around resistance to federal intervention, first in defense of segregation and later in opposition to busing, affirmative action, voting-rights enforcement and social programs perceived as benefiting “others.” The civil rights revolution shattered the old Democratic South, and the parties gradually sorted themselves: black voters became overwhelmingly Democratic, while many white voters moved Republican. That racial sorting reinforced ideological sorting.

Religion also matters. White evangelical Protestantism is more institutionally powerful in the Deep South than in most other regions. Churches are not just places of worship; they are social networks, moral authorities and political information systems. On abortion, same-sex marriage, school prayer and gender roles, they help sustain a worldview in which national liberalism appears alien and threatening.

Finally, conservatism in the South is cultural as well as economic. Many white voters see federal power through a long regional memory of outside coercion, even when they rely on federal spending. Appeals to self-reliance, gun rights, military service and local control resonate because they are tied to identity.

None of this means the South is static. Atlanta, Charlotte, the Research Triangle and minority population growth complicate the picture. But among white working-class voters, history, religion, race and party identity still reinforce one another with unusual force.

"The South's enduring conservatism is the result of a layered history involving religion, race, and economic geography that modern development has failed to dissolve."

The South's distinctiveness isn't a puzzle so much as the residue of a long, layered history that economic modernization has not dissolved. Three forces, working together, explain the persistence.

First, religion. The Deep South remains the most evangelical region in the country, and white evangelical Protestantism since the 1970s has been tightly fused to a politics of social traditionalism—on abortion, gay marriage, school prayer, gender roles. PRRI's own surveys consistently show that religiosity, more than income, predicts conservative positioning among Southern whites. A working-class voter in Birmingham attends church at rates a counterpart in Akron does not.

Second, the unfinished business of racial realignment. The Voting Rights Act and the Southern Strategy converted the Solid Democratic South into a Solid Republican one, but the conversion was generational. As V.O. Key argued decades ago, Southern politics has long been organized around race, and scholars like Earl and Merle Black have shown that white racial conservatism still structures partisanship below the Mason-Dixon line in ways it doesn't elsewhere. Obama's presidency has, if anything, sharpened that cleavage.

Third, political economy and geography. The "New South" boom enriched Atlanta, Charlotte, and the Research Triangle, but vast stretches of Mississippi, Alabama, and rural Georgia were bypassed. Union density collapsed, populist economic appeals withered, and right-to-work norms hardened. Working-class whites there encounter Democrats as cultural strangers, not as economic allies.

Post-racial New South notwithstanding, religion, race, and a stunted labor tradition continue to make the region's politics genuinely exceptional.

The persistence of Southern conservatism defies simple explanation, rooted in a confluence of historical memory, cultural identity, and economic structure that has proven remarkably durable. The Civil War and Reconstruction left deep psychological and institutional scars; Southern whites developed a fierce independence from federal authority that later fused with Republican conservatism. This political identity solidified through the mid-twentieth-century realignment, when Democratic support for civil rights legislation forced a wholesale party switch that left the region's political infrastructure intact under a new partisan banner.

Beyond partisan politics, the South's religious landscape—dominated by evangelical Protestantism with its emphasis on personal morality, individual responsibility, and traditional family structures—provides a cultural framework aligned with conservative ideology. Decades of political strategy have linked Democratic liberalism with federal overreach on race, even as actual racial conditions have evolved. Economic anxieties, particularly among working-class whites facing deindustrialization, easily attach to cultural grievances about changing social norms.

None of these factors exists in isolation. Together they create a self-reinforcing political culture where conservative positions on social issues, economics, and race interlock. Demographic and economic change alone have proven insufficient to dissolve these attachments—identity, memory, and community prove more enduring than pundit predictions of imminent transformation.

The Deep South is more conservative because the Democratic Party’s civil rights achievement came with a political cost it has never recovered from among white voters in the region. That is the blunt answer, and polite euphemisms do not improve it.

From Reconstruction through Jim Crow, Southern politics was organized around white supremacy. For decades, the “Solid South” was Democratic not because it was liberal but because the Republican Party was remembered as the party of Lincoln. When national Democrats embraced civil rights, and when the federal government enforced desegregation, the old arrangement broke apart. The migration of white Southerners into the Republican Party was not instantaneous and not caused by only one issue, but race was the central fault line.

Today’s conservatism is not usually expressed in the language of George Wallace. It is expressed through opposition to “big government,” resentment of “redistribution,” concern about “voter fraud,” anger at “welfare,” and hostility to a president who is seen as alien to traditional America. Not every white Southern conservative is motivated primarily by racial resentment, but the region’s politics cannot be understood without recognizing how racial meanings attach to these terms.

The white working class in the South is particularly Republican because racial identity has often overridden class interest. In many Southern states, conservative economic policies have produced low wages, weak unions, inadequate health systems and underfunded schools. Yet Republican candidates can win by making politics about cultural belonging rather than material bargaining. A white worker may distrust a corporate executive, but he may distrust a liberal coalition of black voters, feminists, secularists, immigrants and professors more.

This is why the “New South” story has always been only half true. The South has new highways, new industries and new suburbs. It has black mayors, integrated universities and a growing professional class. But political modernization did not eliminate the old racial order; it transformed it. Instead of defending segregation openly, politicians defend state sovereignty, tax cuts, punitive criminal justice and resistance to federal social policy.

The South will not remain frozen forever. Demographic change, urbanization and generational replacement matter. But as of 2012, the Deep South’s white working-class conservatism remains a direct descendant of the region’s unresolved racial history.

Something specific to the region is doing work, and the historical candidates for that "something" are not mysterious.

Until commentators are willing to name this honestly, we will keep writing the same puzzled essay every four years, marveling at the South's "distinctiveness" while carefully avoiding the one variable that has organized its politics since Reconstruction.

"The Deep South remains more conservative than the rest of the country because political culture is shaped by deep-seated historical experiences and path dependence that persist despite modern economic and demographic changes."

History is sticky. That is the unfashionable lesson of Southern politics, and it's why each generation of commentators is surprised anew that economic growth and demographic change haven't produced the predicted convergence with the rest of the country.

Consider what we are actually asking the South to do. We are asking a region that lost a war 150 years ago, endured a punitive Reconstruction, built and then was forced to dismantle a comprehensive racial caste system, and watched its agricultural economy collapse twice in the twentieth century — we are asking that region to develop the same political culture as Massachusetts. The wonder is not that the Deep South remains distinctive but that anyone ever expected otherwise.

Path dependence in politics is well established. Robert Putnam's work on Italy showed that the civic patterns of medieval city-states still predict regional governance outcomes today. Closer to home, work by economists like Nathan Nunn has documented that American counties with higher historical concentrations of slavery still have worse contemporary outcomes on a striking range of measures, including political attitudes among white residents. The institutions are gone; the cultural residue persists across generations.

What does this residue look like in practice? A deep skepticism of federal power, born in Reconstruction and renewed by the civil rights era. A populist suspicion of Northern elites that translates easily into suspicion of coastal media, universities, and Democratic politicians. A honor-based culture, documented by Nisbett and Cohen, that responds to perceived insults with disproportionate force — and that finds in modern conservatism a vocabulary of cultural defense. A particular Protestant tradition shaped by frontier revivalism rather than by the more establishmentarian Northern denominations.

None of these things turn off when a Mercedes plant opens in Alabama or when Atlanta builds another light rail line. Economic modernization changes the surface of Southern life; the underlying political culture changes on a much slower clock, measured in generations rather than business cycles.

The PRRI numbers, then, are less a snapshot of 2012 than a long-exposure photograph in which the nineteenth and twentieth centuries are still faintly visible. Expecting them to fade quickly is to misunderstand how political cultures work.

The conventional explanations for Southern conservatism miss the mark. It's not primarily about race, religion, or economic anxiety—it's about institutional path dependencies that made conservative parties the natural home for Southern voters.

Consider: when the national Democratic Party abandoned segregation in the 1960s, Southern whites needed a new political home. The Republican Party, which had been the conservative counterweight to Democratic dominance, was ready to welcome them. Once this realignment happened, it created self-reinforcing institutions—local party organizations, interest groups, media ecosystems—that maintained conservative identification across generations.

Children inherit their parents' party identification. Young conservatives in the South grow up absorbing conservative assumptions from family, churches, schools, and local media. Breaking this pattern requires some catalyzing event—college education, exposure to diverse viewpoints, direct experience of economic failure of conservative policies. Such catalysts are available, but they're not universal.

This institutional explanation suggests that Southern conservatism is more fragile than it appears. When institutions change—when local media dies, when churches decline, when manufacturing communities collapse—the inherited conservative identification may erode. The 2008 election, where Obama won several Southern states, hinted at this possibility.

The "enduring" part of Southern conservatism may prove less enduring than we assume. But in October 2012, we're not there yet.
