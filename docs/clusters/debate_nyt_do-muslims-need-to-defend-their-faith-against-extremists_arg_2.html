Diversified Claude

"The most effective defense of Islam against extremism will come from the leadership and resistance of Muslim women rather than from theological debates led by male clerics."

Let me make a narrower argument: the most important defense of Islam against extremism will come not from theologians but from Muslim women.

ISIS, the Taliban, Boko Haram, and al-Shabaab share a near-obsessive preoccupation with controlling female bodies. The enslavement of Yazidi women, the kidnapping of the Chibok schoolgirls, the shooting of Malala Yousafzai, the public floggings, the forced marriages — these are not incidental to the ideology. They are its core. Wherever this strain of Islamism takes power, the first thing it does is close the schools to girls and confine women to the home.

This is why female Muslim voices are uniquely powerful in the counter-argument. When Malala speaks about education as an Islamic value, she is doing something a male cleric cannot do, however learned. When Pakistani lawyer Asma Jahangir defends religious minorities, when Mona Eltahawy writes about misogyny in the Arab world, when ordinary mothers in Tunisia or Turkey or Bangladesh organize against extremist creep in their schools and neighborhoods — they are not just resisting the extremists politically. They are demonstrating, by their existence and authority, that the extremists' vision of Islam is a vision that requires erasing half the ummah.

The reform of Islam, if it comes, will not be a doctrinal reform led from above. It will be a sociological reform, driven by the simple fact that Muslim women — increasingly educated, urbanized, and connected — are not going back to the seventh century, and any version of the faith that requires them to do so will lose them. ISIS knows this, which is why its recruitment of Western women is so disproportionately important to its propaganda: it needs to manufacture consent for a project that the vast majority of Muslim women reject.

So when we ask how Muslims should defend their faith, we should ask in particular how we can support the women who are already doing it — often at enormous personal risk. Funding girls' education, protecting female activists from blasphemy laws, amplifying their voices: this is the practical agenda. Theology will follow demography. It usually does.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Extremist ideologies like those of ISIS and the Taliban are fundamentally centered on the control and erasure of women, making female resistance a direct challenge to their core mission.
  2. 02Female Muslim voices possess a unique authority to demonstrate that extremist interpretations of the faith are incompatible with the existence and rights of half the Muslim population.
  3. 03The reform of Islam will be a sociological shift driven by the reality that educated and connected Muslim women will no longer accept archaic, restrictive versions of the faith.
  4. 04Defending Islam requires practical support for women on the ground, such as funding girls' education and protecting female activists, because theological change follows demographic and social progress.
