Diversified Claude

"Stephen Walt’s proposed 'grand bargain' for nuclear nonproliferation is fundamentally misconceived because it overestimates the impact of American concessions on the behavior of rogue states and fails to address the technical realities of modern proliferation."

Walt's essay is a serious effort, and on the nuclear question — which he rightly identifies as the gravest threat to American security — he proposes a "grand bargain" that I want to examine closely. I think the bargain is misconceived in important ways, and that getting nonproliferation right requires a different framework than the one he provides.

Walt's proposal, in outline, is: the United States agrees to (1) abandon plans for new nuclear weapons, (2) significantly reduce its existing arsenal toward "a few hundred warheads," and (3) sign nonaggression pacts with so-called rogue states, in exchange for (a) verifiable abandonment of nuclear ambitions by states like Iran and North Korea, (b) a strengthened nonproliferation regime, and (c) intensified efforts against nuclear smuggling and loose materials.

The diagnosis underlying this bargain is partially correct. The United States has been a poor steward of its Article VI obligations. The 2002 Nuclear Posture Review, with its talk of new tactical weapons and "earth-penetrators" for hardened targets, has done genuine damage to the moral authority on which any nonproliferation regime depends. El Baradei's cigarette analogy lands because it is fair. If we want others to forgo what we ourselves treat as indispensable, we have to demonstrate that we are reducing our own dependence on these weapons in a credible, sustained way. On this point Walt is right and the Bush administration has been wrong.

But Walt's framework has three problems that I want to identify.

First, the bargain treats the demand for nuclear weapons as fundamentally a response to American behavior. If we are less threatening, the argument goes, others will have less reason to seek deterrents. But this misreads the history of proliferation. The countries that have most actively pursued nuclear weapons in recent decades — Pakistan, India, North Korea, Iran, Iraq, Libya before its reversal — have done so for reasons that have little to do with American behavior toward them. Pakistan went nuclear because India did. India went nuclear because of China and because of postcolonial prestige imperatives. North Korea pursued the bomb under Kim Il-Sung in the 1980s when American policy toward Pyongyang was, if anything, neglectful rather than threatening. Iran's program began under the Shah, was paused under Khomeini, and resumed in the 1990s under Rafsanjani and Khatami — pragmatists and reformers, not the regimes most plausibly described as facing American regime-change threats. Libya pursued WMDs because Qaddafi wanted them, not because the United States pushed him toward them; and gave them up only when the strategic calculus shifted decisively, partly through coercive pressure.

The implication: even if the United States offered nonaggression pacts and dramatically reduced its arsenal, the demand for nuclear weapons in these states would not disappear. Iran's clerical regime is not seeking the bomb because it fears American preventive war; it is seeking the bomb because it seeks regional hegemony, deterrence against Israel, and immunity from outside interference in its domestic affairs. North Korea wants the bomb because the regime believes (correctly, in light of the Iraqi precedent) that nuclear weapons are the most reliable insurance policy for its survival. A nonaggression pact from Washington might be useful at the margin, but it will not address the deeper drivers.

Second, the bargain understates the verification problem. Walt asks the United States to make concrete, irreversible concessions — dismantling warheads, abandoning weapons programs, signing binding pacts — in exchange for "verifiable abandonment" by states whose record on transparency is, to put it mildly, problematic. The Iranian dossier of the past three years has been one of repeated revelations of undeclared facilities — Natanz, Arak, Lavizan — that the IAEA discovered through intelligence rather than through declared inspections. The North Korean experience is even worse: the Agreed Framework of 1994 was unraveled in 2002 when we discovered an undeclared enrichment program operating in parallel to the frozen Yongbyon facility. The lesson of both cases is that "verifiable abandonment" is harder than the phrase suggests, and that any bargain structured as American concessions for adversary verifiable behavior is structurally biased toward our disappointment.

Third, and most fundamentally, the bargain treats nonproliferation as a transactional relationship between sovereign states. But the most worrying proliferation pathway is no longer state-to-state. It is the A.Q. Khan network and its successors. Khan demonstrated that a single individual with access to enrichment technology and weapons designs could, over twenty years, transfer the makings of a nuclear weapon to multiple regimes outside any state-to-state framework. The post-Khan world requires us to think about proliferation as a problem of materials, networks, scientists, and front companies — not just of regimes pursuing programs. The grand bargain Walt describes does not really address this pathway, because Khan's customers were not states bargaining for nonaggression pacts; they were regimes pursuing nuclear capability through clandestine channels.

What should we do instead? I would propose a different framework, which I'll call "differentiated containment" of proliferation. Its elements:

First, accept that some current proliferators are nearly past the point of reversal and focus on managing the consequences. North Korea probably has a small number of weapons; Iran will likely cross the threshold within a decade unless something dramatic intervenes. The question is not how to roll these programs back, which may be impossible, but how to deter their use, prevent further proliferation outward, and limit the regional consequences.

Second, dramatically expand the Proliferation Security Initiative and related interdiction efforts. The PSI is one of the genuinely creative innovations of recent American policy, and it deserves bipartisan support. Walt mentions it favorably but does not give it the centrality it deserves.

Third, do exactly what Walt recommends on Article IV — close the loophole that allows non-weapons states to develop the full fuel cycle under the guise of peaceful nuclear energy. This is a critical structural reform, and it requires American leadership.

Fourth, accelerate cooperative threat reduction. The Nunn-Lugar program has been one of the most cost-effective security investments in American history. It should be expanded, extended beyond the former Soviet Union, and protected from the budget axe.

Fifth, on the American arsenal: yes, reduce, but do not naively assume that reductions buy us reciprocal behavior. Reduce because deep reductions serve our own strategic interests, not because they purchase Iranian or North Korean restraint.

The threat of nuclear terrorism is real, and Walt is right to elevate it. But the response cannot be a grand bargain that overpromises what unilateral American concessions can achieve. It must be a relentless, technical, sustained effort to secure materials, interdict transfers, harden borders, and deter use. That effort requires more American engagement, not less.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01The demand for nuclear weapons in states like Iran and North Korea is driven by regional ambitions and internal survival imperatives rather than a reaction to American military posturing.
  2. 02The 'grand bargain' is structurally flawed because it requires irreversible American concessions in exchange for 'verifiable abandonment' from regimes with long histories of clandestine activity and deception.
  3. 03Walt’s framework focuses on state-to-state transactions and fails to address the primary modern threat of non-state proliferation networks, such as the A.Q. Khan model.
  4. 04Effective nonproliferation requires 'differentiated containment'—a strategy focused on securing materials, interdicting transfers, and closing treaty loopholes rather than seeking a singular diplomatic trade-off.
  5. 05While the United States should reduce its nuclear arsenal to regain moral authority, it must do so for its own strategic interests rather than under the naive assumption that it will purchase restraint from adversaries.
