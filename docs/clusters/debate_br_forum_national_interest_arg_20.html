Diversified Claude

"While offshore balancing is a useful corrective for many regions, it is an inadequate grand strategy for managing the rise of China, which requires a more sustained and forward-leaning alliance-based presence in East Asia."

Walt's essay is admirable in its sweep and useful as a corrective to the present administration's strategic confusion. But it suffers from a striking blind spot: China. The word appears, almost in passing, three or four times in nine thousand words. The most consequential geopolitical fact of the next half-century gets a footnote.

Let me sketch the problem Walt elides. China is now the second-largest economy in the world by purchasing-power parity and rising rapidly in nominal terms. Its defense budget has grown at double-digit annual rates for over a decade. Its naval procurement is shifting from a green-water coastal force to a blue-water force capable of operating in the Western Pacific and beyond. Its diplomats are quietly displacing American influence in Southeast Asia, in parts of Africa, and in Latin America. Its leadership has been consistent about the eventual reintegration of Taiwan and consistent about the long-term goal of restoring China's historical centrality in East Asia. None of this is hostile in the manner of Cold War Soviet behavior; it is something more interesting and harder to manage — the methodical, patient assertion of a returning great power.

How does Walt's offshore-balancing framework handle this? He proposes maintaining "a significant military presence in Asia (primarily air and naval forces)" and building "cooperative security partnerships" as the foundation for a future containment effort "in the event that China's rising power eventually leads to a more ambitious attempt to establish a hegemonic position in East Asia." This is sensible as far as it goes. But it understates the difficulty of three problems that the offshore framework is not well designed to solve.

First, the timing problem. Walt's strategy is to wait until a hegemonic challenge materializes and then to organize regional balancing against it. But hegemonic challenges, when they come, tend to come fast. By the time China's intentions are unambiguous, the local correlation of forces may have shifted enough that balancing becomes very expensive or impossible. The classic example is the British failure to deter German naval expansion before 1914. The Royal Navy was preeminent; the offshore balancer was on the case; and yet the structural shift happened anyway. A purely reactive posture forfeits the chance to shape Chinese choices while they are still being made.

Second, the local-allies problem. Walt assumes that East Asian states will balance against rising Chinese power. Some will; some are. Japan is rearming gradually. India is hedging. Vietnam is interested in better relations with us. But Walt's framework underestimates the alternative response, which is bandwagoning — the tendency of weaker states facing a powerful neighbor to accommodate that neighbor rather than to resist. South Korea is already drifting in this direction, both because of generational change in Korean attitudes toward the United States and because of the looming reality that China is now South Korea's largest trading partner. Australia is openly debating whether its security alliance with us is compatible with its economic dependence on China. The smaller states of Southeast Asia — Thailand, Malaysia, Indonesia, the Philippines — are not eager to be drafted into a containment coalition against the wealthy neighbor whose markets they need. Offshore balancing only works if the locals will balance. They may not.

Third, the Taiwan problem. Walt's framework provides no clear answer about how to handle Taiwan. A direct defense commitment is the kind of forward-leaning posture his framework counsels against. But abandoning Taiwan — letting it fall into Beijing's orbit without resistance — would have cascading effects throughout the region, signaling that American commitments are negotiable and that bandwagoning is the prudent course. The current posture of strategic ambiguity is a kind of compromise, but it is unstable: Chinese capabilities are growing faster than Taiwanese capabilities, and the credibility of our deterrent is eroding with each passing year. Walt does not say what he would do, and I suspect that is because the offshore framework has no good answer.

There is also a deeper conceptual problem with applying offshore balancing to a peer competitor. Offshore balancing presumes that the offshore power is more powerful than any potential regional hegemon and can therefore intervene decisively when needed. This was true of Britain vis-à-vis the European continent in the nineteenth century and is true of the United States today vis-à-vis the Persian Gulf. It is becoming less true of the United States vis-à-vis East Asia. A China that achieves rough parity with us in regional naval and air power — which is plausible by mid-century, perhaps sooner — cannot be balanced from offshore in the classical sense. It will have to be balanced through dense, sustained, forward presence and tight alliances, or it will not be balanced at all.

None of this is an argument for the Bush administration's particular missteps, which have been considerable. The diversion of military attention and intelligence capacity into Iraq has been a strategic gift to Chinese planners, who watched our last decade's preoccupation with the Middle East and concluded that they have a window. Walt is right that we have squandered resources and goodwill. But his prescription — leaner footprint, regional balancing, intervention only against direct threats — would deepen the China problem rather than solve it.

What is needed instead is something Walt's framework cannot quite accommodate: a forward, patient, alliance-based strategy of engagement-plus-hedging toward China specifically, combined with the lighter footprint elsewhere that Walt rightly recommends. We should be doing less in Europe and the Middle East precisely so that we can do more in Asia. We should be deepening security ties with Japan, India, Australia, and Vietnam. We should be welcoming Chinese integration into the global economy while quietly building the military and diplomatic architecture that would constrain a future Chinese bid for regional hegemony. This is not containment of the Cold War variety; it is structural balancing of the kind British strategists practiced toward Wilhelmine Germany — except, one hopes, with a better outcome.

Walt has given us an elegant strategy for the world we wish we lived in. The world we actually inhabit has a rising China at its center, and that fact deserves more than three mentions.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01A purely reactive offshore posture forfeits the opportunity to shape Chinese strategic choices before a hegemonic challenge becomes irreversible.
  2. 02The strategy incorrectly assumes regional actors will naturally balance against China, ignoring the strong economic and political incentives for states to bandwagon with a powerful neighbor.
  3. 03Offshore balancing fails to provide a viable solution for the Taiwan problem, where either a defense commitment or abandonment would undermine the strategy's own goals.
  4. 04The framework's reliance on intervening only when necessary is insufficient against a potential peer competitor that may soon achieve regional military parity with the United States.
  5. 05The United States should reduce its military footprint in Europe and the Middle East specifically to redirect those resources toward a robust, forward-leaning security architecture in Asia.
