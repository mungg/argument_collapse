"Agnes Callard’s claim that anger leads to inevitable moral corruption is flawed because it relies on a narrow, retributive framework that ignores the transformative potential of restorative justice."

In "The Reason to Be Angry Forever," Agnes Callard argues that the philosophical attempt to separate the moral utility of anger from its darker manifestations—grudges and vengeance—is a failure. Because the past cannot be altered, the reasons for anger are eternal, validating the grudge. Furthermore, because holding someone accountable requires imposing our moral reality upon them, revenge is justified as the mechanism of accountability. Thus, she concludes, responding to injustice inherently corrupts us. Callard’s argument is provocative and intuitively gripping, but it falls into a critical trap: it assumes an entirely individualistic and zero-sum paradigm of human relationships. By ignoring the profound insights of restorative justice and relational ethics, Callard mischaracterizes the nature of moral repair.

Callard’s "Argument for Revenge" is predicated on a strictly adversarial model of human interaction. She writes: "Your 'bad' is my 'good.' If you are to hold me accountable for this... you make my bad your good. This is the opposite of trying to undo or reverse my action. You hold me accountable by holding onto my theft... forcing my own thinking down my throat."

This is an accurate description of the *retributive* impulse, but it is not the only, nor the most rational, way to hold someone accountable. Restorative justice provides a radically different framework—one that is not a "philosopher's fiction" like Nussbaum’s transition anger, but a lived, empirically validated practice used in communities, schools, and justice systems worldwide.

In a restorative framework, accountability is not achieved by turning the offender's "bad" into the victim's "good." Accountability is achieved by requiring the offender to actively participate in the repair of the harm they caused. When a theft occurs, a gap is indeed created, as Callard notes. But the restorative response is not to inflict a mirroring pain upon the thief. It is to bring the thief into a direct encounter with the human cost of their actions, facilitating a process where the thief takes responsibility, makes amends, and works to reintegrate into the moral community.

Callard dismisses apologies and restitution as irrelevant because they "do nothing to cancel or alter the fact that I stole." This reflects a fundamental misunderstanding of what moral repair aims to achieve. The goal of restorative justice is not time travel. It is not trying to erase the physical event of Monday’s theft. The goal is the transformation of the *relationship* going forward.

When Callard argues that a victim has "a reason to be angry forever" because the past is unchangeable, she treats human rationality as entirely backward-looking. But rationality is deeply concerned with the future—with flourishing, with community cohesion, and with psychological well-being. If I hold a grudge forever, refusing to accept sincere restitution and profound personal transformation on the part of the offender, I am not being hyper-rational. I am actively destroying the possibility of future relational harmony. I am prioritizing a sterile fidelity to a past fact over the living reality of present human potential.

To illustrate her point, Callard shares the story of her grandmother, who survived the Holocaust and reacted with furious prejudice against a young German girl decades later. Callard frames this as a tragic but logical consequence of her thesis: the grandmother’s oppression corrupted her, filling her with a justified but immoral anger that precluded innocence.

But a relational perspective allows us to view the grandmother’s reaction not as a logical deduction from the "Argument for Grudges," but as a profound tragedy of unhealed trauma. Extreme trauma fractures the ability to engage in restorative relationality. The grandmother's inability to differentiate between Nazi perpetrators and an innocent child is not a demonstration of anger's "complete correctness." It is a demonstration of how extreme violence shatters the human capacity for forward-looking moral reasoning.

Callard is right that we shouldn't pretend anger is a polite, sanitized emotion. Anger is messy, hot, and often carries the urge for retaliation. But having an urge does not make the urge philosophically justified or inescapable as a rule of action. The very project of human civilization and moral development is to build practices that channel that initial, rightful anger away from the endless cycle of vengeance (which Girard rightly identified as disastrous) and toward constructive repair.

We are not doomed to the moral corruption of eternal vengeance. Through the hard, grueling, and deeply emotional work of restorative justice, we can acknowledge the unchangeable past while choosing to build a different future. We do not have to be good in a bad world by suppressing our anger; we can be good by transforming it.

Sub-arguments
  1. 01Accountability does not require an adversarial imposition of pain but can be achieved by requiring offenders to actively repair the harm they caused.
  2. 02Moral repair aims to transform future relationships and community cohesion rather than attempting the impossible task of erasing past facts.
  3. 03Treating human rationality as purely backward-looking ignores the rational necessity of prioritizing psychological well-being and future flourishing over eternal grudges.
  4. 04The inability to move past anger, as seen in cases of extreme trauma, represents a tragic breakdown of moral reasoning rather than a validation of anger's logical correctness.
  5. 05Human civilization is defined by the capacity to channel the initial urge for retaliation into constructive practices of repair rather than endless cycles of vengeance.
