"Mandatory labeling of genetically engineered crops should be embraced as a tool for democratic transparency and consumer trust rather than dismissed as a mere scare tactic."

Pamela Ronald’s essay rightly criticizes the crude habit of treating every genetically engineered crop as if it posed the same risk. But she is wrong to dismiss mandatory labeling as though it were merely a scare tactic or a boutique marketing scheme. A GMO label need not be a skull and crossbones. It can be a democratic instrument: a modest disclosure about how food was produced in a marketplace where consumers routinely make decisions for reasons that go beyond immediate toxicity.

Ronald says consumers have a right to know about potentially harmful ingredients, not harmless production methods. But American food labels already disclose many facts that are not warnings of danger. Kosher certification, country-of-origin information, “wild-caught,” “cage-free,” “irradiated,” “organic,” and “fair trade” all speak to values, processes, or institutions as much as to measurable nutritional differences. A vegetarian’s wish to avoid animal rennet is not invalid because rennet is safe. A consumer’s wish to avoid foods produced with certain patent regimes, herbicide systems, or transgenic methods is not invalid simply because the food is not poisonous.

Indeed, one reason the GMO debate has become so inflamed is that consumers suspect information is being withheld. When industry spends heavily to defeat labeling initiatives, it confirms the suspicion that there must be something to hide. Scientists then find themselves defending not only a breeding method but a political strategy of opacity. That is a mistake. Transparency can reduce anxiety; secrecy magnifies it.

Of course labels can mislead. A mandatory “contains GMOs” label, standing alone, may suggest that all engineered crops share a hazard. But the answer to bad labeling is better labeling, not no labeling. We could require a neutral phrase: “Produced with genetic engineering.” We could pair it with public education explaining that federal review evaluates specific crops and that the label is not a safety warning. We already manage similar complexities. Irradiated food labeling exists, though irradiation can improve food safety. Organic labels exist, though organic does not mean pesticide-free or nutritionally superior. Consumers can handle nuance if institutions stop treating them as children.

Ronald is also too quick to invoke the Food and Drug Administration as a sufficient guardian. The FDA’s approach to genetically engineered foods has long relied substantially on producer consultation and the concept of “substantial equivalence.” That may often be scientifically reasonable, but it is not a substitute for public legitimacy. In Europe, distrust over food regulation was shaped by crises such as mad cow disease. In the United States, regulators have had their own failures in pharmaceuticals, finance, and environmental protection. It is not irrational for citizens to ask for more disclosure when powerful companies profit from technologies approved by agencies subject to political pressure.

To defend labeling is not to oppose biotechnology. I do not want ringspot virus to destroy Hawaiian papayas. I do not want Ugandan farmers to lose bananas to Xanthomonas wilt if a safe resistant variety can be produced. I do not want children to go blind from vitamin A deficiency while wealthy activists romanticize peasant agriculture from afar. Ronald is persuasive that genetic engineering can reduce insecticide use and can solve breeding problems that conventional methods cannot solve quickly. We should not sabotage public-sector research or frighten countries away from crops that could save harvests and lives.

But labeling and innovation are not enemies. The real enemy is a brittle all-or-nothing politics. Biotechnology advocates often say: “Judge each crop by its traits.” Very well. Then give consumers and citizens enough information to do that. A label could be linked—through packaging, websites, or public databases—to the trait involved: insect resistance, herbicide tolerance, virus resistance, nutritional enhancement. Such disclosure would encourage exactly the case-by-case thinking Ronald favors.

Nor should we pretend that “GMO” concerns are only about molecules. The largest commercial use of genetic engineering in American row crops has been herbicide tolerance. That has changed weed management, seed markets, and farming practices. Some consequences have been positive, including reduced tillage. Others are troubling, including glyphosate-resistant weeds and increased reliance on herbicide packages. Consumers may reasonably wish to signal disapproval of that system even if the soybean oil on the shelf is chemically indistinguishable from conventional oil.

A healthy democracy does not require citizens to prove imminent bodily harm before they may ask how their food is produced. The burden should not be impossible: disclosure should be factual, neutral, and not designed to frighten. But Ronald’s attack on labeling risks reinforcing the very distrust she wants to overcome.

The path forward is transparency plus scientific literacy. Label genetically engineered foods without demonizing them. Fund public breeding. Regulate traits according to actual risk. Punish false claims from activists and companies alike. And trust citizens enough to let them know how their food was made. If the technology is as useful as Ronald argues—and often it is—it should be able to survive the truth on a label.
