"While the actions of the Flight 93 passengers provide a vital standard for democratic defense based on citizen agency and consent, national security should be structured as a layered system that combines accountable government action with the preservation of civic deliberation."

Elaine Scarry has done something both simple and rare: she has asked us to treat September 11 not only as an occasion for grief, vengeance, or commemoration, but as evidence. What did our institutions do? What did they fail to do? Who actually defended American lives? Her juxtaposition of Flight 77 and Flight 93 is morally bracing because it refuses the consoling story that all meaningful defense must come from above. On that morning, the most successful act of defense came from ordinary people who gathered information, deliberated, consented to terrible risk, and acted.

That fact should unsettle us. It should not, however, lead us to suppose that the passengers of Flight 93 provide a sufficient model for national defense. They provide instead a standard by which national defense must be judged: does it preserve citizen agency, public deliberation, and consent, or does it disable them in the name of protecting them?

Scarry is right to identify “speed” as one of the great alibis of the modern national security state. Presidents have gone to war without declarations of war, ordered interventions with only ambiguous congressional authorization, and reserved to themselves the power to initiate nuclear catastrophe, all while invoking a world too fast for constitutional procedure. The argument has always been seductive. Missiles move quickly; crises develop quickly; enemies do not wait for roll-call votes. But the argument has also been elastic enough to cover nearly everything. If a president can act alone because a Soviet missile may arrive in half an hour, why can he also act alone in Vietnam, Panama, Kosovo, or now Iraq, where the danger is described as grave but hardly instantaneous? “Speed” becomes not an exception to constitutionalism but a habit of rule.

September 11 exposed the weakness of that habit. Not because the military pilots were cowardly or incompetent; they plainly were not. The problem was structural. The national defense apparatus had been designed for certain imaginable enemies and certain imaginable attacks, and it was clumsy in the face of a domestic civil aircraft turned into a weapon. Information was fragmented; chains of command were uncertain; no one had clear rules for deciding whether a plane full of civilians could be shot down. The United States had spent decades imagining minutes-long nuclear exchanges yet had not prepared adequately for an hour-long attack using our own aircraft and our own domestic airspace.

But the lesson I draw is not that internal defense should replace external defense. It is that a democratic society needs layered defense, and each layer must be accountable to the public. Passengers on Flight 93 could act because they knew the facts immediately relevant to their fate and because the lives they risked were their own. Their action depended on access to communication, mutual trust, courage, and a rough-and-ready procedure of consent. That is an inspiring model of citizenship. Yet it was also born of extremity. We should not design an aviation security system that depends on passengers having to storm a cockpit. The fact that citizens can become the last line of defense does not absolve government of building better first lines.

Cockpit doors should be strengthened. Screening should be improved. Air marshals may be useful on some flights. Crews should be trained for the post-September 11 reality that hijacking may mean mass murder, not bargaining. Passengers should not be treated as inert cargo; they should be informed, when necessary, as adults capable of judgment. Communication between aircraft and the ground should be preserved and, where possible, improved. These are all “internal” measures in Scarry’s sense, compatible with citizen action rather than hostile to it.

At the same time, external defense cannot simply be dismissed. There may be cases in which fighters can intercept a hostile aircraft, force it away from a target, or shoot it down as a last resort. The consent problem is real and agonizing, but it cannot be solved by pretending that officials will never face it. If an aircraft is moments from striking a nuclear plant, the Capitol, or a crowded city, someone in authority may have to choose between killing those aboard and allowing many more to die. Such a choice should not be hidden in classified rules known only to a few. The public cannot decide the details of an emergency in advance, but it can insist on law: who may authorize such an act, under what evidentiary threshold, with what record, and subject to what later review. Democracy does not require that every tactical decision be made by plebiscite. It does require that the rules governing such decisions be publicly justified.

Scarry’s strongest point concerns not fighter interception but the broader drift of emergency government. The danger since September 11 is that the executive will treat the public as a population to be managed rather than as a citizenry to be engaged. Detentions without names, tribunals with curtailed procedures, secret evidence, and the rapid movement toward war with Iraq all reflect a politics in which citizens are asked to trust rather than to deliberate. We are told that the dangers are too grave for ordinary argument, too complex for public judgment, too urgent for constitutional patience.

But the passengers of Flight 93 did not act because they were shielded from terrible facts. They acted because they were given them. They did not become passive when they learned the truth; they became capable. The same principle applies to the republic. If the administration believes Iraq poses an imminent threat requiring war, it should make its case openly, submit that case to Congress, and accept the discipline of public debate. If the evidence is strong, debate will strengthen the country’s resolve. If it is weak, debate may spare us a grave mistake. Either way, constitutional procedure is not a luxury; it is part of our defense.

Where I hesitate is at Scarry’s suggestion that returning to an egalitarian model of defense may require a renewed draft and a conventional-only military. A draft can democratize the burden of war, but it can also militarize citizenship in ways that should trouble us. A citizen army is not automatically a democratic army; much depends on who commands it, what wars it is asked to fight, and whether dissent is protected. Likewise, nuclear weapons are indeed anti-democratic in their concentration of destructive power, but unilateral abolition is not an easy path to safety in a world where Russia retains thousands of weapons, India and Pakistan have tested nuclear arms, and others may seek them. The goal should be arms control, de-alerting, no-first-use, deep reductions, and ultimately abolition through reciprocal arrangements—not a romantic leap from nuclear monarchy to civic virtue.

Still, Scarry’s essential challenge stands. The question is not whether citizens can personally repel every danger. They cannot. The question is whether our institutions enlarge or diminish the capacity of citizens to know, judge, consent, and act. On September 11, the government’s most elaborate defense structures failed to prevent catastrophe. A group of passengers, linked by phones to loved ones and by duty to strangers, succeeded in preventing something worse. We dishonor them if we turn their courage into a mere patriotic icon while ignoring its political meaning.

The emergency did not suspend citizenship. It revealed it. Our task now is to build a security policy worthy of that revelation: strong enough to protect us, restrained enough to remain constitutional, and democratic enough to trust the people whose lives it claims to defend.

"True homeland security should be built on a model of distributed civic capacity and democratic resilience rather than centralized federal command and executive secrecy."

Elaine Scarry’s argument is framed around national defense, but its deepest insight may be civic rather than military: resilient societies are defended by distributed capacities. On September 11, Americans saw both the failure of centralized systems and the power of ordinary people linked by communication, trust, improvisation, and local knowledge. That lesson applies not only to hijacked aircraft or war powers, but to the broader homeland security agenda now taking shape.

The phrase “homeland security” can suggest command from Washington: a cabinet department, color-coded alerts, federal directives, centralized databases, emergency plans written by experts. Some of that is necessary. Terrorism crosses jurisdictions; intelligence must be coordinated; airports, borders, ports, and public health systems need national standards. But if homeland security becomes only a federal command structure, it will reproduce the weakness Scarry identifies. It will treat citizens as dependents rather than participants, and it will miss the knowledge embedded in communities.

Consider what actually happens in disaster. The first responders are often not federal agents but coworkers, neighbors, passengers, teachers, nurses, firefighters, police officers, transit workers, and bystanders. On September 11, people in the towers helped one another down stairwells. Boat crews evacuated lower Manhattan. Firefighters and police entered danger before any national plan could matter. Families and phone operators became information nodes. In the anthrax attacks that followed, mail handlers, doctors, laboratory workers, journalists, and local public health officials were indispensable to detection and response. A society’s defense is not located in one building, even if that building has five sides.

Scarry is right that Flight 93 reveals the speed of distributed action. The passengers did not wait for a complete official picture. They assembled a usable picture from fragments. They did not need a perfect command hierarchy because they had proximity to the threat and communication with the outside. That is precisely how many emergencies are managed in their first minutes. Central authorities may arrive later with resources, but local actors must interpret ambiguous signs and act.

The policy implication is not that we should abolish hierarchy. It is that we should design hierarchies that support local agency rather than smother it. For homeland security, that means several things.

First, communication systems must be open enough to allow information to flow upward, downward, and sideways. Scarry’s warning about government priority over cellular networks is important. Emergency personnel need reliable communications; the failures among police and firefighters in New York showed that painfully. But citizens also need communication. A person trapped in a building, a passenger on a plane, a doctor seeing unusual symptoms, or a neighbor noticing suspicious activity may possess crucial information. Systems that simply preempt public communication in favor of officials may deprive responders of the very knowledge they need.

Second, public health must be treated as national defense. The anthrax letters exposed weaknesses in laboratory capacity, disease surveillance, risk communication, and coordination among agencies. Unlike a missile attack, a biological attack may unfold slowly, through symptoms first seen in clinics and emergency rooms. The “sensors” are doctors, nurses, pharmacists, veterinarians, and patients. Investing in public health infrastructure—labs, training, reporting networks, hospital surge capacity—is a democratic form of defense because it strengthens capacities that serve people every day, not only in war. It is also less glamorous than weapons systems, which may be why it is neglected.

Third, citizens need honest information. The instinct to avoid panic often leads officials to vague reassurance. But reassurance unsupported by facts breeds distrust. During emergencies, people can handle uncertainty if it is acknowledged. They need to know what is known, what is not known, what they can do, and how recommendations may change. The passengers on Flight 93 were not spared panic by ignorance; they were enabled by truth. Public communication should aim at competence, not calm alone.

Fourth, preparedness should be civic education, not merely instruction in suspicion. There is a danger that homeland security will turn every citizen into an amateur informant scanning neighbors for signs of foreignness. That would corrode the trust on which resilience depends. A better civic preparedness would teach first aid, evacuation planning, emergency communication, recognition of genuine hazards, respect for civil liberties, and ways to help vulnerable people. It would strengthen associations—schools, workplaces, congregations, unions, neighborhood groups—that can respond when official systems are overwhelmed. The goal is a capable public, not a fearful one.

Fifth, burdens should be shared fairly. Emergency policies often fall hardest on immigrants, Muslims, Arabs, South Asians, and other minorities treated as suspect communities. That is morally wrong and operationally foolish. Communities that fear law enforcement are less likely to share information. A democratic defense strategy must protect equal citizenship and legal rights. Trust is infrastructure. Once destroyed, it cannot be quickly purchased with federal grants.

Scarry’s essay also raises the question of war. A resilient republic should be slow to transform homeland security into overseas military campaigns only loosely connected to actual threats. Afghanistan had a clear relation to al Qaeda’s sanctuary, though even there citizens deserved fuller explanation of aims and endpoints. Iraq is different. Saddam Hussein is dangerous, but the public case connecting Iraq to September 11 has not been made. War may create new burdens: retaliation risks, regional instability, occupation responsibilities, and diversion of resources from domestic preparedness. Before Congress authorizes force, it should ask not only whether we can defeat Iraq’s army but whether war will make American communities safer.

This is where Scarry’s critique of centralized speed matters. Washington is now moving quickly toward decisions whose consequences will be lived locally: by soldiers’ families, by reservists called from towns and workplaces, by cities facing budget strains, by immigrants facing suspicion, by public agencies asked to do more with less. Democratic deliberation is not delay for its own sake. It is the process by which dispersed consequences become visible.

I am less persuaded by Scarry’s suggestion that long-term democratic defense requires a return to a draft. A draft may distribute the human burden of war more broadly, but it may also expand state power over bodies in troubling ways. Civic responsibility need not be identical with military service. In the age of terrorism, public health workers, firefighters, engineers, translators, teachers, computer security specialists, and community organizers all contribute to defense. The deeper egalitarian principle is that security should not be outsourced to a narrow professional caste while the rest of society shops, watches, and applauds. But shared service can take many forms.

Similarly, nuclear weapons are anti-democratic in their concentration of authority, but eliminating them will require international strategy as well as moral clarity. De-alerting, arms reductions, test-ban commitments, nonproliferation diplomacy, and stronger control of fissile material are practical steps toward reducing the danger. A resilient society does not confuse maximal destructive power with safety.

The best answer to September 11 is neither a garrison state nor a romantic militia. It is a republic of capable institutions and capable citizens. Federal agencies should coordinate, but not monopolize. Experts should advise, but not mystify. Citizens should participate, but not be abandoned. Rights should be protected, not suspended until further notice. Emergency planning should strengthen the everyday systems—health, communication, transportation, law, local association—that make a society livable in ordinary times and durable in crisis.

Flight 93 teaches that citizenship can survive terror. The question now is whether policy will cultivate that citizenship or replace it with management. If homeland security becomes another name for secrecy, surveillance, and executive war, we will have narrowed our defenses to the instruments that failed us. If it becomes an investment in public competence, lawful authority, honest communication, and shared responsibility, we may build a country harder to terrorize because it is harder to isolate, deceive, or paralyze.

"The government must not use the pretext of national security or the 'argument from speed' to bypass constitutional constraints, public debate, and legal protections, as democratic participation and transparency are essential to intelligent security."

The most dangerous sentence in politics is “there is no time.” Sometimes it is true. Often it is an instrument. Elaine Scarry’s essay asks us to notice how frequently, for fifty years, Americans have been told there is no time for Congress, no time for public debate, no time for ordinary legal protections, no time for consent. September 11 was a day of real emergency. Yet the year since has shown how quickly a real emergency can be transformed into a governing style.

I write as someone who believes the terrorist threat is grave. Al Qaeda murdered thousands of people and would murder more if it could. The state has a duty to prevent that. But the question is not whether government should act. It is whether government may free itself from constitutional constraint whenever it invokes security. Scarry’s answer is no, and that answer deserves emphatic support.

Her account of Flight 93 has a meaning larger than aviation. The passengers were not protected by paternal secrecy. They were protected, insofar as protection remained possible, by communication. They called spouses, parents, friends, operators. They learned what had happened in New York. They asked whether it was true. They compared information. They deliberated. They said goodbye. They acted. Their courage was inseparable from knowledge.

The government’s conduct since September 11 has too often rested on the opposite premise: that the people are safest when they know least. Hundreds of noncitizens have been detained in terrorism-related investigations, many under immigration pretexts, with names withheld and proceedings closed. The Justice Department has argued for broad secrecy. Military tribunals have been proposed for noncitizens designated by the president. Attorney-client communications have come under strain. The USA PATRIOT Act, passed in haste, expanded surveillance and investigative authorities in ways that may contain useful tools but also require vigilant oversight. Citizens are told that asking too many questions aids the enemy.

This is not citizenship in emergency. It is spectatorship in emergency.

The secrecy surrounding detentions is especially corrosive. The ancient safeguard against arbitrary imprisonment is not sentimental; it is practical. If the government can hold people without public accounting, errors multiply and abuses hide. Some detainees may be dangerous. Some may be guilty of immigration violations unrelated to terrorism. Some may be innocent people caught in sweeps. The public cannot know because the executive has claimed the power to decide how much the public may know. That reverses the democratic relation. The government is the servant of the people, not the proprietor of danger.

Noncitizens are often the first to feel emergency powers, but they are rarely the last. A society that tolerates secret detention because the detainees are foreign nationals will find the habit spreading. The same logic—trust us, there is no time, the threat is too grave—can justify surveillance of citizens, disruption of political groups, and punishment without ordinary proof. We have seen versions of this before: the Palmer Raids, Japanese American internment, COINTELPRO, loyalty programs. Each episode was defended as necessary. Each later became a lesson in fear’s capacity to deform law.

Scarry’s critique of executive war-making belongs in the same frame. We are now being prepared for war with Iraq. The administration speaks of weapons of mass destruction, defiance of the United Nations, and possible links between regimes and terrorists. These claims must be examined, not absorbed. Iraq is a brutal dictatorship; Saddam Hussein has used chemical weapons and invaded neighbors. But the question for the United States is whether war now is necessary, lawful, and wise. The question cannot be answered by executive assertion. If Congress authorizes force, it must do so after real debate, not under the emotional shadow of September 11. The hijackers were mostly Saudis; al Qaeda’s camps were in Afghanistan; Iraq’s relation to the attack has not been shown to the public. War cannot be justified by atmospheric association.

The civil-libertarian objection is sometimes caricatured as indifference to security. That is false. Liberty is not the opposite of security. It is one of the conditions of intelligent security. Governments that operate in secrecy make mistakes. Agencies shield themselves from embarrassment. Informants exaggerate. Ethnic and religious stereotypes substitute for evidence. Dissenting analysis is suppressed. Courts, journalists, lawyers, families, and legislators are not obstacles to truth; they are part of the machinery by which truth is tested.

This is why Scarry’s emphasis on distributed knowledge matters. On Flight 93, no single central authority possessed all necessary knowledge. The passengers knew what was happening in the cabin. Relatives knew what had happened on television. Operators kept lines open. The resulting network was imperfect but alive. By contrast, centralized emergency government often narrows information as it rises. Officials receive filtered reports and issue commands to people they do not see. That may be unavoidable in some military contexts, but it is a poor model for a free society.

To be sure, not everything can be public. Intelligence sources may need protection. Ongoing investigations may require confidentiality. Military operations may depend on surprise. Civil libertarians should acknowledge this. But secrecy must be specific and temporary, not a general entitlement. Courts can review classified evidence. Inspectors general can audit programs. Congress can receive reports. Sunset clauses can force reconsideration. The press can investigate without being accused of treason. The burden should be on the government to justify secrecy, not on citizens to justify curiosity.

Scarry’s nuclear argument may seem far from airport security and immigration detention, but it is part of the same moral architecture. Nuclear first use places unimaginable killing power in the hands of one person. It is the purest form of “trust us.” The American people have never meaningfully consented to a policy that allows their president to initiate nuclear war on their behalf. The likely targets, of course, have not consented at all. We have grown accustomed to this arrangement because it is old. Age should not be mistaken for legitimacy. At minimum, first use should be renounced, weapons should be taken off hair-trigger alert, and Congress should assert authority over nuclear policy. No democracy should make annihilation administratively routine.

The objection will come: our enemies do not deliberate, so we cannot afford to deliberate. But that is precisely backward. Our enemies are defined in part by contempt for civilian life and free judgment. If we imitate that contempt in milder form—treating civilians as objects to be managed, legal rights as inconveniences, public debate as weakness—we defend territory while surrendering principle. The point is not to remain pure while doing nothing. The point is to act through law because law is one of the things we are defending.

Flight 93 is often invoked as a story of sacrifice, and rightly so. But it is also a story of democratic competence. Ordinary people, given facts and means of communication, made a decision of unbearable difficulty. The lesson for government is not that citizens should be left to save themselves. It is that citizens should not be disabled in the name of being saved.

We need investigations, trials, intelligence work, international cooperation, and, where necessary, force. We also need names of detainees, access to counsel, open courts wherever possible, congressional authorization for war, public evidence, and limits on executive power. These are not peacetime luxuries. They are emergency necessities. For if citizenship disappears in emergency, it may not return intact when officials announce that the emergency has passed.

"The central lesson of September 11 and Elaine Scarry’s analysis is that national defense must be rooted in the moral authority of informed democratic consent rather than centralized, secretive, or preemptive executive power."

Elaine Scarry places consent at the center of defense, and that is the most radical and necessary feature of her essay. We are accustomed to thinking of national security in terms of capability: weapons, bases, intelligence, readiness, speed. Scarry asks a prior question: who has the moral authority to risk whose life? September 11 forced that question into the open with unbearable clarity. The passengers on Flight 93 risked their own lives to save others. A fighter pilot ordered to shoot down a passenger plane would be risking, or rather taking, the lives of others without their consent. A president who initiates nuclear war risks the lives of millions, including the citizens he claims to protect and the foreign civilians he designates as enemy, without anything resembling consent.

This line of thought is uncomfortable because it exposes a contradiction in ordinary patriotic speech. We say that in a democracy “we” decide questions of war and peace. But in practice the most consequential decisions are concentrated in a few hands. The people may cheer, grieve, pay taxes, enlist, or mourn. They rarely decide. Nuclear weapons make this contradiction grotesque. A single executive decision can unleash violence beyond meaningful democratic repair. What kind of consent can possibly authorize that?

Scarry’s account of Flight 93 matters because it shows consent under the most compressed conditions imaginable. The passengers did not have ideal deliberation. They were terrified, rushed, and trapped. Yet they did several things that political theory treats as essential: they gathered evidence, tested it through multiple sources, consulted one another, considered consequences for strangers on the ground, prayed or prepared themselves according to conscience, and voted. The vote is not a decorative detail. It marks the difference between being sacrificed and choosing sacrifice.

No one should sentimentalize that choice. It was made under coercion created by murderers. It was a choice among horrors. But all morally serious choices in war are made under constraint. The passengers’ decision has authority because those who bore the immediate cost participated in making it. They were not abstractions on a screen, not “collateral damage,” not a number in a classified estimate. They were agents.

Modern war constantly turns agents into objects. From altitude, a city becomes a target set. From a command center, civilians become projected casualties. From a nuclear war plan, entire societies become “assets” to be destroyed or held at risk. The language is designed to protect decision-makers from the reality of bodies. Scarry, whose earlier work has explored pain and the body, understands that democracy begins by refusing this abstraction. People are not merely to be defended; they are the beings whose consent gives defense its legitimacy.

The impending debate over Iraq should be judged by this standard. If the United States goes to war, who consents and who pays? American soldiers will be ordered into danger. Iraqi civilians, already suffering under dictatorship and sanctions, will face bombardment, displacement, and deprivation. American citizens will be told that war is necessary to prevent future danger. Congress may be asked to authorize force. But authorization should not be confused with genuine democratic consent unless the case is public, the evidence tested, the alternatives honestly weighed, and the likely human consequences named. Euphemism is a form of coercion because it deprives citizens of the facts needed to consent.

The administration’s doctrine of preemption, articulated at West Point and in recent national security discussions, is especially troubling. There may be cases in which striking first is genuinely defensive: an attack is imminent, evidence is clear, no alternative exists. But preventive war against possible future threats stretches self-defense into domination. It asks citizens to consent not to repel violence but to trust official predictions about what another state may someday do. When weapons of mass destruction enter the argument, fear can overwhelm judgment. We must insist on precision: what weapons, what evidence, what delivery capacity, what connection to terrorists, what imminence, what alternatives? Without such precision, consent is manufactured rather than obtained.

Scarry’s treatment of nuclear first use should become a central public issue. It is astonishing how little democratic debate surrounds the most destructive power ever created. We debate tax rates, school curricula, and prescription drugs in detail, yet the authority to initiate nuclear war remains enveloped in secrecy and ritual. The president is followed by the “football”; plans exist; missiles stand ready. Citizens are reassured by the professionalism of the system or frightened into silence by the enormity of the subject. But enormity is the reason for debate, not a reason against it.

A democratic nuclear policy would begin with no-first-use. If nuclear weapons are said to deter nuclear attack, then their use should be limited to that purpose, and even that purpose should be constrained by law and morality. To threaten nuclear destruction in response to chemical or biological attack, or to vague support for terrorism, is to make mass killing of civilians thinkable as an administrative option. De-alerting weapons would reduce the tyranny of minutes. Shared authorization procedures would reduce monarchical power. Arms control and deep reductions would move us toward the only morally coherent long-term goal: abolition.

Critics will say this is naïve. They will point to Russia’s arsenal, China’s modernization, India and Pakistan, Iraq’s ambitions, North Korea’s opacity. These dangers are real. But realism that ignores moral psychology is not realistic enough. As long as the United States insists that nuclear weapons are indispensable to its security, other states will draw the same conclusion. As long as we reserve first use, we teach the world that nuclear weapons are instruments of power, not merely deterrents of last resort. Nonproliferation cannot rest forever on a hierarchy in which some states claim eternal entitlement and others are threatened for aspiration.

Consent also illuminates the domestic side of emergency. Secret detentions, closed hearings, surveillance expansion, and emergency control of communications all operate by disabling the governed. They say: you need not know; you need not decide; professionals will manage danger. But people who are not allowed to know cannot meaningfully consent. They can only obey or trust. Trust may be appropriate in limited circumstances, but a political order built on trust in secret power is not democracy. It is guardianship.

There are, of course, hard cases. A passenger plane turned weapon may have to be shot down to prevent greater loss of life. A military operation may require secrecy. A terrorist plot may need to be disrupted before evidence can be presented publicly. Scarry’s consent principle does not eliminate tragedy. It disciplines it. It tells us that when consent cannot be obtained, the burden of justification is at its highest; the authority must be lawful; the evidence must be compelling; the action must be necessary; and accountability afterward must be real. Exceptional necessity must not become routine power.

The passengers of Flight 93 are often praised for saying, in effect, “Let’s roll.” But before action came speech: phone calls, questions, reports, prayers, farewells, a vote. That sequence matters. It is the difference between violence and collective moral action. A democracy facing danger should follow the same order. First facts. Then deliberation. Then consent. Then action, if action is necessary.

If we reverse the order—act first, deliberate later, classify the facts, and call consent unpatriotic—we will have learned the wrong lesson from September 11. The dead of Flight 93 do not command us to be fearless followers. They remind us that even in the narrowest corridor of time, human beings can claim the dignity of judgment. Our national defense should be built not around the fantasy that leaders can save passive citizens, but around the principle that citizens are never merely cargo.
