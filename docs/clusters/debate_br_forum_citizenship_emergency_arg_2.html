"The events of September 11 validate the constitutional necessity of a distributed, citizen-based defense model over the centralized, executive-led system that has been justified by the false premise of 'speed' for fifty years."

Yes, And Further: The Constitutional Case Scarry Doesn't Quite Make

Elaine Scarry's essay is the most important thing written about September 11 in the past year, and almost no one will pay it the attention it deserves. The reason is partly the bluntness of its conclusion — that we should give up nuclear weapons and restore a draft — and partly the difficulty of its premise, which forces the reader to look squarely at a day most Americans would prefer to mythologize rather than analyze. I want to defend her central argument and push it one step further than she takes it.

The central argument, stripped to its frame, is this. For fifty years, the constitutional structure of American war-making has been progressively dismantled in the name of speed. The framers required a congressional declaration of war; we have had none since 1941. The framers rejected a standing executive army in favor of a distributed militia; we now have the largest standing army in the history of the world, available to the president on his sole authority. The framers wanted war-making to be slow, deliberative, public, painful, so that the country would feel the weight of its decisions; we now have war-making that is fast, executive, invisible, anesthetized, and in the nuclear case, genocidal. Each erosion has been excused by the claim that modern weapons move too fast for the Constitution. September 11 ought to disabuse us of that claim. On the day in question, the executive defense apparatus had over an hour to act and could not. A group of citizens with cell phones had twenty-three minutes and succeeded.

This is not, as some will say, an argument from anecdote. It is an argument from structure. Scarry is not claiming that civilians will always succeed and fighter pilots will always fail; she is claiming that a defense apparatus that has detached itself from the population it nominally protects cannot, *in principle*, respond to threats that operate within the population. The Pentagon could not defend the Pentagon because the Pentagon was structurally external to the plane that was about to hit it. The passengers on Flight 93 could defend their plane because they were structurally internal to it. This is the same insight that Madison, Mason, and the other Anti-Federalists pressed in the ratification debates: a defense that floats free of the people becomes both ineffective against the actual threats people face and dangerous to the people themselves.

I want to push beyond Scarry on three points.

First, she is too gentle on the framing of nuclear weapons. She calls them "monarchic weapons" — a useful European term — and notes that they are "wholly external to any powers of consent or dissent." But she stops short of saying what the framing implies: that the existence of presidential first-use authority means that the United States has not, in any meaningful constitutional sense, been a republic since approximately 1947. The capacity to destroy hundreds of millions of human beings on the personal authority of one elected official is incompatible with a Constitution whose central architectural feature is the separation of powers and whose theory of legitimacy rests on the consent of the governed. We have lived for two generations inside a constitutional fiction. September 11 ought to make us notice.

Second, Scarry's draft argument deserves more than the brief mention she gives it. She writes that a return to conventional, distributed defense "will almost certainly mean the return of a draft" and treats this as a cost. It is a cost, but it is also the *mechanism* by which her larger reform would work. A volunteer army recruited disproportionately from the rural poor, fought in countries most Americans cannot find on a map, financed by deficits rather than taxes, and conducted in news cycles that have nothing to do with the experience of the soldiers — this is not an army that Congress will have any difficulty deploying. The whole point of the framers' militia was that war would touch the families of the people who voted for it. Restore that connection, and you restore the friction that makes deliberation possible. The current administration's apparent eagerness for an Iraq war that polls indicate Americans do not particularly want would not survive the prospect that one's own son might be drafted to fight it.

Third, Scarry's argument has implications for the present moment that she only gestures at. As I write, the country is being walked, week by week, into a war against Iraq that has no demonstrated connection to the September 11 attacks, no congressional declaration, no UN authorization, and a doctrinal foundation — "preemption" — that the President announced at West Point this June with language that pointedly did not exclude nuclear weapons. The administration's apparent view is that the events of September 11 have so transformed the security environment that the ordinary constraints of constitutional, international, and prudential deliberation no longer apply. Scarry's essay is a direct rebuke to this view. The events of September 11 did not show that deliberation is impossible. They showed that the executive defense apparatus is fallible and that distributed citizen judgment is sometimes more effective than centralized command. The lesson is the opposite of the one the administration is drawing.

Where I am less sure of Scarry is on the question of what, practically, citizens are supposed to do with this analysis. She calls on us to deliberate, to question, to refuse our seats on the ship that is taking us somewhere we did not buy tickets for. Fine. But the institutional levers of war-making have been steadily removed from citizen reach for half a century. Congressional war powers have atrophied not only because presidents have taken them but because Congress has handed them over, again and again, with the gratitude of an institution relieved of responsibility. The 1973 War Powers Resolution was a feeble effort to claw some back, and even it has been routinely ignored. What would it actually take to restore congressional war powers? Probably a constitutional crisis — a Congress willing to defund a presidential war already underway, a Supreme Court willing to enforce the result. I do not see those conditions being met.

So Scarry's essay leaves me half hopeful and half despairing. Hopeful, because she has named something true that almost no one else is naming: that the architecture of American defense has been disfigured by the cult of speed, and that September 11 exposed the disfigurement. Despairing, because I suspect the country will spend the next decade doubling down on exactly the centralization she warns against, and citing September 11 as the reason. The Pentagon, having failed to defend itself, will now demand the resources to defend everything, and Congress will give them. The lesson of Flight 93 will be reduced to a slogan on a coffee mug.

Still, Scarry has done what an essayist is supposed to do. She has made an argument visible. The rest of us now have the responsibility of acting on it, or of being honest about why we cannot.

Vanilla Claude

"The failure of centralized military defense on September 11 invalidates the 'argument from speed' used to justify executive war-making and necessitates a return to a distributed, democratic model of national defense."

Elaine Scarry has done something rare and valuable: she has taken the temporal arithmetic of September 11 seriously and held it up against the temporal arithmetic that has been used, for half a century, to justify removing the most consequential decisions of war and peace from democratic deliberation. The juxtaposition is devastating. If a fleet of fighter jets, with one hour and twenty-one minutes of warning, could not defend the building that houses our military command, then the casual confidence that we can intercept ICBMs in "a matter of minutes," or that a president must be able to launch nuclear weapons in three minutes without consulting anyone, deserves to be regarded as something closer to liturgy than analysis. I want to affirm the core of her argument while pressing on two places where I think the picture needs complication.

First, the affirmation. The argument from speed has functioned, as Scarry shows, as a kind of trump card in American constitutional debate since 1945. Every constitutional objection to executive war-making, every demand that Congress reclaim its Article I powers, every worry about citizens being reduced to spectators of decisions made on their behalf, has been parried with the same reply: the missiles travel too fast for democracy. September 11 ought to retire that reply. The hijacked planes were slower than missiles, more visible than missiles, accompanied by six layers of corroborating evidence that no incoming warhead would ever generously provide—and still the centralized, top-down defense apparatus could not act in time. If the system cannot meet the easy case, why should we believe its claims about the hard one? Scarry's demand that we re-examine the timetables is not utopian; it is empirical.

Her second move—that the passengers of Flight 93 modeled an alternative, distributed, deliberative form of defense rooted in the original constitutional vision of the militia—is more provocative, and here I want to register both admiration and caution. The reconstruction of the Flight 93 deliberations is genuinely moving, and the structural point is correct: the passengers had what the F-16 pilots did not, namely sensory access to the threat and moral authority over the lives at stake. Informed consent was possible on the plane because the deciders and the dying were the same people. That is exactly the condition that vanishes when the president alone is permitted to authorize the incineration of populations he has never met and who have never consented.

But the analogy between Flight 93 and national defense can be stretched too far. The plane was a self-contained civic unit of forty-odd people facing a single, immediate, sensorily available threat. The polity of 280 million faces threats whose evidence is distributed, classified, ambiguous, and contested. A "town meeting" model for deciding whether to invade Iraq is not impossible—the Constitution in fact prescribes something like it under the name of a congressional declaration of war—but it cannot be expected to deliver the kind of crystalline moral clarity that twenty-three minutes of cell-phone calls produced on Flight 93. Scarry knows this; her short-run prescription is precisely a return to congressional declaration, civil-liberties protections, and citizen access to communications. I would only add that the romance of Flight 93 must not be allowed to obscure how hard genuine democratic deliberation about war actually is, and how easily it can be manipulated when the executive controls the information.

This brings me to the present moment. Scarry is writing while the administration is openly preparing a war against Iraq with no demonstrated connection to the September 11 attacks, while floating doctrines of preemption that—as the West Point speech suggested—seem to leave open the use of nuclear weapons. The congressional resolution now being drafted on the Hill threatens to be a blank check rather than a declaration, the very evasion of deliberation she warns against. If her essay has a single urgent practical implication, it is this: the country is about to repeat, at vastly greater scale, the structural mistake whose smaller version killed 125 people at the Pentagon. The centralized authority is again claiming that speed and secrecy require us to delegate everything; the citizenry is again being asked to sit quietly in its seat.

I would push Scarry's argument one step further. The failure on September 11 was not only a failure of speed but a failure of imagination at the center—a failure that distributed knowledge could have corrected. FAA controllers, airline dispatchers, passengers, and family members all knew pieces of the truth before the Pentagon was hit; what was missing was any structure to aggregate that distributed knowledge into action. A democratic defense is not merely ethically superior; it is epistemically superior, because no centralized authority can match the sensory reach of a citizenry. That is the lesson of Flight 93, and it is the lesson we are now, with terrifying speed, refusing to learn.

Diversified DeepSeek

"The events of September 11 demonstrate that centralized, top-down military structures are structurally ineffective and unconstitutional, necessitating a return to a distributed, egalitarian model of national defense rooted in civilian consent."

Elaine Scarry’s “Citizenship in Emergency” performs an essential act of democratic witness. By juxtaposing the inert machinery of our national security state with the swift, deliberative, and ultimately sacrificial action of the passengers on United Flight 93, she illuminates a truth that official Washington has been loath to face: our country’s defense has been hollowed out by a decades-long drift away from the citizenry and toward an unaccountable executive. Scarry’s argument that the events of September 11 expose the hollowness of the “speed” alibi—the perennial excuse that democratic procedures are too slow for modern warfare—is devastating and, I believe, irrefutable. Her essay is not merely a critique; it is a call to reclaim our constitutional inheritance, and it could not have come at a more urgent hour.

The core of Scarry’s case rests on a meticulous reconstruction of two timelines. On the one hand, American Airlines Flight 77, the plane that struck the Pentagon, gave the military ample warning by the very standards the Pentagon itself has promulgated for decades. One hour and twenty-one minutes of escalating certainty—radio silence, transponder loss, a passenger’s phone call to the Justice Department, radar sightings—could not produce an effective defense. On the other hand, the passengers of United Flight 93, working with far less time and with no access to AWACS or F-16s, managed to deliberate, vote, and act to prevent their plane from becoming a weapon. The contrast is not incidental. It illustrates something profound about the relationship between knowledge, consent, and effective action. The passengers could act because the threat was immediate to their senses, because they could verify information through multiple independent sources, and because they alone possessed the moral authority to decide whether to sacrifice their own lives. The F-16 pilots, by contrast, operated within a chain of command that structurally could not grant them that authority, and they were forced to chase ghosts in an information fog that centralized systems inevitably create.

Scarry’s argument that the military was “unable” to defend the Pentagon is not an indictment of individual pilots or soldiers; it is a structural critique. The very attributes that are touted as the strengths of centralized command—hierarchical decision-making, secrecy, reliance on sophisticated but fragile technical systems—proved to be weaknesses when confronted with an unconventional threat. Meanwhile, the distributed, egalitarian model of the passengers, messy and improvised though it was, succeeded. This inversion of official dogma should shake the foundations of our strategic thinking. For fifty years, we have been told that nuclear weapons and supersonic delivery systems necessitate a hair-trigger presidency, that Congress is too slow, that the people cannot be trusted with the levers of survival. September 11 demonstrated that the reverse is true: the people, acting in concert and informed by open communication, are capable of swifter and more legitimate defense than the state’s remote guardians.

What makes Scarry’s argument so politically potent is the connection she draws between the failure of centralized defense and the ongoing erosion of constitutional government. The doctrine of presidential first use of nuclear weapons is not merely a military posture; it is a direct negation of the republican principle that decisions of war and mass death must be shared by the many, not concentrated in one person. Scarry is right to insist that nuclear weapons are “monarchic weapons,” incompatible with democratic self-governance. The fact that the president can, theoretically within minutes, order the incineration of millions without any prior deliberation with Congress or the citizenry is an ongoing constitutional emergency. The Bush administration’s current bellicosity toward Iraq, which seems to be proceeding with only the most perfunctory gestures toward Congress, is a direct extension of this logic. When a president has genocidal power at his sole command, what need does he have to seek authorization for lesser wars?

Some will charge Scarry with romanticizing the passengers of Flight 93 or with naively suggesting that a militia of cell-phone-wielding citizens could replace the United States military. This misreads her. She is not proposing that we disband the armed forces and hand every citizen a rifle. What she insists upon—and what the Constitution insists upon—is that the military be held “within a civil frame.” This means, at minimum, that wars cannot be initiated without the genuine, informed consent of the people’s representatives after full public debate. It means that weapons whose very existence annihilates the possibility of democratic deliberation—above all, nuclear weapons—must be renounced. And it means that homeland defense should not be designed in ways that systematically exclude and disempower the citizenry, whether by commandeering their cell phones or by arresting foreign residents in secret.

Her call for a return to a draft is a bracing one. A draft forces every family to confront the human cost of military action, ensuring that the decision for war is tested against the intimate knowledge that one’s own children may be sent to kill and die. The all-volunteer force, for all its professionalism, has facilitated the compartmentalization of sacrifice and the public’s disengagement from its own defense. Reinstating conscription would not instantly democratize foreign policy, but it would make it far harder for presidents to launch wars of choice without a broad national consensus.

The most uncomfortable implication of Scarry’s essay is that our entire nuclear posture is not only unconstitutional but also useless as a defense against the kinds of threats we actually face. The billions poured into missile defense and the maintenance of an arsenal that can destroy the world multiple times over did nothing to stop nineteen men with box cutters. Worse, our nuclear stance fuels proliferation, as other nations reasonably conclude that the only way to deter American intervention is to acquire their own doomsday weapons. If we are serious about preventing nuclear terrorism and rolling back the arsenals of rogue states, we must lead by example. The day the United States renounces first use and begins verifiable disarmament is the day we regain the moral and political authority to demand the same of others.

“Citizenship in Emergency” forces us to ask: Who is defending whom, and on whose authority? The passengers on Flight 93 answered that question with their lives. They reclaimed their citizenship in the most extreme circumstances and, in doing so, reminded us that the ultimate responsibility for the republic’s safety rests not in the Pentagon or the White House, but in the consent and courage of the governed. We ignore their lesson at our peril.

Diversified Claude

"The events of September 11 demonstrate that the rhetoric of 'speed' used to justify centralized, authoritarian defense is a form of false consciousness, and we must instead recover a model of defense based on human-scale, democratic deliberation."

Speed, Fear, and the Texture of Democratic Time

What Elaine Scarry has given us, beneath the timelines and the floor plans, is a meditation on time. Her essay is, at its heart, about the difference between two kinds of temporality: the artificial, hyper-compressed time of "minutes" and "hairtriggers" and "supersonic" responses that has structured American defense rhetoric for half a century, and the human, deliberative time in which citizens actually decide what they value and how they want to be defended. The argument is that the first kind of time has crowded out the second, and that the events of September 11 — paradoxically, given that they have been used to accelerate the crowding-out — actually demonstrate the superiority of the second.

This is a striking thesis and I want to take it seriously on its own terms, without getting bogged down in the question of whether fighter jets could have intercepted Flight 77 or whether ballistic missiles are analogous to hijacked airliners. Those are important questions but they are not Scarry's deepest question. Her deepest question is about the kind of time within which a free people can govern itself.

Consider the rhetorical history of "speed" in American defense culture. From the 1950s onward, the official discourse around nuclear weapons has consistently emphasized the impossibility of deliberation. Herman Kahn wrote about "ladders" of escalation that climbed in minutes. RAND analysts modeled crisis interactions in which the side that hesitated lost. The hardware itself — the silo-based ICBM, the submarine-launched ballistic missile, the early-warning satellite — was designed to compress the decision cycle to the point where human judgment became almost vestigial. The Permissive Action Link, the football, the launch-on-warning posture: these are the artifacts of a defense doctrine that has internalized the principle that there is no time. And as Scarry observes, this principle has migrated from the nuclear domain into the conventional one, and from defense doctrine into political culture more generally. There is no time to consult Congress. There is no time to inform the public. There is no time to debate. The bombs are falling, or about to fall, or might be about to fall, and decisions must be made now, by people who cannot explain themselves, on grounds we cannot examine.

What Scarry has noticed, I think correctly, is that this temporal framing is at least partly false consciousness. The actual events of September 11 — which were as close as American defense planners have come in a generation to a literal "matter of minutes" crisis — unfolded over a period within which a great deal of consultation and deliberation could in principle have occurred. The President was reading to schoolchildren. The Vice President was being moved to a secure location. The Secretary of Defense was, by his own later account, helping to carry the wounded out of the Pentagon. Congress was sitting in session. The conditions for genuine deliberation were available; they were not used, partly because the rules for using them had atrophied from disuse and partly because the temporal framing of national defense had convinced everyone involved that deliberation was not part of the job.

If Scarry is right that the speed rhetoric is partly false consciousness, then the response is partly a matter of consciousness change. We need to learn to hear claims of urgency with skepticism, to ask "how much time, exactly, do we have?" when officials say we have none, to insist that the burden of proof for foreclosed deliberation rests on those who would foreclose it. This is a modest demand and an enormous one. It is modest because it does not require any structural reform; it merely requires that citizens take seriously the deliberative obligations they already have. It is enormous because the political culture has spent fifty years training us out of those obligations, and the training has taken.

There is a further dimension to Scarry's temporal argument that I want to draw out. The Flight 93 passengers did not just deliberate; they deliberated *under pressure*, with full awareness of their situation and full awareness that the cost of inaction was probably death. This is the kind of deliberation that political theorists since Aristotle have considered the highest form of practical wisdom — *phronesis*, the capacity to judge well in the particular case. It is not the leisurely deliberation of a Sunday afternoon book club. It is decision under conditions of incomplete information and irrecoverable stakes.

What makes the Flight 93 case extraordinary is not that the passengers had a lot of time. They had twenty-three minutes. What makes it extraordinary is that they used the time they had to perform the full deliberative cycle — gathering information, verifying it, consulting, weighing options, deciding, acting — at human speed rather than at machine speed. They did not surrender their judgment to a central authority that promised to act faster than they could. They did not assume that their best course was to remain passive and trust the system. They acted as citizens.

The contrast with the rest of the American population, including the population sitting in front of televisions on the morning of September 11, is instructive. Most of us did not deliberate. We watched. We absorbed images. We assumed that someone, somewhere, was deciding what to do, and we waited to be told the result. This was not cowardice; it was the appropriate response of citizens who have been trained for half a century to understand themselves as spectators of national defense rather than as participants in it. The Flight 93 passengers had the singular advantage of being unable to behave as spectators, because they were *in* the event.

Scarry's deeper proposal, then, is that we ought to recover the capacity to behave as participants even when we are not in the event. We ought to be able to deliberate about the Iraq war the way the Flight 93 passengers deliberated about their hijackers — gathering information from multiple independent sources, verifying it, consulting widely, weighing options, voting, acting. The current political culture is not configured for this. The information environment is dominated by a small number of media outlets with overlapping interests; the political process is dominated by an executive branch that treats consultation as a courtesy rather than an obligation; the citizenry is configured as audience rather than agent. To recover the participatory mode would require changes at every level of the system.

I do not know whether this recovery is possible. The forces working against it are formidable. But I am persuaded by Scarry that the alternative — accepting the speed rhetoric and the centralization it justifies — leads to a country that is neither secure nor free. The Pentagon could not defend itself on September 11. The largest defense budget in human history could not stop nineteen men with box-cutters. What stopped one of them was a small group of citizens with cell phones, twenty-three minutes, and the courage to use their own judgment. There is a lesson in this somewhere. Scarry has done us the service of trying to spell it out.

"While Elaine Scarry correctly identifies the failure of centralized defense on September 11, a truly democratic defense requires more than just domestic decentralization; it necessitates a fundamental dismantling of the American global empire and its focus on power projection over genuine security."

Elaine Scarry’s essay arrives at a moment when the American public is being asked to accept an unprecedented centralization of executive power in the name of an endless war. Her insistence that we evaluate our defense arrangements through the lens of democracy is not only timely but essential. Where she goes astray is in her assumption that the problem is one of scale and speed alone, and that the remedy lies primarily in returning to a mythic constitutional past of citizen-soldiers. In fact, the structural failure of September 11 was not merely that the Pentagon was too hierarchical to defend itself, but that the United States has constructed a global military apparatus that is fundamentally incapable of providing genuine security because it is designed to project power, not to protect people. A democratic defense, if we mean it seriously, would require not just the decentralization Scarry advocates within American borders, but a profound rethinking of the American empire abroad.

Scarry’s juxtaposition of Flight 77 and Flight 93 works brilliantly to expose the vacuity of the speed argument. But she does not push the analysis far enough. Why did the military have no effective plan to intercept Flight 77? Not simply because of bureaucratic sluggishness, but because its attention and resources are overwhelmingly focused on threats that serve the interests of global hegemony. The F-15s and F-16s that patrol American skies on an intermittent basis represent a tiny fraction of the air power that the United States maintains in bases across Europe, the Middle East, and East Asia. The country that spends more on its military than the next fifteen nations combined was unable to scramble jets in time to protect its own capital. This is not an accident of misallocation; it is the logical consequence of a strategic posture that treats the homeland as a sanctuary while projecting force onto others. For decades, the Pentagon’s definition of “defense” has meant the capacity to intervene anywhere on earth, not the capacity to protect Americans from a handful of hijackers. That imbalance is the dark corollary of the executive war power Scarry decries.

Her call to renounce nuclear weapons is the strongest part of the essay, and it deserves to be heard not merely as a domestic constitutional argument but as a matter of global justice. Nuclear weapons are indeed monarchic, but they are also imperial. The United States is the only nation ever to have used them in war, and it now maintains them on hair-trigger alert while insisting that others forswear them. Scarry is right that the world will not take seriously our demands for nonproliferation until we begin to disarm ourselves. But here, too, the vision must go deeper. Disarmament cannot be a unilateral act of American virtue; it must be embedded in a system of mutual, verifiable, and legally binding commitments that eventually eliminate nuclear weapons everywhere. That requires the United States to subject itself to the same international legal constraints it imposes on others—something the current administration is deeply hostile to, as seen in its rejection of the Biological Weapons Convention protocol, the International Criminal Court, and other treaty regimes.

Scarry’s emphasis on the Second Amendment and the militia tradition illustrates the pitfalls of framing democratic defense in purely national terms. The militia was historically a force for settler-colonial expansion and the violent suppression of Native peoples and slave revolts. The “egalitarian model” she praises has a dark lineage that cannot be wished away.
