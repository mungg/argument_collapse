"The Silicon Valley innovation model fails to create inclusive prosperity because it is fundamentally designed for financial extraction rather than local value creation."

Breznitz has performed a valuable service by dismantling the mythology surrounding Silicon Valley's innovation model. Yet I want to push his argument further, because the real problem isn't just that the VC model fails to create inclusive prosperity—it is that the model is fundamentally extractive by design. The moment we acknowledge this, the policy prescriptions become clearer and the scale of the challenge more apparent.

The venture capital industry operates on a specific logic: identify startups with high growth potential, inject capital to scale them rapidly, and exit via acquisition or public listing within a compressed timeframe, typically five to seven years. This timeline is not incidental. It reflects the incentive structure of limited partners—pension funds, endowments, and high-net-worth individuals—who demand returns within their investment horizons. The founders who accept this capital are not building companies; they are building financial instruments. The difference matters enormously.

When Apple emerged from Cupertino, it built manufacturing capacity locally, employed thousands of assembly workers, and created an ecosystem of suppliers across the region. When Silicon Valley operates today, the financial returns flow to Sand Hill Road while the production jobs flow to Shenzhen or, increasingly, to fully automated facilities. The workers who might once have assembled iPhones now compete for gig economy deliveries or precarious warehouse work. This is not a failure of execution; it is the logical outcome of a system designed to maximize financial extraction rather than local value creation.

Breznitz correctly identifies Israel's cautionary tale, but we should understand why the Israeli case unfolded as it did. When 95 percent of venture capital is foreign, and when that capital seeks rapid exits through acquisitions by multinational corporations, the profits necessarily flow outward. The high-tech workers who benefit from fabulous riches are often the same talent that multinationals recruit directly, further draining local capacity. Israel created impressive statistics—NASDAQ listings, R&D intensity ratios—while hollowing out the broader economy. The poverty statistics Breznitz cites are not anomalies; they are evidence that the model works exactly as designed for its primary beneficiaries: foreign investors and the technical elite.

The four-stage framework Breznitz proposes is analytically useful, but I would add a fifth stage that he underemphasizes: the stage of financial engineering and corporate restructuring. Much of what passes for innovation in advanced economies today is actually the application of financial techniques to extract value from existing assets—share buybacks, merger arbitrage, intellectual property securitization. These activities create enormous wealth for those positioned to benefit but generate nothing for local communities. The rise of patent trolls, the offshoring of intellectual property to low-tax jurisdictions, the proliferation of complex corporate structures designed to minimize tax contributions—all of these represent a form of "innovation" that actively undermines inclusive prosperity.

What would a genuinely inclusive innovation policy look like? First, it would treat the flow of profits to foreign investors not as an inevitable feature of globalization but as a policy choice to be examined critically. Countries that have achieved inclusive prosperity through manufacturing—from Germany to South Korea to Taiwan—have done so partly through policies that retain value locally: local content requirements, technology transfer agreements, restrictions on profit repatriation, and industrial policies that favor domestic firms. Second, it would recognize that the "innovation ecosystem" Breznitz rightly emphasizes requires public investment in physical and social infrastructure that cannot be left to market forces. Universities, technical schools, shared manufacturing facilities, and the R&D institutions that bridge them require sustained public commitment that transcends electoral cycles.

Breznitz concludes that communities must make choices about which innovation stages to pursue. This is correct, but the choice cannot be made in a vacuum. It requires a national and international policy environment that supports local capacity building rather than one that systematically advantages financial extraction over productive investment. The Cobalt story is not merely a tale of a mining boom and bust; it is a warning about the structural forces that make local prosperity so difficult to achieve and sustain in the contemporary global economy.

  3. 03High levels of foreign venture capital investment, as seen in Israel, ensure that profits and talent are drained away from the local economy to benefit international stakeholders.

  5. 05Achieving inclusive prosperity requires active policy choices that retain value locally, such as technology transfer agreements and sustained public investment in social infrastructure.
